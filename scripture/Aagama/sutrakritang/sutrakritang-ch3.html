Sutrakritanga / Chapter 3

ConsciousnessCetana

The soul that knows is different from the matter it knows.

The soul is conscious; matter is not. The earth, water, fire, and air that make up the body are not the self. The self is the pure knowing awareness that inhabits the body. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of genuine non-attachment and the doorway to liberation.

चेयणं परिजाणिया, अचेयणं च जाणिया।
एवं जे बुज्झइ धीरो, स मुच्चइ न बज्झइ॥

"Understanding what is conscious and what is unconscious — the wise one who thus comprehends is freed and not bound." — Sutrakritanga 3

About This Chapter

Cetana

The third chapter addresses one of the deepest questions in Jain philosophy: what is the soul, and what is it not? The teaching systematically distinguishes consciousness — the knowing, aware soul — from all forms of matter, including the four classical elements (earth, water, fire, air) and the body they compose. Several philosophical schools of the period taught that the soul is identical to the body or to some material element. This chapter refutes each version of that claim.

The practical consequence of this distinction is non-attachment. When the monk truly understands that he is the conscious knowing principle — not the body, not the mind, not the accumulated personality — he naturally stops clinging to these material things. The chapter closes with the universal teaching: understanding the soul in oneself is understanding the soul in all beings, which is the philosophical root of non-violence.

Part I — Consciousness and Matter Distinguished
3.1

चेयणं परिजाणिया, अचेयणं च जाणिया। ॥३.१॥

Having understood what is conscious and having understood what is unconscious — one is freed.

The chapter opens with its central distinction: consciousness versus unconsciousness, soul versus matter. In Jain philosophy, the soul is by definition conscious — it knows, it experiences, it is aware. Matter is by definition unconscious — it does not know; it is known. Confusing the two is the root of bondage. Identifying the conscious self with an unconscious body, with unconscious sensations, with unconscious social roles — this confusion generates attachment, and attachment generates karma.

The simple version: The soul is the knower; the body is what is known. Mixing up the two is where suffering begins.

SoulMatterConsciousness
3.2

जो य जीवो सो अजीवो, एयं नाणं न विज्जइ। ॥३.२॥

That which is the soul is not the non-soul — this knowledge does not reside in the ignorant.

The sutra states what sounds obvious but is, in practice, deeply counterintuitive: the soul is not the body. The soul is not the emotions. The soul is not the accumulated opinions and preferences and habits that make up the personality. All of these are material in the Jain sense — they are processes of matter interacting with matter. Only the knowing awareness at the center is the soul. The ignorant person does not have this knowledge — not because they haven't heard the teaching, but because they haven't realized it.

The simple version: The soul and the body are genuinely different things. Most people live as though they are the same — that confusion is ignorance.

Soul vs Non-SoulIgnoranceKnowledge
3.3

आया अणायाए मिस्से, मिस्सं नाणी विभावए। ॥३.३॥

The soul is mingled with non-soul — the knowing one distinguishes them.

This sutra introduces a crucial nuance: the soul and matter are intermixed in ordinary experience, not neatly separated. The soul is embedded in a body, surrounded by karmic matter, expressed through material faculties like speech and action. The task of the monk is not to physically separate soul from body — that is impossible while alive — but to distinguish them in understanding. The knowing one sees through the mixture to what is soul and what is matter.

The simple version: In everyday life, soul and matter are mixed together. The monk's job is to see clearly which is which — not to physically separate them.

MixtureDiscernmentWisdom
3.4

न सो आया जो गिण्हइ, न सो आया जो मुयइ। ॥३.४॥

The soul is not that which grasps; the soul is not that which releases.

This is a subtle and important sutra. Grasping and releasing are functions of karma and the senses — they are material processes. The soul itself neither grasps nor releases; it is the pure knowing that witnesses these processes. When a person grasps at a pleasant object, it is the karmic and sensory apparatus that grasps — not the soul. The soul that is confused with the grasping identifies with it. The soul that is understood as distinct remains the witness.

The simple version: The soul doesn't grab or let go — it knows. Grabbing and letting go are what the material self does. The real self watches.

Pure WitnessGraspingSoul Nature
3.5

चेयणा सा ण माइया, पडिलेहाए जाणिया। ॥३.५॥

Consciousness is not measurable — it is known through direct insight.

Matter can be measured, quantified, weighed, counted. Consciousness cannot. The soul is not a quantity of anything. It is pure knowing awareness. This means that consciousness cannot be confirmed through the methods used to verify material things. It is known through a different kind of knowing — direct inner insight, the immediate awareness of awareness itself. This sutra points toward the meditative dimension of Jain practice.

The simple version: You can't measure the soul the way you measure a stone. It is known by turning awareness inward, not outward.

ConsciousnessDirect InsightImmeasurable
3.6

परमाणुं न जाणंति, ण य जाणंति दव्वियं। ॥३.६॥

They do not know the ultimate particle, nor do they know the nature of substance.

This sutra critiques those who reduce everything to matter — materialist thinkers who claim the universe is nothing but particles. Even if one knew every particle in the universe, one would still not have explained consciousness, because consciousness is not a particle. The Jain view holds that the universe contains two fundamental categories: matter (including what we would call energy and physical phenomena) and soul (consciousness). Neither is reducible to the other.

The simple version: Even complete knowledge of the physical world doesn't explain why there is experience at all. The soul is a different kind of thing.

Materialism RefutedSoul RealismPhilosophy
3.7

चेयणं जाण अप्पाणं, अचेयणं च जाणिया। ॥३.७॥

Know the self as conscious; know the non-self as unconscious.

The sutra restates the core distinction in the form of a direct instruction. "Know" here means more than intellectual acknowledgment — it means realize, embody, act from. The monk who truly knows himself as conscious — as pure knowing awareness — and knows everything else as unconscious matter, has the foundation for genuine non-attachment. There is nothing in the material world to identify with, because the self is clearly something altogether different.

The simple version: You are the one who is aware. Everything else — including your body, your thoughts, your feelings — is what you are aware of. Know the difference.

Self-KnowledgeConsciousnessNon-Attachment
3.8

ण य मे कायसंपएसे, नाणं दसणं च विज्जइ। ॥३.८॥

Knowledge and perception do not reside in the body — they are properties of the soul alone.

This sutra makes the philosophical point explicit: knowing and perceiving are not functions of the brain or the nervous system in the Jain view. They are properties of the soul. The body is the instrument through which the soul engages with the material world, but the knowing is the soul's own. When the body dies, the soul's capacity to know does not die — it continues, embodied in a new form, until it reaches liberation.

The simple version: Knowing and seeing are what the soul does, not what the body does. The body is a vehicle; the soul is the driver.

KnowledgePerceptionSoul Property
3.9

जो उवलंभए आया, सो मुच्चइ न बज्झइ। ॥३.९॥

The one who realizes the soul is freed and not bound.

"Realizes" — not merely understands but directly experiences — the soul as distinct from matter. This realization is the turning point of the spiritual life. Before it, all practice is preparation. After it, liberation is inevitable. The monk who has this realization acts without clinging to results, speaks without craving approval, and endures hardship without resentment, because he knows himself to be the soul — and the soul is untouched by any of these things.

The simple version: The moment you truly realize what you are — the soul — freedom begins. Everything else follows from that.

RealizationLiberationFreedom
3.10

एवं भेयं विजाणिया, धम्मे ठाइज्ज पंडिए। ॥३.१०॥

Thus knowing this distinction, the learned one should stand firm in the teaching.

The first section closes with a call to action: knowing the distinction between soul and matter, stand firm in it. Don't just understand it theoretically and then live as if the body is the self again the moment hardship arises. "Standing firm in the teaching" means making this understanding the stable orientation of one's life — the lens through which every sensation, every interaction, every moment of practice is viewed.

The simple version: Understanding the soul-matter distinction is the beginning, not the end. The monk must live from this understanding, not just know it.

Part II — The Four Elements Are Not the Soul
3.11

पुढवी आउ तेउ वाऊ, एए चत्तारि धाउणो। ॥३.११॥

Earth, water, fire, and air — these are the four elements.

The four-element theory was common to several Indian philosophical traditions of the era. Here the Jain text introduces it in order to systematically refute the claim that the soul is identical to any element or to the elements combined. The elements are material substances; the soul is not material. The distinction will be developed in the sutras that follow.

The simple version: Earth, water, fire, air — these four are what the physical world is made of. The soul is none of them.

Four ElementsMatterFoundation
3.12

न आया पुढवीकाए, न आया आउकाए। ॥३.१२॥

The soul is not the earth-body; the soul is not the water-body.

Some philosophical schools taught that the soul is identical to the body, and since the body is made of earth and water (and fire and air), the soul would be identical to these elements. The Jain refutation is direct: the elements are unconscious; the soul is conscious. An unconscious thing cannot become conscious by being arranged in a particular way — the Jain argument holds that consciousness must have an independent origin.

The simple version: You are not made of earth and water. The part of you that knows — that is aware — is not a material element.

EarthWaterSoul Distinct
3.13

न आया तेउकाए, न आया वाउकाए। ॥३.१३॥

The soul is not the fire-body; the soul is not the air-body.

The refutation continues through all four elements. Fire and air were sometimes proposed as particularly close to consciousness — fire because it is active and consuming, air because it is invisible and all-pervasive. But the Jain teaching holds that neither heat nor movement is consciousness. The soul is distinct from all material processes, however subtle or energetic they may be.

The simple version: Not fire, not air, not any physical thing — the soul is none of these. It is something categorically different.

FireAirSoul Distinct
3.14

एएहिं धाउहिं भिन्ने, आया अण्णो विभाविए। ॥३.१४॥

Distinct from these four elements, the soul is understood as something altogether different.

"Altogether different" is a strong philosophical claim. The soul does not emerge from the elements, is not a property of the elements combined, and is not reducible to them. This position — called soul-realism — is one of the defining features of Jain philosophy. The soul has its own existence, its own qualities, its own modes of development and liberation.

The simple version: The soul is not a by-product of matter. It is its own kind of thing, with its own existence.

Soul RealismIndependent SoulJain Philosophy
3.15

सरीरसामग्गि नाम, न सो आया ति नाणिणो। ॥३.१५॥

The assemblage of the body — that is not the soul, say the knowers.

This sutra addresses a more sophisticated materialist claim: that the soul is not one element but the system of elements working together — the body as a whole. The knowers, the realized teachers, say no: the body-as-system is still material, still unconscious, still subject to birth and death and decay. The soul is not the system. The soul is what uses and inhabits the system.

The simple version: Even the whole body put together is not the soul. The soul is what lives in the body, not what the body is.

Body-SystemMaterialism RefutedSoul
3.16

न देहो न इंदिया, न मणो न य आसवा। ॥३.१६॥

Not the body, not the senses, not the mind, not the influxes — none of these is the soul.

The sutra expands the list of what the soul is not. Even the mind — the thinking, reasoning, remembering, imagining faculty — is not the soul in Jain philosophy. The mind is a subtle material faculty. The soul is what uses the mind, not what the mind is. Similarly, the influxes of karma — the tendencies and passions that drive action — are not the soul but the soul's temporary condition. This precise negative definition clears away every layer of misidentification.

The simple version: The soul is not your body, not your senses, not your thoughts, not your emotional tendencies. It is what remains when you see through all of those.

Neti NetiMindSoul Identity
3.17

जो चेव जाणए भावे, सो आया ति निच्छए। ॥३.१७॥

That which knows the states of existence — that, it is established, is the soul.

After all the negative definitions, here is the positive one: the soul is that which knows. It is the knowing itself — not any particular content of knowledge, not any particular object known, but the pure knowing awareness that underlies all experience. This is both philosophically precise and practically useful: when you want to find the soul, you look not at any object of experience but at the awareness that makes experience possible.

The simple version: The soul is the knowing. Whatever it is in you that is aware — that is the soul.

Pure KnowingSoul DefinedAwareness
3.18

एयं जाणिय आया, बाहिरं नाणुसोयइ। ॥३.१८॥

Having known the soul thus, one does not follow after external things.

The practical consequence of knowing the soul is non-attachment to externals. When you know yourself to be the pure knowing awareness, you do not need objects to complete you. You do not follow after wealth, status, pleasure, or approval, because you have found what you were actually looking for in all those pursuits — the awareness that was already there. The monk who knows the soul rests in that knowing rather than chasing what is outside.

The simple version: When you know what you really are, you stop chasing things outside yourself. The restless search ends.

Part III — The Path of Correct Understanding
3.19

जे बुज्झइ अप्पाणं, से बुज्झइ परं च। ॥३.१९॥

One who understands the self also understands others.

This is a remarkable statement about the relationship between self-knowledge and knowledge of others. When one truly understands the soul as the conscious knowing principle, one understands that every other being also has this same conscious principle at their core. This is the philosophical root of the Jain commitment to non-violence: every living being is a soul in the process of trying to find liberation. Understanding this makes harm to others the same as harm to oneself.

The simple version: Know yourself and you know everyone. Every living being has the same soul — the same conscious knowing — at their center.

Self-KnowledgeUniversal SoulNon-Violence Root
3.20

जो आया सो परो आया, एयं सम्मं विजाणिया। ॥३.२०॥

What is the soul of oneself is also the soul of others — truly understanding this.

The sutra makes the universality of the soul explicit. Not "similar souls" or "souls of the same kind" — the soul-nature is the same. The specific contents of karma and personality differ from soul to soul, but the conscious, knowing principle itself is the same quality in every living being. This insight — when genuinely realized rather than merely repeated — dissolves the felt distinction between self and other that underlies all violence and exploitation.

The simple version: Your soul and every other soul are the same kind of thing. What matters to you matters to them. What hurts you hurts them.

Universal ConsciousnessOnenessCompassion
3.21

समयाए सव्वभूएसु, दंडं न निक्खिवे। ॥३.२१॥

Seeing all beings equally, one should lay down the stick — causing no harm to any.

"Laying down the stick" is a vivid image of renouncing violence in thought, word, and action. The monk who has understood the soul-nature of all beings cannot pick up the stick — cannot harm, cannot threaten, cannot even wish harm — because to harm another is to harm what is essentially the same as oneself. Seeing equally does not mean ignoring difference; it means recognizing the shared consciousness beneath all the differences.

The simple version: When you see yourself in every being, violence becomes impossible. You put down the weapon because there is no "enemy" anymore.

Non-ViolenceEqual SeeingAhimsa
3.22

न य आया कत्ता कम्मस्स, न य भोत्ता अहंकारे। ॥३.२२॥

The soul is not the doer of karma; it is not the experiencer through ego.

This sutra presents one of the most subtle teachings in Jain philosophy. In the ignorant state, the soul appears to be both the doer (creating karma through action driven by passion) and the experiencer (suffering the results through the sense of "I"). But in reality, in its pure nature, the soul is neither. The doing and the experiencing belong to the karmic-material complex — the soul's pure mode is knowing, not doing and experiencing in the bondage-creating sense.

The simple version: In its true nature, the soul doesn't create karma and doesn't suffer karma. It's only the confused soul — wrapped in ego — that does. The pure soul just knows.

Pure SoulEgoNon-Doer
3.23

अहंकार निवत्तिया, परिण्णाय समाहिए। ॥३.२३॥

With ego dissolved, having fully understood, he remains composed.

"Ego" in the Jain sense is not the healthy sense of self-respect or agency — it is the specific confusion that identifies the soul with the body, with social roles, with accumulated possessions and relationships. When this specific confusion dissolves through correct understanding, what remains is not emptiness but pure knowing, established in equanimity. The monk does not lose himself — he finds himself, without the distorting overlay of ego.

The simple version: When ego is dissolved, what's left is pure, clear awareness. Not nothing — something much cleaner and freer than before.

Ego DissolutionEquanimityPure Awareness
3.24

न जाइ न जरा न मच्चू, आयाए नत्थि णिच्छए। ॥३.२४॥

There is no birth, no old age, no death — this is the established truth of the soul.

The soul does not actually undergo birth, aging, and death — those are processes of the body. The soul transmigrates, takes new embodiments, and ultimately reaches liberation — but the soul itself is not born and does not die. In liberation, the soul exists in its pure state, free from all embodiment. This sutra is pointing toward the soul's immortality — not the continuation of the personality, but the persistence of the pure conscious principle.

The simple version: The real you doesn't age or die. The body does. The soul continues, until it reaches final liberation.

Soul ImmortalityBirth and DeathLiberation
3.25

एस मग्गे विसोहीए, जेण मुच्चंति मेहावी। ॥३.२५॥

This is the path of purification by which the wise are freed.

The chapter identifies its teaching as "the path of purification." Purification means the progressive removal of karmic matter from the soul — not making the soul better (the soul is already perfect in its nature) but uncovering what the soul already is. The wise are freed through this path: through understanding the distinction between soul and matter, practicing non-attachment, enduring sensation without reaction, and thereby exhausting accumulated karma without creating new karma.

The simple version: The path is purification — not improving the soul but uncovering it. Remove what's in the way, and what remains is freedom.

PurificationThe PathLiberation
3.26

एवं चेयणियं नाऊण, जाणिया सव्वसंपहू। ॥३.२६॥

Thus, having understood the chapter on consciousness, one knows all things fully. — iti bemi

The closing "iti bemi" seals the chapter with the traditional affirmation of the Tirthankaric transmission. The claim — that understanding the soul-matter distinction amounts to knowing all things fully — is the Jain view that the fundamental philosophical problem of existence is the confusion of soul and matter. Resolve that confusion through genuine understanding, and the entire structure of bondage, karma, and liberation becomes transparent. Nothing further need be added.

The simple version: Understand the soul, understand everything. The teacher has spoken.
