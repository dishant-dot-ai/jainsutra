# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 8: Mahababhaviya (महाभविय)

**Translation by:** JainSutra.org
**Source:** Ardhamagadhi Prakrit — Sutrakritanga, Book 1 (Prathama Shrutaskandha)
**Chapter:** Eighth Adhyayana (अष्टम् अध्ययन)
**Total Sutras:** 25
**Theme:** The companion chapter to Chapter 7 — describing both the higher rebirths available to those who practice restraint and the hellish rebirths awaiting those who commit grave violence. Vivid descriptions of heavenly peace are contrasted with the torments of hell. The central teaching: karma determines the quality of every next existence, and complete liberation comes only from total renunciation — not merely from better rebirth within the cycle.

---

## PART I — The Heavenly Rebirths (Sutras 1–10)

---

### SUTRA 8.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे संजया सीलवंता, तवस्सी णिव्वियारया ।
ते देवलोए उववज्जंति, सुद्धं सुक्कं पहाणयं ॥८.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je saṃjayā sīlavantā, tavassi ṇivviyārayā |
te devaloe uvavajjaṃti, suddhaṃ sukkaṃ pahāṇayaṃ || 8.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who are restrained, virtuous, austere, and free from passions — they are reborn in the divine realm, in purity, brightness, and excellence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je* = those who | *saṃjayā* = are restrained | *sīlavantā* = virtuous, of good conduct | *tavassi* = austere, given to austerity | *ṇivviyārayā* = free from passions, without turbulence | *te* = they | *devaloe* = in the divine realm, heavenly world | *uvavajjaṃti* = are reborn | *suddhaṃ* = in purity | *sukkaṃ* = in brightness, in whiteness of soul | *pahāṇayaṃ* = in excellence, in the foremost state

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter opens with the positive counterpoint to Chapter 7's catalog of lower rebirths. The qualities that produce heavenly rebirth are named clearly: restraint (not harming), virtue (ethical conduct), austerity (reducing desires through discipline), and freedom from passions (the four inner enemies of anger, pride, deceit, and greed). These are not obscure mystical requirements but the practical qualities cultivated through consistent practice of the five great vows. The heavenly realm is described with three adjectives: pure, bright, and excellent. In Jain cosmology, the soul's natural state is pure luminosity — hellish rebirths represent the darkening of this natural light, while heavenly rebirths represent the partial recovery of it. But even the heavenly state is not the final destination.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who practice restraint, live virtuously, do genuine discipline, and overcome their inner passions are reborn in heavenly realms of purity and light.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the quality of your inner light right now — do you feel it getting clearer or cloudier through your daily choices?

### SUTRA 8.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

देवलोए सुहं खाइं, णंदणंदणवणेसु य ।
विमाणेसु य रम्मेसु, देवी णच्चंति तत्थ य ॥८.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

devaloe suhaṃ khāiṃ, naṃdanaṃdaṇavaṇesu ya |
vimāṇesu ya rammesu, devī naccaṃti tattha ya || 8.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In the divine realm there are pleasures, in the groves of joy and delight, in the beautiful divine chariots — and divine beings dance there.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*devaloe* = in the divine realm | *suhaṃ* = pleasures | *khāiṃ* = enjoyments, experiences | *naṃdana-naṃdaṇa-vaṇesu* = in the groves of Nandana (the heavenly pleasure grove), in the delight-groves | *vimāṇesu* = in divine chariots/abodes | *rammesu* = beautiful, delightful | *devī* = divine beings, goddesses | *naccaṃti* = dance | *tattha* = there

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra describes the sensory pleasures of the heavenly realm in vivid terms — lush pleasure-groves, beautiful divine vehicles that serve as both abodes and transport, and the dancing of divine beings. Jain cosmology does not present heaven as a place of formless meditation but as an existence of refined and intense pleasurable experience. The pleasures are real, beautiful, and far more vivid than anything available in human life. However — and this is crucial — they are still pleasures of the senses, which means they are still within the domain of karma and attachment. The divine beings who dance in these groves are not liberated; they are enjoying the fruits of their good karma, just as animals suffer the consequences of their bad karma. The pleasures are extraordinary but temporary.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Heavenly existence is genuinely wonderful — beautiful gardens, amazing abodes, divine beings full of joy and dance — but it is still within the cycle, not beyond it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If heaven is still within the cycle of karma and not truly free, why do many spiritual traditions present it as the ultimate goal — and what does that tell you about what those traditions value?

### SUTRA 8.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दिव्वाइं भोगभोगाइं, भुंजंता देवया तहिं ।
आउखयं च पावंति, आउखीणे चवंति ते ॥८.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

divvāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ, bhuṃjaṃtā devayā tahiṃ |
āukhayaṃ ca pāvaṃti, āukhīṇe cavaṃti te || 8.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The divine beings there, enjoying divine pleasures — they reach the exhaustion of their lifespan; when the lifespan is exhausted, they fall.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*divvāiṃ* = divine | *bhogabhogāiṃ* = pleasures of enjoyment | *bhuṃjaṃtā* = enjoying | *devayā* = divine beings | *tahiṃ* = there | *āukhayaṃ* = exhaustion of lifespan | *ca* = and | *pāvaṃti* = reach, attain | *āukhīṇe* = when the lifespan is exhausted | *cavaṃti* = fall, descend | *te* = they

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is the pivot point of the chapter's understanding of heaven: it ends. Divine beings enjoy immense pleasures for lifespans measured in cosmic time periods — but eventually the karma that sustained the heavenly birth is exhausted, and the divine being falls. "Falls" (cavaṃti) is a strong word — the descent from heavenly existence when karma runs out can be dramatic. If the being has not used any of the heavenly opportunity to cultivate spiritual practice, they may fall to lower forms of existence as their accumulated good karma was all spent on heavenly pleasure rather than on spiritual progress. This is the Jain critique of heaven as a goal: seeking heaven rather than liberation means you are still playing within the rules of karma, not transcending them.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even divine beings eventually exhaust their good karma and fall from heaven — it was never permanent, just a very long and pleasant stay.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you work toward pleasant temporary situations in your spiritual life — or toward the complete, permanent freedom that doesn't end?

### SUTRA 8.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तम्हा सव्वे पहिट्ठा, पुण्णकम्मस्स संचयं ।
णो मुच्चंति भवात्तो, विण्णू ण रज्जए तहिं ॥८.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tamhā savve pahiṭṭhā, puṇṇakammassa saṃcayaṃ |
ṇo muccaṃti bhavatto, viṇṇū ṇa rajjae tahiṃ || 8.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Therefore, though all are delighted (in heaven), through the accumulation of meritorious karma — they are not freed from existence; the wise one is not attached there.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tamhā* = therefore | *savve* = all | *pahiṭṭhā* = delighted, pleased | *puṇṇakammassa* = of meritorious karma | *saṃcayaṃ* = accumulation | *ṇo muccaṃti* = are not freed | *bhavatto* = from existence, from the cycle | *viṇṇū* = the wise one | *ṇa rajjae* = is not attached | *tahiṃ* = there

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the essential philosophical point the chapter makes about heaven: accumulated good karma produces heavenly birth, and heavenly beings are genuinely happy there — but they are still not free. The cycle of existence (bhava) has not been broken. Freedom from the cycle requires something different from good karma: it requires the dissolution of all karma — both good and bad — through complete non-accumulation and the burning away of accumulated karma through austerity and equanimity. The wise person, understanding this mechanism, does not become attached to the heavenly rebirth as a goal. They aim higher: not a temporary heavenly stay but permanent liberation. This is not pessimism about heaven but realism about what liberation actually means.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even though heaven is wonderful and everyone there is happy, they are still not free — and the truly wise person isn't even attached to going there, because they want complete freedom, not just a very good next life.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In your own spiritual aspiration, are you working toward lasting freedom — or toward a more comfortable situation, even if that situation is only temporary?

### SUTRA 8.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मो य कित्तिई सगग, दुच्चरे लोगुत्तमे ।
अहिंसा परमो धम्मो, जो ण पाओ ण णिंदिए ॥८.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammo ya kittiī sagga, duccāre loguttame |
ahiṃsā paramo dhammo, jo ṇa pāo ṇa ṇindīe || 8.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Religion leads to heaven and to what is supreme in the world, difficult to practice; non-violence is the supreme religion, which is neither sinful nor blameworthy.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*dhammo ya* = and religion | *kittiī* = leads to, produces | *sagga* = heaven | *duccāre* = difficult to practice | *loguttame* = supreme in the world | *ahiṃsā* = non-violence | *paramo* = supreme | *dhammo* = religion | *jo* = which | *ṇa pāo* = not sinful | *ṇa ṇindīe* = not blameworthy, not to be blamed

**5. COMMENTARY**

Religion — the true religion of non-violence and the five vows — leads to heaven as an intermediate reward and ultimately to what is supreme. But the teaching emphasizes that this religion is "difficult to practice" (duccāre) — not in the sense that the rules are hard to memorize, but that actually following them requires sustained effort against the grain of habitual behavior, social norms, and biological instincts. Non-violence in the complete Jain sense is one of the most demanding ethical commitments any tradition has ever articulated. The description of non-violence as "neither sinful nor blameworthy" seems obvious until you recall the context: in Mahavira's time and society, critics of Vedic sacrifice could be accused of impiety. The teaching asserts that the religion of non-violence is beyond reproach.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The path of non-violence leads to heavenly existence and ultimately to what is highest — it is genuinely demanding to practice but completely pure and beyond blame.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What makes the practice of genuine non-violence genuinely difficult for you personally — not in theory but in your actual life?

### SUTRA 8.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जो देवलोए उज्जोए, तं ण सक्को अणुगंतए ।
सव्वो वि तित्थयरो लोए, णाणेण य तवेण य ॥८.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jo devaloe ujjoe, taṃ ṇa sakko aṇugaṃtae |
savvo vi titthayaro loe, ṇāṇeṇa ya taveṇa ya || 8.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The radiance that exists in the divine realm — even the lord of the gods cannot follow it; the ford-maker is supreme in the world through knowledge and through austerity.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jo* = which | *devaloe* = in the divine realm | *ujjoe* = radiance, light | *taṃ* = that | *ṇa sakko* = the lord of the gods (Shakra/Indra) cannot | *aṇugaṃtae* = follow, match | *savvo vi* = supreme among all | *titthayaro* = ford-maker, Tirthankara | *loe* = in the world | *ṇāṇeṇa ya* = through knowledge | *taveṇa ya* = and through austerity

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra makes a striking comparative claim: the Tirthankara — the ford-maker, the one who has attained complete liberation through total renunciation — surpasses even the most powerful gods in radiance and excellence. Indra (Shakra), the king of the gods, is the most powerful divine being in the cosmic hierarchy, yet he cannot match the soul-radiance of a fully liberated being. This reflects the Jain understanding that liberation is categorically different from even the highest form of existence within the cycle. The gods are powerful because of accumulated good karma; the Tirthankara is beyond karma entirely. Power within the cycle (even divine power) is fundamentally different from the transcendence of the cycle itself. The path there is knowledge and austerity — the two wings of liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even the most powerful god cannot match the inner radiance of a fully liberated being — liberation is something even greater than the best heaven.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you imagine liberation to actually feel like — can you form any genuine intuition of it, or does it remain abstract?

### SUTRA 8.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सीलेण तवसा चेव, संजमेण य णिच्चसो ।
अप्पाणमेव भावेज्जा, जाइजरामरणाणुगं ॥८.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sīleṇa tavasā ceva, saṃjameṇa ya niccaso |
appāṇameva bhāvejjā, jāijarāmaraṇāṇugaṃ || 8.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Through virtue, through austerity, and through restraint always — let one cultivate the self, following along with birth, old age, and death.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sīleṇa* = through virtue | *tavasā* = through austerity | *ceva* = indeed, certainly | *saṃjameṇa ya* = and through restraint | *niccaso* = always, constantly | *appāṇam* = the self | *eva* = indeed | *bhāvejjā* = let one cultivate, develop | *jāijarāmaraṇāṇugaṃ* = following birth, old age, and death (throughout the cycle)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The practice is described as continuous — not seasonal, not occasional, but always. The triad of virtue, austerity, and restraint must be maintained throughout all phases of human life — through youth, middle age, and old age, through health and illness, through prosperity and hardship. The phrase "following along with birth, old age, and death" is a reminder that practice must persist through the natural human trajectory. It is easy to be virtuous in youth; maintaining it through the humiliations and limitations of old age and approaching death is the true test. The instruction to "cultivate the self" (appāṇameva bhāvejjā) reinforces the consistent theme: only you can do this work, and it must be done throughout the span of this existence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Through virtue, discipline, and self-restraint — not just sometimes but always — keep working on yourself throughout your entire life.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

At what point in your life do you imagine you will stop needing to work on yourself — and is that expectation realistic?

### SUTRA 8.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तवस्सी संजए भिक्खू, संजोगपरिवज्जए ।
अप्पमत्तो सया जागो, णिव्वाणं सो अहिगच्छए ॥८.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tavassi saṃjae bhikkhū, saṃjogaparivajjae |
appamatto sayā jāgo, ṇivvāṇaṃ so ahigaccae || 8.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The austere, restrained monk, who avoids attachments, ever un-negligent, always awake — that one attains liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tavassi* = austere | *saṃjae* = restrained | *bhikkhū* = monk, one who lives on alms | *saṃjogaparivajjae* = avoids attachments, renounces connections | *appamatto* = un-negligent, not heedless | *sayā* = always | *jāgo* = awake, alert | *ṇivvāṇaṃ* = liberation | *so* = that one | *ahigaccae* = attains, reaches

**5. COMMENTARY**

The liberation formula is compressed into this sutra: austerity plus restraint plus avoidance of attachments plus unbroken wakefulness equals liberation. The word "attachments" (saṃjoga) here refers not just to obvious possessions but to all the connections — social, emotional, material — that anchor the soul to the cycle. The monk who has gradually loosened these connections has reduced the karmic pull that would bind them to another rebirth. "Always awake" (sayā jāgo) connects directly to the earlier warning about negligence: the monk who practices diligently but falls asleep spiritually — becomes automatic, mechanical, habitual — loses the decisive quality of present awareness that makes practice effective. Liberation requires both the outer form (austerity, restraint) and the inner quality (unbroken wakefulness).

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk who is genuinely disciplined, avoids all clinging attachments, and stays spiritually awake throughout — that person attains liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would "always spiritually awake" actually look like in your daily life — and how far from that are you right now?

### SUTRA 8.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

चउव्विहाहारविरओ, इंदियाइं निरुंभए ।
परिसहाइं खमे धीरे, भवसायर तरइ सो ॥८.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

cauvvihāhāravirao, iṃdiyāiṃ niruṃbhae |
parisahāiṃ khame dhīre, bhavasāyara tarai so || 8.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who has renounced the four types of food, who restrains the senses, who endures hardships with steadiness — that one crosses the ocean of existence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*cauvvihāhāra* = four types of food (eating, drinking, tasting, breathing-in of particles) | *virao* = renounced, abstained from | *iṃdiyāiṃ* = senses | *niruṃbhae* = restrains, stops | *parisahāiṃ* = hardships, afflictions | *khame* = endures, bears | *dhīre* = with steadiness, with courage | *bhavasāyara* = ocean of existence | *tarai* = crosses | *so* = that one

**5. COMMENTARY**

The four types of food (āhāra) in Jain understanding encompass not just what we eat and drink but also what we taste (condiments, flavors taken in the mouth) and the fine material particles taken in through breathing. This comprehensive definition of "food" shows how thoroughly the Jain tradition analyzes intake and its karmic consequences. Restraining the senses goes beyond not eating meat — it means training all five senses not to grasp at their objects of pleasure. Enduring hardship with steadiness (parisaha-khamā) — being able to face cold, heat, hunger, thirst, discomfort, and social challenges without losing inner composure — is the specific practice that burns accumulated karma. The image of crossing the ocean of existence is one of the most vivid in the Sutrakritanga: liberation is the far shore, and the ocean between is vast.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The one who controls what they take in, restrains their senses, and endures discomfort with steady courage — that person crosses over the enormous sea of repeated existence.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What hardship in your current life could you bear more steadily — using it as practice rather than simply enduring or trying to escape it?

### SUTRA 8.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य णरगा जे य सुरगा, कम्मेण पच्छिया ते ।
धीरो कम्मं परिक्खाय, मुच्चइ भवसायरा ॥८.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya naragā je ya suragā, kammeṇa pacchiyā te |
dhīro kammaṃ parikkāya, muccai bhavasāyarā || 8.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Hells and heavens — both are purchased by karma; the wise one, having examined karma fully, is freed from the ocean of existence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = those | *naragā* = hells | *je ya* = and those | *suragā* = heavens | *kammeṇa* = by karma | *pacchiyā* = purchased, acquired | *te* = they (both) | *dhīro* = the wise one, the steady | *kammaṃ* = karma | *parikkāya* = having examined fully | *muccai* = is freed | *bhavasāyarā* = from the ocean of existence

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the chapter's featured verse and its central philosophical statement. Both hells and heavens are created by karma — one by bad karma, one by good karma — and both are, therefore, within the cycle of existence, not beyond it. The ordinary religious person aims to go to heaven through good karma and avoid hell through avoiding bad karma. The Jain teaching goes deeper: the truly wise person examines the mechanism of karma itself — understands that any karma, good or bad, binds the soul to continued existence — and therefore aims to be free from all karma, not merely to have better karma. "Having examined karma fully" means not just knowing intellectually that karma binds but investigating one's own karma as a daily practice: where is it coming in? where am I accumulating? what habits produce it? This thorough examination is what enables liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Both hell and heaven are produced by karma — and the truly wise person, understanding how karma works completely, aims to be free from the whole mechanism, not just to have a better next life.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever seriously examined how karma actually accumulates in your daily life — not the concept but the actual moment-to-moment mechanics in your own actions and reactions?

### SUTRA 8.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य हिंसा करेंति लोए, उद्दामा उत्थिया बहुसो ।
ते णरगं उव्वज्जंति, घोरं दुक्खं वेयंति ते ॥८.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya hiṃsā karaṃti loe, uddāmā utthiyā bahuso |
te ṇaragaṃ uvvajjaṃti, ghoraṃ dukkhaṃ veyaṃti te || 8.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who commit violence in the world — unbridled, arisen in wrong ways many times — they are reborn in hell, experiencing terrible suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = those who | *hiṃsā* = violence | *karaṃti* = do, commit | *loe* = in the world | *uddāmā* = unbridled, without restraint | *utthiyā* = arisen (in wrong paths) | *bahuso* = many times | *te* = they | *ṇaragaṃ* = hell | *uvvajjaṃti* = are reborn, descend into | *ghoraṃ* = terrible, frightful | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering | *veyaṃti* = experience

**5. COMMENTARY**

The second section of the chapter pivots from heavenly rebirths to hellish ones. The person who commits violence "unbridled" — without any internal restraint, driven by passion, habit, or deliberate cruelty — accumulates the karma that produces hellish rebirth. The word "arisen many times" (utthiyā bahuso) suggests repeated, habitual violence: not an isolated incident but a pattern of life. Jain cosmology describes multiple levels of hell, each with intensifying degrees of suffering, corresponding to the severity and extent of the karma accumulated. The "terrible suffering" (ghoraṃ dukkhaṃ) described in hells is not metaphorical in the Jain tradition — it is considered an actual description of a realm of existence that certain souls genuinely inhabit.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who live violently without any restraint — doing it over and over — are headed for hellish rebirths where the suffering they caused comes back to them intensified.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What habitual patterns in your life involve harm — and are they getting better or more entrenched over time?

### SUTRA 8.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णरगे भोंति महावाया, सत्ता बहुविहाइं च ।
तिव्वा दुक्खा अणंताओ, तत्थ वेयंति णारया ॥८.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇarage bhoṃti mahāvāyā, sattā bahuvihāiṃ ca |
tivvā dukhā aṇaṃtāo, tattha veyaṃti ṇārayā || 8.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In hell there are great winds, beings of many kinds, and sharp, endless sufferings — which the hell-beings experience there.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇarage* = in hell | *bhoṃti* = exist, are | *mahāvāyā* = great winds | *sattā* = beings | *bahuvihāiṃ* = of many kinds | *ca* = and | *tivvā* = sharp, intense | *dukhā* = sufferings | *aṇaṃtāo* = endless, without end | *tattha* = there | *veyaṃti* = experience | *ṇārayā* = hell-beings

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Jain description of hell is vivid and physical. Great winds — searingly hot or bitterly cold — constantly assault hell-beings. Various types of beings (including beings that torment the hell-dwellers) populate the hellish realms. The sufferings are described as both intense and endless — not in the absolute sense (Jain philosophy holds that no rebirth lasts forever) but in the experiential sense: from within the experience of hell, there appears to be no end. The hell-being has no reliable awareness of when their punishment will conclude; they exist in a state of seemingly limitless suffering with no clear horizon of relief. This describes the karma of extreme violence: those who caused suffering without limit in their lives experience suffering without apparent limit in their next existence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Hell is full of intense and seemingly endless suffering — violent winds, torments of every kind — experienced by beings who created enormous harm in their previous lives.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever experienced a state of suffering that seemed as though it would never end — and what did that teach you about the nature of suffering itself?

### SUTRA 8.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

असी-मुसल-पासेहिं, सत्तीहिं य महाबहूहिं ।
णारया हण्णंति तत्थ, कूडसाल्मलिभेयणं ॥८.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

asī-musala-pāsehiṃ, sattīhiṃ ya mahābahūhiṃ |
ṇārayā haṇṇaṃti tattha, kūḍasālmalibheyaṇaṃ || 8.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

By swords, clubs, and snares, by many great spears — the hell-beings are struck there, and by the piercing thorns of the kūṭa-shalmali tree. ⚠️

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*asī* = swords | *musala* = clubs | *pāsehiṃ* = snares, traps | *sattīhiṃ* = spears | *mahābahūhiṃ* = many great ones | *ṇārayā* = hell-beings | *haṇṇaṃti* = are struck, are harmed | *tattha* = there | *kūḍasālmali* = a hellish tree with sharp thorns (⚠️ the kūṭa-shalmali is a specific tree described in Jain hell cosmology as having razor-sharp thorns used to torment hell-beings) | *bheyaṇaṃ* = piercing, cutting

**5. COMMENTARY**

The specific instruments of hellish torment are described — swords, clubs, snares, spears, and the thorns of the shalmali tree. These descriptions should be understood within the Jain cosmological framework, not as literal rooms with weapons but as the natural conditions of the karmic environment a violent soul creates for itself. Just as a violent person in life surrounded themselves with the instruments of harm, the soul reborn in a hellish realm exists in an environment that is itself an expression of the harm they created. The karmic environment mirrors the soul's accumulated actions. The shalmali tree in particular is described in multiple Jain texts as a feature of certain hell realms — its thorns pierce those who climbed illusory trees seeking pleasure, only to find pain.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Hell-beings experience constant harm from every direction — swords, clubs, traps, spears, and trees full of sharp thorns — all of it the natural karmic echo of the harm they caused in previous lives.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If your inner environment mirrors your accumulated habits and actions — what kind of environment are you creating for yourself right now?

### SUTRA 8.14

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

उव्वहंति य अण्णोण्णं, उव्वहेंति य ते बहू ।
भमइ आउट्टए तत्थ, चक्कवाय व्व जीविया ॥८.१४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

uvvahaṃti ya aṇṇoṇṇaṃ, uvvaheti ya te bahū |
bhamai āuṭṭae tattha, cakkavāya vva jīviyā || 8.14 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

They torment each other repeatedly, many torment each other; life there revolves and turns like a wheel.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*uvvahaṃti* = torment, cause suffering | *aṇṇoṇṇaṃ* = each other, one another | *uvvaheti* = are tormented | *te bahū* = they, many | *bhamai* = revolves, wanders | *āuṭṭae* = turns, spins | *tattha* = there | *cakkavāya* = like a wheel | *jīviyā* = life, existence

**5. COMMENTARY**

The hell-beings torment each other — not because they are assigned as tormentors and victims but because the entire atmosphere of a hellish realm is one of mutual aggression. Every soul there carries the karmic pattern of violence and is surrounded by others who carry the same pattern. The natural result is constant mutual harm. This is the Jain understanding of the self-reinforcing nature of violent karma: it creates an environment of violence that generates more violence. The image of life turning like a wheel captures both the cyclical nature of this mutual torment and the mechanism of the broader cycle of existence — which also spins, bringing souls back around through similar conditions until the karma is exhausted.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In hell, beings are constantly hurting each other because they are all carrying the pattern of violence — it's a self-reinforcing cycle of mutual harm, spinning like a wheel.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your own life do you see a cycle of mutual harm — where the same patterns keep producing the same kinds of conflict?

### SUTRA 8.15

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तत्थ णत्थि सुहं किंचि, णत्थि णिव्वुइ कयावि ।
णत्थि मित्ता य बंधवा, एगओ वेयई दुक्खं ॥८.१५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tattha natthi suhaṃ kiṃci, natthi ṇivvui kayāvi |
natthi mittā ya baṃdhavā, egao veyaī dukkhaṃ || 8.15 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

There is no pleasure there whatsoever; there is no peace there at any time; there are no friends, no relatives — one experiences suffering alone.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tattha* = there | *natthi* = there is not | *suhaṃ* = pleasure | *kiṃci* = whatsoever, any | *natthi* = there is not | *ṇivvui* = peace, comfort | *kayāvi* = at any time | *natthi* = there are not | *mittā* = friends | *baṃdhavā* = relatives | *egao* = alone | *veyaī* = experiences | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering

**5. COMMENTARY**

The three-fold isolation of hellish existence is named: no pleasure, no peace, and no companions. The human social fabric — friends, family, community — is completely absent. The comfort that humans instinctively seek in difficulty — another person to share the burden, to offer sympathy, to give meaning to suffering — is unavailable. The suffering is experienced alone, without relief, without context, without purpose that could make it bearable. This sutra does not speak merely of physical isolation but of the complete absence of the ordinary consolations of existence. The phrase "at any time" emphasizes that this is not a temporary deprivation but the consistent condition: no moment of peace in an existence defined entirely by suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In hell there is no pleasure, no peace, and no companions — you experience the suffering entirely alone, with no relief of any kind at any time.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The deepest suffering humans experience often combines physical pain with isolation and meaninglessness — what does recognizing this tell you about what really matters in your current relationships and connections?

### SUTRA 8.16

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं णरगं भीमं पेच्च, कम्मविहिट्ठिया ।
तम्हा कम्मं ण कायव्वं, जेण णरगं उवज्जए ॥८.१६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ ṇaragaṃ bhīmaṃ pecca, kammavihiṭṭhiyā |
tamhā kammaṃ ṇa kāyavvaṃ, jeṇa ṇaragaṃ uvajjae || 8.16 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This terrible hell is attained after death by those who are bound by karma; therefore, karma that leads to hell should not be done.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *ṇaragaṃ* = hell | *bhīmaṃ* = terrible, frightful | *pecca* = after death, in the next life | *kammavihiṭṭhiyā* = by those bound by karma | *tamhā* = therefore | *kammaṃ* = karma | *ṇa kāyavvaṃ* = should not be done | *jeṇa* = by which | *ṇaragaṃ* = hell | *uvajjae* = is attained, one arrives at

**5. COMMENTARY**

The practical conclusion from the description of hell: do not do the karma that produces it. The teaching is explicit about the mechanism: the terrifying hellish existence is attained "after death" — it is a consequence that follows necessarily from the karma accumulated in this life. The logic is straightforward: understanding the consequence, stop creating the cause. This is different from avoiding hell out of fear — it is avoiding hell through understanding of karma's mechanism. A person who understands clearly that violence produces hellish karma and hellish rebirth will, through that very understanding, naturally reduce violent behavior. The teaching relies on clarity of perception, not coercion or threat.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Hell is real and is attained after death by those who have accumulated violent karma — so simply: don't do what produces that karma.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are you more motivated by understanding consequences clearly or by fear of punishment — and does that difference matter for how effective your motivation is?

### SUTRA 8.17

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

हिंसा मोसं च थेयं च, अब्रह्मं परिग्गहो ।
एयाइं पंच पावाइं, ते णरगे उवज्जए ॥८.१७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

hiṃsā mosaṃ ca theyaṃ ca, abrahmaṃ pariggaho |
eyāiṃ paṃca pāvāiṃ, te ṇarage uvajjae || 8.17 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Violence, falsehood, theft, non-celibacy, and possession — these five evils: one who (practices) these is reborn in hell.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*hiṃsā* = violence | *mosaṃ* = falsehood | *theyaṃ* = theft | *abrahmaṃ* = non-celibacy | *pariggaho* = possession | *eyāiṃ paṃca* = these five | *pāvāiṃ* = evils, sinful acts | *te* = those | *ṇarage* = in hell | *uvajjae* = are reborn

**5. COMMENTARY**

The five evils that lead to hellish rebirth are exactly the five great vows in reverse: where the vows prescribe non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession, the five evils are violence, falsehood, theft, non-celibacy (sensual indulgence), and possessiveness. This mirror structure is intentional: it shows that the five great vows are not arbitrary rules but the precise opposite of the five causes of hellish rebirth. Each vow represents the transformation of a specific type of harmful action into its constructive opposite. The person who practices the five great vows fully is definitionally eliminating the five causes of hellish rebirth and cultivating the five causes of liberation. The ethical teaching and the cosmological teaching are two aspects of the same reality.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The five things that lead to hellish rebirth are exactly the opposite of the five great vows: violence, lying, stealing, sensual indulgence, and possessiveness.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Looking at your daily life honestly, which of these five plays a role — even in subtle forms — and what would it take to root it out?

### SUTRA 8.18

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य दंडं ण धारेंति, ण दंडं च पवट्टए ।
अपरिग्गहिया सव्वं, ते दुक्खा परिमुच्चए ॥८.१८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya daṃḍaṃ ṇa dhāreṃti, ṇa daṃḍaṃ ca pavaṭṭae |
apariggahiyā savvaṃ, te dukhā parimuccae || 8.18 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who do not wield force, who do not exercise force, and who are without possessions in every way — they are released from suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = those who | *daṃḍaṃ* = force, punishment, the rod | *ṇa dhāreṃti* = do not wield, do not hold | *ṇa daṃḍaṃ* = force | *pavaṭṭae* = do not exercise, do not employ | *apariggahiyā* = without possessions, non-possessive | *savvaṃ* = in every way, completely | *te* = they | *dukhā* = from suffering | *parimuccae* = are released, freed

**5. COMMENTARY**

The positive formulation — what releases from suffering — gives us the opposite of the five evils: not wielding force (non-violence), not exercising coercion (truth and non-stealing as their positive forms), and complete non-possession. The word "force" (daṃḍa) literally means a rod or stick but is used to indicate any use of power to coerce, harm, or control. The person who has genuinely released all three — force, its exercise, and possessiveness — has dismantled the three primary mechanisms of karma accumulation. This is the path to release from all suffering: not just the avoidance of the five evils but the genuine cultivation of their opposites as the dominant orientation of one's life.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Those who never use force or coercion, and who hold onto nothing as their own, become genuinely free from suffering.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In what ways do you use force — subtle or obvious — to control your environment, your relationships, or your circumstances?

### SUTRA 8.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं णाऊण मेहावी, धम्मे ठाए महामुणी ।
झाणजोगसमाहिट्ठो, मुच्चइ सव्वबंधणा ॥८.१९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ ṇāūṇa mehāvī, dhamme ṭhāe mahāmuṇī |
jhāṇajogasamāhiṭṭho, muccai savvabaṃdhaṇā || 8.19 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having understood this, the wise great monk, standing in the teaching, established in the practice of meditation — is freed from all bondage.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *ṇāūṇa* = having understood | *mehāvī* = wise | *dhamme* = in the teaching | *ṭhāe* = standing, established | *mahāmuṇī* = great monk, great silent one | *jhāṇajoga* = practice of meditation | *samāhiṭṭho* = established in, fixed in | *muccai* = is freed | *savvabaṃdhaṇā* = from all bondage

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter's third section opens with the complete liberation formula: understanding the full teaching (both the catalog of hells and heavens and the mechanism of karma), standing firmly in the teaching, and being established in meditation produces freedom from all bondage. The sequence is important — understanding precedes practice, and practice must be rooted in the genuine teaching. A person who meditates without understanding the teaching may achieve calm but will not achieve liberation; a person who understands the teaching without meditating has the map but has not traveled the road. The great monk who has both — deep understanding and established meditative practice — is described as moving toward the dissolution of all karmic bonds.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Understanding the full teaching about karma, hells, and heavens — and then actually practicing deep meditation as a result — leads to being freed from every single bond.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does your meditation or contemplative practice arise from genuine understanding — or is it more of a technique you apply without deep connection to why it matters?

### SUTRA 8.20

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णो हु सक्को णिवारेउं, कम्मकारेण कीरइ ।
जो हु कम्मं करेइ राई, सो तस्स फलमश्नुए ॥८.२०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo hu sakko nivāreūṃ, kammakāreṇa kīrai |
jo hu kammaṃ karei rāī, so tassa phalamasṇue || 8.20 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

It is not possible to prevent — karma operates through what the doer does; whoever commits karma at night, that one experiences its fruit.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇo hu sakko* = it is not possible | *nivāreūṃ* = to prevent | *kammakāreṇa* = by the doer of karma | *kīrai* = is produced, operates | *jo hu* = whoever | *kammaṃ* = karma | *karei* = does, commits | *rāī* = at night (i.e., secretly, in the dark) | *so* = that one | *tassa* = of it | *phalam* = fruit | *asṇue* = experiences

**5. COMMENTARY**

Karma cannot be hidden or evaded. The phrase "at night" (rāī) means secretly, when no human observer is present — a person who behaves differently when observed versus when alone, believing that the concealment changes the moral result. The Jain teaching is clear: karma is generated by the action itself, not by whether anyone sees it. The fruit of the action follows the actor with the same certainty whether they committed the action in public or in secret. This is a profound teaching on moral integrity: the person who maintains ethical conduct only when observed has not internalized the teaching. True practice is the same whether witnessed or not, because it is the karma generated within the soul — not the social judgment — that matters.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Karma can't be hidden or escaped — even actions done secretly, in the dark when no one is watching, still produce their full consequences for the one who did them.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How does your behavior change when you believe no one is watching — and what does that gap tell you about the depth of your ethical practice?

### SUTRA 8.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण कप्पए ते उवमा, कम्मस्स फलमागए ।
तम्हा कम्मं णिवारेज्ज, जो इच्छे अप्पणो सुहं ॥८.२१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa kappae te uvamā, kammassa phalamagae |
tamhā kammaṃ nivārejja, jo icche appoṇo suhaṃ || 8.21 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

No comparison is possible when the fruit of karma arrives; therefore, one who desires happiness for the self should prevent (the accumulation of) karma.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa kappae* = is not possible, does not fit | *te uvamā* = comparison, simile | *kammassa* = of karma | *phalam* = fruit | *āgae* = arrived, come | *tamhā* = therefore | *kammaṃ* = karma | *nivārejja* = should prevent, should stop | *jo icche* = who desires | *appoṇo suhaṃ* = happiness for the self

**5. COMMENTARY**

When the fruit of karma actually arrives — when the consequence of a past harmful action is fully experienced — the intensity of the experience surpasses any description or anticipation. No comparison can capture what it is actually like to experience the karmic consequence of major violence, major deception, or major possessiveness. This sutra uses this inexpressibility as a motivation: since the experience of bad karma's fruit exceeds anything you can currently imagine, and since you genuinely desire your own happiness, take action now to prevent further karmic accumulation. The teaching is not fatalistic — you cannot undo past karma instantly, but you can completely stop adding to it, and that is the beginning of liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When bad karma's consequences finally arrive, they are beyond any description — so if you want genuine happiness, start preventing the accumulation of karma right now.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there consequences of your past actions that are already arriving in your current life — and are you genuinely changing the behavior that produced them?

### SUTRA 8.22

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अणिच्चाइं सुक्खाइं, अणिच्चो देहसंभवो ।
अणिच्चा सव्वसंजोगा, णिच्चं णिव्वाणमव्वयं ॥८.२२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

aṇiccāiṃ sukhāiṃ, aṇicco dehasaṃbhavo |
aṇiccā savvasaṃjogā, niccaṃ ṇivvāṇamavvayaṃ || 8.22 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Pleasures are impermanent; the existence in the body is impermanent; all connections are impermanent; nirvana alone is permanent, unchanging.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*aṇiccāiṃ* = impermanent | *sukhāiṃ* = pleasures | *aṇicco* = impermanent | *dehasaṃbhavo* = existence in the body | *aṇiccā* = impermanent | *savvasaṃjogā* = all connections | *niccaṃ* = permanent | *ṇivvāṇam* = liberation | *avvayaṃ* = unchanging, without decay

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra provides the philosophical foundation for all spiritual practice through the logic of impermanence. Pleasures are impermanent — every pleasant experience ends; there is no pleasure in the world that lasts. Physical embodiment is impermanent — every birth leads to death; no body lasts. All relationships and connections are impermanent — every person you love, every community you belong to, every bond you cherish will eventually dissolve. Against this universal impermanence, there is one thing that is permanent: nirvana — the state of the liberated soul, beyond the cycle of arising and passing. The logic is clear: clinging to impermanent things guarantees suffering when they pass; the only rational object of pursuit is the permanent. And the permanent is nirvana.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every pleasure ends; every body dies; every relationship passes — the only thing that is permanent is liberation itself, which is why liberation is worth pursuing above all else.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What impermanent thing are you currently clinging to as though it were permanent — and what would it mean to hold it more lightly?

### SUTRA 8.23

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धुयकम्मो णिरासाओ, संसारभयभेरवो ।
अप्पाणमेव संवरे, ण पमाए कयाइ वि ॥८.२३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhuyakammo ṇirāsāo, saṃsārabhayabheravo |
appāṇameva saṃvare, ṇa pamāe kayāi vi || 8.23 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having shaken off karma, without craving, fearful of the terrifying cycle of existence — let one restrain the self; never be negligent at any time.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*dhuyakammo* = having shaken off karma | *ṇirāsāo* = without craving, desireless | *saṃsārabhaya* = fear of the cycle of existence | *bheravo* = terrifying | *appāṇam* = the self | *eva* = indeed | *saṃvare* = let one restrain | *ṇa pamāe* = not be negligent | *kayāi vi* = at any time, ever

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra combines two things that might seem contradictory: having shaken off karma (a description of genuine spiritual progress) and the urgency never to be negligent. Even someone who has made real progress in dissolving their karmic accumulation cannot afford complacency. The path of liberation requires sustained, unbroken practice until completion — because even a nearly-liberated soul can re-accumulate karma through a moment of negligence, anger, or attachment. The "fear" of the cycle is not neurotic anxiety but the appropriate sobriety of someone who has genuinely understood how easy it is to fall back into it. This fear motivates the continued restraint that keeps the practitioner on track.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even when you've made real spiritual progress and shed a lot of old karma — never become lazy or careless, because one moment of negligence can pull you back into the cycle.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does spiritual progress ever make you feel like you can relax your vigilance — and is that feeling trustworthy?

### SUTRA 8.24

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

लोगस्स सारमादाय, पच्चक्खाय असेसओ ।
विणएण य संपण्णो, सिद्धिं पाहिइ पंडिए ॥८.२४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

logassa sāramādāya, paccakkāya asesao |
viṇaeṇa ya saṃpaṇṇo, siddhiṃ pāhii paṃḍie || 8.24 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having taken the essence of the world (the true teaching), having completely renounced everything, endowed with humility — the wise one will attain liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*logassa* = of the world | *sāram* = essence | *ādāya* = having taken | *paccakkāya* = having renounced | *asesao* = completely, without remainder | *viṇaeṇa ya* = and with humility | *saṃpaṇṇo* = endowed | *siddhiṃ* = liberation, attainment | *pāhii* = will attain | *paṃḍie* = the wise one

**5. COMMENTARY**

The phrase "taking the essence of the world" is beautiful: the entire experience of existence, with all its suffering, impermanence, and cycles, serves to extract one essential insight — this is not the way; liberation is possible; the path is here. Having extracted this essence from the experience of the world, the wise person then renounces everything — completely, without retaining anything. And with humility as the final quality, they proceed to liberation. The three movements — understanding, renunciation, and humility — describe the complete arc of spiritual maturation. Understanding without renunciation stays theoretical; renunciation without understanding becomes mechanical; both without humility create spiritual pride that negates the work. All three together lead inevitably to liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Having understood what the world is truly teaching you — then giving everything up completely — and walking with genuine humility: that is the complete path to liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What has your life experience been trying to teach you about what matters — and have you fully received that teaching yet?

### SUTRA 8.25

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं सुणित्ता मेहावी, णिव्वाणं अहिगच्छए ।
विणएण य संपण्णो, अणंतं सुक्खमश्नुए ।
इइ बेमि ॥८.२५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ suṇittā mehāvī, ṇivvāṇaṃ ahigaccae |
viṇaeṇa ya saṃpaṇṇo, aṇaṃtaṃ sukkhamasṇue |
iti bemi || 8.25 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having heard this, the wise one attains liberation; endowed with humility, one experiences infinite happiness. — iti bemi (Thus I say.)

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *suṇittā* = having heard | *mehāvī* = wise one | *ṇivvāṇaṃ* = liberation | *ahigaccae* = attains | *viṇaeṇa ya* = and with humility | *saṃpaṇṇo* = endowed | *aṇaṃtaṃ* = infinite | *sukkham* = happiness, joy | *asṇue* = experiences | *iti bemi* = thus I say

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra closes the chapter on the chapter's highest note: the wise person who has heard this complete teaching — the catalog of hells and heavens, the mechanism of karma, the path of restraint and meditation — and who has absorbed it with genuine humility, attains liberation and experiences infinite happiness. The phrase "infinite happiness" is the ultimate definition of liberation: not merely the absence of suffering, not merely peace, but a positive state of unbounded bliss that is the natural condition of the pure soul free from all karmic covering. This is the destination that makes the entire demanding path worthwhile — not a comfortable heaven that will eventually end, but infinite, permanent, unbounded joy. The traditional closing formula "Thus I say" anchors this vision in authentic transmission.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Hearing and genuinely absorbing this teaching — and walking forward with humility — leads to liberation and to a happiness that is infinite and has no end. — Thus I say.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can you touch, even briefly, the possibility of infinite, unbounded happiness — not as a concept but as something you can feel the direction of right now?
