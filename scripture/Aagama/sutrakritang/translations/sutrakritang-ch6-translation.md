# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 6: Dharma-Katha (धम्म-कहा)

**Translation by:** JainAwaken.org
**Source:** Ardhamagadhi Prakrit — Sutrakritanga, Book 1 (Prathama Shrutaskandha)
**Chapter:** Sixth Adhyayana (छठम् अध्ययन)
**Total Sutras:** 27
**Theme:** A dialogue chapter in which a wandering monk asks what constitutes true religion. The teacher — drawing on Mahavira's teaching — answers that non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession are the five eternal vows that form the only true path. Rituals, sacrifices, and Vedic recitation are shown to fail because they involve harm to living beings and thus cannot produce liberation.

---

## PART I — What Is True Religion? (Sutras 1–10)

---

### SUTRA 6.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

किं तं धम्मं समाइक्खसि, जेण मुच्चेज्ज बंधणा ।
पावकम्मं च हासेज्जा, दुक्खाण अंतमाहिए ॥६.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kiṃ taṃ dhammaṃ samāikkhasī, jeṇa muccejja baṃdhaṇā |
pāvakammaṃ ca hāsejjā, dukkhaṇaṃ aṃtamāhie || 6.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

What is that religion you speak of, by which one would be freed from bondage, by which evil karma would be destroyed, and the end of suffering attained?

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kiṃ* = what | *taṃ* = that | *dhammaṃ* = religion, teaching, way of life | *samāikkhasī* = you declare, you describe | *jeṇa* = by which | *muccejja* = one would be freed, released | *baṃdhaṇā* = from bondage, from the chains | *pāvakammaṃ* = evil karma, sinful action | *hāsejjā* = would be reduced, destroyed | *dukkhaṇaṃ* = of suffering | *aṃtam* = the end | *āhie* = reached, attained

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra frames the entire chapter as a direct question: what is true religion? The questioner is a monk in training or an earnest seeker who has heard many teachings and wants a clear answer — not ritual, not tradition, not authority, but the specific path that actually breaks the chains of karma and ends suffering. The question is profoundly practical. It does not ask "what is philosophically true" but "what actually works — what produces freedom?" This is the Jain approach at its core: religion is evaluated by its results. Does it lead to liberation? Does it reduce harmful karma? Does it end suffering? If not, it is not the true path. The seeker asks all three in sequence — freedom from bondage, reduction of evil karma, and the end of suffering — because in Jain understanding these are three phases of the same process.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A seeker asks: "Tell me what true religion is — the kind that actually frees a person from being trapped, destroys harmful actions, and ends suffering."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you call "religion" in your own life — and have you tested whether it actually produces freedom?

### SUTRA 6.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अहिंसा सच्चमक्कोहो, बंभचेरं अपरिग्गहो ।
एसो धम्मो समाइट्ठो, जिणेहिं पत्तेयबुद्धिहिं ॥६.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ahiṃsā saccamakkohо, baṃbhaceraṃ apariggaho |
eso dhammo samāiṭṭho, jiṇehiṃ patteyabuddhihiṃ || 6.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Non-violence, truth, non-anger, celibacy, and non-possession — this is the religion declared by the Jinas and the independently enlightened ones.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ahiṃsā* = non-violence, non-harming | *saccam* = truth | *akkohо* = non-anger, freedom from wrath | *baṃbhaceraṃ* = celibacy, complete continence | *apariggaho* = non-possession, non-grasping | *eso* = this | *dhammo* = religion, the true path | *samāiṭṭho* = declared, prescribed | *jiṇehiṃ* = by the Jinas (the liberated victors) | *patteyabuddhihiṃ* = by the independently enlightened ones (pratyekabuddhas)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The answer to the seeker's question comes immediately and clearly: five ethical commitments constitute true religion as declared by all who have ever attained liberation. Non-violence is listed first because it is the foundation — all other restraints flow from the commitment not to harm. Truth is second because deception is a subtle form of violence against others and against one's own soul. Non-anger matters because anger is the emotion that most quickly destroys spiritual progress — even a person who otherwise lives well will accumulate heavy karma through outbursts of wrath. Celibacy refers to complete restraint of sensory indulgence, not merely physical chastity but the taming of the entire wanting-mind. Non-possession is last but not least — the attachment to things, people, and status is the deepest root of continued rebirth. The teaching is attributed not only to Mahavira but to all Jinas and all independently enlightened beings across time — this is not a new invention but the eternal discovery of every liberated soul.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The teacher answers: "Non-violence, truth, non-anger, celibacy, and non-possession — these five are the real religion, declared by every liberated being who ever lived."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which of these five do you find most natural in your life — and which one do you most resist?

### SUTRA 6.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयाइं पंच सिक्खाइं, आयरित्ता महव्वया ।
जे समाहिमणुप्पत्ता, ते सिद्धा परिणिव्वुया ॥६.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyāiṃ paṃca sikkhaīṃ, āyarittā mahavvayā |
je samāhimaṇupattā, te siddhā pariṇivvuyā || 6.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having practiced these five trainings as great vows, those who have attained inner stillness — they are the accomplished ones, the completely liberated.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyāiṃ* = these | *paṃca* = five | *sikkhaīṃ* = trainings, disciplines | *āyarittā* = having practiced, having followed | *mahavvayā* = as great vows (mahāvratas) | *je* = those who | *samāhim* = inner stillness, meditative composure | *aṇupattā* = have attained, have reached | *te* = they | *siddhā* = accomplished, perfected souls | *pariṇivvuyā* = completely liberated, fully at peace

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra gives the proof that the five disciplines are the true path: every single being who has ever attained liberation did so by practicing these five as complete vows. The word "great vows" (mahāvratas) is important — the Jain tradition distinguishes between the minor vows of lay practitioners and the great vows of fully renounced monastics. This sutra is addressed primarily to monks and nuns who are on the path of complete renunciation. The connection between the five vows and liberation is presented as a causal certainty: practice these five fully, attain inner stillness, and liberation follows. The word translated as "inner stillness" — samādhi — refers not just to meditative calm but to the complete settling of the agitated mind that results from having no more unresolved karma generating internal turbulence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Everyone who has ever truly become liberated did so by completely following these five disciplines as full vows and reaching a state of inner peace.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would your life look like if you took even one of these five as a complete, unwavering commitment?

### SUTRA 6.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तस्स धम्मस्स सुद्धस्स, सम्मं पालेउकामेहिं ।
ण याए ण य दायव्वं, ण य परिग्गहधारणं ॥६.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tassa dhammassa sudddhassa, sammaṃ pāleukāmehiṃ |
ṇa yāe ṇa ya dāyavvaṃ, ṇa ya pariggahadhāraṇaṃ || 6.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For those who wish to correctly practice this pure religion, there should be no killing, no giving (permission to kill), and no holding of possessions.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tassa* = of that | *dhammassa* = religion | *sudddhassa* = pure | *sammaṃ* = correctly, properly | *pāleukāmehiṃ* = for those wishing to follow, for those who desire to practice | *ṇa yāe* = no killing, not killing | *ṇa ya dāyavvaṃ* = no giving (permission or encouragement to harm) | *ṇa ya* = and no | *pariggahadhāraṇaṃ* = holding of possessions, carrying of property

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra specifies the practical behavior that flows from accepting the five vows as one's religion. The teaching recognizes three levels of involvement in harm: doing it yourself (killing), ordering or encouraging others to do it (giving), and the indirect violence of possessiveness which motivates harm in the world. All three are prohibited for the sincere practitioner. This is not a moral ideal to aspire to but the concrete practical content of what "practicing the true religion" actually means day to day. The qualification "those who wish to practice this pure religion correctly" is significant — the teaching acknowledges that not everyone will choose this path, but those who do must follow all three dimensions of non-harm without compromise. You cannot claim to follow the religion of non-violence while encouraging slaughter or while stockpiling goods that create the conditions for exploitation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

If you really want to follow this pure religion, you must not hurt living beings, must not tell others to hurt them, and must not hold onto unnecessary possessions.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In how many indirect ways does your daily life involve harm that you have given permission to — through what you buy, eat, or approve of?

### SUTRA 6.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सव्वे जीवा वि इच्छंति, जीविउं ण मरिज्जिउं ।
तम्हा पाणिवहं घोरं, निग्गंथा वज्जयंति णं ॥६.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

savve jīvā vi icchaṃti, jīviuṃ ṇa marijjiuṃ |
tamhā pāṇivhaṃ ghoraṃ, niggaṃthā vajjayaṃti ṇaṃ || 6.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

All living beings wish to live and not to die; therefore, the unattached ones (monks) completely avoid the terrible act of killing living beings.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*savve* = all | *jīvā vi* = living beings even/also | *icchaṃti* = wish, desire | *jīviuṃ* = to live | *ṇa marijjiuṃ* = not to die | *tamhā* = therefore | *pāṇivhaṃ* = killing of living beings | *ghoraṃ* = terrible, dreadful | *niggaṃthā* = the unattached ones, monks free from knots | *vajjayaṃti* = avoid, renounce | *ṇaṃ* = completely, entirely

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra offers the foundational moral reasoning behind non-violence: every living being shares the same basic wish — to live and not to die. This is not a philosophical argument from abstract principles but an appeal to what anyone can directly observe. You yourself want to live; so does every creature you encounter. The desire for life is universal and equal. It does not vary by species, size, or intelligence. A worm fleeing a shoe does not want to die any more than you do. The monks who are described as "unattached ones" (niggaṃthā, literally "those without knots") avoid killing because they have genuinely understood this equality of all life. They do not need a law commanding them to be non-violent; their own developed compassion and perception make violence unthinkable.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every living being — from insects to humans — wants to keep living and does not want to die, so monks who truly understand this never harm anything.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you last harmed something living — a mosquito, a plant, an animal in your food — did you notice that it too was trying to live?

### SUTRA 6.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य कामभोगरत्ता, ते ण मुच्चंति बंधणा ।
जे यरागदोससंजुत्ता, ते ण पावंति मुत्तिं ॥६.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya kāmabhogarattā, te ṇa muccaṃti baṃdhaṇā |
je yarāgadosasaṃjuttā, te ṇa pāvaṃti muttiṃ || 6.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who are attached to sensual pleasures are not freed from bondage; those who are joined to attraction and aversion do not attain liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = those who | *kāmabhogarattā* = attached to sensual pleasures, delighting in sense enjoyment | *te* = they | *ṇa muccaṃti* = are not freed | *baṃdhaṇā* = from bondage | *je ya* = those who | *rāgadosasaṃjuttā* = joined to attachment and aversion | *te* = they | *ṇa pāvaṃti* = do not attain | *muttiṃ* = liberation, freedom

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who are hooked on pleasures and driven by likes and dislikes stay trapped — they never get free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which desire or aversion in you has the strongest grip — and what would it cost you to loosen it?

**5. COMMENTARY**

The teaching here identifies the two psychological roots of continued bondage: attraction (rāga) and aversion (dosa). These are not incidental problems but the core mechanism of karmic accumulation. When the mind moves toward what it wants and away from what it dislikes, it is actively generating new karma with every movement. Sensual pleasure is the most obvious example — the pursuit of pleasant sights, sounds, tastes, and touches keeps the mind in constant agitation, constantly reaching and grasping. But the teaching applies equally to aversion: the person who is fastidiously disgusted by dirt, noise, or unpleasant people is equally bound as the person who craves luxuries. Liberation requires the dissolution of both directions — neither craving nor revulsion, but equanimity.

### SUTRA 6.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मा हु पमायं कुणह, पमत्तस्स ण विज्जए ।
धम्मो ण पाविए तं तु, जो पमाए रओ मुणी ॥६.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

mā hu pamāyaṃ kuṇaha, pamattassa ṇa vijjae |
dhammo ṇa pāvie taṃ tu, jo pamāe raо muṇī || 6.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Do not commit negligence — for the negligent one, the path is not found; that monk who is given over to negligence does not attain the teaching.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*mā hu* = do not | *pamāyaṃ* = negligence, carelessness, heedlessness | *kuṇaha* = commit, do | *pamattassa* = for the negligent, heedless one | *ṇa vijjae* = is not found, does not exist | *dhammo* = the true teaching, the path | *ṇa pāvie* = does not attain | *taṃ tu* = and that | *jo* = who | *pamāe* = in negligence | *raо* = absorbed, delighting | *muṇī* = monk, sage

**5. COMMENTARY**

Negligence (pamāya) is called the single greatest obstacle on the spiritual path — more dangerous even than outright wickedness. The negligent monk may nominally follow the rules, recite the prayers, wear the robes, but his mind wanders, his alertness is slack, his practice is mechanical. Such a person will not find the true teaching even if it is placed directly in front of him. The word "heedlessness" captures this better than "negligence" — it means the failure to be awake and present to what you are doing and why. Jain practice demands constant wakefulness: every step taken carefully to avoid harming creatures underfoot, every word chosen with awareness of its effect, every bite of food taken with mindfulness of the life involved. A monk absorbed in daydream, social comfort, or spiritual complacency has effectively left the path even while appearing to be on it.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Don't be careless or asleep in your practice — a monk who spaces out and drifts through the day will never truly find what he is looking for.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What parts of your spiritual practice are you doing mechanically, without real attention — going through the motions?

### SUTRA 6.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगो कुज्जा सया धम्मं, एगस्स फलमोहए ।
एगस्स सव्वसंकप्पा, एगो भुंजइ कम्मफलं ॥६.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ego kujjā sayā dhammaṃ, egassa phalamohaе |
egassa savvasaṃkappā, ego bhuṃjai kammaphalaṃ || 6.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One alone should always practice the teaching, for the fruits and rewards are for the individual alone; all intentions belong to the individual alone, and one alone experiences the fruits of karma.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ego* = one alone | *kujjā* = should do, should practice | *sayā* = always | *dhammaṃ* = the teaching, religion | *egassa* = for the individual, for oneself alone | *phalam* = fruit, result | *ohae* = is given, falls | *savvasaṃkappā* = all intentions, all thoughts | *ego* = one alone | *bhuṃjai* = experiences, enjoys | *kammaphalaṃ* = the fruits of karma

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra establishes the radical individuality of spiritual practice. No one can practice for you, save you, or transfer their merit to you. The fruits of religious discipline — and equally the consequences of negligence — fall to the individual alone. This is not a statement about isolation but about responsibility. Just as you alone experience pain and pleasure in your own body, you alone experience the karmic results of your own actions and intentions. The word "intentions" (saṃkappā) is especially significant: not just your actions but your mental movements, your wishes and plans, are your own and theirs alone. This teaching counters the idea prevalent in other traditions that a teacher, god, priest, or community can carry a person to liberation. In the Jain view, liberation must be earned by each soul individually through its own purification.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

You have to do your own spiritual work — no one else can do it for you, and you alone will experience the results of your own actions and intentions.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are you relying on someone else's practice, merit, or grace to save you — or are you doing the actual work yourself?

### SUTRA 6.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अत्थाभिलासिणो मूढा, धम्माभिलासिणो सुई ।
अत्थं च धम्ममाइक्खे, को हु धम्मं विआणए ॥६.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

atthābhilāsiṇo mūḍhā, dhammābhilāsiṇo suī |
atthaṃ ca dhammamāikkhe, ko hu dhammaṃ viāṇae || 6.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who desire material gain are confused; those who desire the teaching are wise; one who teaches both gain and teaching — who among them truly knows the teaching?

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*atthābhilāsiṇo* = those desiring wealth, material gain | *mūḍhā* = confused, deluded | *dhammābhilāsiṇo* = those desiring the teaching/religion | *suī* = wise, learned | *atthaṃ* = material gain | *ca* = and | *dhammam* = the teaching | *āikkhe* = teaches, explains | *ko hu* = who indeed | *dhammaṃ* = the teaching | *viāṇae* = truly knows, fully understands

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra draws a sharp distinction between two orientations: toward material gain and toward spiritual truth. The word used for "confused" (mūḍhā) is strong — it means genuinely deluded, not just misdirected. The person who pursues wealth, comfort, reputation, and sensory pleasure is not merely making a different choice; they are fundamentally confused about what will actually bring them peace. The wise person desires the teaching itself — not as a means to something else, but as the end. The final question cuts deeper: a teacher who presents both the path of material gain and the path of liberation cannot truly know the path of liberation. True knowledge of the teaching is incompatible with giving it equal value alongside worldly pursuits. This challenges spiritual teachers who try to please everyone by blessing both material ambition and renunciation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who chase wealth are confused about what matters; people who seek the true teaching are wise — and any teacher who mixes the two together probably doesn't fully understand the teaching.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are you really seeking when you engage with spiritual practice — liberation, or some comfort or benefit within this world?

### SUTRA 6.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मो वोच्चिय सो धम्मो, जो समाहिकरो मुणी ।
जहा सीओदगं वारि, उवसंतं तहा मुणी ॥६.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammo vocciya so dhammo, jo samāhikaro muṇī |
jahā sīodagaṃ vāri, uvasaṃtaṃ tahā muṇī || 6.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That which brings inner stillness — that truly is religion for the monk; just as cool water is calm, so is the monk who is at peace.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*dhammo* = religion, the true teaching | *vocciya* = having been said, truly stated | *so* = that | *dhammo* = is religion | *jo* = which | *samāhikaro* = brings inner stillness, produces composure | *muṇī* = for the monk | *jahā* = just as | *sīodagaṃ* = cool water | *vāri* = water | *uvasaṃtaṃ* = calm, settled | *tahā* = so | *muṇī* = the monk

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra offers the experiential test for true religion: does it produce inner calm? The metaphor of cool, still water is perfect — water is calm when there is no agitation from wind or current, and a monk's mind is calm when there is no agitation from desires, fears, or passions. Religion that produces drama, excitement, emotional frenzy, or agitation — even in service of noble goals — is not the true teaching in the Jain understanding. True practice produces a progressive quieting. The monk who has genuinely internalized the five vows and practices them continuously finds that the mind gradually loses its turbulence, settles like undisturbed water, becomes clear. This clarity — samādhi — is both the mark that you are on the right path and the precondition for the final breakthrough to liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

True religion is whatever actually makes your mind peaceful and still — like calm, cool water — not what makes you excited or dramatic.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does your spiritual practice genuinely calm your mind, or does it mostly stimulate emotions — and what does that tell you?

## PART II — The Failure of Ritual (Sutras 11–18)

### SUTRA 6.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य यण्णविही भणिया, सा ण धम्मस्स सोहणी ।
पाणाइवायसंजुत्ता, मोसेण य समागया ॥६.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya yaṇṇavihi bhaṇiyā, sā ṇa dhammassa sohaṇī |
pāṇāivāyasaṃjuttā, moseṇa ya samāgayā || 6.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The method of sacrifice that is described — it is not purifying for religion; it is combined with the killing of living beings and accompanied by untruth.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = that which | *yaṇṇavihi* = the method of sacrifice, the ritual procedure | *bhaṇiyā* = is described, is taught | *sā* = that | *ṇa* = not | *dhammassa* = of religion | *sohaṇī* = purifying, cleansing | *pāṇāivāyasaṃjuttā* = combined with killing of living beings | *moseṇa ya* = and with untruth, with falsehood | *samāgayā* = accompanied, associated

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter now turns to critique. Having established what true religion is, the teaching examines what is false religion — and the first target is animal sacrifice. This was not an abstract critique in Mahavira's time: elaborate Vedic rituals involving the slaughter of horses, cattle, and other animals were actively practiced and considered the height of religious merit. The Jain response is blunt: any practice involving the killing of living beings cannot be religion, cannot purify, and cannot lead to liberation — regardless of what scriptures authorize it or what priests prescribe it. The addition of "untruth" is also significant: the claim that killing animals in a ritual context produces spiritual merit is itself a falsehood, a fundamental misrepresentation of cause and effect in the universe.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Animal sacrifice is not religion — it involves killing living beings, which means it's actually the opposite of what leads to liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What religious or cultural practices do you follow that might involve harm you have simply never questioned?

### SUTRA 6.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य अग्गिं जुहंति ते, पावकम्मंति णो कुसलं ।
पाणाइवायसंजुत्तं, हिंसापरिणयाणयं ॥६.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya aggiṃ juhaṃti te, pāvakammanti ṇo kusalaṃ |
pāṇāivāyasaṃjuttaṃ, hiṃsāpariṇayāṇayaṃ || 6.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who perform fire sacrifices — they accumulate evil karma, not skillfulness; it is combined with the killing of living beings and its natural result is violence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya* = those who | *aggiṃ* = fire | *juhaṃti* = offer into, perform sacrifice into | *te* = they | *pāvakammanti* = accumulate evil karma | *ṇo kusalaṃ* = not skillfulness, not merit | *pāṇāivāyasaṃjuttaṃ* = combined with killing of living beings | *hiṃsāpariṇayāṇayaṃ* = naturally resulting in violence, whose natural outcome is harm

**5. COMMENTARY**

Fire sacrifice (homa/yajna) was among the most prestigious religious acts in ancient India. Priests would pour clarified butter, grain, and sometimes animals into a sacred fire while reciting Vedic hymns, believing this act would please the gods and produce spiritual and material merit. The Jain teaching categorically rejects this: fire itself contains living beings (fire-body souls), and the act of burning anything — grain, butter, wood — destroys those beings. Furthermore, the preparation of fire sacrifices involved chopping wood, gathering materials, and often slaughtering animals. The practitioner accumulates massive karma from all of this harm, regardless of the religious intent. Karma follows action, not intention alone — the harm done is real harm and produces real consequences.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Performing fire sacrifices actually creates bad karma, not good — because fire involves burning living things, which is violence no matter what religious reason you give.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you assume that doing something with a religious or good intention automatically makes it spiritually beneficial — even if it causes real harm?

### SUTRA 6.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

Ritual bathing at sacred rivers and pilgrimage sites was — and remains — among the most widely practiced forms of religious observance in India. It is the result of actions, intentions, and mental states — and it can only be dissolved through their opposites: non-violence, truth, restraint, equanimity. No amount of ritual bathing removes the karma accumulated through years of harmful living. The teaching is not anti-pilgrimage for its own sake but corrects the dangerous delusion that external acts can undo internal accumulation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Repeating sacred chants or mantras by themselves does not free you from being trapped — words alone, without real change in how you live, don't do the job.

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

This sutra states the Jain position with great clarity and directness: Vedic knowledge — however extensive, however precisely memorized — does not produce liberation. **6. SIMPLY PUT**

### SUTRA 6.16

This is perhaps the most striking sutra in the chapter because it turns the critique inward — toward ascetic practitioners who follow external forms of renunciation without genuine inner transformation. **6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even someone who walks around naked, shaves their head, and endures extreme discomfort doesn't get liberated if they haven't cleaned up what's going on inside.

### SUTRA 6.17

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

devatāo ya je maṇṇe, devayāo bhayāṇuge |
ṇa te pāvaṃti ṇivvāṇaṃ, devapūyā aṇatthiyā || 6.17 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*devatāo ya* = gods, deities | *je* = those who | *maṇṇe* = propitiate, worship | *devayāo* = the gods | *bhayāṇuge* = following out of fear | *ṇa te* = they do not | *pāvaṃti* = attain | *ṇivvāṇaṃ* = liberation | *devapūyā* = worship of gods | *aṇatthiyā* = without benefit, purposeless (for liberation)

**5. COMMENTARY**

Fear-driven propitiation of gods — offering prayers, gifts, and rituals to avoid divine punishment or gain divine favor — was widespread in ancient India and remains so today. The Jain teaching is that this approach, however sincere, cannot produce liberation because gods themselves are not liberated. The gods of Indian cosmology — Indra, Brahma, Vishnu, and the others — are beings in the middle of their own karmic journeys. Only you can dissolve your own karma through your own ethical transformation. No external being — however divine — can do it for you.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Praying to and worshipping gods out of fear or to ask for favors does not lead to liberation — the gods themselves are still in the cycle, and they can't free you from yours.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 6.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अहिंसा परमो धम्मो, सव्वेसिं उवदेसिया ।
उवओगं च कित्तेइ, सव्वपाणहियंकरो ॥६.१९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ahiṃsā paramo dhammo, savvesim uvаdesiyā |
uvаogaṃ ca kittei, savvapāṇahiyaṃkaro || 6.19 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Non-violence is the supreme religion, instructed for all; it is proclaimed as the foremost practice, beneficial to all living beings.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ahiṃsā* = non-violence, non-harming | *paramo* = supreme, highest | *dhammo* = religion, the teaching | *savvesim* = for all | *uvаdesiyā* = instructed, taught | *uvаogaṃ* = practice, application | *ca* = and | *kittei* = is proclaimed, is declared | *savvapāṇahiyaṃkaro* = beneficial for all living beings, welfare-producing for all souls

**5. COMMENTARY**

Non-violence is elevated here to the highest position — not merely one of five vows but the supreme religion itself. This reflects the internal logic of Jain ethics: every other vow flows from non-violence. Truth is a form of non-violence toward others' trust; non-stealing honors others' possessions; celibacy avoids exploiting living beings for pleasure; non-possession removes the hoarding that necessitates harm. The phrase "instructed for all" is remarkable — this is not a teaching for monks alone or for the spiritually advanced. Non-violence, at its applicable level, is the universal path. Even a lay practitioner who cannot follow the great vows in their fullness is still called to observe non-violence in their sphere of action. The teaching is "beneficial for all living beings" — not only for the practitioner but for every being whose life is spared by that practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Non-violence is the highest religion of all — taught to everyone, good for all living beings, not just for monks.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you took non-violence as your highest personal principle — not just a nice ideal but the actual supreme standard — what would change in your daily life?

### SUTRA 6.20

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सच्चं पि परमो धम्मो, मोसं च विवज्जए ।
णिरवज्जेण भासेण, सच्चं भासेइ पंडिए ॥६.२०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*saccaṃ pi* = truth also | *paramo* = supreme | *dhammo* = religion | *mosaṃ* = falsehood, untruth | *ca* = and | *vivajjae* = should avoid, renounce | *ṇiravajjeṇa* = blameless, without fault | *bhāseṇa* = through speech | *saccaṃ* = truth | *bhāsei* = speaks | *paṃḍie* = the wise, the learned one

**5. COMMENTARY**

Truth is presented here as equally supreme — non-violence and truth are co-equals at the pinnacle of Jain ethics, which is why they are listed first and second among the five vows. The word for truth (sacca) in Jain ethics carries a more comprehensive meaning than simple factual accuracy — it includes internal honesty (not deceiving yourself), fidelity to what you have committed to, and speech that is genuinely aimed at the good of the listener. The qualification "blameless speech" introduces an important nuance: not all technically true statements are right speech. A true statement that unnecessarily causes pain, that humiliates someone, or that serves ego rather than genuine communication violates the spirit of this vow even if it avoids literal falsehood. The wise person speaks truth with care, with blamelessness in mind, not as a weapon.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

### SUTRA 6.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अदत्तादाणविरओ, परिग्गहं च विज्जए ।
णिग्गंथो अणगारो वा, एसो धम्मो सणातणो ॥६.२१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

adattādāṇavirao, pariggahaṃ ca vijjae |
niggaṃtho aṇagāro vā, eso dhammo saṇātaṇo || 6.21 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Refraining from taking what is not given, and non-possession — whether one is an unattached monk or a householder, this is the eternal law.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*adattādāṇavirao* = one who refrains from taking what is not given, abstaining from theft | *pariggahaṃ* = possession, grasping | *ca* = and | *vijjae* = non-possession, absence | *niggaṃtho* = unattached monk | *aṇagāro vā* = or a householder | *eso* = this | *dhammo* = religion, law | *saṇātaṇo* = eternal, ancient

**5. COMMENTARY**

Non-stealing and non-possession are paired in this sutra as two aspects of the same ethical reality. Not taking what is not given is the negative form (don't take others' things); non-possession is the positive form (hold nothing beyond what is genuinely necessary). Together they define an entire orientation toward material reality: seeing things as belonging to their users temporarily, not as possessions to be accumulated. The phrase "eternal law" (saṇātana dharma) — the same phrase other traditions use for their own universal law — is deliberately claimed here for the Jain five vows. And crucially, this law applies to both monks and householders. The monk applies it absolutely; the householder applies it in a modified form appropriate to their life. But neither escapes its claim.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

### SUTRA 6.22

Celibacy receives the most emphatic treatment in this chapter — declared four times as supreme in four different domains: austerity, scripture, religion, and refuge. Every sense pleasure, however mild, involves the soul reaching outward and grasping at objects. Sexual desire is this grasping at its most concentrated and powerful. Complete continence — which in Jain monasticism means not only abstaining from sexual acts but training the entire mind to be unattracted by the prospect — represents the master practice that subsumes all others. ### SUTRA 6.23

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तवसा धुयकम्मंसा, झाणजोगेण संजया ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tavasā dhuyakammaṃsā, jhāṇajogena saṃjayā |
saṃtī uvasamaṃ pattā, siddhā bhavaṃti ṇivvuyā || 6.23 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Through austerity, having shaken off karma, restrained through the practice of meditation, having attained peace and equanimity — they become accomplished ones, fully liberated.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tavasā* = through austerity, by means of penance | *dhuyakammaṃsā* = having shaken off karma, karma displaced | *jhāṇajogena* = through the practice of meditation | *saṃjayā* = restrained, controlled | *saṃtī* = peaceful | *uvasamaṃ* = equanimity, subsidence (of passions) | *pattā* = having attained | *siddhā* = accomplished ones | *bhavaṃti* = become | *ṇivvuyā* = fully liberated, at peace

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra describes the full sequence of liberation: austerity shakes karma loose from the soul; meditation provides the focused restraint needed to stop new karma while the old is being shed; peace and equanimity are the experiential result of this progressive unburdening; and the final state is full liberation. The word "shaking off karma" is vivid — karma is described in Jain philosophy as particles that cling to the soul and cloud its natural luminosity. Austerity (tapa) creates, in metaphorical terms, a kind of heat or fire that burns away these clinging particles. Meditation (jhāna) maintains the inner environment in which this dissolution can occur without interference from new karmic influx. The person who completes this process does not die as a karmic being and get reborn — they dissolve the very mechanism of rebirth and become a siddha, a perfected soul.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Through discipline and deep meditation, you gradually shed the accumulated baggage of past actions, become peaceful, and eventually become completely free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 6.25

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अहिंसा समया दंडो, एसो धम्मो सणातणो ।
एत्थ वट्टंति धीरा, मुच्चते जे जियप्पभू ॥६.२५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ahiṃsā samayā daṃḍo, eso dhammo saṇātaṇo |
ettha vaṭṭaṃti dhīrā, muccate je jiyappabhū || 6.25 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Non-violence and equanimity — this is the eternal law. The wise and courageous ones practice this; those who have conquered the self are freed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ahiṃsā* = non-violence | *samayā* = equanimity, inner balance | *daṃḍo* = discipline, restraint (of body, mind, and speech) | *eso* = this | *dhammo* = religion, the law | *saṇātaṇo* = eternal, ancient | *ettha* = in this | *vaṭṭaṃti* = walk, practice, are engaged | *dhīrā* = the brave, the steady, the courageous | *muccate* = are freed | *je* = those who | *jiyappabhū* = have conquered the self, lords of self-victory

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the chapter's featured verse — the fullest statement of the Jain teaching in compressed form. Non-violence and equanimity together constitute the eternal law. Non-violence is the ethical dimension — how you act toward other beings. Equanimity is the psychological dimension — how you stand inside yourself toward all experiences and all beings equally, without preference, aversion, or disturbance. Together they describe a person who neither harms others nor is disturbed by the world. The "brave ones" or "steady ones" (dhīrā) are those who actually practice this — it requires genuine courage to maintain non-violence and equanimity in a world that constantly offers provocations, temptations, and provocations. And the freed ones are those who have conquered the self — not an external enemy but the internal tyrant of ego, desire, and passion.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Non-violence and inner balance together are the eternal religion. The brave people who actually practice this — and who have conquered their own inner passions — become truly free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which challenges your equanimity most — is it external difficulties or your own reactions to them?

### SUTRA 6.26

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खु णायपुत्तेण, भगवया देसिया समा ।
धम्मो एसो वियाहिओ, सव्वदुक्खप्पहीणओ ॥६.२६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ khu ṇāyaputtena, bhagavatā desiyā samā |
dhammo eso viyāhio, savvadukkha-ppahīṇao || 6.26 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus indeed was the teaching presented with equanimity by the blessed Lord, the son of the Nāya clan; this religion is declared as the one that removes all suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evaṃ khu* = thus indeed | *ṇāyaputtena* = by the son of the Nāya (Jnatri) clan — Mahavira | *bhagavatā* = by the blessed Lord | *desiyā* = taught, instructed | *samā* = with equanimity | *dhammo* = religion | *eso* = this | *viyāhio* = is declared | *savvadukkha* = all suffering | *ppahīṇao* = remover, that which eliminates

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra attributes the entire teaching to Mahavira — "the son of the Nāya clan" is a specific genealogical title identifying him as belonging to the Kshatriya (warrior) clan of the Jnatri (Nāya) family. That the Lord taught this with equanimity is noted because it models the very quality being taught: Mahavira did not deliver these truths with passion, advocacy, or drama but with the settled composure of one who has completely realized them. The description of this religion as "the remover of all suffering" is the ultimate claim — not the reducer or the mitigator of suffering, but the complete remover of all suffering. This is liberation: the complete cessation of suffering at its root, not mere comfort or consolation within the continuing cycle.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The Lord Mahavira taught all of this with complete inner peace — and this religion he taught is the one that removes all suffering completely.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you share wisdom or teachings with others, do you share them from a place of inner peace — or from a place of needing them to agree, be impressed, or follow your lead?

### SUTRA 6.27

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं सुणित्ता धम्माओ, सिद्धिमग्गमणुत्तरं ।
आयाणमेयं कप्पिस्सं, इइ बेमि ॥६.२७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ suṇittā dhammāo, siddhimaggamaṇuttaraṃ |
āyāṇameyaṃ kappissaṃ, iti bemi || 6.27 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having heard this from the teaching — the unsurpassed path to liberation — I will make this my own practice. — iti bemi (Thus I say.)

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *suṇittā* = having heard | *dhammāo* = from the teaching | *siddhimaggam* = the path to liberation | *aṇuttaraṃ* = unsurpassed, supreme | *āyāṇam* = my own, belonging to oneself | *eyaṃ* = this | *kappissaṃ* = I will make, I will adopt | *iti bemi* = thus I say, so do I declare

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra is a declaration of personal commitment — the seeker who began by asking "what is true religion?" now closes by saying: "I have heard it. I accept it. I will practice it." This is the structure the Sutrakritanga consistently uses: teaching is not passive information but a call to action. The hearing is followed by the decision. The declaration "Thus I say" (iti bemi) is the traditional closing formula of the Sutrakritanga, attributed to the compiler Sudharma Swami, affirming that this is authentic teaching from the lineage of Mahavira. The entire arc of the chapter — from the question about true religion, through the positive declaration of the five vows, through the critique of false paths, through the elaboration of each vow — arrives here at the moment of personal acceptance and commitment.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Having understood the supreme path to liberation from this teaching, I commit to making it my own practice. — Thus I say.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What teaching have you heard but not yet committed to practicing — and what is holding you back from making it your own?
