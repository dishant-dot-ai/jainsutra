# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 12: Mahapathika (The Great Wanderer)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** First Shrutaskandha  
**Chapter:** 12  
**Prakrit Title:** Mahapathika  
**Approximate Sutras:** 49  
**Theme:** The advanced monk who has mastered all 22 endurances and stands at the threshold of liberation — what full accomplishment looks like in the world.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> से य महप्पहाए महाणुभागे, महावीरिए महाजसे।  
> जियक्किले य जियद्दंडे, से भिक्खू न पमायए॥

*The one of great splendor, great power, great heroism, great fame — who has conquered affliction and conquered the rod of karma — that monk does not become negligent.*

---

## Part I — The Great Monk Described (Sutras 1–15)

**12.1**  
*Prakrit:* महाणुभागे महावीरे, जियसत्तू जियिंदिए।  
*Translation:* Of great power, great heroism — he has conquered his enemies and conquered his senses.  
*Commentary:* The "enemies" conquered here are internal — the four great passions of anger, pride, deceit, and greed. These are the forces that generate karma and keep the soul bound. The "senses" conquered are the five doors through which the world enters — sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch — as pathways for attachment. The monk described in this chapter has done both. He is not a beginner enduring hardship; he is the accomplished one who has systematically dismantled the architecture of bondage.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has done the inner work all the way through.** He has conquered both the outer distractions and the inner passions.

**12.2**  
*Prakrit:* से भिक्खू एयचरिए, न पमज्जइ कयाइ वि।  
*Translation:* That monk, walking the solitary path, is never negligent at any time.  
*Commentary:* "Never negligent at any time" is a description of complete wakefulness. The ordinary monk works to maintain vigilance; the great monk has made vigilance his natural state. There is no longer a gap between the intention to practice and the practice itself — the two have merged. Walking, eating, sleeping, speaking — every activity of the great monk is suffused with the same quality of awareness that a beginning monk brings only in formal practice.  
*The simple version:* **Advanced practice means being awake all the time, not just during formal practice.** Vigilance becomes as natural as breathing.

**12.3**  
*Prakrit:* तवस्सी संजए धीरे, महाणुभावे महव्वए।  
*Translation:* The austere one, restrained, courageous, of great power, under the great vows.  
*Commentary:* The great vows — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession — are the skeleton of the monk's life. "Great" distinguishes these vows from the lesser vows of the householder, who observes partial versions of the same principles. The monk under the great vows has committed to the complete form of each. The austerities are the flesh built on that skeleton: fasting, exposure to heat and cold, rough sleeping conditions, standing meditation, silence. Together they constitute a life of total discipline.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has built a complete life of discipline.** The vows are the foundation; the austerities are the daily expression of that foundation.

**12.4**  
*Prakrit:* अदीणे अपरिस्सावे, अकुहे अमड़े महं।  
*Translation:* Not dejected, not anxious, not deceitful, not arrogant — the great one.  
*Commentary:* These four absences describe the great monk's inner climate. Dejection and anxiety are responses to circumstances — the mind that swings low when things go badly. Deceit and arrogance are distortions of speech and self-image. The great monk has worked through all four. His inner state is not dependent on circumstances being favorable. His speech is clear because there is no hidden agenda. His self-view is accurate because neither pride nor false humility distort it.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk's mind is stable, his speech is clean, his self-view is accurate.** These are not achievements of personality — they are results of years of inner work.

**12.5**  
*Prakrit:* न य पणइत्थीणं सद्धिं, थी य पुरिसे य संसए।  
*Translation:* He does not associate with women attached to sense pleasures, nor does he doubt men or women.  
*Commentary:* The instruction on association protects the monk's practice from the specific pull of sensory attraction. But the second half is equally important: he does not doubt men or women in the sense of holding people in contempt or suspicion. The great monk maintains pure non-judgmental awareness of all beings while still exercising careful discernment about which environments sustain the practice. Avoidance of certain associations is not the same as hostility toward the people involved.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk avoids seductive environments but holds no contempt for anyone.** Wise discernment and genuine goodwill coexist without contradiction.

**12.6**  
*Prakrit:* से य कंखं न विज्जइ, पत्थयाए किंचि लोगम्मि।  
*Translation:* He has no longing, no desire for anything in the world.  
*Commentary:* The complete absence of longing is not the same as the absence of preference or pleasure. The great monk can taste food, feel warmth, hear music — sensation continues. But the cognitive structure of longing — "I want this," "I need to have this," "I cannot be well without this" — this structure has been dismantled. The world passes through his awareness as weather passes through an open door: nothing is refused entry, but nothing is grabbed and held.  
*The simple version:* **Longing is the hunger that is never satisfied.** The great monk has stopped feeding it, and so it has died.

**12.7**  
*Prakrit:* न य सोयइ न य हरिसइ, लाभालाभे सुहे दुहे।  
*Translation:* He neither grieves nor rejoices in gain and loss, pleasure and pain.  
*Commentary:* Gain and loss, pleasure and pain — these are the four basic swings of worldly experience. The great monk has moved beyond all four as drivers of his emotional state. This is the completion of what Chapter 11 began to describe. The wanderer endures hardships; the great wanderer has moved beyond being affected by the categories of hardship and ease altogether. The equanimity is no longer maintained by effort — it is the monk's natural ground.  
*The simple version:* **Beyond endurance is a state where there is nothing left to endure.** The great monk does not work to stay balanced — balance is simply what he is.

**12.8**  
*Prakrit:* न य से इत्थिओ काहिइ, न य से आहारसंसए।  
*Translation:* He has no thought of women, no anxiety about food.  
*Commentary:* The two specific anxieties named here — sexual attraction and food anxiety — represent the body's two most persistent claims on consciousness. The drive for sexual union and the fear of not having enough food are among the deepest animal impulses in human nature. The great monk has moved past both. Not through suppression — suppression would require constant effort — but through genuine transformation of how the body's signals are received and processed.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk is no longer driven by the body's deepest hungers.** This is not suppression but genuine freedom from what most people consider unavoidable.

**12.9**  
*Prakrit:* जियपुलोगे जियकसाए, जियद्दंडे जियासए।  
*Translation:* He has conquered distraction, conquered passions, conquered the rod of karma, conquered desire.  
*Commentary:* The four conquests describe the full scope of the inner battle. Distraction (the mind's tendency to wander from the practice); the passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed — the four great generators of karma); the rod of karma (the force of past actions that drives the soul through successive births); and desire (the basic craving that initiates all karma-creating action). Conquering all four means the mechanism of bondage has been fully disabled.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has won on every front of the inner battle.** Nothing remains that generates new bondage.

**12.10**  
*Prakrit:* समो य वायावरणे, समो णं उण्हे सीए।  
*Translation:* He is the same in wind and shade, the same in heat and cold.  
*Commentary:* Physical equanimity mirrors the inner state. The monk who is equally comfortable in extreme heat and extreme cold has not found a way to avoid sensation — he feels both fully. But sensation no longer commands his behavior. He does not rush to shade when the sun is brutal; he does not seek warmth when the cold bites. The body's reports are received without urgency. This physical equanimity is the visible expression of the inner work that has been completed.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk's body and mind respond to all conditions the same way.** Temperature, weather, comfort — none of these move him.

**12.11**  
*Prakrit:* समो य पूया अपूयाए, समो यावि पसंसणे।  
*Translation:* He is the same whether honored or dishonored, the same whether praised or not.  
*Commentary:* Social weather — praise, blame, honor, dishonor — is the most powerful force acting on ordinary human behavior. People shape their lives around managing how they are perceived. The great monk has become genuinely indifferent to social weather. Not indifferent in the sense of withdrawn from human relationships, but indifferent in the precise sense: how he is perceived by others does not alter who he is or what he does. The inner state is independent of the social temperature.  
*The simple version:* **Most people live for praise and fear shame.** The great monk has freed himself from both, completely.

**12.12**  
*Prakrit:* अलद्धपुव्वे पुरिसे, कप्पइ णं एमेव धम्मे।  
*Translation:* The man who has never found this before — for him this teaching is appropriate.  
*Commentary:* This sutra addresses the newcomer — the person who has lived their entire life without encountering this framework for understanding the self and liberation. For such a person, the teaching of the great wanderer is not immediately attainable — but it is the correct aspiration. The description of the great monk is not meant to discourage the beginner by showing how far there is to go; it is meant to show what is possible, to make the highest attainment visible as a real destination.  
*The simple version:* **The teaching of the great wanderer is for everyone, including those just beginning.** Knowing where the path leads is essential for walking it.

**12.13**  
*Prakrit:* से महाणुभागे महावीरे, विगयलेस्से विगयमाले।  
*Translation:* Of great power, great heroism — free from impurities, free from garlands.  
*Commentary:* The garlands here refer to the ceremonial decorations of worldly life — the markings of status, celebration, belonging. The monk who has released "garlands" has released the entire system of social ornament that ordinary life uses to mark significance. "Free from impurities" refers to the karmic colorations that cloud the soul's natural luminosity. The soul of the great monk is beginning to shine through — not metaphorically but, in Jain understanding, literally.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has let go of both inner pollution and outer decoration.** What remains is the soul itself, unobscured.

**12.14**  
*Prakrit:* असत्थसत्थे अभए, अकिंचणे विगयरागे।  
*Translation:* Without weapon or scripture as shelter, without fear, without possessions, without attachment.  
*Commentary:* This sutra is remarkable for its inclusion of "scripture as shelter." The great monk does not hide behind his learning. He has moved beyond needing the texts as psychological protection — the doctrines of karma and liberation that were once reference points he needed to consult have been internalized so completely that they are no longer external authorities. He lives the teaching rather than citing it. Combined with possessionlessness and the absence of fear, this describes a person who has become entirely self-sufficient in the deepest sense.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk does not need anything to lean on — not possessions, not status, not even scriptural authority.** He has become the teaching.

**12.15**  
*Prakrit:* एयं च मग्गं जाणित्ता, सम्मं पव्वइए इह।  
*Translation:* Understanding this path, he has gone forth correctly here in this world.  
*Commentary:* The going-forth — the formal renunciation that marks the monk's entrance into the monastic life — is described here as something done "correctly" when it is done with genuine understanding. Many people renounce the world for wrong reasons: flight from failure, social pressure, imitation of a respected person, desire for security. Correct going-forth is based on genuine understanding of what the world is and what the path offers. The great monk went forth correctly — and this is part of why he has arrived where he has.  
*The simple version:* **How you begin the path matters.** Renunciation done from genuine understanding builds a different foundation than renunciation done from circumstance or imitation.

## Part II — Conquest of the Senses (Sutras 16–30)

**12.16**  
*Prakrit:* पंचिंदियाइं जाणित्ता, संजए लहुइज्जमाणे।  
*Translation:* Understanding the five senses, he is restrained, moving lightly.  
*Commentary:* "Moving lightly" describes the great monk's relationship to the world — he does not press hard against it, does not grip, does not leave deep marks of desire or aversion wherever he goes. Understanding the five senses means understanding them as pathways for karma — every sensory encounter can either bind or be navigated with awareness. The monk who has mastered this navigates the sensory world like a boat that moves through water without being absorbed by it.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding the senses means knowing how they pull you.** The monk who understands this moves through the world without being captured by what he encounters.

**12.17**  
*Prakrit:* फासेहिं रूवेहिं सद्देहिं, रसेहिं गंधेहिं अमुच्छिए।  
*Translation:* Unattached to touch, form, sound, taste, and smell.  
*Commentary:* The five sensory objects are enumerated completely: touch, form, sound, taste, and smell. These are the specific contents that flow through the five sense-doors. The great monk is "unattached" to all five — which means he encounters all five without producing the grasping response that generates karma. He feels, sees, hears, tastes, and smells exactly as other people do. The difference is in what happens after the sensation registers: for others, a chain of reaction begins; for the great monk, awareness registers and the chain does not start.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk experiences the world fully but does not react to it with craving.** Sensation happens; attachment does not follow.

**12.18**  
*Prakrit:* सद्दे य रूवे य पसे य रसे, गंधे य पंचिंदियजोगे।  
*Translation:* Sound, form, touch, taste, smell — the five sensory engagements.  
*Commentary:* The repetition of the five senses across multiple sutras is pedagogical — the teacher wants the student to internalize these categories completely, to be able to identify them in real time as they arise. Each time a sensory object is encountered, the monk ideally recognizes the category and the response pattern that the category triggers. Recognition is the first step toward freedom: you cannot release something you have not identified.  
*The simple version:* **Know your sensory triggers by name.** Identification is the beginning of freedom from automatic reaction.

**12.19**  
*Prakrit:* जे णं लोगे महेसिणो, विरया भोगपारगा।  
*Translation:* Those great seekers in the world who are detached and have passed beyond enjoyments.  
*Commentary:* The "great seekers" — the Tirthankaras and the great ascetics who have preceded this generation — are invoked here as examples. They have "passed beyond enjoyments" in the sense of having moved through the stage where sensory pleasure has any hold on them. They are not people who have never enjoyed the world; they are people who enjoyed the world, understood it, and moved beyond it. The path runs through life, not around it.  
*The simple version:* **The great ones passed through enjoyment and came out the other side.** They are not people who never lived — they are people who fully understood what living costs.

**12.20**  
*Prakrit:* एयं च धम्मं परिण्णाय, जे य अण्णे तहेव य।  
*Translation:* Having fully understood this teaching, and those others who have done likewise.  
*Commentary:* The great monk does not walk alone through history — he walks in a lineage of those who have made the same journey. "Those others who have done likewise" names the community of accomplished monks across time. This is not just encouragement; it is ontological orientation. The monk understands himself as part of a continuous lineage of practice, receiving from those who preceded him and passing to those who will follow. He is a link in the chain, not an isolated achievement.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk is part of a long tradition of those who have done the same work.** Knowing this is itself a form of support.

**12.21**  
*Prakrit:* सुयपरिण्णे चरित्तपरिण्णे, तवपरिण्णे तेयलेस्से।  
*Translation:* He has fully understood scripture, conduct, and austerity — radiant with inner brilliance.  
*Commentary:* Three complete understandings are described: of scripture (the theoretical knowledge of doctrine), of conduct (the practical expression of doctrine in behavior), and of austerity (the transformative practice that burns away existing karma). Together these constitute the complete curriculum of the Jain monastic path. "Radiant with inner brilliance" describes what appears when these three are genuinely mastered — a quality of presence that others recognize without being able to fully articulate.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has mastered the theory, the practice, and the transformation.** The combination produces a visible brilliance.

**12.22**  
*Prakrit:* असंजय गिहत्थाणं, आयारो होइ उत्तमो।  
*Translation:* For the undisciplined householder, a higher standard of conduct now becomes appropriate.  
*Commentary:* The sutra shifts to address the householder who encounters the great monk. The monk's example is not just inspiration — it is a specific kind of teaching that raises the standard the householder sets for themselves. Even if the householder cannot replicate the monk's full discipline, they can move their own conduct closer to the ideal. The monk's presence in the world functions as a calibration instrument, reminding everyone around him of what human beings are capable of.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk raises everyone's standard just by being present.** You don't need to be a monk to be changed by encountering one.

**12.23**  
*Prakrit:* एवं महप्पहाए से, ण य भोगेसु रज्जई।  
*Translation:* Thus the one of great splendor does not delight in sensory enjoyments.  
*Commentary:* "Does not delight" is different from "avoids." The great monk does not arrange his life to avoid encountering pleasurable things — he walks through markets, hears music, smells food. But when pleasure arises, no delight is generated in the sense of the mind reaching out to hold it. The lotus that grows in water without being wet is the traditional image: fully in the world, not stained by it. This is the advanced stage of practice where avoidance is no longer necessary because clinging has stopped.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk does not avoid pleasure — he simply doesn't cling to it.** There is a difference between suppression and genuine non-attachment.

**12.24**  
*Prakrit:* जे केई कसाया लोगे, ते सव्वे परिण्णाए।  
*Translation:* Whatever passions exist in the world — all of them he has fully understood.  
*Commentary:* The word "understood" is again doing heavy work. The great monk has not simply recognized the names of the passions; he has understood them from the inside — how they arise, what feeds them, how they produce karma, and how they dissolve when they are not fed. This insider understanding is only available through direct practice. Reading about anger is not the same as having worked through anger completely. The great monk has done the latter.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk knows the passions from having worked through them, not just from reading about them.** That is a different kind of knowledge entirely.

**12.25**  
*Prakrit:* से महाणुभाए विगयरागे, विगयदोसे विगयमोहे।  
*Translation:* The one of great power, free from attachment, free from aversion, free from delusion.  
*Commentary:* The three root afflictions of Jain philosophy — attachment (raga), aversion (dvesha), and delusion (moha) — are named here as conquered. These three are the deep roots from which all karma-producing action grows. Attachment drives the soul toward what it craves; aversion drives it away from what it fears; delusion causes it to misidentify itself as the body and the social persona rather than as the pure conscious soul. The great monk has cut all three roots.  
*The simple version:* **The three roots of bondage — craving, hatred, confusion — have all been cut.** When the roots are gone, no new karma grows.

**12.26**  
*Prakrit:* न य रागेण सिज्झइ, न य दोसेण मुच्चई।  
*Translation:* One does not succeed through attachment, nor is one liberated through aversion.  
*Commentary:* This sutra is a correction of two common errors on the path. The error of attachment: seeking liberation through intense desire for it — wanting liberation as an object to possess, meditating from a place of craving rather than clarity. The error of aversion: rejecting the world violently, practicing with hatred of the body or contempt for ordinary beings. Both attachment and aversion generate karma, regardless of what they are directed toward. The middle path — equanimity, not passion in either direction — is the only way forward.  
*The simple version:* **You cannot want your way to liberation, and you cannot hate your way there.** Passionate craving and passionate rejection both bind the soul.

**12.27**  
*Prakrit:* मोहेण विप्परामुट्ठो, जाइमरणसंसारे।  
*Translation:* Seized by delusion, one wanders in the cycle of birth and death.  
*Commentary:* Delusion is the root cause of the wandering in the cycle of rebirth. The deluded soul believes it is the body, believes it is the social identity, believes that acquiring and protecting certain objects and relationships constitutes a meaningful life. This delusion generates the activities that generate karma that generates rebirth. Break the delusion and the cycle has been interrupted at its root. The entire path of Jain practice is, at its core, the systematic dissolution of this fundamental misidentification.  
*The simple version:* **The deepest problem is not bad behavior — it is not knowing who you are.** When the soul misidentifies itself, everything that follows is wrong.

**12.28**  
*Prakrit:* सम्मद्दिट्ठी सया होज्जा, मोहं चेव विवज्जए।  
*Translation:* He should always have right vision and always abandon delusion.  
*Commentary:* Right vision — the correct perception of the soul's true nature as pure consciousness, distinct from the body and from the social persona — is not a one-time achievement. It must be maintained continuously. The instruction "should always have right vision" acknowledges that delusion can return: the pressures of social life, the sensory pull of the world, the habit-patterns of a lifetime can temporarily cloud the clarity that practice has produced. The monk remains vigilant about his vision.  
*The simple version:* **Right vision needs to be maintained, not just achieved.** Delusion is always looking for an opening.

**12.29**  
*Prakrit:* सम्मद्दिट्ठी सया जाणइ, कम्मणो बंधणं तहा।  
*Translation:* The one with right vision always understands the bondage of karma.  
*Commentary:* Right vision and understanding of karma are inseparable. The soul that sees itself correctly as pure consciousness automatically understands karma as something foreign that has adhered to it through its own actions — not a punishment from outside but a self-created coating. This understanding is both technically accurate (as a description of how karma works) and practically liberating (because it identifies the source of bondage as something the soul can address through its own conduct).  
*The simple version:* **Right vision means seeing karma clearly — as something you made and can therefore unmake.** This is empowering, not deterministic.

**12.30**  
*Prakrit:* एयं च मग्गं जाणित्ता, से भिक्खू अनुवट्टए।  
*Translation:* Understanding this path, the monk follows it.  
*Commentary:* The simplicity of this sutra is its power. Understanding leads to following. The monk who has genuinely understood the path — not just intellectually grasped it but understood it in the way that changes behavior — naturally follows it. This is the Jain teaching's optimism about human nature: people do not fail to follow the right path because of some fundamental wickedness, but because they have not yet understood it clearly. Real understanding is self-executing.  
*The simple version:* **Real understanding automatically produces right action.** You don't need willpower on top of understanding — understanding is enough.

## Part III — Approaching Liberation (Sutras 31–49)

**12.31**  
*Prakrit:* सव्वदुक्खविमुक्काए, सव्वसंसारपारगए।  
*Translation:* Freed from all suffering, having crossed to the far shore of the cycle of rebirth.  
*Commentary:* The description begins to shade into what awaits at the end of the path. Freedom from all suffering is not a gradual diminishment of pain — it is a qualitative transition. The soul that has crossed to the far shore of the cycle of rebirth has exited the system entirely. It is no longer subject to karma, to birth, to the conditions of embodied existence. The freedom described here is total — not better circumstances but the end of circumstance as a constraint on the soul.  
*The simple version:* **Liberation is not a better life — it is freedom from the entire system of birth, suffering, and death.** It is a different order of existence.

**12.32**  
*Prakrit:* से य भिक्खू महाणुभाए, जियपरिसहे जियकिलेसे।  
*Translation:* That monk of great power has conquered all hardships, conquered all afflictions.  
*Commentary:* The great monk approaching liberation has not merely endured hardships — he has conquered them. The distinction matters: endurance implies ongoing struggle, a battle still being fought. Conquest implies that the struggle is over. The afflictions — the hardships from outside and the passions from inside — no longer present as contests. They arise, and the monk simply does not engage. There is no longer a war because only one side is fighting.  
*The simple version:* **There is a difference between fighting hardship and having moved past it.** The great monk is past the fighting.

**12.33**  
*Prakrit:* कम्मुणा बज्झई जीवे, कम्मुणा मुच्चई जीवे।  
*Translation:* The soul is bound by karma; the soul is freed by karma.  
*Commentary:* This is one of the most precise statements in Jain philosophy. The soul is bound not by some external force but by its own actions — and freed by the same mechanism running in reverse. The actions that produce binding karma (rooted in attachment, aversion, and delusion) are replaced by actions that produce no new karma and burn away existing karma (rooted in right vision, restraint, and austerity). The same system that enslaves is the system of liberation. Nothing external is required.  
*The simple version:* **You bound yourself, and you can free yourself.** The mechanism of bondage and the mechanism of liberation are the same — just running in different directions.

**12.34**  
*Prakrit:* जे णं धम्मं विजाणिया, सो णं दुक्खा पमुच्चई।  
*Translation:* He who truly understands the teaching is freed from suffering.  
*Commentary:* The causal chain is direct: understand the teaching, be freed from suffering. No intermediary is required — no ritual, no priest, no divine favor. The understanding itself is the operative mechanism. This directness is characteristic of Jain teaching: liberation is a consequence of understanding and action, not of belief or devotion in the sense of seeking approval from an external being. The monk who understands and acts accordingly walks directly toward freedom.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding the teaching is not just intellectually satisfying — it is functionally liberating.** Real understanding changes the karma-producing patterns.

**12.35**  
*Prakrit:* नो तस्स संगो लोगम्मि, जस्स नत्थि ममायणं।  
*Translation:* He has no entanglement in the world who has no sense of "mine."  
*Commentary:* The sense of "mine" — possessiveness — is the specific mechanism of entanglement. It is not objects that entangle but the mind's claim on objects. The person who says "this is mine" about a house, a person, a reputation, or a belief has created a bond that will now need to be defended, maintained, and eventually lost with suffering. The person who has released the "mine" structure has released entanglement itself. Objects can still be used; nothing needs to be held.  
*The simple version:* **Entanglement is not caused by things — it is caused by the word "mine."** Release that word and you are released from the world.

**12.36**  
*Prakrit:* न य तस्स सिया भारो, जहा तुलाए समं ठिए।  
*Translation:* There is no burden for him, as one stands even on a scale.  
*Commentary:* The image of the balanced scale captures the inner state of the great monk: perfectly level, equally responsive to everything, not weighted down on either side by preference or aversion. The "burden" refers not to physical weight but to the psychological weight of attachment — the heaviness that comes from carrying many "mine" objects through life. The great monk has set these down. He stands upright, level, unencumbered.  
*The simple version:* **When you carry nothing, nothing is heavy.** The monk who has released his claims on the world moves through it as though weightless.

**12.37**  
*Prakrit:* एयं महप्पहाए से, महाणुभागे महावीरिए।  
*Translation:* This is the great splendor, great power, great heroism of that one.  
*Commentary:* The chapter returns to the opening epithets — great splendor, great power, great heroism — and they now carry the full weight of everything described in between. These are not honorific titles given by others; they are descriptions of actual states. Great splendor is the luminosity of a soul largely freed from karmic coloration. Great power is the strength that comes from having no inner contradictions. Great heroism is the courage of having done the inner work completely.  
*The simple version:* **The monk's greatness is real, not ceremonial.** It is the result of what has been accomplished, not a title that was given.

**12.38**  
*Prakrit:* तस्स अत्थि पराक्कमो, जो सयं सत्थमाहिए।  
*Translation:* He has the strength of one who is himself the authority.  
*Commentary:* The great monk is his own authority. Not in the sense of making up his own doctrine, but in the sense that he has internalized the teaching so completely that he no longer needs external validation or instruction. He knows what is right because he has cultivated the faculty of right vision to the point where it operates clearly and reliably. This self-authority is the completion of the path from dependence on teachers to independence in practice.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk has internalized the teaching so deeply that he knows what is right from within.** He no longer needs to be told.

**12.39**  
*Prakrit:* से भिक्खू एयचरिए, पालए सव्वसंजमं।  
*Translation:* That monk, walking the solitary path, maintains all restraint.  
*Commentary:* "All restraint" is the totality again. Not most restraint, not restraint in the areas that come naturally — all restraint. The monk at this advanced stage does not have categories of practice that are difficult and categories that are easy. The entire field of conduct has been brought to the same level of effortless precision. The restraint is total not because the monk is white-knuckling the hard parts but because the desire to abandon restraint has been removed at its root.  
*The simple version:* **Complete restraint is not exhausting at this stage — it is natural.** The monk has changed, not just his behavior.

**12.40**  
*Prakrit:* जाणइ लोगमेयं सव्वं, जाणइ आयाणगोयरं।  
*Translation:* He knows this entire world; he knows the domain of taking and giving.  
*Commentary:* To know the entire world is not encyclopedic knowledge of all phenomena. It is understanding the structure of the world as a field of karmic cause and effect — how beings move through births and deaths, how actions create consequences, how the universe operates as a moral system of precise reciprocity. "The domain of taking and giving" refers to the specific karmic transactions that constitute daily life. The monk navigates this domain with complete understanding of what each action produces.  
*The simple version:* **The great monk sees the whole system clearly.** He knows exactly how the world works and how each action fits into the larger pattern.

**12.41**  
*Prakrit:* से धीरो महानुभागो, धम्मं जाणइ पंडिए।  
*Translation:* That courageous, powerful one — the learned one knows the teaching.  
*Commentary:* The combination of courage, power, and learning that the chapter has been building toward is here brought together in a single description. Learning alone — without courage and power — produces a scholar who knows the teaching but cannot live it. Courage alone — without learning — produces an ascetic who endures much but cannot understand what the endurance is for. Power — without learning and courage — is misdirected. Together they produce the complete monk.  
*The simple version:* **The complete monk is learned, courageous, AND powerful.** None of the three alone is enough.

**12.42**  
*Prakrit:* अहिगच्छइ धम्मं सोहम्मं, से पहीणकम्मे।  
*Translation:* He arrives at the excellent teaching, his karma worn away.  
*Commentary:* "Arriving at the excellent teaching" after karma has been worn away is a description of the final stages before liberation. The monk who has burned away most of his accumulated karma begins to see the teaching with a clarity that was not possible when karma was thick. The doctrine of the path begins to illuminate itself — the monk understands not because he is clever but because the obstructions to understanding have been removed. Karma was the fog; its removal is the dawn.  
*The simple version:* **As karma burns away, understanding deepens automatically.** The teaching becomes clearer as the inner pollution is removed.

**12.43**  
*Prakrit:* न य पावाइं करेज्जासि, कम्माइं बहुयाइं वा।  
*Translation:* He does not commit harmful actions, not even many small ones.  
*Commentary:* The refinement at this stage extends to the smallest actions. The beginner works on avoiding large obvious harm. The intermediate monk works on avoiding harm in the difficult situations. The great monk near liberation has refined his practice to the point where even the small, easy-to-rationalize harms have been removed. Careless words, minor possessiveness, subtle self-deception — all of these are as unacceptable to him as murder is to the beginner.  
*The simple version:* **Advanced practice means working on the small things.** At this stage, it is the minor harms that need attention.

**12.44**  
*Prakrit:* न य मुसं भासेज्जासि, वयणं गाढं न भासए।  
*Translation:* He does not speak falsehood, does not speak harsh words.  
*Commentary:* Truthfulness at this stage is not just the avoidance of deliberate lies. It is the complete alignment of inner perception, inner speech, and outer expression. The great monk does not manage his speech — it is naturally truthful because there is nothing inside that needs to be hidden. Harsh speech similarly disappears naturally when aversion has been conquered. You cannot speak harshly when you genuinely hold no ill will toward any being.  
*The simple version:* **When the inner life is clean, speech cleans itself.** Honesty and gentleness are not things the great monk works at — they are what naturally comes out.

**12.45**  
*Prakrit:* समाहियमणे भिक्खू, सव्वत्थ ण विसीयई।  
*Translation:* The monk of composed mind does not fail anywhere.  
*Commentary:* Composed mind — the settled, stable, inward-dwelling awareness — is the quality that prevents failure. "Does not fail anywhere" means there is no situation, no encounter, no level of hardship at which the great monk's composure breaks down. This is not because every situation is manageable but because the monk's inner resources have been developed to the point where they are adequate to everything that can arise.  
*The simple version:* **A truly composed mind handles everything.** There is no situation that can break it because it has been built to cover the whole range of experience.

**12.46**  
*Prakrit:* गाहावइकुलं पविट्ठो, समे भूए विहरमाणए।  
*Translation:* Entering the householder's home, he dwells in an even state.  
*Commentary:* The test of the monk's equanimity in domestic environments is significant. The householder's home is full of the specific conditions that ordinary renunciation tries to flee: family bonds, possessions, comfort, the smell of food prepared with attachment. The great monk enters this environment and is not disturbed by it. His evenness is not maintained by avoidance but by genuine transformation. He can sit in the most tempting environment and remain exactly as he is.  
*The simple version:* **Real equanimity doesn't need special conditions to hold.** The great monk is the same in the householder's dining room as in a forest cave.

**12.47**  
*Prakrit:* एयं च धम्मं परिण्णाय, संजमेण तवेण य।  
*Translation:* Having fully understood this teaching, through restraint and austerity.  
*Commentary:* The two instruments of the path are named together: restraint (which prevents new karma from forming) and austerity (which burns away existing karma). Understanding the teaching is the map; restraint and austerity are the movement. The monk who understands without practicing has the map but stays in place. The monk who practices without understanding might move but may not move in the right direction. Together they constitute the complete path.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding tells you where to go; restraint and austerity actually take you there.** You need both.

**12.48**  
*Prakrit:* अयं सो महाणुभागे, जो लोगं परिजाणिया।  
*Translation:* He is the great one of power who has fully understood the world.  
*Commentary:* The chapter's major theme returns for its final statement: the greatness of the great monk is grounded in understanding. Not in the endurance of hardship for its own sake, not in the performance of austerities as a display, not in the accumulation of merit — but in the complete understanding of the world as a system of karmic cause and effect through which the soul is moving toward or away from liberation. This understanding is the source of everything else the chapter has described.  
*The simple version:* **Greatness in the Jain sense is understanding-based, not achievement-based.** The great monk is great because he understands, and his understanding produces everything else.

**12.49**  
*Prakrit:* से भिक्खू महाणुभाए, लोगं परिजाणिया। — iti bemi  
*Translation:* That monk of great power, having fully understood the world. — Thus I say.  
*Commentary:* The final sutra seals the teaching with the traditional phrase "thus I say" — the signature of the transmission. The great monk is identified not by any external marker but by a single internal quality: complete understanding of the world. Everything else — the conquest of the senses, the burning of karma, the approach to liberation, the radiant inner brilliance — follows from that understanding. The chapter began and ends with power and understanding as one.  
*The simple version:* **The great wanderer is great because of understanding, not achievement.** — Thus I say.
