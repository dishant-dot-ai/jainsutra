# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 15: Nganathaputra (Questions for the Knower of the Path)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** First Shrutaskandha  
**Chapter:** 15  
**Prakrit Title:** Nganathaputra  
**Approximate Sutras:** 24  
**Theme:** A question-and-answer chapter where disciples ask Mahavira direct questions about the path — bondage, liberation, right conduct, and the signs of genuine progress.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> किं मग्गो? किं बंधणं? किं मुक्खो? किं सुहासुहं?  
> एयं पुच्छामो भगवं, अणुसत्थु अम्हे तुमं॥

*What is the path? What is bondage? What is liberation? What is happiness and suffering? We ask you, Lord — teach us.*

---

## Part I — What Is Bondage? (Sutras 1–8)

**15.1**  
*Prakrit:* किं मग्गो? किं बंधणं? भगवं, एयं पुच्छामहे तुमं।  
*Translation:* What is the path? What is bondage? Lord, this we ask you.  
*Commentary:* The chapter opens with the disciples' direct question to Mahavira. "Lord" — the honorific given to the Tirthankara — signals that this is a teaching given from the highest authority, the one who has crossed to liberation and can describe both the path and the mechanism of bondage from direct experience, not from inference or tradition. The directness of the question models the directness that good students bring: not elaborate philosophical preamble, but the core question stated plainly.  
*The simple version:* **Good students ask direct questions.** The disciples here go straight to what matters most: what binds and what frees.

**15.2**  
*Prakrit:* बुद्धि तिउट्टे पाणे, चित्तमंते अचित्तए।  
*Translation:* The souls — with consciousness and without consciousness — should be understood.  
*Commentary:* Mahavira begins his answer to the bondage question by distinguishing between living beings with consciousness (souls) and non-living matter without consciousness. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of understanding bondage: bondage is what happens when the conscious soul becomes entangled with non-conscious matter through the activities of mind, speech, and body. Without understanding what the soul is, one cannot understand what it means for the soul to be bound.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding bondage starts with understanding what a soul is.** You cannot understand what binds the soul if you do not first know what the soul is.

**15.3**  
*Prakrit:* चित्तमंते पलिउंचइ, अचित्तं चित्तजं चेव।  
*Translation:* The conscious one gets entangled; the non-conscious arises from the conscious.  
*Commentary:* The mechanism of bondage: consciousness (the soul) acts, and those actions involve non-conscious matter (physical and subtle). The "non-conscious arising from the conscious" describes karma: karmic particles are non-conscious matter, but they adhere to the soul through the conscious activities of mind, speech, and body. The soul's own activities produce the coating that binds it. This is why liberation requires changing those activities — not changing the environment, but changing what the soul does.  
*The simple version:* **The soul creates its own bondage through its own actions.** Nothing external is doing this — the soul is doing it to itself.

**15.4**  
*Prakrit:* एवं बंधणमाहिए, मोक्खस्स य इमो विही।  
*Translation:* Thus bondage has been described; and this is the procedure for liberation.  
*Commentary:* The answer to the bondage question is complete, and the teacher transitions to liberation. The precision of the transition is important: once you understand how bondage works, the liberation procedure follows logically. Bondage results from the soul's karma-generating activities; liberation results from stopping those activities and burning away accumulated karma through restraint and austerity. The two are mirror images — understanding one is understanding both.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding bondage is already half the answer about liberation.** The path out is the reverse of the path in.

**15.5**  
*Prakrit:* ण कुज्झे ण य माणे, ण य माया ण लोभए।  
*Translation:* No anger, no pride, no deceit, no greed.  
*Commentary:* The four passions — anger, pride, deceit, and greed — are named as the generators of bondage. Each of these produces karma when it drives action. Anger produces violence through speech and body. Pride produces the mental claim of superiority that distorts action. Deceit produces false representations that spread wrong understanding. Greed produces the accumulation of more and more, which requires more and more harmful action to acquire and protect. Eliminating the four passions is the core work of liberation.  
*The simple version:* **The four passions are the engines of bondage.** Every action driven by anger, pride, deceit, or greed adds to the chains.

**15.6**  
*Prakrit:* कोहं जेइ माणं जेइ, मायं जेइ लोहं जेइ।  
*Translation:* He conquers anger, conquers pride, conquers deceit, conquers greed.  
*Commentary:* The four conquests — anger, pride, deceit, greed — describe what the liberation procedure actually consists of. These conquests are not achieved once and then finished; they are the ongoing work of the path. Anger is conquered in the moment of provocation by recognizing the passion before it produces action. Pride is conquered by accurate self-assessment. Deceit is conquered by maintaining truthfulness even when it is costly. Greed is conquered by the constant practice of sufficiency.  
*The simple version:* **Liberation is built from these four conquests, repeated until they are complete.** Each victory over a passion weakens it permanently.

**15.7**  
*Prakrit:* न कुणइ न कारवइ, न अणुजाणइ पाणिणं।  
*Translation:* He does not do, does not cause to be done, does not approve of harming living beings.  
*Commentary:* The three modes of participation in harm are named: direct action, having others act, and approving of actions. All three generate karma. The monk who does not personally harm but who directs an attendant to remove an obstacle by harming creatures is equally guilty. The monk who passively approves of a harmful action — by saying nothing when he should speak, by accepting the fruits of harm without objection — is also generating karma. Non-violence must extend to all three modes.  
*The simple version:* **Non-violence covers three things: what you do, what you cause others to do, and what you approve of.** All three count.

**15.8**  
*Prakrit:* एवमेव भगवं मग्गो, एयं बंधणं तहा।  
*Translation:* Thus, Lord, is the path; and thus is bondage.  
*Commentary:* The first part of the teaching is sealed. Bondage: the soul's karma-generating activities driven by the four passions, producing entanglement with non-conscious matter. The path: conquering the four passions, practicing non-harm in all three modes, and understanding the soul's true nature. The disciple who asked is confirmed in their understanding. The "thus" is the signal that the teaching has been accurately received and reflected back to the teacher for confirmation.  
*The simple version:* **The teaching is confirmed by the student reflecting it back.** Understanding is verified by being able to say it back accurately.

## Part II — What Is the Path? (Sutras 9–16)

**15.9**  
*Prakrit:* किं मुक्खो? भगवं पुच्छामहे, मुक्खो य दुक्खस्स किं?  
*Translation:* What is liberation, Lord, we ask? And what is liberation from suffering?  
*Commentary:* The disciples deepen the questioning. Having understood bondage, they now press on liberation — both as a state and as the liberation from suffering specifically. These are two angles on the same reality: liberation is the soul's return to its natural state of pure consciousness, and this return is simultaneously the end of all suffering. The separation of the question into these two angles shows the disciples' genuine engagement: they want to understand liberation from both the metaphysical and the experiential direction.  
*The simple version:* **What is liberation? And what does it feel like from the inside?** Both angles on the question are worth asking.

**15.10**  
*Prakrit:* जे णं धम्मं विजाणइ, सो मुच्चइ सव्वदुक्खाओ।  
*Translation:* He who truly understands the teaching is freed from all suffering.  
*Commentary:* The answer to both questions at once: understanding the teaching is liberation, and it is simultaneously liberation from all suffering. This is not a consolation prize — "follow the teaching and you'll suffer less." It is a precise description of what happens when the understanding is complete. The soul that fully understands what it is, how it got bound, and what the path out consists of has, in that understanding, begun to dissolve the bondage. Complete understanding is the final event of liberation.  
*The simple version:* **Liberation and the end of suffering are the same event.** Understanding completely is the moment the suffering structure collapses.

**15.11**  
*Prakrit:* जाणइ बंधणं मोक्खं, जाणइ लोगं अणाइयं।  
*Translation:* He knows bondage and liberation; he knows the world as beginningless.  
*Commentary:* The monk who has arrived at this understanding knows the full scope of what he is dealing with: a world of bondage that has no identifiable beginning, a cycle of rebirth that extends back further than any memory or record. The enormity of what is being worked through is fully visible to him. And he also knows liberation — knows it as real, achievable, and precisely described. Holding both — the scope of the problem and the certainty of the solution — is the monk's specific orientation.  
*The simple version:* **The problem is vast; the solution is clear and achievable.** Holding both truths at once is the monk's orientation.

**15.12**  
*Prakrit:* सम्मद्दिट्ठी सया होज्जा, लोगदिट्ठी विवज्जए।  
*Translation:* He should always have right vision, and abandon the worldly view.  
*Commentary:* "Worldly view" is the view of the ordinary person who takes the body to be the self, the social persona to be the soul's identity, and the satisfaction of desire to be the purpose of existence. Right vision is the correction of this view: the soul is pure consciousness, temporarily associated with matter through karma, capable of liberation. The shift from worldly view to right vision is the most fundamental change on the path — everything else follows from it or is blocked without it.  
*The simple version:* **The shift from worldly view to right vision is the most fundamental change on the path.** Everything else follows from making this shift — or is blocked without it.

**15.13**  
*Prakrit:* ण य पावाइं करेज्जासि, ण अण्णे कारवेज्जासि।  
*Translation:* He should not do harmful acts, should not cause others to do them.  
*Commentary:* The behavioral expression of right vision: when you see correctly what harmful action produces — karma, bondage, continued suffering for the soul — you stop doing it and stop encouraging others to do it. The instruction is symmetrical: personal conduct and influence over others' conduct are both within the scope of the monk's responsibility. The monk's conduct sets a standard; the monk's influence extends that standard to the wider community.  
*The simple version:* **Right vision changes both what you do and what you encourage.** Genuine understanding produces both personal change and changed influence on others.

**15.14**  
*Prakrit:* एयमेव सयं जाणे, एयं च परिण्णाए।  
*Translation:* He should know this himself, and understand it completely.  
*Commentary:* Personal, direct knowledge is required. The monk is not asked to take the teaching on faith or on the authority of the teacher alone. The instruction is to know it oneself — to verify the teaching through one's own practice and experience. The tradition values this direct verification: the teaching of karma, bondage, and liberation is not a matter of belief but of observation. The monk who practices carefully will observe the correspondence between action and consequence in his own life.  
*The simple version:* **Verify the teaching through your own experience.** The tradition does not ask for blind faith — it asks you to observe and confirm.

**15.15**  
*Prakrit:* एयमेव विजाणित्ता, मग्गमेयं समाचरे।  
*Translation:* Having understood exactly this, he should follow this path.  
*Commentary:* The sequence is again stated precisely: understand, then follow. The order matters. The monk who follows the path without understanding it will follow it accurately when conditions are favorable but will fail when conditions are difficult, because there is no deep understanding to support the practice when it becomes hard. The monk who understands first follows naturally and stably, because the following is an expression of understanding rather than an imposition on it.  
*The simple version:* **Understanding is what makes following stable.** Rules that are not understood fail under pressure; understanding holds.

**15.16**  
*Prakrit:* एयं सोच्चा परिण्णाय, एसो मग्गो समाहिया।  
*Translation:* Having heard and understood this, composed in this path.  
*Commentary:* Being "composed in the path" is the goal that understanding and following converge toward. Composed means the path is no longer an external set of rules being navigated carefully — it is the monk's natural ground. The effort of practice has been absorbed into the character of the practitioner. The monk is composed because the path has become constitutive, not regulatory. This is the fulfillment of what hearing and understanding were meant to produce.  
*The simple version:* **Composure in the path is the sign that understanding has been fully absorbed.** You are not trying to follow the path — you are the path.

## Part III — Signs of Progress (Sutras 17–24)

**15.17**  
*Prakrit:* किं लक्खणो पव्वइए, किं लक्खणो सावए।  
*Translation:* What are the signs of the one who has gone forth? What are the signs of the devoted follower?  
*Commentary:* The disciples now ask the practical identification question: how do you recognize someone who is genuinely on the path, as opposed to someone who is performing the appearance of being on the path? The question applies to both monks and lay followers. The teaching's answer will be in terms of conduct — observable behavior, not inner claims. This is important: the tradition does not rely on self-report or social status to identify genuine practitioners.  
*The simple version:* **How do you recognize genuine practice?** The tradition answers in terms of visible conduct, not claimed inner states.

**15.18**  
*Prakrit:* जे पव्वइए सावए, तेसिं एयाइं लक्खणाइं।  
*Translation:* For the one who has gone forth and for the devoted follower — these are the signs.  
*Commentary:* The teaching addresses both categories simultaneously, acknowledging that the signs of genuine practice appear in both monastics and laypeople, though at different levels of intensity and completeness. The signs are not different in kind between the monk and the lay follower — they are different in degree. Both show the marks of non-violence in their behavior; both show restraint; both show the movement toward understanding. The monk's version is more complete; the lay follower's is appropriately partial.  
*The simple version:* **The signs of genuine practice are the same for monks and lay followers, just at different levels of completeness.**

**15.19**  
*Prakrit:* सव्वपाणभूयजीवाणं, अभयं दइज्ज पंडिए।  
*Translation:* The learned one gives fearlessness to all living beings.  
*Commentary:* The most fundamental sign of genuine practice is fearlessness given to others — the specific quality that makes all living beings feel safe in the monk's presence. The animals do not flee; the insects are not swept carelessly aside; the birds land near him. This fearlessness is the visible expression of the non-violence vow: when a being genuinely does not harm, others sense it. This is not magic but simple behavioral reality — beings read the intentions of those around them and respond accordingly.  
*The simple version:* **The sign of genuine non-violence is that living beings feel safe near you.** This is not an achievement but a natural result of genuine practice.

**15.20**  
*Prakrit:* न य अण्णेसु विरायए, न य भोगेसु गिद्धए।  
*Translation:* He is not averse to others, not attached to sensory enjoyments.  
*Commentary:* Two specific signs are named as a pair: absence of aversion and absence of attachment. These are the behavioral fingerprints of right vision — the person who sees correctly neither rejects what they dislike nor clings to what they enjoy. The ordinary person oscillates between these two poles constantly. The genuine practitioner shows a notable steadiness at the center of this oscillation. This steadiness is observable and is the clearest external sign that inner transformation has occurred.  
*The simple version:* **The two behavioral fingerprints of genuine practice are: not reacting against what you dislike, and not clinging to what you enjoy.** Both are visible in how the practitioner moves through daily life.

**15.21**  
*Prakrit:* जीवाजीवं परिण्णाय, कम्मं च परिहरेज्ज से।  
*Translation:* Having understood living and non-living, he avoids karma.  
*Commentary:* The sign of genuine understanding is in the behavior it produces: the monk who has understood the nature of living and non-living beings avoids karma in every aspect of daily life. This avoidance is not anxious rule-following but natural movement — the way a person who understands gravity naturally avoids walking off cliffs. Understanding produces natural avoidance; rule-following produces careful avoidance. The quality of the avoidance reveals whether understanding is present or absent.  
*The simple version:* **Natural avoidance of harm is the sign that understanding is genuine.** Effortful avoidance suggests the understanding is still incomplete.

**15.22**  
*Prakrit:* से लहुइज्जमाणे सव्वत्थ, ण य निज्जरए किंचि।  
*Translation:* He moves lightly everywhere, without losing anything of his conduct.  
*Commentary:* "Moving lightly" is a recurring description of the genuine practitioner — the lightness that comes from not carrying the weight of possessions, attachments, and social obligations. "Without losing anything of his conduct" means the lightness does not come from abandoning the practice — it comes from the practice having been so thoroughly internalized that it no longer requires effort to maintain. Nothing is lost; everything is present; the carrying of it has become effortless.  
*The simple version:* **The genuine practitioner is light and complete at the same time.** Light because there is nothing unnecessary being carried; complete because nothing of the practice has been abandoned.

**15.23**  
*Prakrit:* एयमेव परिण्णाय, भवइ मुक्खस्स कारणं।  
*Translation:* Knowing exactly this becomes the cause of liberation.  
*Commentary:* The direct causal statement: knowing exactly this — the nature of bondage, the path, the signs of progress, the mechanism of karma — becomes the cause of liberation. Not a supporting factor, not a helpful aid, but the cause. This strong claim is characteristic of the Jain teaching on knowledge: genuine knowledge is not just orientation for action, it is itself the operative mechanism of liberation. The knowing and the freeing are not sequential events — they are the same event at full intensity.  
*The simple version:* **Real understanding is not just helpful for liberation — it is the cause of it.** When understanding is complete, liberation follows as a consequence.

**15.24**  
*Prakrit:* एयं सोच्चा मुणी धीरे, मग्गमेयं समाहिया। — iti bemi  
*Translation:* Having heard this, the courageous monk is composed in this path. — Thus I say.  
*Commentary:* The chapter closes with the image of the courageous monk who has heard the teaching from the Knower of the Path, understood it, and is now composed within it. The "thus I say" of Mahavira is the seal — this teaching is authentic, was received from the lineage of liberated ones, and has been transmitted without distortion. The monk who hears it correctly and becomes composed within it is the teaching's intended fruit. One who was confused about the path, bondage, and liberation has been given the complete answer. The path is clear. Thus I say.  
*The simple version:* **The questions have been answered; the path is clear.** The courageous monk who has heard this can now walk forward without confusion. — Thus I say.
