# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 19: Sevala (The Solitary Monk / The Independent Ascetic)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** Second Shrutaskandha (Book 2)
**Chapter:** 19 (Book 2, Chapter 3)
**Prakrit Title:** Sevala
**Approximate Sutras:** 20
**Theme:** The nature and limits of solitary practice. A monk who practices alone outside the recognized community is examined: what makes solitary practice genuine, what makes it dangerous, and what true independence actually means.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> एगो ठाएज्ज, एगो सेएज्ज, एगो जाएज्ज।
> एगत्तं च बहुत्तं च, जाणेज्जा भिक्खू मेहावी॥

*One should stand alone, one should sleep alone, one should wander alone. The wise monk understands both solitude and community.*

---

## Part I — The Solitary Path (Sutras 1–8)

**19.1**
*Prakrit:* एगो ठाएज्ज साहू, एगो सेएज्ज य।
*Translation:* A monk should stand alone; a monk should sleep alone.
*Commentary:* The chapter opens with a principle that sounds like an affirmation of solitary practice. The monk who stands alone and sleeps alone is free of the social entanglements that can compromise practice: the pressure to conform to the group's habits, the temptation to rely on others' assessments, the distraction of communal life. Solitude, the sutra suggests, is a genuine and valuable dimension of the path. There is something essential in facing practice alone — without the social cushion that community provides.
*The simple version:* **Some aspects of spiritual practice must be done alone.** You can't borrow someone else's inner work.

**19.2**
*Prakrit:* एगो य संजमे वट्टइ, एगो य आयावेइ।
*Translation:* One practices restraint alone; one endures hardship alone.
*Commentary:* Restraint and the endurance of hardship are both fundamentally solitary events — even if they take place within a community, they occur in the interior of the individual. No one can be restrained on your behalf. No one can endure your hardship for you. This sutra identifies the irreducibly personal dimension of practice. Community can support, teach, and encourage; but the actual work — the moment-by-moment choice not to be swept away by passion — is always done alone.
*The simple version:* **Practice ultimately happens in you, not around you.** Community supports it; only you can do it.

**19.3**
*Prakrit:* अप्पणा एव संजाए, अप्पणा एव पणाम।
*Translation:* One is born by oneself; one dies by oneself.
*Commentary:* This sutra moves from the practice context to the existential one. Birth and death are solitary events in the Jain view: the soul enters a body alone and leaves it alone, carrying its karma alone. No companion crosses the threshold of birth or death with you. The social forms of life — family, community, sangha — are temporary arrangements in which the soul participates but which the soul ultimately transcends. Understanding this is not cause for despair but for clarity: one's karma is one's own responsibility.
*The simple version:* **You arrive alone and you leave alone.** The relationships that fill the middle are real, but they don't follow you across the threshold.

**19.4**
*Prakrit:* एगो य कम्मं बंधइ, एगो य विप्पमुच्चइ।
*Translation:* One binds karma alone; one is freed alone.
*Commentary:* The logical consequence of birth and death alone: karmic binding and liberation are also solitary processes. No one else's karma applies to you; no one else can work off your karma for you. The priest's ritual on your behalf does not reduce your karmic debt. Another's austerities do not count toward your account. This is the Jain doctrine of individual karmic responsibility, stated with stark simplicity. It is both the most demanding teaching in the tradition and its most empowering: your liberation depends entirely on you.
*The simple version:* **You created your karma alone; you resolve it alone.** No one can do your spiritual work for you.

**19.5**
*Prakrit:* एगाकियाए संतोसं, लहइ विरले विरले।
*Translation:* Contentment in solitude is attained only by the rare few.
*Commentary:* Having established the value and necessity of solitude, the sutra now introduces a qualifying note: genuine contentment in solitude is rare. Most people who attempt solitary practice find it difficult, not because they are weak, but because the solitary mind tends to amplify everything — fears, doubts, self-criticism, fantasies. The monk who can actually rest contentedly in solitude, without fleeing into distraction or social contact, has achieved something genuinely difficult. This rarity is not discouraging but orienting: it tells the practitioner what they are working toward.
*The simple version:* **Being genuinely at peace alone — not restless, not lonely, not afraid — is a real spiritual achievement.** Most people struggle with it.

**19.6**
*Prakrit:* एगत्तं सेविया सव्वं, जाणइ भिक्खू मेहावी।
*Translation:* Having cultivated solitude, the wise monk comes to understand everything.
*Commentary:* Solitude is described here as a practice of cultivation — something that is developed over time rather than simply entered. The monk who has genuinely cultivated the inner capacity for solitude develops a kind of comprehensive understanding. This is because solitude strips away the noise of social interaction and leaves the practitioner face to face with the actual contents of the mind — its patterns, its attachments, its tendencies. In that clarity, understanding deepens across all dimensions.
*The simple version:* **Time genuinely spent alone, without distraction, deepens understanding in unexpected ways.** The noise of social life masks a lot that solitude reveals.

**19.7**
*Prakrit:* समो समंतेण वट्टेज्ज, न वियाणिज्ज भेयजोगं।
*Translation:* He should act with equanimity on all sides; he should not perceive occasions for discrimination.
*Commentary:* Equanimity is described here as a spatial orientation — it extends "on all sides," not just in selected directions. The monk who is equanimous to his friends but partial to his enemies, or kind to the high-born and contemptuous of the low-born, has not yet achieved real equanimity. Equanimity is non-discriminating — not in the sense of lacking discernment, but in the sense of not having the inner pull toward some beings and push away from others that characterizes passion-driven action.
*The simple version:* **Real equanimity doesn't have exceptions.** It extends equally in all directions, not just toward the people you like.

**19.8**
*Prakrit:* एगोहं एगो मेहावी, एगे धम्मे पइट्ठिए।
*Translation:* Alone I stand; alone the wise one; alone, established in the teaching.
*Commentary:* This sutra captures something of the inner voice of genuine solitary practice. The practitioner who has gone deep into solitude discovers a form of aloneness that is not loneliness but completeness: the soul established in its own nature, which is the teaching itself. "Alone in the teaching" is not isolation but integration — the practitioner has found the place where self and path are no longer in tension. This is the highest form of solitude: being the teaching rather than having received it.
*The simple version:* **True solitude isn't lonely — it's complete.** When you're fully established in practice, aloneness becomes sufficiency.

## Part II — Dangers of Isolation (Sutras 9–14)

**19.9**
*Prakrit:* एगत्ते य उम्मग्गो, एगत्ते य पमाओ।
*Translation:* In solitude there is the danger of wrong path; in solitude there is the danger of negligence.
*Commentary:* The chapter pivots here from the value of solitude to its dangers. Solitude has a shadow side: without the corrective influence of community, a practitioner can drift onto a wrong path without anyone to notice. And without the accountability that community provides, negligence can creep in — the gradual relaxation of standards, the quiet abandonment of practices that no one is watching. These are real dangers that the tradition names honestly. Solitude is not automatically virtuous.
*The simple version:* **Practicing alone has real dangers.** You can drift without noticing, and no one is there to tell you when you've slipped.

**19.10**
*Prakrit:* एगत्ते य माणो होइ, एगत्ते य विसाओ।
*Translation:* In solitude there arises pride; in solitude there arises despondency.
*Commentary:* Two psychological dangers of isolation are named. Pride arises because without comparative context — without others who also practice, who have more or less experience, who demonstrate different qualities — the solitary practitioner can lose perspective on where they actually are. Despondency arises for the opposite reason: without community's support and encouragement, the inner landscape can become overwhelming. Both extremes — the inflation of pride and the deflation of despondency — are obstacles to practice.
*The simple version:* **Alone, you can become either arrogant or despairing.** Community provides the reality-check that keeps you in the middle.

**19.11**
*Prakrit:* एगत्ते य अणाराहणा, एगत्ते य विराहणा।
*Translation:* In solitude there is failure of right practice; in solitude there is transgression of the path.
*Commentary:* "Anardhana" — failure to please, failure to honor, failure of right practice — names what happens when a practitioner loses their orientation without the touchstone of community. "Viraddhana" — transgression, going against — describes the more active failure: not just drifting but actually walking the wrong path. Both can happen in isolation without the practitioner being aware of it. The teaching tradition exists in community partly because community is a self-correcting system that individuals alone cannot be.
*The simple version:* **Without community, practice can quietly fall apart — and you might not notice until it's already happened.**

**19.12**
*Prakrit:* अपत्तेयं पि य होइ, एगत्ते एगविहरणे।
*Translation:* Going alone, wandering alone — one can fall into untrustworthiness.
*Commentary:* Untrustworthiness — the quality of being someone whose practice cannot be relied upon — is identified as a risk of solitary wandering. A monk who wanders alone is not accountable to anyone except himself. His vows are witnessed by no one. This lack of external accountability does not corrupt everyone who practices alone, but it removes one of the structures that supports consistent practice. The tradition's concern here is not with control but with the practical conditions that make sustained practice more or less likely.
*The simple version:* **When no one is watching, it's easier to let things slip.** Accountability is a practical support for practice, not just a social norm.

**19.13**
*Prakrit:* न एगत्तं पसंसंति, सव्वे जाणंति मेहावी।
*Translation:* The wise ones do not universally praise solitude — all the knowledgeable ones understand this.
*Commentary:* This sutra represents the tradition's verdict on solitary practice as the sole path: it is not universally praised. The wise ones — those with deep experience of practice — do not promote solitary wandering as the ideal for everyone or as a replacement for community. They understand its value in the right context and its dangers in the wrong one. The teaching is not against solitude but against the romanticization of solitude as automatically superior to community life.
*The simple version:* **The wisest teachers don't tell everyone to go practice alone.** They know both the value and the danger of it.

**19.14**
*Prakrit:* एगत्तं च बहुत्तं च, उभयं जाणइ संजमी।
*Translation:* Both solitude and community — the restrained one knows both.
*Commentary:* The chapter's central wisdom is expressed here: genuine understanding encompasses both solitude and community, knowing the value of each and the dangers of each. The restrained one — the monk who has genuinely worked through the practice — is not committed to either form as an end in itself. They can inhabit solitude without drifting, and community without being absorbed by it. They have the inner freedom to work appropriately in whatever context they find themselves.
*The simple version:* **The truly practiced monk can do both — live alone and live with others — without losing the thread of practice in either setting.**

## Part III — True Independence (Sutras 15–20)

**19.15**
*Prakrit:* न संगे न पगासे य, न य अन्नेसु आसत्तो।
*Translation:* Not attached to crowds, not attached to display, not attached to others.
*Commentary:* True independence is defined here by what it is not: not attachment to crowds (the desire for social belonging and collective validation), not attachment to display (the desire to be seen and admired in practice), not attachment to others in the dependency sense. This is a subtle and demanding standard. Many practitioners escape physical aloneness only to create a more sophisticated dependence — on their teacher's approval, on the community's opinion, on the recognition of others. True independence is freedom from all of these.
*The simple version:* **Real independence isn't about being alone — it's about not needing the crowd's approval.** That's a much harder thing.

**19.16**
*Prakrit:* अप्पणा एव अप्पाणं, झाएज्ज अणिस्सिओ।
*Translation:* The monk should contemplate the self by the self — unsupported.
*Commentary:* Meditation on the self — the soul in its pure nature — is here described as an act of the self by the self, without external support. This describes the deepest form of solitary practice: the practitioner and the object of practice have become the same. The soul turns its knowing awareness back on itself and rests in that self-recognition. "Unsupported" does not mean without a path or a tradition; it means that at the moment of this turning, the practitioner stands alone in the full sense.
*The simple version:* **In the deepest practice, you turn awareness back on itself — and you do that alone.** No teacher can do that for you.

**19.17**
*Prakrit:* से य एगो जगे सव्वे, जीवो पुव्वकयाणि।
*Translation:* He is alone in all the world — the soul bearing its previously made karma.
*Commentary:* The existential aloneness of the soul is restated from the perspective of karma. In all the world, with all its apparent connections and relationships, the soul is alone with its karma. The karma accumulated across countless lives is the soul's own burden — not shared, not reducible by another's effort, not transferable. This aloneness is not a melancholy fact but a liberating one: it means that liberation is fully within the practitioner's power to achieve, without requiring anyone else's cooperation.
*The simple version:* **You and your karma are alone together, in a sense.** No one else carries your burden — but that also means no one else can stop you from being free.

**19.18**
*Prakrit:* न य से कोई साहाई, न य से कोई बंधवो।
*Translation:* There is no helper for him; there is no relative for him.
*Commentary:* At the level of karma and liberation, there are no helpers and no relatives. The social bonds that feel so real and so defining — family relationships, community ties, friendships — are karmic relationships of the moment, not features of the soul's fundamental nature. This sutra is not asking the practitioner to deny the value of these relationships in daily life but to understand their ultimate limit. At the boundary of life and death, at the boundary of karma and liberation, the soul stands alone.
*The simple version:* **At the deepest level, you're on your own.** That's not depressing — it's clarifying.

**19.19**
*Prakrit:* तम्हा एगत्तविहारी, जाणिज्जा एगत्तमप्पणो।
*Translation:* Therefore one who dwells in solitude should understand the aloneness of the self.
*Commentary:* The solitary practitioner's task is precisely this: to understand the nature of the soul's aloneness — not to flee it in social distraction, not to inflate it into pride, not to collapse into it in despair, but to understand it. This understanding is itself liberation — or the beginning of liberation. The monk who understands the soul's aloneness can be alone without loneliness, with others without losing themselves, and at the moment of death without fear.
*The simple version:* **Solitary practice is an opportunity to understand the deepest fact about existence: the soul stands alone.** That understanding is the path's destination.

**19.20**
*Prakrit:* एगो ठाएज्ज, एगो सेएज्ज, एगो जाएज्ज। एगत्तं च बहुत्तं च, जाणेज्जा भिक्खू मेहावी॥ — iti bemi.
*Translation:* One should stand alone, one should sleep alone, one should wander alone. The wise monk understands both solitude and community. — Thus I say.
*Commentary:* The chapter's featured verse serves also as its closing sutra, sealed with the authoritative "iti bemi." The verse holds both truths in balance: the necessity of genuine solitude — standing, sleeping, wandering alone — and the necessity of understanding both solitude and community. The truly wise monk is not a committed solitary or a committed communitarian; he is someone who understands both, can inhabit either, and is bound to neither. This is the freedom the chapter has been pointing toward throughout. The final word is both instruction and affirmation: this is what the teaching says, and this is what it asks.
*The simple version:* **The wise monk can stand alone and can be in community — and is not trapped by either.** That balance is the teaching. Thus Mahavira says.
