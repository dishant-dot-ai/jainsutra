# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 1: Samaya (The Doctrine)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Shri Suyagadang Sutra (First Shrutaskandha), Gujarati commentary
**Chapter:** First Adhyayana (Samaya — समय)
**Total Gathas:** 88
**Overview:** The opening chapter of the Sutrakritanga establishes the foundational teaching — that liberation depends on right doctrine and right conduct. Mahavira systematically refutes twelve wrong philosophical positions (materialism, monism, body-soul identity, karma denial, soul denial, fatalism, agnosticism, Buddhist non-self, creationism, and more) and concludes with the true path: non-violence, equanimity, and the conquest of passions. The chapter closes with Mahavira's signature phrase: "Thus I say."

---

## Uddesaka 1 — Causes of Bondage and Wrong Views (Gathas 1–27)

---

### SUTRA 1.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

बुद्धिं जियाइं, बन्धणं परिजाणिया ।
किमाहु बन्धणं वीरो? एगं वा जाणइ तिउट्टिं ॥१.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

buddhiṃ jiyāiṃ, bandhaṇaṃ parijāṇiyā |
kimāhu bandhaṇaṃ vīro? egaṃ vā jāṇai tiuṭṭiṃ || 1.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Let the wise one attain understanding and know the nature of bondage fully — what does the Hero call bondage, and what single truth, when known, breaks bondage completely?

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*buddhiṃ* = understanding, enlightened intelligence | *jiyāiṃ* = having obtained, having won | *bandhaṇaṃ* = bondage, karmic entanglement | *parijāṇiyā* = should know completely, should understand thoroughly | *kimāhu* = what does he call, what does he say? | *vīro* = the Hero (Mahavira) | *egaṃ vā* = what single thing? | *jāṇai* = by knowing | *tiuṭṭiṃ* = breaks bondage, is freed from it

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra frames the entire chapter as a question: what must a wise person understand to become free? The questioner is Jambu Swami; the teacher is Sudharma Swami transmitting Mahavira's words. The word *buddhi* here does not mean ordinary intelligence — it refers to enlightened self-understanding, the awakening to one's own nature as a soul distinct from matter. Bondage (*bandhaṇa*) is not physical restraint but the karmic entanglement that keeps the soul cycling through births. The Hero (*vīro*) — a title for Mahavira — is asked to define it. The structure of the question is itself the answer's shape: there is one truth, one understanding, that when fully grasped, breaks all bondage. That truth is the subject of the entire chapter that follows.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The chapter opens with a question: what does the enlightened teacher say bondage is — and what single insight, when truly understood, sets you free?

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you believe is the one thing standing between you and complete freedom — and have you truly examined it?

### SUTRA 1.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

चित्तमंतमचित्तं वा, परिगिह्ह किसामिव ।
अण्णं वा अणुजाणाइ, एवं दुक्खा न मुच्च‌इ ॥१.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

cittamantamacittaṃ vā, parigihha kisāmiva |
aṇṇaṃ vā aṇujāṇāi, evaṃ dukhā na muccai || 1.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who possesses conscious beings or unconscious things — even the least trifle — or who approves of others possessing them, is not freed from suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*cittamantaṃ* = animate, conscious beings (humans, animals) | *acittaṃ vā* = or inanimate things (gold, silver, goods) | *parigihha* = possessing, holding as one's own | *kisāmiva* = even the smallest, even a trifle, even a blade of grass | *aṇṇaṃ vā* = or of others | *aṇujāṇāi* = approves, gives consent to their possessing | *evaṃ* = in this way | *dukhā* = from suffering | *na muccai* = is not freed

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra defines the first cause of bondage: possession (*parigraha*). The definition is remarkably comprehensive — it includes both living possessions (servants, animals) and non-living ones (gold, land, goods), and even the mental act of consenting to another's possession. The phrase "even a trifle" (*kisāmiva*) closes every loophole: there is no threshold below which possession is harmless. The Jain understanding of *parigraha* is not merely physical accumulation but the internal attitude of attachment — the feeling that something belongs to "me." This sutra establishes that as long as that attitude exists, liberation is impossible, because the attachment generates fresh karma moment by moment.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Clinging to anything — people, objects, wealth — or even silently approving of others clinging keeps you trapped in suffering, no matter how small the thing you're clinging to.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What in your life do you grip so tightly that losing it would feel like losing yourself?

### SUTRA 1.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सयं तिवायए पाणे, अदुवा अण्णेहिं घायए ।
हणंतं वाऽणुजाणाइ, वेरं वड्ढेइ अप्पणो ॥१.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sayaṃ tivāyae pāṇe, aduvā aṇṇehiṃ ghāyae |
haṇantaṃ vā'ṇujāṇāi, veraṃ vaḍḍhei appaṇo || 1.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who himself kills living beings, or causes others to kill them, or approves of the killing, increases enmity toward himself.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sayaṃ* = himself, by himself | *tivāyae* = kills, destroys | *pāṇe* = living beings, life | *aduvā* = or | *aṇṇehiṃ* = through others, by others | *ghāyae* = causes to be killed, has killed | *haṇantaṃ* = one who is killing | *vā aṇujāṇāi* = or approves of | *veraṃ* = enmity, hostility | *vaḍḍhei* = increases, multiplies | *appaṇo* = toward himself, of his own

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra identifies violence as the second root cause of bondage, and gives it the same triple structure used for possession: doing it oneself, having others do it, or approving of it. The word *veram* (enmity) is philosophically precise — violence is not simply a moral wrong but a self-multiplying force. Every act of violence creates karmic enmity that rebounds on the perpetrator across future lives. The word *appaṇo* (toward himself) makes the injury reflexive: one who destroys others destroys their own prospect of liberation. This is not a punishment imposed from outside — it is the natural mechanics of karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Whether you kill directly, hire someone else to kill, or simply agree with killing — you are increasing the weight of harm that comes back to you.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in daily life do you approve of harm you would never directly commit — and what does that silent approval cost you?

### SUTRA 1.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जस्स कुलसमुप्पण्णे, जेहिं वा संवसे नरे ।
ममाइ लुप्पइ बाले, अण्णमण्णेहिं मुच्छिए ॥१.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jassa kulasamuppaṇṇe, jehiṃ vā saṃvase nare |
mamāi luppai bāle, aṇṇamaṇṇehiṃ mucchhie || 1.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ignorant one is afflicted by attachment to those in whose family he is born and among whom he lives — deeply ensnared in mutual craving.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jassa* = in whose | *kula* = family, clan | *samuppaṇṇe* = born into | *jehiṃ vā* = or with whom | *saṃvase* = one lives, dwells | *nare* = a person | *mamāi* = with the thought of "mine," with possessive attachment | *luppai* = is afflicted, is tormented | *bāle* = the ignorant one, the unwise | *aṇṇamaṇṇehiṃ* = mutually, in each other | *mucchhie* = ensnared, infatuated, deluded

**5. COMMENTARY**

Having established possession and violence as root causes of bondage, the sutra now examines the psychological mechanism underneath both: the sense of "mine" (*mamāi*). The family one is born into and the community one inhabits become the primary field of this possessive attachment. The word *bāle* (the ignorant one) is not a moral judgment but a technical term — it refers to the soul that has not yet awakened to its own independent nature and therefore projects its identity onto external relationships. The mutual nature of this entanglement (*aṇṇamaṇṇehiṃ*) is significant: everyone in a family network is simultaneously clinging to and being clung to by others. This mutual craving sustains cycles of rebirth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The unawakened person suffers because they are deeply attached to family and community — everyone clinging to each other in a web that keeps them all bound.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is the love you feel for your family a source of genuine giving, or does it sometimes become a form of clinging that causes suffering?

### SUTRA 1.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वित्तं सोयरिया चेव, सव्वमेयं न ताणए ।
संखाए जीवियं चेव, कम्मुणा उ तिउट्टइ ॥१.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vittaṃ soyariyā ceva, savvameyaṃ na tāṇae |
saṃkhāe jīviyaṃ ceva, kammuṇā u tiuṭṭai || 1.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Wealth and kinsmen — all of this — do not protect one; knowing life to be brief, the wise one breaks free from karma.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vittaṃ* = wealth, riches | *soyariyā* = kinsmen, brothers and sisters | *ceva* = indeed, also | *savvameyaṃ* = all of this | *na tāṇae* = does not protect, is no refuge | *saṃkhāe* = knowing, understanding fully | *jīviyaṃ* = life | *ceva* = also | *kammuṇā* = from karma, from karmic bondage | *u* = indeed | *tiuṭṭai* = breaks free, separates

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra makes the argument for renunciation in compressed form: nothing external offers real protection. Wealth cannot buy off death or karma. Kinsmen cannot carry your karmic burden. The sutra frames this not as pessimism but as clear-sighted realism — and crucially, it leads to action. The one who *knows* (*saṃkhāe*) the brevity of life and the unreliability of external refuge does not collapse into despair; they break free from karma. The verb *tiuṭṭai* (breaks, separates) is the same used in 1.1 — the chapter is building a vocabulary of liberation that requires understanding bondage before one can leave it.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

No amount of money or family relationships can protect you from death or karma — and knowing this is what motivates a person to actually do the work of becoming free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you truly believed that nothing external could protect you, what would you do differently today?

### SUTRA 1.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे गंथे विउक्कम्म, एगे समण-माहणा ।
यायंता विउस्सिट्ठा, सत्ता कामेहिं माणवा ॥१.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege ganthe viukkamma, ege samaṇa-māhaṇā |
yāyaṃtā viussitthā, sattā kāmehiṃ māṇavā || 1.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some ascetics and priests, having abandoned one set of entanglements, are still deeply addicted to pleasures — attached and deluded in their own doctrines.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege* = some | *ganthe* = entanglements, knots of attachment | *viukkamma* = having abandoned, having crossed over | *ege* = some | *samaṇa-māhaṇā* = ascetics (śramaṇas) and priests (brahmins) | *yāyaṃtā* = going, proceeding | *viussitthā* = tightly bound, deeply attached | *sattā* = beings | *kāmehiṃ* = in sense pleasures | *māṇavā* = human beings, men

**5. COMMENTARY**

With this sutra begins the critique of rival paths. Even those who have formally renounced — the *śramaṇas* and *māhaṇas* (ascetics and brahmins) of other traditions — may abandon certain external entanglements while remaining deeply bound by inner ones. The word *ganthe* (knots) is important: these are not mere habits but structural binding points in the soul's relationship with matter. To cut one knot while leaving others intact is not liberation. This sutra introduces the theme that dominates the rest of the chapter: the difference between genuine inner freedom and the performance of renunciation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Some religious people give up certain things on the outside but remain deeply attached to pleasure and to their own doctrines on the inside — and that inner attachment is still bondage.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever given up something visible while holding more tightly to something invisible?

### SUTRA 1.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुढवी आउ तेऊ वाऊ, वणस्सइ तसा य जे ।
ते जीवा ते य अजीवा, इइ केइ वियाणइ ॥१.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

puḍhavī āu teū vāū, vaṇassai tasā ya je |
te jīvā te ya ajīvā, iai kei viyāṇai || 1.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and mobile beings — some say these are both souls and non-souls.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*puḍhavī* = earth | *āu* = water | *teū* = fire | *vāū* = air | *vaṇassai* = vegetation, plant life | *tasā ya je* = and those that are mobile (animals, humans) | *te jīvā* = these are souls | *te ya ajīvā* = and also non-souls | *iai* = thus | *kei* = some (philosophers) | *viyāṇai* = teach, hold, understand

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra begins the systematic refutation of wrong philosophical views. The view being examined here identifies the five material elements plus mobile beings as both souls and non-souls — a materialist-idealist hybrid that confuses the categories of self and matter. From the Jain perspective, this error is philosophically disastrous: if you cannot distinguish soul from matter, you cannot know what bondage is, and therefore cannot know what liberation requires. The chapter will spend many sutras unpacking why each wrong view is wrong — not as academic exercise but because wrong understanding of the soul's nature directly causes continued bondage.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Some philosophers teach that the elements of nature are both souls and non-souls — a confused view that makes it impossible to understand what needs to be freed and from what.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you have a clear sense of what you are beyond your body — and does that clarity change how you live?

### SUTRA 1.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुढवी आउ तेऊ वाऊ, वणस्सइ तसा य जे ।
एते जीवा ममाहंसु, तं सव्वं नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

puḍhavī āu teū vāū, vaṇassai tasā ya je |
ete jīvā mamāhaṃsu, taṃ savvaṃ nāyaga-bbhuṇo || 1.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and mobile beings — these are all souls, say some; but this everything-is-soul view is not recognized by the knower of the path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*puḍhavī āu teū vāū* = earth, water, fire, air | *vaṇassai* = vegetation | *tasā ya je* = and mobile beings | *ete jīvā* = these are all souls | *mamāhaṃsu* = thus they say, so they declare | *taṃ savvaṃ* = all of that | *nāyaga-bbhuṇo* = is not recognized by / does not lead to the knower (the one who knows the path to liberation)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This refutes the extreme monist view — that everything is soul (*jīva*). This was a position held by some Vedantic and Upanishadic thinkers. From the Jain standpoint, while it is true that conscious souls exist in all six categories (earth bodies, water bodies, fire bodies, air bodies, plants, mobile beings), those souls are not identical to the material substances they inhabit. Collapsing the distinction between soul and matter makes karma unintelligible — if everything is already soul, what is it that karma binds? The refutation is not that these beings lack souls, but that identifying *the soul* with *the material element* is the error.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The idea that everything is just one universal soul sounds spiritual, but it actually makes it impossible to understand karma or liberation — because it erases the distinction between the soul and what traps it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is it possible to feel connected to all things without losing the clarity that you — as a soul — have a specific path to walk?

### SUTRA 1.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अत्थि पाणा विसेसेण, नाणत्तं च विजाणइ ।
जीवो देहो त्ति एगत्तं, तं पि नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

atthi pāṇā visesseṇa, nāṇattaṃ ca vijāṇai |
jīvo deho tti egattaṃ, taṃ pi nāyaga-bbhuṇo || 1.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Living beings exist in their specific forms, and one should know their diversity — but the view that the soul and the body are one thing is also not recognized by the knower of the path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*atthi pāṇā* = living beings exist | *visesseṇa* = specifically, distinctly | *nāṇattaṃ* = diversity, multiplicity | *ca vijāṇai* = one should know | *jīvo* = the soul | *deho* = the body | *tti* = thus, this view | *egattaṃ* = oneness, identity | *taṃ pi* = that also | *nāyaga-bbhuṇo* = is not recognized by the knower of the path

**5. COMMENTARY**

This refutes the body-soul identity view (*tajjīva-taccharīra-vāda*) — the materialist teaching that the self is nothing more than the living body. This was held by the Cārvāka materialists and some other schools. The sutra makes two points simultaneously: yes, living beings exist in diverse specific forms (affirming that souls are real and plural), and no, the soul is not simply the body. The body is material and perishable; the soul is immaterial and does not perish with the body. Confusing them means failing to understand what continues after death, what karma attaches to, and therefore what liberation means.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The idea that you are just your body — that there is nothing more to you than flesh and blood — is a mistake that makes liberation literally unthinkable.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you were not your body, what would be left — and what would that mean for how you treat yourself and others?

### SUTRA 1.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे अकिरियं वयंति भो, जीव-अजीव-वागरे ।
ते नासिया दुविहे ठाणे, उभये दुक्खं पावंति ॥१.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je akiriyaṃ vayaṃti bho, jīva-ajīva-vāgare |
te nāsiyā duvīhe ṭhāṇe, ubhaye dukkhaṃ pāvaṃti || 1.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who teach non-action — who say there is no moral consequence to action — regarding soul and non-soul: they are lost in both realms and experience suffering in both.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je* = those who | *akiriyaṃ* = non-action, the view that actions have no karma-generating effect | *vayaṃti* = say, teach | *bho* = indeed | *jīva-ajīva-vāgare* = with respect to soul and non-soul | *te* = they | *nāsiyā* = are lost, ruined | *duvīhe ṭhāṇe* = in both places (this world and the next) | *ubhaye* = in both | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering | *pāvaṃti* = they experience, they obtain

**5. COMMENTARY**

This refutes the *akriyāvāda* — the doctrine that actions have no moral consequence, that karma does not accumulate, that there is nothing that binds or frees. This teaching, associated with certain schools of Mahavira's time, destroyed the entire foundation of ethical conduct. If actions have no fruits, there is no reason to practice restraint, no reason for compassion, no basis for liberation. The consequence described here is direct: such a person is lost in both this life and the next. They suffer without understanding why, and without any path out, because their philosophy has eliminated the very concept of a path.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who teach that your actions have no consequences end up suffering in both this life and the next — because once you believe nothing matters, you have no map for getting free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever noticed that believing "it doesn't matter what I do" actually leads to more suffering, not less?

### SUTRA 1.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे य आया न मुयंति, जे य मुत्ता न पुणरागच्छंति ।
एयं मग्गं विजाणित्ता, एयं धम्मं तु नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je ya āyā na muyaṃti, je ya muttā na puṇarāgacchaṃti |
eyaṃ maggaṃ vijāṇittā, eyaṃ dhammaṃ tu nāyaga-bbhuṇo || 1.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some souls do not become freed; those who are freed do not return — knowing this path, knowing this doctrine, is what the knower of the path recognizes.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je ya āyā* = those souls who | *na muyaṃti* = are not freed (due to wrong conduct) | *je ya muttā* = those who are freed | *na puṇarāgacchaṃti* = do not return again (to the cycle) | *eyaṃ maggaṃ* = this path | *vijāṇittā* = having known, understanding | *eyaṃ dhammaṃ* = this doctrine | *tu* = indeed | *nāyaga-bbhuṇo* = is recognized by / belongs to the knower of the path

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra introduces the positive counterpart to the refutations: the true doctrine holds that liberation is real, possible, and irreversible. Some souls — those following wrong doctrine — remain bound and do not attain freedom. Those who do attain freedom never return to the cycle of births. This irreversibility (*na puṇarāgacchaṃti*) is a defining feature of Jain liberation — *moksha* is permanent. The sutra creates a sharp contrast with the Buddhist view (which will be addressed later) and with the fatalist view (to come): liberation requires right knowledge and right effort, and once attained, it is complete.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Some souls stay trapped, and those who do get free never come back — and understanding this clearly is at the heart of true doctrine.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean to you if freedom were truly permanent — not something that comes and goes?

### SUTRA 1.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे नत्थि परो लोगो, न किंचि अकरोहि ते ।
अप्पाणं पि य नासंति, मिच्छद्दिट्ठी नरा भवे ॥१.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege natthi paro logo, na kiṃci akarohi te |
appāṇaṃ pi ya nāsaṃti, micchadditthī narā bhave || 1.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some say there is no other world, "do nothing" — they destroy even themselves, for wrong-viewing persons exist in delusion.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege* = some | *natthi* = there is not | *paro logo* = another world, life after death | *na kiṃci* = nothing | *akarohi* = do | *te* = they | *appāṇaṃ pi* = even themselves | *ya nāsaṃti* = they destroy | *micchadditthī* = wrong-viewing, those with false views | *narā* = persons | *bhave* = exist, are

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the soul-denial position: there is no afterlife, no self that continues, therefore no consequences to one's actions. This *nāstika* (negating) view is directly linked to moral nihilism — if there is nothing after death, why restrain oneself at all? The consequence stated here is the strongest yet: they destroy even themselves. This is not merely a statement about future karmic consequence — it is a statement about the present. A person who lives without recognizing their own soul does not merely fail to liberate it; they actively harm it through every unguarded act of passion and violence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who say there's no such thing as an afterlife or a continuing self — and therefore nothing matters — end up destroying themselves, because that belief removes every reason to live with care.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What does the way you live day to day say about what you actually believe awaits you after this life?

### SUTRA 1.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण कत्थ नत्थि आयाओ, एवं तिउट्टई तसा ।
ण होइ केइ नत्थित्तं, एवं जाणाहि पंडिए ॥१.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa kattha natthi āyāo, evaṃ tiuṭṭaī tasā |
ṇa hoi kei natthittaṃ, evaṃ jāṇāhi paṃḍie || 1.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

There is nowhere no soul — thus mobile beings are freed; there is no such thing as non-existence — thus know, O wise one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa kattha natthi* = there is nowhere no (i.e., everywhere there is) | *āyāo* = soul | *evaṃ* = thus | *tiuṭṭaī* = breaks free | *tasā* = mobile beings (with two or more senses) | *ṇa hoi* = there is no | *kei natthittaṃ* = any non-existence of anything | *evaṃ* = thus | *jāṇāhi* = know | *paṃḍie* = O wise one, O learned one

**5. COMMENTARY**

Having refuted soul denial, the text now affirms the positive: the soul exists everywhere — in every mobile being. This is not pantheism (the view that everything *is* soul) but the Jain view that souls are present throughout nature in different degrees of development. The second line makes an even broader claim: there is no such thing as absolute non-existence. This is the Jain doctrine of *sat* — existence is real and continuous; nothing simply vanishes. This metaphysical point is not abstract — it directly implies that your actions, your karma, and your soul are all real and persistent, which is why the path of liberation is both necessary and possible.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Souls exist everywhere, and nothing truly ceases to exist — so know this, and understand why your choices have real and lasting effects.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does believing in the soul's reality make you feel more responsible — or more free?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who does not harm any living being — knowing, restrained, composed — shaking off karma, free of delusion, with no remaining karma, is known as a Conqueror.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇo hiṃsae* = does not harm, does not kill | *kiṃcaṇa pāṇe* = any living being | *jāṇaṃto* = knowing (with right knowledge) | *virae* = restrained | *samāhie* = composed, meditative | *dhuṇṇiya-kamme* = having shaken off karma, karma destroyed | *vigaya-mohe* = free of delusion | *aviṭṭhakamme* = with no remaining/new karma | *jiṇe* = Conqueror (Jina) | *viyāṇiyā* = is known as, is recognized as

**5. COMMENTARY**

After the long arc of refutations, this sutra presents the positive portrait: what does a truly liberated person look like? The description has five elements: non-violence in conduct, right knowledge, restraint, composure (equanimity), freedom from delusion, and the complete exhaustion of karma. The title *Jiṇe* (Conqueror) is the source of the word "Jain" — the tradition of those who follow the Conquerors. What is conquered? Not the world, not enemies, but the inner forces of passion, delusion, and karma. This sutra is the positive culmination of Uddesaka 1.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A Conqueror — the ideal of the Jain path — is someone who harms nothing, knows truly, lives with restraint and peace, and has burned off all their karma through right living.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of these qualities — non-violence, knowledge, restraint, composure, freedom from delusion — which do you most need to cultivate right now?

## Uddesaka 2 — Refutation of Fatalism, Agnosticism, and Views on Suffering (Gathas 28–59)

### SUTRA 1.28

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

नियइवाइं च ते तेसिं, जे नियइं वयंति भो ।
पुरिसक्कारेण णत्थि किंचि, तेसिं णायरियं ण लद्धं ॥१.२८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

niyaivāiṃ ca te tesīṃ, je niyaiṃ vayaṃti bho |
purisakkkāreṇa ṇatthi kiṃci, tesīṃ ṇāyariyaṃ ṇa laddhaṃ || 1.28 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those fatalists — those who teach fate (*niyati*) — say that through personal effort nothing can be achieved; they have not attained the teaching of the Knower.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*niyaivāiṃ* = fatalists, believers in fate | *te tesīṃ* = those, among them | *je* = who | *niyaiṃ* = fate, destiny | *vayaṃti* = say, teach | *bho* = indeed | *purisakkkāreṇa* = through personal effort, through human striving | *ṇatthi kiṃci* = nothing exists, nothing can be done | *tesīṃ* = for them | *ṇāyariyaṃ* = the teaching of the Knower (Mahavira) | *ṇa laddhaṃ* = not attained

**5. COMMENTARY**

Uddesaka 2 opens with the refutation of *niyativāda* — fatalism, the doctrine that all events are absolutely predetermined by fate and that human effort is therefore powerless. This was the teaching of the Ājīvika school, whose most famous teacher was Makkhali Gosāla, a contemporary of Mahavira's. The Jain rejection of fatalism is total: if nothing can be achieved through personal effort, the entire path of liberation is meaningless. The concept of *puruṣakāra* (personal effort, human agency) is central to Jain ethics — one's choices genuinely matter and genuinely determine one's trajectory.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who believe that everything is fated — that your effort makes no difference — have not understood the teaching. If fate decides everything, then there's no point in trying to be good or to become free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your life do you use the idea of fate to avoid taking responsibility for change?

### SUTRA 1.29

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे उ सुक्कस्स कम्मस्स फलं, अणुभवंति अप्पणो ॥१.२९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je u sukkassa kammassa phalaṃ, aṇubhavaṃti appaṇo || 1.29 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Fatalists are indeed unwise, entered into delusion and karma — but those who experience the fruit of pure karma do so through their own effort.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*niyaivāiṇo* = fatalists | *khalu* = indeed | *te bālā* = they are ignorant | *mohena* = through delusion | *ya kammena* = and through karma | *paviṭṭhā* = entered into, immersed in | *je u* = those indeed who | *sukkassa* = pure, bright | *kammassa* = of karma | *phalaṃ* = fruit, result | *aṇubhavaṃti* = experience | *appaṇo* = through their own effort, by themselves

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra makes explicit the counter-position to fatalism: the fruit of positive karma (*sukka-kamma*) is experienced by those who generated it through their own effort. The Jain position is not that fate plays no role — past karma does shape one's circumstances — but that present effort genuinely matters and can transform those circumstances. The fatalist, immersed in delusion, fails to see this. What is particularly pointed here is that the fatalists are said to be *immersed in karma* — they are not free of karma's effects even while denying karma's significance. Fatalists are fooled by their own confusion — the good results that come from living well come from your own sincere effort, not from fate.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Let him not be intoxicated, let him not harm, let him not speak falsehood — let him not injure living beings, let him not perform any harmful karma whatsoever.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa majjejjā* = should not be intoxicated (through food, drink, or excitement) | *ṇa hiṃsejjā* = should not harm | *ṇa musaṃ* = not falsehood | *vattumarihāi* = is worthy to speak | *ṇa vihiṃse* = should not injure | *pāṇiṇo* = living beings | *ṇa kujjā* = should not do | *pāvakamma* = harmful karma | *kiṃcaṇa* = any, whatsoever

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is a compressed code of monastic ethics: no intoxication (which clouds the clarity needed for right conduct), no harm, no falsehood, no injury to living beings, and absolutely no harmful karma of any kind. The four prohibitions correspond to the first four of the five great restraints (*mahāvratas*): non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing (implied in the complete harmlessness), and the general prohibition of harmful karma. The phrase *kiṃcaṇa* — "any, whatsoever" — closes every loophole. This is not a graduated code with exceptions; it is an absolute standard.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk's code in one verse: no getting intoxicated, no harming, no lying, no injuring anyone, no harmful action at all — none.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of these four — intoxication, harm, falsehood, injury — where do you most consistently fail, even in subtle ways?

### SUTRA 1.63

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सुत्ते आहाकम्मकए, जे य गाहावई-कुले ।
सो भिक्खू अणुवाहार-मेव, विरिंचिया संजए ॥१.६३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sutte āhākamma-kae, je ya gāhāvaī-kule |
so bhikkhū aṇuvāhārameva, viriṃciyā saṃjae || 1.63 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

⚠️ As stated in the teaching regarding food prepared for guests, in the householder's family — that monk, avoiding even such incidental food, is truly restrained.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sutte* = in the teaching, as declared | *āhākamma-kae* = regarding food-karma, food prepared (specifically for guests or monks) | *je ya* = and which | *gāhāvaī-kule* = in the householder's family | *so bhikkhū* = that monk | *aṇuvāhārameva* = avoiding even incidental food | *viriṃciyā* = having given up | *saṃjae* = is restrained

**5. COMMENTARY**

⚠️ This sutra addresses a subtle but important category: food specially prepared for a visiting monk in a householder's home. In the Jain monastic code, a monk is not to accept food that was prepared specifically for him, because such preparation necessarily involves some activity and harm specifically directed by or on behalf of the monk. Even this incidental harm (*aṇuvāhāra*) — less obvious than direct violence — is to be avoided by the truly restrained monk. The sutra establishes a high standard: even socially acceptable offering of special food must be declined if it generates karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

⚠️ Even food specially prepared for a visiting monk by a generous family is to be avoided — the truly disciplined monk won't accept food that was made specifically for him.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there harm you accept — even welcome — because it's wrapped in generosity and good intentions?

### SUTRA 1.64

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

किच्चावाइणं च लोगंसि, जे कत्तारं वयंति भो ।
नाणाहि कत्तारं लोगे, कहं सो मुच्चइ बंधणाओ ॥१.६४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kiccāvāiṇaṃ ca logaṃsi, je kattāraṃ vayaṃti bho |
ṇāṇāhi kattāraṃ loge, kahaṃ so muccai baṃdhaṇāo || 1.64 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those in the world who teach that everything is done by a Creator — by knowing (their view of) the Creator of the world, how can such a one be freed from bondage?

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kiccāvāiṇaṃ* = those who teach that everything is done by a Creator, Kriyāvādins | *ca logaṃsi* = and in the world | *je kattāraṃ* = who a Creator | *vayaṃti* = say, teach | *bho* = indeed | *ṇāṇāhi* = knowing, by this knowledge | *kattāraṃ* = a Creator | *loge* = in the world | *kahaṃ* = how | *so* = one | *muccai* = is freed | *baṃdhaṇāo* = from bondage

**5. COMMENTARY**

This begins the refutation of *Kriyāvāda* — the doctrine that a divine Creator (*kartā*) is the cause of all worldly events. This was associated with certain theistic traditions of Mahavira's time. The Jain critique is precise: if a Creator made everything, then the soul's bondage is the Creator's doing, and its liberation would also require the Creator's action. This eliminates personal effort (*puruṣakāra*) and personal responsibility from the equation. A person who believes liberation depends on a divine actor rather than on their own karma and conduct cannot take the steps required for liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

If you believe a Creator made everything and controls everything — including your bondage — then it's not clear how you are supposed to do the work of freeing yourself.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much of your spiritual life is based on hoping someone or something external will save you — versus doing the inner work yourself?

### SUTRA 1.65

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

लोग-मूलए लोगं पस्सह, को ईसरो को य किंचि करेज्ज ।
देव-सिद्धिं कप्पिउं, अप्पाणं चेव साहए ॥१.६५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

loga-mūlae logaṃ passaha, ko īsaro ko ya kiṃci karejja |
deva-siddhiṃ kappiu ṃ, appāṇaṃ ceva sāhae || 1.65 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

See the world from the world's own root — what God, and what would he do? Having conceived of divine perfection, one must work out one's own (liberation).

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*loga-mūlae* = from the world's own root, from the foundation of the world | *logaṃ* = the world | *passaha* = see | *ko īsaro* = what God? (who is God?) | *ko ya kiṃci* = and what would | *karejja* = do | *deva-siddhiṃ* = divine perfection | *kappiu ṃ* = having conceived of | *appāṇaṃ* = one's own self | *ceva* = itself | *sāhae* = works out, achieves

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the Jain philosophical response to theism — not a denial of divine perfection but a reorientation of its significance. The liberated souls (Siddhas) exist in a state of divine perfection (*deva-siddhi*), but this perfection was achieved through their own effort, not granted by an external deity. The world has its own roots — it is self-existing and self-sustaining, governed by the laws of karma rather than divine will. The question "what would God do?" is rhetorical: in the Jain universe, no entity has the power to intervene in another's karma. Liberation is therefore entirely the work of the self on the self.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The world runs on its own laws. Even if you imagine divine perfection as your ideal, the work of reaching it is entirely your own — no one can do it for you.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would shift in your spiritual practice if you truly accepted that no one is going to rescue you — that it is entirely your work?

### SUTRA 1.66

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अत्थि एगे पवयणं वयंति, लोगं च सव्वं णिच्च-भावियं ।
एगे य लोगं अणिच्च-भावियं, दुवे वि एए णायरियं ण लद्धं ॥१.६६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

atthi ege pavayaṇaṃ vayaṃti, logaṃ ca savvaṃ ṇicca-bhāviyaṃ |
ege ya logaṃ aṇicca-bhāviyaṃ, duve vi ee ṇāyariyaṃ ṇa laddhaṃ || 1.66 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some declare the entire world to be permanent by nature; others declare the world to be impermanent by nature — neither of these two has attained the teaching of the Knower.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*atthi ege* = there are some | *pavayaṇaṃ* = teaching, declaration | *vayaṃti* = who say | *logaṃ* = the world | *savvaṃ* = entirely | *ṇicca-bhāviyaṃ* = permanent by nature | *ege ya* = and others | *logaṃ* = the world | *aṇicca-bhāviyaṃ* = impermanent by nature | *duve vi ee* = both of these | *ṇāyariyaṃ* = teaching of the Knower | *ṇa laddhaṃ* = have not attained

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the Jain doctrine of *syādvāda* (the doctrine of conditional predication) in embryo. The absolutist positions — "everything is eternal" (held by the Vedantic tradition) and "everything is impermanent" (the Buddhist position) — are both partial. The Jain analysis holds that reality is *both* permanent and impermanent in different respects: the soul is permanent in its essential nature (*dravya*) but impermanent in its modes (*paryāya*). To seize on one aspect and declare it the whole truth is to fall into a one-sided view (*ekānta*) that does not capture reality fully. Neither extreme has attained the Knower's teaching.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Some say everything lasts forever; others say nothing lasts at all. The Jain teaching says both are half-truths — reality is more subtle than either extreme.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your thinking do you cling to absolute positions — "this is always true" or "this is never true" — that might be missing the fuller picture?

### SUTRA 1.67

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

लोगो निच्चो य अणिच्चो य, एवमाहु जिणा पुव्वे ।
जह णाम रुक्खो कट्ठे, पत्ते पुफे फले तहा ॥१.६७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

logo nicco ya aṇicco ya, evamāhu jiṇā pubbe |
jaha ṇāma rukkho kaṭṭhe, patte puppe phale tahā || 1.67 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The world is both permanent and impermanent — thus the Conquerors declared previously — just as a tree (is permanent) in its wood but (impermanent) in its leaves, flowers, and fruits.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*logo* = the world | *nicco ya* = is permanent | *aṇicco ya* = and impermanent | *evamāhu* = thus declared | *jiṇā* = the Conquerors | *pubbe* = previously, in the past | *jaha ṇāma* = just as | *rukkho* = a tree | *kaṭṭhe* = in its wood, in its trunk | *patte* = in its leaves | *puppe* = in its flowers | *phale* = in its fruits | *tahā* = so also

**5. COMMENTARY**

The tree metaphor is one of the most elegant illustrations in the Agamic texts. A tree is permanent in its essential wood-nature — it persists through seasons — but impermanent in its modes: leaves fall and grow, flowers bloom and fade, fruits appear and are consumed. Similarly, the soul is permanent in its essential existence but undergoes continuous modification in its states, karmic coatings, and modes of experience. The Conquerors (*jiṇā*) are invoked in the plural to indicate that this is not Mahavira's unique invention but the consistent teaching of all Tirthankaras across time.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Reality is both permanent and changing — like a tree that stays a tree while its leaves and flowers constantly change. The Conquerors have always taught this middle understanding.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In your own experience, what aspects of yourself feel permanent — and what aspects are constantly changing?

### SUTRA 1.68

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अत्थि एगे वयंति भो, सासए मोक्ख-वाइणो ।
अत्थि एगे ण मुच्चंति, दुवे वि एए ण जाणिया ॥१.६८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

atthi ege vayaṃti bho, sāsae mokkha-vāiṇo |
atthi ege ṇa muccaṃti, duve vi ee ṇa jāṇiyā || 1.68 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some say liberation is eternal — some say liberation does not exist — both of these two do not know (the truth).

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*atthi ege* = some exist who | *vayaṃti* = say | *bho* = indeed | *sāsae* = eternal | *mokkha-vāiṇo* = speaking of liberation | *atthi ege* = some exist who | *ṇa muccaṃti* = liberation does not exist / souls are never freed | *duve vi ee* = both of these | *ṇa jāṇiyā* = do not know

Two further wrong views about liberation: that liberation is just a form of eternal existence in some heavenly realm (a view that would make liberation indistinguishable from a very long samsaric life), and that liberation does not exist at all (a nihilistic view). ### SUTRA 1.69

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे आहु जीव-संबंधो, एगे णत्थि णिरामिसो ।
एगे आहु अकत्तारो, दुवे वि एए ण जाणिया ॥१.६९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege āhu jīva-saṃbandho, ege ṇatthi ṇirāmiso |
ege āhu akattāro, duve vi ee ṇa jāṇiyā || 1.69 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

⚠️ Some say the connection with the soul is — some say there is no connection without attachment; some say they are non-doers — both of these do not know.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege āhu* = some say | *jīva-saṃbandho* = connection/relationship with the soul | *ege ṇatthi* = some (say) there is not | *ṇirāmiso* = without attachment, without worldly involvement | *ege āhu* = some say | *akattāro* = non-doers, that souls do not act | *duve vi ee* = both of these | *ṇa jāṇiyā* = do not know

⚠️ This sutra addresses the question of the soul's agency. Some teach that the soul is inherently connected to and conditioned by its material involvement (a form of materialist entanglement theory). ### SUTRA 1.70

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे आहु परिणाए, एगे आहु अपरिणाए ।
एगे आहु अहकम्मए, दुवे वि एए ण जाणिया ॥१.७०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege āhu pariṇāe, ege āhu apariṇāe |
ege āhu ahakammae, duve vi ee ṇa jāṇiyā || 1.70 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

⚠️ Some say (the soul) transforms — some say it does not transform; some say one's own karma (alone determines) — both of these do not know.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

⚠️ This sutra addresses the question of the soul's transformation. Both extremes miss this distinction.

### SUTRA 1.71

एगे आहु अस्सिए जीवे, सो चेव होइ नाणादी ।
एगे आहु अणस्सिए जीवे, दुवे वि एए ण जाणिया ॥१.७१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some say the soul is dependent on something — that same soul possesses knowledge and other qualities; some say the soul is independent — both of these do not know.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege āhu* = some say | *assie jīve* = the soul is dependent, relying on something | *so ceva* = that same | *hoi* = has, possesses | *ṇāṇādī* = knowledge and other qualities | *ege āhu* = some say | *aṇassie jīve* = the soul is independent, not relying on anything | *duve vi ee* = both | *ṇa jāṇiyā* = do not know

### SUTRA 1.72

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे आहु अत्थिए जीवे, एगे आहु णत्थिए जीवे ।
एगे आहु अव्वत्तव्वे, दुवे वि एए ण जाणिया ॥१.७२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege āhu atthie jīve, ege āhu ṇatthie jīve |
ege āhu avvattavve, duve vi ee ṇa jāṇiyā || 1.72 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Some say the soul exists — some say the soul does not exist — some say it is inexpressible — both (of any two of these) do not know.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege āhu* = some say | *atthie jīve* = the soul exists | *ege āhu* = some say | *ṇatthie jīve* = the soul does not exist | *ege āhu* = some say | *avvattavve* = it is inexpressible, cannot be stated | *duve vi ee* = any two | *ṇa jāṇiyā* = do not know

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is a crucial sutra in Jain epistemology. The three positions — soul exists, soul does not exist, soul is inexpressible — each capture a partial truth. The Jain *syādvāda* (doctrine of conditional predication) would say: the soul exists from the perspective of its essential being; it does not exist as a permanent unchanging self-identical entity across all modes; and its full nature may be difficult to express in any single statement. What is wrong is to take any one of these positions as the absolute and complete truth. Even the "inexpressible" position, taken as absolute, becomes a claim — and a wrong one.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How comfortable are you with the idea that reality might require multiple, seemingly contradictory statements to describe — and that no single answer is the whole truth?

### SUTRA 1.74

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एए सव्वे वि य एए, मिच्छ-दंसण-संपया ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

All of these — all such people — seized by wrong perception, thereby attain un-understanding (wrong views), and all of them (end up) in the states of suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ee sabbe vi ya* = all of these indeed | *ee* = these | *micchadaṃsaṇa-saṃpayā* = seized by wrong perception | *abohiṃ* = un-understanding, wrong views | *pāvaṃti* = attain, obtain | *teṇa* = thereby, because of that | *te sabbe* = all of them | *naraesyu* = in the states of suffering | *ya* = and

After the extended series of philosophical refutations, this sutra provides the summary consequence: all those who hold wrong views (*micchadaṃsaṇa*), regardless of the specific wrong view, end up in states of suffering. Wrong perception (*micchadaṃsaṇa*) is specifically identified as the root cause: it generates wrong understanding (*abodhi*), which generates wrong conduct, which generates suffering. This is the logical structure the entire chapter has been building.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 1.75

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे विप्परिणमंति एए, मिच्छत्त-परिणया भवंति ।
ते ण जाणंति दुक्ख-मोक्खं, एस मग्गे इइ वुत्तं ॥१.७५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je vippaRiṇamaṃti ee, micchaṃtapaRiṇayā bhavaṃti |
te ṇa jāṇaṃti dukkha-mokkhaṃ, esa magge iai vuttaṃ || 1.75 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who are transformed by these (wrong views), who are shaped by wrong-orientation — they do not know liberation from suffering; this is the path, thus it has been declared.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je vippaRiṇamaṃti* = who are transformed by | *ee* = these (wrong views) | *micchaṃtapaRiṇayā* = shaped by wrong orientation | *bhavaṃti* = become | *te ṇa jāṇaṃti* = they do not know | *dukkha-mokkhaṃ* = liberation from suffering | *esa magge* = this is the path | *iai vuttaṃ* = thus it has been declared

**5. COMMENTARY**

This closing sutra of the philosophical refutation section is remarkable for its psychological observation: people are *transformed by* wrong views — the views shape the person, not merely inform them. This is the Jain understanding of *mithyātva* (wrong orientation): it is not merely an incorrect idea but a fundamental disposition that determines how one processes all experience. A person shaped by wrong orientation cannot see liberation clearly because their entire perceptual apparatus is distorted by the wrong-orientation. Only right orientation (*samyag-darśana*) can begin to restore clear seeing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Wrong views don't just give you incorrect information — they change how you see everything, making liberation invisible to you. That's why right orientation matters so much.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever changed a fundamental belief — and noticed that it changed not just one thing but how you saw many other things?

## Uddesaka 4 — True Conduct Versus False Conduct (Gathas 76–88)

---

### SUTRA 1.76

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

उड्ढं अहो य तिरियं च, सव्वओ तसा-थावरे ।
ण हिंसेज्जा ण हंतव्वे, एस धम्मे विसोहिए ॥१.७६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

uḍḍhaṃ aho ya tiriyaṃ ca, savvao tasā-thāvare |
ṇa hiṃsejjā ṇa haṃtavve, esa dhamme visohi e || 1.76 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Above, below, and sideways — in all directions — mobile and immobile beings should not be harmed, should not be killed; this is the doctrine, purified.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*uḍḍhaṃ* = above | *aho* = below | *ya tiriyaṃ* = and sideways | *ca savvao* = and in all directions | *tasā* = mobile beings | *thāvare* = immobile beings | *ṇa hiṃsejjā* = should not be harmed | *ṇa haṃtavve* = should not be killed | *esa dhamme* = this doctrine | *visohi e* = purified, the purified doctrine

**5. COMMENTARY**

Uddesaka 4 opens with the broadest possible formulation of non-violence: all directions (above, below, all sides) and all categories of life (mobile and immobile). The phrase *tasā-thāvara* is a technical Jain classification: mobile beings (animals, humans, etc.) and immobile beings (one-sensed beings: earth, water, fire, air, plant souls). Most traditions protect only the most visible, sentient creatures. Jain ethics extends protection to every living thing in every direction. This universality is the defining characteristic of Jain ethics and the most demanding aspect of the monastic life.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In all directions, every living thing — those that move and those that stay still — should not be harmed. The Jain teaching is completely without exceptions.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you genuinely tried to extend care to all living beings in all directions, what would be the most immediate change in your behavior?

### SUTRA 1.77

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पाण-भूय-जीव-सत्ते, एगे देव-वाइणो ।
न किंचि दुक्खं काउं, परिव्वएज्जासि मुणी ॥१.७७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

pāṇa-bhūya-jīva-satte, ege deva-vāiṇo |
ṇa kiṃci dukkhaṃ kāūṃ, parivvaejjāsi muṇī || 1.77 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For all living beings, all creatures, all souls, all sentient ones — some speak of (them as) divine — the monk should wander without causing any suffering whatsoever.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pāṇa-bhūya-jīva-satte* = living beings, creatures, souls, sentient ones | *ege* = some | *deva-vāiṇo* = speak of as divine, say they are of divine nature | *ṇa kiṃci* = not any | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering | *kāūṃ* = causing, making | *parivvaejjāsi* = should wander | *muṇī* = the monk

**5. COMMENTARY**

The phrase *deva-vāiṇo* — "some speak of them as divine" — is an interesting acknowledgment: some traditions attribute divine status to all beings, which provides their own internal justification for non-violence. Regardless of the theological framework — whether you see all beings as divine, as fellow souls, or simply as beings that feel pain — the practical conclusion is the same: the monk wanders without causing suffering. The Jain path achieves the same behavioral conclusion through different reasoning than the divine-nature view, but does not dismiss the value of that insight in motivating compassion.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Whether or not you see all beings as divine, the monk's practice is to move through the world without causing suffering to any of them.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does seeing something as sacred change how carefully you treat it? What would it mean to extend that feeling to all living things?

### SUTRA 1.78

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे परो य अपरो वा, सेहे संजए धुवे ।
परियाय-बुद्धी भवे, सव्व-भूएसु मेहावी ॥१.७८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je paro ya aparo vā, sehe saṃjae dhuye |
pariyāya-buddhī bhave, savva-bhūesu mehāvī || 1.78 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whether toward others or toward himself — established, restrained, steady — the wise one has understanding of the cycle (of all beings), toward all beings.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je paro ya aparo vā* = whether toward others or oneself | *sehe* = established | *saṃjae* = restrained | *dhuye* = steady, firm | *pariyāya-buddhī* = understanding of the cycle (of births), discernment of conditions | *bhave* = has | *savva-bhūesu* = toward all beings | *mehāvī* = the wise one

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra introduces the quality of *pariyāya-buddhi* — discernment of the cyclic nature of all beings. The wise monk understands that every being he encounters is cycling through births just as he is, that every being has occupied every possible position in the cycle relative to every other being. This understanding produces neither special attachment to those close to him nor special aversion to those distant — because from the perspective of the long arc of samsara, everyone has been everything to everyone. This is not sentimentality but the natural result of clear seeing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The wise monk treats himself and all others the same — because he understands that all beings are cycling through the same process of birth and rebirth together.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you genuinely believed that every being you encountered had at some point been your most beloved — what would change in how you met them?

### SUTRA 1.79

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पण्णासयाए य वट्टमाणे, से भिक्खू सुद्धसंजए ।
ण विग्गहं कुज्जा कयाइ, भिक्खू आयाणमाचरे ॥१.७९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

paṇṇāsayāe ya vaṭṭamāṇe, se bhikkhū suddhasaṃjae |
ṇa viggahaṃ kujjā kayāi, bhikkhū āyāṇamācare || 1.79 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Moving about with wisdom, the monk — purely restrained — should never enter into conflict, the monk should practice the discipline of the path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*paṇṇāsayāe* = with wisdom, with discernment | *ya vaṭṭamāṇe* = moving about, living | *se bhikkhū* = the monk | *suddhasaṃjae* = purely restrained | *ṇa viggahaṃ* = should not enter into conflict, should not cause disputes | *kujjā* = should do | *kayāi* = ever, at any time | *bhikkhū* = the monk | *āyāṇamācare* = should practice the discipline

**5. COMMENTARY**

The prohibition on conflict (*viggaha*) covers more than physical fighting — it includes verbal disputes, philosophical arguments aimed at winning rather than illuminating, and interpersonal conflict of any kind. The monk who moves about with wisdom (*paṇṇāsayāe*) naturally avoids conflict, not because he is passive but because he has no ego investment in outcomes that would make him defensive or aggressive. This sutra is particularly relevant in the context of the preceding philosophical discussions: the monk who has understood the errors of rival teachings does not attack those teachers personally but simply practices the discipline of the true path.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The wise monk never gets into arguments or conflicts — not out of weakness, but because he has nothing to defend and nothing to attack.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you get into conflict with others — verbal, intellectual, interpersonal — what are you actually trying to protect?

### SUTRA 1.80

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

नो दंडे आयरे भिक्खू, नो गारत्थिय-माणवे ।
अंजली-कडे विणए, परिव्वएज्जासि मुणी ॥१.८०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

no daṃḍe āyare bhikkhū, no gāratthiya-māṇave |
aṃjalī-kaḍe viṇae, parivvaejjāsi muṇī || 1.80 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Let not the monk practice the rod (punishment, violence), let him not (act like) a householder's man — with folded hands in reverence, the monk should wander.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*no daṃḍe āyare* = should not practice the rod, should not use punishment | *bhikkhū* = the monk | *no gāratthiya-māṇave* = not like a householder's man, not with the behavior of a worldly person | *aṃjalī-kaḍe* = with folded hands | *viṇae* = with reverence, in humility | *parivvaejjāsi* = should wander | *muṇī* = the monk

**5. COMMENTARY**

The contrast between the true monk and the "householder's man" (*gāratthiya-māṇava*) is the central theme of Uddesaka 4. The monk should not use power over others (*daṃḍa* — the rod of authority), should not behave with the social conventions of a worldly person, and should not carry the pride and self-assertion of someone with social standing. The image of folded hands (*aṃjalī*) and reverence (*viṇaya*) is not merely about outward gesture but about the inner attitude of humility that the monk embodies — the opposite of the self-assertion that drives worldly behavior.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk doesn't dominate or punish anyone, and doesn't behave like a worldly social climber — he walks with folded hands and genuine humility.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your life do you carry social pride or authority in ways that separate you from genuine humility?

### SUTRA 1.81

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे खलु पव्वइए मुणी, ण भुंजे वि य कडुय-तित्तयं ।
मद्दव-भावेण संजए, एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे ॥१.८१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je khalu pavvaie muṇī, ṇa bhuṃje vi ya kaḍuya-tittayaṃ |
maddava-bhāveṇa saṃjae, esa magge aṇuttare || 1.81 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The monk who has truly renounced does not eat even what is bitter or pungent — restrained with a disposition of gentleness; this is the highest path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je khalu* = who truly | *pavvaie* = has renounced, has gone forth | *muṇī* = the monk | *ṇa bhuṃje vi* = does not consume even | *kaḍuya-tittayaṃ* = bitter and pungent foods | *maddava-bhāveṇa* = with a disposition of gentleness, with softness | *saṃjae* = restrained | *esa magge* = this is the path | *aṇuttare* = highest

**5. COMMENTARY**

Pungent and bitter foods (onion, garlic, hot peppers, etc.) are avoided in Jain monastic practice not merely for reasons of taste preference but because such foods are believed to stimulate the passions — they increase desire, agitation, and restlessness, making meditation and equanimity more difficult to maintain. The disposition of *mārdava* (gentleness, softness) is the inner quality that corresponds to the outer food practice: a gentle inner disposition is both cultivated by and expressed in gentle dietary choices. This sutra is specific and practical — the highest path shows itself in the smallest daily choices.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The truly renounced monk avoids even pungent or bitter foods — not just for health but because gentle living shows in every small choice, including what you eat.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What small daily choices are quietly reflecting — or contradicting — the inner state you want to cultivate?

### SUTRA 1.82

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अकिंचणे अनासत्ते, निम्मले धुय-संसए ।
आत्म-गुत्ते सया जागरे, एस मग्गे सुसाहिए ॥१.८२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

akiṃcaṇe aṇāsatte, nimmale dhuya-saṃsae |
ātta-gutte sayā jāgare, esa magge susāhie || 1.82 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Possessing nothing, unattached, pure, with doubts shaken off — self-guarded, always vigilant — this is the doctrine, well-established.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*akiṃcaṇe* = possessing nothing | *aṇāsatte* = unattached | *nimmale* = pure | *dhuya-saṃsae* = with doubts shaken off, free of uncertainty | *ātta-gutte* = self-guarded | *sayā jāgare* = always vigilant, always awake | *esa magge* = this is the doctrine | *susāhie* = well-established

**5. COMMENTARY**

Six qualities of the true monk are given here in compressed sequence: possessing nothing (*akiṃcaṇa*), unattached (*aṇāsatta*), pure (*nimmala*), free of doubt (*dhuya-saṃsaya*), self-guarded (*ātta-gutta*), and always vigilant (*sayā jāgara*). These form a progressive deepening: possessing nothing is the outer condition; being unattached is the inner attitude; purity is the resulting clarity; freedom from doubt is the epistemological security; self-guarding is the active practice; and always-vigilance is the continuous state that holds the entire structure together. The phrase *susāhie* (well-established) indicates this is a verified, tested path, not a speculation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The true monk: no possessions, no attachment, inner purity, no doubt, self-watching, always alert. That is the well-established path.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which of these six qualities feels most distant from where you are right now — and what would cultivating it look like?

### SUTRA 1.83

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सव्वत्थ विरए भिक्खू, सव्वत्थ विगय-मोहे ।
सव्व-दुक्खाण पारगए, एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे ॥१.८३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

savvattha virae bhikkhū, savvattha vigaya-mohe |
savvadukhāṇa pāragae, esa magge aṇuttare || 1.83 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The monk who is restrained in all things, free of delusion in all things, who has crossed over all suffering — this is the highest path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*savvattha* = in all things, everywhere | *virae* = restrained | *bhikkhū* = the monk | *savvattha* = in all things | *vigaya-mohe* = free of delusion, with delusion removed | *savvadukhāṇa* = of all suffering | *pāragae* = having crossed over | *esa magge* = this is the path | *aṇuttare* = highest, unsurpassed

**5. COMMENTARY**

The threefold *savvattha* (in all things) is the key to this sutra. It is not enough to be restrained in most things, free of delusion in some things, past some suffering. The Jain path requires completeness — total restraint, complete freedom from delusion, total crossing of suffering. This completeness is not perfectionism in the ordinary sense but the recognition that partial liberation is still bondage. As long as any remnant of delusion remains, any attachment continues, any karma is still being generated — the soul is not yet free. The bar is high, and the chapter sets it high deliberately.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk who is restrained about everything, free from all delusion, past all suffering — that is the highest path. Nothing partial, nothing halfway.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a part of your life you've quietly decided is exempt from the standard you hold the rest of your life to?

### SUTRA 1.84

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण अट्ठिं ण य पट्ठिं, ण वि देहे मनोरमे ।
आयाण-मग्गे अट्ठिए, से भिक्खू भाविए सया ॥१.८४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa aṭṭhiṃ ṇa ya paṭṭhiṃ, ṇa vi dehe mannorame |
āyāṇa-magge aṭṭhie, se bhikkhū bhāvie sayā || 1.84 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Without craving, without desire for what is pleasing, and without (attachment to) the delightful body — established on the path of discipline, the monk is always cultivated.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa aṭṭhiṃ* = without craving | *ṇa ya paṭṭhiṃ* = and without desire for what is pleasing | *ṇa vi dehe mannorame* = and not even in the pleasing body | *āyāṇa-magge* = on the path of discipline | *aṭṭhie* = established | *se bhikkhū* = the monk | *bhāvie* = cultivated, developed | *sayā* = always

**5. COMMENTARY**

The reference to the body (*dehe mannorame* — the pleasing, attractive body) is significant: the monk's practice extends to the absence of attachment to his own physical form. Vanity, comfort-seeking, and identification with the body as "my beautiful/my important body" are all forms of the subtle attachment that perpetuate karma. The monk who has severed even this attachment — who does not think of the body as pleasing or precious — is "always cultivated" (*bhāvie sayā*). The word *bhāvita* (cultivated) refers to the inner development that is the real meaning of spiritual practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk doesn't crave anything, doesn't desire what's pleasant, and doesn't even identify with or feel attached to his own body. He is always developing inwardly.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How attached are you to your own body's appearance, comfort, or status — and what does that attachment cost you?

### SUTRA 1.85

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे तारिसे भिक्खू, जितिंदिए जिए-मणे ।
धुय-पाव-कम्मंसे, ण मुच्चए से कयाइ ॥१.८५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je tārise bhikkhū, jitiṃdie jie-maṇe |
dhuya-pāva-kammaṃse, ṇa muccae se kayāi || 1.85 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The monk who is like that — with senses conquered, with mind conquered, having shaken off harmful karma — is never bound again.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je tārise* = the monk who is like that | *bhikkhū* = monk | *jitiṃdie* = with senses conquered | *jie-maṇe* = with mind conquered | *dhuya-pāva-kammaṃse* = having shaken off harmful karma | *ṇa muccae* = is not bound | *se* = he | *kayāi* = ever again, at any time

**5. COMMENTARY**

Conquest of the senses (*jitendriya*) and conquest of the mind (*jita-manas*) are two distinct achievements that together constitute the inner transformation required for liberation. The senses pull outward toward objects; the mind generates intentions, plans, and desires. A monk who has conquered both has effectively dismantled the two primary mechanisms by which karma is generated. Combined with the prior shaking-off of harmful karma (*dhuya-pāva-kamma*), the monk who embodies all three is described as one who will never be bound again — a statement of irreversible liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The monk who has conquered his senses, conquered his mind, and shaken off harmful karma — that monk is never bound again.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which do you find harder to conquer: the pull of the senses toward external things, or the mind's generation of desires and plans?

### SUTRA 1.86

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं अहा-समाहिए, सव्वसो विगय-मोहे ।
सव्व-दुक्खाण पारगए, सिद्धिं पडिवज्जइ ॥१.८६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ ahā-samāhie, savvaso vigaya-mohe |
savvadukhāṇa pāragae, siddhiṃ paḍivajjai || 1.86 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus, composed in accordance with the teaching, with delusion completely removed, having crossed over all suffering — one attains liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evaṃ* = thus | *ahā-samāhie* = composed in accordance with the teaching | *savvaso* = completely, entirely | *vigaya-mohe* = with delusion removed | *savvadukhāṇa* = of all suffering | *pāragae* = having crossed over | *siddhiṃ* = liberation, perfection | *paḍivajjai* = attains

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra completes the positive description of the liberated soul. The phrase *ahā-samāhie* — "composed in accordance with the teaching" — is important: liberation is not achieved through random effort or self-invented practice but through the specific discipline taught by the Conquerors. The three conditions — composure in the teaching, complete removal of delusion, crossing of all suffering — are sequential: the teaching produces composure, composure enables the removal of delusion, and the removal of delusion enables the crossing of all suffering. Liberation (*siddhi*) is the natural result of this sequence, not a grace or a gift.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When you truly follow the teaching, all delusion falls away, all suffering is crossed — and liberation is attained.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean for you to follow the teaching not selectively but completely?

### SUTRA 1.87

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एस खलु लोग-सारे, एस धम्मे जिण-भासिए ।
एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे, एसो अट्ठे सनातणे ॥१.८७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

esa khalu loga-sāre, esa dhamme jiṇa-bhāsie |
esa magge aṇuttare, eso aṭṭhe sanātaṇe || 1.87 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This indeed is the essence of the world — this is the doctrine declared by the Conqueror — this is the highest path — this is the truth, eternal.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*esa khalu* = this indeed | *loga-sāre* = essence of the world | *esa dhamme* = this doctrine | *jiṇa-bhāsie* = declared by the Conqueror | *esa magge* = this is the path | *aṇuttare* = highest | *eso aṭṭhe* = this is the meaning, this is the substance | *sanātaṇe* = eternal, timeless

**5. COMMENTARY**

The penultimate sutra serves as a fourfold seal of the entire chapter. Four declarations are made in sequence: this teaching is the world's essence, this is the doctrine spoken by the Conqueror, this is the highest path, and this is the eternal truth. The word *sanātana* (eternal) is deliberately chosen — it is a term associated with the Vedic tradition's claim to eternal truth, here appropriated for the Jain teaching: the Jain path is not new to Mahavira but is the eternal truth rediscovered and re-declared by each Tirthankara in each cycle of time.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

This teaching is the essence of the world — declared by the Conqueror, the highest path, eternal truth. Four seals in one verse.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean to treat what you know of the true teaching as genuinely the most important thing in the world — not spiritually polite, but actually most important?

### SUTRA 1.88

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एस धम्मे धुवे नीए, आणाए अरिहंताणं ।
समयं जाणाहि एयं, इइ बेमि ॥१.८८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

esa dhamme dhuye nīe, āṇāe arihantāṇaṃ |
samayaṃ jāṇāhi eyaṃ, iai bemi || 1.88 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This is the doctrine — permanent, certain — by the authority of the Arihantas; know this Samaya (true doctrine) — thus I say.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*esa dhamme* = this is the doctrine | *dhuye* = permanent, firm | *nīe* = certain, established | *āṇāe* = by the authority, by the teaching | *arihantāṇaṃ* = of the Arihantas (the Worthy Ones, those who have destroyed the inner enemies) | *samayaṃ* = the Samaya, the true doctrine (and the name of this chapter) | *jāṇāhi* = know | *eyaṃ* = this | *iai bemi* = thus I say — Mahavira's closing signature

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra closes the entire chapter with Mahavira's signature phrase: *iai bemi* — Thus I say. This phrase appears at the conclusion of every major section of the Sutrakritanga and is the traditional marker of Mahavira's direct declaration. The content of the closing is precise: the doctrine is permanent (*dhuya*) and certain (*nīya*) — not a theory, not a possibility, but an established certainty. It is given under the authority of the Arihantas — those who have conquered the inner enemies of attachment, aversion, and delusion. The word *Samaya* in the closing refers both to the doctrine and to the chapter's title — "Know this Samaya," know this doctrine: the subject of the entire first adhyayana is encapsulated in its own name.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The doctrine is permanent and certain, coming from those who have conquered their inner enemies. Know this teaching — Thus says Mahavira. — iti bemi

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean to live as if this teaching — this Samaya — were the truest, most certain thing you know?
