# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 9: Samkhya (सम्खाय)

**Translation by:** JainAwaken.org
**Source:** Ardhamagadhi Prakrit — Sutrakritanga, Book 1 (Prathama Shrutaskandha)
**Chapter:** Ninth Adhyayana (नवम् अध्ययन)
**Total Sutras:** 35
**Theme:** A systematic enumeration of Jain metaphysical categories — providing the philosophical scaffolding for all the ethical teachings. Distinguishes soul from non-soul, living from non-living, the types of karma, and the structure of bondage and liberation. This is the Sutrakritanga's contribution to systematic Jain philosophy: showing that the path of liberation is grounded in accurate understanding of reality.

---

## PART I — Living Beings and Non-Living Matter (Sutras 1–10)

---

### SUTRA 9.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवा य अजीवा य, बंधो मोक्खो य ।
एयं जाणंतो सम्मं, णिव्वाणं अहिगच्छए ॥९.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jīvā ya ajīvā ya, baṃdho mokkho ya |
eyaṃ jāṇaṃto sammaṃ, ṇivvāṇaṃ ahigaccae || 9.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Souls and non-souls, bondage and liberation — one who knows these correctly attains liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jīvā* = souls, living beings | *ya* = and | *ajīvā* = non-souls, non-living matter | *baṃdho* = bondage | *mokkho* = liberation | *eyaṃ* = these | *jāṇaṃto* = knowing, one who knows | *sammaṃ* = correctly, rightly | *ṇivvāṇaṃ* = liberation | *ahigaccae* = attains, reaches

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra presents the entire philosophical framework of the chapter in one verse: four categories — souls, non-souls, bondage, and liberation — comprise the complete map of reality that one must understand to attain liberation. The simplicity is deceptive. Each category opens into extraordinary philosophical depth. Soul (jīva) in Jain philosophy is the conscious, knowing, inherently luminous subject — distinct from the body, distinct from matter, capable of liberation. Non-soul (ajīva) is everything else: matter (pudgala), space (ākāsha), motion-medium (dharma-substance), rest-medium (adharma-substance), and time. Bondage is the state in which karma — a form of subtle matter — clings to the soul and obscures its natural capacities. Liberation is the complete separation of soul from all karmic matter. Knowing these four categories "correctly" means not merely as concepts but as living understanding that transforms perception and behavior.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are just four things you really need to understand: souls, non-souls, how they get tied together, and how that tie gets cut — know these correctly and you attain liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you currently know yourself as a soul — as something separate from your body, your thoughts, and your karma — or do you experience yourself as the body and its contents?

### SUTRA 9.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

चेयणा य अचेयणा, जीवस्स लक्खणं ।
संसारसत्ता सव्वे वि, जाणंतो मुच्चए मुणी ॥९.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ceyaṇā ya aceyaṇā, jīvassa lakkhaṇaṃ |
saṃsārasattā savve vi, jāṇaṃto muccae muṇī || 9.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Consciousness and non-consciousness — the mark of the soul; knowing all the beings in the cycle of existence, the monk is freed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ceyaṇā* = consciousness, sentience | *ya* = and | *aceyaṇā* = non-consciousness, insentience | *jīvassa* = of the soul | *lakkhaṇaṃ* = mark, characteristic | *saṃsārasattā* = beings in the cycle of existence | *savve vi* = all of them | *jāṇaṃto* = knowing | *muccae* = is freed | *muṇī* = monk, sage

**5. COMMENTARY**

The fundamental distinction between soul and non-soul comes down to consciousness (cetanā). The soul is characterized by consciousness — the capacity to know, to perceive, to experience. Non-soul matter is characterized by the absence of consciousness — it does not know, does not perceive, does not experience. This distinction is philosophically precise: the body is matter and therefore non-conscious; the soul that inhabits it is conscious. The confusion between soul and body — mistaking the material vehicle for the conscious passenger — is, in Jain philosophy, the root cause of all bondage. A monk who knows all the beings in the cycle of existence — knows their nature as souls temporarily clothed in karma and bodies — has the understanding necessary for liberation. This knowledge is not encyclopedic information but direct perception of the soul-reality in all beings.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul is what has consciousness — what knows and feels; everything else is matter without consciousness. Knowing this difference clearly is how the monk gets free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In this moment, can you sense the difference between the knowing awareness in you and the thoughts and sensations that awareness is aware of?

### SUTRA 9.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

खित्तं काल-भावेण, वण्णओ गंधओ रसओ ।
फासओ संठाणओ य, अजीवो पुग्गलो भणिए ॥९.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

khittaṃ kāla-bhāveṇa, vaṇṇao gaṃdhao rasao |
phāsao saṃṭhāṇao ya, ajīvo puggalo bhaṇie || 9.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Characterized by extension, time, and modification — by color, smell, taste, touch, and shape — the non-soul is called matter.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*khittaṃ* = extension, space-occupation | *kāla* = time | *bhāveṇa* = by modification, by change | *vaṇṇao* = by color | *gaṃdhao* = by smell | *rasao* = by taste | *phāsao* = by touch | *saṃṭhāṇao* = by shape, form | *ajīvo* = the non-soul | *puggalo* = matter (pudgala) | *bhaṇie* = is called

**5. COMMENTARY**

Matter (pudgala) — the non-soul substance — is defined here through its observable characteristics: it occupies space (has extension), exists in time, undergoes modification, and is detectable through the five senses as color, smell, taste, touch, and shape. This is a remarkably comprehensive characterization of matter that maps well onto modern scientific understanding: all material things have spatial extension, temporal existence, change over time, and physical properties detectable by the senses. In Jain philosophy, karma is a particular form of subtle matter — invisible to ordinary perception but having the same fundamental characteristics as gross matter. Understanding matter in this comprehensive way enables the practitioner to identify it as the "other" — the non-self — which helps in progressively dis-identifying with the body and its contents.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Matter — the non-soul aspect of reality — is everything that has color, smell, taste, touch, and shape, that takes up space and changes over time.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Everything your physical senses can perceive is matter — including the thoughts and emotions that arise in your body. Does recognizing this change how you relate to them?

### SUTRA 9.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मो अधम्मो आयासं, एए अजीवा भणिया ।
कालो य दव्वपज्जाओ, जीवा उ सत्तभेयओ ॥९.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammo adhammo āyāsaṃ, ee ajīvā bhaṇiyā |
kālo ya davvapajjāo, jīvā u sattabheyao || 9.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The motion-medium, the rest-medium, and space — these are declared as non-souls; also time and the modifications of substances; souls, however, are of seven kinds.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*dhammo* = the motion-medium (dharma-substance, that which enables motion) | *adhammo* = the rest-medium (adharma-substance, that which enables rest) | *āyāsaṃ* = space | *ee* = these | *ajīvā* = non-souls | *bhaṇiyā* = are declared | *kālo ya* = and time | *davva-pajjāo* = modifications of substance | *jīvā* = souls | *u* = however | *sattabheyao* = of seven kinds, seven categories

**5. COMMENTARY**

Jain metaphysics identifies six fundamental substances (dravyas): souls, matter, the motion-medium, the rest-medium, space, and time. This sutra lists the non-soul substances: the motion-medium (a subtle substance that enables all movement — without it, nothing could move), the rest-medium (which enables rest — without it, nothing could be still), space (which provides room for all other substances to exist), and time. These are remarkable philosophical constructs: Jain philosophy recognized that motion itself requires an enabling condition, as does rest. The "seven kinds of souls" refers to the classification of living beings by number of senses (one through five) plus humans and liberated souls — the complete taxonomy of consciousness. This systematic enumeration is the "samkhya" (enumeration) that gives the chapter its name.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Reality is made up of six fundamental substances: souls and five non-soul realities — matter, a substance that enables motion, a substance that enables rest, space, and time.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The teaching says there is a medium that enables rest as well as one that enables motion — what would it mean for your life to find what enables genuine inner rest?

### SUTRA 9.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णिरुवक्कमा पज्जया, जीवस्स पुग्गलस्स य ।
धम्माधम्मागासाणं, कालस्स य सुया इमा ॥९.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇiruvakkamā pajjayā, jīvassa puggalassa ya |
dhammādhammāgāsāṇaṃ, kālassa ya suyā imā || 9.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The modifications are not without cause — of soul and matter, of the motion-medium, the rest-medium, space, and of time — thus it has been heard.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇiruvakkamā* = not without cause, not random | *pajjayā* = modifications, modes | *jīvassa* = of soul | *puggalassa* = of matter | *dhammādhammāgāsāṇaṃ* = of the motion-medium, rest-medium, and space | *kālassa* = of time | *ya* = and | *suyā* = heard (from the teacher), received teaching | *imā* = this

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra establishes that all changes and modifications in reality — of souls, matter, and the other substances — are not random or without cause. Everything that happens in the universe arises from prior causes and conditions. This is the metaphysical basis of karma theory: the state a soul finds itself in right now is the result of prior causes, and the future state will result from present causes. There are no accidents in the Jain metaphysical framework. This principle has both sobering and liberating implications: sobering because it means you cannot blame your situation on random fate; liberating because it means your future is genuinely in your hands — by understanding and working with the causal mechanism, you can change the trajectory of your soul's journey.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Nothing in the universe changes randomly — every change in every substance, including in your soul, has causes; this is why karma works and why your choices truly matter.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If everything in your current situation has causes — both the good and the difficult — what earlier choices are producing the situation you find yourself in right now?

### SUTRA 9.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे जीवा अणंता य, पुग्गला य अणंतया ।
धम्मधम्माकासाणि, कालो य अज्जिय भणिय ॥९.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege jīvā aṇaṃtā ya, puggalā ya aṇaṃtayā |
dhammadhamāgāsāṇi, kālo ya ajjiya bhaṇiya || 9.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Souls are one (in kind) yet infinite in number; matter particles too are infinite; the motion-medium, the rest-medium, and space are each one; and time — thus it has been declared.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege* = one (in nature/kind) | *jīvā* = souls | *aṇaṃtā ya* = and infinite in number | *puggalā ya* = and matter particles | *aṇaṃtayā* = infinite | *dhamma-adhamma-āgāsāṇi* = the motion-medium, rest-medium, space (each one substance) | *kālo ya* = and time | *ajjiya* = indeed, truly | *bhaṇiya* = has been declared

**5. COMMENTARY**

Jain cosmology specifies the quantity of each fundamental substance. Souls are infinite in number — there are literally unlimited numbers of individual souls in the universe, each distinct, each undergoing its own journey. Matter particles are also infinite. But the motion-medium, the rest-medium, and space are each singular — there is one space that encompasses everything, one motion-medium that enables all motion, one rest-medium that enables all rest. Time is also singular. This enumeration serves practical philosophy: because souls are infinite and eternal (never created, never destroyed), the liberation of any soul does not diminish the universe; there are always more souls still in bondage. This understanding motivates compassion: every being you encounter is a soul on a journey similar to your own.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are infinitely many souls in the universe, each one distinct and on its own journey — this is why every life matters and why the teaching of universal compassion makes sense.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If every living being around you is a soul on a cosmic journey — some advanced, some struggling — how does that change how you see the people and creatures you interact with?

### SUTRA 9.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण कोइ जीवो उप्पणो, ण वि णासइ कहिं चि ।
पुव्वं पि भवे जीवो, होहिइ य एवमेव ॥९.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa koi jīvo uppaṇo, ṇa vi ṇāsai kahiṃ ci |
puvvaṃ pi bhave jīvo, hohii ya evameva || 9.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

No soul ever came into being (from nothing), nor does any soul perish anywhere; the soul existed before and will continue to exist — just so.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa koi* = no | *jīvo* = soul | *uppaṇo* = came into being, was created | *ṇa vi ṇāsai* = nor perishes | *kahiṃ ci* = anywhere | *puvvaṃ pi* = also before, previously | *bhave* = existed | *jīvo* = the soul | *hohii* = will exist, will be | *ya* = and | *evameva* = just so, in this way

**5. COMMENTARY**

The soul is declared to be eternal — without beginning and without end, uncreated and indestructible. This is one of the most fundamental tenets of Jain metaphysics, distinguishing it sharply from materialism (which holds that consciousness is produced by matter and perishes with it) and from certain theistic traditions (which hold that souls are created by God). The Jain soul has always existed and will always exist in some form — what changes is the form of its embodiment (or, in liberation, the absence of embodiment). This has profound implications for ethics: every soul you encounter has always existed and will always exist; there is no such thing as a disposable being. The violence that destroys a body does not destroy the soul — it simply forces it into another existence — but it generates the karma that will have consequences for the violent soul.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul has never been created and can never be destroyed — it has always existed and will always exist; what changes is only what form of existence it is currently experiencing.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If your soul has always existed, what do you imagine it was doing before this particular human birth — and what does that timeless continuity mean for how you live now?

### SUTRA 9.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ससरीरा य जीवा ये, विसरीरा य जे जिया ।
सरीरमेव पोग्गलं, जीवो तेण ण लिप्पए ॥९.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sasarīrā ya jīvā ye, visarīrā ya je jiyā |
sarīrameva poggalaṃ, jīvo teṇa ṇa lippae || 9.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Souls who are embodied and those who are disembodied (liberated) — the body is simply matter; the soul is not stained by it.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sasarīrā* = embodied, with body | *jīvā* = souls | *ye* = those | *visarīrā* = disembodied, without body | *je jiyā* = those who are liberated (conquerors) | *sarīram* = the body | *eva* = simply, indeed | *poggalaṃ* = matter | *jīvo* = the soul | *teṇa* = by it | *ṇa lippae* = is not stained, is not attached

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra draws the crucial distinction between the soul in embodied form (still in the cycle of existence) and the soul in disembodied form (liberated). The liberating philosophical insight is in the last line: the body is simply matter — the soul is not intrinsically stained or attached by having a body. This means liberation is possible because the soul's essential nature is already pure; it is covered but not corrupted. Just as gold found in ore is not intrinsically ore but only coated by it, the soul's purity is intrinsic and needs only to be uncovered, not manufactured. This is why liberation is achievable: you are not trying to create purity where none exists but to remove the covering from purity that has always been there. The practical implication is enormous: you are not your body, not your karma, not your current state — you are the pure awareness underneath all of that.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

You are the soul, not the body — the body is just matter surrounding you; the soul itself is not corrupted by having a body, just temporarily covered.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can you sense the difference between being the body and inhabiting the body — between being your pain, your emotions, your thoughts, versus being the awareness that notices them?

### SUTRA 9.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवो उ कम्म कुव्वंतो, पुग्गले परिणामए ।
तेण बद्धो जायइ, दुहदुहं वेयए पाणी ॥९.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jīvo u kamma kuvvaṃto, puggale pariṇāmae |
teṇa baddho jāyai, duhadуhaṃ veyae pāṇī || 9.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The soul, while doing karma, causes transformation in matter; bound by that, it is reborn and experiences suffering again and again.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jīvo* = the soul | *u* = indeed | *kamma* = karma | *kuvvaṃto* = while doing, while performing | *puggale* = matter | *pariṇāmae* = causes to transform, modifies | *teṇa* = by that | *baddho* = bound | *jāyai* = is reborn | *duha-duhaṃ* = suffering again and again | *veyae* = experiences | *pāṇī* = the living being

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra explains the mechanism of karmic bondage. When the soul acts through passion (anger, pride, deceit, or greed), it causes actual transformations in subtle matter — karmic particles are attracted to the soul and adhere to it, taking on specific qualities that correspond to the nature of the action. This is not metaphor: Jain philosophy holds that karma is literally a form of subtle matter that becomes bound to the soul through the soul's own activity. This bound karma then produces the conditions of rebirth — the type of existence, the length of life, the experiences of pleasure and pain. The soul experiences suffering "again and again" because each rebirth is accompanied by further karma-accumulation, which produces further rebirth, in a self-reinforcing cycle that only deliberate practice can interrupt.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When the soul acts with passion, it attracts and binds karmic material to itself — that bound karma then determines its next rebirth and causes it to keep suffering over and over.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Right now, when you act from anger or desire or ego, can you sense that something is being added to you — that you are taking on a new weight?

### SUTRA 9.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णो अत्थि ओरालो, मज्झिमो य अणुत्तरो ।
सो सव्वगओ जीवो, ण वि दिट्ठो कयाइ वि ॥९.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo atthi orālo, majjhimo ya aṇuttaro |
so savvagao jīvo, ṇa vi diṭṭho kayāi vi || 9.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

There is no gross, middle, or finest (soul) — that soul which pervades everything has never been seen by anyone.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇo atthi* = there is not | *orālo* = gross (form of soul) | *majjhimo* = middle | *aṇuttaro* = finest, most subtle | *so* = that | *savvagao* = all-pervading, present everywhere | *jīvo* = soul | *ṇa vi diṭṭho* = has not been seen | *kayāi vi* = ever, by anyone

**5. COMMENTARY**

The soul does not have a physical size in the conventional sense — it is not gross, not of medium size, not infinitely small. This distinguishes the Jain concept of the individual soul from both materialist views (which would make the soul a physical object of some size) and certain pantheist views (which would make all souls merge into one all-pervading substance). The Jain soul pervades the body it inhabits completely but is not detectable by ordinary sensory perception. It has never been seen by physical eyes — not because it is too small to see but because it is non-material and consciousness cannot be seen through physical instruments. This sets important parameters: the soul is real, it is individual, it is knowable — but only through the direct experience of the soul knowing itself, not through external observation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul doesn't have a physical size and can't be seen with physical eyes — it's not physical, but it's completely real, present throughout the body it inhabits.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a way to know your own soul directly — not through thinking about it, but through a direct inner contact with the knowing awareness itself?

## PART II — Types of Karma (Sutras 11–20)

### SUTRA 9.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणावरणीयं च, दंसणावरणं च णं ।
मोहणीयं च वेयणी, कम्म अट्ठविहं जिणा ॥९.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇāvaraṇīyaṃ ca, daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ ca ṇaṃ |
mohaṇīyaṃ ca veyaṇī, kamma aṭṭhavihaṃ jiṇā || 9.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Knowledge-obscuring, perception-obscuring, deluding, and feeling-producing — karma is of eight kinds, (as declared by) the Jinas.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇāṇāvaraṇīyaṃ* = knowledge-obscuring karma | *daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ* = perception-obscuring karma | *mohaṇīyaṃ* = deluding karma | *veyaṇī* = feeling-producing karma | *kamma* = karma | *aṭṭhavihaṃ* = of eight kinds | *jiṇā* = the Jinas (have declared)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra introduces the systematic eight-fold classification of karma — one of the most sophisticated and precise taxonomies in ancient philosophy. The four types mentioned here are the first group: knowledge-obscuring karma clouds the soul's inherent capacity to know directly; perception-obscuring karma clouds the soul's capacity to perceive directly (both are analogous to different clouds covering the same sun); deluding karma is the most dangerous — it generates the wrong attitude toward reality (wrong beliefs) and the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed); feeling-producing karma determines the type and intensity of sensory and emotional experiences (pleasure and pain). Each type of karma has specific causes, specific effects on the soul's experience, and specific ways of being dissolved. This classification is not merely academic: knowing which type of karma you are working against allows more targeted practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Karma comes in eight types — the first four are the ones that obscure knowledge, obscure perception, cause delusion, and produce feelings of pleasure and pain.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which type of karma do you feel most strongly in your own experience — the one that clouds your understanding, or the one that generates powerful emotions?

### SUTRA 9.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आउयं णामं गोत्तं च, आंतराइं च अट्ठमं ।
एयाइं अट्ठ कम्माइं, जीवबंधणकारणं ॥९.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āuyaṃ nāmaṃ gottaṃ ca, āṃtarāiṃ ca aṭṭhamaṃ |
eyāiṃ aṭṭha kammāiṃ, jīvabaṃdhaṇakāraṇaṃ || 9.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Lifespan-determining, body-determining, status-determining, and obstructing — the eighth; these eight karmas are the causes of the soul's bondage.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*āuyaṃ* = lifespan-determining karma | *nāmaṃ* = name/body-determining karma | *gottaṃ* = status/family-determining karma | *āṃtarāiṃ* = obstructing karma | *aṭṭhamaṃ* = the eighth | *eyāiṃ aṭṭha* = these eight | *kammāiṃ* = karmas | *jīvabaṃdhaṇa* = the soul's bondage | *kāraṇaṃ* = cause

**5. COMMENTARY**

The second group of four karma types: lifespan-determining karma fixes the duration of each rebirth (how long this particular existence lasts); body-determining karma specifies the type of body, its qualities, its beauty or ugliness, its species; status-determining karma assigns high or low status, honored or dishonored birth-circumstances; and obstructing karma creates specific obstacles to the soul's natural powers — blocking generosity, blocking enjoyment, blocking willpower. Together these eight karmas cover the complete range of what the soul experiences as fate, circumstance, personality, and limitation. Understanding these categories shows that nothing in one's circumstance is arbitrary: every quality of birth, body, duration of life, and social standing reflects specific karma accumulated in prior lives.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The remaining four types of karma determine how long you live, what kind of body you get, what social circumstances you're born into, and what specific obstacles block your natural capacities.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What seems fixed and unchosen about your life — your body, your circumstances, your apparent limitations — and how does understanding these as karmic might change your relationship to them?

### SUTRA 9.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मिच्छत्तमविरमणं, पमाओ कसाया जोगे ।
एए पंच बंधहेऊ, जीवाणं भवणिस्सिया ॥९.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

miccattamaviramaṇaṃ, pamāo kasāyā joge |
ee paṃca baṃdhaheū, jīvāṇaṃ bhavaṇissiyā || 9.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Wrong view, non-restraint, negligence, the passions, and activity — these five are the causes of bondage for souls in existence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*miccattam* = wrong view, incorrect perception of reality | *aviramaṇaṃ* = non-restraint, lack of control | *pamāo* = negligence, heedlessness | *kasāyā* = passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed) | *joge* = activities of body, speech, and mind | *ee paṃca* = these five | *baṃdha-heū* = causes of bondage | *jīvāṇaṃ* = for souls | *bhavaṇissiyā* = in existence, conditioned by existence

**5. COMMENTARY**

The five causes of bondage are enumerated in a specific hierarchy. Wrong view is first because it is the most fundamental: a soul that misperceives reality — that identifies itself with matter, believes liberation is impossible, or holds false views about the universe — accumulates karma from this very misperception. Non-restraint means lacking control over body, speech, and mind in their interaction with the world. Negligence (pamāda) — the spiritual carelessness discussed earlier in the Sutrakritanga — allows karma to accumulate even when the other causes are addressed. The four passions are the engines of karmic momentum — anger, pride, deceit, and greed stain each action with their specific qualities. And finally, mere activity of body, speech, and mind generates some karma even for a practitioner who is otherwise careful, because activity in a physical world inevitably involves disturbance of matter and the lives within it.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

### SUTRA 9.14

### SUTRA 9.15

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणं दंसणमेगट्ठं, चरणं खलु पालणं ।
एए तिण्णि समा सव्वे, रयणत्तयमिइ सुयं ॥९.१५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇaṃ daṃsaṇamegaṭṭhaṃ, caraṇaṃ khalu pālāṇaṃ |

Knowledge, right vision, and conduct — which is conduct, indeed, means keeping (the vows); these three together are the Three Jewels — thus it has been heard.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

The Three Jewels (ratnatraya) of Jain practice are formally declared: right knowledge, right vision, and right conduct. ### SUTRA 9.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

भासाओ य सोयव्वा, जीवे णेयव्वए सया ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One should listen to the teachings, and always recognize the soul; one who has not fully accepted (the path) — that one is not freed, there is no doubt.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*bhāsāo* = teachings, discourses | *soyavvā* = should be listened to | *jīve* = the soul | *ṇeyavvae* = should be recognized, should know | *sayā* = always | *ṇa samаggo* = not fully, not completely | *paḍivaṇṇo* = accepted (the path) | *muccae* = is freed | *natthi* = there is not | *saṃsayo* = doubt

The sutra specifies two fundamental practices for the path of liberation: listening to teachings (soyavvā) and recognizing the soul (jīve ṇeyavvae). ### SUTRA 9.23

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जस्स णत्थि पमाओ, जस्स ण य परिग्गहो ।
जस्स णत्थि अरई रई, तस्स बंधो ण विज्जए ॥९.२३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jassa natthi pamāo, jassa ṇa ya pariggaho |
jassa natthi araī raī, tassa baṃdho ṇa vijjae || 9.23 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who has no negligence, who has no possessiveness, who has neither aversion nor attachment — for that one, bondage is not found.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jassa* = for whom | *natthi* = there is not | *pamāo* = negligence | *pariggaho* = possessiveness | *araī* = aversion, dislike | *raī* = attachment, liking | *tassa* = for that one | *baṃdho* = bondage | *ṇa vijjae* = is not found

**5. COMMENTARY**

Liberation is described here through three absences: no negligence, no possessiveness, no aversion or attachment. These three form a complete picture. Negligence is the failure of sustained watchfulness that allows karma to accumulate passively. Possessiveness is the active grasping at external things that creates karmic bonds through desire. Aversion and attachment are the two movements of the agitated mind — toward what it likes, away from what it dislikes — and every such movement is itself a karmic act. The person who has eliminated all three has effectively stopped all inflow of new karma. With no new karma coming in and existing karma being burned away through practice, the soul moves inexorably toward liberation. This is the mathematical formula of freedom: no new input, existing stock depleting, result: liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Not the Vedas, not white-robed ones, not Buddhists, not those with external marks — those who are engaged in harmful karma do not know the teaching.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa veyāiṃ* = not the Vedic ones | *seyāiṃ* = the white-robed ones (Svetambara reference or other white-robed sect) | *buddhāiṃ* = Buddhists | *liṃgiyā* = those with sectarian marks, those wearing outward signs | *dhammo* = the teaching | *tehiṃ* = by those | *ṇa jāṇijjā* = is not known | *je* = who | *hiṃsākamma* = harmful karma | *rayaṇā* = engaged in, absorbed in

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra takes a bold stance: no tradition — not Vedic, not Buddhist, not any sect with outward marks — provides liberation if its practitioners are engaged in harmful karma. The criterion is not the label you wear, the scripture you quote, the teacher you follow, or the marks on your body. The only criterion that matters is whether your actual life involves harm to living beings. A person of any tradition who practices genuine non-harm is on the path; a person of any tradition who is engaged in harmful karma, regardless of their religious label, does not know the true teaching. ⚠️ (Note: the specific identity of "seyāiṃ" — white-robed ones — may refer to early Jain sectarian groups, proto-Svetambara communities, or other white-clad wanderers; the exact reference is uncertain.)

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Wearing the right label — Vedic priest, Buddhist, Jain, or any other — means nothing if your life involves harming others; no tradition has a monopoly on the true teaching, and no label substitutes for genuine non-harm.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you identify with a tradition or label in ways that sometimes substitute for actually practicing what that tradition teaches?

### SUTRA 9.26

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

समता सव्वभूयेसु, एगे वि कमओ महं ।
तं च सिद्धेहिं पण्णत्तं, एसो धम्मो सणातणो ॥९.२६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

samatā savvabhūyesu, ege vi kamao mahaṃ |
taṃ ca siddhehiṃ paṇṇattaṃ, eso dhammo saṇātaṇo || 9.26 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Equanimity toward all beings — even one step (in this direction) is great; and that which has been declared by the accomplished ones: this is the eternal law.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*samatā* = equanimity, impartial attitude | *savvabhūyesu* = toward all beings | *ege vi* = even one step | *kamao* = step, movement | *mahaṃ* = great | *taṃ ca* = and that | *siddhehiṃ* = by the accomplished (liberated) ones | *paṇṇattaṃ* = declared, taught | *eso* = this | *dhammo* = teaching, law | *saṇātaṇo* = eternal

**5. COMMENTARY**

Equanimity toward all beings — treating every living being with the same impartial, non-harming regard — is declared the eternal law by all liberated beings. The beautiful addition is "even one step is great." This is unusual encouragement within a demanding text: the teaching acknowledges that full equanimity toward all beings is a long journey, and that even one genuine movement in that direction — one moment of treating a creature you would usually ignore with the same regard as a being you cherish — is spiritually significant. This prevents the perfect from being the enemy of the good: you do not need to arrive at complete equanimity today; you need to take the next step toward it.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Treating every living being with equal compassion and care is the eternal law — and even one genuine step in that direction is considered great.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Toward which beings do you most easily lose equanimity — treating them with less care, more indifference, or more hostility than others?

### SUTRA 9.27

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

बहिया पज्जवसाणे, बंधुणो जिय संसय ।
अब्भंतरं च सोहेज्ज, एवं मुच्चइ बंधणा ॥९.२७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

bahiyā pajjavasāṇe, baṃdhuṇo jiya saṃsaya |
abbhaṃtaraṃ ca sohejja, evaṃ muccai baṃdhaṇā || 9.27 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having removed the external attachments, having conquered doubt, having purified the inner — thus one is freed from bondage.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*bahiyā* = external | *pajjavasāṇe* = having removed, having ended | *baṃdhuṇo* = attachments | *jiya* = having conquered | *saṃsaya* = doubt | *abbhaṃtaraṃ* = inner | *sohejja* = having purified | *evaṃ* = thus | *muccai* = is freed | *baṃdhaṇā* = from bondage

**5. COMMENTARY**

The three stages of liberation practice are named in sequence: first, remove external attachments (the possessions, relationships, and social identities that bind the soul to the external world); second, conquer doubt (the inner uncertainty about whether the path is true, whether liberation is real, whether one is capable — doubt is itself a form of spiritual bondage); and third, purify the inner realm (address the passions, the wrong views, the residual karma that remains after external attachments have been loosened). This three-stage movement — outer, then doubt, then inner — is a realistic description of how spiritual practice actually unfolds. Many practitioners succeed with external renunciation but remain bound by inner doubt and unresolved passions. True liberation requires all three.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Liberation comes in stages: first let go of outer attachments, then conquer inner doubt, then purify the inner passions — do all three and you are free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which stage is your current practice at — outer detachment, conquering doubt, or inner purification — and what is the next genuine step?

### SUTRA 9.28

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगे अत्ता परिण्णाओ, सव्वभूयाणि पासए ।
एगस्स य अत्तणो, एगो हु परिभासए ॥९.२८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ege attā pariṇṇāo, savvabhūyāṇi pāsae |
egassa ya attaṇo, ego hu paribhāsae || 9.28 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who has fully known the self sees all beings (as connected); one who speaks of the individual self speaks of the individual alone.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ege* = one | *attā* = self, soul | *pariṇṇāo* = having fully known | *savvabhūyāṇi* = all beings | *pāsae* = sees | *egassa* = of the individual | *attaṇo* = self | *ego hu* = one alone | *paribhāsae* = speaks of

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra touches a profound philosophical point: one who has fully known their own soul sees all beings in that knowing. This does not mean the Jain tradition merges all souls into one (it does not — individual souls remain distinct in liberation). It means that genuine self-knowledge reveals the universal nature of the soul — the knowing, experiencing, inherently pure quality — which is the same in every being. Seeing this in yourself enables you to recognize it in every creature, however different their external form. The second line suggests that a teaching that focuses only on the individual soul without this larger recognition is incomplete — it remains trapped in a narrow self-concern that misses the compassion that full self-knowledge naturally generates.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When you truly know your own soul, you naturally see the same soul-nature in every other living being — knowledge of yourself becomes compassion for all.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you look at other people or creatures, do you see yourself reflected — another soul on a journey like yours — or do you see only difference and separation?

### SUTRA 9.29

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जो य जाणइ जीवे, अजीवं च अणुत्तरं ।
तस्स णत्थि परभावो, सो परं णेइ आयडं ॥९.२९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jo ya jāṇai jīve, ajīvaṃ ca aṇuttaraṃ |
tassa natthi parabhāvo, so paraṃ ṇei āyaḍaṃ || 9.29 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who knows the soul and the non-soul, supreme — for that one there is no state of "other" (no more taking on alien natures); that one leads the self to the highest.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jo ya jāṇai* = one who knows | *jīve* = the soul | *ajīvaṃ* = the non-soul | *aṇuttaraṃ* = supremely, completely | *tassa* = for that one | *natthi* = there is not | *parabhāvo* = state of "other," alien nature | *so* = that one | *paraṃ* = to the highest | *ṇei* = leads | *āyaḍaṃ* = the self

**5. COMMENTARY**

Complete knowledge of the distinction between soul and non-soul produces a state in which no further taking on of alien nature occurs. When the soul is firmly established in knowledge of its own pure nature, it stops identifying with and absorbing the qualities of matter and karma. This is the practical liberation: not a dramatic exit from the world but the cessation of the confusion that has been causing the soul to keep taking on new forms of embodiment. The one who knows the soul supremely then leads themselves — through the force of that knowledge — to the highest state. Knowledge is not merely passive: it actively guides the soul upward toward its liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When you truly know the difference between your soul and everything that isn't your soul, you stop taking on new karmic identities — and that knowledge itself guides you to the highest state.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What identities have you taken on from the outside world — from your culture, your family, your experiences — that are not actually you?

### SUTRA 9.30

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ओरालं वेदणीयं च, तेयं कम्मण्णं तहा ।
भासापज्जाय ते पंच, छट्ठो य मणपज्जाओ ॥९.३०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

orālaṃ vedaṇīyaṃ ca, teyaṃ kammaṇṇaṃ tahā |
bhāsāpajjāya te paṃca, chaṭṭho ya maṇapajjāo || 9.30 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The gross (body), the feeling-body, the luminous body, the karmic body — and speech modification, those are five; and the sixth is the mind modification. ⚠️

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*orālaṃ* = gross body | *vedaṇīyaṃ* = feeling body (vaidārya — the body of sensation) | *teyaṃ* = luminous body (tejas — the energy body) | *kammaṇṇaṃ* = karmic body | *bhāsāpajjāya* = speech modification | *paṃca* = five | *chaṭṭho* = sixth | *maṇapajjāo* = mind modification

⚠️ The Jain taxonomy of bodies/modifications is technically complex and varies between sources. This translation follows the most commonly cited six-fold classification but the exact correspondence of terms to specific bodies in Digambara vs Svetambara traditions may differ.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Jain analysis of the embodied soul identifies multiple bodies or modification-layers. The gross body is the physical body of flesh and bones that others can see. The feeling body or sensation body corresponds to the subtle layer of experience — how pleasure and pain are actually transmitted to the soul. The luminous or tejas body is an energy layer associated with the force of spiritual practice (tapas). The karmic body is the most fundamental layer — the actual karmic material that accompanies the soul from existence to existence and determines the nature of each rebirth. Speech and mind are then identified as additional modes through which the soul interacts with the world. Understanding these layers helps the practitioner identify which aspects of their experience belong to the soul and which belong to the successive layers of covering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul in embodiment has multiple layers beyond the physical body — including sensation, energy, and karma — and understanding these layers helps you know what is you and what is covering you.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Beyond your physical body, can you sense the layers of feeling, energy, and habitual pattern that also shape your experience — and are any of these layers who you actually are?

### SUTRA 9.31

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण सो कम्मं करिज्जाइ, जो य कम्मं ण करिज्जइ ।
जो कम्मं ण करइ, से लहइ परमं सुहं ॥९.३१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa so kammaṃ karijjāi, jo ya kammaṃ ṇa karijjai |
jo kammaṃ ṇa karai, se lahai paramaṃ suhaṃ || 9.31 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That one does not accumulate karma who does not perform karma; one who does not do karma, that one attains supreme happiness.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇa so* = that one does not | *kammaṃ* = karma | *karijjāi* = accumulate | *jo ya* = one who | *kammaṃ* = karma | *ṇa karijjai* = does not perform | *jo* = one who | *kammaṃ* = karma | *ṇa karai* = does not do | *se* = that one | *lahai* = attains | *paramaṃ* = supreme | *suhaṃ* = happiness

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is as direct as the teaching gets: not doing karma means not accumulating it; not accumulating it leads to supreme happiness. The challenge, of course, is that simply existing in a physical body involves some level of karmic activity — breathing, moving, eating. The resolution in Jain philosophy is that karma accumulates not merely through action but through action combined with passion and negligence. A monk who moves carefully, eats mindfully, and maintains equanimity throughout is performing actions but minimizing karmic accumulation — because the passionate, careless quality that causes karma to strongly adhere is absent. Perfect non-accumulation is the final goal: attained completely only in the final stages before liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The less karma you create through harmful or passionate actions, the less trapped you are — eventually, creating no new karma at all leads to supreme happiness.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What actions are you performing right now primarily out of passion — desire, anger, or ego — rather than out of genuine necessity or compassion?

### SUTRA 9.32

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कम्मस्स दोण्हि पक्खा, बंधो य मोक्खो य ।
बंधंतरो भवे जीवो, मोक्खंतरो मोक्खमाहिए ॥९.३२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kammassa doṇhi pakkhā, baṃdho ya mokkho ya |
baṃdhaṃtaro bhave jīvo, mokkhaṃtaro mokkhamāhie || 9.32 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Karma has two aspects: bondage and liberation; the soul that is within bondage is in existence; the soul within liberation is declared to be liberated.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kammassa* = of karma | *doṇhi* = two | *pakkhā* = aspects, sides | *baṃdho ya* = bondage | *mokkho ya* = and liberation | *baṃdhaṃtaro* = within bondage | *bhave* = is in existence | *jīvo* = the soul | *mokkhaṃtaro* = within liberation | *mokkham* = liberation | *āhie* = is declared

**5. COMMENTARY**

The entire framework of Jain metaphysics reduces to two states of the soul: bound and free. A soul with karma is bound and in the cycle of existence; a soul without karma is free and liberated. There is no middle ground, no third option, no partial liberation that is also partial bondage. This binary clarity is pedagogically important: it eliminates the comfortable but false middle position of being "mostly free" while retaining just enough attachment to keep the cycle going. The practical implication is that every act of genuine renunciation, every reduction of karmic accumulation, every dissolution of accumulated karma moves the soul in a specific direction — toward liberation. There is no movement that is neutral; every moment is either toward bondage or toward liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are only two states for a soul: bound by karma and in the cycle, or free from karma and liberated — every choice you make right now is moving you toward one or the other.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In this moment, in this choice you are currently facing — which direction are you moving?

### SUTRA 9.33

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जो य बंधं विजाणाइ, मोक्खं च अणुत्तरं ।
एगे ते सिद्धिगामिणो, संसारविरओ मुणी ॥९.३३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jo ya baṃdhaṃ vijāṇāi, mokkhaṃ ca aṇuttaraṃ |
ege te siddhigāmiṇo, saṃsāravirae muṇī || 9.33 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who knows bondage and liberation, supreme — those ones alone are heading toward liberation; the monk who is dispassionate from the cycle of existence.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jo ya* = one who | *baṃdhaṃ* = bondage | *vijāṇāi* = knows | *mokkhaṃ* = liberation | *aṇuttaraṃ* = supreme | *ege* = alone, only | *te* = they | *siddhigāmiṇo* = heading toward liberation | *saṃsāravirae* = dispassionate from the cycle | *muṇī* = monk

**5. COMMENTARY**

Liberation is not available to everyone equally in the sense that not everyone is equally oriented toward it. The monk who has genuine knowledge of bondage and liberation — not just intellectual knowledge but knowledge that has generated genuine dispassion toward the cycle of existence — is the one who is actually heading toward liberation. Dispassion (virāga) from the cycle is the decisive quality: it means the cycle has lost its appeal, that the pleasures and satisfactions it offers no longer attract. This dispassion is not depression or nihilism — it is the natural result of seeing clearly what the cycle actually is: impermanent, unsatisfying, and without ultimate purpose. Once seen clearly, the soul naturally turns away from it toward liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Only those who genuinely know both bondage and liberation — and who have genuinely lost their attachment to the cycle of existence — are actually heading toward freedom.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you genuinely lost your attraction to the satisfactions the cycle offers — or does part of you still find it appealing and want to continue in it?

### SUTRA 9.34

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

लोगे जे सुक्खिया जीवा, जे य लोए दुहट्ठिया ।
ते सव्वे अवग्गहिया, धम्मेण परिरक्खिया ॥९.३४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

loge je sukhiyā jīvā, je ya loe duhāṭṭhiyā |
te savve avagghahiyā, dhammeṇa parirakkiyā || 9.34 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The living beings in the world who are happy, and those who are afflicted with suffering — all of them are grasped (encompassed) and protected by the teaching.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*loge* = in the world | *je* = those | *sukhiyā* = happy | *jīvā* = living beings | *je ya* = and those | *duhāṭṭhiyā* = afflicted with suffering | *te savve* = all of them | *avagghahiyā* = grasped, encompassed | *dhammeṇa* = by the teaching | *parirakkiyā* = protected

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra expresses the universal scope of the true teaching. Both the happy and the suffering — every state of existence — is encompassed and protected by the dharma. This is a statement of radical inclusivity: the teaching is not only for those who are already suffering and seeking relief, nor is it only for the philosophically sophisticated. The happy person who does not know they are in a temporary state of fortunate karma is equally within the scope of the teaching's protection. The protection comes precisely from the teaching's comprehensiveness — it describes the full range of existence (as this chapter has done through its systematic enumeration) so that no state of being is outside the map.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The true teaching protects and encompasses every living being — whether happy or suffering — because it describes the complete reality of every possible state of existence.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Does the teaching you follow feel equally relevant to you in times of happiness as in times of suffering — or does it mostly arise when things are difficult?

### SUTRA 9.35

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं संखाइयं णाऊण, संजए सव्वभूयेसु ।
सम्मत्तमेव पडिवज्जह, इइ बेमि ॥९.३५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ saṃkhāiyaṃ ṇāūṇa, saṃjae savvabhūyesu |
sammattameva paḍivajjaha, iti bemi || 9.35 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having known this enumeration, be restrained toward all beings — embrace right conduct alone. — iti bemi (Thus I say.)

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *saṃkhāiyaṃ* = enumeration, systematic classification | *ṇāūṇa* = having known | *saṃjae* = be restrained | *savvabhūyesu* = toward all beings | *sammattam* = right conduct, rightness | *eva* = alone | *paḍivajjaha* = embrace | *iti bemi* = thus I say

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter closes with the translation of metaphysical knowledge into practical conduct. Having enumerated the complete framework of reality — souls and non-souls, eight types of karma, the five causes of bondage, the Three Jewels, the fourfold community, the nature of liberation — the teaching immediately and directly converts this knowledge into action: be restrained toward all beings. Not merely understand the enumeration as philosophy, but let it change how you treat every living being you encounter. The final instruction — "embrace right conduct alone" — places conduct as the final word after all the philosophy. Understanding without conduct is incomplete; the knowledge serves the practice, and the practice serves liberation. "Thus I say" closes the chapter with the transmission seal.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Having understood all of this systematic teaching about reality, put it into practice: be restrained toward every living being — that is what this knowledge is for. — Thus I say.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What specific change in how you treat living beings — however small — would be the most direct translation of everything you have just read into actual daily life?
