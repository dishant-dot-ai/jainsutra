# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 7: Khuddabhaviya (खुद्दभविय)

**Translation by:** JainSutra.org
**Source:** Ardhamagadhi Prakrit — Sutrakritanga, Book 1 (Prathama Shrutaskandha)
**Chapter:** Seventh Adhyayana (सप्तम् अध्ययन)
**Total Sutras:** 25
**Theme:** A vivid description of the lower forms of existence into which careless, violent souls are reborn — as insects, worms, earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, air-bodies, and tiny sensory beings. The chapter's purpose is not to frighten but to motivate genuine care: every act of violence, no matter how small, plants the seed of a corresponding rebirth. A practitioner who protects even the smallest life is walking away from these forms of existence.

---

## PART I — The Seeds of Lower Rebirth (Sutras 1–10)

---

### SUTRA 7.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सव्वे पाणा सव्वे भूता, सव्वे जीवा सव्वे सत्ता ।
ण हंतव्वा ण अज्जावेयव्वा, ण परिघेत्तव्वा ॥७.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

savve pāṇā savve bhūtā, savve jīvā savve sattā |
ṇa haṃtavvā ṇa ajjāveyavvā, ṇa parigheṭṭavvā || 7.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

All breathing beings, all existing beings, all living beings, all sentient beings — none should be slain, none enslaved, none harmed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*savve* = all | *pāṇā* = breathing beings, those with breath | *bhūtā* = existing beings, those that have come into being | *jīvā* = living beings, souls | *sattā* = sentient beings, those with consciousness | *ṇa haṃtavvā* = should not be slain, not to be killed | *ṇa ajjāveyavvā* = should not be enslaved, not to be forced | *ṇa parigheṭṭavvā* = should not be harmed, not to be seized

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra is among the most complete and absolute statements of non-violence in all of world literature. Four different terms for living beings are used — breathing beings, existing beings, living beings, and sentient beings — to leave no gaps: every category of conscious existence is covered. Three forms of harm are then prohibited with equal absoluteness: killing (the most obvious), enslaving or coercing (forcing beings against their will), and seizing or harming (causing injury short of death). The triple prohibition covers the spectrum from lethal harm to subtle coercion. This verse is the foundation of all that follows in this chapter — the descriptions of lower rebirths are not presented to create fear but to show what happens when this absolute teaching is violated. Every lower form of existence described in the following sutras is the natural consequence of a soul that has failed to live by this principle.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every living thing — every creature that breathes, exists, or feels — must not be killed, forced, or harmed in any way.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there any living being in your daily life that you harm or coerce while telling yourself it doesn't count?

### SUTRA 7.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे इमं वयणं बुज्झे, वयणं सव्वपाणहियं ।
से णं पंडिए आहिए, जे ण हिंसइ किंचण ॥७.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je imaṃ vacaṇaṃ bujjhe, vacaṇaṃ savvapāṇahiyaṃ |
se ṇaṃ paṃḍie āhie, je ṇa hiṃsai kiṃcaṇa || 7.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who understands this teaching — the teaching beneficial for all living beings — that person is called a wise one, who harms nothing whatsoever.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je* = one who | *imaṃ* = this | *vacaṇaṃ* = teaching, word | *bujjhe* = understands | *savvapāṇahiyaṃ* = beneficial for all living beings | *se ṇaṃ* = that person | *paṃḍie* = wise, learned | *āhie* = is called | *je* = who | *ṇa hiṃsai* = does not harm | *kiṃcaṇa* = anything, whatsoever

**5. COMMENTARY**

Wisdom is defined here not by the quantity of knowledge accumulated but by a single criterion: not harming anything. This is the Jain reversal of the common understanding of "being smart" — the most educated, most philosophically sophisticated person who still causes harm has not understood the teaching. The one who "understands" the word means one who has absorbed it deeply enough that it changes behavior. Understanding that is not behavioral is not understanding at all in this context — it is mere information. The phrase "beneficial for all living beings" connects the teaching of non-violence not to ascetic self-denial but to universal benefit: practicing non-violence does not just benefit the practitioner's soul; it actively protects and benefits every being the practitioner encounters.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A truly wise person is not someone with lots of knowledge — it is someone who has understood the teaching deeply enough that they genuinely don't harm anything.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a gap between your understanding of non-violence as a concept and your actual daily practice of it?

### SUTRA 7.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे पुण पाणे विहिंसंति, अप्पबुद्धा असंजया ।
ते दुग्गइं पावमेंति, रोयंति बहुदुक्खिया ॥७.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je puṇa pāṇe vihiṃsaṃti, appubuddhā asaṃjayā |
te duggaiṃ pāvameti, royaṃti bahudukkiyā || 7.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who harm living beings — lacking wisdom, without restraint — they fall into bad destinations and weep, struck by much suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je puṇa* = those who again, who indeed | *pāṇe* = living beings | *vihiṃsaṃti* = harm, injure | *appubuddhā* = lacking wisdom, with small understanding | *asaṃjayā* = without restraint, uncontrolled | *te* = they | *duggaiṃ* = bad destinations, unfortunate rebirths | *pāvameti* = fall into, reach | *royaṃti* = weep, cry | *bahudukkiyā* = struck by much suffering, greatly afflicted

**5. COMMENTARY**

The causal mechanism of lower rebirth is stated directly: harming living beings leads to bad rebirths, which involve suffering. The two qualities of the violent person are noted — lack of wisdom and lack of restraint. These are not separate deficiencies but deeply connected: wisdom would show you that harm is self-destructive because of karma, and restraint would prevent you from acting on impulses even when wisdom is temporarily clouded. The violent person weeps in their next existence — the suffering experienced in lower rebirths is the direct karmic echo of the suffering one caused in previous lives. This is not punishment administered by an external judge but the natural consequence of karmic law: the soul experiences what it inflicted.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

People who harm other beings — because they lack wisdom and self-control — end up reborn in miserable places, experiencing exactly the kind of suffering they caused.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever caused suffering and then experienced a similar suffering yourself later? Did you connect the two?

### SUTRA 7.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुढवी-उदग-अग्गी वाऊ, वणस्सई तसे तहा ।
एए जीवा समा सव्वे, णो हंतव्वा कयाइ वि ॥७.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

puḍhavī-udaga-aggī vāū, vaṇassaī tase tahā |
ee jīvā samā savve, ṇo haṃtavvā kayāi vi || 7.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Earth beings, water beings, fire beings, air beings, vegetation, and mobile beings — all these living beings are equal; they should never be killed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*puḍhavī* = earth-body beings | *udaga* = water-body beings | *aggī* = fire-body beings | *vāū* = air-body beings | *vaṇassaī* = vegetation beings, plant-bodies | *tase* = mobile beings, beings with two or more senses | *tahā* = also, likewise | *ee* = these | *jīvā* = living beings, souls | *samā* = equal | *savve* = all | *ṇo haṃtavvā* = should not be killed | *kayāi vi* = ever, at any time

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra introduces the comprehensive Jain classification of living beings that is central to the chapter's teaching about lower rebirths. Jain philosophy recognizes that life extends far beyond the animal kingdom that is visible to the naked eye. The earth beneath your feet contains earth-body souls; water is teeming with water-body souls; fire contains fire-body souls; air carries air-body souls; every plant and blade of grass is alive with a plant soul; and all mobile creatures (animals, insects, humans) complete the full range. All of these beings are declared "equal" — equal in their capacity for suffering, equal in their desire not to be harmed, equal in their fundamental nature as souls. This equality is the foundation of Jain non-violence: there is no hierarchy of lives that makes some worth protecting and others expendable.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Life exists in the earth, water, fire, air, plants, and all moving creatures — every single one of them is equal as a living being and should never be killed.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What lives do you currently consider too small or too simple to matter — and what would change if you took their equal status seriously?

### SUTRA 7.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एइंदिय-दुइंदिया, तिइंदिय-चउरिंदिया ।
पंचिंदिया वि णो हिंसे, एसो संजमलक्खणो ॥७.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eiṃdiya-duiṃdiyā, tiiṃdiya-cauriṃdiyā |
paṃciṃdiyā vi ṇo hiṃse, eso saṃjamalakkhaṇo || 7.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One-sensed beings, two-sensed beings, three-sensed beings, four-sensed beings — even five-sensed beings should not be harmed: this is the mark of restraint.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eiṃdiya* = one-sensed beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants — sense of touch only) | *duiṃdiyā* = two-sensed beings (worms, etc. — touch and taste) | *tiiṃdiyā* = three-sensed beings (lice, ants — touch, taste, smell) | *cauriṃdiyā* = four-sensed beings (flies, mosquitoes — touch, taste, smell, sight) | *paṃciṃdiyā* = five-sensed beings (animals, humans — all five senses) | *vi* = even, also | *ṇo hiṃse* = should not harm | *eso* = this | *saṃjamalakkhaṇo* = the mark of restraint, the sign of self-control

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Jain classification of living beings by the number of senses they possess is one of the most comprehensive and ecologically sophisticated taxonomies in ancient Indian thought. At the most basic level are one-sensed beings — they have only the sense of touch. Moving up through two-sensed (worms, shellfish), three-sensed (lice, ants), and four-sensed beings (flies, mosquitoes, bees), the taxonomy reaches five-sensed beings (all animals and humans with a full sensory apparatus). Crucially, the teaching does not say "don't harm the more complex beings" — it explicitly says "even five-sensed beings" and works downward, implying the reverse direction of ethical concern. Restraint (saṃyama) is defined as the practice of protecting beings across all levels of this taxonomy. A monk who carefully avoids stepping on ants but has no problem harming larger animals has not understood restraint.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Whether a being has one sense or five senses, it should not be harmed — that total protection of all life is what true self-restraint actually means.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where does your protection of life stop — at what level of smallness or simplicity do you stop caring?

### SUTRA 7.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पाणाइवायपेल्लिओ, जीवो पच्चायाइ दुग्गइं ।
तम्हा पाणाइवायं तु, पंडिए परिवज्जए ॥७.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

pāṇāivāyapellio, jīvo paccāyāi duggaiṃ |
tamhā pāṇāivāyaṃ tu, paṃḍie parivajjae || 7.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The soul driven by the killing of living beings returns to bad destinies; therefore the wise one completely avoids the killing of living beings.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pāṇāivāya* = killing of living beings | *pellio* = driven, propelled | *jīvo* = the soul | *paccāyāi* = returns, goes back | *duggaiṃ* = to bad destinies, unfortunate rebirths | *tamhā* = therefore | *pāṇāivāyaṃ tu* = the killing of living beings | *paṃḍie* = the wise one | *parivajjae* = completely avoids, fully renounces

**5. COMMENTARY**

The causal logic of rebirth is laid out with clarity: killing is the cause; bad rebirth is the effect; and the wise person, understanding this, avoids killing. The word "driven" (pellio) is significant — it suggests not that the soul chooses its bad rebirth but that it is propelled there by the momentum of the karma accumulated through violence. Just as a ball thrown hard in one direction continues in that direction, a soul that has accumulated heavy violence karma is propelled toward the kinds of existence that correspond to that karma. This is not a moral punishment imposed by a judge; it is the natural movement of the soul toward the form of existence that matches its accumulated quality. The wise person understands this mechanism and therefore does not kill — not out of fear but out of genuine understanding.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A soul that causes harm gets pushed into miserable rebirths by the momentum of that harm — which is why a wise person avoids causing any harm at all.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much of your good behavior is motivated by genuine understanding of consequences versus fear of punishment versus actual compassion?

### SUTRA 7.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे पुण जीवा इह पाणिणो, ते सव्वे अहियासिया ।
ण ते वि मरणं इच्छंति, सव्वे जीवणमाहिया ॥७.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je puṇa jīvā iha pāṇiṇo, te savve ahiyāsiyā |
ṇa te vi maraṇaṃ icchaṃti, savve jīvaṇamāhiyā || 7.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

All the living beings here — all those that breathe — are capable of suffering; none of them wish for death; all of them cherish life.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*je puṇa* = those who | *jīvā* = living beings | *iha* = here, in this world | *pāṇiṇo* = breathing, with breath | *te savve* = all of them | *ahiyāsiyā* = capable of suffering, experience-bearers | *ṇa te vi* = nor do they | *maraṇaṃ* = death | *icchaṃti* = wish for, desire | *savve* = all | *jīvaṇam* = life | *āhiyā* = cherish, value

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra provides the emotional and experiential ground for non-violence: every living being that breathes is capable of suffering, desires to live, and does not want to die. This is an empirically observable fact, not a metaphysical claim that requires faith. Watch any creature when threatened — it tries to escape, to live, to avoid what would kill it. This universal desire for life is the deepest common ground between all living beings and the human practitioner. The practitioner who has genuinely perceived this shared quality in all beings cannot treat other lives as disposable. The word "cherish" for life (jīvaṇamāhiyā) is particularly moving — not merely that beings survive but that they value their existence, hold it dear.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every creature that breathes can suffer, loves life, and does not want to die — this simple fact is the most basic reason to never harm them.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you see a creature trying to escape or survive, can you genuinely feel its desire to live as equal to your own?

### SUTRA 7.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुढवीकाइया बहवे, णिरयेसु उववज्जइ ।
जो पुढवीं पिट्टइ घाई, बाला अ जे ण बुज्झई ॥७.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

puḍhavīkāiyā bahave, ṇirayesu uvavajjai |
jo puḍhavīṃ piṭṭai ghāī, bālā a je ṇa bujjhai || 7.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who strikes and destroys earth-body beings in great numbers is reborn in hell; such a person is a fool, one who has not understood.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*puḍhavīkāiyā* = earth-body beings | *bahave* = in great numbers, many | *ṇirayesu* = in hells | *uvavajjai* = is reborn, arrives | *jo* = who | *puḍhavīṃ* = the earth | *piṭṭai* = strikes, beats | *ghāī* = destroys, killer | *bālā* = fool, child-like in understanding | *a* = and | *je* = who | *ṇa bujjhai* = has not understood, does not comprehend

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter now begins its systematic description of lower rebirths by starting with the most basic form of life — earth-body beings. In Jain understanding, the earth itself is alive. Soil contains innumerable one-sensed souls whose entire experience is the sense of touch. Someone who compulsively digs, strikes, or destroys earth — perhaps through thoughtless construction, excavation, or simply violent disturbance of soil — is destroying great numbers of living beings and accumulating corresponding karma. The rebirth in hell is not poetic metaphor but literal Jain cosmology: the soul that has accumulated such karma through indiscriminate harm is reborn in hellish conditions. The description of such a person as a "fool" (bālā) indicates not moral wickedness but spiritual immaturity — they have not understood the first principle.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even the earth is full of living beings — someone who constantly destroys them carelessly ends up in hellish rebirths because they never understood that life is everywhere.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How do you treat the ground you walk on, the soil you dig in — as dead matter, or as something teeming with life?

### SUTRA 7.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

उदगंसि वि जीवा अत्थि, ते ण हिंसिज्ज कयाइ वि ।
तज्जोणिसु वि जायंते, जे ण जाणंति बालया ॥७.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

udagaṃsi vi jīvā atthi, te ṇa hiṃsijja kayāi vi |
tajjoṇisu vi jāyaṃte, je ṇa jāṇaṃti bālayā || 7.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In water also there are living beings; they should never be harmed; those fools who do not know this are born in those very wombs.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*udagaṃsi vi* = in water also | *jīvā* = living beings | *atthi* = there are | *te* = they | *ṇa hiṃsijja* = should not be harmed | *kayāi vi* = ever | *tajjoṇisu vi* = in those very wombs, in that very birth-place | *jāyaṃte* = are born | *je* = those who | *ṇa jāṇaṃti* = do not know | *bālayā* = the fools

**5. COMMENTARY**

Water is recognized as containing living beings — the water-body souls are one-sensed beings that exist throughout all water. This understanding predates modern microbiology by millennia but resonates with it: we now know that every drop of natural water contains vast numbers of microorganisms. The karmic consequence described here is particularly fitting: those who harm water-body beings are reborn as water-body beings themselves, experiencing existence as one of those tiny creatures they previously destroyed without thought. This principle — rebirth in the form of life you have violated — runs throughout the chapter. It is not punishment but the natural resonance between the kind of karma accumulated and the kind of existence that karma produces.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Water is full of living beings too — harm them carelessly and you may be reborn as one of those very creatures, experiencing their existence from the inside.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you pour away water, boil it, or waste it — are you aware that you are affecting living beings?

### SUTRA 7.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वणस्सईसु जीवा अत्थि, ते ण हिंसिज्ज कयाइ वि ।
तज्जोणिसु वि जायंते, जे ण जाणंति बालया ॥७.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vaṇassaīsu jīvā atthi, te ṇa hiṃsijja kayāi vi |
tajjoṇisu vi jāyaṃte, je ṇa jāṇaṃti bālayā || 7.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In vegetation also there are living beings; they should never be harmed; those fools who do not know this are born in those very wombs.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vaṇassaīsu* = in vegetation, in plant bodies | *jīvā* = living beings | *atthi* = there are | *te* = they | *ṇa hiṃsijja* = should not be harmed | *kayāi vi* = ever | *tajjoṇisu vi* = in those very wombs/birth-forms | *jāyaṃte* = are born | *je* = those | *ṇa jāṇaṃti* = do not know | *bālayā* = the fools

**5. COMMENTARY**

Plants are living beings with souls — this is a central and distinctive tenet of Jain philosophy. Unlike most ethical frameworks that grant moral consideration only to sentient animals, Jainism extends the circle of concern to all vegetation. Every tree, every grass blade, every flowering plant contains a living soul with the sense of touch. This is why Jain monks eat only once a day, avoid eating root vegetables (which require killing the entire plant), and take great care to minimize harm to vegetation. The modern science of plant communication — the discovery that plants respond to stimuli, have electrical signals, and in complex ways "experience" their environment — is consonant with this ancient teaching. Careless destruction of vegetation creates karmic bonds to plant-body rebirth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Plants are alive too — every tree and blade of grass contains a living being, and careless destruction of plants has karmic consequences just like harming animals.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much vegetation do you destroy in your daily life without a second thought — and does knowing it is alive change how you see that?

## PART II — The Sufferings of Small Existences (Sutras 11–18)

### SUTRA 7.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अग्गिकाइयजीवाणं, अग्गी दुक्खं पवेदए ।
तज्जोणिसु वि जायंते, जे ण जाणंति बालया ॥७.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

aggikāiyajīvāṇaṃ, aggī dukkhaṃ pavеdае |
tajjoṇisu vi jāyaṃte, je ṇa jāṇaṃti bālayā || 7.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For fire-body living beings, fire itself is the suffering experienced; those fools who do not know this are born in those very wombs.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*aggikāiya* = fire-body | *jīvāṇaṃ* = of living beings | *aggī* = fire | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering | *pavеdае* = is experienced, is felt | *tajjoṇisu vi* = in those very birth-forms | *jāyaṃte* = are born | *je* = those | *ṇa jāṇaṃti* = do not know | *bālayā* = the fools

**5. COMMENTARY**

Fire-body beings are described as souls that exist within the element of fire itself. In Jain understanding, fire is not merely a chemical reaction but an environment inhabited by one-sensed living beings. The remarkable point made here is that fire itself constitutes suffering for these beings — they exist within the very element that destroys other forms of life. To be a fire-body being is to exist in a state of constant burning, constant dissolution. The person who casually plays with fire, who lights unnecessary fires, who burns things without cause, is accumulating karma through harm to these beings and may find themselves reborn in this form. The teaching is ecological in its scope: no element of the natural world is a dead, exploitable resource; every element is a community of living beings.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Even fire contains living beings — and a person who carelessly burns things may be reborn as a fire-being, existing within fire itself as a form of constant suffering.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How do you relate to fire — as a tool, as a living element, or something in between?

### SUTRA 7.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वाउकाइयजीवाणं, वाऊ दुक्खं पवेदए ।
तज्जोणिसु वि जायंते, जे ण जाणंति बालया ॥७.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vāukāiyajīvāṇaṃ, vāū dukkhaṃ pavеdае |
tajjoṇisu vi jāyaṃte, je ṇa jāṇaṃti bālayā || 7.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For air-body living beings, air itself is the suffering experienced; those fools who do not know this are born in those very wombs.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vāukāiya* = air-body | *jīvāṇaṃ* = of living beings | *vāū* = air | *dukkhaṃ* = suffering | *pavеdае* = is experienced | *tajjoṇisu vi* = in those very birth-forms | *jāyaṃte* = are born | *je* = those | *ṇa jāṇaṃti* = do not know | *bālayā* = the fools

**5. COMMENTARY**

Air-body beings are the souls that inhabit the element of air — wind, breath, atmosphere. For these beings, the constant movement and disturbance of air is itself their mode of existence and suffering. The Jain monk takes extreme care not to generate unnecessary air movement — not waving hands carelessly, being mindful of where one breathes. The parallel structure used in sutras 7.8 through 7.12 creates a pedagogical rhythm: in each element, there are living beings; those who harm these beings are reborn in those forms; existence in those forms involves the particular suffering of that element. The cumulative effect is to build a complete picture of how violence toward any element of nature creates binding with that element at the level of one's next existence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

### SUTRA 7.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

After the vivid catalog of lower rebirths, the chapter pivots to the path out. The "wise one who has understood the meaning" is the person who has taken in the teaching of this chapter not as philosophical information but as a direct motivation to change. Having seen clearly where carelessness leads, they now commit to the path away from it.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The triple rule of non-violence — don't do it, don't cause others to do it, don't approve of it — covers the complete range of involvement in harm. ### SUTRA 7.25

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं सुणित्ता णिसमिच्च धम्मं, अहिंसगं सव्वपाणहियं ।
पडिवज्जह खिप्पमेव धम्मं, इइ बेमि ॥७.२५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ suṇittā nisamicca dhammaṃ, ahiṃsagaṃ savvapāṇahiyaṃ |
paḍivajjaha khippameva dhammaṃ, iti bemi || 7.25 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having heard and understood this teaching of non-violence, beneficial for all living beings — take up this teaching quickly. — iti bemi (Thus I say.)

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eyaṃ* = this | *suṇittā* = having heard | *nisamicca* = having understood, having absorbed | *dhammaṃ* = teaching | *ahiṃsagaṃ* = of non-violence | *savvapāṇahiyaṃ* = beneficial for all living beings | *paḍivajjaha* = take up, embrace | *khippameva* = quickly, without delay | *dhammaṃ* = the teaching | *iti bemi* = thus I say

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra closes the chapter with an urgency that has been building since the beginning: "take it up quickly." The word khippa — quickly, without delay — carries a specific charge. The teaching of this chapter — the catalog of lower rebirths, the causes of suffering, the path away — is precisely the kind of teaching that benefits from being absorbed and acted upon now, not stored as future knowledge. The human birth is described elsewhere as extraordinarily rare and precious; to have this birth and this teaching available simultaneously is doubly rare. To receive it and then delay implementing it is to waste a profound opportunity. The traditional closing formula "Thus I say" anchors the teaching in the authentic transmission from Mahavira through Sudharma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Having truly understood this teaching of non-violence — which benefits every living being — take it up now, without delay. — Thus I say.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What teaching have you fully understood intellectually but keep postponing putting into actual practice — and how much longer will you wait?
