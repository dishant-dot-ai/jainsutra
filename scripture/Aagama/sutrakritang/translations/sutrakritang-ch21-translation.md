# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 21: Nirgrantha-pravachana (The Nigantha's Sermon / The Jain Teaching)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** Second Shrutaskandha (Book 2)
**Chapter:** 21 (Book 2, Chapter 5)
**Prakrit Title:** Nirgrantha-pravachana
**Approximate Sutras:** 35
**Theme:** A positive summary and affirmation of the core Jain teaching. In contrast to the preceding chapters' refutations and warnings, this chapter is a creed — the teaching of the Nirgranthas (the knot-free ones) stated directly, comprehensively, and with full conviction.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> जीवो अत्थि य, णत्थि य बंधो, अत्थि य मोक्खो।
> एयं जिणेहिं पण्णत्तं, सम्मं जो सद्दहइ सो॥

*The soul exists; bondage exists; liberation exists. This is what the Jinas have proclaimed — one who correctly has faith in this is on the path.*

---

## Part I — The Soul and Karma (Sutras 1–10)

**21.1**
*Prakrit:* जीवो अत्थि य लोगे, एयं जिणेहिं पण्णत्तं।
*Translation:* The soul exists in the world — this is what the Jinas have proclaimed.
*Commentary:* The chapter opens with the most fundamental affirmation in Jain metaphysics: the soul is real. This is not a claim to be established through argument alone but one transmitted by the Jinas — the fully liberated beings who have seen reality directly. Against philosophical traditions that deny the soul's existence, the Jain teaching stands as an affirmation: there is a real, conscious, knowing entity that is distinct from the body and its processes. This existence of the soul is the foundation on which everything else in the teaching rests.
*The simple version:* **The soul is real.** This is the first and most basic affirmation of the Jain teaching, and it comes from those who have directly experienced liberation.

**21.2**
*Prakrit:* जीवो कत्ता य भोत्ता य, कम्मफलाण य।
*Translation:* The soul is both the agent of action and the experiencer of karma's fruits.
*Commentary:* Two crucial qualities of the soul are established: agency and experience. The soul acts — it is the author of karma, not a passive passenger in a deterministic universe. And the soul experiences the results of those actions — it is not shielded from karmic consequences by circumstances or intercession. This double quality — agent and experiencer — means that the soul is fully responsible for its situation and fully capable of changing it. It is neither the victim of fate nor the puppet of divine will.
*The simple version:* **You create your karma, and you experience it.** Both the cause and the effect are genuinely yours.

**21.3**
*Prakrit:* अत्थि य बंधो जीवस्स, कम्मेण परिणामिओ।
*Translation:* There is bondage for the soul — brought about by karma.
*Commentary:* Having established the soul's existence, the sutra now establishes the fact of bondage. Bondage is real — the soul is genuinely constrained, limited, and affected by the karmic matter that has accumulated on it through past actions driven by passion. This bondage is not a metaphor for unhappiness or a poetic description of psychological states; it is a real condition with real effects on the soul's capacities, perceptions, and experiences. Acknowledging bondage as real is the first step toward addressing it.
*The simple version:* **The soul's current state of limitation is real.** It's not just a feeling or a metaphor — karma actually constrains the soul's freedom.

**21.4**
*Prakrit:* अत्थि य निज्जरा, अत्थि य संवरो।
*Translation:* There is the shedding of karma; there is the stopping of new karma.
*Commentary:* Two critical technical concepts are introduced: "nirjara" — the shedding or exhaustion of existing karma — and "samvara" — the stopping or blockage of new karma from entering. Both are real processes that the path is designed to produce. The path has two simultaneous effects: it exhausts what has already accumulated and it prevents further accumulation. Understanding both processes gives the practitioner a complete picture of what practice is accomplishing.
*The simple version:* **Practice does two things: it burns off old karma and it stops new karma from building up.** Both are happening at the same time.

**21.5**
*Prakrit:* अत्थि य मोक्खो जीवस्स, कम्मक्खए।
*Translation:* There is liberation for the soul — through the exhaustion of karma.
*Commentary:* Liberation is real, possible, and has a definite cause: the exhaustion of all karma. This sutra completes the foundational structure: bondage exists because karma exists; liberation exists because karma can be exhausted. The path is the process of exhaustion. There is no liberation without the complete exhaustion of karma, but there is also no complete exhaustion of karma that does not result in liberation. The connection is absolute and reliable.
*The simple version:* **Liberation is real and achievable.** When all karma is gone, the soul is free. It's that direct.

**21.6**
*Prakrit:* एए पंच अत्थिया, जिणेहिं पण्णत्ता।
*Translation:* These five existents — proclaimed by the Jinas.
*Commentary:* The five fundamental realities (soul, bondage, the shedding of karma, the stopping of karma, liberation) are identified as "astikayas" — entities that exist. The Jain tradition is philosophically committed to realism about all five: they are not merely useful concepts or psychological categories but actual features of reality that have been directly perceived by the Jinas in their state of complete liberation. The teaching is not invented but revealed — disclosed by those who have directly seen.
*The simple version:* **These five aren't philosophical theories — they're realities that the fully liberated directly perceived.** The teaching is grounded in direct knowledge.

**21.7**
*Prakrit:* जो सद्दहइ एयाई, से निग्गंथे त्ति वुच्चइ।
*Translation:* One who has faith in these — he is called a Nirgrantha.
*Commentary:* The Nirgrantha — literally "the knot-free one" — is the name the Jain tradition uses for itself. The name comes from "nir" (without) and "grantha" (knot) — one who has untied, or is untying, the knots of karmic bondage. Here the term is defined by what distinguishes a Nirgrantha: faith in the five fundamental realities. This faith is not blind assent but the orientation of the whole person toward what the Jinas have proclaimed. Having this faith is what places one within the Jain path.
*The simple version:* **A Jain is literally "one who is freeing themselves from knots."** That identity begins with genuine faith in what the liberated ones have taught.

**21.8**
*Prakrit:* न य देवकिया, न य नियइकिया, कम्मकिया।
*Translation:* Not made by a god, not made by fate — made by karma.
*Commentary:* The sutra explicitly distinguishes the Jain view from two alternative accounts of the soul's situation. It is not divinely created or determined by a god — the Jain tradition has no creator deity who dispenses good and bad fortune. It is not determined by fate — the Jain tradition rejects pure determinism. The soul's situation is karma-made: it arises from the soul's own past actions, driven by its own passions. This is simultaneously more demanding and more empowering than either alternative.
*The simple version:* **No god put you in your current situation. Fate didn't either.** Your own past actions created it — which means your own present actions can change it.

**21.9**
*Prakrit:* रागो दोसो य मोहो य, तिण्णि कम्मबंधणा।
*Translation:* Passion, aversion, and delusion — three causes of karmic bondage.
*Commentary:* The three roots of karma are named: "raga" (attachment, desire, passion), "dosa" (aversion, hostility, repulsion), and "moha" (delusion, confusion, ignorance of the soul's true nature). These three are mutually reinforcing: delusion prevents correct understanding, which allows passion and aversion to operate without being recognized for what they are. Recognizing all three is necessary; eliminating all three is liberation.
*The simple version:* **Three things bind the soul: wanting, hating, and being confused about what's real.** The path addresses all three.

**21.10**
*Prakrit:* जहा बीयं न जायइ, कम्मं खीणे न जायइ।
*Translation:* Just as a burnt seed does not germinate — when karma is exhausted, it does not arise again.
*Commentary:* The image of the burnt seed is one of the Jain tradition's most clear and evocative metaphors. A seed that has been burned cannot sprout — the generative capacity has been destroyed. Karma that has been fully exhausted through practice cannot generate new rebirth, new suffering, or new experience. This is the irreversibility of liberation: once karma is genuinely exhausted rather than merely suppressed, it is gone permanently. The exhaustion is real and complete.
*The simple version:* **Karma that's been genuinely burned away doesn't come back.** Liberation, when achieved, is permanent.

## Part II — The Path Declared (Sutras 11–22)

**21.11**
*Prakrit:* सम्मं णाणं सम्मं दंसणं, सम्मं चरित्तं।
*Translation:* Right knowledge, right faith, right conduct — the three jewels.
*Commentary:* The three jewels — triratna — are the core structure of the Jain path. They work together as an integrated system: right faith (samyak darshana) is the correct orientation of the whole person toward the teaching; right knowledge (samyak jnana) is the accurate understanding of the soul, karma, and the path; right conduct (samyak charitra) is the behavioral expression of that faith and knowledge. None alone is sufficient. Faith without knowledge is blind. Knowledge without conduct is academic. Conduct without faith and knowledge lacks direction.
*The simple version:* **Three things together make the path: believing rightly, understanding rightly, and acting rightly.** All three must be present.

**21.12**
*Prakrit:* एयाणि तिण्णि वि रयणाणि, निग्गंथाणं।
*Translation:* These three jewels — they belong to the Nirgranthas.
*Commentary:* The three jewels are the defining possession of the Jain tradition. They are what the Nirgranthas have that other traditions, despite their various valid insights, lack in their complete and correctly integrated form. This is not a claim of superiority based on cultural identity but on the precise structure of the teaching: the integration of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct as a unified system is what the Jinas have transmitted.
*The simple version:* **These three together — in their correct, integrated form — are what the Jain tradition offers.**

**21.13**
*Prakrit:* सम्मत्तं पढमं रयणं, णाणं बीयं तइयं चरित्तं।
*Translation:* Right faith is the first jewel; right knowledge the second; right conduct the third.
*Commentary:* The order of the three jewels is not incidental. Faith comes first because it is the foundation: without the correct orientation toward the teaching, knowledge and conduct will be misdirected. Knowledge comes second because it gives specific content and direction to the faith that has been established. Conduct comes third because it expresses, in daily action, the faith and knowledge that have been developed. The sequence is pedagogical — it describes how the teaching enters a person and unfolds in their life.
*The simple version:* **The order matters: first the right orientation, then understanding, then right action.** Each builds on the previous.

**21.14**
*Prakrit:* सम्मत्तविहूणो नाणं, न होइ सुद्धं।
*Translation:* Without right faith, knowledge is not pure.
*Commentary:* This sutra guards against a common error: believing that intellectual understanding alone is sufficient. Knowledge without the correct foundational orientation — right faith in the Jinas and what they have taught — can become a sophisticated form of intellectual grasping, a tool for debate and display rather than liberation. The knowledge that functions as a jewel is knowledge that arises from and serves the correct orientation toward the teaching. Otherwise it is mere information.
*The simple version:* **You can know a lot about the teaching and still not be on the path.** Knowledge has to come from the right place and serve the right purpose.

**21.15**
*Prakrit:* णाणविहूणो चरित्तं, न होइ सुद्धं।
*Translation:* Without right knowledge, conduct is not pure.
*Commentary:* The parallel point applies to conduct: action without knowledge is blind. A person can behave in ways that superficially resemble the monk's conduct while lacking the understanding that gives that conduct its correct meaning and direction. The Jain tradition has always insisted that the path requires understanding, not just behavior. Practice without understanding is superstition. Understanding without practice is merely theoretical. Both are needed, and they must be grounded in right faith.
*The simple version:* **You can act in the right ways for the wrong reasons, or without understanding what you're doing.** Conduct needs to come from genuine knowledge.

**21.16**
*Prakrit:* तम्हा तिण्णि वि एगयं, पडिवज्जेज्ज पंडिए।
*Translation:* Therefore the wise one takes up all three together.
*Commentary:* The conclusion of the argument: because each jewel requires the others, the wise practitioner takes up all three at once — not sequentially (first faith, then later knowledge, then eventually conduct) but as an integrated commitment. Entering the Jain path means entering it in its fullness: the orientation, the understanding, and the behavior all shifting together in the direction of liberation. This is not as overwhelming as it sounds; all three are present from the beginning in seed form and develop together through practice.
*The simple version:* **Don't try to take up the path one piece at a time — take it up whole.** All three aspects grow together.

**21.17**
*Prakrit:* निग्गंथाणं सुद्धो धम्मो, सव्वलोए।
*Translation:* The teaching of the Nirgranthas is pure — throughout all the world.
*Commentary:* The Jain teaching is described as "pure" — not contaminated by sectarian bias, not distorted to serve the interests of a particular group, not mixed with error. This claim of purity is grounded not in institutional authority but in the source of the teaching: the direct perception of fully liberated beings. What the Jinas have seen and transmitted is pure because they themselves are pure — freed from all the distortions that passion and karma produce.
*The simple version:* **The teaching is pure because it came from beings who were themselves completely free of the distortions that produce impure teaching.**

**21.18**
*Prakrit:* न य सो अण्णत्थ, न य सो अण्णेहिं।
*Translation:* Not found elsewhere; not taught by others.
*Commentary:* The claim is that the specific integration and precision of the Jain teaching — the three jewels in their correct form, the correct understanding of soul and karma, the correct path to liberation — is not found in this exact form in other traditions. This is not a claim that other traditions have nothing of value; it is a claim about the completeness and precision of the Nirgrantha teaching. The tradition is confident in what it has without requiring the denigration of everything else.
*The simple version:* **This specific teaching, in this complete form, comes from the Jinas.** That's the tradition's confidence — not that others have nothing, but that this has everything needed.

**21.19**
*Prakrit:* अणादिया जिणसासणा, सासया।
*Translation:* The teaching of the Jinas is without beginning; it is eternal.
*Commentary:* The Jain teaching is not new — it is not Mahavira's invention. The "Jinas" refers not only to the historical Mahavira but to the entire lineage of liberated beings who have appeared across vast stretches of cosmic time. The teaching is eternal because the realities it describes — the soul, karma, bondage, liberation — are eternal features of existence that any fully liberated being will perceive correctly. Mahavira is the latest in an infinite series of teachers, all of whom have transmitted the same truths.
*The simple version:* **The teaching is eternal because the truths it describes are eternal.** Mahavira didn't invent it — he rediscovered and retransmitted it.

**21.20**
*Prakrit:* मोक्खपहो जिणेहिं पण्णत्तो, अण्णेहिं न लब्भइ।
*Translation:* The path to liberation proclaimed by the Jinas — it is not obtained from others.
*Commentary:* The specific path to liberation — as opposed to general moral teaching or spiritual inspiration — is what the Jinas possess uniquely. Other traditions may teach goodness, kindness, and wisdom. But the precise technology of liberation — the understanding of karma and its exhaustion, the three jewels in their correct integration, the understanding of the soul's nature — is what the Jain tradition claims uniquely to possess in its complete form. This claim is the tradition's reason for existing as a distinct path.
*The simple version:* **Other teachers may have wisdom, but the complete path to liberation — as the Jains understand it — comes from the Jinas.**

**21.21**
*Prakrit:* तम्हा पडिवज्जेज्ज धम्मं, निग्गंथाणं पंडिए।
*Translation:* Therefore the wise one should take up the teaching of the Nirgranthas.
*Commentary:* The logical conclusion of the preceding argument: if the Jain teaching is correct, complete, pure, and the only path that contains everything needed for liberation, then the wise person should take it up. This is not a demand or a condemnation of those who have not taken it up; it is the teacher's earnest recommendation based on their assessment of the situation. The sutra's rhetoric is not threatening but inviting: here is what is real; here is what is complete; take it up.
*The simple version:* **Given everything that has been established — the wise move is to actually take up the path.**

**21.22**
*Prakrit:* रयणत्तयं पालंतो, निव्वाणं गच्छइ।
*Translation:* Maintaining the three jewels, one goes to liberation.
*Commentary:* "Maintaining" — this word implies not a one-time achievement but an ongoing practice. The three jewels must be maintained: kept alive, kept integrated, kept central through all the changing circumstances of monastic life. The maintenance of the three jewels is both the description of the path and its destination — liberation is what happens naturally when the three jewels are maintained completely and completely enough for all karma to be exhausted. The path and the goal are not separate.
*The simple version:* **Keep practicing all three — right faith, right knowledge, right conduct — and liberation follows.** Maintaining the practice is the path.

## Part III — Faith, Knowledge, and Conduct (Sutras 23–35)

**21.23**
*Prakrit:* सम्मत्तं किं लक्खणं, वोच्छामि जहाबुद्धि।
*Translation:* What are the marks of right faith? I will speak according to my understanding.
*Commentary:* The sutra shifts to a new mode: direct instruction and description. Having established the structure of the three jewels, the text now turns to practical description — what do these jewels actually look like in lived experience? The teacher's humility — "according to my understanding" — is itself a mark of right conduct: not claiming absolute authority for his specific description, while still speaking clearly and helpfully.
*The simple version:* **Now that we know the three jewels exist, let's describe what they actually look like in practice.**

**21.24**
*Prakrit:* जिणे य धम्मे य गणे य, सद्दहणं सम्मत्तं।
*Translation:* Right faith is trust in the Jinas, the teaching, and the community.
*Commentary:* Right faith has a specific object: the three refuges of the Jain path. The Jinas — the liberated ones who have perceived reality directly. The dhamma — the teaching as transmitted by the Jinas. The gana — the community that practices and transmits the teaching. These three are the objects of trust and orientation that constitute right faith. Without this specific grounding, the general good intentions that many people have cannot become the pointed, directed faith that serves as the first jewel of liberation.
*The simple version:* **Right faith means genuinely trusting the teacher, the teaching, and the community.** These are the three places your confidence should be anchored.

**21.25**
*Prakrit:* सम्मत्तस्स य लक्खणं, णिस्संकिय निक्कंखिय।
*Translation:* The marks of right faith: free of doubt, free of desire for the teaching's fruits.
*Commentary:* Right faith has specific qualities that distinguish it from its counterfeits. Free of doubt: not the absence of all questions but the absence of fundamental skepticism about whether the teaching is true. Free of desire for the teaching's fruits: not practicing in order to gain personal advantages — status, supernatural powers, comfort — but practicing because the path is true and liberation is real. These two marks together describe faith that is genuine rather than instrumental.
*The simple version:* **Real faith doesn't hedge against the teaching being wrong, and it doesn't practice in order to get rewards.** It's simply confident and unconditional.

**21.26**
*Prakrit:* सम्मणाणस्स य लक्खणं, जीवाजीव-पयत्थणं।
*Translation:* The mark of right knowledge: understanding the nature of the soul and matter.
*Commentary:* Right knowledge has a specific core: the distinction between soul and matter, between the conscious and the unconscious. This understanding — which Book 1's Chapter 3 explored in detail — is the intellectual foundation of the entire path. Without it, practice lacks direction. With it, practice can be oriented precisely: toward the liberation of what is soul from what is matter, toward the exhaustion of karma which is a material accumulation on the soul.
*The simple version:* **Right knowledge starts with understanding the difference between the soul and everything that isn't the soul.** That distinction is the map.

**21.27**
*Prakrit:* पंचत्थिकाय-परिण्णाणं, सम्मणाणस्स।
*Translation:* Knowledge of the five categories of existence is right knowledge.
*Commentary:* ⚠️ The five categories (panchastikaya) are the five fundamental types of existence in Jain metaphysics: soul, matter, space, time, and motion/rest. Complete right knowledge requires understanding all five in their correct relationships — not just the soul-matter distinction but the full ontological picture. The five categories together constitute the complete framework within which the soul's liberation is understood and pursued.
*The simple version:* **Right knowledge means understanding the whole picture of what exists — not just one part of it.**

**21.28**
*Prakrit:* सम्मचरित्तस्स लक्खणं, अहिंसा।
*Translation:* The mark of right conduct is non-violence.
*Commentary:* Of all the marks of right conduct, the sutra names one as primary: non-violence. This confirms what has been clear throughout the Sutrakritanga: non-violence is not one virtue among many in Jain teaching but the central, defining expression of right conduct. All other aspects of right conduct flow from and support non-violence. Truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possessiveness — all of these can be understood as different dimensions of the commitment to harmlessness.
*The simple version:* **If right conduct has one defining mark, it is non-violence.** Everything else in the behavior of the path flows from this one commitment.

**21.29**
*Prakrit:* संजमो तवो य सम्मचरित्तं, जिणाणं।
*Translation:* Restraint and austerity are right conduct, according to the Jinas.
*Commentary:* Two practical dimensions of right conduct are named: restraint (samjama) — the careful, non-passionate engagement with all action, speech, and thought — and austerity (tapas) — the intentional acceptance of hardship that burns away accumulated karma. Both are described as right conduct according to the Jinas. They are not additions to the path or special practices for advanced monks; they are the substance of right conduct itself.
*The simple version:* **Right conduct in practice means restraint in everything you do, and willingness to accept difficulty for the sake of liberation.**

**21.30**
*Prakrit:* सम्मचरित्तेण होइ, कम्माण खवणं।
*Translation:* Through right conduct there is the exhaustion of karma.
*Commentary:* The causal relationship is stated directly: right conduct produces the exhaustion of karma. The path is not complicated in its logic, though it is demanding in its execution. Right conduct — the behavioral expression of right faith and right knowledge — directly creates the conditions in which karma is exhausted. There is no shortcut, no alternative vehicle. Right conduct is the mechanism through which liberation is achieved.
*The simple version:* **Right action is what actually burns the karma.** The path works through what you do and how you do it.

**21.31**
*Prakrit:* जो सम्मत्ते ठिओ, से धम्मे ठिओ।
*Translation:* One who is established in right faith — he is established in the teaching.
*Commentary:* Faith is not a preliminary to the path but a continuous component of it. One who is genuinely established in right faith — not wobbling, not mixed with fundamental doubt, not hedging with alternative orientations — is established in the teaching itself. Right faith is not the door to the teaching but already the teaching in its first form. This means that deepening one's faith is itself a form of spiritual practice, not merely preparation for practice.
*The simple version:* **Being genuinely faithful to the teaching is itself a form of practicing the teaching.** Faith isn't just the beginning — it continues throughout.

**21.32**
*Prakrit:* जो णाणे ठिओ, सो मोक्खे णाई।
*Translation:* One who is established in right knowledge — he is approaching liberation.
*Commentary:* The progression continues: established in knowledge, approaching liberation. The relationship between right knowledge and liberation is direct and causal. Knowledge in this sense is not memorized doctrine but living understanding — the understanding that transforms how one perceives the soul, karma, and the world, and that informs every moment of practice. Such knowledge, when genuinely established, moves the practitioner toward liberation with the inevitability of a river moving toward the sea.
*The simple version:* **When understanding genuinely takes root — not just as information but as lived wisdom — liberation is the natural destination.**

**21.33**
*Prakrit:* जो चरित्ते ठिओ, से कम्मं खवेइ।
*Translation:* One who is established in right conduct — he exhausts karma.
*Commentary:* The three jewels each have their distinctive effect. Faith establishes one in the teaching. Knowledge approaches liberation. Conduct exhausts karma. Together, they produce the complete result. The monk established in all three — genuinely faithful, genuinely knowing, genuinely conducting himself with non-violence and restraint — is engaged in the complete act of liberation simultaneously on all three levels.
*The simple version:* **Right conduct is what gets the karma done.** Each jewel has its own specific effect on the path to freedom.

**21.34**
*Prakrit:* तिरयणसंपण्णो, निव्वाणमभिलसइ।
*Translation:* The one endowed with the three jewels aspires to liberation.
*Commentary:* The sutra gathers the three jewels together in the image of the practitioner who possesses all three. Such a person does not merely hope for liberation in a vague sense but aspires to it with the active longing that arises when one genuinely understands what liberation means and what bondage costs. This aspiration — born of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct — is itself a form of energy that drives practice forward.
*The simple version:* **When you have all three jewels working together, you naturally aspire to liberation — because you understand both the goal and the cost of not reaching it.**

**21.35**
*Prakrit:* जीवो अत्थि य, णत्थि य बंधो, अत्थि य मोक्खो। एयं जिणेहिं पण्णत्तं, सम्मं जो सद्दहइ सो॥ — iti bemi.
*Translation:* The soul exists; bondage exists; liberation exists. This is what the Jinas have proclaimed — one who correctly has faith in this is on the path. — Thus I say.
*Commentary:* The chapter's featured verse closes it as the final word, sealed by "iti bemi." The three existential affirmations — soul, bondage, liberation — are restated. And the single condition for being on the path is named: correct faith in these three realities. Not elaborate achievement, not years of practice, not mastery of doctrine — correct faith. The path begins with this, and in a sense it is always this: the sincere, accurate, unconditional trust in what the Jinas have seen and declared. The closing is a door left open, an invitation extended to anyone who will accept it on these terms.
*The simple version:* **The soul is real. Bondage is real. Liberation is real. The Jinas said so. Believe it correctly, and you're on the path.** That's the whole teaching, in its essential form. Thus says Mahavira.
