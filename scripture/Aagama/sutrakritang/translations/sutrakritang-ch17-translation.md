# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 17: Papasramana (False Monks / Evil Ascetics)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** Second Shrutaskandha (Book 2)
**Chapter:** 17 (Book 2, Chapter 1)
**Prakrit Title:** Papasramana
**Approximate Sutras:** 30
**Theme:** External renunciation without internal transformation produces not a monk but a danger. The chapter warns against those who wear the monk's garb while remaining slaves to passion.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> बाहिरे सावयाए, अंतरे मिच्छत्ती।
> एस पावसमणो, न से धम्मो सुचिण्णो॥

*Externally wearing the ascetic's guise, internally holding wrong views — such a one is an evil monk; his religion is not well-practiced.*

---

## Part I — The Outer Monk, Inner Householder (Sutras 1–10)

**17.1**
*Prakrit:* सुणह मे भिक्खवो धम्मं, पावसमणलक्खणं।
*Translation:* Listen to me, O monks — the characteristics of the evil ascetic.
*Commentary:* The chapter opens not with doctrine but with a warning addressed directly to monks. The teacher invites careful listening before describing what a false monk looks like. This framing is deliberate: it is the monks themselves who must be vigilant — not only about others who might claim the title falsely, but about the patterns that can emerge within their own practice. The danger of false monasticism is a danger from within the community, not only from outside it. Attentive listening is the first act of self-examination.
*The simple version:* **Pay attention — this chapter is a warning.** Not everyone who looks like a monk is one.

**17.2**
*Prakrit:* बाहिरे मुंडिए केसे, अंतरे रागसेविए।
*Translation:* Outwardly the head is shaved; inwardly one indulges in passion.
*Commentary:* The shaved head is the most visible mark of Jain renunciation — it signals that one has given up the householder's life and its vanities. But the sutra immediately cuts beneath the surface. Shaving the head is a physical act. Renouncing passion is an inner transformation. The two do not automatically accompany each other. One can shave one's head and still maintain every internal habit of the householder: craving pleasure, fearing pain, seeking status, nursing resentment. This is the definition of the false monk — the gap between the outer sign and the inner reality.
*The simple version:* **The shaved head doesn't mean the mind is empty of craving.** The outer sign must match an inner change.

**17.3**
*Prakrit:* वत्थं परिहरइ धुवं, मणसा कामभोगिणो।
*Translation:* He renounces clothing permanently, yet in mind he enjoys sensual pleasures.
*Commentary:* The Digambara practice of abandoning clothing entirely is one of the most radical acts of outer renunciation in the Jain tradition. Yet the sutra teaches that even this extreme outer act does not guarantee inner freedom. The mind can continue to dwell in sensual fantasy — imagining pleasures, planning how to obtain them, reliving past enjoyments. If the mind remains a householder's mind, then the naked body is merely an unusual outfit. The transformation required is mental, not merely physical.
*The simple version:* **You can own nothing outwardly and still be attached inwardly.** Physical renunciation without mental renunciation is incomplete.

**17.4**
*Prakrit:* अंतरे कोहसेविए, बाहिरे खमियं वएइ।
*Translation:* Inside he is driven by anger; outside he speaks of forgiveness.
*Commentary:* Forgiveness — the release of resentment and the wish for no being's harm — is a foundational Jain virtue. But the evil ascetic has learned to speak the language of forgiveness while secretly feeding anger. This is perhaps more dangerous than open anger, because it hides the infection beneath a socially acceptable surface. The community around such a person cannot see the anger. Over time the false monk may even deceive himself. This sutra identifies a specific form of spiritual hypocrisy: using virtue-language as social currency while the actual virtue is absent.
*The simple version:* **Saying "I forgive" while holding a grudge is not forgiveness.** Real forgiveness is an inner event, not a public performance.

**17.5**
*Prakrit:* माणं पोसेइ बाहिरे, सीलं भणइ मुहेण वि।
*Translation:* He nourishes pride within, yet speaks of moral discipline with his mouth.
*Commentary:* Pride — the inflated sense of one's own importance or achievements — is one of the four great passions in Jain teaching. The evil ascetic has found a particularly refined way to feed pride: he has become proud of his moral discipline. He speaks of discipline constantly, and in doing so, showcases his own supposed accomplishment. The words are correct; the inner movement they produce is poison. There is a cruel irony here that the sutra names precisely: discipline, when used as a vehicle for pride, becomes its own obstacle.
*The simple version:* **Being proud of how disciplined you are is still pride.** Talking about your renunciation can itself be a form of attachment.

**17.6**
*Prakrit:* माया मायावियस्स हु, बाहिरे साहुलक्खणं।
*Translation:* Deception belongs to the deceiver — wearing the external marks of a holy one.
*Commentary:* The word for deception here — maya — is one of the four fundamental passions in Jain thought alongside anger, pride, and greed. Maya is the tendency to misrepresent, to project a false image, to manipulate how others perceive you. The evil ascetic has made this a full-time practice: using the recognizable marks of sainthood — the gestures, the bearing, the speech patterns — as instruments of deception. His outer display is not expression but concealment.
*The simple version:* **Wearing a holy person's appearance to be seen as holy — while not being holy inside — is a form of lying.**

**17.7**
*Prakrit:* लोहं पोसेइ संसारे, भिक्खायरियं करेइ बाहिरे।
*Translation:* He nourishes greed in the world of rebirth, while externally he practices mendicancy.
*Commentary:* Mendicancy — depending on alms, owning nothing — is designed to cut the root of greed. But greed is a disposition of mind, not merely a pattern of property ownership. A monk who eats only what is offered can still be greedy: greedy for status, for admiration, for the best food, for the most comfortable sleeping place, for the attention of students. The practice of mendicancy does not automatically eliminate greed; it simply removes one vehicle through which greed can act. The inner disposition must be addressed directly.
*The simple version:* **A beggar can still be greedy.** Giving up possessions doesn't automatically end the craving for more.

**17.8**
*Prakrit:* न सो धम्मो बाहिरे, जो न पविट्ठो अंतरे।
*Translation:* That is not true religion which has not entered within — even if it is practiced outwardly.
*Commentary:* This sutra offers the chapter's governing principle in its clearest form. Religion that is only outer — only behavior, only ritual, only social performance — is not true religion. For religion to be real, it must have penetrated into the interior of the person: into intention, into feeling, into the quality of moment-to-moment awareness. Outer behavior without inner transformation may be better than nothing, but it is not yet the path. The teaching is uncompromising: unless it has gone in, it has not really been received.
*The simple version:* **Real practice changes how you feel inside, not just how you act in public.** If nothing has changed inside, the outer form is empty.

**17.9**
*Prakrit:* विसयासत्तचित्तस्स, किं पावयणस्स फलं।
*Translation:* What is the fruit of hearing the teaching, for one whose mind is attached to sense objects?
*Commentary:* This is a rhetorically sharp sutra. The question is the answer: there is no fruit. Hearing the teaching with a mind that remains attached to sensory pleasure is like pouring water into a cracked vessel — the teaching does not hold. It washes through. The sutra challenges a common spiritual error: the belief that exposure to teachings automatically produces transformation. Transformation requires that the teaching land in a receptive ground — a mind that is willing to be changed, not merely entertained.
*The simple version:* **Hearing a great teaching while your mind is still chasing pleasure produces nothing.** The soil must be ready for the seed.

**17.10**
*Prakrit:* बाहिरेण य संजमेण, अंतरे बंधणाई।
*Translation:* Through external restraint — but internally, there are still bonds.
*Commentary:* Restraint of body and speech is one part of the monk's discipline. But internal restraint — of desire, aversion, and delusion — is what actually severs karmic bonds. The false monk achieves and maintains external restraint while leaving internal bonds intact. This produces a particular kind of suffering: the body is caged while the mind remains a wild animal. External restraint without internal freedom is not liberation but a more refined form of imprisonment.
*The simple version:* **Controlling your behavior while your mind runs wild is only half-discipline.** The real restraint is of what goes on inside.

## Part II — Passions in Disguise (Sutras 11–20)

**17.11**
*Prakrit:* कोहो माणो माया लोहो, चत्तारि पावकम्मणो।
*Translation:* Anger, pride, deception, greed — these four are the causes of evil karma.
*Commentary:* The four great passions — anger, pride, deception, and greed — are the engines of karmic bondage in Jain teaching. They are the internal forces that drive harmful action. When the false monk continues to harbor these four while performing outward renunciation, he is continuing to accumulate karma even as he performs acts designed to exhaust it. This is the core tragedy: every gesture of outer purity is undermined by the inner fires still burning.
*The simple version:* **Anger, pride, deception, and greed are the four roots of karma.** A monk who still has all four has not really started the path.

**17.12**
*Prakrit:* कोहेण हणइ मित्ते, माणेण य गुरुं।
*Translation:* By anger he harms friends; by pride he harms teachers.
*Commentary:* The passions do not remain merely internal — they inevitably damage relationships. Anger directed at those who care for you breaks the bonds of friendship that sustain community. Pride directed at teachers — the people who offer wisdom and correction — prevents learning and poisons the relationship of transmission. The false monk, unable to see these patterns, blames the friends who leave and the teachers who stop teaching, never tracing the damage to its actual source.
*The simple version:* **Anger ruins friendships. Pride ruins the ability to learn from teachers.** Both destroy the relationships that make spiritual growth possible.

**17.13**
*Prakrit:* मायाए य मिच्छत्तं, लोहेण य परिग्गहं।
*Translation:* Through deception comes wrong belief; through greed comes possessiveness.
*Commentary:* Deception and greed produce specific downstream effects. Deception — the habit of misrepresenting reality — naturally leads to wrong views, because one who deceives others eventually deceives himself and loses his grip on what is true. Greed — the drive to accumulate and hold — leads directly to possessiveness, which is the opposite of the monk's essential vow of non-possession. The four passions do not stay in their lanes; they infect the entire spiritual enterprise.
*The simple version:* **Deception twists how you see reality. Greed creates the drive to own and control.** Both make the monk's path impossible.

**17.14**
*Prakrit:* एयाणि चत्तारि वि, जो न जहइ से पावो।
*Translation:* One who does not abandon these four — he is evil.
*Commentary:* The language here is direct and unsparing. Not abandoning the four passions — not working to reduce and eliminate them through practice — is what makes one a "pava," an evil or harmful person. This is not a moral condemnation in the sense of judging a person's essential worth; it is a practical designation: one who has not done the work of abandoning the passions remains a source of harm, including harm to himself. The path requires this work, not as a prerequisite, but as its substance.
*The simple version:* **If you haven't started working on the four passions, you haven't started the path.** That's not a judgment — it's a description.

**17.15**
*Prakrit:* जहा नाम समणो होइ, तहा नाम न होइ से।
*Translation:* He is called a monk, but is not one in truth.
*Commentary:* This sutra draws a clean distinction between name and reality. Having the title, wearing the marks, living in the monastery — all of these make one a monk in the social sense. But the title is not the thing. The sutra points to the gap that can open between designation and actuality. The tradition is not interested in enforcing categories; it is interested in the reality behind the category. One can be called anything and be something else entirely.
*The simple version:* **The label "monk" doesn't make someone a monk.** What matters is what's actually happening inside.

**17.16**
*Prakrit:* अहिंसाए न वट्टइ, विरहिओ सव्वसंजमे।
*Translation:* He does not abide in non-violence; he is devoid of all restraint.
*Commentary:* Non-violence — not harming any living being — is the first and most fundamental vow of Jain practice. The false monk violates this not necessarily through dramatic acts of harm but through the subtle violence of passion: the anger that damages relationships, the pride that dismisses the worth of others, the deception that manipulates those who trust him, the greed that takes more than his share. Internal violence — violence of intention and attitude — is still violence in the Jain view.
*The simple version:* **You can harm beings without touching them.** Anger, manipulation, and contempt are forms of violence too.

**17.17**
*Prakrit:* कसाएहिं य आउट्टो, बहुं हिंसइ जगे जीवे।
*Translation:* Seized by the passions, he harms many living beings in the world.
*Commentary:* The passions do not remain merely internal afflictions — they radiate outward as harm. A person governed by anger creates an atmosphere of fear and tension around them. A person governed by deception harms everyone who trusts them. A person governed by greed takes what is needed by others. The false monk, perhaps without any dramatic violent act, is continuously harmful simply through the quality of presence and action that the passions generate. Every moment governed by passion is a moment of harm to the world.
*The simple version:* **Passionate people harm others even without intending to.** The passions themselves are a form of continuous harm.

**17.18**
*Prakrit:* न सो मुक्खं लहइ जंतू, जावं कसाया न खीणा।
*Translation:* A being does not attain liberation as long as the passions are not exhausted.
*Commentary:* This sutra states the logical consequence of everything that has come before. Liberation — freedom from karma, from rebirth, from suffering — requires the complete exhaustion of the passions. Not their temporary suppression, not their redirection, not their refinement, but their exhaustion. This means the work of the path is not complete until all four passions have been worked through to their root. The false monk's performance of outer renunciation without inner work is not merely insufficient — it actively delays liberation.
*The simple version:* **No passion, no freedom.** Liberation waits until anger, pride, deception, and greed are genuinely gone.

**17.19**
*Prakrit:* तम्हा य सव्वपावेहिं, निवत्तिं कुज्ज पंडिए।
*Translation:* Therefore the wise one should turn back from all evils.
*Commentary:* The chapter does not leave the practitioner in despair. Having described the false monk's predicament in detail, it turns to the imperative: turn back. The word used here suggests a physical reversal — turning away from a direction one has been traveling in. This is accessible to anyone at any point. No matter how deep the passions have gone, the instruction remains the same: turn back. The wise one — the genuine practitioner — is the one who actually does this turning, regardless of what he has been doing before.
*The simple version:* **The solution is simply to turn around.** However far down the wrong path you've gone, the instruction is the same: turn back.

**17.20**
*Prakrit:* पावसमणो न संजओ, न य मुणी न य तावसो।
*Translation:* The evil ascetic is not restrained, not a sage, not a true renouncer.
*Commentary:* Three designations of honor are denied to the false monk in a single sutra. He is not "samyata" — restrained, self-controlled. He is not "muni" — a sage, one who has realized wisdom. He is not "tapas" — a true practitioner of austerity, one who has genuinely burned away karma through intentional hardship. Wearing the trappings of all three, he is none of them. The sutra is not cruel — it is precise. The designations belong to realities, and where the realities are absent, the designations do not apply.
*The simple version:* **The name is not the thing.** Calling yourself restrained, wise, or austere while none of those things are true is itself a form of self-deception.

## Part III — The True Monk Contrasted (Sutras 21–30)

**17.21**
*Prakrit:* जो पुण सव्वभूयेसु, दयावंतो अहिंसओ।
*Translation:* But one who has compassion for all beings and practices non-violence —
*Commentary:* The chapter now pivots. Having spent twenty sutras describing the false monk, it turns to describe the true one. The pivot is marked by "jO puna" — "but one who." The contrast is total. The true monk begins where the false one fails: with genuine compassion for all beings, not merely those who benefit him, and genuine practice of non-violence, not merely its performance. These two qualities — compassion and non-harm — are the foundation from which everything else follows.
*The simple version:* **The real monk starts from genuine care for all beings.** That feeling is the root of everything the path requires.

**17.22**
*Prakrit:* से हु समणो सुचिण्णधम्मो, पावकम्मविवज्जिओ।
*Translation:* He is truly a monk with well-practiced religion, free from evil karma.
*Commentary:* "Sucinnadhamma" — well-practiced religion — is the direct opposite of the state criticized throughout the chapter. Religion that is well-practiced is not just regularly performed but genuinely internalized. The monk who has compassion and non-violence as actual inner realities is described as "free from evil karma" — not because he is perfect, but because his orientation has genuinely shifted. The direction of his practice now moves toward exhausting karma rather than accumulating it.
*The simple version:* **Well-practiced religion is religion that has actually changed you.** It shows in the absence of the harm that passion creates.

**17.23**
*Prakrit:* जिइंदिए जियकसाए, समे सव्वत्थ संजओ।
*Translation:* Having conquered the senses, having conquered the passions, equanimous in all situations, truly restrained.
*Commentary:* Four qualities are named in sequence: conquered senses, conquered passions, equanimity, restraint. Each builds on the previous. Conquering the senses — not being pulled and pushed by what is attractive and repulsive — makes it possible to address the passions directly. Conquering the passions produces the natural equanimity that is not a mood but a settled state. From that settled state, restraint is no longer an effortful performance but an expression of what has genuinely become one's nature.
*The simple version:* **When the senses and passions are conquered, equanimity and restraint follow naturally.** You don't have to force them — they become who you are.

**17.24**
*Prakrit:* न य किंचि इच्छइ, न य किंचि दोसं करेइ।
*Translation:* He does not desire anything; he does not hold aversion toward anything.
*Commentary:* The state described here is one of complete evenness — not the flatness of someone who has suppressed all feeling, but the stability of someone who is no longer controlled by desire and aversion. This does not mean the true monk has no preferences or no discernment; it means that preferences do not harden into demands, and dislikes do not harden into hostility. He moves through the world without the friction of grasping and pushing away.
*The simple version:* **Not craving anything, not hating anything — that's the inner state the path points toward.** Everything flows smoothly from there.

**17.25**
*Prakrit:* धम्मं धम्मेण वट्टइ, सुद्धो सुद्धेण वट्टइ।
*Translation:* He practices the teaching in accordance with the teaching; pure, he acts with purity.
*Commentary:* There is a beautiful consistency described here. The monk whose inner state is aligned with the teaching practices the teaching as an expression of what he already is — not as a performance of something he is trying to become. Pure action flows from a pure source. This is the difference between discipline as effort and discipline as expression. The false monk performs discipline while being internally different; the true monk expresses discipline because it is what he actually is.
*The simple version:* **When inner and outer are aligned, practice stops being work and becomes expression.** You're not pretending to be what you already are.

**17.26**
*Prakrit:* न चेव य अत्ताणं पसंसइ, न परं विगरहइ।
*Translation:* He does not praise himself; he does not condemn others.
*Commentary:* Self-praise and condemnation of others are among the most common behaviors of pride. The false monk is defined in part by his tendency to elevate himself and diminish others — comparing his austerities favorably, noting others' failings. The true monk does neither. This is not modesty as a social pose; it is the genuine absence of the comparative mind that needs to establish its own superiority. Without that need, there is nothing to self-praise about and nothing to condemn others for.
*The simple version:* **The true monk doesn't brag about himself or criticize others.** That impulse just doesn't arise when pride has been genuinely worked through.

**17.27**
*Prakrit:* सव्वे जीवे दयाभावे, जहा अप्पाणं तहा परे।
*Translation:* Regarding all living beings with compassion — as oneself, so others.
*Commentary:* "As oneself, so others" — this is the golden rule in its Jain form. The monk who has genuinely understood the nature of soul — that every living being is a soul, as real and as deserving of liberation as one's own — naturally extends compassion in all directions. This is not a moral rule imposed from outside but the natural expression of correct knowledge. When one truly understands that all souls are equal, treating others with care follows as naturally as treating oneself with care.
*The simple version:* **The deepest reason for treating all beings well is this: they are exactly like you.** Every living being wants freedom from pain, just as you do.

**17.28**
*Prakrit:* एयं लक्खणं साहुस्स, न बाहिरं रूवं।
*Translation:* This is the mark of a true monk — not outer form.
*Commentary:* The chapter's central teaching is restated with maximum clarity: the mark of genuine monasticism is not external appearance but inner quality. All the outer marks — the robe, the shaved head, the begging bowl, the gestures of reverence — are secondary to this one question: is there genuine compassion, genuine non-violence, genuine freedom from the four passions? If yes, there is a monk. If no, there is a performance.
*The simple version:* **The real mark of a monk is what's happening inside.** Outer signs can be faked; inner transformation cannot.

**17.29**
*Prakrit:* जो य एयं जाणइ धम्मं, से य धम्मे पइट्ठिए।
*Translation:* One who knows this teaching — he is established in the teaching.
*Commentary:* Knowledge of this distinction — between outer and inner, between performance and transformation, between the false monk and the true — is itself a kind of establishment in the path. To know clearly that outward marks are insufficient, and to direct one's effort accordingly, is to take up one's position in the actual teaching rather than its shadow. Many hear teachings without being changed; the one who is changed by what they hear is the one who has truly received it.
*The simple version:* **Understanding the difference between real practice and fake practice is itself the beginning of real practice.**

**17.30**
*Prakrit:* बाहिरे सावयाए, अंतरे मिच्छत्ती। एस पावसमणो, न से धम्मो सुचिण्णो॥ — iti bemi.
*Translation:* Externally wearing the ascetic's guise, internally holding wrong views — such a one is an evil monk; his religion is not well-practiced. — Thus I say.
*Commentary:* The chapter closes with the same verse that opened its featured teaching, now placed as the final word — an emphatic and crystalline summary. The repetition is intentional: these words have framed the entire chapter, and they close it with the same force. The "iti bemi" — "thus I say" — is the traditional closing formula attributed to Mahavira himself, indicating that this teaching comes from the highest source and is to be taken with full seriousness. The false monk's defining characteristic is this gap: outer guise, inner wrong view. The entire chapter has been an extended commentary on this single verse.
*The simple version:* **The conclusion is exactly the same as the beginning: outer form without inner truth is not religion.** This is what Mahavira himself says.
