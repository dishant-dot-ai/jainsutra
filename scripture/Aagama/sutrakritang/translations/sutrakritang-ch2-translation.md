# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 2: Vedaniyam (Sensation / Karma-Ripening)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** First Shrutaskandha  
**Chapter:** 2  
**Prakrit Title:** Vedaniyam  
**Approximate Sutras:** 25  
**Theme:** Karma produces sensation; the wise monk cultivates equanimity toward both pleasure and pain.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> जे य दुक्खा जे य सुहा, ते य कम्मसमुप्पया।  
> तेसिं परिण्णाय धीरो, समाहितो न हासए॥

*Whatever pains and whatever pleasures arise — they are the results of karma. The wise one, having understood them, remains composed and does not grieve.*

---

## Part I — The Nature of Sensation (Sutras 1–10)

**2.1**  
*Prakrit:* जे केइ दुक्खा संसारे, ते सव्वे कम्मसंभवा।  
*Translation:* Whatever sufferings exist in the world — all of them arise from karma.  
*Commentary:* The Sutra opens by anchoring suffering in its cause: karma. Nothing that hurts is random, accidental, or divinely willed. Every form of pain — physical, emotional, circumstantial — is the ripening of past action. This is not fatalism but diagnosis. Understanding the cause correctly is the first step toward eliminating it. The teacher places the entire domain of experience within the framework of cause and effect, which is the Jain philosophical foundation.  
*The simple version:* **All suffering has a reason — past actions ripening into present experience.** Nothing painful is meaningless or random.

**2.2**  
*Prakrit:* जे केइ सुहा संसारे, ते वि कम्मसमुप्पया।  
*Translation:* Whatever pleasures exist in the world — they too arise from karma.  
*Commentary:* The teaching is symmetric: pleasure is also karma-born. This is the point that distinguishes Jain teaching from ordinary religious thinking, which often treats pleasure as divine reward. Pleasure is not the goal; it is simply the ripening of merit-karma. Since it arose from karma, it will also end when that karma is exhausted. The wise person sees pleasure exactly as they see pain — as a passing result of past cause.  
*The simple version:* **Pleasures are also karma ripening.** They are not rewards to pursue — they are temporary results that will pass.

**2.3**  
*Prakrit:* तम्हा वेयणं जाणइ, धीरो लोगस्स पारगू।  
*Translation:* Therefore the wise one who has crossed to the other shore understands sensation.  
*Commentary:* "Crossing to the other shore" is a classical metaphor for liberation — moving from the turbulent near bank of worldly existence to the stable far shore of freedom. The one who has truly crossed understands sensation for what it is: a result, not a reality to cling to or flee. This understanding is not intellectual alone; it changes how the monk lives, breathes, and responds to everything that happens to the body and mind.  
*The simple version:* **True understanding of sensation means not being controlled by it.** The wise person sees sensation as a guest — it arrives, it departs.

**2.4**  
*Prakrit:* सुहं वा जइ वा दुक्खं, तं तितिक्खेज्ज पंडिए।  
*Translation:* Whether it is pleasure or pain, the learned one should endure it.  
*Commentary:* The word "endure" here does not mean passive suffering. It means active, aware tolerance — the capacity to stay present with sensation without being swept away by it. The word used for "learned" connects this to knowledge: the one who truly knows endures, because they understand what they are enduring. Endurance without understanding is mere stoicism. Endurance with understanding is practice.  
*The simple version:* **The monk's job is to stay steady through both pleasure and pain,** not suppressing sensation but not chasing or fleeing it either.

**2.5**  
*Prakrit:* जहा पुरिसे वाहिए रोगेण, कुसले वेज्जे चिकिच्छए।  
*Translation:* Just as a man afflicted by disease is treated by a skilled physician — so too the soul afflicted by karma is treated by understanding.  
*Commentary:* The metaphor of the physician is powerful in Jain teaching. The teacher is the physician, the teaching is the medicine, and karma is the disease. A skilled physician does not deny the disease exists, does not pretend it is something other than what it is, and does not simply offer comfort — the physician diagnoses and treats. Likewise, the path to liberation requires correct diagnosis: sensation is karma-born, karma is caused by attachment, attachment is ended by understanding and restraint.  
*The simple version:* **The monk approaches suffering like a good doctor approaches illness** — not by hiding from it, but by understanding its cause and treating it at the root.

**2.6**  
*Prakrit:* से जाणइ दुक्खस्स मूलं, कम्मं जाणइ बंधणं।  
*Translation:* He knows the root of suffering; he knows karma as bondage.  
*Commentary:* Knowledge is operational here — it is not theoretical awareness but the understanding that changes behavior. To know that suffering has karma as its root is to know that creating more karma will create more suffering. To know karma as bondage is to stop adding to one's chains. This sutra marks the transition from diagnosis to practice: once the cause is known, the path is clear.  
*The simple version:* **Knowing the root of suffering — karma — changes how you live.** You stop adding to the very thing that causes pain.

**2.7**  
*Prakrit:* न सोयइ न हरिसइ, समलोट्ठकंचणे।  
*Translation:* He does not grieve nor does he rejoice — he sees a lump of clay and a lump of gold as equal.  
*Commentary:* The image of clay and gold is among the most important in Jain renunciant teaching. Gold is what the world values most; clay is what the world considers worthless. The monk who has genuinely understood the nature of sensation does not assign these values. Not because both are worthless — but because both are equally impermanent, equally material, equally unable to touch the soul's nature. This equanimity is not indifference; it is clarity.  
*The simple version:* **The monk doesn't get excited by gold or depressed by mud.** He sees through the labels the world puts on things.

**2.8**  
*Prakrit:* अट्ठिमंसचम्मपत्थडे, देहे नत्थि ममत्तणं।  
*Translation:* In the body — a framework of bone, flesh, and skin — there is no ownership.  
*Commentary:* The body is described anatomically and without sentiment. It is a temporary structure, not a self. The monk's non-attachment to the body is not self-hatred — it is accurate perception. The body will become ill, will age, will die. The one who identifies deeply with the body will suffer proportionally with every change the body undergoes. The monk who sees the body clearly is free to use it well without being enslaved to it.  
*The simple version:* **The body is a temporary structure.** The monk uses it without clinging to it.

**2.9**  
*Prakrit:* जे इंदिएहिं विजिए, से न विज्जइ वेयणाए।  
*Translation:* One who has conquered the senses is not overwhelmed by sensation.  
*Commentary:* Conquest of the senses does not mean destroying them — the senses continue to function in a realized monk. It means the monk is not driven by them. When the eyes see, the monk is not pulled by craving. When the body feels pain, the monk is not shattered by it. This freedom from being driven by the senses is what makes endurance of sensation possible. Sensation continues; enslavement to sensation ends.  
*The simple version:* **Mastering the senses means sensation doesn't master you.** Pain and pleasure still happen — but they don't run your life.

**2.10**  
*Prakrit:* अग्गिणा जलइ कट्ठं, कम्मं सो वि झाविए।  
*Translation:* Just as fire burns away wood — so too does the monk burn away his karma.  
*Commentary:* This is a central metaphor in Jain soteriology. Karma accumulates like wood — it is fuel for the fire of future experience. The ascetic's practice — endurance, non-attachment, restraint — is itself a kind of fire that burns the accumulated karma without adding new fuel. The monk who endures sensation without craving or aversion is actually consuming their karmic fuel faster than they are adding to it. This is the mechanics of liberation.  
*The simple version:* **Every moment of patient endurance burns away old karma.** The monk's practice is a controlled fire that clears the fuel of past action.

## Part II — The Monk's Response (Sutras 11–18)

**2.11**  
*Prakrit:* आउरस्स न भे दुक्खं, ण य हिट्ठइ वेयणा।  
*Translation:* For one who is immersed in practice, there is no fear of suffering; sensation does not disturb him.  
*Commentary:* "Immersed in practice" describes a monk who has made restraint habitual — not through gritting teeth but through genuine internalization. For such a one, suffering loses its sting. This is not numbness; it is the result of years of practice in which the monk has learned to meet sensation without the reactive layer of fear and craving. When that reactive layer is gone, sensation itself is neutral — a signal, not a threat.  
*The simple version:* **Steady practice removes the fear from suffering.** The monk who has trained well can face pain without panic.

**2.12**  
*Prakrit:* परिस्सहे य अभिभूए, न वित्तिं अज्जुवागए।  
*Translation:* Overpowered by hardship, he does not seek escape into comfort.  
*Commentary:* The Jain path specifically trains the monk against the impulse to seek relief. When the body is cold, the monk does not rush to warmth. When hungry, the monk does not break the fast early. This is not cruelty to the body — it is training the mind to observe the impulse to escape without immediately acting on it. Over time this builds a kind of inner space between impulse and action, and liberation is found in that space.  
*The simple version:* **When things are hard, the monk doesn't immediately run toward comfort.** He trains himself to stay steady.

**2.13**  
*Prakrit:* न रोसेइ न पसीयइ, समसुहदुक्खे विहरइ।  
*Translation:* He does not become angry nor does he become pleased — he lives with pleasure and pain treated equally.  
*Commentary:* This sutra describes the lived reality of equanimity. It is not that the monk feels nothing — the text does not claim that. It is that his inner state does not spike into anger when things go badly or into elation when things go well. He inhabits a stable middle ground. This stability is itself transformative: it allows clear seeing, compassionate action, and the absence of the karmically binding reactions of rage and greed.  
*The simple version:* **The monk doesn't fly into anger or get swept up in excitement.** He stays even — and that evenness itself is liberation in progress.

**2.14**  
*Prakrit:* आहारगहणे पमत्ते, तस्स बंधो न विज्जइ।  
*Translation:* In taking food without heedlessness, bondage does not arise for him.  
*Commentary:* Even something as ordinary as eating is a site of practice. Taking food with awareness — eating because the body needs fuel, not because the mind craves pleasure — is a form of non-attachment in action. Heedlessness in eating means eating driven by craving, by habit, by distraction. The monk eats with full attention, knowing why he eats and eating accordingly. No new karma is generated by this conscious, minimal, purposeful action.  
*The simple version:* **How you eat matters as much as what you eat.** Eating with awareness and without craving generates no new bondage.

**2.15**  
*Prakrit:* सव्वे पाणा न हंतव्वा, न य वाए वियाहिया।  
*Translation:* No living beings should be harmed; this is what the Lord declared.  
*Commentary:* Non-violence is the foundational practice that underlies all the others. It is placed here in the middle of the chapter on sensation because the insight connects: if all sensation arises from karma, and if karma is generated through harmful action, then the most direct way to stop accumulating painful karma is to stop harming. Non-violence is not an ethical ideal added on top of the path — it is structurally required by the karma theory that the whole chapter rests on.  
*The simple version:* **Non-violence is the practical consequence of understanding karma.** If harm creates karma, and karma creates suffering, then the monk who understands this stops harming.

**2.16**  
*Prakrit:* एगे नाणी समभावे, दुक्खं सहइ अक्खोभे।  
*Translation:* The one who is truly wise remains in equanimity, enduring suffering without agitation.  
*Commentary:* "Without agitation" is precise: the monk's surface may be still, but the sutra points to the absence of inner turbulence. Agitation is the internal storm of resistance — the part of us that says "this should not be happening." When that storm is calmed by understanding, what remains is the capacity to endure fully without breaking. This is not heroic suppression; it is the natural result of having understood the nature of sensation.  
*The simple version:* **Real wisdom shows up in how calmly you face hard things.** The monk's inner stillness is not performance — it is understanding made visible.

**2.17**  
*Prakrit:* जे य इट्ठा जे य अणिट्ठा, सव्वे कम्मस्स फलिया।  
*Translation:* Whatever is pleasant and whatever is unpleasant — both are the fruits of karma.  
*Commentary:* The sutra restates the core teaching with deliberate symmetry: both poles of experience, the attractive and the aversive, are equally karma-born. This symmetry is the teaching. Most people flee unpleasantness and pursue pleasantness. The monk who truly understands both are karma-born has no need to flee or pursue. Both will arise according to their karmic causes. Both will pass when those causes are exhausted. The monk can simply be present.  
*The simple version:* **Pleasures and pains are both just karma playing out.** Neither is worth chasing; neither is worth running from.

**2.18**  
*Prakrit:* जे धीरे से ण संरंभे, तितिक्खाए उवट्ठिए।  
*Translation:* The steadfast one is not rash — he stands established in endurance.  
*Commentary:* Rashness is the failure mode of impatience: acting impulsively to escape a difficult sensation, or to grasp at a pleasant one. The monk who is "established in endurance" has made endurance not a temporary strategy but a stable orientation. He is steady because steadiness has become his natural posture, not because he is suppressing an impulse to act otherwise. This is the fruit of long practice.  
*The simple version:* **The trained monk doesn't react rashly.** His patience is not effort — it has become who he is.

## Part III — Liberation from Sensation (Sutras 19–25)

**2.19**  
*Prakrit:* जो य वेयणं जाणइ, से मोक्खस्स पारगे।  
*Translation:* The one who truly knows sensation has reached the shore of liberation.  
*Commentary:* "Truly knows" is doing all the work here. Many people know intellectually that sensation is impermanent. Few have so thoroughly understood this that they no longer react to sensation with craving and aversion. The one who has reached this lived knowledge — not just the concept but the transformation — is already at the threshold of liberation. Knowledge of sensation, at this depth, is itself the liberating insight.  
*The simple version:* **Really understanding sensation — not just knowing about it — is itself liberation.** The insight goes all the way through.

**2.20**  
*Prakrit:* वेयणा खविया जस्स, न से पुणो उवज्जइ।  
*Translation:* For one whose sensation-karma has been exhausted, it does not arise again.  
*Commentary:* This sutra describes the mechanics of final liberation. Karma is exhausted — completely burnt away — through practice without the addition of new karma. When the last of the sensation-producing karma is consumed, sensation as a vehicle of bondage ends. The soul does not become incapable of experience in liberation; rather, it is freed from the compelled experience of karmic ripening. This is the highest aim of the path.  
*The simple version:* **When all past karma burns away, the cycle ends.** No more compelled sensation — just the soul in its pure nature.

**2.21**  
*Prakrit:* अप्पाणं संजमे धीरो, सुद्धभावेण भावए।  
*Translation:* The wise one restrains himself and cultivates purity of being.  
*Commentary:* Restraint and purity are the two practical pillars described here. Restraint means the active practice of not adding karma through harmful thoughts, speech, and action. Purity of being means cultivating the soul's inherent qualities — clarity, peace, compassion — rather than the reactive patterns of attachment and aversion. Together, these practices create the conditions in which liberation becomes possible.  
*The simple version:* **The monk's practice has two sides: stopping harm and growing purity.** Both together move him toward freedom.

**2.22**  
*Prakrit:* न भाए न य उव्वेगे, न रागे न विसाए।  
*Translation:* Neither fear nor anxiety, neither attachment nor despair — he lives beyond these.  
*Commentary:* This sutra catalogs the four reactions that normally accompany sensation: fear of pain, anxiety about what may come, attachment to pleasure, and despair when pleasure ends. The monk who has genuinely understood sensation has moved beyond all four. These are not suppressed — they have been understood so clearly that they no longer arise. This is the emotional freedom that comes with the deep knowledge described in the previous sutras.  
*The simple version:* **When you truly understand sensation, the emotional turbulence it normally causes simply stops.** Not by force — by understanding.

**2.23**  
*Prakrit:* जो य भिक्खू परिण्णाय, कम्मं न संचिणाइ।  
*Translation:* The monk who has fully understood does not accumulate karma.  
*Commentary:* Non-accumulation of karma is the practical outcome of understanding. Every action taken without craving and aversion generates no new binding karma. The monk who has internalized the teaching of this chapter — that all sensation is karma-born, that sensation is to be endured with equanimity, that endurance itself burns karma — is no longer adding to his burden. He is only consuming the old karma without creating new karma, moving steadily toward freedom.  
*The simple version:* **Real understanding stops the accumulation of new karma.** The monk lives without adding to his debt.

**2.24**  
*Prakrit:* सव्वदुक्खाण मोक्खस्स, मग्गो एसो वियाहिए।  
*Translation:* This is declared to be the path to liberation from all suffering.  
*Commentary:* The sutra explicitly names what the entire chapter has been building toward: this path of understanding sensation, cultivating equanimity, enduring without craving or aversion — this is the path to complete liberation from all forms of suffering. Not a path among many options, but the path as taught by the Tirthankara himself. The declaration carries the weight of the entire Jain philosophical tradition.  
*The simple version:* **Everything taught in this chapter is the path to freedom.** This is not theory — this is the way.

**2.25**  
*Prakrit:* एवं वेयणियं नाऊण, जाणिया सव्वसंपहू।  
*Translation:* Thus, having understood the chapter on sensation, one knows all things fully. — iti bemi  
*Commentary:* The closing formula "iti bemi" — "thus I speak" — is the traditional seal placed at the end of a Jain sutra, affirming that what has been taught comes from the tradition of the Tirthankaras as transmitted through the line of teachers. The sutra also makes a remarkable claim: understanding this chapter on sensation is a kind of complete understanding. This is because sensation is the interface between karma and the soul — understanding it correctly means understanding the whole mechanism of bondage and liberation.  
*The simple version:* **This chapter is complete.** The teacher has spoken. Whoever truly understands sensation understands the whole teaching.
