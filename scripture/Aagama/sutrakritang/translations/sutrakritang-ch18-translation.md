# Sutrakritanga Sutra — Chapter 18: Raniyam (The Queen's Path / The Way of Queen Mrigavati)
## Translation and Commentary

**Book:** Second Shrutaskandha (Book 2)
**Chapter:** 18 (Book 2, Chapter 2)
**Prakrit Title:** Raniyam
**Approximate Sutras:** 25
**Theme:** High social status is neither an obstacle nor an advantage on the spiritual path. What matters is the quality of one's inner practice. The queen who genuinely renounces is closer to liberation than the king who performs elaborate rituals.

---

### Featured Prakrit Verse

> रायलच्छी ण मोक्खो, तवेण य संजमेण य।
> एत्थ वट्टंतो सो मुच्चइ, जो सम्मं पडिवज्जए॥

*Royal wealth is not liberation — through austerity and restraint one is freed; one who correctly takes up the path is released.*

---

## Part I — The Queen Hears the Teaching (Sutras 1–8)

**18.1**
*Prakrit:* रायगिहे नयरे आसी, मिगावई नाम रायिणी।
*Translation:* In the city of Rajagriha there lived a queen named Mrigavati.
*Commentary:* The chapter opens with a specific place — Rajagriha, one of the great cities of ancient India and a recurring setting for important teachings in both Jain and Buddhist texts — and a specific person: Queen Mrigavati. The specificity is theologically significant. The teaching that follows is not abstract; it was received by a real person in a real place. This grounding in narrative makes the chapter unusual among the Sutrakritanga's more doctrinal material. The story form is being used here to carry philosophical content.
*The simple version:* **The teaching comes alive through a real person's story.** Philosophy embedded in narrative lands differently than philosophy stated abstractly.

**18.2**
*Prakrit:* सा बहुरयणसमिद्धा, सुक्खभोगेसु निव्विण्णा।
*Translation:* She possessed great wealth and riches, yet she had grown weary of sensual enjoyments.
*Commentary:* This sutra establishes the dramatic situation. Mrigavati is not a poor woman dreaming of something better; she has everything the world considers desirable. And she has found it insufficient. Weariness with pleasure is not the same as failure to obtain pleasure; it is the saturation point, the moment when one has had enough of the cycle to see it clearly. In Jain teaching, this disillusionment — "nirvinna" — is itself a kind of grace, the opening through which the teaching can enter.
*The simple version:* **Having everything the world offers and finding it hollow — that is the moment when real practice becomes possible.** Mrigavati reached that moment.

**18.3**
*Prakrit:* सा सुणेइ भगवंतं, महावीरं जिणं जगे।
*Translation:* She heard of the Blessed One — Mahavira, the Conqueror, in the world.
*Commentary:* The queen's path begins with hearing. This is the classical Jain framework for receiving the teaching: first hearing (shruta), then understanding (jnana), then practice (charitra). Hearing the existence and presence of Mahavira — a being who has crossed the ocean of existence — produces in Mrigavati what her wealth and pleasures could not: genuine longing for something real. The Conqueror is one who has conquered not armies but the passions; and the queen who has grown weary of the world is ready to hear what such a conquest requires.
*The simple version:* **Hearing about someone who has genuinely achieved inner freedom changes something.** For Mrigavati, it opened the door.

**18.4**
*Prakrit:* सा गया तस्स सभाए, सुणेउं धम्मं सुंदरं।
*Translation:* She went to his assembly to hear the beautiful teaching.
*Commentary:* The queen acts immediately on her longing. She does not wait, deliberate, or ask permission. She goes to the assembly — the open gathering where Mahavira's teaching could be heard by anyone who came. This is a social fact of significance: the teaching was available to the queen on the same terms as a farmer or a beggar. No special audience, no royal privilege. She sat in the assembly as a seeker, setting aside — for the moment of hearing — everything her social position usually meant.
*The simple version:* **She went to hear the teaching not as a queen, but as a seeker.** That shift — from status to seeking — is itself a spiritual act.

**18.5**
*Prakrit:* सुणिया य धम्मं, पडिलाभिया य भगवंतेण।
*Translation:* Having heard the teaching and having been blessed by the Blessed One —
*Commentary:* The sutra describes a double event: hearing the teaching and receiving the blessing of the teacher's attention and recognition. In Jain teaching, the blessing of a fully liberated teacher is not magical but communicative: it conveys, through the quality of presence, something of what liberation actually feels like. Mahavira's acknowledgment of Mrigavati is not favoritism — he taught whoever came — but it is transformative, because her readiness allowed the teaching to take full effect.
*The simple version:* **The teaching and the teacher work together.** She heard the words, and she received something from the quality of his presence.

**18.6**
*Prakrit:* सा चिंतेइ एत्थ, रायलच्छी नत्थि मे सरणं।
*Translation:* She reflected: "This royal wealth is not my refuge."
*Commentary:* This is the moment of genuine turning. Mrigavati's reflection is precise: she does not say royal wealth is bad, or evil, or that she is ashamed of it. She says it is not her refuge — not the place of lasting safety and freedom that she is looking for. The Jain tradition makes this distinction carefully. Worldly things are not condemned absolutely; they are identified as insufficient. They can provide comfort but not refuge. Refuge requires something that does not change, deteriorate, or run out.
*The simple version:* **She wasn't hating wealth — she was recognizing its limits.** Wealth can give comfort; it cannot give freedom.

**18.7**
*Prakrit:* तव-संजम-सीलेण, सा निवट्टइ संसाराओ।
*Translation:* Through austerity, restraint, and moral discipline, she turns back from the cycle of existence.
*Commentary:* The three instruments of liberation are named: austerity (the intentional hardship that burns karma), restraint (the careful non-violence in action, speech, and mind), and moral discipline (the systematic ethical framework that guides behavior). These three, working together, constitute the practical path. They are not ends in themselves but vehicles. Mrigavati's turning back from the cycle of existence is not a single dramatic act but the cumulative effect of sustained practice across all three dimensions.
*The simple version:* **Austerity, restraint, and discipline — these three together are how the cycle of rebirth gets reversed.** None alone is sufficient; all three together are the path.

**18.8**
*Prakrit:* एसा राइणी सूरा, पडिवण्णा जिणवयणे।
*Translation:* This queen is truly courageous — she has entered the teaching of the Conqueror.
*Commentary:* The sutra names the queen's act as courageous. This is meaningful: to take up the Jain path from a position of wealth and social power is genuinely difficult. Those who have much to give up face a different kind of test than those who have little. Mrigavati's courage is the courage to release a grip that most people would tighten. Entering the teaching of the Conqueror means committing to a path whose demands are real and whose rewards are not of the kind the world recognizes.
*The simple version:* **Courage isn't only for people who have nothing to lose.** Sometimes it takes more courage to give up a lot than to give up a little.

## Part II — The Path Is Open to All (Sutras 9–17)

**18.9**
*Prakrit:* न जाई न कुलं न बलं, न य रूवं न य वयं।
*Translation:* Not birth, not family, not strength, not beauty, not age —
*Commentary:* This sutra begins a systematic dismantling of the categories that ordinary society uses to determine spiritual qualification. None of these — birth caste, family prestige, physical strength, physical beauty, age — determines whether one can enter the path or how far one can progress on it. This is a radical statement in a social context where all of these things conferred or denied access to religious life. The teaching strips away every conventional barrier to entry.
*The simple version:* **None of the things society uses to rank people matter on the spiritual path.** Not your caste, your looks, your age, or your connections.

**18.10**
*Prakrit:* न वित्तं न य राज्जं वा, मोक्खस्स कारणं।
*Translation:* Not wealth, not kingdom — these are not causes of liberation.
*Commentary:* The sutra completes the thought begun in 18.9 by adding wealth and political power to the list of things that do not produce liberation. This is directed precisely at the social category Mrigavati represents: the wealthy and powerful. The queen's situation is not an advantage on the path — but crucially, it is also not a disqualification. The teaching is neutral on social position: it neither requires wealth nor requires poverty. It requires practice.
*The simple version:* **Being rich and powerful doesn't help you get liberated.** But it doesn't prevent it either. Status simply isn't relevant.

**18.11**
*Prakrit:* कम्मं हणंतो य विसेसेण, निव्वाणं समभिलसइ।
*Translation:* Destroying karma in particular ways, one aspires to liberation.
*Commentary:* The path to liberation is specific: it involves the deliberate exhaustion of accumulated karma through practice, and the prevention of new karma accumulation through careful, non-passionate action. The word "visesena" — "in particular ways" — points to the importance of method. Not all effort is equally effective. The teaching provides a specific framework for destroying karma most efficiently: the three jewels of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct.
*The simple version:* **Liberation requires knowing how karma works and working on it directly.** Vague spiritual striving without a method doesn't get there.

**18.12**
*Prakrit:* राया वि मुच्चइ एत्थ, दासो वि मुच्चइ एत्थ।
*Translation:* A king can be freed through this; a slave can be freed through this.
*Commentary:* The sutra makes the point with maximum directness and rhetorical force. Not "a king might be freed if conditions are right" — a king can be freed. Not "a slave could be freed in theory" — a slave can be freed. Both the most socially elevated and the most socially diminished have equal access to the path. This is not an idealized claim but a practical one: the karma-exhaustion process works the same way regardless of the social position in which one's body finds itself.
*The simple version:* **The path is equally available to a king and a slave.** Social position is the background, not the story.

**18.13**
*Prakrit:* इत्थी वि मुच्चइ एत्थ, पुरिसो वि मुच्चइ एत्थ।
*Translation:* A woman can be freed through this; a man can be freed through this.
*Commentary:* The Sutrakritanga explicitly includes women's liberation here, which is theologically significant. In many ancient Indian traditions, women's spiritual liberation was considered possible only after rebirth as a man. The Jain text affirms directly that women can achieve liberation through this path — here, in this birth, without requiring a prior reincarnation as a male. Mrigavati's story makes this point through narrative; this sutra makes it through direct statement.
*The simple version:* **Women and men have equal access to liberation in Jain teaching.** This is stated plainly: both can be freed.

**18.14**
*Prakrit:* गिहिणी वि पडिवज्जइ, पव्वइया वि पडिवज्जइ।
*Translation:* A householder woman can take up the path; an ordained nun can take up the path.
*Commentary:* The sutra further specifies that even within the category of women, the path is open across different forms of life. A woman does not have to become a full-fledged nun to practice; a householder woman who takes up the vows appropriate to lay practice is also "on the path." This reflects the Jain conception of the four-fold community — monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen — all of whom are practicing within the same framework, at different levels of intensity.
*The simple version:* **You don't have to become a monk or nun to be on the path.** Both householder practice and monastic practice are genuine.

**18.15**
*Prakrit:* धम्मे समाहिए चित्तं, से जाणइ भवपारगं।
*Translation:* With mind settled in the teaching, he knows the one who has crossed the ocean of existence.
*Commentary:* "Crossing the ocean of existence" is a classical metaphor for liberation — the achievement of the far shore of freedom from karma and rebirth. The sutra describes the relationship between settled practice and recognition of the liberated one: when the mind is genuinely established in the teaching, one can recognize a truly liberated being. This is a practical point: only a practitioner can fully appreciate a teacher. Surface encounter with a teacher is transformed by depth of practice.
*The simple version:* **You understand the teacher in proportion to how deeply you practice.** Practice opens the eyes.

**18.16**
*Prakrit:* न य कुलं न जाई य, न य लिंगं मुणीणं।
*Translation:* Neither family, nor birth caste, nor outer appearance mark the sage.
*Commentary:* The word "linga" — outer appearance, mark, or sign — is used deliberately here. It covers the entire range of external indicators: the robe, the style of renunciation, the physical appearance, the caste background from which the monk came. None of these identify the true sage. Having established (in Chapter 17) that outer marks can be false indicators of inner reality, the chapter here reaffirms: the sage is not defined by any of the external signs that ordinary people use to recognize a holy person.
*The simple version:* **You can't identify a true sage by looking at them.** The inner quality matters, not the outer presentation.

**18.17**
*Prakrit:* तवसंजमेण सुद्धो, सुचित्तो जिणसासणे।
*Translation:* Purified by austerity and restraint, with clear mind in the Jina's teaching —
*Commentary:* The two main instruments of purification are named again: austerity and restraint. Austerity — the intentional acceptance of hardship — burns away accumulated karma. Restraint — the careful non-passionate engagement with action — prevents new karma from forming. Together they produce purification. And purification produces clarity of mind — "shuchitta" — a mind that is no longer clouded by the stains of passion and wrong view. In that clarity, the Jina's teaching is not heard but seen.
*The simple version:* **Austerity and restraint clean the mind.** When the mind is clean, understanding comes naturally.

## Part III — The Practice (Sutras 18–25)

**18.18**
*Prakrit:* जो धम्मं धारेइ सव्वं, सो समणो इह उच्चइ।
*Translation:* One who upholds the entire teaching — he is called a monk in this world.
*Commentary:* The standard for being called a monk is stated plainly: upholding the entire teaching. Not selected parts, not the convenient portions, not the impressive-looking elements — the entire teaching. This is demanding because the teaching includes not only the dramatic elements of renunciation (giving up possessions, etc.) but the subtle ones: equanimity in all circumstances, compassion for all beings, absolute honesty, complete non-possessiveness in body and mind. Upholding all of this is what the title "monk" actually designates.
*The simple version:* **A monk is someone who actually practices all of it — not just the parts that are easy or impressive.**

**18.19**
*Prakrit:* जहा कमलं जले जायं, न य लिप्पइ जलेण।
*Translation:* Just as the lotus is born in water, yet is not stained by water —
*Commentary:* The lotus in the water is one of the most ancient and durable images in Indian philosophy. The lotus grows in muddy water, yet rises above it and blooms without being touched by the mud. Applied here, the image describes the monk who lives in the world — receives food from the world, depends on the world, moves through worldly situations — yet is not stained by the world's qualities of passion and attachment. Presence in the world without corruption by the world is the mark of genuine practice.
*The simple version:* **You can live in the world without being defined by it.** The lotus is in the mud, but the mud is not in the lotus.

**18.20**
*Prakrit:* एवं साहू य साहुणी य, संसारे न य लिप्पंति।
*Translation:* In this way, monks and nuns are not stained by the cycle of existence.
*Commentary:* The lotus simile is applied directly to the monastic community. Monks and nuns live in the world — they move through it, depend on its householders for food, teach within its communities — but their inner practice keeps them from being stained by its patterns. They are not corrupted by the world because their relationship to the world is one of engaged non-attachment: they are fully present without being possessed by what they encounter.
*The simple version:* **Monks and nuns are in the world but not of it.** That's not detachment from care — it's freedom from being owned by what happens.**

**18.21**
*Prakrit:* सा मिगावई राइणी, परिव्वाओ गया सुद्धा।
*Translation:* Queen Mrigavati, purified, went forth into the renunciant life.
*Commentary:* The narrative of Mrigavati concludes here with her formal entry into the renunciant life. "Suddha" — purified — is the state in which she took this step. She did not go forth in desperation, or out of grief, or under social pressure. She went forth in a state of genuine purification — having heard the teaching, reflected on it, internalized it, and aligned her inner state with its direction. Her going forth is the culmination of a process, not an impulsive act.
*The simple version:* **She didn't renounce out of despair or pressure — she renounced from clarity.** That's the difference between running away and genuinely going forward.

**18.22**
*Prakrit:* सा तवं चरइ उग्गं, संजमेण य वट्टइ।
*Translation:* She practices severe austerity; she lives by restraint.
*Commentary:* The chapter does not spare us the difficulty of what Mrigavati undertook. "Ugga" — intense, severe — describes the quality of her austerity. This is not comfortable spiritual practice but genuinely demanding work: fasting, exposure to hardship, sitting and standing for long hours, sleeping on hard ground. These are not romantic but practical: they create the conditions in which karma is exhausted most quickly. The queen who once enjoyed royal comforts voluntarily embraces discomfort as the vehicle of liberation.
*The simple version:* **Real practice is demanding.** Mrigavati went all the way — she didn't practice half-heartedly.

**18.23**
*Prakrit:* न य सा सोयइ रज्जं, न य सा हरिसइ तवे।
*Translation:* She does not grieve for the kingdom; she does not exult in her austerity.
*Commentary:* This sutra describes a precise inner equilibrium. On one side: she does not grieve the kingdom she has given up. On the other: she does not become proud of the austerity she now practices. Both movements — grief over what was lost and pride in what is gained — would be forms of attachment. The genuine practitioner is equanimous in both directions: no regret for what has been released, no self-congratulation for what is being practiced. This is the equanimity the path aims at.
*The simple version:* **She's not mourning what she gave up, and she's not proud of how much she's practicing.** Both would be forms of attachment.

**18.24**
*Prakrit:* रायलच्छी ण मोक्खो, तवेण संजमेण मुच्चइ।
*Translation:* Royal wealth is not liberation — through austerity and restraint one is freed.
*Commentary:* The chapter's central sutra is restated here, near the end, as the explicit lesson drawn from Mrigavati's story. The story has been the vehicle for this teaching, and the teaching is now declared plainly. Royal wealth — the apex of worldly achievement — is categorically not liberation. Liberation is achieved through austerity and restraint. The story of the queen who gave up the first to pursue the second makes this not a theoretical statement but a demonstration.
*The simple version:* **The story proves the lesson: wealth doesn't free you; practice does.** Mrigavati is the evidence.

**18.25**
*Prakrit:* रायलच्छी ण मोक्खो, तवेण य संजमेण य। एत्थ वट्टंतो सो मुच्चइ, जो सम्मं पडिवज्जए॥ — iti bemi.
*Translation:* Royal wealth is not liberation — through austerity and restraint one is freed; one who correctly takes up the path is released. — Thus I say.
*Commentary:* The closing sutra repeats the chapter's featured verse as its final statement, sealed with the "iti bemi" formula. "One who correctly takes up the path" — "sammam padivajjae" — is the crucial phrase. Not one who merely declares the path, or hears about the path, or admires those on the path, but one who correctly, fully, with right faith and right knowledge, actually takes it up. Mrigavati took it up. The chapter closes by affirming that anyone — regardless of social position — can do the same.
*The simple version:* **The final word is for anyone listening: take up the path correctly, and you will be freed.** Status doesn't matter. Practice does. Thus Mahavira says.
