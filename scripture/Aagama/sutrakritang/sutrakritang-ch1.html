Sutrakritanga Sutra

The Doctrineसमय

On true doctrine versus false doctrine — the foundation of liberation

The opening chapter of the Sutrakritanga establishes the foundational teaching: that liberation depends entirely on right understanding of doctrine. Mahavira systematically refutes twelve wrong philosophical positions — from materialism and fatalism to agnosticism and body-soul identity — and shows why only the Jain path of non-violence, restraint, and right knowledge leads to freedom.

बुद्धिं जियाइं, बन्धणं परिजाणिया ।
किमाहु बन्धणं वीरो? एगं वा जाणइ तिउट्टिं ॥

"Let the wise one attain understanding and know the bonds of karma fully — what does the Hero call bondage, and what single truth, when known, breaks bondage completely?" — Sutrakritanga 1.1

About This Chapter

The Doctrine

Samaya — the first chapter of the Sutrakritanga — is one of the most philosophically dense chapters in the entire Jain canon. The word "Samaya" means doctrine, right understanding, or the true path. The chapter opens by asking what a wise person must know, and proceeds to answer by systematically refuting every major wrong philosophical position of Mahavira's time.

The chapter is divided into four sections. The first refutes materialism, body-soul identity, and karma denial. The second addresses fatalism, agnosticism, and Buddhist views. The third examines wrong views about God, creation, and liberation. The fourth concludes with the contrast between false monks who behave like householders and true monks who practice non-violence and equanimity. It closes with Mahavira's signature: "Thus I say."

Section 1 — Causes of Bondage and Wrong Views (Gathas 1–27)
1.1

बुद्धिं जियाइं, बन्धणं परिजाणिया ।
किमाहु बन्धणं वीरो? एगं वा जाणइ तिउट्टिं ॥१.१॥

Let the wise one attain understanding and know the nature of bondage fully — what does the Hero call bondage, and what single truth, when known, breaks bondage completely?

This opening sutra frames the entire chapter as a question: what must a wise person understand to become free? The questioner is Jambu Swami; the teacher is Sudharma Swami transmitting Mahavira's words. The word "buddhi" here does not mean ordinary intelligence — it refers to enlightened self-understanding, the awakening to one's own nature as a soul distinct from matter. Bondage is not physical restraint but the karmic entanglement that keeps the soul cycling through births. The Hero — a title for Mahavira — is asked to define it. The structure of the question is itself the answer's shape: there is one truth, one understanding, that when fully grasped, breaks all bondage.

The simple version: The chapter opens with a question — what does the enlightened teacher say bondage is, and what single insight, when truly understood, sets you free?

BondageRight KnowledgeOpening Question
1.2

चित्तमंतमचित्तं वा, परिगिह्ह किसामिव ।
अण्णं वा अणुजाणाइ, एवं दुक्खा न मुच्चइ ॥१.२॥

One who possesses conscious beings or unconscious things — even the least trifle — or who approves of others possessing them, is not freed from suffering.

This sutra defines the first cause of bondage: possession. The definition is remarkably comprehensive — it includes both living possessions (servants, animals) and non-living ones (gold, land, goods), and even the mental act of consenting to another's possession. The phrase "even a trifle" closes every loophole: there is no threshold below which possession is harmless. The Jain understanding of possession is not merely physical accumulation but the internal attitude of attachment — the feeling that something belongs to "me." As long as that attitude exists, liberation is impossible, because the attachment generates fresh karma moment by moment.

The simple version: Clinging to anything — people, objects, wealth — or even silently approving of others clinging keeps you trapped in suffering, no matter how small the thing you're clinging to.

1.3

सयं तिवायए पाणे, अदुवा अण्णेहिं घायए ।
हणंतं वाऽणुजाणाइ, वेरं वड्ढेइ अप्पणो ॥१.३॥

One who himself kills living beings, or causes others to kill them, or approves of the killing, increases enmity toward himself.

This sutra identifies violence as the second root cause of bondage, and gives it the same triple structure used for possession: doing it oneself, having others do it, or approving of it. Violence is not simply a moral wrong but a self-multiplying force. Every act of violence creates karmic enmity that rebounds on the perpetrator across future lives. The word "toward himself" makes the injury reflexive: one who destroys others destroys their own prospect of liberation. This is not a punishment imposed from outside — it is the natural mechanics of karma.

The simple version: Whether you kill directly, hire someone else to kill, or simply agree with killing — you are increasing the weight of harm that comes back to you.

1.4

जस्स कुलसमुप्पण्णे, जेहिं वा संवसे नरे ।
ममाइ लुप्पइ बाले, अण्णमण्णेहिं मुच्छिए ॥१.४॥

The ignorant one is afflicted by attachment to those in whose family he is born and among whom he lives — deeply ensnared in mutual craving.

Having established possession and violence as root causes of bondage, the sutra now examines the psychological mechanism underneath both: the sense of "mine." The family one is born into and the community one inhabits become the primary field of this possessive attachment. The term "the ignorant one" is not a moral judgment but a technical term — it refers to the soul that has not yet awakened to its own independent nature and therefore projects its identity onto external relationships. The mutual nature of this entanglement is significant: everyone in a family network is simultaneously clinging to and being clung to by others.

The simple version: The unawakened person suffers because they are deeply attached to family and community — everyone clinging to each other in a web that keeps them all bound.

1.5

वित्तं सोयरिया चेव, सव्वमेयं न ताणए ।
संखाए जीवियं चेव, कम्मुणा उ तिउट्टइ ॥१.५॥

Wealth and kinsmen — all of this — do not protect one; knowing life to be brief, the wise one breaks free from karma.

This sutra makes the argument for renunciation in compressed form: nothing external offers real protection. Wealth cannot buy off death or karma. Kinsmen cannot carry your karmic burden. The sutra frames this not as pessimism but as clear-sighted realism — and crucially, it leads to action. The one who knows the brevity of life and the unreliability of external refuge does not collapse into despair; they break free from karma. The verb "breaks free" is the same used in 1.1 — the chapter is building a vocabulary of liberation that requires understanding bondage before one can leave it.

The simple version: No amount of money or family relationships can protect you from death or karma — and knowing this is what motivates a person to actually do the work of becoming free.

1.6

एगे गंथे विउक्कम्म, एगे समण-माहणा ।
यायंता विउस्सिट्ठा, सत्ता कामेहिं माणवा ॥१.६॥

Some ascetics and priests, having abandoned one set of entanglements, are still deeply addicted to pleasures — attached and deluded in their own doctrines.

With this sutra begins the critique of rival paths. Even those who have formally renounced — the ascetics and priests of other traditions — may abandon certain external entanglements while remaining deeply bound by inner ones. The word "knots" refers to structural binding points in the soul's relationship with matter. To cut one knot while leaving others intact is not liberation. This sutra introduces the theme that dominates the rest of the chapter: the difference between genuine inner freedom and the performance of renunciation.

The simple version: Some religious people give up certain things on the outside but remain deeply attached to pleasure and to their own doctrines on the inside — and that inner attachment is still bondage.

1.7

पुढवी आउ तेऊ वाऊ, वणस्सइ तसा य जे ।
ते जीवा ते य अजीवा, इइ केइ वियाणइ ॥१.७॥

Wrong View 1Materialism

The belief that matter is all that exists — the soul is not separate from the body or the physical elements.

Earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and mobile beings — some say these are both souls and non-souls.

This sutra begins the systematic refutation of wrong philosophical views. The view being examined here identifies the five material elements plus mobile beings as both souls and non-souls — a materialist-idealist hybrid that confuses the categories of self and matter. From the Jain perspective, this error is philosophically disastrous: if you cannot distinguish soul from matter, you cannot know what bondage is, and therefore cannot know what liberation requires. The chapter will spend many sutras unpacking why each wrong view is wrong — not as academic exercise but because wrong understanding directly causes continued bondage.

The simple version: Some philosophers teach that the elements of nature are both souls and non-souls — a confused view that makes it impossible to understand what needs to be freed and from what.

1.8

पुढवी आउ तेऊ वाऊ, वणस्सइ तसा य जे ।
एते जीवा ममाहंसु, तं सव्वं नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.८॥

Wrong View 2Monism

The belief that everything in existence is ultimately one single soul — no real distinction between self and world.

Earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and mobile beings — these are all souls, say some; but this everything-is-soul view is not recognized by the knower of the path.

This refutes the extreme monist view — that everything is soul. This was a position held by some Vedantic and Upanishadic thinkers. From the Jain standpoint, while it is true that conscious souls exist in all six categories, those souls are not identical to the material substances they inhabit. Collapsing the distinction between soul and matter makes karma unintelligible — if everything is already soul, what is it that karma binds? The refutation is not that these beings lack souls, but that identifying the soul with the material element is the error.

The simple version: The idea that everything is just one universal soul sounds spiritual, but it actually makes it impossible to understand karma or liberation — because it erases the distinction between the soul and what traps it.

1.9

अत्थि पाणा विसेसेण, नाणत्तं च विजाणइ ।
जीवो देहो त्ति एगत्तं, तं पि नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.९॥

Wrong View 3Body-Soul Identity

The belief that the body and soul are the same thing — when the body dies, the self ceases entirely.

Living beings exist in their specific forms, and one should know their diversity — but the view that the soul and the body are one thing is also not recognized by the knower of the path.

This refutes the body-soul identity view — the materialist teaching that the self is nothing more than the living body. This was held by the Carvaka materialists. The sutra makes two points simultaneously: yes, living beings exist in diverse specific forms (affirming that souls are real and plural), and no, the soul is not simply the body. The body is material and perishable; the soul is immaterial and does not perish with the body. Confusing them means failing to understand what continues after death, what karma attaches to, and therefore what liberation means.

The simple version: The idea that you are just your body — that there is nothing more to you than flesh and blood — is a mistake that makes liberation literally unthinkable.

1.10

जे अकिरियं वयंति भो, जीव-अजीव-वागरे ।
ते नासिया दुविहे ठाणे, उभये दुक्खं पावंति ॥१.१०॥

Wrong View 4Karma Denial

The belief that actions carry no moral consequences — nothing you do creates binding karma or affects your liberation.

Those who teach non-action — who say there is no moral consequence to action — regarding soul and non-soul: they are lost in both realms and experience suffering in both.

This refutes the doctrine that actions have no moral consequence, that karma does not accumulate, that there is nothing that binds or frees. This teaching destroyed the entire foundation of ethical conduct. If actions have no fruits, there is no reason to practice restraint, no reason for compassion, no basis for liberation. The consequence described here is direct: such a person is lost in both this life and the next. They suffer without understanding why, and without any path out, because their philosophy has eliminated the very concept of a path.

The simple version: People who teach that your actions have no consequences end up suffering in both this life and the next — because once you believe nothing matters, you have no map for getting free.

1.11

जे य आया न मुयंति, जे य मुत्ता न पुणरागच्छंति ।
एयं मग्गं विजाणित्ता, एयं धम्मं तु नायग-ब्भुणो ॥१.११॥

Some souls do not become freed; those who are freed do not return — knowing this path, knowing this doctrine, is what the knower of the path recognizes.

This sutra introduces the positive counterpart to the refutations: the true doctrine holds that liberation is real, possible, and irreversible. Some souls — those following wrong doctrine — remain bound and do not attain freedom. Those who do attain freedom never return to the cycle of births. This irreversibility is a defining feature of Jain liberation. The sutra creates a sharp contrast with fatalist and nihilist views: liberation requires right knowledge and right effort, and once attained, it is complete and permanent.

The simple version: Some souls stay trapped, and those who do get free never come back — and understanding this clearly is at the heart of true doctrine.

1.12

एगे नत्थि परो लोगो, न किंचि अकरोहि ते ।
अप्पाणं पि य नासंति, मिच्छद्दिट्ठी नरा भवे ॥१.१२॥

Wrong View 5Soul Denial

The belief that no soul or afterlife exists — death is total annihilation, and there is no self to liberate.

Some say there is no other world, "do nothing" — they destroy even themselves, for wrong-viewing persons exist in delusion.

This is the soul-denial position: there is no afterlife, no self that continues, therefore no consequences to one's actions. This nihilistic view is directly linked to moral nihilism — if there is nothing after death, why restrain oneself at all? The consequence stated here is the strongest yet: they destroy even themselves. This is not merely a statement about future karmic consequence — it is a statement about the present. A person who lives without recognizing their own soul does not merely fail to liberate it; they actively harm it through every unguarded act of passion and violence.

The simple version: People who say there's no such thing as an afterlife or a continuing self — and therefore nothing matters — end up destroying themselves, because that belief removes every reason to live with care.

1.13

ण कत्थ नत्थि आयाओ, एवं तिउट्टई तसा ।
ण होइ केइ नत्थित्तं, एवं जाणाहि पंडिए ॥१.१३॥

There is nowhere no soul — thus mobile beings are freed; there is no such thing as non-existence — thus know, O wise one.

Having refuted soul denial, the text now affirms the positive: the soul exists everywhere — in every mobile being. This is not pantheism but the Jain view that souls are present throughout nature in different degrees of development. The second line makes an even broader claim: there is no such thing as absolute non-existence. This is the Jain doctrine of existence — nothing simply vanishes. This metaphysical point directly implies that your actions, your karma, and your soul are all real and persistent, which is why the path of liberation is both necessary and possible.

The simple version: Souls exist everywhere, and nothing truly ceases to exist — so know this, and understand why your choices have real and lasting effects.

1.14

आउट्टमाणे पडिसाहरेज्जा, अप्पाणमेव अहिगिच्च धीरे ।
जे केइ लोए वयमाणे नरा वा, नारी वा तेसिं सच्चं विजाणासु ॥१.१४॥

When turned aside from the path, the resolute one should draw himself back — attending to himself alone — and among all people speaking in the world, men or women, know truly among them.

After a long sequence of philosophical refutations, this sutra pivots to practice: what does a monk actually do when surrounded by competing teachings? He turns inward — attending to himself alone. This phrase does not mean selfishness; it means the spiritual self-reliance that is the foundation of the Jain path. No teacher, no external authority, can do the inner work for you. Simultaneously, the monk must maintain discernment — he lives among the world's voices and must know what is true. Equanimity is not withdrawal from discernment; it is discernment that is no longer disturbed.

The simple version: When you get pulled off course, draw yourself back — focus on your own inner work — but keep your eyes open and keep discerning what is true among all the voices around you.

1.15

से दाणि पंडिए वि य, आया मे दिट्ठ-पुव्वगं ।
तिव्वेहिं सल्लेहिं विद्धे, न सुहं पावइ साहिए ॥१.१५॥

Now even one who considers himself learned — "I have seen the soul before" — pierced by sharp thorns of passion, does not attain happiness as stated.

This is a sharp caution against intellectual pride. The person who says "I have seen the soul" — who has had some experience or understanding of their own nature — but who remains pierced by the thorns of passion, does not attain liberation. Knowledge without the transformation of conduct is incomplete. This is the core Jain position that recurs throughout the chapter: right knowledge must be accompanied by right conduct. A monk who can quote the texts but is still driven by anger, pride, greed, or desire has not yet traveled the path he is describing.

The simple version: Just knowing about the soul — even having studied or experienced it — does not free you if you are still driven by strong passions. Knowledge without transformation is not enough.

1.16

संसारभयं च पेहाय, दुक्खं चेव जाणिया ।
आउट्टमाणे पडिसाहरेज्जा, आरंभाओ विरमेज्जासि ॥१.१६॥

Having seen the fear of the cycle of births and knowing suffering, one who is drawn aside should pull back — and should cease from all harm-causing activity.

This sutra provides the motivation that makes withdrawal from worldly activity intelligible: seeing the nature of the cycle of births, not merely intellectually knowing about it. The Jain path is fundamentally experiential — you must see the fear and the suffering clearly enough that withdrawal from harmful activity becomes natural rather than forced. The sutra pairs seeing with knowing and knowing with acting — a complete epistemological sequence. Ceasing from worldly enterprise and harm-causing action is the behavioral expression of the understanding developed over the previous sutras.

The simple version: When you truly see the suffering that comes from living without awareness, you naturally want to stop doing things that cause harm — not because someone told you to, but because you see clearly.

1.17

जे केइ लोए परियाए संति, तेसिं अकिरिया-मग्गो ण अत्थि ।
कम्मेहिं तेहिं बद्धा ते नरा, आउट्टमाणा परिहारिया न सिक्खिया ॥१.१७॥

Whatever doctrines exist in the world — for none of them does the path of non-action exist; those men are bound by those very karmas, having been turned away, they have not learned renunciation.

This is a comprehensive statement: across all doctrines and traditions in the world, the path of inaction — the idea that karma does not bind — does not exist as a real option. Those who follow such doctrines are bound by the very karmas they deny. "They have not learned renunciation" is the key: renunciation is not merely giving up possessions but the deep learning of how to cease generating karma. This cannot be bypassed through philosophical argument; it requires practice, conduct, and transformation.

The simple version: No matter what tradition you follow, the idea that your actions have no consequences is simply not how reality works — those who believe it end up bound by the very karma they pretend doesn't exist.

1.18

जं सव्वे पाणा सव्वे भूया, सव्वे जीवा सव्वे सत्ता ।
न हंतव्वा न अज्जाणेयव्वा, न परिग्गहेयव्वा न परिताविया ॥१.१८॥

All living beings, all beings with life-force, all souls, all sentient creatures — they should not be killed, should not be commanded to be killed, should not be possessed, should not be tormented.

This is one of the most important sutras in the chapter — a comprehensive statement of non-violence that serves as both the summary of the refutations and the positive statement of Jain ethics. The fourfold enumeration covers every possible category of living being without exception. The fourfold prohibition (not killing, not commanding, not possessing, not tormenting) matches the four ways harm can be done. The scope is absolute and admits no exceptions — not for ritual, not for food, not for livelihood. This sutra is the ethical foundation from which the entire Jain way of life is derived.

The simple version: Every single living being — no matter what kind — should not be killed, ordered killed, owned, or made to suffer. This is the whole foundation of Jain ethics in one verse.

1.19

एस धम्मे धुवे नीए, सासए जिणभासिए ।
तं परिण्णाय मेहावी, नो पमाए मणीसिए ॥१.१९॥

This is the doctrine — permanent, certain, eternal, spoken by the Conquerors — knowing this fully, the wise one, the thoughtful one, should not be negligent.

After the long sequence of refutations, this sutra offers a moment of affirmation: this is the doctrine. The Jain teaching is described with three qualities — permanent, certain, and eternal. These directly counter the philosophical uncertainty of the rival schools: agnosticism (which doubts knowability), skepticism (which holds no position firm), and relativism (which makes truth time-bound). The closing call to non-negligence is characteristic of the Agamic texts — vigilance is not just an ethical virtue but a metaphysical necessity.

The simple version: The Jain teaching — that all beings should be protected and that right conduct is the path to freedom — is permanent and certain. Knowing this, don't be lazy about it.

1.20

लोगो लोगे सुपइट्ठिए, अपट्ठिओ परलोगंसि ।
दुल्लहे खलु बोहिलाभे, मा पमाए कहिंचि ॥१.२०॥

One is well-established in this world but not established in the other world — right understanding is indeed difficult to attain; do not be negligent in any respect.

This sutra contains the teaching on the preciousness of right understanding. A person may be financially secure, socially established, physically comfortable — and yet completely unestablished in the only world that matters for liberation. Right understanding is not automatic, not inherited, and not guaranteed by social position. The vast majority of souls cycle endlessly through births without ever attaining the clarity needed to begin the path. This makes the opportunity of a human birth with the capacity for right understanding extraordinarily rare and precious.

The simple version: You may be comfortable and settled in this life, but you have no security in what comes next — and the real understanding that leads to freedom is genuinely rare. Don't waste the chance.

1.21

अणाइणिय-णाइया, बहुसो संसाराभिमुहे ।
परिभमेह ससरीरे, जणयाओ जीवे ॥१.२१॥

⚠️ The soul, without beginning and without known origin, has wandered many times through the cycle of birth and death, taking on bodies, born from various wombs.

⚠️ This sutra introduces the beginninglessness of the cycle of existence — a fundamental Jain teaching. The soul has no starting point; it has always existed and has always been cycling. This has a profound psychological implication: there is no original innocent state to return to. Liberation is not a return but a first-time attainment of freedom that was always possible but never achieved. The wandering described here — through countless bodies, countless wombs — is not cruel fate but the natural result of the soul's ongoing entanglement with karma. This sutra grounds the urgency of the chapter: each human birth is a rare window of opportunity in an otherwise endless cycle.

The simple version: Your soul has been wandering through births since before time began — there is no start to this cycle. Every human life is a chance to finally end it.

1.22

बहुणि वासाणि भमित्तु जीवो, गब्भाण माऊण गिलाऊण वाहिं ।
जाई-जरा-मरणं पुणो पुणो, दुक्खेण बद्धे ण सुहं पावइ ॥१.२२॥

Having wandered for many lifetimes, the soul — enduring the torments of the womb, illness, and disease — again and again undergoes birth, old age, and death, bound by suffering, not attaining happiness.

This sutra describes the mechanics of cyclic suffering in visceral terms. The three sufferings of the womb, illness, and disease are paired with the three stages of birth, old age, and death. What distinguishes the Jain version is the causal emphasis: the soul is bound by suffering, not merely experiencing it. Suffering is not accidental; it is the direct product of karmic bondage. And as long as that bondage is not addressed, the cycle repeats endlessly without real happiness ever being found.

The simple version: Life after life, the soul goes through the suffering of the womb, sickness, aging, and death — and as long as it remains bound by karma, it never finds real happiness.

1.23

⚠️ Those who speak of a path of purification — they have not attained the teaching of the Knower; for they have not fully understood the root of karma and do not abandon attachment-based possession.

⚠️ This is a pointed critique aimed at rival ascetic traditions that speak of "purification" without addressing the root cause. The target may include certain Brahmanical or other renunciate schools that engaged in purification rituals without eliminating craving-attachment. The distinction is precise: external purification practices do not cut the root of karma if the inner attitude of attachment remains. The root of karma — wrong belief, lack of restraint, negligence, passions, and activity — must be reached. Surface purification without inner transformation is insufficient.

The simple version: People who talk about "purifying themselves" through rituals or practices but never let go of craving and clinging haven't actually understood what creates karma.

एगे समण-माहणे विउसिट्ठे, जे लोगं पेच्चाभिणंदंति ।
संसारमेव अनुपरियट्टंति, एवं आहु से लोग-निच्छए ॥१.२४॥

Some ascetics and brahmins, tightly bound, who rejoice in the world and in what comes after — they only go around in the cycle of birth and death; thus says one who has mastered the world.

Even formal renunciates who still rejoice in the world — finding satisfaction in worldly pleasures and heavenly rewards — are merely continuing the cycle. This includes rejoicing in the afterlife rewards of ritual action — the heavenly enjoyments promised by the Vedic tradition. From the Jain perspective, heaven is still the cycle. A monk who practices austerity in order to enjoy heavenly pleasures has substituted one form of desire for another. Only the complete cessation of rejoicing in continued existence — in any form — leads out of the cycle.

The simple version: Even religious people who practice austerities but still look forward to enjoying heaven or rebirth are just taking a different route around the same cycle — they haven't escaped it.

1.25

णो हिंसए किंचण पाणे, जाणंतो विरए समाहिए ।

One who does not harm any living being — knowing, restrained, composed — shaking off karma, free of delusion, with no remaining karma, is known as a Conqueror.

After the long arc of refutations, this sutra presents the positive portrait: what does a truly liberated person look like? The description has five elements: non-violence in conduct, right knowledge, restraint, composure, freedom from delusion, and the complete exhaustion of karma. The title "Conqueror" (Jina) is the source of the word "Jain" — the tradition of those who follow the Conquerors. What is conquered? Not the world, not enemies, but the inner forces of passion, delusion, and karma. This sutra is the positive culmination of Section 1.

The simple version: A Conqueror — the ideal of the Jain path — is someone who harms nothing, knows truly, lives with restraint and peace, and has burned off all their karma through right living.

Section 2 — Fatalism, Agnosticism, and Views on Suffering (Gathas 28–59)
1.28

नियइवाइं च ते तेसिं, जे नियइं वयंति भो ।
पुरिसक्कारेण णत्थि किंचि, तेसिं णायरियं ण लद्धं ॥१.२८॥

Wrong View 6Fatalism

The belief that all events are predetermined by fate — personal effort is powerless and cannot change your destiny.

Those fatalists — those who teach fate — say that through personal effort nothing can be achieved; they have not attained the teaching of the Knower.

Section 2 opens with the refutation of fatalism — the doctrine that all events are absolutely predetermined by fate and that human effort is powerless. This was the teaching of the Ajivika school. The Jain rejection of fatalism is total: if nothing can be achieved through personal effort, the entire path of liberation is meaningless. The concept of personal effort is central to Jain ethics — one's choices genuinely matter and genuinely determine one's trajectory. Karma shapes circumstances, but effort transforms those circumstances.

The simple version: People who believe that everything is fated — that your effort makes no difference — have not understood the teaching. If fate decides everything, then there's no point in trying to become free.

1.29

नियइवाइणो खलु ते बाला, मोहेण य कम्मेण य पविट्ठा ।
जे उ सुक्कस्स कम्मस्स फलं, अणुभवंति अप्पणो ॥१.२९॥

Fatalists are indeed unwise, entered into delusion and karma — but those who experience the fruit of pure karma do so through their own effort.

This sutra makes explicit the counter-position to fatalism: the fruit of positive karma is experienced by those who generated it through their own effort. The Jain position is not that fate plays no role — past karma does shape one's circumstances — but that present effort genuinely matters and can transform those circumstances. The fatalist, immersed in delusion, fails to see this. What is particularly pointed: fatalists are said to be immersed in karma even while denying karma's significance. Their very belief in fate is a consequence of deluded views.

The simple version: Fatalists are fooled by their own confusion — the good results that come from living well come from your own sincere effort, not from fate.

All living beings, all creatures — knowing both benefit and suffering — wander in the cycle of births through birth, old age, and death.

This sutra affirms the universal scope of suffering as experienced by all beings, regardless of philosophical position. All living beings, from the simplest to the most complex, experience both what they seek as beneficial and what comes as suffering. The cycle of birth, old age, and death is not a human problem but a universal one. This universality is the basis of compassion — if every being suffers, then the practice of non-violence is not merely a rule but a recognition of shared condition.

The simple version: Every living being — not just humans — goes through birth, aging, and death, experiencing both good and bad. This is a fact about all life, not just yours.

Whoever knows this doctrine — the resolute one, the wise one, who has seen clearly — should not perform harmful karma, and should not approve of it either.

The connection between knowledge and conduct is made explicit here: knowing the doctrine is not enough unless it produces a change in behavior. The monk who genuinely knows the Jain teaching will both avoid performing harmful action and will withdraw his approval from others who do it. Mental and verbal approval of harm generates karma even without physical participation. Right understanding must produce right action — and right restraint from action — to be real.

The simple version: If you truly understand the teaching, you won't do harmful things — and you won't even silently agree when others do them.

The ignorant do not know this highest truth — they do not know liberation from suffering; thus says one who has mastered the world — for such a one there is no doctrine.

The ignorant person — one without right knowledge — fails at the most fundamental level: they do not know what liberation is, and therefore cannot pursue it. "There is no doctrine for such a one" does not mean the doctrine is unavailable; it means they are not living within the doctrine's framework. Right doctrine is not just a set of beliefs; it is a way of life oriented toward liberation. Without that orientation, the outward forms of religious life remain empty.

The simple version: People without real understanding don't even know what freedom from suffering means — and because of that, their religious behavior has no real doctrine underneath it.

Wrong View 7Spiritual Pride

The belief that claimed religious status or superior knowledge alone grants spiritual standing — regardless of actual conduct.

The ignorant, the wrong-viewing, those who praise themselves and are laden with karma — those who do not know this doctrine go to a state of suffering.

Three qualities cluster together here: ignorance, wrong views, and self-praise. The third — self-praise — might seem surprising in this list, but in the Jain analysis it is the social expression of the inner vice of pride, one of the four passions that drive karma accumulation. The religious person who claims spiritual superiority while actually being laden with karma is precisely the target of this critique. The consequence — a state of suffering — is direct and proportional.

The simple version: People who are ignorant, hold wrong views, and boast about their spiritual standing while actually loaded with karma — they head toward greater suffering, not freedom.

Whoever is ignorant and without right understanding, joined with wrong perception — know certainly that this is bondage; the pure soul is freed from karma.

This sutra offers one of the cleanest definitions of bondage in the chapter: the combination of ignorance, absence of right understanding, and wrong perception constitutes bondage — certainly and definitively. The contrast with the second half is equally clean: the pure soul is freed from karma. The word "pure" in the Jain context refers not to ritual purity but to the soul's natural state unclouded by karma and wrong views. Liberation is the soul returning to its own pure nature — not acquiring something new but removing the obscurations.

The simple version: Ignorance plus wrong views equals bondage — that's certain. And the soul that becomes clear and pure is what frees itself from karma.

This sutra specifies three of the five major restraints for monks: celibacy, non-stealing, and non-harming through enterprise. These are not arbitrary rules but the specific practices that prevent the three major categories of karma generated through desire, taking, and doing harm. The Jain monastic code is precise: each restraint addresses a specific mechanism of karma generation. This sutra is a positive statement of practice within the broader philosophical discussion of wrong views.

The monk who fears suffering, who maintains restraint, who has cut through attachment and aversion — let him practice the discipline of the path.

The two central obstacles to liberation — attachment and aversion — are named here and must be cut through, not merely reduced. The monk's conduct flows from this inner cutting: when attachment and aversion are gone, restraint is not a burden but the natural expression of clarity. The phrase "having seen suffering, one who fears it" is not timidity but discernment. One who sees clearly what suffering is and where it comes from is naturally motivated to live in a way that does not generate more of it.

There are many great teachers in the world, speakers of wisdom — they praise each other, but my truth is not known by them.

This sutra introduces the perspective of Mahavira himself. There is no shortage of teachers and traditions in the world — many who praise each other and establish mutual credibility. But mutual praise among teachers does not constitute access to truth. The Jain critique is not that other traditions have nothing of value; it is that the specific truth Mahavira teaches — about the soul, karma, and the precise mechanics of liberation — is not accessible to those who remain attached to their own doctrines. Truth is not arrived at by consensus among scholars but by the direct experience of the liberated.

The simple version: There are many teachers in the world who praise each other — but mutually agreeing doesn't make something true. The truth Mahavira teaches is not reached through intellectual debate.

The phrase "attends to himself alone" is the practical expression of this: the sage's primary and ultimately his only responsibility is the cultivation of his own soul. This is not selfishness — it is the recognition that no one can do this work for another, and that a soul in the process of purification best serves others by completing that purification.

The simple version: The sage who completely untangles himself from the world's demands and focuses entirely on his own inner cultivation — that is the Jain ideal of the practicing monk.

This is the path to be followed by one who knows this doctrine — he is not afraid, not agitated, not negligent even in minor matters.

The psychological portrait of the practicing monk is here given three qualities: fearlessness, non-agitation, and total non-negligence. Fearlessness comes from having understood the true nature of things. Non-agitation means the monk is not thrown off by challenges or provocations. Total non-negligence is perhaps the most demanding: the Jain path holds that even small careless acts generate karma, and therefore attention must be maintained constantly, not just in dramatic moments.

The simple version: The person who truly follows this path is not afraid, not rattled, and never careless — even about small things. That combination is the mark of someone actually walking the path.

The great sage, cautious of what is born of ignorance, who has crossed over all suffering — one who knows the world, this single knower.

The great sage is defined by two qualities: he is cautious of everything born of ignorance, and he has crossed over all suffering. The first quality is the means; the second is the result. Anything produced by ignorance — any thought, speech, or action arising from wrong understanding — is to be approached with caution, because ignorance-born acts generate binding karma. The sage who has internalized this caution completely has crossed to the other shore. Being called "one who knows the world" connects this sage to the tradition of those who have fully understood the world's nature.

The simple version: The great sage is careful about everything that comes from ignorance — and by being careful, he has crossed over all suffering.

The image of being struck by a sharp instrument and yet remaining resolute is a test of the sage's equanimity under extreme physical hardship. Even in such a state, the great sage remains resolute and turns inward. The capacity to maintain equanimity under extreme provocation — pain, insult, loss — is the practical demonstration of having truly "understood." This is not stoic suppression of feeling but the transformation of the inner relationship to experience that comes through genuine practice.

The simple version: Even deeply wounded, the great sage stays steady and turns inward — that's what it means to have truly understood the teaching, not just memorized it.

He is neither hopeful nor despairing, neither elated nor lamenting in sorrow — in complete suffering and in complete happiness alike, standing in equanimity, without agitation.

This sutra gives the clearest formulation of equanimity in the chapter. The sage's equanimity is not coldness or indifference — it is stability of being that is unaffected by the extremes of experience. He does not hope for good outcomes (thereby creating attachment) nor despair at bad ones (thereby creating aversion). In the depths of suffering and in the heights of happiness, the inner state remains the same. This is the equanimity that is the precondition for liberation — because liberation requires the complete cessation of the reactive swing between attraction and repulsion.

The simple version: The sage is not swinging between hope and despair, joy and grief — whether life goes wonderfully or terribly, he stays in the same steady place inside.

Thus right knowledge and right perception and right conduct together — having attained the words of the Conqueror, this is the path declared by the Conqueror.

This is a landmark sutra in Jain philosophy: the explicit naming of the Three Jewels — right knowledge, right perception, and right conduct. Together these three constitute the complete path to liberation. The sequence is deliberate: right perception (the fundamental orientation of the soul toward truth) comes before right knowledge (the specific understanding of reality) which grounds right conduct (the daily practice that generates no new karma). All three must be present together — any one alone is insufficient. This sutra is the positive heart of the entire chapter.

The simple version: Right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct together — that is the complete path the Conqueror declared.

The monk's restraint in food is a mirror of his inner state.

Let him not be intoxicated, let him not harm, let him not speak falsehood — let him not injure living beings, let him not perform any harmful karma whatsoever.

This sutra is a compressed code of monastic ethics: no intoxication, no harm, no falsehood, no injury to living beings, and absolutely no harmful karma of any kind. The four prohibitions correspond to the first four of the five great restraints. The phrase "any, whatsoever" closes every loophole. This is not a graduated code with exceptions; it is an absolute standard. Intoxication specifically clouds the clarity needed for right conduct, making it the precondition for other failures.

The simple version: The monk's code in one verse: no getting intoxicated, no harming, no lying, no injuring anyone, no harmful action at all — none.

The freedom from fear described here is not the absence of dangerous circumstances but the transformation of one's relationship to those circumstances. The monk who genuinely understands all states of being and has withdrawn from all harmful activity has nothing left to fear. Fear is generated by the uncertainty that comes from attachment: we fear losing what we are attached to. The monk who has let go completely has no such attachment, and therefore no such fear. This is freedom as an inner state, not an outer condition.

The Three Jewels appear again in a closing declarative: the one who possesses all three — right conduct, right knowledge, right perception — is the truly wise one. This contrasts with the earlier uses of "wise one" where the scholar who knows the teachings but acts wrongly was shown to be not truly wise. Three forms of physical harm — cutting, splitting, burning — are specifically named because they represent the most common forms of harm in daily activities. From the Jain perspective, each of these activities injures earth, water, fire, and plant souls. The instruction to "abandon what brings little benefit" is a cost-benefit analysis in favor of the living: if the benefit is minor and the harm is real, the benefit is not justification enough. This sutra is among the most ecologically specific in the Jain canon.

The simple version: Don't cut, split, or burn anything for trivial reasons. All souls deserve to be known and respected — once you know them, you won't want to start harming them.

The repetition of the formula "now even the wise one" throughout this chapter creates a rhythmic structure that functions almost like a refrain, drawing the reader back to practical implication. Here it is coupled with the complete knowledge of suffering. The full understanding of suffering — its origin, its mechanisms, its forms across all six categories of beings — is the motivational foundation of the path. Section 3 opens with a meditation on purposeless desire. The unwise pursue things that have no real benefit for the soul — sensory pleasures, social status, worldly security — and in doing so, wander endlessly. The ocean metaphor is one of the most persistent in the Agamic texts: existence is an ocean that can be crossed only with the right vessel of right conduct, and those without it simply drift. The word "beginninglessly" restates the theme from 1.21: the cycle has no starting point, which is why the urgency of the present opportunity is so great.

The concept of harm generated through the act of eating is unique to Jain ethics. Because even plant life has souls, eating is inherently an act involving harm to living beings, and therefore must be carefully regulated. The monk who has understood what appropriate food means — food that was not prepared specifically for him, that does not involve deliberate harm, that is consumed in the right quantity and manner — lives in a way that minimizes this unavoidable harm. Those in the world who teach that everything is done by a Creator — by knowing (their view of) the Creator of the world, how can such a one be freed from bondage?

This begins the refutation of the doctrine that a divine Creator is the cause of all worldly events. The Jain critique is precise: if a Creator made everything, then the soul's bondage is the Creator's doing, and its liberation would also require the Creator's action. This eliminates personal effort and personal responsibility from the equation. A person who believes liberation depends on a divine actor rather than on their own karma and conduct cannot take the steps required for liberation.

See the world from the world's own root — what God, and what would he do? Having conceived of divine perfection, one must work out one's own liberation.

This is the Jain philosophical response to theism — not a denial of divine perfection but a reorientation of its significance. The liberated souls exist in a state of divine perfection, but this perfection was achieved through their own effort, not granted by an external deity. The world has its own roots — it is self-existing and self-sustaining, governed by the laws of karma rather than divine will. In the Jain universe, no entity has the power to intervene in another's karma. Liberation is therefore entirely the work of the self on the self.

The simple version: The world runs on its own laws. Even if you imagine divine perfection as your ideal, the work of reaching it is entirely your own — no one can do it for you.

Some declare the entire world to be permanent by nature; others declare the world to be impermanent by nature — neither of these two has attained the teaching of the Knower.

This is the Jain doctrine of qualified truth in embryo. The absolutist positions — "everything is eternal" (held by the Vedantic tradition) and "everything is impermanent" (the Buddhist position) — are both partial. The Jain analysis holds that reality is both permanent and impermanent in different respects: the soul is permanent in its essential nature but impermanent in its modes. To seize on one aspect and declare it the whole truth is to fall into a one-sided view that does not capture reality fully.

The simple version: Some say everything lasts forever; others say nothing lasts at all. The Jain teaching says both are half-truths — reality is more subtle than either extreme.

The tree metaphor is one of the most elegant illustrations in the Agamic texts. A tree is permanent in its essential wood-nature — it persists through seasons — but impermanent in its modes: leaves fall and grow, flowers bloom and fade, fruits appear and are consumed. Similarly, the soul is permanent in its essential existence but undergoes continuous modification in its states, karmic coatings, and modes of experience. The Conquerors are invoked in the plural to indicate this is not Mahavira's unique invention but the consistent teaching of all Tirthankaras.

The simple version: Reality is both permanent and changing — like a tree that stays a tree while its leaves and flowers constantly change. The Conquerors have always taught this middle understanding.

Two further wrong views about liberation: that liberation is just a form of eternal existence in some heavenly realm (making liberation indistinguishable from a very long samsaric life), and that liberation does not exist at all (a nihilistic view). The Jain teaching is that liberation is real and final — it involves the permanent cessation of karma and the soul's attainment of its own natural, infinitely conscious state. It is not "eternal" in the sense of endless samsaric time, and it is not nothing. Both extremes fail to understand what liberation actually is.

This is a crucial sutra in Jain epistemology. The three positions — soul exists, soul does not exist, soul is inexpressible — each capture a partial truth. The Jain doctrine of conditional predication would say: the soul exists from the perspective of its essential being; it does not exist as a permanent self-identical entity across all modes; and its full nature may be difficult to express in any single statement. What is wrong is to take any one of these positions as the absolute and complete truth, including the "inexpressible" position when held as an absolute claim.

The simple version: Three different answers about whether the soul exists — exists, doesn't exist, can't be described — and all three miss the full truth when held as absolute positions.

Section 4 — True Conduct Versus False Conduct (Gathas 76–88)
1.76

उड्ढं अहो य तिरियं च, सव्वओ तसा-थावरे ।
ण हिंसेज्जा ण हंतव्वे, एस धम्मे विसोहिए ॥१.७६॥

Above, below, and sideways — in all directions — mobile and immobile beings should not be harmed, should not be killed; this is the doctrine, purified.

Section 4 opens with the broadest possible formulation of non-violence: all directions and all categories of life. The technical Jain classification of mobile and immobile beings extends protection to every living thing — one-sensed beings (earth, water, fire, air, plant souls) through five-sensed beings. Most traditions protect only the most visible, sentient creatures. Jain ethics extends protection to every living thing in every direction. This universality is the defining characteristic of Jain ethics and the most demanding aspect of the monastic life.

The simple version: In all directions, every living thing — those that move and those that stay still — should not be harmed. The Jain teaching is completely without exceptions.

Universal Non-ViolenceAll DirectionsMobile Immobile
1.77

पाण-भूय-जीव-सत्ते, एगे देव-वाइणो ।
न किंचि दुक्खं काउं, परिव्वएज्जासि मुणी ॥१.७७॥

For all living beings, all creatures, all souls, all sentient ones — some speak of them as divine — the monk should wander without causing any suffering whatsoever.

The phrase "some speak of them as divine" is an interesting acknowledgment: some traditions attribute divine status to all beings, which provides their own internal justification for non-violence. Regardless of the theological framework — whether you see all beings as divine, as fellow souls, or simply as beings that feel pain — the practical conclusion is the same: the monk wanders without causing suffering. The Jain path achieves the same behavioral conclusion through different reasoning than the divine-nature view, but does not dismiss the value of that insight in motivating compassion.

The simple version: Whether or not you see all beings as divine, the monk's practice is to move through the world without causing suffering to any of them.

Divine BeingsNon-Causing SufferingMonk's Practice
1.78

जे परो य अपरो वा, सेहे संजए धुवे ।
परियाय-बुद्धी भवे, सव्व-भूएसु मेहावी ॥१.७८॥

Whether toward others or toward himself — established, restrained, steady — the wise one has understanding of the cycle of all beings, toward all beings.

This sutra introduces the quality of discernment of the cyclic nature of all beings. The wise monk understands that every being he encounters is cycling through births just as he is, that every being has occupied every possible position in the cycle relative to every other being. This understanding produces neither special attachment to those close to him nor special aversion to those distant — because from the perspective of the long arc of existence, everyone has been everything to everyone. This is not sentimentality but the natural result of clear seeing.

The simple version: The wise monk treats himself and all others the same — because he understands that all beings are cycling through the same process of birth and rebirth together.

Cyclic UnderstandingUniversal EqualityWisdom
1.79

पण्णासयाए य वट्टमाणे, से भिक्खू सुद्धसंजए ।
ण विग्गहं कुज्जा कयाइ, भिक्खू आयाणमाचरे ॥१.७९॥

Moving about with wisdom, the monk — purely restrained — should never enter into conflict, the monk should practice the discipline of the path.

The prohibition on conflict covers more than physical fighting — it includes verbal disputes, philosophical arguments aimed at winning rather than illuminating, and interpersonal conflict of any kind. The monk who moves about with wisdom naturally avoids conflict, not because he is passive but because he has no ego investment in outcomes that would make him defensive or aggressive. This sutra is particularly relevant in the context of the preceding philosophical discussions: having understood the errors of rival teachings, the monk does not attack those teachers personally but simply practices the true path.

The simple version: The wise monk never gets into arguments or conflicts — not out of weakness, but because he has nothing to defend and nothing to attack.

Non-ConflictWisdomDiscipline
1.80

नो दंडे आयरे भिक्खू, नो गारत्थिय-माणवे ।
अंजली-कडे विणए, परिव्वएज्जासि मुणी ॥१.८०॥

Let not the monk practice the rod, let him not act like a householder's man — with folded hands in reverence, the monk should wander.

The contrast between the true monk and the "householder's man" is the central theme of Section 4. The monk should not use power over others, should not behave with the social conventions of a worldly person, and should not carry the pride and self-assertion of someone with social standing. The image of folded hands and reverence is not merely about outward gesture but about the inner attitude of humility that the monk embodies — the opposite of the self-assertion that drives worldly behavior.

The simple version: The monk doesn't dominate or punish anyone, and doesn't behave like a worldly social climber — he walks with folded hands and genuine humility.

HumilityNon-DominanceTrue Monk
1.81

जे खलु पव्वइए मुणी, ण भुंजे वि य कडुय-तित्तयं ।
मद्दव-भावेण संजए, एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे ॥१.८१॥

The monk who has truly renounced does not eat even what is bitter or pungent — restrained with a disposition of gentleness; this is the highest path.

Pungent and bitter foods (onion, garlic, hot peppers, etc.) are avoided in Jain monastic practice because such foods are believed to stimulate the passions — they increase desire, agitation, and restlessness, making meditation and equanimity more difficult to maintain. The disposition of gentleness is the inner quality that corresponds to the outer food practice: a gentle inner disposition is both cultivated by and expressed in gentle dietary choices. This sutra is specific and practical — the highest path shows itself in the smallest daily choices.

The simple version: The truly renounced monk avoids even pungent or bitter foods — not just for health but because gentle living shows in every small choice, including what you eat.

Gentle DispositionFood PracticeDaily Choices
1.82

अकिंचणे अनासत्ते, निम्मले धुय-संसए ।
आत्म-गुत्ते सया जागरे, एस मग्गे सुसाहिए ॥१.८२॥

Possessing nothing, unattached, pure, with doubts shaken off — self-guarded, always vigilant — this is the doctrine, well-established.

Six qualities of the true monk are given here in compressed sequence: possessing nothing, unattached, pure, free of doubt, self-guarded, and always vigilant. These form a progressive deepening: possessing nothing is the outer condition; being unattached is the inner attitude; purity is the resulting clarity; freedom from doubt is the epistemological security; self-guarding is the active practice; and always-vigilance is the continuous state that holds the entire structure together. This is a verified, tested path.

The simple version: The true monk: no possessions, no attachment, inner purity, no doubt, self-watching, always alert. That is the well-established path.

Six QualitiesNon-PossessionVigilance
1.83

सव्वत्थ विरए भिक्खू, सव्वत्थ विगय-मोहे ।
सव्व-दुक्खाण पारगए, एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे ॥१.८३॥

The monk who is restrained in all things, free of delusion in all things, who has crossed over all suffering — this is the highest path.

The threefold "in all things" is the key to this sutra. It is not enough to be restrained in most things, free of delusion in some things, past some suffering. The Jain path requires completeness — total restraint, complete freedom from delusion, total crossing of suffering. This completeness is not perfectionism in the ordinary sense but the recognition that partial liberation is still bondage. As long as any remnant of delusion remains, any attachment continues, any karma is still being generated — the soul is not yet free.

The simple version: The monk who is restrained about everything, free from all delusion, past all suffering — that is the highest path. Nothing partial, nothing halfway.

Complete RestraintComplete FreedomHighest Path
1.84

ण अट्ठिं ण य पट्ठिं, ण वि देहे मनोरमे ।
आयाण-मग्गे अट्ठिए, से भिक्खू भाविए सया ॥१.८४॥

Without craving, without desire for what is pleasing, and without attachment to the delightful body — established on the path of discipline, the monk is always cultivated.

The reference to the body as something not to be attached to is significant: the monk's practice extends to the absence of attachment to his own physical form. Vanity, comfort-seeking, and identification with the body as "my beautiful body" are all forms of subtle attachment that perpetuate karma. The monk who has severed even this attachment is "always cultivated." The word "cultivated" refers to the inner development that is the real meaning of spiritual practice — not the acquisition of outer achievements.

The simple version: The monk doesn't crave anything, doesn't desire what's pleasant, and doesn't even identify with or feel attached to his own body. He is always developing inwardly.

Body AttachmentInner CultivationNo Craving
1.85

जे तारिसे भिक्खू, जितिंदिए जिए-मणे ।
धुय-पाव-कम्मंसे, ण मुच्चए से कयाइ ॥१.८५॥

The monk who is like that — with senses conquered, with mind conquered, having shaken off harmful karma — is never bound again.

Conquest of the senses and conquest of the mind are two distinct achievements that together constitute the inner transformation required for liberation. The senses pull outward toward objects; the mind generates intentions, plans, and desires. A monk who has conquered both has effectively dismantled the two primary mechanisms by which karma is generated. Combined with the prior shaking-off of harmful karma, the monk who embodies all three is described as one who will never be bound again — a statement of irreversible liberation.

The simple version: The monk who has conquered his senses, conquered his mind, and shaken off harmful karma — that monk is never bound again.

Sense ConquestMind ConquestIrreversible Freedom
1.86

एवं अहा-समाहिए, सव्वसो विगय-मोहे ।
सव्व-दुक्खाण पारगए, सिद्धिं पडिवज्जइ ॥१.८६॥

Thus, composed in accordance with the teaching, with delusion completely removed, having crossed over all suffering — one attains liberation.

This sutra completes the positive description of the liberated soul. The phrase "composed in accordance with the teaching" is important: liberation is not achieved through random effort or self-invented practice but through the specific discipline taught by the Conquerors. The three conditions — composure in the teaching, complete removal of delusion, crossing of all suffering — are sequential: the teaching produces composure, composure enables the removal of delusion, and the removal of delusion enables the crossing of all suffering. Liberation is the natural result of this sequence, not a grace or a gift.

The simple version: When you truly follow the teaching, all delusion falls away, all suffering is crossed — and liberation is attained.

Liberation AttainedComposureDelusion Removed
1.87

एस खलु लोग-सारे, एस धम्मे जिण-भासिए ।
एस मग्गे अणुत्तरे, एसो अट्ठे सनातणे ॥१.८७॥

This indeed is the essence of the world — this is the doctrine declared by the Conqueror — this is the highest path — this is the truth, eternal.

The penultimate sutra serves as a fourfold seal of the entire chapter. Four declarations are made in sequence: this teaching is the world's essence, this is the doctrine spoken by the Conqueror, this is the highest path, and this is the eternal truth. The word "eternal" is deliberately chosen — it is a term associated with the Vedic tradition's claim to eternal truth, here used for the Jain teaching: the Jain path is not new to Mahavira but is the eternal truth rediscovered and re-declared by each Tirthankara in each cycle of time.

The simple version: This teaching is the essence of the world — declared by the Conqueror, the highest path, eternal truth. Four seals in one verse.

Fourfold SealEternal TruthWorld's Essence
1.88

एस धम्मे धुवे नीए, आणाए अरिहंताणं ।
समयं जाणाहि एयं, इइ बेमि ॥१.८८॥

This is the doctrine — permanent, certain — by the authority of the Arihantas; know this Samaya (true doctrine) — thus I say.

The final sutra closes the entire chapter with Mahavira's signature phrase: iti bemi — Thus I say. This phrase appears at the conclusion of every major section of the Sutrakritanga and is the traditional marker of Mahavira's direct declaration. The content of the closing is precise: the doctrine is permanent and certain — not a theory, not a possibility, but an established certainty. It is given under the authority of the Arihantas — those who have conquered the inner enemies of attachment, aversion, and delusion. The word Samaya in the closing refers both to the doctrine and to the chapter's title: "Know this Samaya" — the subject of the entire first adhyayana is encapsulated in its own name.

The simple version: The doctrine is permanent and certain, coming from those who have conquered their inner enemies. Know this teaching — Thus says Mahavira. — iti bemi
