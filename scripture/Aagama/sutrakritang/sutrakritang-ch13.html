Shramana-Brahmanश्रमण-ब्राह्मण

The True Monk and the Brahmin Scholar

A philosophical teaching contrasting the Jain monk with the Brahmin scholar. The Brahmin has learning, ritual mastery, and social prestige. The monk has inner restraint and non-violence. The teaching: true religious excellence lies in transformation, not performance.

णाणं च दंसणं च, चरित्तं तवसंजमं।
एत्थ वट्टंति धम्मस्स, सारं जाणंत साहवो॥

"Knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity, and restraint — in these lie the essence of the teaching, as the wise ones know." — Sutrakritanga 13

About This Chapter

The True Monk and the Brahmin Scholar

Chapter 13 is one of the Sutrakritanga's most philosophically direct chapters. It confronts the central religious question of its time: what makes a person genuinely holy? The Brahmin tradition answered with learning, birth, and ritual performance. The Jain teaching answers with inner transformation — non-violence, right vision, and the actual conduct of daily life.

The chapter does not attack the Brahmin tradition as worthless. It honors genuine learning and genuine Brahminhood while insisting that without inner transformation, learning is ornament without substance. A person who has harmed living beings — however many scriptures they have memorized — has not achieved what scripture was pointing toward.

Part I — The Question of True Knowledge (1–12)
13.1

कुसले अकुसले धम्मे, जाणइ लोगे मेहावी ॥13.1॥

The wise person knows what is good and what is harmful in the world.

The chapter opens by distinguishing two types of knowledge: knowledge of what leads toward liberation and knowledge of what leads away from it. This is not the knowledge of scriptures, languages, or ritual procedures — it is the knowledge that functions as a guide to action. The "wise person" is the one whose knowledge is operationally relevant to the question of how to live. Knowledge that cannot answer this question, however impressive, is not what the teaching values most.

The simple version: True knowledge tells you what helps and what harms. If your learning cannot answer that question, something essential is missing.

True KnowledgeOperational WisdomGuide to Action
13.2

सम्मं दिट्ठी य जाणइ, अहिंसा सच्चमेव य ॥13.2॥

The one with right vision knows non-violence and truth.

Right vision is not abstract — its first two products are non-violence and truthfulness. The person who has genuinely seen the nature of living beings as souls engaged in the same struggle will naturally refrain from harming them. The person who has seen the soul as pure consciousness will naturally speak truthfully. These are not virtues added on top of understanding; they are the direct expressions of understanding.

The simple version: Right vision produces right action automatically. Non-violence and honesty are not rules to follow — they are what naturally comes from seeing clearly.

Right VisionNon-ViolenceAutomatic Right Action
13.3

अज्झयणजुत्ते बहुस्सुए, धम्मं न जाणइ बाहिरो ॥13.3॥

The external one devoted to learning and hearing much does not know the teaching.

This is the chapter's central challenge. The Brahmin who has memorized vast quantities of scripture, who is devoted to study, who is praised as greatly learned — this person, if their knowledge has remained external, does not know the teaching. "External" means not integrated into conduct, not transformed into right action, not productive of inner change. Knowing about the teaching and knowing the teaching are not the same thing.

The simple version: You can memorize everything and still not know anything that matters. Knowledge that stays in the head has not yet become teaching.

External KnowledgeIntegrationBrahmin Critique
13.4

जे य बंभणे चणिहे, वेयं सहस्सं अहीयं ॥13.4॥

The Brahmin who has studied the Vedas by the thousand — yet still harms living beings.

The Vedas represent the pinnacle of Brahmin learning — thousands of verses memorized, studied, and recited. The sutra names this achievement explicitly and then pivots to the central question: does this learning prevent harm to living beings? If the answer is no — if the person still uses animals in sacrifice, still acts from attachment and aversion — then what has the learning actually accomplished?

The simple version: The question is not "how much do you know?" but "has your knowledge made you less harmful?" If the answer is no, the learning has not yet become wisdom.

Vedic LearningHarm TestWisdom vs Knowledge
13.5

ण स सामण्णं, ण से बंभण्णं ॥13.5॥

He is not a true monk, he is not a true Brahmin.

This is the chapter's boldest statement. The title of "Brahmin" in Jain teaching is not inherited or earned through study — it is earned through conduct. The person who harms living beings is not a true Brahmin regardless of birth or how many verses they know. The titles are redefined by the teaching: they name what someone is, not what someone is called.

The simple version: Titles mean nothing without the conduct that justifies them. Being called a monk or a Brahmin is not the same as being one.

Titles vs RealityConduct-Based IdentityCentral Claim
13.6

सच्चे एव सया सन्ने, पंडिए सील संजए ॥13.6॥

The truly learned one is always absorbed in truth, disciplined in virtue and restraint.

The contrast is being developed carefully. The Brahmin scholar is absorbed in texts; the truly learned person is absorbed in truth itself. The difference is between looking at a map and actually traversing the territory. Scripture points toward truth but is not itself the destination. The monk who is "absorbed in truth" has moved from the pointing to the pointed-at.

The simple version: Being absorbed in truth is different from being absorbed in texts about truth. Conduct and restraint are what make the inner stillness possible.

Absorbed in TruthMap vs TerritoryVirtue
13.7

जे हु बंभणे य समणे, तेण सब्भाव भिक्खू ॥13.7॥

The true Brahmin, the true monk — that is the true mendicant.

The teaching converges the two titles onto a single description: the true mendicant. Whether one came from Brahmin tradition or renunciant tradition, the same qualities define the genuine practitioner: non-violence, truth, inner restraint, the absence of attachment and aversion. The Jain teacher does not argue that one tradition is superior — the argument is that both are pointing toward the same qualities.

The simple version: True Brahmin and true monk describe the same thing. The quality of conduct is what matters, not which tradition you belong to.

ConvergenceTrue MendicantEcumenism
13.8

जे बंभणे संजए धीरे, अयारं णाइतिक्कमे ॥13.8॥

The Brahmin who is restrained, courageous, does not transgress right conduct.

The Brahmin who achieves this standard is being honored, not dismissed. The Sutrakritanga is not arguing against Brahminism as such — it is arguing against the version that substitutes ritual knowledge for inner transformation. The Brahmin who practices genuine restraint and courage, who does not transgress right conduct, who lives non-violently — this Brahmin is a genuine practitioner regardless of tradition.

The simple version: The teaching honors anyone who genuinely practices restraint and non-violence, regardless of which tradition they belong to.

Genuine BrahminRestraintTradition Honored
13.9

तेसिं तित्थे पडिबुद्धे, जे अणण्णसरणे ॥13.9॥

For those awakened at the ford, who have no other refuge.

The "ford" is the place of crossing — the spiritual crossing point where the soul passes from bondage to liberation. The person who is "awakened at the ford" has arrived at the point where the crossing is actually taking place. "Having no other refuge" describes the monk's complete commitment: there is no backup plan, no arrangement to return to ordinary life if the practice becomes too difficult.

The simple version: The real practitioner has burned the boats. There is no return planned, no fallback arranged.

The FordComplete CommitmentNo Return
13.10

सम्मत्तदिट्ठी य विरए, कम्मस्स अपिइस्सरे ॥13.10॥

The one with right vision, detached, is not a servant of karma.

"Servant of karma" describes the condition of the ordinary person — the one whose behavior is driven by the unexamined patterns of past action. Without right vision, the impulse to anger, to craving, to possessiveness is followed automatically. The person with right vision sees these impulses for what they are — old karma trying to generate new karma — and does not follow them.

The simple version: Without right vision, you are driven by old patterns without knowing it. With right vision, you can see the patterns and choose not to follow them.

Servant of KarmaPattern RecognitionRight Vision
13.11

एयं च बंभणत्तं से, एयं सामण्णमुत्तमं ॥13.11॥

This is true Brahminhood; this is the excellent monkhood.

The double naming is deliberate — the same qualities earn both titles simultaneously. Stop competing over which tradition is superior and recognize that both traditions at their best are pointing toward the same qualities. The person who embodies right vision, restraint, non-violence, and truthfulness is both a true Brahmin and a true monk — the traditions meet at the level of genuine practice.

The simple version: At the highest level, all genuine traditions point to the same qualities. The competition between traditions is a distraction from the practice both demand.

UnificationSame QualitiesBeyond Competition
13.12

एत्थेव बंभणे बुद्धे, एत्थेव समणे ति वुच्चइ ॥13.12॥

Precisely here is the true Brahmin called wise; precisely here is the monk so called.

The word "precisely here" — in this conduct, in this quality of inner life — is where the titles are earned. Not in the family of birth, not in the years of study, not in social recognition. The label follows the reality, not the other way around. If the reality is present — right vision, non-harm, restraint — the title is warranted. If absent, the title is empty.

The simple version: You earn the title by embodying the qualities, not by being born into them or studying for them.

Precisely HereReality FirstLabel Follows
Part II — Ritual vs. Inner Practice (13–22)
13.13

जे बंभणे अग्गिं सेवइ, जे य हव्वं जुहोइ ॥13.13॥

The Brahmin who tends fires and performs oblations.

The Vedic ritual of fire-tending and oblation is the centerpiece of Brahminic religious practice. These are not criticized as meaningless in themselves — the sutra acknowledges them as genuine religious activities. The question being set up is not whether ritual is sincere but whether sincerity in ritual is sufficient for the highest spiritual goal.

The simple version: The question is not whether ritual is sincere but whether sincerity alone in ritual is enough. Sincerity is necessary but not sufficient.

Vedic RitualSinceritySufficiency Question
13.14

पाणे हिंसइ अयाणमाणे, सो बंभणे किं काहिइ ॥13.14॥

He harms living beings without knowing it — what will that Brahmin accomplish?

The critique is specific and concrete. "Without knowing it" points to a subtler problem: ritual activity can become so routinized that its practitioner stops seeing the living beings affected by it. The person who lights the fire without seeing the insects who die in it is harming without awareness. Unconscious harm is still harm.

The simple version: Ritual can make you blind to the harm you are causing. When practice becomes routine, you stop seeing what you are doing to living beings.

Unconscious HarmRitual BlindnessAwareness
13.15

जे बंभणे बहुसूए, अज्झाइए महासूए ॥13.15॥

The Brahmin who has heard much, who is devoted to learning, who is greatly learned.

The Brahmin scholar's credentials are again laid out fully before the critique arrives. The teaching does not dismiss learning — it acknowledges it, honors it, and then asks what it produces. A scholar with enormous learning who remains unrestrained in conduct, who still harms living beings, who still acts from attachment and aversion — what has the learning accomplished for the soul?

The simple version: Learning is being assessed here, not dismissed. The question is what learning is for and whether it is achieving its purpose.

Assessment of LearningPurposeCredentials
13.16

दव्वट्ठयाए महिलाए, अधिज्जइ सो परक्कमे ॥13.16॥

For the sake of wealth and worldly goods, that one strives with great effort.

The chapter turns a diagnostic eye on the motivation behind much religious learning and practice. When learning is in service of acquiring wealth and status, it is not spiritual learning — it is career building with religious tools. The Brahmin who has spent years mastering scripture in order to earn patronage and live comfortably has used the tools of religion for worldly ends.

The simple version: Religious learning used to gain worldly goods has become a different activity altogether. The tools of the sacred used for worldly profit are still worldly tools.

MotivationWorldly EndsCorruption of Learning
13.17

ण स सामण्णसंपन्ने, ण स बंभणसंपन्ने ॥13.17॥

He is not endowed with monkhood, he is not endowed with true Brahminhood.

The teaching distinguishes between having the quality and bearing the label. The person who has used religious learning for worldly advantage has neither the inner quality of monkhood nor the inner quality of Brahminhood. The labels may still apply socially; inwardly, they are empty.

The simple version: You can have the title without having the thing. The teaching is about the thing.

Quality vs LabelEmpty TitlesInner Quality
13.18

जे य बंभणे किरियावाई, जायकम्माइं करेइ ॥13.18॥

The Brahmin who performs actions according to birth-rites and ceremonies.

Birth-rites and life-cycle ceremonies have genuine social value: they create meaning at life's transitions, connect individuals to community, and affirm shared values. The critique is not of the ceremonies themselves but of the claim that performing them is what constitutes spiritual accomplishment. Social function and spiritual realization are not the same.

The simple version: Performing social religious functions is valuable, but it is not the same as spiritual transformation. Confusing the two is the problem.

Social CeremonySpiritual RealizationDistinction
13.19

जायकम्मे य उव्वाहे, अमारी होज्ज सव्वओ ॥13.19॥

In birth-rites and marriages, let there be no killing of any kind.

The teaching makes a concrete demand: take the ceremonies seriously enough to perform them without harm. Many life-cycle ceremonies in ancient India involved animal sacrifice. The teaching says: keep the ceremony, remove the killing. This is not a rejection of ritual but a refinement of it. Ceremony that can be performed without harm is ceremony that takes its own professed values seriously.

The simple version: If the ceremony claims to honor life, it should not involve taking life. The principle and the practice should match.

Reform of RitualNo KillingConsistency
13.20

एसो सच्चे बंभणे, एसो सामणिए खलु ॥13.20॥

This is truly the Brahmin; this is truly the renunciant.

The teaching has arrived at its positive definition: the true Brahmin and the true monk are the person who performs life without harm, who has refined both ceremony and conduct to the point where the inner values and the outer actions are aligned. The teaching is not against the Brahmin tradition — it is for the best version of it.

The simple version: The best version of any religious tradition is the one where inner values and outer conduct are fully aligned.

Positive DefinitionAlignmentBest Version
13.21

सच्चे धम्मे पयासिए, एत्थ वट्टई सो पंडिए ॥13.21॥

The true teaching has been declared; the learned one moves within it.

The teaching "moves within" the doctrine in the sense that every action is contained within the framework of non-harm, restraint, and truth. There is no part of the monk's day that falls outside the teaching. Waking, eating, speaking, walking, sleeping — all of these are conducted with the teaching as the operating environment.

The simple version: The teaching is not something you do for part of the day. It is the operating environment for everything.

All-Day PracticeOperating EnvironmentNo Exemptions
13.22

परिण्णाय धम्मं संजमं, जाणइ महाणुभागो ॥13.22॥

Having fully understood the teaching and restraint, the great one knows.

Understanding and restraint are again paired — these are the intellectual and practical pillars of the path. Understanding without restraint is philosophy without transformation. Restraint without understanding is discipline without direction. The person who has fully understood the teaching knows why restraint is the path, and this understanding makes the restraint a natural expression of insight rather than an imposed rule.

The simple version: Understanding is why you practice; restraint is the practice. Together they are the complete path.

Understanding and RestraintTwo PillarsNatural Expression
Part III — What Makes a True Monk (23–35)
13.23

से पंडिए जे भिक्खू, जीवाजीवे परिण्णाए ॥13.23॥

The learned monk is the one who has fully understood living and non-living beings.

The learned monk's knowledge is specific: the nature of living beings (souls undergoing karma and rebirth) and non-living things (matter, space, time, and motion). Understanding it is not academic — it determines how the monk treats everything he encounters. Knowing that a flame contains living beings changes how one treats fire. Doctrine produces behavior.

The simple version: The monk's knowledge is applied knowledge. Understanding what is living and what is not determines how every action is performed.

Living and Non-LivingApplied KnowledgeDoctrine to Behavior
13.24

जीवाजीवं परिण्णाय, कम्मं च परिहरेज्ज से ॥13.24॥

Having understood living and non-living, he should avoid karma.

The chain of understanding and action is explicit here. Understanding living and non-living leads directly to avoidance of karma-producing activity. If you know that beings are everywhere and that harming them generates karma, you modify every action to reduce harm. Understanding is not just intellectually satisfying — it has an immediate behavioral output.

The simple version: Understanding changes what you do, not just what you know. The test of understanding is in the behavior it produces.

Understanding to ActionKarma AvoidanceBehavioral Output
13.25

पाणेहिंसे य आदाणे, माया मोसे य परिग्गहे ॥13.25॥

Harming living beings, taking what is not given, deceit, falsehood, and possessiveness.

The five causes of karmic bondage are named: violence, taking what is not given, deceit, falsehood, and possessiveness. These are the negative versions of the five great vows. The monk who has taken the great vows has committed to their exact opposites. This sutra identifies precisely what the vows are against — the specific activities that generate the karma that keeps the soul bound.

The simple version: These five activities are the chains. The great vows are the key that opens each lock.

Five Causes of BondageGreat VowsChains
13.26

एए पंच महव्वए, सव्वे जाणइ पंडिए ॥13.26॥

These five great vows — the learned one knows them all.

Knowing the five great vows means understanding how each one operates in daily life, what situations test each vow, what the subtle forms of each violation look like, and what the inner state must be to maintain each vow without constant vigilance. This detailed practical knowledge distinguishes the trained monk from the person who has merely read about the vows.

The simple version: Knowing the vows means knowing how to actually live them, not just what they are called.

Five Great VowsPractical KnowledgeLiving the Vows
13.27

अहिंसा सच्च अत्थेण, अदत्तादाणमेव य ॥13.27॥

Non-violence, truth in deed, and the non-taking of what is not given.

Three of the five vows are unpacked in terms of "in deed" — meaning in actual behavior, not just in intention. Non-violence "in deed" means not harming through any of the three channels: mind, speech, or body. Truth "in deed" means not speaking, gesturing, or acting in ways that create false impressions. The emphasis on deed keeps the teaching practical and verifiable.

The simple version: The vows are about what you actually do, not just what you intend. Deeds are the evidence of practice.

Deeds not IntentionsThree ChannelsVerifiable
13.28

बंभचेरे य नियमे, अपरिग्गहे य संजमे ॥13.28॥

Celibacy and the rule of non-possessiveness and restraint.

The remaining two vows — celibacy and non-possessiveness — address the two deepest forms of the "mine" structure: I want to possess this person; I want to possess this object. Celibacy is the complete release of the body's most powerful possessive drive. Non-possessiveness is the complete release of the accumulative drive. Together they address what binds the soul most deeply.

The simple version: Celibacy and non-possessiveness both address the same thing — the drive to claim things as "mine." Releasing that drive in its deepest forms is the completion of the vow practice.

CelibacyNon-PossessivenessMine Structure
13.29

एयं धम्मं परिण्णाय, से य बंभणे समणे ॥13.29॥

Having fully understood this teaching, he is both the Brahmin and the monk.

The person who has fully understood the teaching — who knows and lives the five great vows, who practices non-violence as the ground of all action — this person is simultaneously the true Brahmin and the true monk. The distinction between traditions dissolves at the level of full practice. The labels become descriptions of the same accomplished inner life.

The simple version: At the level of full practice, the distinctions between traditions disappear. The person who fully embodies the teaching is what every tradition's title was meant to name.

Traditions DissolvedFull PracticeOne Description
13.30

णाणेण य दंसणेण य, चरित्तेण तवेण य ॥13.30॥

Through knowledge, through right perception, through conduct, through austerity.

The four pillars of Jain practice: knowledge (understanding doctrine and reality), right perception (the correct orientation of the soul toward truth), conduct (the behavioral expression of that orientation), and austerity (the transformative practice that burns existing karma). These four are not sequential stages — they develop together, each supporting the others. Progress in one deepens all four simultaneously.

The simple version: The four pillars of practice strengthen each other simultaneously. You cannot fully advance in one while neglecting the others.

Four PillarsSimultaneous DevelopmentIntegrated Practice
13.31

एत्थ वट्टंति धम्मस्स, सारं जाणंत साहवो ॥13.31॥

In these lies the essence of the teaching, as the wise ones know.

The essence of the teaching is not a set of beliefs about the cosmos — it is these four activities: knowing, perceiving rightly, conducting oneself rightly, and practicing transformative austerity. The teaching's "essence" is processual — it is something you do, not something you hold. The wise ones know this not because someone told them but because they have found it true in their own experience of the path.

The simple version: The heart of the teaching is what you do, not what you believe. Practice is the essence, not doctrine.

EssenceProcessPractice Over Belief
13.32

जे य धम्मं न जाणंति, ते य बद्धा अणाइया ॥13.32॥

Those who do not know the teaching are bound from beginningless time.

"Beginningless time" is the Jain description of the soul's condition before liberation — the soul has been bound since before memory, before the beginning of the current lifetime. The bondage is not a recent event that can be traced to a specific mistake. It is the default condition of the embodied soul. Knowing the teaching is therefore urgent — as the operative knowledge that can begin to undo beginningless bondage.

The simple version: The soul has been bound for longer than anyone can remember. Every moment of genuine practice is working on something very old.

Beginningless BondageUrgencyDefault Condition
13.33

जे य धम्मं विजाणंति, ते भवंति विमोक्खिया ॥13.33॥

Those who truly know the teaching become liberated.

The direct statement of the teaching's promise: know the teaching truly and become liberated. This is not a hope or a probability — it is stated as a consequence. The path has been walked before, many times. The result is known: genuine understanding of the teaching produces liberation. The only question is whether the understanding is genuine.

The simple version: This is the promise: genuine understanding leads to liberation. The path works. The only question is whether you are truly walking it.

PromiseLiberationGenuine Understanding
13.34

एयं च सोच्चा परिण्णाय, विरए य ओहिं ॥13.34॥

Having heard and understood this, he is detached and knows the ocean of existence.

"The ocean of existence" is the cycle of rebirth — vast, deep, difficult to cross. The monk who has heard the teaching, understood it, and become detached has grasped the nature of the ocean he is trying to cross. He knows how deep it is (beginningless), how wide it is (all possible forms of birth), and where the crossing leads (liberation). This complete orientation allows the monk to navigate the ocean with purpose rather than flailing.

The simple version: Knowing the scale of the problem is part of solving it. The monk who understands the full scope of the cycle of rebirth knows exactly why the practice matters.

Ocean of ExistenceScale of ProblemNavigation
13.35

एवं बंभणे समणे, धम्मं जाणइ केवले ॥13.35॥ — iti bemi

Thus the true Brahmin, the true monk, knows the teaching completely. — Thus I say.

The chapter closes with its central thesis restated as its conclusion: the true Brahmin and the true monk are identical in description — both are the person who knows the teaching completely. "Completely" means not partially, not theoretically, not selectively. Complete knowledge of the teaching means complete understanding of the nature of the soul, of karma, of the path, and of liberation. This complete knowledge is the ground from which complete practice and complete liberation become possible. Thus I say.

The simple version: The true Brahmin and the true monk are the same person: one who knows the teaching completely. — Thus I say.

Complete KnowledgeIti BemiSynthesis
