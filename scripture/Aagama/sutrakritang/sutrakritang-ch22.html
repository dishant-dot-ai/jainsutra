Mahasramanaमहाश्रमण

The Great Monk

A lyrical, elevated portrait of the fully accomplished monk. This chapter describes in precise and beautiful terms what complete mastery of the path looks like — a figure of perfect non-violence, inner peace, and radiant equanimity that serves as the ideal toward which all practice points.

जो सव्वभूयहिए, जो सव्वत्थ समाहिए।
जो सव्वपावविरए, सो महासमणो भवे॥

"One who is beneficial to all beings, equanimous in all situations, withdrawn from all evil — such a one is the Great Monk." — Sutrakritanga 22.40

About This Chapter

Mahasramana

Mahasramana — "The Great Monk" — is the second-to-last chapter of the Sutrakritanga and its most lyrical. Having described false monks (Chapter 17), the fourfold community (Chapter 20), and the Jain teaching (Chapter 21), the text now offers a sustained portrait of what full accomplishment actually looks like: the Great Monk who is beneficial to all, equanimous in all situations, withdrawn from all evil.

This chapter is the ideal made visible — the direction every sincere practitioner is walking, the destination the path leads to. Its 40 sutras move through every dimension of the fully accomplished life: non-violence, inner state, how he lives, and the radiance that emanates from complete practice.

The 40 Sutras

Each sutra is presented with the original Prakrit, English translation, and a plain-language commentary.

Part I — Non-Violence in All Forms
22.1

महासमणो सो भणिओ, जो सव्वभूयहिए ॥२२.१॥

He is called the Great Monk who is beneficial to all beings.

The chapter opens by naming the Great Monk's defining quality: being genuinely beneficial to all beings. Not beneficial to some beings while indifferent to others — beneficial to all beings without exception. This universal benefit is not primarily an active quality but a passive one: his presence, his practice, and his existence benefit the world simply by what they are. He harms nothing and in refraining from harm he benefits everything.

The simple version: The Great Monk's basic quality is that his existence is good for everything around him. Simply by being what he is, he helps.

Beneficial to AllGreat MonkUniversal
22.2

न हणइ न हणावेइ, न हणंते सम्मए ॥२२.२॥

He does not harm, does not cause harm, does not approve of those who harm.

The triple formula of complete non-violence — not harming directly, not causing harm indirectly through others, not consenting to or approving harm done by others — is the standard to which the Great Monk holds himself. This three-part formula covers the full range of ways in which violence can occur: direct action, instrumental action through others, and moral complicity in others' violence. The Great Monk is clean of harm in all three dimensions simultaneously.

The simple version: He doesn't harm himself, he doesn't use others to harm, and he doesn't approve when others harm. Three layers of complete non-violence.

Triple Non-ViolenceNo ComplicityComplete
22.3

एगिंदिय बेइंदिय, तेइंदिय चउरिंदिय ॥२२.३॥

One-sensed, two-sensed, three-sensed, four-sensed —

The Jain classification of living beings by the number of senses they possess is invoked here to indicate the full scope of the Great Monk's non-violence. He is gentle with beings at every level of the hierarchy of life, not only with the beings most people instinctively recognize as deserving consideration. The single-sensed being — a plant, a microorganism — is as real and as deserving of care as the five-sensed being.

The simple version: The Great Monk's non-violence extends all the way down to the simplest forms of life — not just the obvious ones.

All Life FormsSenses ClassificationComplete
22.4

पंचिंदिया य ते सव्वे, न हणइ महासमणो ॥२२.४॥

And five-sensed beings — the Great Monk harms none of them.

The progression is completed: five-sensed beings — including humans, animals, and other complex creatures — are added to the complete list, and the sutra affirms that the Great Monk harms none of them. The entire range of living beings is covered. This comprehensive non-violence is not achieved through careful rule-following but through a transformation of the monk's entire relationship to other life. The impulse to harm has been removed at its root.

The simple version: No living being, from the simplest to the most complex, is harmed by the Great Monk. The non-violence is total.

Five-SensedTotal Non-ViolenceTransformed
22.5

न छिंदइ न भिंदइ, न उद्दवेइ ॥२२.५॥

He does not cut, does not break, does not uproot.

Three specific forms of harm are named — cutting, breaking, uprooting — which cover both the direct destruction of living things and the disturbance of their life processes. The Great Monk moves through the world with a quality of care that makes him attentive to even these forms of incidental damage. He does not break branches carelessly, does not step on plants unnecessarily, does not uproot anything that is living. The attention required to maintain this standard is itself a form of mindfulness practice.

The simple version: The Great Monk is careful in how he moves through the physical world. He avoids small harms that most people don't even think about.

Careful MovementMindfulnessSmall Harms
22.6

तसे थावरे य, रक्खइ महासमणो ॥२२.६॥

Both mobile and stationary beings — the Great Monk protects.

Mobile beings (animals, humans) and stationary beings (plants, earth bodies, water bodies, fire bodies, air bodies) together encompass every category of living entity in the Jain framework. The Great Monk does not merely refrain from harming these beings — he "protects" them, in the sense of moving through the world with a quality of care that creates a zone of safety around him. His presence is experienced by other beings not as a threat but as a refuge.

The simple version: Everything that lives — moving or still — is safer because the Great Monk is nearby. His practice is itself a form of protection.

ProtectsMobile and StationaryRefuge
22.7

अद्दंते सीए उण्हे य, भूए वि अच्छेज्ज समाहिए ॥२२.७॥

In heat, in cold, in all conditions — he remains equanimous.

The Great Monk's equanimity is tested by physical conditions and he passes: neither heat nor cold, neither physical comfort nor discomfort, disturbs his settled inner state. This is not the numbness of someone who has suppressed sensation but the stability of someone who has genuinely ceased to be controlled by it. He feels the heat and the cold — perception remains intact — but the distress response that normally accompanies extreme sensation is absent.

The simple version: Hot, cold, comfortable, uncomfortable — his inner state doesn't change. He feels everything; nothing controls him.

Physical EquanimityAll ConditionsSettled
22.8

न य रोसेइ न य तुसेइ, समो सव्वत्थ ॥२२.८॥

He does not become angry; he does not become pleased — equal in all situations.

The Great Monk's equanimity extends beyond physical conditions to social and emotional ones. Anger and elation are the two poles of the reactive emotional life. The person who gets angry at offense and pleased at flattery is being controlled by others' actions. The Great Monk is not controlled: not moved into anger by what would ordinarily provoke anger, not moved into elation by what would ordinarily produce pleasure.

The simple version: He can't be provoked into anger and he can't be worked up into excitement. Both reactions would mean others are controlling him.

No AngerNo ElationEqual
22.9

सव्वरसेसु निव्विण्णो, न य आसत्तो ॥२२.९॥

Dispassionate toward all tastes; not attached.

Taste is one of the most immediate and powerful sensory experiences, and attachment to food is among the most deeply rooted forms of sensory attachment. The Great Monk is dispassionate toward all tastes: he eats what is offered without preference, without relish, without the seeking and comparing that characterizes an attached relationship to food. He eats to sustain practice, not to enjoy sensation.

The simple version: He doesn't have a favorite food, doesn't enjoy eating as a pleasure, doesn't complain about what he's given. Taste stopped mattering to him.

DispassionateTasteNo Attachment
22.10

न य सद्दे न य रूवे, न य गंधे य ॥२२.१०॥

Not in sounds, not in forms, not in smells —

The sutra begins listing the remaining sense objects — sound, form, smell — toward which the Great Monk maintains dispassion. Each can be a hook for attachment: beautiful music, attractive visual forms, appealing fragrances. The Great Monk perceives all of these — sensory perception remains active — but none of them hook him. He can hear beautiful music and remain equanimous; he can see beautiful forms and remain undisturbed.

The simple version: Beautiful sounds, beautiful sights, pleasant smells — none of these pull him off center. He's present to them but not captured by them.

SensesDispassionPresent Not Captured
Part II — The Great Monk's Inner State
22.11

न य फासे य, तत्थ वट्टइ समाहिए ॥२२.११॥

Not in touch either — he remains settled there.

The final sense — touch — completes the list, and the sutra's closing phrase is characteristic: "he remains settled." The Great Monk's inner state is described with this word — settled, composed, at rest — precisely because that is what is most remarkable about him. In the midst of all five sense objects, across all conditions, he is settled. Not withdrawn from experience but settled within it.

The simple version: All five senses are active, all five kinds of sensation are present — and he remains perfectly settled through all of it.

TouchSettledFive Senses
22.12

महासमणो जिइंदिए, जियकसाओ ॥२२.१२॥

The Great Monk has conquered the senses; he has conquered the passions.

Two conquests define the Great Monk's achievement: the senses and the passions. Conquering the senses means they have ceased to be commanding — they convey information but no longer drive action. Conquering the passions means the four roots of karmic bondage — anger, pride, deception, and greed — have been genuinely eliminated, not merely suppressed. These two conquests together constitute the condition of genuine liberation, or close approach to it.

The simple version: The senses report to him; they no longer run him. The passions are gone; they no longer exist in him. This is what conquest means.

Conquered SensesConquered PassionsAchievement
22.13

अविगयबंभयारी, अणगारो ॥२२.१३॥

Unbroken in his practice of celibacy — the homeless one.

"Unbroken" is the specific quality named for the Great Monk's celibacy. Not merely practiced, but unbroken: no lapses, no hedging, no internal erosion of the commitment over time. And "homeless" is paired with it, as if the two qualities belong together. The monk who practices complete celibacy is also completely homeless: neither attached to a body's pleasures nor attached to a place's comforts.

The simple version: His celibacy has never been broken, and he has no home. Both forms of rootedness — in bodily pleasure and in place — are absent.

Unbroken CelibacyHomelessNon-Attachment
22.14

सव्वोवहिए विरओ, परिग्गहविवज्जिओ ॥२२.१४॥

Withdrawn from all means of existence; free of all possessions.

The Great Monk has withdrawn from all means of existence. He possesses nothing. The tools, habits, social roles, and material props through which ordinary people maintain their existence are all absent. This is not poverty in the ordinary sense — it is a completely different relationship to material reality, one in which the soul's existence requires nothing from the material world beyond what is freely given in the moment.

The simple version: He has no tools, no inventory, no property, no means of maintaining himself. He exists in the world without claiming anything from it.

No PossessionsWithdrawnFreedom
22.15

न य कुज्जइ परिग्गहं, जाणंतो ॥२२.१५॥

He does not create possessions — knowing what they are.

The Great Monk's non-possessiveness arises from knowledge, not rule-following. He does not create possessions because he knows what possessions actually are: hooks for karmic attachment, forms of the "mine"-making that perpetuates the soul's bondage. This knowledge is vivid and present, not theoretical. He sees a potential possession for what it is — an invitation to attachment — and the invitation finds no taker.

The simple version: He doesn't avoid possessions because he was told to — he avoids them because he can see exactly what they do. Knowledge is the rule.

Knowledge-BasedNo PossessionsUnderstanding
22.16

अकिंचणो महासमणो, निरावासो ॥२२.१६॥

The Great Monk owns nothing; he has no dwelling.

"Akinchana" — owning nothing, having nothing — is one of the great designations of the Jain ideal. The Great Monk who has achieved this state has eliminated the last hooks of material attachment. He has no home to return to, no possessions to protect, no accumulation that defines a territory of "mine." This total emptiness of possession is experienced not as deprivation but as an extraordinary freedom — the freedom of someone who has nothing to lose.

The simple version: Nothing to protect, nowhere to return to. That is a kind of freedom most people cannot imagine.

AkinchanaNo DwellingTotal Freedom
22.17

सव्वत्थ समभावेण, वट्टइ ॥२२.१७॥

He lives with the attitude of equanimity in all situations.

Equanimity here is described as an "attitude" or orientation — a settled way of approaching all situations — rather than a particular emotional state. It is more fundamental than a feeling. The Great Monk brings this equanimous orientation to every situation without exception: honored or insulted, given good food or poor food, meeting gentle people or hostile ones. The orientation is stable across all variation.

The simple version: Equanimity isn't a mood — it's a way of approaching everything. His approach doesn't change based on what situation he's in.

EquanimityAll SituationsOrientation
22.18

सव्वपाणभूयजीवसत्ते, दयावंतो ॥२२.१८॥

Compassionate toward all breathing beings, all organisms, all living beings, all sentient ones.

The fourfold enumeration covers every possible category: breathing beings, organisms, living beings, sentient ones. The Great Monk's compassion extends to all of it, not as a rule but as an inner reality. He actually feels compassion for every living being. This is not a performance or an achievement; it is the natural expression of a being from whom the passions have been removed. Without passion blocking it, compassion flows naturally in all directions.

The simple version: His compassion isn't selective — it genuinely extends to every form of life. When passion is gone, compassion is what's left.

Universal CompassionNaturalAll Life
22.19

न य लुद्धो न य माई, न य मिच्छाईणो ॥२२.१९॥

Not greedy, not deceitful, not of wrong views.

Three qualities are named in the negative: no greed, no deception, no wrong view. These are three of the great obstacles of the path. The Great Monk's freedom from all three is not a matter of ongoing effortful restraint; these qualities simply are not present. There is nothing to restrain because the roots from which they grew have been eliminated.

The simple version: He's not greedy, he's not sneaky, and he's not confused about what's real. These aren't things he's controlling — they're things that are genuinely absent.

No GreedNo DeceptionNo Wrong View
22.20

न य रोसेइ, न य पसंसइ, समो ॥२२.२०॥

He does not become angry; he does not praise himself; he is equal.

Two specific behavioral absences — anger and self-praise — are named as expressions of his equality. These are among the most visible markers of the passions: anger reveals aversion; self-praise reveals pride. Their absence in the Great Monk is not a matter of polite restraint but of genuine transformation. He does not suppress the urge to anger and self-praise; the urge itself has been removed because the passions that produce it are gone.

The simple version: He never gets angry, he never talks himself up. Not because he's holding himself back — there's nothing to hold back.

No AngerNo Self-PraiseEqual
22.21

सव्वपावविरए, सव्वदुक्खाओ मुक्को ॥२२.२१॥

Withdrawn from all evil, freed from all suffering.

The causal relationship is stated: withdrawal from evil produces freedom from suffering. Evil in the Jain sense is passion-driven action that creates karma; karma creates suffering. When the monk is genuinely withdrawn from all evil — all passion-driven action — he has cut the root of suffering. He may still experience sensations that would be called painful by others, but he does not suffer in the sense of being distressed, disturbed, or afflicted by what happens to him.

The simple version: When you stop creating the cause of suffering, suffering stops. The connection is direct and complete.

No EvilNo SufferingCause and Effect
22.22

आयावेइ उग्गं तवं, अण्णउत्थियपत्थाणं ॥२२.२२॥

He practices intense austerity, going beyond what other teachers require.

The Great Monk's austerity is described as exceeding the standards of other ascetic traditions. The Jain path's full austerities — including fasting to the point of death in the ultimate practice, complete bodily equanimity in all physical conditions, lifelong celibacy — are genuinely more demanding than most other traditions require. The Great Monk practices this fullest form without complaint or display.

The simple version: The Great Monk's practice is genuinely demanding — not ordinary spiritual discipline. He goes as far as the path requires.

Intense AusterityBeyond OthersFull Practice
Part III — How the Great Monk Lives
22.23

भिक्खायरियं चरइ, जहाविहि ॥२२.२३॥

He practices begging for alms — according to the proper method.

"According to the proper method" signals that the Great Monk does not improvise his approach to begging. The Jain tradition has detailed guidelines: the monk should approach only appropriate households, accept only food prepared without causing harm, accept only what is freely offered without manipulation, and take only enough to sustain practice. Every element of the begging practice is an expression of the monk's non-violence and non-possessiveness.

The simple version: Even begging has a right way to do it. The Great Monk does it correctly — not just effectively.

BeggingProper MethodExpression
22.24

न य आहारं अभिलसइ, न य दोसेइ ॥२२.२४॥

He neither craves food nor finds fault with it.

The two dysfunctional relationships with food — craving it and rejecting it — are both absent. The Great Monk is not driven to the begging round by appetite and not disappointed when what is offered does not meet expectations. He is equally at ease with all food or with none. This inner evenness about food — one of the most basic biological drives — demonstrates the depth of his equanimity. If he is even about food, he is likely even about everything.

The simple version: He's not excited when food is good, not upset when it's bad. Food is sustenance for practice; it's not a pleasure to chase or a discomfort to avoid.

No Food CravingNo Fault-FindingEquanimity
22.25

उवसग्गं सहइ, खमेइ य ॥२२.२५॥

He endures disturbances; he is patient.

Disturbances and obstacles — including those deliberately sent by hostile beings — were described in earlier chapters as tests the monk encounters. The Great Monk endures them without breaking and without retaliation. The two words used — "saha" (to endure, bear with) and "khame" (to be patient, forbear) — together indicate both the inner steadiness required and its sustained quality. Patience is not a single act but a continuous disposition.

The simple version: When things go wrong — including when people deliberately cause trouble — he bears it and stays patient. Not once, but always.

EndurancePatienceContinuous
22.26

न कुज्जइ परिस्सहं, सव्वत्थ ॥२२.२६॥

He does not create hardships for himself — in any situation.

This sutra introduces an important qualification on the discussion of austerity. While the Great Monk practices severe austerity, he does not create artificial or unnecessary hardship for himself. The distinction is between the genuine austerity that is part of the practice and the self-punishing behavior that arises from wrong view or hidden pride. The Great Monk's hardship is productive; it is not self-punishment.

The simple version: He accepts hardship as part of the path but doesn't seek it out needlessly. Austerity is a tool, not a performance of suffering.

No Self-PunishmentTool not PerformanceAusterity
22.27

न सेयइ पणीयं, न य कसायं ॥२२.२७॥

He does not seek out pleasant things; he does not seek out harsh things.

The Great Monk is not oriented toward either end of the pleasure-pain spectrum. He is not a pleasure-seeker and not a self-mortifier. This middle quality — genuine equanimity between comfort and discomfort — is what distinguishes the Jain path from both hedonism and extreme asceticism. The Great Monk inhabits a middle ground that is not compromise but the achievement of someone who has transcended both orientations.

The simple version: He's not chasing comfort or chasing suffering. Both chases have stopped, because both are driven by something that's no longer present.

Middle WayNo ChasingTranscendence
22.28

विगयलोहो विगयमोहो, विगयरागो ॥२२.२८॥

Free of greed, free of delusion, free of passion.

Three of the great obstacles are declared absent — not in the process of being overcome, not suppressed — absent. "Vigaya" — gone, departed, dispelled — is the word used. This is a description of achievement, not aspiration. The Great Monk is not working on greed; greed is gone. Not working on delusion; delusion is gone. Not working on passion; passion is gone. This is the portrait of the fully accomplished practitioner.

The simple version: Greed, delusion, and passion are genuinely gone — not held back, not managed. They departed. He achieved that.

GoneAchievementPassions Departed
22.29

न लालेइ न य रोसेइ, समो सव्वेसु ॥२२.२९॥

He does not flatter; he does not resent — equal toward all.

Flattery and resentment are the two faces of a mind still governed by desire: flattery toward those who give you what you want, resentment toward those who deny it. The Great Monk is equal toward all because he desires nothing from anyone. With no desires to be satisfied or frustrated, the entire machinery of flattery and resentment has nothing to run on. He is genuinely equal — not diplomatically, not strategically, but genuinely.

The simple version: He doesn't need to sweet-talk people and he doesn't hold grudges. Both require wanting something from people — and he doesn't.

No FlatteryNo ResentmentEqual
22.30

अप्पणा एव जहा, तहा य अन्ने ॥२२.३०॥

As he regards himself, so he regards others.

The golden rule in its Jain expression returns here as a description of the Great Monk's achieved state. For most people, this rule is an aspiration — difficult to actually achieve because one's own pain feels more real than another's. For the Great Monk, it is simply a description of how things are. Having understood that all souls are equal in their fundamental nature, he regards others exactly as he regards himself — not more, not less, not differently.

The simple version: He genuinely doesn't feel a difference between his own pain and someone else's. That understanding is complete.

Golden RuleSoul EqualityAchieved
22.31

न य मे कोई मित्तो, न य मे कोई अमित्तो ॥२२.३१॥

"There is no one who is my friend; there is no one who is my enemy."

This sutra quotes the inner voice of the Great Monk. The distinction between friend and enemy — the most fundamental division of social reality for most people — has dissolved. Not because he is cold or distant, but because the partial love that constitutes friendship in the ordinary sense has been replaced by universal equanimity. He has no enemies because he has no opponents to his desires; he has no friends in the partial sense because he is equally present with everyone.

The simple version: Friend and enemy are categories of the mind that picks and chooses based on what serves it. That mind is gone. So are the categories.

No FriendNo EnemyUniversal
22.32

सव्वे जीवा समा, एवं वट्टइ ॥२२.३२॥

"All souls are equal" — in this way he lives.

The principle "all souls are equal" is not a philosophical position the Great Monk holds; it is the orientation from which he actually lives. The sutra's structure — principle, then "in this way he lives" — indicates that the equality of all souls has moved from doctrine to perception to lived reality. He does not remind himself to treat all beings equally; equality is what he perceives. The principle has been internalized all the way down.

The simple version: He doesn't try to believe all souls are equal — he actually sees them that way. The knowing has become the living.

All Souls EqualLived RealityInternalized
22.33

सव्वपावविरओ, निव्वाणमभिलसइ ॥२२.३३॥

Withdrawn from all evil, he aspires to liberation.

The description of the Great Monk converges toward its goal. Withdrawn from all evil — all passion-driven action that creates karma — he naturally aspires to liberation. The aspiration itself has been purified of self-seeking: it is not a greedy desire for the bliss of liberation but the natural orientation of a soul that has exhausted its bondage and is drawn toward the freedom that is its original nature.

The simple version: When the causes of bondage are gone, the soul naturally moves toward freedom. Liberation is the soul's home direction.

No EvilNatural AspirationHome Direction
Part IV — The Radiance of Practice
22.34

जत्थ गच्छइ महासमणो, तत्थ होइ पभासिओ ॥२२.३४॥

Wherever the Great Monk goes, there he is luminous.

The chapter moves into genuinely lyrical territory here. The Great Monk is described as "luminous" — radiating a quality that transforms whatever environment he enters. This is not a physical description but a spiritual one: the quality of the Great Monk's inner state is so settled, so purified, that his presence communicates something to those around him. Being in his presence is different from being away from it.

The simple version: The Great Monk changes the atmosphere wherever he goes. People feel something in his presence that they don't feel otherwise.

LuminousRadiancePresence
22.35

तं दट्ठूण सम्मं, बहुए सद्दहंति ॥२२.३५॥

Seeing him rightly, many have correct faith.

The Great Monk's radiance has a specific effect: it produces right faith in those who encounter it. "Seeing him rightly" — not just looking at him but perceiving what he actually is — generates the first jewel of the path in the people around him. This is one of the great practical values of the fully accomplished monk: by being what he is, he makes the path real and accessible to others. He is the teaching made visible.

The simple version: Being around someone who has genuinely achieved something makes you believe it's achievable. He inspires correct faith just by existing.

Right FaithSeeing RightlyTeaching Made Visible
22.36

न य सो आयावइ अत्तुकसट्टुं, न य परं वव्हेइ ॥२२.३६॥

He does not extol himself; he does not disparage others.

This quality — the absence of self-promotion and the absence of disparagement — appears again here in the chapter's elevated, lyrical section. It is worth noting how consistently this quality appears throughout the text's description of the genuine monk. The false monk promotes himself and diminishes others; the great monk does neither. Self-promotion and disparagement are the final, most persistent residues of pride.

The simple version: He never claims credit and never puts others down. Pride, in all its forms, is simply gone.

No Self-PromotionNo DisparagementPride Gone
22.37

पासंति णं सावया, पव्वजिया ॥२२.३७॥

Both laypeople and the ordained see him — and are moved.

The Great Monk's transformative radiance affects both the lay community and the ordained community. His impact is not limited to those who share his form of practice; anyone who encounters him and "sees" — which here means perceives truly — finds something real. The lay community and the ordained community both find something in the Great Monk that speaks to their practice and inspires it.

The simple version: He moves people from every part of the community — not just other monks, but ordinary practitioners too.

All CommunitiesMovedUniversal Impact
22.38

जिणगुणा जिणसासणं, दिट्ठा णेण पयाणुगे ॥२२.३८॥

The qualities of the Jinas, the teaching of the Jinas — following the path, he has seen them.

The Great Monk is not merely following rules; he has seen what the Jinas have seen. The path has led him to the same perception. His qualities are described as "the qualities of the Jinas" — not copies or approximations but the actual qualities that define the liberated ones. This is what the path is for: not merely the improvement of character but the gradual emergence of the nature that the fully liberated beings embody.

The simple version: By following the path, he has come to see and be what the fully liberated ones are. The path leads to the destination — completely.

22.39

सव्वकम्मक्खयं गंतुं, सो महासमणो भवे ॥२२.३९॥

Going toward the exhaustion of all karma — he is the Great Monk.

The final definition of the Great Monk is this: he is moving toward the complete exhaustion of all karma. The "going toward" indicates that even the Great Monk may not have achieved final liberation in this moment; but his movement is certain and irreversible. He has gone too far along the path to turn back. Every quality described in this chapter is a step on this single journey toward the complete freedom that is liberation.

The simple version: The Great Monk is heading toward total freedom — and nothing is going to stop him. He's too far along the path.

22.40

जो सव्वभूयहिए, जो सव्वत्थ समाहिए।
जो सव्वपावविरए, सो महासमणो भवे॥ — iti bemi

One who is beneficial to all beings, equanimous in all situations, withdrawn from all evil — such a one is the Great Monk. — Thus I say.

The chapter closes with the featured verse restored as the final word, sealed with "iti bemi." Three qualities define the Great Monk in their most condensed form: beneficial to all beings, equanimous in all situations, withdrawn from all evil. The progression moves from his effect on the world, to his inner state, to his relationship to action. These three are the signature of the fully accomplished practitioner — the direction every sincere monk is walking, the reality the Great Monk has achieved. This is what Mahavira says the path produces. This is the ideal, made plain.

The simple version: Good to all. Steady in all. Free from all evil. That is the Great Monk, and that is the path's destination, described in its simplest and most beautiful form. Thus says Mahavira.
