Sutrakritanga Sutra

Equal SeeingNapumsaka

Attachment is the problem — always, in every form, toward every being.

A shorter parallel to Chapter 4, this chapter universalizes the teaching on non-attachment. Desire is the obstacle regardless of its object. The monk who sees all beings equally — without attraction or aversion — is already close to liberation.

सव्वेसु चेव जीवेसु, समदंसी हवेज्ज जो।
अगारम्मि अणागारे, तस्स मोक्खो न दूरओ॥

"One who sees equally in all living beings — in household and homeless — for such a one, liberation is not far." — Sutrakritanga 5

About This Chapter

Napumsaka

The fifth chapter is a shorter companion to Chapter 4. It addresses monks regarding attachment toward persons of the third gender — those who are neither fully male nor female in the Jain classification. The teaching is identical in structure: attachment is the problem, regardless of its object. The Jain tradition recognized three genders, and the teaching on non-attachment is explicitly extended to all three.

The chapter's central contribution is universalization. By the end of Chapter 5, the teaching on non-attachment covers all possible forms of sensory craving directed toward persons. The chapter closes with the teaching on equal seeing — seeing the soul-nature equally in every living being, in every social position. This equal seeing is the positive vision of liberation that emerges after all forms of attachment have been understood and relinquished.

The 12 Sutras

Each verse is presented with the original Ardhamagadhi Prakrit, English translation, and commentary.

Part I — The Teaching Extended
5.1

जहा इत्थीसु संगम्मि, तहा नपुंसएसु वि। ॥५.१॥

Just as in the case of attachment to women, so too in the case of attachment to persons of the third gender.

The chapter opens by establishing its relationship to Chapter 4. The teaching is not being introduced fresh — it is being extended. Whatever was said about the danger of attachment in Chapter 4 applies equally here. The Jain philosophical tradition recognized three genders — male, female, and napumsaka — and the teaching on non-attachment is explicitly applied to all three. The principle is that attachment is the problem, and its object does not change the nature of the problem.

The simple version: The same teaching applies here as in the previous chapter. The object of attachment changes; the nature of attachment does not.

ExtensionSame PrincipleUniversal
5.2

मा पमायए भिक्खू, नपुंसएसु संगमे। ॥५.२॥

The monk should not be negligent — he should not practice attachment toward persons of the third gender.

The direct address to the monk repeats the opening of Chapter 4, signaling that this is a parallel warning rather than a new or different one. Negligence — the allowing of the mind to wander into channels of craving without awareness — is the same failure whether it arises in response to one type of person or another. The monk's vigilance must be complete and impartial, covering every form that attachment might take.

The simple version: The monk must be equally alert to all forms of attachment. There is no version of this that is acceptable or harmless.

VigilanceAll FormsNegligence
5.3

संसारे वड्ढए मग्गो, नपुंसाणमवि सो। ॥५.३॥

The path through this attachment also increases wandering in the cycle of birth and death.

The mechanics of karma-binding are the same regardless of the object of attachment. Craving generates karma; karma drives rebirth; rebirth is suffering. The particular object of craving does not change this chain of causation. The sutra is making a universality claim: the teaching about attachment is not conditional on any specific type of person or relationship. It applies wherever attachment arises.

The simple version: Attachment of this kind binds the soul to rebirth, regardless of who the attachment is toward. The karma mechanism doesn't distinguish.

RebirthKarma MechanismUniversal Law
5.4

न तेण सक्किज्जइ धम्मो, न सील न बंभचेर। ॥५.४॥

By such a one, the teaching cannot be practiced, nor celibacy, nor moral conduct.

This is a direct parallel to Chapter 4's teaching: the monk who is captured by this form of attachment cannot maintain the three pillars of the monastic path. The repetition across chapters is deliberate — the Sutra is establishing that these pillars are incompatible with attachment in any form. Celibacy is not merely physical continence; it is a total orientation of the mind away from sensory craving, and any form of this craving undermines it.

The simple version: Any strong attachment — whatever its form — destroys the monk's ability to practice. The specifics don't matter; the principle holds.

Three PillarsCelibacyPractice Destroyed
5.5

इंदियाणि अजिए भिक्खू, बज्झए न य मुच्चए। ॥५.५॥

The monk who has not conquered the senses is bound and not freed.

Conquest of the senses is the necessary precondition for liberation. This sutra states the negative formulation: without that conquest, the monk remains bound. Bound by what? By the unmastered senses that respond to pleasant and unpleasant stimuli with craving and aversion, generating karma with every unmonitored reaction. The unmastered monk may follow the outer form of monastic life — wearing the robes, keeping the schedule — but without inner mastery, liberation is not occurring.

The simple version: Outer forms of practice are not enough. Without inner mastery of the senses, the monk is still bound.

Sense MasteryBoundInner vs Outer
5.6

एयं विण्णाय पंडिए, नपुंसएसु न पसज्जए। ॥५.६॥

The learned one, understanding this, does not become attached to persons of the third gender.

Understanding — genuine, transformative understanding, not intellectual acknowledgment — is the medicine for attachment in all its forms. The learned one who truly understands the chapter does not need rules and prohibitions backed by social pressure; the understanding itself removes the craving. This is the Jain teaching on right knowledge as the root of right practice: when you truly see the nature of attachment, attachment loses its power.

The simple version: Real understanding removes the attachment. The monk who truly sees what's happening doesn't need to fight it — it dissolves.

Understanding FreesRight KnowledgeNatural Freedom
Part II — Equal Seeing
5.7

सव्वेसु चेव जीवेसु, समदंसी हवेज्ज जो। ॥५.७॥

One who sees equally in all living beings — for such a one, liberation is not far.

The chapter's featured verse introduces the positive teaching. Equal seeing — seeing the soul-nature in every living being without the distortions of preference and aversion, of attraction and repulsion — is itself a description of liberation beginning to happen. The monk who has cultivated equal seeing has removed the primary engine of karma-binding: the differential response to experience that craves some beings and rejects others.

The simple version: Equal seeing in all beings — that is what liberation looks like from the inside. The monk who sees this way is already close to freedom.

Equal SeeingLiberation NearAll Beings
5.8

अगारम्मि अणागारे, सव्वेसु समभावए। ॥५.८॥

In the householder and in the homeless, he maintains equanimity toward all.

The sutra specifies the scope of equal seeing: it extends across the social categories of householder (the person living ordinary worldly life) and homeless (the renunciant monk). The monk does not see himself as superior to householders or other monks as his peers and worldly people as below. He sees the soul-nature in each, living in different circumstances but all engaged in the long journey toward liberation. Equanimity here is not condescension but genuine recognition.

The simple version: The freed monk doesn't rank people by their spiritual status. He sees the soul in everyone, wherever they are on the path.

HouseholderHomelessNo Hierarchy
5.9

न पिए न य दोस्सए, न रज्जए न विरज्जए। ॥५.९॥

He has no favorites and no enemies — he is neither attracted nor repelled.

Favorites and enemies are the two poles of partial seeing — the seeing that is filtered through personal preference. The monk with no favorites and no enemies is not emotionally flat; he is emotionally clear. He can engage with everyone fully, can be genuinely helpful and compassionate, precisely because he is not distorted by the turbulence of attraction and repulsion that compromises the quality of his attention.

The simple version: No favorites, no enemies — just clear, undistorted attention for every person. This is what equal seeing feels like in practice.

No FavoritesNo EnemiesClear Attention
5.10

आयाणं च परं चेव, एगत्तं संपजाणए। ॥५.१०॥

He recognizes the oneness of self and other.

"Oneness of self and other" does not mean that the individual souls merge into one — Jain philosophy maintains that souls remain distinct even in liberation. It means that the conscious, knowing principle — the soul — is the same kind of thing in every living being. Recognizing this oneness removes the felt distinction between self and other that motivates all harm: if what I am is what you are, harming you is harming what I am.

The simple version: The soul in me is the same kind of thing as the soul in you. That recognition makes harm to others impossible.

OnenessSelf and OtherNon-Violence Root
5.11

सव्वेसिं च जीवाणं, सुहं एइच्छए तहा। ॥५.११॥

For all living beings equally, he wishes happiness.

Universal goodwill — wishing happiness for all living beings without exception — is the emotional counterpart of equal seeing. It is active rather than passive: not just the absence of ill-will but the presence of genuine care. This goodwill is possible because the monk sees the soul-nature in every being, and the soul in every being is, in its deepest nature, oriented toward freedom and peace. To wish all beings happiness is to wish them all liberation.

The simple version: The freed monk doesn't just avoid harming others — he genuinely wants good for all beings. Equal seeing produces equal care.

Universal GoodwillAll BeingsCompassion
5.12

एवं नपुंसयं नाऊण, जाणिया सव्वसंपहू। ॥५.१२॥

Thus, having understood the chapter on the third gender, one knows all things fully. — iti bemi

The closing "iti bemi" seals the chapter. The parallel with Chapter 4 and the earlier chapters is complete: the teaching on non-attachment has been universalized. Attachment is the problem regardless of its object — regardless of who it is directed toward, regardless of what form it takes, regardless of the social or personal circumstances in which it arises. The monk who has understood this universality has understood the complete teaching on attachment. Equal seeing is the door, and liberation is not far.

The simple version: The teaching is universal: attachment is the problem, always, in every form. When you see this completely, you know everything that needs to be known. The teacher has spoken.

Iti BemiUniversal TeachingCompletion
