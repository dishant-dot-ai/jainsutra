The Wanderer

The wandering monk endures all hardships — cold, heat, hunger, hostility — with perfect equanimity. Equanimity is not passivity: it is the highest form of engagement with reality.

सीयं उसिणं य खुत्पिपासं, दंसमसयं च अहियासए।
एवमेव परिसहे जए, गिहे य संगं न करेज्ज सो॥

"Cold and heat, hunger and thirst, insect bites — the monk endures all these. Conquering the hardships, he does not form attachment to the household life." — Sutrakritanga 11

About This Chapter

The Wanderer

Chapter 11 of the Sutrakritanga is a meditation on the wandering monk — possibly a portrait of Mahavira himself during his twelve-and-a-half years of intense solitary practice. The monk moves from village to village without attachment to place, person, or comfort. The world throws everything at him: hostility, ridicule, physical hardship, extreme weather, the pull of family, the temptation of hospitality.

The chapter's central teaching is that equanimity is not passivity. The monk who endures all things unchanged is not absent from experience — he is more fully present to it than anyone. Equanimity is the highest form of engagement: full contact with reality, zero reactivity. This chapter shows what that looks like from the outside and from the inside.

Part I — The Way of Wandering (1–12)
11.1

एस पंथो अणगाराणं, जे पव्वइया महब्बया ॥11.1॥

This is the path of the homeless ones — those who have gone forth under the great vows.

The chapter opens by naming the path directly: the monk's life is the path itself. The word "homeless" is not a description of poverty but of freedom — the monk has voluntarily released attachment to shelter, comfort, and belonging. The great vows — non-violence, truthfulness, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession — are the monk's only dwelling. Everything else is background, passing through.

The simple version: The monk's life is the path. There is no road outside the practice of the great vows.

RenunciationGreat VowsHomelessness
11.2

चरइ मुणी एयकए, गामाणुगामं दूइज्जमाणे ॥11.2॥

The monk wanders alone, moving from village to village.

The wandering is not aimless — it is the structure of the monk's practice. By moving constantly, the monk avoids accumulating bonds of place, of habit, of relationship with specific households. Each village is new; each day is new. Attachment cannot take root in soil that keeps shifting. The solitude is also significant: the monk walks alone because the inner work is ultimately solitary, even when surrounded by people.

The simple version: Constant movement prevents the settling of attachment. The monk wanders so that nothing can be called home.

WanderingNon-AttachmentSolitude
11.3

सीयं उसिणं अहियासए, जं दंसेंति मसया य ॥11.3॥

He endures cold and heat, and the biting of insects.

The physical hardships are catalogued with precision — cold, heat, insect bites. These are not poetic flourishes but the actual conditions of a monk's outdoor life. Sleeping without shelter, walking barefoot, eating whatever alms bring — the body is exposed to everything. The teaching is not that the monk is made of stone but that the monk does not run from what the body encounters. Sensation arises; the monk remains.

The simple version: The body will feel everything. The practice is to stay present through discomfort without reacting.

EndurancePhysical Hardship22 Hardships
11.4

न सोइ न हरिसइ, समे भावे विहरमाणए ॥11.4॥

He neither grieves nor rejoices — he dwells in an even state.

This is not numbness. The monk who neither grieves nor rejoices is not dead inside — he is fully alive but no longer reactive. Grief and rejoicing are both responses to events that have been judged as bad or good. The monk who dwells in an even state has not stopped perceiving; he has stopped adding his own panic or excitement to what perception delivers. This is equanimity as active practice.

The simple version: Equanimity is not about feeling nothing. It is about feeling everything without being thrown off balance.

EquanimityEven-MindednessNon-Reactivity
11.5

विजहइ पिंडेसणाए, भिक्खू जायगिलाणे य ॥11.5॥

The monk goes forth for alms, whether healthy or ill.

The monk does not pause the practice when unwell. The ordinary person rests, withdraws, demands care when ill. The monk continues. This is not cruelty to the body — it is the monk's refusal to let physical condition determine the practice. The body is a vehicle for practice, not an obstacle to it. Walking for alms while ill is itself the teaching of non-attachment in action.

The simple version: Practice continues regardless of how the body feels. Illness is not a reason to stop — it is a reason to deepen.

Alms RoundContinuity of PracticeNon-Attachment to Body
11.6

न कुज्झइ परेहिं, न य हरिसइ पूयणाए ॥11.6॥

He is not angered by others, nor pleased by their worship.

The monk walking through a village will encounter both hostility and reverence. Some households will offer abuse; some will bow and offer food with great ceremony. The instruction is symmetrical: neither extreme moves him. The anger that hostility provokes and the pleasure that reverence produces are both disturbances of the inner state. The monk is training to remain at the same level regardless of what the world delivers.

The simple version: Insults and compliments are equally traps. The monk who can be angered OR flattered has not yet achieved equanimity.

EquanimityPraise and BlameSymmetry
11.7

जे केइ उवसग्गा, ते सव्वे अहियासए मुणी ॥11.7॥

Whatever trials come, the monk endures them all.

The word for "trials" refers to afflictions coming from outside — harsh people, biting animals, extreme weather, physical illness, social hostility. These are the twenty-two hardships the Jain tradition enumerates systematically. The teaching is that the monk's practice must be broad enough to include all of them. Selective endurance — "I can handle cold but not insults" — is not real equanimity.

The simple version: Real equanimity has no exceptions. If there is one hardship that breaks the monk's composure, the training is incomplete.

22 HardshipsComplete EnduranceTrials
11.8

संखाए पाणे न हिंसए, विरयस्स सच्चमेयं ॥11.8॥

Understanding living beings, he does not harm them — this is the truth of the one who is restrained.

The wandering monk moves carefully because movement itself can cause harm. Insects, microorganisms, earthworms, and small creatures inhabit every path. The monk who understands this does not simply avoid large obvious harm — he moves with awareness of the full field of life around him. Non-violence is not just the avoidance of murder; it is the constant orientation of the monk's entire movement through the world.

The simple version: Non-violence is how the monk walks, not just what he avoids. Every step taken with awareness is a step of practice.

Non-ViolenceAwarenessLiving Beings
11.9

न आवसइ रत्तिं, अविणीयाण आवसे ॥11.9॥

He does not stay the night in dwellings of the undisciplined.

The instruction about where to sleep is a teaching about what environments sustain the practice. A householder's home, however generous, is full of attachments — possessions, family bonds, the smells and sounds of ordinary life. The monk who sleeps there night after night begins to lose the edge of renunciation. The teaching is protective: the monk's inner state is shaped by the environment he inhabits, and he must choose accordingly.

The simple version: Environment shapes the practitioner. Monks do not dwell where the conditions work against their practice.

EnvironmentRestraintRenunciation
11.10

न य तत्थ विसेज्जेज्जा, जत्थ बज्झंति माणवा ॥11.10॥

He does not settle in a place where people become bound.

"Bound" refers to entanglement in the attachments of worldly life — property, relationship, reputation, daily comfort. The monk sees these bonds operating around him constantly. His choice to not settle is not misanthropy — it is clear-eyed recognition that the conditions of household life produce bondage. By moving on, the monk both protects his own practice and lives as a visible example of the alternative.

The simple version: The monk does not put down roots where others are entangled. He keeps moving so that what binds them cannot bind him.

BondageNon-SettlementFreedom
11.11

रहस्से य विविक्ते य, सयणासणजोगिए ॥11.11॥

In seclusion and in solitude, he applies himself to sleeping and sitting.

Seclusion is the condition the monk seeks for rest. Not the seclusion of withdrawal from duty, but the seclusion of removing unnecessary inputs — noise, social pressure, the demand to respond. In the quiet of a forest or empty space, the monk can sit without the constant perturbation of community life, and the practice deepens. Rest itself becomes practice.

The simple version: Quiet solitude is not laziness — it is the condition where deep practice happens. The monk seeks it deliberately.

SeclusionSolitudeMeditation
11.12

एवं विहरइ धीरे, वओगसंपन्ने विहरमाणए ॥11.12॥

Thus the courageous one wanders, endowed with the excellence of conduct.

The word "courageous" completes the first section. Wandering without fixed shelter, without known hospitality, without protection — this requires genuine courage. The monk is not wandering because he has nowhere else to go; he is wandering because he has understood that the wandering itself is the path. The excellence of conduct — the vows, the restraint, the awareness — is what gives the wandering its meaning.

The simple version: It takes real courage to live without security. The monk's wandering is brave, not desperate.

CourageConductPurpose
Part II — Hostility and Equanimity (13–22)
11.13

जं च लोगो पयासइ, तं सव्वं अहियासए ॥11.13॥

Whatever the world inflicts upon him, he endures it all.

The world's hostility toward the monk is a recurring theme in the Sutrakritanga. People mock ascetics, throw things, drive them away, accuse them of laziness or fakery. This sutra acknowledges the full weight of that hostility and says: endure it all. The teaching is not that the world is wicked but that the monk's practice must be large enough to hold whatever comes — praise, abuse, silence, violence.

The simple version: The world will test you. The monk's response is always the same: endure, remain, continue.

HostilityEnduranceWorld
11.14

आलुद्धा अणुसासंति, मूढा वि खलु पंडियं ॥11.14॥

The greedy instruct him; even the foolish lecture the learned monk.

One of the specific humiliations the monk must endure: being lectured by people who know far less but who feel entitled to instruct. The greedy householder who cannot control his own possessions tells the monk how to live. The person who knows nothing of the inner life offers spiritual advice. The monk's response is equanimity — not contempt for the lecturer, not internal resentment, just steady absorption of the moment.

The simple version: Ignorant people will try to instruct you. Receiving their words with patience is itself part of the practice.

PatienceHumilitySocial Endurance
11.15

अक्कोसंति पणइत्था, थद्धा थद्धं अभिक्खणं ॥11.15॥

The arrogant repeatedly insult the monk.

To the outside observer, the monk's refusal to be moved, to beg for mercy, to alter his conduct under social pressure looks like stubbornness. From the inside, it is steadiness. The arrogant person cannot tolerate being unable to disturb the monk, and so the insults escalate. The monk receives each one the same way he received the first.

The simple version: Refusing to react makes some people angrier. The monk holds steady anyway — not out of stubbornness, but out of understanding.

ArroganceSteadinessInsults
11.16

न तं तुलेज्ज मणसा, जं दुक्खं परिसहेज्ज सो ॥11.16॥

He should not weigh in his mind the suffering of the hardship he is enduring.

The instruction is subtle: do not measure the hardship. The ordinary mind constantly evaluates — "this is too much," "this is manageable," "when will this end?" Each evaluation is a layer added to the raw sensation of the hardship. The monk is asked to stop the mental calculus. The hardship is what it is. Adding measurement to it does not reduce it; it actually increases suffering by adding mental resistance to the physical fact.

The simple version: Don't measure your suffering. Counting and evaluating hardships makes them heavier, not lighter.

Mental CalculusEquanimityHardship
11.17

उड्ढं अहे य तिरियं च, सव्वओ समिए मुणी ॥11.17॥

Above, below, and in all directions — the monk is careful on all sides.

Carefulness in all directions refers to the monk's constant awareness of living beings in the environment. The Jain understanding of life includes beings above, below, and all around. The monk who walks carelessly, breathes carelessly, or moves carelessly causes harm to living beings he cannot see. The monk's awareness must extend in every direction, at every moment, as a continuous act of non-violent orientation.

The simple version: Non-violence is 360 degrees. The monk must be aware of life in every direction, not just what is directly in front.

Non-Violence360-Degree AwarenessLiving Beings
11.18

न य सद्धिं वसेज्जासि, थी य पुरिसे य कीलए ॥11.18॥

He should not live together with those engaged in play and sensory enjoyment.

The instruction is about the kind of social environment that undermines the practice. "Play" means the informal mixing of households, the conversations that lighten and distract, the social ease that gradually erodes the monk's edge of renunciation. The monk who lives among people who are relaxed in their worldly bonds begins to relax too. The instruction is to maintain the conditions that support practice.

The simple version: Social environments have gravity. The monk must choose environments that pull toward the practice, not away from it.

EnvironmentSocial GravityVigilance
11.19

न कहेज्जासि सो पंथं, न सुणेज्जासि मिच्छयं ॥11.19॥

He should not speak the false path, nor listen to falsehood.

The monk's speech is disciplined not just by truthfulness but by relevance. The monk does not engage in spreading doctrines that will lead people toward greater bondage. The instruction to not listen to falsehood is equally demanding — the monk who passively absorbs false teachings allows them to muddy the inner clarity he has developed. Both hearing and speaking are portals through which wrong views can enter or spread.

The simple version: Words are not neutral. False teaching harms both speaker and listener, and the monk avoids both.

Right SpeechFalse TeachingTruth
11.20

एयाइं परिण्णाय, सव्वाइं परिहरेज्ज सो ॥11.20॥

Understanding all these things, he should abandon them all.

Understanding is the prerequisite for abandonment. The monk does not abandon attachment, possessions, and harmful habits through force of will alone — he understands them first, sees what they produce, sees how they bind, and then abandonment follows naturally. This is why the Sutrakritanga spends so much time on understanding: the teaching presupposes that real knowledge changes behavior. Superficial knowledge changes nothing.

The simple version: Understanding comes before letting go. You cannot abandon what you have not yet truly seen.

UnderstandingAbandonmentKnowledge
11.21

से भिक्खू अणगारे, णाणदंसणसंपन्ने ॥11.21॥

That monk, the homeless one, is endowed with knowledge and vision.

Knowledge and vision are the monk's two fundamental faculties. Knowledge refers to the understanding of doctrine, of cause and effect, of the nature of karma. Vision refers to the right orientation of the soul — seeing reality correctly, not through the distortions of craving, aversion, or delusion. These two are not separate — right vision produces right knowledge, and right knowledge confirms right vision. Together they are the monk's inner equipment.

The simple version: The monk is not just disciplined — he is equipped with understanding. Restraint without knowledge is just suppression.

KnowledgeRight VisionInner Equipment
11.22

अप्पं च परं च जाणइ, आयाणे य पयाणे य ॥11.22॥

He knows the self and others, knows taking and giving.

The monk's knowledge is practical and relational. To know the self is to understand one's own soul as a field of karmic action. To know others is to recognize that all living beings share the same basic predicament: bound by karma, capable of liberation. Knowing "taking and giving" means understanding what actions produce bondage and what actions produce liberation. This is applied knowledge, not philosophy.

The simple version: The monk knows himself, understands others, and understands consequences. This triple knowing is what makes the practice real.

Self-KnowledgeApplied KnowledgeKarma
Part III — The Wanderer's Inner State (23–34)
11.23

मोणं समाहिए जाणइ, जाणइ विरए य अप्पाणं ॥11.23॥

He knows silence and composure; he knows the restrained self.

Silence here is not the absence of speech alone — it is the inner stillness that is the monk's natural state when external disturbances have been reduced. Composure is the active expression of that inner stillness in the face of external events. The "restrained self" is the self that has learned to respond rather than react — where action arises from understanding rather than from impulse. These three — silence, composure, restraint — describe the monk's normal inner landscape.

The simple version: The monk's inner world is quiet, steady, and self-directed. These are not achieved in one moment — they are cultivated over years of practice.

SilenceComposureInner Life
11.24

जाणइ लोगं जाणइ कम्मं, जाणइ आसवपज्जवे ॥11.24॥

He knows the world, knows karma, knows the channels of influx.

The channels of influx — the pathways through which karma enters the soul — are the monk's primary area of study. These include the activities of mind, speech, and body; the passions of anger, pride, deceit, and greed; and carelessness in the various aspects of conduct. Knowing these channels is not academic — it is the monk's practical map for which activities to stop, which passions to uproot, which habits to change.

The simple version: The monk knows exactly how karma gets in. Understanding the channels is the first step to closing them.

Karma InfluxPassionsPractical Knowledge
11.25

से नो पमायए भिक्खू, नो य गिद्धे रसे रए ॥11.25॥

That monk is not negligent, and is not attached to tastes and pleasures.

Negligence is the enemy of all practice. A moment of carelessness with the tongue produces speech karma. A moment of carelessness with the hand causes physical harm. A moment of negligence in the mind allows old attachments to resurface. The monk who has made significant progress remains vigilant because he knows how quickly negligence undoes what discipline has built.

The simple version: Negligence is expensive. The monk who has worked hard on his practice guards it carefully against the moments when carelessness could undo everything.

VigilanceNegligenceNon-Attachment
11.26

न पडिबंधे कत्थइ, मुणी लहुइज्जमाणे ॥11.26॥

The monk does not form attachment anywhere, even when treated with respect.

Respectful treatment is a trap that abuse is not. Abuse is easy to remain unattached to. But when someone is genuinely kind, genuinely admiring, genuinely grateful — the pull toward attachment is strong. The monk who has endured hostility with equanimity must now endure respect with equal equanimity. The instruction is clear: do not form attachment anywhere — not to places, not to people, and not to the feeling of being valued.

The simple version: Respect is as trapping as insult. The monk who can only resist hatred but not warmth has not yet found true freedom.

Non-AttachmentRespectFreedom
11.27

खेत्तं वत्थु पसू दारं, णाइसंगं न सेवए ॥11.27॥

Fields, property, livestock, family — he does not associate with attachment to relatives.

The list is a complete inventory of what a householder's life consists of — land, possessions, animals, and family bonds. The monk has voluntarily released all of these. The instruction is not that he avoids people who have these things but that he does not re-enter the psychological structure of ownership, of "mine and ours." The monk's release is internal, not just external.

The simple version: The monk has released the ownership structure of his mind, not just his property. He walks through the world without mentally claiming anything.

Non-PossessivenessReleaseOwnership
11.28

चरइ एगागि संजए, ण छुहा ण पिपासए भए ॥11.28॥

He wanders alone, restrained, without fear of hunger or thirst.

Fear of hunger and fear of thirst are among the most primitive human anxieties. The monk who has genuinely released attachment to the body's survival has also released these fears. This is not recklessness — the monk still accepts alms and eats appropriately. But the anxiety that drives ordinary people to hoard, to plan, to worry about tomorrow's meals — this fear is absent. The monk trusts the path to provide what the body needs.

The simple version: The monk does not fear going without. This fearlessness around basic needs is itself a form of liberation.

FearlessnessHungerLiberation
11.29

निम्ममे निरहंकारे, छेयं भासइ पंडिए ॥11.29॥

Without possessiveness, without ego — the learned one speaks wisely.

The quality of the monk's speech changes when possessiveness and ego are absent. The person who speaks to defend their position, to preserve their reputation, to demonstrate their knowledge — this person's speech is always slightly bent. The monk who has nothing to defend, no reputation to protect, no status to maintain — this monk can say exactly what is true without distortion. Wisdom in speech is the natural result of internal freedom.

The simple version: Speech becomes honest when there is nothing to protect. The monk with no ego has no motive to distort the truth.

EgolessnessWise SpeechTruth
11.30

जे हु अत्थे मुणिस्स से, तं सम्मं परिजाणिया ॥11.30॥

Whatever is the goal of the monk — that he understands completely.

The monk's goal is singular: liberation. Every other activity is in service of that single aim. The monk who understands this completely does not become confused about priorities, does not get pulled into secondary goals, and does not waste practice on activities that feel spiritual but move away from liberation. The clarity of goal produces clarity of path.

The simple version: The monk has one aim — liberation. When the goal is absolutely clear, every decision about conduct becomes simple.

Singularity of GoalLiberationClarity
11.31

जाणइ आगमणगमणं, जाणइ लोगस्स पारगए ॥11.31॥

He knows coming and going; he knows who has crossed to the other shore of the world.

"Coming and going" refers to the cycle of birth and death — the monk understands the mechanism of rebirth, how karma produces new births, how liberation ends the cycle. Knowing "who has crossed" means recognizing the liberated beings — the Tirthankaras, the perfected souls — as his models and guides. This knowledge orients the monk: he knows where he is on the path and who has gone before him.

The simple version: The monk understands the cycle of rebirth and knows the direction out of it. He is not wandering blindly — he knows where the path leads.

RebirthLiberationTirthankaras
11.32

तस्स धम्मं पयासामि, सुयमेयं सुधम्मिया ॥11.32॥

I declare this teaching for him — this has been heard, O followers of right conduct.

The chapter shifts to the voice of the transmitter — the teaching being offered here is not invented but received. "This has been heard" invokes the lineage of transmission: from the liberated Mahavira through the disciples who heard him, through the chain of teachers who preserved the teaching, to the listener now. The instruction to right-conduct followers is a direct address: this teaching is for those already committed to the path.

The simple version: This teaching was received, not invented. It comes from the liberated ones, and it is being passed to those who are ready to walk the path.

TransmissionLineageAuthentic Teaching
11.33

सव्वमेत्थ समाहित्ता, सव्वे परिसहे सहे ॥11.33॥

Composing himself in all things, he endures all hardships.

The completeness of the instruction is emphasized again: all things, all hardships. There is no domain of life excluded from the monk's composure, and there is no hardship excluded from the monk's endurance. This totality is what distinguishes genuine practice from selective asceticism. Selective asceticism — enduring the hardships one finds manageable while avoiding the rest — is comfortable practice. Real practice extends to everything.

The simple version: There are no exceptions. The monk's composure covers all situations; the monk's endurance covers all hardships.

CompletenessAll HardshipsGenuine Practice
11.34

एवं खु मुणिणो पंथो, जे लोगं परिजाणिया ॥11.34॥ — iti bemi

Such is indeed the path of the monk who has fully understood the world. — Thus I say.

The chapter closes with the seal of the teaching. The path belongs to the monk who has understood — not merely read or heard, but truly comprehended — how the world operates, what binds the soul, and what leads to freedom. The phrase "thus I say" is the tradition's signature on each teaching, affirming that this is the authentic transmission of Mahavira's word as it was received and preserved by his disciples across generations.

The simple version: Understanding the world completely is what makes you a genuine wanderer. The monk's path and his understanding of the world are one and the same thing.
