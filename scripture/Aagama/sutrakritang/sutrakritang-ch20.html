Sutrakritanga Sutra

Sthanangaस्थानांग

The Fourfold Order

The Chaturvidha Sangha — the fourfold Jain community of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen — each with their own vows, duties, and path to liberation. The community itself is a vehicle of the teaching.

साहू य साहुणी य, सावया य सावियाओ।
चत्तारि वि य सिक्खंति, धम्मो एसो जिणाण॥

"Monks and nuns, laymen and laywomen — all four study and practice. This is the teaching of the Jinas." — Sutrakritanga 20.30

About This Chapter

Sthananga

Sthananga — "The Standings" — describes the four-fold Jain community (Chaturvidha Sangha): monks taking the five great vows, nuns taking the same five great vows on equal terms, laymen taking the five small vows, and laywomen doing the same. All four orders together constitute the living vehicle of the Jain teaching.

The chapter is notable for its explicit affirmation of women's equal spiritual standing — nuns take the same vows as monks, at the same level — and its inclusion of lay practice as a genuine path to liberation, not merely a lesser alternative.

The 30 Sutras

Each sutra is presented with the original Prakrit, English translation, and a plain-language commentary.

Part I — The Monk's Order
20.1

साहू साहुणी सावया य, सावियाओ चउव्विहो संघो ॥२०.१॥

Monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen — the community is fourfold.

The opening sutra establishes the structural architecture of the Jain community. The Chaturvidha Sangha — the fourfold community — is one of the Jain tradition's most distinctive social and theological achievements. Unlike traditions that reserve full religious participation for a single ordained class, Jainism explicitly includes four distinct orders, each with its own role, its own vows, and its own path to liberation. The fourfold structure means that the teaching is embedded in a complete social reality — it is not a philosophy for the few but a living practice for all.

The simple version: The Jain community has four orders, not one. Everyone has a place, a role, and a path — not just the monks.

Fourfold CommunitySanghaStructure
20.2

साहूणं महव्वया पंच, पंच अणुव्वया सावयाण ॥२०.२॥

For monks there are five great vows; for laypeople there are five small vows.

The distinction between the great vows (mahavrata) of the monk and the small vows (anuvrata) of the layperson is fundamental to the Jain social structure. The great vows are absolute: complete non-violence, complete truthfulness, complete non-stealing, complete celibacy, complete non-possessiveness. The small vows are partial — allowing for the demands of household life. Both are genuine vows; both constitute genuine practice. The difference is degree, not kind.

The simple version: Monks take the full vows; laypeople take partial vows. Both are real commitments, appropriate to different forms of life.

Five Great VowsFive Small VowsMahavrata
20.3

साहूणं अहिंसा पुव्वं, सव्वभूयाण न हणेज्जा ॥२०.३॥

For monks, non-violence comes first — one should not harm any living being.

Non-violence — the first and foundational great vow — is the entry point for describing the monk's path. The monk's non-violence is absolute: it extends to all living beings without exception, from the most complex to the simplest, from the easily visible to the microscopic. This absoluteness is the defining character of the monastic vow. Every action, every movement, every decision of the monk is made with this vow as the primary consideration. Nothing takes precedence over it.

The simple version: The monk's first and most important commitment is to harm nothing. Every other practice flows from this one.

Non-ViolenceFirst VowAll Beings
20.4

साहूणं सच्चं मुहेण, मणसा दव्वसच्च य ॥२०.४॥

For monks, truth in speech, and truth of mind and material reality.

The monk's truth-telling is not merely about avoiding false words; it extends to the alignment of mind, speech, and action with what is real. Material truth means not making claims about physical reality that are not accurate — not pretending to have supernatural knowledge, not distorting perceptions to manage how others see things. The monk's truthfulness is comprehensive, covering all layers of communication.

The simple version: The monk's honesty goes deeper than just not lying. It means aligning mind, speech, and action with what is actually true.

TruthfulnessSecond VowComprehensive
20.5

न गिण्हाइ अदिण्णं, साहूणं अचोरियं ॥२०.५॥

The monk does not take what is not given — for monks, there is no theft.

The vow of non-stealing extends beyond the obvious case of taking property that belongs to someone else. For the Jain monk, it includes any appropriation of anything — food, shelter, knowledge, attention — that has not been explicitly offered. The monk waits to be given rather than taking. This includes subtle forms of appropriation: expecting hospitality that hasn't been offered, claiming credit for others' work, using another's resources without permission.

The simple version: The monk takes nothing that hasn't been freely given. That principle extends further than it first appears.

Non-StealingThird VowNon-Taking
20.6

साहूणं बंभचेरं, सव्वेहिं भावेहिं ॥२०.६॥

For monks, celibacy — in all aspects of being.

Brahmacharya — celibacy — is not merely the avoidance of sexual contact. The great vow of celibacy covers all four dimensions of person: body, speech, mind, and the subtle activity that connects them. A monk who avoids physical sexual contact but entertains sexual thoughts, speaks suggestively, or handles situations in sexually charged ways has not maintained brahmacharya in the full sense. The vow covers the entire person.

The simple version: Celibacy as a monk's vow is not just physical — it includes thoughts, words, and subtle behavior. The whole person must be integrated in this direction.

CelibacyFourth VowComplete
20.7

अपरिग्गहो साहूणं, न किंचि मिच्छइ ॥२०.७॥

Non-possessiveness for monks — one should desire nothing.

The fifth great vow — complete non-possessiveness — means desiring nothing. The monk has given up not only the legal ownership of things but the mental habit of claiming, holding, and considering things "mine." This extends from physical objects to people (no one belongs to the monk), to status (the monk does not own a reputation), to even practice itself (the monk does not hold on to spiritual experiences as possessions). The gesture of non-possessiveness is total.

The simple version: The monk's goal is to desire nothing at all — not to own objects, not to claim people, not to hold on to spiritual achievements.

Non-PossessivenessFifth VowTotal
20.8

एए पंच महव्वया, साहूणं संजमो भणिओ ॥२०.८॥

These five great vows are declared to be the restraint of monks.

The sutra closes the description of the monk's five great vows with a summary identification: these five together constitute "samjama" — restraint. Restraint, in Jain teaching, is not mere behavioral control but the systematic condition in which karma ceases to accumulate. The five great vows, taken together and practiced completely, create the conditions in which new karma cannot attach. They are simultaneously ethical commitments and karmic technologies.

The simple version: The five great vows aren't just moral rules — they're the technology of liberation. Together they create the conditions in which karma stops building up.

Five Vows TogetherRestraintKarma Technology
Part II — The Nun's Order
20.9

साहुणीणं पि महव्वया, एवमेव पंच ॥२०.९॥

For nuns too, the great vows are the same five.

The sutra explicitly establishes that the five great vows of the nun's order are identical to those of the monk's order. This is a statement of equal spiritual standing: the nun takes the same vows at the same level of completeness. There is no lighter or modified version for women. The Jain tradition's inclusion of women's full ordination on the same terms as men's is one of its historically distinctive features, and this sutra states it plainly.

The simple version: Nuns take the same vows as monks — no lower standard, no modifications. Equal vows, equal standing.

NunsEqual VowsEqual Standing
20.10

साहुणी वि अहिंसाए, सव्वत्थ समा ॥२०.१०॥

The nun too practices non-violence — equal in all respects.

"Equal in all respects" — this phrase is the heart of the sutra. The nun's non-violence is not a modified or partial version of the monk's. The standard is the same, the completeness is the same, and the spiritual outcome is the same. This equality of vow corresponds to equality of spiritual capacity: nuns can achieve liberation, can become great teachers, can reach the highest stages of the path.

The simple version: The nun's practice and the monk's practice are equal in every way that matters.

Equal PracticeNon-ViolenceWomen
20.11

साहुणीणं सच्चं परिण्णाय, सव्वेहिं भावेहिं ॥२०.११॥

The nun, having understood truth, practices it in all aspects.

Understanding truth before practicing it — this is the sequence described here. The nun's commitment to truthfulness is not a rule she has been given to follow; it arises from genuine understanding. She has understood what truth is, what falsehood does, how deception creates karma and damages the conditions needed for liberation. From that understanding, her truthfulness is not an imposition but an expression of what she knows.

The simple version: Honesty that comes from understanding is stronger and truer than honesty that comes from being told to be honest.

TruthfulnessUnderstandingNun
20.12

साहुणी न गिण्हाइ, न दाइ न कारावेइ ॥२०.१२॥

The nun neither takes what is not given, nor causes others to take it.

The vow of non-stealing is extended here to its full scope: not only not taking oneself, but not directing or encouraging others to take on one's behalf. It is possible to maintain personal non-taking while instrumentalizing others to acquire things for you — a loophole the tradition explicitly closes. The nun is not to create the conditions of theft indirectly by using her authority or influence to have others acquire things she cannot take herself.

The simple version: Not stealing includes not having someone else steal for you. The indirect route doesn't escape the vow.

Non-StealingIndirect ActionCompleteness
20.13

साहुणीणं बंभचेरं, उग्गं तवं य ॥२०.१३॥

For nuns, celibacy and severe austerity.

The same demanding standard of celibacy described for monks applies equally to nuns — and it is paired here with severe austerity. For women in the ancient world who took ordination, the demands of practice were real and the social risks of choosing the monastic life were real. The tradition honors this by naming their practice with the same unqualified terms it uses for monks.

The simple version: Nuns practice the same demanding austerity as monks — no softened version for women.

CelibacySevere AusterityEqual Standard
20.14

साहुणी न परिग्गहं, न इच्छइ न सेवइ ॥२०.१४॥

The nun does not possess, does not desire, does not enjoy possessions.

Three aspects of non-possessiveness are named: not having, not wanting, not enjoying. Each addresses a different level of possession. Not having is the outer level. Not wanting is the inner level — the mental orientation of desire. Not enjoying is the experiential level — the pleasure of possession even without formal ownership. The nun's non-possessiveness covers all three.

The simple version: Non-possessiveness isn't just about not owning things — it's about not wanting them and not enjoying them even when they're around.

Non-PossessivenessThree LevelsComplete
20.15

सा य सुपडिवण्णा, निव्वाणमग्गे ॥२०.१५॥

She is well-established on the path to liberation.

The summary statement about the nun who practices all five great vows is direct: she is well-established on the path to liberation. Not approximately on the path, not beginning the path, not aspiring toward the path — well-established. The language here refuses to hedge or qualify in ways that would implicitly diminish the nun's spiritual standing. A nun who fully practices the five great vows is on the path to liberation in the same sense and to the same degree as a monk who does the same.

The simple version: A nun who fully practices the vows is fully on the path to liberation. No qualification, no secondary status.

Liberation PathWell-EstablishedNun
20.16

साहूणं साहुणीणं च, एसो धम्मो समो ॥२०.१६॥

For monks and for nuns, the teaching is the same.

One final, summary statement closes the monastic section: the teaching is the same for monks and nuns. Same vows, same standard, same path, same destination. The community of the ordained has two orders — male and female — but one teaching. This unity of teaching across the gender division of the ordained community is what the sutra affirms. The Chaturvidha Sangha's first two orders stand on equal footing.

The simple version: Same teaching, same standard, for monks and nuns. The path doesn't split at the gender line.

Same TeachingGender EqualityUnity
Part III — The Lay Community
20.17

सावयाणं पंच अणुव्वया, जं च पयंपइ जिणो ॥२०.१७॥

For laypeople, the five small vows — as declared by the Jina.

The lay vows — the five "anuvratas" or small vows — are the form of practice appropriate to the householder life. The word "small" does not imply that the lay vows are less important or less genuine; it means they are calibrated to the demands of a life that includes family, livelihood, and social responsibility. The Jina declared these vows specifically for laypeople, acknowledging that full renunciation is not the only form of Jain practice.

The simple version: The Jinas created a set of vows specifically for people who live in the world. The path is designed for them too, not just for monks.

Five Small VowsLaypersonHouseholder
20.18

थूलं हिंसं विरमइ, सावओ य जहाबलं ॥२०.१८॥

The layman abstains from gross violence, according to his capacity.

The first lay vow — partial non-violence — is defined by two modifying phrases: "gross" (severe, major) and "according to his capacity." The householder cannot avoid all violence — farming, cooking, walking, and breathing all involve some degree of harm to small beings. The tradition acknowledges this honestly and instead asks the layperson to avoid gross, deliberate, unnecessary violence. The "according to his capacity" phrase acknowledges the individual's actual situation.

The simple version: Laypeople don't have to avoid all harm, but they commit to avoiding serious, unnecessary harm — as much as they're able.

Partial Non-ViolenceGross ViolenceCapacity
20.19

मुसावायं परिहरइ, थूलमेव य सावओ ॥२०.१९॥

The layman avoids falsehood — major falsehood in particular.

The layperson's truth vow follows the same pattern: a commitment to avoid major falsehood — the serious lies that harm others, that constitute fraud or manipulation, that damage the conditions of community trust. Minor social diplomacy is not the target here. What the lay vow addresses is the substantial dishonesty that causes real harm — false testimony, fraudulent business practice, serious manipulation.

The simple version: The lay vow of honesty focuses on serious lies — the kind that actually harm people and break trust.

TruthfulnessMajor FalsehoodLay Vow
20.20

अदिण्णं न गिण्हाइ, थूलेण सावओ ॥२०.२०॥

The layman does not take what is not given — avoiding major theft.

The layperson's version of non-stealing applies the "major" qualifier. The commitment is to avoid serious theft — taking what clearly belongs to others, engaging in fraud, taking more than one's fair share in business dealings. The lay tradition's approach to ethics is realistic without being lax: it acknowledges that perfect non-possessiveness is not achievable in householder life while insisting that a genuine ethical standard must still apply.

The simple version: The lay person commits to not stealing in any serious sense — no fraud, no taking what belongs to others.

Non-StealingMajor TheftEthics
20.21

पर-दार-परिवज्जणं, सावयस्स य ॥२०.२१॥

For the layperson, the avoidance of another's spouse.

The lay vow of partial celibacy is specifically defined as the avoidance of sexual relations with anyone other than one's own partner — the commitment to faithfulness within the appropriate relationship for a householder. This is the layperson's version of celibacy: it does not require complete abstention but does require fidelity and the absence of exploitative sexual behavior. It reflects the tradition's understanding that the household vow is a genuine vow.

The simple version: For laypeople, the celibacy vow means faithfulness to your own partner — no others.

FidelityLay CelibacyHousehold Vow
20.22

परिमिय-परिग्गहो सावओ, जहासत्ती ॥२०.२२॥

The layperson limits possessions — according to capacity.

The lay version of non-possessiveness involves setting a deliberate limit on the accumulation of wealth, property, and material goods. The householder does not give up all possessions but voluntarily sets a ceiling on what they will allow themselves to accumulate. This practice of deliberate limitation is itself spiritually formative: it creates the habit of choosing sufficiency over maximization, which is the beginning of the disposition of non-possessiveness.

The simple version: The layperson doesn't give up all possessions, but they set a deliberate limit. Enough, not as much as possible.

Limited PossessionsSufficiencyLay Practice
20.23

एए पंच अणुव्वया, सावयाणं सुचिण्णा ॥२०.२३॥

These five small vows, well-practiced by laypeople.

"Well-practiced" — the same phrase used throughout the text to indicate genuine rather than merely formal practice — is applied here to the lay vows. A layperson who genuinely practices the five partial vows is doing something real and meaningful: they are reducing the accumulation of karma, purifying their actions, and moving toward the conditions in which fuller practice will eventually be possible.

The simple version: Lay practice done genuinely is real practice. The partial vows aren't just a second-best option — they're a genuine path.

Well-PracticedGenuine PathKarma
20.24

सावओ वि सुद्धसावओ, निव्वाणमग्गे ॥२०.२४॥

The pure layperson — he too is on the path to liberation.

The sutra states plainly what the whole structure of the lay vows implies: a genuine layperson — one who practices the partial vows with full sincerity — is on the path to liberation. The word "pure" indicates that the claim is not about someone who calls themselves a layperson while ignoring the vows, but about someone who genuinely practices them. And for that person: the path to liberation is open, even within householder life.

The simple version: A sincere layperson, genuinely practicing the vows, is on the path to liberation. The monastery is not the only route.

Lay LiberationSincere PracticePath
Part IV — The Community Together
20.25

चत्तारि वि य सिक्खंति, एगो धम्मो जिणाण ॥२०.२५॥

All four study and practice — one teaching of the Jinas.

One teaching, four expressions. The Chaturvidha Sangha is united not by sharing the same form of practice but by following the same teaching — the single body of doctrine transmitted by the Jinas. This doctrinal unity sustains the community's coherence across its four different forms of life. Monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen are not practicing four separate religions; they are practicing one religion in four forms appropriate to different life situations.

The simple version: Same teaching, four different forms of practice. The community is diverse in structure but unified in direction.

One TeachingFour FormsUnity
20.26

साहू साहुणि य सावया, सावियाओ परस्परं ॥२०.२६॥

Monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen — they support one another.

The four orders are not simply parallel; they are mutually supporting. Monks and nuns depend on laypeople for food, shelter, and material support. Laypeople depend on monks and nuns for teaching, inspiration, and the maintenance of the tradition's fullest expression. The whole system is a fabric of interdependence — each supporting the others' practice in different ways.

The simple version: The four orders need each other. The sangha is a system of mutual support, not just four separate paths that happen to be called by the same name.

Mutual SupportInterdependenceCommunity
20.27

साहूणं आहारं दंति, सावया पुण्णाणुबंधिणो ॥२०.२७॥

Laypeople give food to monks — they are thus connected to merit.

The act of giving food to monks — the central practice of the lay-monastic relationship — is described as producing merit for the lay donor. This is not a transaction but a relationship: the layperson's generosity creates the conditions in which the monks' practice can continue, and the monks' practice generates the teachings and models of renunciation from which laypeople benefit. The merit of giving is the formal recognition of this mutual contribution.

The simple version: When laypeople support the monastics, everyone benefits — the monk can practice, and the layperson generates good karma.

GivingMeritLay-Monastic Relationship
20.28

साहू धम्मं पयावेंति, सावयाणं हियं सया ॥२०.२८॥

Monks transmit the teaching — always for the benefit of laypeople.

The monks' role in the fourfold community is specified: they transmit the teaching. This transmission is described as always for the benefit of laypeople — not for the monks' benefit, not as a display of learning, not as a means of social power, but genuinely for the benefit of those who receive it. The monk who teaches with any other motivation has departed from this role. The relationship is defined by the welfare of the recipient.

The simple version: The monk's teaching role is fundamentally one of service. The teaching is for the listener's benefit, not the teacher's status.

TeachingServiceBenefit
20.29

एयं चउव्विहं संघं, पूयंति देवा मणुया ॥२०.२९॥

This fourfold community is honored by gods and humans.

The fourfold community — the Chaturvidha Sangha — is described as the object of reverence by both divine and human beings. This is not a claim about social prestige but about spiritual reality: the community that embodies the Jain path in all its forms is recognized as genuinely valuable by all who can see clearly. The tradition places the sangha in a cosmic context — the teaching matters not only to humans but to the broader reality in which humans participate.

The simple version: The community that genuinely practices the teaching is honored by everyone who can see its value — in this world and beyond it.

HonoredCosmic ContextSangha
20.30

साहू य साहुणी य, सावया य सावियाओ।
चत्तारि वि य सिक्खंति, धम्मो एसो जिणाण॥ — iti bemi

Monks and nuns, laymen and laywomen — all four study and practice. This is the teaching of the Jinas. — Thus I say.

The chapter closes with the featured verse restored as the final word, sealed with "iti bemi." The four orders are named once more, the act of studying and practicing is affirmed, and the source — the teaching of the Jinas — is identified. The closing formula attributes this declaration to Mahavira himself, grounding the entire chapter's teaching in its highest authority. The fourfold community is not a human organizational invention but the structure of the path as the fully liberated ones have understood and declared it.

The simple version: All four orders, all practicing together — this is the Jain path. This is what the Jinas declared. Thus says Mahavira.
