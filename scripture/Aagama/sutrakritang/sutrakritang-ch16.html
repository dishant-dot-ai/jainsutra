The Scripture Repositoryगणिपिडग (Ganipidag)

The Seal of Book One

The closing chapter of Book 1 — a summary and seal of everything transmitted in the first Shrutaskandha. Mahavira declares the entire teaching as heard and comprehended, identifies the four pillars of the path, and closes with the promise that has anchored the whole first book: the monk established in this teaching burns away karma. Thus I say.

एयं सुयं भगवया, परिण्णाय वियागरे।
जे भिक्खू एत्थ ठिए, सोहम्मं कम्मं खवेज्ज सो॥ — iti bemi

"This has been heard and declared by the Blessed One, having fully comprehended it. The monk who is established in this — he burns away his karma. — Thus I say." — Sutrakritanga 16.15

About This Chapter

Ganipidag

Chapter 16 is the closing chapter of Book 1 — the Ganipidag, or "Basket of Teaching." Its function is the seal: everything transmitted across the sixteen chapters of the first Shrutaskandha is here affirmed, summarized, and attributed to Mahavira as its authentic source. The four pillars of the entire first book — right vision, conduct, austerity, and restraint — are named together as the essence of the teaching.

The chapter moves from summary to Mahavira's direct declaration: this teaching comes from the one who comprehended it completely. It closes with the promise that the monk who is established in this teaching — not merely acquainted with it, but established in it as his ground — burns away karma. The final "iti bemi" (thus I say) is the tradition's seal on the entire first book: faithfully heard, faithfully transmitted, faithfully declared.

The 15 Sutras

Each verse is presented with the original Ardhamagadhi Prakrit, English translation, and commentary.

Part I — The Teaching Sealed (1–8)
16.1

एयं सुयं भगवया, धम्मं जाणह पंडिया।

This has been heard from the Blessed One — know the teaching, O learned ones.

The final chapter opens with the signal of authentic transmission. "Heard from the Blessed One" situates everything that has been taught in Book 1 within the chain of transmission from Mahavira through his principal disciple Sudharman to the community of monks who preserved and passed on the teaching. This is not simply a literary convention — the Jain tradition places great weight on the unbroken chain of transmission as the guarantee of the teaching's authenticity. What was heard has been preserved exactly as heard.

The simple version: This teaching comes from the one who knew it from direct experience. The chain of transmission matters because it guarantees that what was heard was preserved accurately.

Authentic TransmissionMahaviraChain of Teaching
16.2

सम्मद्दिट्ठी य चरित्ते, तवेण संजमेण य।

Right vision, and conduct, and austerity, and restraint.

The four pillars of Book 1's teaching are named together as the summary of everything that has been transmitted. Right vision — the correct understanding of the soul's nature. Conduct — the behavioral expression of right vision through the five great vows. Austerity — the transformative practice that burns existing karma. Restraint — the ongoing discipline that prevents new karma from forming. These four are not a ladder to be climbed sequentially but an integrated whole that must develop together.

The simple version: The entire first book comes down to these four things. Know correctly, act rightly, transform through practice, maintain discipline.

Four PillarsRight VisionConductAusterityRestraint
16.3

एयं धम्मस्स सारं, जाणंत पंडिया।

This is the essence of the teaching, as the learned ones know.

The "essence" is not the most difficult part or the most esoteric — it is the most essential. The essence of the teaching is what remains when everything non-essential is removed: right vision, conduct, austerity, and restraint. Everything else in the Sutrakritanga — the philosophical debates, the descriptions of wrong views, the narratives of the wandering monk, the detailed instructions for village conduct — all of it is in service of helping the practitioner understand and embody these four. The essence is this.

The simple version: Strip away everything secondary and this is what remains. Four things. Learn them. Live them. They are enough.

EssenceDistillationFour Things
16.4

जे भिक्खू एत्थ ठिए, सो णं खवेइ कम्माणि।

The monk who is established in this burns away karma.

"Established" is the key word — not merely acquainted with, not occasionally practicing, not theoretically committed to, but established. The monk whose entire life is organized around right vision, conduct, austerity, and restraint is established. And the specific result of being established is the burning away of karma. Not reducing karma, not managing karma, but actually burning it away — the accumulated karmic coating that has covered the soul through countless lifetimes beginning to dissolve. This is the promise and the purpose of the entire first book.

The simple version: "Established" means it is the ground you stand on, not a practice you do occasionally. That kind of establishment burns karma.

EstablishedBurns KarmaPromise of Book 1
16.5

सव्वेसिं पाणभूयाणं, अभयं देइ सव्वओ।

He gives fearlessness to all living beings from all sides.

The summary returns to the foundational expression of right vision in behavior: fearlessness given to all living beings. This is not just non-violence in the sense of not causing direct harm — it is the active quality of safety that genuine non-violence radiates. The monk established in the teaching is a being from whom no living creature has anything to fear. This fearlessness given is the monk's greatest gift to the world — greater than any teaching, any food, any material support.

The simple version: The established monk's greatest gift is the safety he radiates. Every being is safer because he exists.

Fearlessness GivenAll Living BeingsRadiated Safety
16.6

न कुणइ न कारवइ, पाणाणं किंचि हिंसए।

He does not harm, does not cause harm to living beings in any way.

The completeness of non-violence is restated: neither direct harm nor indirect harm (causing others to harm) in any way whatsoever. The "any way" extends to all three channels — mind, speech, and body — and to all forms of harm, however subtle. The monk who has completed the work of Book 1's teaching does not harm in any conceivable way. This total non-harm is not achieved through exhaustive vigilance at this stage — it is the natural expression of a fully transformed inner life.

The simple version: Complete non-violence is not achieved through constant effort. At this stage, it is simply what the monk is.

Total Non-HarmNatural ExpressionTransformed Life
16.7

एयमेव समाहिया, एयमेव महब्बए।

Composed in exactly this, under exactly the great vows.

The "great vows" — the five complete vows of the monk — are the structural expression of right vision in binding commitment. Being composed in them means the vows are no longer external constraints maintained by willpower. They have been absorbed into the monk's character so completely that violating them would feel as unnatural as violating one's own identity. The monk who is "composed in the great vows" is the monk in whom the vows have been fulfilled in the deepest sense — they have become who he is.

The simple version: The great vows fulfilled are not chains — they are the shape of a free soul. At this stage, they feel like liberation, not constraint.

Great VowsComposedIdentity
16.8

एयं सव्वस्स सारं, परिण्णाय विहरए।

This is the essence of everything — having understood it, he wanders.

The wandering — the constant movement through the world that Chapter 11 described — here receives its final framing. The monk wanders with this understanding as his only possession, his only shelter, his only companion. The essence of everything that can be known — the nature of the soul, of karma, of the path, of liberation — he carries within him as he moves through village and forest and mountain cave. The teaching and the monk have become one. The wandering IS the teaching.

The simple version: The monk who has understood the essence of everything carries the entire teaching within him wherever he goes. His wandering is itself the teaching's continuation.

Wandering MonkTeaching EmbodiedEssence Carried Within
Part II — Mahavira's Declaration (9–15)
16.9

एयं सुयं भगवया, परिण्णाय वियागरे।

This has been heard and declared by the Blessed One, having fully comprehended it.

The shift to Mahavira's direct declaration marks the climax of Book 1. Everything that has been taught across the sixteen chapters has been heard from the Blessed One — Mahavira — who declared it having "fully comprehended" it. "Fully comprehended" is the technical Jain description of the Omniscient One's knowledge: direct, complete, non-inferential knowledge of all things at all times. What Mahavira declared was not theory or tradition — it was knowledge from direct seeing.

The simple version: What Book 1 contains is the declaration of the one who saw everything directly. This is not interpretation or tradition — it is omniscient knowledge transmitted faithfully.

Mahavira's DeclarationOmniscient KnowledgeDirect Seeing
16.10

जे भिक्खू एत्थ ठिए, सोहम्मं कम्मं खवेज्ज सो।

The monk who is established in this burns away excellent karma.

The featured verse's core line: the monk established in this teaching burns away his karma. "Excellent karma" — the best possible karma — is karma that is being actively dissolved rather than merely not added to. The monk who is established in right vision, conduct, austerity, and restraint is not just preventing new bondage — he is dismantling existing bondage. The distinction matters: many practices reduce the rate of new karma formation; the teaching of Book 1 actively burns what has accumulated over lifetimes.

The simple version: Established practice does not just stop new bondage — it dissolves old bondage. This is the most efficient relationship to karma available.

Burns KarmaDissolves Old BondageEstablished Practice
16.11

से भिक्खू लहुइज्जमाणे, सव्वपाणभूयाणं सुणेइ।

That monk, moving lightly, listens to all living beings.

The image of "listening to all living beings" is one of the most tender in Book 1. The monk who moves lightly is not rushing past the world — he is fully present to it. Listening to all living beings means being sensitive to the full range of life's expression: the suffering of beings who cannot speak, the struggle of creatures who cannot ask for help, the silent need that the world constantly presents. The monk who hears all of this and responds with non-harm has made the teaching into compassion.

The simple version: The established monk is not disconnected from the world — he is more sensitive to it. Moving lightly means being more present, not less.

Moving LightlyListeningFull PresenceCompassion
16.12

एयं च मग्गं जाणित्ता, से भिक्खू न पमायए।

Having understood this path, that monk is not negligent.

Negligence is the enemy that the teaching returns to again and again throughout Book 1. Even at this late stage — in the closing chapter, after sixteen chapters of teaching — the instruction is not negligent. The warning is not pessimistic: it acknowledges that even the monk who has understood the path still faces the daily challenge of maintaining the quality of practice. Negligence is the one thing that understanding alone cannot prevent — only vigilance, applied continuously, guards against it.

The simple version: Understanding the path is not the same as walking it without slipping. Vigilance is required from the first step to the last.

Non-NegligenceVigilanceContinuous Practice
16.13

एयं सुयं धम्ममेयं, जाणह पंडिया तहा।

This teaching has been heard — know it thus, O learned ones.

The repeated insistence that this teaching has been "heard" — from the Blessed One, across the generations of transmission — is the tradition's way of affirming its authenticity. Each repetition of the phrase is a link in the chain: from Mahavira to his disciple, from disciple to student, from generation to generation, the teaching has been carried forward without distortion. The learned ones addressed here are those responsible for receiving the teaching accurately and passing it forward in the same way.

The simple version: The teaching's survival depends on each generation receiving and passing it accurately. Being a "learned one" means taking this responsibility seriously.

Transmission ChainResponsibilityLearned Ones
16.14

एयं खलु सव्वधम्मे, जाणह लोगुत्तमं।

This indeed is the teaching of all teachings — know it as the highest in the world.

The declaration is sweeping: this teaching is the highest in the world. Not among many competing paths — it is the teaching that other teachings approximate and point toward. The basis for this claim is not sectarian pride but the logical argument made throughout Book 1: non-violence, right vision, restraint, and the burning of karma are the conditions of liberation, and any teaching that embodies these — regardless of what tradition it belongs to — is the teaching. The claim is substantive, not merely institutional.

The simple version: This teaching is called the highest not because of its tradition but because of what it contains. Non-violence and right vision are the conditions of liberation — this has been demonstrated, not merely asserted.

Highest TeachingSubstantive ClaimUniversal Conditions
16.15

एयं सुयं भगवया, परिण्णाय वियागरे। जे भिक्खू एत्थ ठिए, सोहम्मं कम्मं खवेज्ज सो॥ — iti bemi

This has been heard and declared by the Blessed One, having fully comprehended it. The monk who is established in this — he burns away his karma. — Thus I say.

The final sutra of Book 1 is the seal on everything that has been transmitted. "This has been heard and declared by the Blessed One" — the entire first Shrutaskandha, all sixteen chapters, all the teaching on karma, bondage, liberation, conduct, austerity, non-violence, right vision — has come from the direct comprehension of the one who knew it completely. "The monk who is established in this burns away his karma" — the promise of the entire book, stated in its simplest and most complete form. "Thus I say" — the signature of authentic transmission, the tradition's affirmation that these words have been faithfully preserved and declared exactly as they were received. Book 1 of the Sutrakritanga is complete.

The simple version: The book that began with questions about bondage ends with the promise of liberation. The monk who takes this teaching as his ground — not as a text to read but as a life to live — burns away what has bound the soul since beginningless time. Thus I say.
