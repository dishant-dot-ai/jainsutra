Nalanadaनालंद

The Final Chapter — The Brahmin of Nalanda / Absolute Non-Violence

The final chapter of the entire Sutrakritanga. Mahavira's dialogue with a wise Brahmin of Nalanda — the great center of ancient Indian learning. The Brahmin poses his deepest philosophical questions; Mahavira answers with clarity and calm. The text closes with the most famous verse in all of Jain literature, sealed with "iti bemi."

एत्तो चेव मे पण्णत्तं, भगवइं च सुत्तकडंगे।
सव्वे पाणा, सव्वे भूया, सव्वे जीवा न हंतव्वा।
— iti bemi॥

"This indeed has been declared by me, the Blessed One, in the Sutrakritanga: all breathing beings, all beings, all living beings should not be slain. — Thus I say." — Sutrakritanga 23.25

About This Chapter

Nalanada

Nalanada — "The Brahmin of Nalanda" — is the final chapter of the entire Sutrakritanga. Set in Nalanda, the ancient seat of the highest philosophical inquiry, it records a direct dialogue between Mahavira and the most capable representative of the Brahminical tradition. The Brahmin asks the four fundamental questions of Indian metaphysics and three questions of practice; Mahavira answers each one with precision and calm.

The chapter closes not with ceremony but with the simplest and most complete declaration of the entire teaching: all beings should not be harmed. This is the summary of every chapter, every sutra, every argument. The Brahmin hears it and takes up the path. And then — "iti bemi." Thus I say. The Sutrakritanga is complete.

The 25 Sutras

Each sutra is presented with the original Prakrit, English translation, and a plain-language commentary.

Part I — The Brahmin's Questions (Sutras 1–10)
23.1

नालंदाए नयरीए, माहणो पण्णवंतो।

In the city of Nalanda, there lived a wise Brahmin.

The final chapter opens with a named setting — Nalanda, the great center of ancient Indian learning — and a named figure: a wise Brahmin. The choice of setting is not incidental. Nalanda was associated with the highest standards of philosophical inquiry and debate. By placing the final dialogue here, the text signals that what follows is the highest-level engagement the Jain teaching will offer — a direct encounter between Mahavira and the tradition of scholarly Brahminical learning at its most capable. The Brahmin is described as "pannvanta" — wise, learned, clear-minded. He is not a fool; he is the best of what his tradition has to offer.

The simple version: This is the final dialogue of the entire text, and it happens between Mahavira and the smartest, most learned representative of the Brahminical tradition. The stakes are high.

NalandaBrahminFinal Dialogue
23.2

सो आगओ जिणं पासे, पुच्छिउं धम्मसंसयं।

He came before the Jina to ask about doubts in the teaching.

The Brahmin's approach is worthy of respect: he comes to ask, not to argue. His doubts are real doubts — genuine philosophical questions that have arisen from thinking carefully about the Jain teaching as he has encountered it. The word "samsaya" — doubt, uncertainty — describes the state of a sincere inquirer who has encountered something they do not fully understand. Mahavira's willingness to receive such questions, and the text's recording of this encounter, signals that the Jain tradition does not fear inquiry.

The simple version: He didn't come to win an argument — he came with real questions. That kind of honest inquiry deserves honest answers.

Sincere InquiryDoubtOpen Approach
23.3

किं जीवो किं अजीवो, किं बंधो किं मोक्खो।

"What is the soul? What is the non-soul? What is bondage? What is liberation?"

The Brahmin's questions are the most fundamental questions in the Jain framework — and indeed in all Indian philosophy. Soul versus non-soul, bondage versus liberation: these four terms define the entire landscape of Jain metaphysics. By asking these questions, the Brahmin is not dismissing the Jain answers he has heard; he is asking to hear them directly from the source, to test them against his own understanding, to engage with the highest authority rather than with secondhand accounts.

The simple version: He asks the four most important questions: what is the soul, what is not-soul, what is bondage, what is freedom. Everything in the teaching comes back to these four.

SoulNon-SoulBondageLiberation
23.4

किं अहिंसा, किं धम्मो, किं संजमो।

"What is non-violence? What is the teaching? What is restraint?"

Three more foundational questions follow. These are not purely metaphysical but practical: they ask about the core content of Jain practice. Non-violence — what does it actually mean in its deepest sense? The teaching — what is its essential content? Restraint — what is it actually restraining, and how? The Brahmin is asking for the heart of the matter, not the details. He wants to understand the structure before the details.

The simple version: Now the questions turn practical: what is non-violence, what is the teaching, what is restraint? He wants to understand the essence.

AhimsaDharmaRestraint
23.5

किं तवो किं मोक्खमग्गो, कहं मुच्चइ।

"What is austerity? What is the path to liberation? How is one freed?"

The questioning continues with the practical mechanics of the path. Austerity — not just as hardship but as a specific technique; the path — not as a general direction but as a specific road; how one is freed — the actual process of liberation rather than its description. The Brahmin's questions are technically precise. He has thought carefully enough about these things to ask about them in this way, which means he will understand precise answers when they are given.

The simple version: He wants technical answers about austerity and liberation — the "how," not just the "what." These are good, sharp questions.

AusterityPath to LiberationHow to Be Freed
23.6

अण्णेहिं सासणेहिं य, किं भेओ जिणसासणे।

"What is the difference between other teachings and the Jina's teaching?"

This is perhaps the sharpest question — the comparative one. The Brahmin knows other traditions. He has studied and perhaps practiced within them. He asks: what specifically distinguishes the Jain path from other teachings that also claim the authority of liberated teachers, also claim to lead to liberation, also emphasize some form of non-violence and restraint? This question demands a specific, defensible answer rather than a general claim of superiority.

The simple version: He's asking: given that there are other traditions that also claim to lead to liberation, what specifically makes the Jain path different and correct? This is the question that cuts deepest.

ComparativeDistinctive TeachingJain Path
23.7

सुणेउं इच्छामि भगवं, तुमं ति वयमि।

"I wish to hear, Blessed One — I say this to you."

The closing formula of the Brahmin's questions is beautifully direct. "I wish to hear" — he has not come to lecture or to defeat; he has come to listen. "I say this to you" — the directness of address is respectful without being servile. The Brahmin speaks to Mahavira as one serious inquirer to the teacher he considers worthy of the most serious questions. This is the correct posture for receiving the teaching.

The simple version: He ends by saying: I genuinely want to hear. That posture — wanting to hear, not wanting to win — is the beginning of receiving the teaching.

Wanting to HearCorrect PostureReceptivity
23.8

भगवं महावीरो, सोच्चा अभिगय।

The Blessed Mahavira, having heard and understood —

Mahavira's response begins not with words but with the act of hearing. He hears the questions completely, understands them — the word "abhigaya" indicates thorough understanding — before speaking. This is itself a teaching about the nature of right communication: full hearing before responding. The Brahmin asked with directness and sincerity; Mahavira hears with completeness before answering with clarity.

The simple version: Mahavira listens to the questions fully before answering. That's how real teaching works.

Full ListeningMahaviraRight Communication
23.9

पयइ य पव्वइ य, एव भणेइ महावीरो।

Calmly and thoughtfully, Mahavira spoke thus.

"Paya" — calm, composed. "Pavva" — thoughtfully, with full consideration. The manner of Mahavira's speech is described before its content. He speaks from the same settled equanimity that the Great Monk embodies. He is not hurried, not defensive, not performing — he speaks with the natural calm of someone who knows what is true and communicates it without any inner turbulence. The quality of the teacher's presence is as important as the content of his teaching.

The simple version: He responds calmly and thoughtfully — not defensively, not urgently. The inner state of the teacher is part of what's being transmitted.

CalmEquanimityTeacher's Presence
23.10

जीवो अत्थि य लोगे, सुण माहणा।

"The soul exists in the world — listen, O Brahmin."

Mahavira begins exactly where the teaching must begin: with the existence of the soul. The invitation to listen — "suna" — is warm and direct. He is not condescending; he is engaging the Brahmin as a serious practitioner who can receive what is about to be said. The phrase "the soul exists" is the foundation of the entire Jain worldview, and Mahavira plants it here at the beginning of his response with the confidence of someone who has directly perceived what he is asserting.

The simple version: The answer starts with the most basic fact: the soul is real. Everything else in the teaching follows from this.

Soul ExistsFoundationDirect Perception
Part II — Mahavira's Answers (Sutras 11–18)
23.11

जीवो कत्ता य भोत्ता य, अप्पा य परमप्पा।

The soul is both agent and experiencer — both the individual soul and the highest soul.

Mahavira's answer to "what is the soul?" contains a subtle but important claim: the soul is both individual (atman) and in its liberated state equivalent to what other traditions call the highest reality. This is the Jain non-theistic answer to the question of ultimate reality: the liberated soul does not merge with a divine being but achieves the condition that other traditions describe as the divine — omniscience, total freedom from karma, complete bliss. There is no external divine; the highest reality is the fully liberated soul.

The simple version: The soul acts, the soul experiences, and in liberation the soul achieves what other traditions call the divine. There is no separate God; the liberated soul is itself the highest reality.

Soul as AgentIndividual SoulHighest Reality
23.12

अजीवो सव्वपोग्गलो, धम्माधम्मं च।

The non-soul is all matter, and also the principles of motion and non-motion.

Mahavira's definition of the non-soul encompasses matter (pudgala) and the fundamental principles of motion (dharma) and non-motion (adharma) — two of the five categories of existence that are distinct from the soul. These are the "medium" in which motion and rest occur; they are real entities in the Jain ontology, not merely abstract concepts. The precision here reflects the Jain tradition's careful philosophical taxonomy of everything that exists.

The simple version: Everything that is not a soul — all matter, and the conditions that allow movement and stillness — is "non-soul." The distinction is comprehensive.

Non-SoulMatterPudgalaJain Ontology
23.13

बंधो कम्ममिस्सणं, जीवस्स पोग्गलेण।

Bondage is the mixing of karma — of the soul with matter.

Bondage is defined precisely: it is the condition of the soul mixed with karmic matter. The soul in its pure state is unmixed — pure consciousness, pure knowing. In bondage, karmic matter has infiltrated the soul, affecting its capacities, perceptions, and experiences. This mixture is not static; it is constantly changing as old karma ripens and is experienced, and new karma is potentially formed. Understanding bondage as a specific condition — the mixing — makes its remedy clear: unmixing.

The simple version: Bondage is what happens when karma — which is material — gets mixed into the soul. The soul's natural state is to be unmixed.

BondageKarmaMixing
23.14

मोक्खो सव्वकम्मक्खयो, जीवस्स।

Liberation is the complete exhaustion of karma — for the soul.

The definition of liberation is equally precise. Not a new state to be achieved, not a place to go, not a relationship to enter — liberation is the exhaustion of all karma. When the last karmic particle has been shed and no new karma is forming, the soul naturally achieves liberation. The teaching is extraordinarily clean in its logic: the soul is naturally free; karma is what obscures and binds it; exhausting karma is what produces liberation. Nothing external is required.

The simple version: Liberation isn't a reward you get — it's what the soul is when nothing is weighing it down anymore. Remove all karma; liberation is what's left.

LiberationKarma ExhaustionNatural Freedom
23.15

अहिंसा परमो धम्मो, एयं जिणाण सासणं।

Non-violence is the highest teaching — this is the instruction of the Jinas.

This sutra is perhaps the most famous single doctrinal statement in the entire Sutrakritanga. "Ahimsa paramo dhamma" — non-violence is the highest teaching — stands as the heart of the Jain tradition. Everything else in the teaching — the metaphysics of soul and karma, the three jewels, the fourfold community, the practices of austerity and restraint — is in service of this one principle. Non-violence is not one virtue among many; it is the highest, the root, the summary. The Jinas proclaimed this, and it cannot be improved upon.

The simple version: Non-violence is not just one teaching among many. It is the highest teaching. This is what the Jinas say.

Ahimsa Paramo DhammaHighest TeachingJinas
23.16

संजमो कसायवजणं, सव्वपावविरई।

Restraint is the abandoning of the passions — withdrawal from all evil.

Restraint is defined not by its behavioral manifestations but by its inner content: the abandoning of the passions. External restraint that does not involve the abandoning of the passions is not what the word means here. The passions — anger, pride, deception, greed — are what drive harmful action; to restrain them is to be restrained in the full sense. "Withdrawal from all evil" is both the description of restraint and its result: when the passions are abandoned, withdrawal from evil follows naturally.

The simple version: Restraint means working directly on anger, pride, deception, and greed — not just on behavior. The behaviors follow when the inner causes are addressed.

RestraintPassionsInner Cause
23.17

तवो य कम्मक्खवणं, सव्वंतो।

Austerity is the exhaustion of karma — completely.

Austerity is defined by its function: exhausting karma. Not suffering for its own sake, not demonstrating spiritual seriousness, not earning social respect as a holy person — austerity is a specific tool for the exhaustion of karma that has already accumulated. The word "savvanto" — completely, totally — indicates that the austerity being described is not partial or selective but comprehensive: it addresses all forms of accumulated karma across all the soul's dimensions.

The simple version: Austerity is karma-burning technology. That's its purpose, and that's how to understand why the tradition takes it seriously.

AusterityKarma-BurningComplete
23.18

मोक्खमग्गो तिरयणं, एवं ति भणामि।

The path to liberation is the three jewels — thus I say.

Mahavira's answer to "what is the path to liberation?" is concise and complete: the three jewels. Right faith, right knowledge, right conduct. No additional requirement, no hidden teaching, no esoteric supplement. The path is fully disclosed, fully accessible, and fully sufficient. "Thus I say" — "evam ti bhanami" — is Mahavira's personal declaration of the answer. He is not citing tradition or logic; he is saying what he himself knows.

The simple version: The path has three parts — belief, understanding, and action — and all three together are sufficient. Mahavira says this directly.

Three JewelsRight FaithRight KnowledgeRight Conduct
Part III — The Final Declaration (Sutras 19–25)
23.19

अण्णेहिं सासणेहिं य, भेओ एसो।

The difference from other teachings is this —

Mahavira now addresses the Brahmin's sharpest question: what distinguishes the Jain teaching from others? The answer that follows in the next sutras is not a dismissal of other traditions but a precise identification of what is unique: the comprehensiveness of non-violence, the precise understanding of karma and soul, and the completeness of the path to liberation as disclosed by the Jinas. Other traditions may touch these things; the Jain teaching addresses them completely and correctly.

The simple version: He's about to give a specific, honest answer about what's different. Not that others are wrong, but what is uniquely complete here.

DistinctionOther TraditionsUnique Teaching
23.20

जो सव्वभूयाण हियकारी, जिणसासणे।

That in the Jina's teaching, one works for the benefit of all beings.

The distinctive quality Mahavira identifies is the universality of the Jain concern: working for the benefit of all beings. Not selected beings — not humans only, not followers only, not the high-born only — all beings. This universality of concern, expressed through the absolute commitment to non-violence, is what gives the Jain path its distinctive character. The path is designed not merely for the liberation of the practitioner but in a way that benefits everything the practitioner encounters.

The simple version: The Jain path doesn't just work on liberating you — it actively works for the good of every being you encounter. That universality is distinctive.

All BeingsUniversal BenefitDistinctive
23.21

सुण माहणा तं उत्तमं, अहिंसा परमो।

Listen, O Brahmin, this is the highest — non-violence is supreme.

Mahavira returns, with special emphasis, to the central teaching: non-violence is supreme. The instruction to listen again — "suna Mahana" — is an invitation to receive this with full attention. Not as one teaching among many that has been heard and filed, but as the supreme principle that reorganizes everything. Non-violence is not one dimension of the Jain ethical system; it is the dimension from which all other dimensions derive. Receiving this correctly is transformative.

The simple version: Listen carefully: non-violence isn't just important — it's supreme. Everything else follows from this one principle.

SupremeAhimsaCentral Teaching
23.22

सव्वे जीवा न हंतव्वा, एसो धम्मो सणातणो।

All living beings are not to be slain — this is the eternal teaching.

The positive declaration and the ancient authority converge here. All living beings — every single one, without exception — are not to be slain. "Sanatana" — eternal, ancient, from time immemorial — places this teaching beyond the merely historical. It is not Mahavira's original contribution; it is the eternal teaching that every fully liberated being has perceived and declared. Non-violence as the fundamental ethical reality is not culturally particular or historically contingent; it reflects what is always true about the nature of living beings and their relationship to each other.

The simple version: "Don't harm any living being" is not a new rule — it's an eternal truth that has been true forever. Mahavira is not inventing it; he's declaring it again.

Eternal TeachingAll LifeSanatana
23.23

एयं सोच्चा माहणो, पडिवण्णो।

Having heard this, the Brahmin took up the teaching.

The Brahmin's response is recorded in its simplest form: having heard, he took up the teaching. No elaborate conversion story, no recitation of his previous errors, no dramatic renunciation ceremony is described. He heard; he understood; he entered the path. The simplicity of this record is itself significant. The great teaching of the Sutrakritanga ends not with institutional ceremony but with a human being being changed by what they heard. That is the teaching's purpose, and the Brahmin's response demonstrates that it can work.

The simple version: After hearing all of this, the Brahmin simply took up the path. That is what the teaching is for.

The BrahminConversionHearing Changes
23.24

सव्वे पाणे सव्वे भूए, सव्वे जीवे न हंतव्वे।

All breathing beings, all organisms, all living beings — they are not to be slain.

This penultimate sutra restates the supreme teaching in its most comprehensive form — the threefold enumeration of all life that covers every possible category: breathing beings (those with breath), organisms (those with biological existence), living beings (those with consciousness of any kind). No form of life is excluded. The restatement here, just before the final closing verse, gives it the quality of a summation — this is what the entire text, across all its chapters and all its arguments and all its warnings and all its descriptions, has been saying. This is the heart of it.

The simple version: Every living thing — every breathing being, every organism, every form of life — is not to be harmed. The entire text has been leading to this.

Threefold EnumerationAll LifeSummation
23.25

एत्तो चेव मे पण्णत्तं, भगवइं च सुत्तकडंगे।
सव्वे पाणा, सव्वे भूया, सव्वे जीवा न हंतव्वा।

This indeed has been declared by me, the Blessed One, in the Sutrakritanga: all breathing beings, all beings, all living beings should not be slain.

The Sutrakritanga closes with one of the most beautiful and definitive statements in all of Jain literature — and perhaps in all of world religious literature. Mahavira speaks in the first person, identifying himself as the author of this teaching, grounding it in his personal declaration rather than in abstract authority. The statement is the simplest possible expression of the entire teaching: all beings should not be slain. Not some beings, not beings above a certain level of complexity, not beings who have done nothing wrong — all beings. And then the closing formula: "iti bemi" — "thus I say." With these two words, the Blessed One seals the entire teaching. The Sutrakritanga is complete. The teaching has been given. What remains is to practice it.

This closing is not merely a literary conclusion but a spiritual seal. Across twenty-three chapters, across hundreds of sutras, across refutations of wrong views and descriptions of evil ascetics and stories of queens and dialogues with Brahmins — the entire text has been moving toward this moment. Non-violence toward all living beings. This is the teaching. This is what Mahavira says. This is iti bemi.

The simple version: The entire Sutrakritanga — every chapter, every sutra, every argument, every story — comes down to this one sentence: all living beings should not be harmed. This is what Mahavira declared. This is the teaching. Thus I say. The text is complete.
