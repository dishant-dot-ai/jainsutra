"Built up by austerity, drained of flesh and blood — like a fire-pit covered over with a heap of glowing ash, by his austerity, his inner radiance, the splendour of austerity-glory, he shone forth ever more and more." — Anuttarovavai 3.A1.S17

About This Chapter

Third Varga of the 9th Anga — The Climactic Narrative

Dhanya Anagar is the most extensively narrated figure in the entire Anuttarovavai. Son of the wealthy merchantess Bhadra Sarthavahi of Kakandi, raised in thirty-two palaces and married on a single day to thirty-two princesses, he hears one sermon from Lord Mahavira and renounces immediately. On the very day of his ordination he undertakes the nine-fold abhigraha — a lifelong vow accepting only food the householder was about to discard, only food no other monk, Brahmana, guest, destitute or beggar still wants, and only on the third day after a continuous two-day fast.

After Dhanya, his real brother Sunakshatra (Adhyayana 2) walks the same path; the remaining eight souls — Isidasa, Pellaka, Ramaputta, Chandima, Pedhalaputta, Potilla, Padma, and Vehalla (Adhyayanas 3–10) — are extended by atidesha (reference). All ten reach Sarvarthasiddha, the highest of the five Anuttara realms — and from there each will be reborn just once, in Mahavideha, where they will attain final liberation.

3.A1.S1

Utkshepa — The Opening Question

भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं दोच्चस्स वग्गस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते, तच्चस्स णं भंते ! वग्गस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ?
एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं तच्चस्स वग्गस्स दस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता । तं जहा—
धण्णे य सुणक्खत्ते य, इसिदासे अ आहिए ।
पेल्लए रामपुत्ते य, चंदिमा पिट्ठिमा इ य ।।
पेढालपुत्ते अणगारे, णवमे पोट्टिले वि य ।
वेहल्ले दसमे वुत्ते, इमे य दस आहिया ।। ॥3.A1.S1॥

bhante ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ doccassa vaggassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte, taccassa ṇaṃ bhante ! vaggassa ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte ? evaṃ khalu jambū ! ... dasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā — dhaṇṇe ya suṇakkhatte ya, isidāse a āhie | pellae rāmaputte ya, caṃdimā piṭṭhimā i ya || peḍhālaputte aṇagāre, ṇavame poṭṭile vi ya | vehalle dasame vutte, ime ya dasa āhiyā ||

"O Lord! You have expounded the meaning of the second Varga of the Anuttaropapatika Dasha — what then has the Lord declared for the third?" "Truly so, O Jambu! The Lord has expounded ten chapters in the third Varga: Dhanya, Sunakshatra, Isidasa, Pellaka, Ramaputra, Chandrima, Prashtika; the homeless monk Pedhalaputra ninth, and Poshtila; Vehalla is told as the tenth — these ten are spoken of."

bhante = O venerable Lord · jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ = the attainer of liberation (canonical epithet) · doccassa vaggassa = of the second Varga · taccassa vaggassa = of the third Varga · dasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā = ten chapters have been declared · dhaṇṇe = Dhanya ("the blessed") · suṇakkhatte = Sunakshatra ("of auspicious star") · isidāse = Rishidasa ("servant of seers") · rāmaputte = Ramaputra ("son of Rama") · vehalle dasame vutte = Vehalla is told as the tenth

This opening sutra is the formal hinge between the second and third Vargas. Jambu, having received the meaning of the second Varga, naturally asks his teacher Sudharma what the third Varga teaches. The dialogue form is more than literary frame — it is the Jain conviction that sacred knowledge always passes person to person, question to answer. Sudharma replies with a small mnemonic verse, two short rhythmic couplets that make the ten names easy to memorize. The names themselves carry meaning: Dhanya means "the blessed" — and the irony of the name will become clear, because Dhanya is fortunate not in the worldly wealth he begins with but in the spiritual fortune that lets him let it all go. This third Varga gives the final ten of the scripture's thirty-three souls — householders of Kakandi, not princes of Rajagriha; a different city, a different social class. Yet the inner story is the same: extraordinary worldly station, decisive renunciation upon hearing Mahavira, severe austerity, and rebirth in one of the highest heavens with liberation now within one human birth's reach.

The simple version: Jambu asks his teacher Sudharma, "You just told me what the second part teaches — now what does the third part teach?" Sudharma names the ten people whose stories make up the third part — beginning with Dhanya — and Jambu then asks, "What does the first chapter, the story of Dhanya, actually teach?"

3.A1.S2

The City of Kakandi and the Mother Bhadra

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं काकंदी णामं णयरी होत्था, रिद्धित्थिमियसमिद्धा । सहस्संबवणे उज्जाणे सव्वोउय पुष्फ-फल-समिद्धे । जियसत्तू राया ।
तत्थ णं काकंदीए णयरीए भद्दा णामं सत्थवाही परिवसइ । अड्ढा दित्ता वित्ता वित्थिण्ण-विउल-भवण-सयणासण-जाणवाहणा बहुधण जायरूव-रयया आओग-पओग-संपउत्ता विच्छड्डिय-पउर-भत्तपाणा बहुदासी-दास-गो-महिस-गवेलग प्पभूया बहुजणस्स अपरिभूया । ॥3.A1.S2॥

evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ kākaṃdī ṇāmaṃ ṇayarī hotthā, riddhitthimiyasamiddhā | sahassaṃbavaṇe ujjāṇe savvouya pussa-phala-samiddhe | jiyasattū rāyā | tattha ṇaṃ kākaṃdīe ṇayarīe bhaddā ṇāmaṃ satthavāhī parivasai | aḍḍhā dittā vittā ... bahujaṇassa aparibhūyā |

"Truly so, O Jambu! In that age, at that time, there was a city named Kakandi — prosperous, stable, abundantly endowed. Outside it lay the garden Sahasramravana ('Grove of a Thousand Mangoes'). The king was Jitashatru. In Kakandi there dwelt a caravan-leading merchantess named Bhadra: wealthy, resplendent, possessed of vast mansions, conveyances, gold and silver; engaged in lending and trading; one in whose home, even after meals had been served, much food and drink still remained; rich in maidservants, manservants, cattle, buffaloes, and flocks; and held in high regard by all — never to be slighted by anyone."

kākaṃdī ṇāmaṃ ṇayarī = a city named Kakandi · riddhitthimiyasamiddhā = prosperous, stable, abundantly endowed (the canonical triple formula) · sahassaṃbavaṇe ujjāṇe = the garden Sahasramravana ("Grove of a Thousand Mangoes") · jiyasattū rāyā = King Jitashatru ("Conqueror of Foes") · bhaddā ṇāmaṃ satthavāhī = a caravan-leader/merchantess named Bhadra ("the auspicious one") · aḍḍhā dittā vittā = wealthy, resplendent, of great fortune · vicchaḍḍiya-paura-bhattapāṇā = abundant food and drink remaining even after distribution · bahujaṇassa aparibhūyā = not to be slighted by anyone — held in respect by all

This sutra opens Dhanya's story by painting the world he is born into. Kakandi is described with the canonical triple formula — abundant material resources, stable governance, general well-being. Outside its walls lies the famous Sahasramravana, "the Grove of a Thousand Mangoes," a name suggesting both physical abundance and the kind of place where seekers and sages would gather. What follows is striking: the scripture introduces not Dhanya's father but his mother, Bhadra, and devotes the entire description of the household to her. She is herself a sārthavāhī — a caravan-leader, a merchant in her own right. The Gujarati commentary draws careful attention to this point: in this period, women in business were not exceptional. Bhadra ran a money-lending and trading enterprise; she owned vast mansions, lent capital, and managed extensive cattle and a large household. The detail that "even after meals had been served, abundant food and drink remained" is a canonical signal of true wealth — wealth that is never strained, that always has more than enough to give. A subtle silence runs through this sutra: Dhanya's father is not named anywhere in the chapter. The Gujarati commentary interprets this directly — it is likely Dhanya's father died when Dhanya was young, and Bhadra herself bore the entire weight of raising him. The whole drama of attachment and release in Dhanya's life will run through her.

The simple version: In a great prosperous city called Kakandi, ruled by a king named Jitashatru, there lived a wealthy businesswoman named Bhadra — Dhanya's mother — a caravan-trader and money-lender so rich that even after every meal there was always food left over, and so respected that no one in the city dared slight her.

3.A1.S3

The Birth and Education of Dhanya Kumar

तीसे णं भद्दाए सत्थवाहीए पुत्ते धण्णे णामं दारए होत्था । अहीणपंचिंदियसरीरे लक्खण-वंजण-गुणोववए एमाणुम्माणपमाणपडिपुण्ण सुजायसव्वंग सुंदरंगे ससिसोमाकारे कंते पियदंसणे सुरूवे पंचधाईपरिगहिए, जहा महब्बलो तहा धण्णं कुमारं अम्मापियरो सातिरेगट्ठवासजायं सोहणंसि तिहिकरणणक्खत्त मुहुत्तंसि कलायरियस्स उवणेंति । तए णं से कलायरिए धण्णं कुमारं लेहाइयाओ गणियप्पहाणाओ सउणरुतपज्जवसाणाओ बावत्तरिं कलाओ सुत्तओ य अत्थओ य करणओ य सेहावेइ, सिक्खावेइ । ॥3.A1.S3॥

tīse ṇaṃ bhaddāe satthavāhīe putte dhaṇṇe ṇāmaṃ dārae hotthā | ahīṇa-paṃciṃdiya-sarīre lakkhaṇa-vaṃjaṇa-guṇovavae ... paṃcadhāī-parigahie | ... bāvattariṃ kalāo suttao ya atthao ya karaṇao ya sehāvei, sikkhāvei |

To the merchantess Bhadra was born a son named Dhanya. Complete in all five senses, endowed with auspicious bodily marks and inner virtues; perfect in measure, weight, and proportion; finely formed, beautifully shaped, with the gentle radiance of the moon. Raised by five wet-nurses. Just as Mahabala was, so Dhanya — when he had passed eight years of age — was, on an auspicious day chosen by tithi, karana, nakshatra, and muhurta, taken to a master of the arts. The master taught and trained him in the seventy-two arts — beginning with writing, with arithmetic as the chief, and ending with the language of bird-cries — instructing him in their texts, their meanings, and their practical application: writing, arithmetic, sculpture, drama, singing, instruments, musical notes, percussion, rhythm, gambling, public dialogue, dice, the chess-like ashtapada, etiquette, pottery, food-craft, drink-craft, clothing, unguents, beds, Aryan metres, riddles, Magadhi poetry, gathas, songs, shlokas, working of silver, gold, and aromatic powders, ornaments, women's adornment, reading the marks of women, men, horses, elephants, cattle, cocks, parasols, staves, swords, jewels, coins; architecture, military camp-planning, city-planning, battle-formations and counter-formations, reconnaissance, the wheel-array, eagle-array, wagon-array, ordinary-special-extreme warfare, staff-fighting, fist-fighting, wrestling, creeper-fighting, archery, sword-craft, the science of the bow, refining of silver and gold, thread-play, ball-play, tube-play, leaf-cutting, bracelet-cutting, making the dead seem alive, making the living seem dead, and understanding the cries of birds.

putte dhaṇṇe ṇāmaṃ dārae = a son named Dhanya, a young child · ahīṇa-paṃciṃdiya-sarīre = with all five senses unimpaired · lakkhaṇa-vaṃjaṇa-guṇovavae = endowed with auspicious bodily marks (lakshana), beauty-marks (vyanjana), and inner virtues · emāṇummāṇa-pamāṇa-paḍipuṇṇa = perfect in measure, weight, and proportion · paṃcadhāī-parigahie = raised by five wet-nurses (canonical formula: nurses for feeding, bathing, dressing, playing, lap-holding) · jahā mahabbalo = "just as Mahabala" — a canonical reference-marker pointing to the parallel story of Mahabala in the Bhagavati Sutra · sātirega-aṭṭha-vāsa-jāyaṃ = having passed eight years of age · sohaṇaṃsi tihi-karaṇa-ṇakkhatta-muhuttaṃsi = on an auspicious day chosen by tithi, karana, nakshatra, and muhurta · bāvattariṃ kalāo = the seventy-two arts · suttao ya atthao ya karaṇao ya = in their texts, meanings, and practical application · sauṇarutaṃ = understanding the cries of birds (the apex skill, often associated with sages)

Some entries (e.g. sajīvaṃ / ṇijjīvaṃ) have been read in the canonical sense given by the commentary ("making the dead seem alive / making the living seem dead"). On its surface, this sutra describes a wealthy boy's education. Beneath the surface it is a careful theological setup. The scripture is showing that Dhanya was not given up to spiritual life out of any lack — he was raised in fullness. What he renounces, then, is not a life of nothing; it is a life of everything. Each phrase has precise meaning. The lakshana are major bodily signs (wheel-mark on foot, balanced shoulders, even teeth) read as evidence of noble destiny; the vyanjana are smaller beauty-marks like moles. He was raised by five wet-nurses — a stock royal-household formula, applied here to a merchantess's son, confirming Bhadra's wealth was on the scale of royalty. The list of seventy-two arts is one of the most famous educational catalogues in Indian literature. It defines a complete classical education — literacy, fine arts, poetry, crafts, military arts, sciences of reading bodily signs — culminating in the apex skill of decoding bird-language. He learned each art three times over: as text, as meaning, and as practical capability. The Gujarati commentary observes that this education was meant not only to make Dhanya capable in the world but to make his eventual renunciation more meaningful. A man who renounces ignorance has renounced little; a man who renounces complete worldly mastery has renounced everything.

The simple version: Bhadra had a son named Dhanya — beautiful, healthy, perfectly formed. She raised him with five nurses, and at the age of eight, on an auspicious day, took him to a master who taught him all seventy-two arts a young noble was expected to know — from writing and arithmetic, to music and dance, to military skills and architecture, all the way to crafts so rare that the last one on the list was understanding what birds are saying.

3.A1.S4

Dhanya's Maturity and Mastery

तए णं धण्णे कुमारे बावत्तरिकलापंडिए णवंगसुत्तपडिबोहिए अट्ठारसविह-देसिप्पगारभासाविसारए गीयरई गंधव्व णट्टकुसले हयजोही गयजोही रहजोही बाहुजोही बाहुप्पमद्दी अलं भोगसमत्थे जाए यावि होत्था । ॥3.A1.S4॥

tae ṇaṃ dhaṇṇe kumāre bāvattarikalāpaṃḍie ṇavaṃgasuttapaḍibohie aṭṭhārasaviha-desippagārabhāsāvisārae gīyaraī gaṃdhavva ṇaṭṭakusale hayajohī gayajohī rahajohī bāhujohī bāhuppamaddī alaṃ bhogasamatthe jāe yāvi hotthā |

Then Dhanya Kumar became a master of the seventy-two arts, awakened in the nine bodily organs (which had lain dormant in childhood), expert in the eighteen kinds of regional dialects, devoted to song and rhythm, skilled in heavenly music and drama, a warrior on horseback, a warrior on elephant, a warrior in chariot, a warrior with his arms; one who could crush opponents by the might of his arms; fully fit for the enjoyments of life — such he became, having entered into the prime of youth.

bāvattari-kalā-paṃḍie = a master of the seventy-two arts · ṇavaṃga-sutta-paḍibohie = the nine bodily organs awakened from childhood dormancy (two ears, two eyes, two nostrils, the tongue, the skin, and the mind, all maturing in adolescence) · aṭṭhārasaviha-desippagāra-bhāsā-visārae = expert in the eighteen kinds of regional/desi dialects · gaṃdhavva = (skilled in) gandharva music — the celestial art of song · haya-johī, gaya-johī, raha-johī, bāhu-johī = warrior on horseback, on elephant, in chariot, with the arms · bāhuppamaddī = able to crush an opponent with arm-strength alone · alaṃ bhoga-samatthe = "fully capable for enjoyment" — the canonical phrase marking the age and condition for full worldly life

This sutra completes the portrait of Dhanya as a young man at the peak of his powers. The phrase about the nine bodily organs awakening is canonical shorthand for the maturation of adolescence: in Jain anatomy these are present from birth but their full capacities lie dormant until youth. He became expert in the eighteen kinds of regional dialects — a remarkable detail meaning Dhanya could communicate fluently across the entire linguistic range of the subcontinent. For the son of a caravan-leading merchantess this skill carries trade-significance: he could deal with people from anywhere. He was skilled in gandharva music — the celestial art that approaches the divine — and a fourfold warrior, master of the four classical battle-modes of ancient India. To master one was honorable; to master all four was extraordinary. The closing phrase alaṃ bhoga-samatthe — "fully capable for enjoyment" — marks that he had reached the age and condition in which the full pleasures of the world were now within his reach. The point of dwelling on this perfection is to make the renunciation that follows feel like the giving up of the highest point of worldly achievement. The greatest renunciants in Jain tradition are precisely those who walked away at the peak of their power, with everything in hand. Dhanya stands here at that peak.

The simple version: Dhanya grew up to be a master of all seventy-two arts, fluent in eighteen regional languages, an accomplished singer and dancer, a fearsome warrior on horseback, on elephant, in chariot, and in hand-to-hand combat — strong enough to crush an opponent with his bare arms — and now in the prime of youth, fully ready for the enjoyments of adult life.

3.A1.S5

The Building of the Thirty-Two Palaces

तए णं सा भद्दा सत्थवाही धण्णं दारयं उम्मुक्कबालभावं विण्णाय परिणयमित्तं जोव्वणगमणुपत्तं ... बत्तीसं पासाय वडिंसए कारेइ, अब्भुगयमूसिए पहसिए विव मणिकणगरयणभत्तिचित्ते वाउद्धूयविजय वेजयंतिपडागाछत्ताइच्छत्तकलिए, तुंगे, गगणतलमभिलंघमाणसिहरे, जालंतर रयण पंजरुम्मिल्लियव्व मणिकणगथूभियाए, वियसिय सयपत्तपुंडरीए तिलय रयणद्ध-चंदच्चिए णाणामणिमयदामालंकिए, अंतो बहिं च सण्हे तवणिज्ज रुइल वालुयापत्थडे, सुहफासे ससिरीयरूवे पासादीए जाव पडिरूवे । ॥3.A1.S5॥

tae ṇaṃ sā bhaddā satthavāhī dhaṇṇaṃ dārayaṃ ummukkabālabhāvaṃ viṇṇāya ... battīsaṃ pāsāya vaḍiṃsae kārei, abbhugayamūsie pahasie viva maṇikaṇagarayaṇabhatticitte ... pāsādīe jāva paḍirūve |

Then the merchantess Bhadra, knowing that her son had passed beyond childhood and just attained mature youth, caused thirty-two excellent palaces to be built for him. The palaces were lofty and tall; bright as if smiling; ornamented with paintings of jewels, gold, and gems; topped with the victory-banner Vaijayanti and royal pennants and parasol-upon-parasol fluttering in the wind; soaring high; their spires reaching up to touch the sky; their windows like jeweled lattice-cages opening into views; crowned with finials of jewel and gold; adorned with images of fully blossomed white lotuses; decorated with the sacred tilaka mark, jewels, and the half-moon design; garlanded with strings of varied jewels; their interiors and exteriors smooth, with floors paved in shining bright sand of pure gold; soft to the touch, lustrous in form — so beautiful that they were beyond the comparable, surpassing in every way.

battīsaṃ pāsāya vaḍiṃsae kārei = caused thirty-two excellent palaces to be built · abbhugayamūsie = lofty and tall · pahasie viva = as though smiling · maṇikaṇagarayaṇabhatticitte = ornamented with designs in jewels, gold, and gems · vāuddhūya-vijaya-vejayaṃti-paḍāgā = the wind-blown Vijaya/Vaijayanti banners · gagaṇatalam-abhilaṃghamāṇa-sihare = with peaks crossing the surface of the sky · tavaṇijja ruila vāluyāpatthaḍe = floors paved with shining lustrous golden sand · pāsādīe jāva paḍirūve = beautiful, beyond compare (canonical four-fold seal)

This is the first of two long, ornate descriptions of the dwellings Bhadra built for her son. The motif is canonical: just as Megha Kumar's father had palaces built for him, so Bhadra — though a widowed merchantess — provides exactly this princely treatment for Dhanya. The fact that a woman of business is doing for her son what kings did for theirs is the scripture's quiet way of underscoring her wealth and her devotion. The number thirty-two is significant: in the next sutras, the entire household will be organized in units of thirty-two — palaces, wives, every category of luxury and gift. Thirty-two is the number of completeness in many enumerations: the thirty-two laws of moral conduct, the thirty-two auspicious bodily marks. To say "thirty-two" is to say "the full canonical set." Dhanya's life is built up in the language of completeness. The phrase "as though smiling" applied to the buildings is a beautiful poetic touch: the palaces gleamed with such inner radiance that they seemed not just to shine but to be alive with cheerful welcome. There is also an unspoken layer: Bhadra builds these palaces because she sees that her son has matured and will soon enter householder life. She is not preparing him to renounce; she is preparing him to live a full worldly life. The tragedy and beauty of what comes later — when she will weep and beg him not to leave — is rooted right here, where she is so carefully building everything that he will eventually let go of.

The simple version: When Bhadra saw that her son had grown into a brilliant, accomplished young man, she had thirty-two magnificent palaces built for him — tall, gleaming with gold and jewels, topped with banners and parasols, their spires almost touching the sky, their floors paved with shining gold dust, so beautiful they almost seemed to be smiling.

This sutra describes one specific mansion at the heart of the thirty-two — the central residence, the most splendid. The columns are made of vaidurya — cat's-eye gem — inlaid with gold and other precious stones. Set between the columns are shalabhanjika figures — sculpted feminine forms posed in playful gestures, often holding the branch of a tree. The shalabhanjika is one of the most ancient and beloved motifs in Indian sacred architecture. The balustrades are studded with vajra — diamond. Between them stand twin figures of vidyādhara — celestial knowledge-bearers, mythical beings who carry mystical sciences and dwell in the higher heavens. To carve such figures into the architecture was to invite the protective and elevating presence of the divine into the everyday space of habitation. The closing four-word seal — beautiful, worth beholding, surpassing in form, beyond compare — is the canonical seal that marks something as having reached the upper limit of describable splendor. The exquisite palace is, in the spiritual logic of the canon, also a kind of teacher. It teaches Dhanya, by giving him everything, that everything is not enough. The detail of the bells on the rooftop deserves a brief note: in classical Indian architecture, bells on temple and palace peaks served not only as ornament but as a kind of soft music played by the wind — the structures sang as the breeze passed through them.

The simple version: The most magnificent of the thirty-two palaces stood at the center: built on hundreds of jeweled columns, decorated with sculpted figures of women and celestial beings in playful poses, its diamond-studded balustrades shining with inlaid gold and gems, its golden finials and bells gleaming on the roof, its walls fresh-plastered and fragrant — a building beautiful beyond comparison.

This sutra is the dramatic high point of Dhanya's worldly life. Bhadra arranges, on a single day, his marriage to thirty-two princesses — daughters of the most distinguished wealthy households of the city. The simultaneity is significant: this is not a series of marriages spread across years but a single great event, a one-day wedding to thirty-two brides. The list of gifts is one of the most extensive enumerations in the Jain canon — not literary excess but theological architecture. Each category represents one aspect of complete worldly fulfillment. The sutra closes with the beautifully spare line: Dhanya now dwelled in his fine high palace, savoring the human pleasures of the senses. After two pages of overflowing detail, this single sentence is the still center. The more carefully the picture of fullness has been built, the more powerfully the next sutra — when Dhanya hears the Lord and turns away from it all — will land.

The Arrival of Mahavira and the Hearing of the Dharma

In that age, at that time, the Ascetic Lord Mahavira arrived in the city of Kakandi. The assembly of citizens went forth. Just as King Konika went forth to greet the Lord in earlier accounts, so did King Jitashatru go forth. Then for Dhanya — just as Jamali had gone forth in his story, so did Dhanya. The only distinction is that Dhanya went on foot — and when he reached the Lord he attended upon him with devotion. Then the Ascetic Lord Mahavira expounded the Dharma to Dhanya Kumar and to that great and luminous assembly of seers. The assembly returned to its homes.

This is the turning point of Dhanya's life. Until this sutra, every line has built up his worldly fullness. From this sutra forward, every line will reveal his inner turning. The hinge is the arrival of one being: Mahavira. The scripture handles the arrival in compressed canonical form, with three nested cross-references in a single passage. King Jitashatru of Kakandi processes out as King Konika of Magadha had done. Dhanya processes out as Mahavira's nephew Jamali had done. But then — beautifully — comes the one detail that distinguishes Dhanya from all the references: ṇavaraṃ pāyacāreṇaṃ — "the only difference is, he went on foot." The man who had thirty-two horses, thirty-two elephants, thirty-two of every kind of carriage, walked. The Gujarati commentary draws specific attention to this. It is the first sign — small but unmistakable — of the shift already beginning in Dhanya's heart. He is, formally, still a householder of the highest opulence. But on this morning he wanted to come to Mahavira not as a man of the world coming with his pomp, but as a soul approaching its teacher. He left the carriages behind. He came on foot. The body was already tracking what the soul had not yet quite named. The closing line — "the assembly returned" — is doing significant narrative work. It is clearing the stage. The crowd has gone home. The next sutra will be a private moment: just Dhanya, the lingering teaching, and the rising of dispassion in his own heart.

The simple version: Lord Mahavira arrived in the city of Kakandi; the citizens, the king, and Dhanya himself all went out to hear him. The Rising of Dispassion in Dhanya's Heart

I delight in this teaching. I am ready to act on it. O Lord, it is exactly so. It is truly so. It is without falsehood. It is without doubt. It is exactly as you say. This is the inner pivot of the entire chapter. The previous sutras built up Dhanya's worldly life with extraordinary care; this sutra describes the inner shift in a few lines that are utterly clear and utterly weighted. The first verb is soccā — "having heard." But the next is ṇisamma — "having taken it in." Many people heard the sermon that day; only some absorbed it. Dhanya's first response is joy — not the troubled response one might expect from a man whose entire worldly life has just been called into question. The teaching has not threatened him; it has freed something in him. The sequence of his declarations is exquisite and cumulative: faith, trust, delight, action — a canonical teaching about how received truth ripens in the soul. It does not stop at intellectual agreement; it does not stop at emotional appreciation; it ripens into acted-upon conviction. Then a second fourfold formula: it is so, truly so, without falsehood, without doubt. By the end Dhanya has placed his entire spiritual weight upon what Mahavira has spoken. The fact that Dhanya names only his mother — not his thirty-two wives, not his palaces, not his fortune — is striking. The wives, the wealth, the buildings: he is ready to walk away from all of them without explanation. But Bhadra is the one human being whose consent he will seek. Mahavira's reply is the briefest possible: do what brings true well-being, and let no obstacle slow the path.

The simple version: After the sermon, Dhanya — alone with what he had heard — found his heart filled with joy. There is only one thing: I want to ask my mother first. Then, with your permission, I will shave my head and become a homeless monk." Mahavira gently replied: "Do whatever brings you true happiness, dear one. But do not let anything delay the path."

tae ṇaṃ se dhaṇṇe kumāre ... haṭṭa-tuṭṭhe ... jāva jeṇeva ammayāo teṇeva uvāgacchai ... evaṃ khalu ammayāo ! mae samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa aṃtiyaṃ dhamme ṇisaṃte, se vi ya me dhamme icchie, paḍicchie, abhirue | ... dhaṇṇesi ṇaṃ tumaṃ jāyā ... | ... saṃsārabhauvvigge, bhīe jamma-jarā-maraṇeṇaṃ ...

Then Dhanya Kumar, having been thus addressed by Mahavira, became delighted and joyful; he bowed and saluted the Lord three times, and came to where his mother was. He greeted his mother with the words of victory and triumph, and spoke thus: "O revered mother, truly I have heard the Dharma in the presence of Mahavira; and that Dharma I have desired, accepted, and delighted in." Then his mother said: "Blessed are you, O son! Fulfilled are you, O son! Of merit accomplished are you! Marked with the auspicious signs are you! — that you have heard the Dharma and embraced it." Then Dhanya spoke a second time: "O revered mother, I am stricken with dread of the cycle of births and deaths, and afraid of birth, old age, and dying; therefore, with your permission I wish to have my head shaved in the presence of Mahavira and to go forth from the household life into the homeless life of a monk."

jaeṇaṃ vijaeṇaṃ vaddhāvei = greeted (her) with "Jaya, Vijaya" — the canonical formula of auspicious greeting · icchie, paḍicchie, abhirue = desired, accepted, delighted in · dhaṇṇesi ṇaṃ tumaṃ jāyā = "O son, you are blessed" — note the wordplay on Dhanya's name, which means "blessed/fortunate" · kayatthesi / kayapuṇṇesi / kayalakkhaṇesi = of accomplished purpose / of accomplished merit / of fulfilled auspicious signs · doccaṃ pi = a second time, again · saṃsārabhauvvigge = stricken / agitated by the dread of the cycle of worldly existence · jamma-jarā-maraṇeṇaṃ = of birth, old age, and death (the canonical triad)

This is the threshold sutra. The teacher's blessing has been received; the resolve has been formed; only the conversation with the mother remains. The greeting Dhanya offers is the canonical phrase "may victory be yours, may triumph be yours" — the standard reverential salutation a son gives to a parent. The detail is not decorative: Dhanya signals, before he speaks a single word about renunciation, that he has come to her with full filial reverence. He does not begin with "I want to leave"; he begins with what happened in his heart. Now an extraordinary thing happens. Before he can even ask his question, the mother responds — and her response is one of the most beautiful in the canon: four praises in the same fourfold pattern Dhanya himself just used, the very first word being dhaṇṇesi — "you are blessed" — using the very root that forms his name. She has named him Dhanya his whole life, and now, in the moment her son tells her of his spiritual joy, she addresses him as "blessed" in the deepest sense the name was always pointing to. So Dhanya speaks again — doccaṃ pi, "a second time." Now he names his actual fear: stricken with dread of the cycle of worldly existence; afraid of birth, old age, and death. These are not problems to be solved within worldly life; they are the conditions of worldly life. Only the path beyond worldly life can answer them.

The simple version: Filled with joy after Mahavira's blessing, Dhanya bowed three times to the Lord, came home to his mother, and greeted her with reverence. He told her, "I heard the Lord's teaching, and I loved it with all my heart." His mother responded with a flood of blessing: "Blessed are you, my son! Fulfilled, full of merit, marked with every auspicious sign — that you have heard such Dharma and embraced it!" Then Dhanya spoke again, more carefully: "Mother, I am afraid of this whole round of birth, old age, and death. With your permission, I want to go to the Lord, have my head shaved, and become a homeless monk."

3.A1.S12

Dhanya Kumar's Words of Detachment

तए णं धण्णे कुमारे अम्मयं एवं वयासी— तहेव णं तं अम्मयाओ ! ... एवं खलु अम्मयाओ ! माणुस्सए भवे अणेगजाइ-जरा-मरण-रोग सारीरमाणस पकामदुक्ख-वेयण-वसण-सओवद्दवाभिभूए, अधुवे, अणिइए, असासए, संज्झब्भ-रागसरिसे, जलबुब्बुयसमाणे, कुसग्गजलबिंदुसण्णिभे, सुविणगदंसणोवमे, विज्जुलयाचंचले, अणिच्चे, सडणपडणविद्धंसणधम्मे, पुव्विं वा पच्छा वा अवस्स विप्पजहियव्वे भविस्सइ, से केस णं जाणइ अम्मयाओ ! के पुव्विं गमणयाए, के पच्छा गमणयाए ? ॥3.A1.S12॥

tae ṇaṃ dhaṇṇe kumāre ... māṇussae bhave ... adhuve, aṇiie, asāsae, saṃjjhabbha-rāgasarise, jalabubbuyasamāṇe, kusaggajalabiṃdusaṇṇibhe, suviṇagadaṃsaṇovame, vijju-layācaṃcale, aṇicce, saḍaṇapaḍaṇaviddhaṃsaṇadhamme, puvviṃ vā pacchā vā avassa vippa-jahiyavve bhavissai, se kesa ṇaṃ jāṇai ammayāo ! ke puvviṃ gamaṇayāe, ke pacchā gamaṇayāe ?

Then Dhanya said to his mother: "It is just so, O revered mother, as you have said — 'O son, you are dear to us, beloved, and so on… you may take ordination only after our death.' But truly, this human life is overpowered by countless births, old age, death, disease, by extreme pains and sufferings of body and mind, by separations and every kind of calamity; it is unstable, impermanent, not lasting — like the colour of the evening twilight, like a bubble on water, like a dewdrop balanced on the tip of a blade of grass, like the seeing of a dream, fickle as a flash of lightning, transient, by its very nature liable to decay, to fall, and to be destroyed; sooner or later it must without fail be left behind. Who knows, O revered mother, who will go first and who will go after? Therefore, with your permission I wish to have my head shaven in the presence of Mahavira and to go forth from the household life into the homeless life of a monk."

māṇussae bhave = the human existence, this human life · adhuve, aṇiie, asāsae = unsteady, not eternal, not lasting · saṃjjhabbha-rāgasarise = like the colour of evening twilight · jalabubbuyasamāṇe = like a bubble on water · kusaggajalabiṃdusaṇṇibhe = like a drop of water on the tip of a blade of kusha grass · suviṇagadaṃsaṇovame = like the seeing of a dream · vijju-layācaṃcale = fickle as a streak of lightning · saḍaṇapaḍaṇaviddhaṃsaṇadhamme = of the very nature of decay, falling, and destruction · se kesa ṇaṃ jāṇai ... ke puvviṃ gamaṇayāe, ke pacchā gamaṇayāe = who knows? — who will go first, who will go after?

This is one of the most beloved passages in the entire Anuttarovavai because it gives, in the voice of a young man on the threshold of renunciation, the clearest enumeration of the impermanence of human existence. Dhanya does not argue with his mother. He honours every word she has spoken. He acknowledges that he is dear to her, beloved. He simply lifts the gaze of love upward toward the truth that no love at the human level can outlast. The series of similes is a famous canonical chain. Each image is chosen with extraordinary precision, and together they form a graduated meditation on transience. The colour of the evening twilight glows for a few minutes and is gone; a bubble on water swells and bursts in an instant; a dewdrop on the sharp tip of a blade of grass trembles and falls; a dream is vivid while it lasts but vanishes the moment one wakes; a flash of lightning is over before the eye can hold it. Each image is shorter-lived than the one before. The closing question is the sharpest stroke: "Who knows, O mother, who will go first and who will go after?" The conventional reasoning of the parent is, "Wait until we are gone, and then renounce" — as if death stood patiently in line. But death does not stand in line. To stake one's spiritual life on a sequence death has never agreed to follow is a foolish wager.

The simple version: Dhanya tells his mother: "I love you, and what you say is true — I am dear to you. But this human life is short and uncertain, like a bubble on water or a flash of lightning; nobody knows who will die first. So please give me your blessing and let me become a monk now, while I still can."

3.A1.S13

Dhanya's Ordination

तए णं तं धण्णं कुमारं भद्दा सत्थवाही जाहे णो संचाएइ जाव जियसत्तुं आपुच्छइ । इच्छामि णं देवाणुप्पिया ! धण्णस्स दारयस्स णिक्खममाणस्स छत्त-मउड-चामराओ य विदिण्णाओ ।
तए णं जियसत्तू राया भद्दं सत्थवाहिं एवं वयासी— अच्छाहि णं तुमं देवाणुप्पिए ! सुणिवुत्तवीसत्था, अहण्णं सयमेव धण्णस्स दारयस्स णिक्खमणसक्कारं करिस्सामि ।
सयमेव जियसत्तू णिक्खमणं करेइ, जहा थावच्चापुत्तस्स कण्हो ।
तए णं धण्णे दारए सयमेव पंचमुट्ठियं लोयं करेइ जाव पव्वइए ।
तए णं धण्णे दारए अणगारे जाए ईरियासमिए जाव गुत्तबंभयारी । ॥3.A1.S13॥

tae ṇaṃ taṃ dhaṇṇaṃ kumāraṃ bhaddā satthavāhī jāhe ṇo saṃcāei jāva jiyasattuṃ āpucchai | ... acchāhi ṇaṃ tumaṃ devāṇuppie ! ... ahaṇṇaṃ sayameva dhaṇṇassa dārayassa ṇikkhamaṇasakkāraṃ karissāmi | ... paṃcamuṭṭhiyaṃ loyaṃ karei jāva pavvaie | ... aṇagāre jāe īriyāsamie jāva guttabaṃbhayārī |

When the merchant-lady Bhadra was unable to dissuade Dhanya, she went and asked King Jitashatru: "O Beloved of the gods! I wish that, for my son Dhanya as he goes forth, the royal parasol, the crown, and the chamaras be granted." The king said: "Stay calm, O Beloved of the gods — I myself will perform the ordination-honour for the boy Dhanya." King Jitashatru himself conducted the ordination-procession, just as Krishna had done for Thavaccaputra. Then Dhanya, with his own hand, plucked out his hair in five fistfuls and was ordained. Thus the boy Dhanya became a homeless monk — careful in walking, perfectly chaste and self-guarded.

jāhe ṇo saṃcāei = when she was not able (to turn him back) · chatta-mauḍa-cāmarāo = the (royal) parasol, the crown, and the chamaras (yak-tail fly-whisks) · jahā thāvaccāputtassa kaṇho = "just as Krishna (did) for the son of Thavachcha" — narrated in the Jnata Dharma Katha · paṃcamuṭṭhiyaṃ loyaṃ karei = he plucks out his hair in five fistfuls (the canonical pañca-muṣṭi loca) · aṇagāre jāe = became a homeless monk · īriyāsamie = endowed with care in walking (the first of the five samitis) · guttabaṃbhayārī = perfectly chaste, fully guarded in celibacy

When the mother Bhadra could not turn Dhanya from his resolve, she did not weep helplessly, nor did she try to bind him by emotional pressure. She rose to the height of the moment. Recognising that her son was passing from her household into the path of the Tirthankara, she went to the king himself and asked that the symbols of royal honour — the parasol, the crown, the yak-tail fly-whisks — be brought out for her son. This is the deepest reverence a mother can offer: she sends him away as a king, because she now sees what kind of king he is becoming. King Jitashatru's response is equally noble. He does not merely grant the symbols; he says, "I myself shall conduct the ordination-procession of your son." The scripture explicitly compares this to the ordination of Thavaccaputra, conducted by Krishna Vasudeva himself. Then comes the moment all the wealth of the world cannot match. Dhanya himself, with his own two hands, performs pañca-muṣṭi loca — the plucking-out of the hair in five fistfuls. This is the unmistakable mark of Jain ordination. No barber's blade is permitted; no scissors. The renouncer, by his own act, removes the hair from the body — accepting in that single gesture all the bodily pain that the ascetic life will henceforth carry without complaint. With this act, Dhanya is no longer a layman in monk's dress; he is a monk.

The simple version: When Dhanya's mother saw she could not change his mind, she went to the king himself and asked that her son be honoured with royal ceremony as he became a monk; the king said yes and led the ordination-procession personally; then Dhanya pulled out his own hair with his bare hands and became a monk who walked with perfect care and perfect self-restraint.

3.A1.S14

Dhanya Muni's Nine-Fold Abhigraha

तए णं से धण्णे अणगारे जं चेव दिवसे मुंडे भवित्ता अगाराओ अणगारियं पव्वइए, तं चेव दिवसं समणं भगवं महावीरं वंदइ णमंसइ ... एवं वयासी— इच्छामि णं भंते ! तुब्भेहिं अब्भणुण्णाए समाणे जावज्जीवाए छट्ठं छट्ठेणं अणिक्खित्तेणं आयंबिलपरिग्गहिएणं तवोकम्मेणं अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरित्तए । छट्टस्स वि य णं पारणयंसि कप्पि मे आयंबिलं पडिगाहित्तए णो चेव णं अणायंबिलं । तं पि य णं संसट्ठं णो चेव णं असंसट्ठं । तं पि य णं उज्झियधम्मियं णो चेव णं अणुज्झिय धम्मियं । तं पि य णं अण्णे बहवे समण माहण अतिहि-किवण वणीमगा णावकंखंति ।
अहासुहं देवाणुप्पिया ! मा पडिबंधं करेह । ॥3.A1.S14॥

tae ṇaṃ se dhaṇṇe aṇagāre jaṃ ceva divase muṃḍe bhavittā ... taṃ ceva divasaṃ ... icchāmi ṇaṃ bhante ! ... jāvajjīvāe chaṭṭhaṃ chaṭṭheṇaṃ aṇikkhitteṇaṃ āyaṃbilapariggahieṇaṃ tavokammeṇaṃ ... saṃsaṭṭhaṃ ṇo ceva ṇaṃ asaṃsaṭṭhaṃ ... ujjhiyadhammiyaṃ ṇo ceva ṇaṃ aṇujjhiya dhammiyaṃ ... aṇṇe bahave samaṇa māhaṇa atihi-kivaṇa vaṇīmagā ṇāvakaṃkhaṃti |

On the very day on which the monk Dhanya was shaven and went forth into the homeless life, on that very day he bowed before Mahavira and said: "O Lord, with your permission I wish to live by this austere practice — for the whole of my life, fasting two days at a stretch without ever a break, and at every parana taking only the ayambil food, suffusing my soul therewith. On the day of breaking the fast it shall be permitted for me to accept only ayambil food and not any other. And that food only when it is samsrishta (already touching the giver's hand) and not asamsrishta. And that food only of the ujjhita-dharma kind (food the householder is otherwise about to throw away). And that food only when no other monk, no Brahmana, no guest, no destitute, and no beggar still desires it." (Mahavira said:) "As is your happy choice, O Beloved of the gods! Do not delay."

jaṃ ceva divase = on that very day · jāvajjīvāe = for the whole of my life · chaṭṭhaṃ chaṭṭheṇaṃ = with continuous chhaṭṭha (two-day fasts, taking food only on the third day) · aṇikkhitteṇaṃ = without ever breaking it (literally "without ever setting it down") · āyaṃbilapariggahieṇaṃ = combined with ayambil (single sitting, dry/spiceless food, no oil, salt, curd, milk, greens, or sweetness) · saṃsaṭṭhaṃ = "touched" — food the giver was about to use themselves, already smeared on the serving-ladle · ujjhiyadhammiyaṃ = of the ujjhita-dharma kind — food the householder considers fit to be discarded · aṇṇe bahave samaṇa māhaṇa atihi-kivaṇa vaṇīmagā ṇāvakaṃkhaṃti = many other monks, Brahmanas, guests, destitute, beggars do not desire (it) · ahāsuhaṃ devāṇuppiyā ! mā paḍibaṃdhaṃ kareha = "as gives you happiness, do not delay"

This is the very heart of the chapter and one of the most extreme austerity-vows in the entire Jain canon. On the very day of his ordination, before even sleeping a single night as a monk, Dhanya stood before Mahavira and asked permission to bind himself for life to a discipline whose terms are almost impossible to imagine. Foundation 1 — the two-day fast, lifelong, unbroken. The base practice is chhaṭṭha — fasting for two whole days, taking food only on the third. Most ascetics undertake this as a periodic austerity; Dhanya undertook it lifelong, without ever once setting it down. Foundation 2 — even on the third day, only ayambil: a single sitting in twenty-four hours, taking only dry, tasteless, plain food — no oil, no salt, no curd, no milk, no green vegetables, no sweetness, no spice. Now come the conditions that make this vow famous as the nava-koti (nine-fold) abhigraha: only ayambil, never non-ayambil; only samsrishta food (the giver's hand or ladle already smeared with that food — i.e., what they were about to eat themselves, not specially prepared for the monk); only ujjhita-dharma food (food the householder considers no longer of use to themselves — stale food, food that would be set aside or thrown away); never anujjhita-dharma food; and even within that, only food that no other monk, no Brahmana, no guest, no destitute, and no beggar still desires. The principle: a monk should be a presence in the world that creates no extra production, no extra consumption — and ideally, a presence that converts what would be wasted into what sustains the path to liberation. Dhanya did not wish to be fed; he wished only to consume what no living being still desired. The body is kept alive only by what would otherwise have rotted. Mahavira's response is, as ever, the Tirthankara's mark of approval: ahāsuhaṃ devāṇuppiyā, mā paḍibaṃdhaṃ kareha — "As gives you happiness. Do not delay." From that day until the end, Dhanya lived inside this nine-fold cage of fire — a cage that, paradoxically, was for him the open sky.

The simple version: On his very first day as a monk, Dhanya promised the Lord: for the rest of my life I will eat at most once every three days, and even then only the simplest, plainest food — and only food the householder was about to throw away, only food that no other monk or beggar or guest still wants. The Lord blessed him and said, "Don't delay — go ahead."

3.A1.S15

Dhanya's First Parana and Request to Beg Alms

तए णं से धण्णे अणगारे पढमछट्ठखमणपारणयंसि पढमाए पोरिसीए सज्झायं करेइ । बीयाए पोरिसीए झाणं झियायइ, तइयाए पोरिसीए अतुरियमचवलमसंभंते मुहपोत्तियं पडिलेहेइ ... जेणेव समणे भगवं महावीरे तेणेव उवागच्छइ ... एवं वयासी— इच्छामि णं भंते ! तुब्भेहिं अब्भणुण्णाए छट्ठक्खमण-पारणगंसि कागंदीए णयरीए उच्च-णीय-मज्झिमाइं कुलाइं घरसमुदाणस्स भिक्खायरियाए अडित्तए ।
अहासुहं देवाणुप्पिया मा पडिबंधं करेह । ॥3.A1.S15॥

tae ṇaṃ se dhaṇṇe aṇagāre paḍhamachaṭṭhakhamaṇapāraṇayaṃsi paḍhamāe porisīe sajjhāyaṃ karei | bīyāe porisīe jhāṇaṃ jhiyāyai | taiyāe porisīe aturiyamacavalamasaṃbhaṃte ... | icchāmi ṇaṃ bhante ! ... kāgaṃdīe ṇayarīe ucca-ṇīya-majjhimāiṃ kulāiṃ gharasamudāṇassa bhikkhāyariyāe aḍittae |

Then Dhanya, on the day of breaking his very first two-day fast, in the first quarter of the day did scripture-study; in the second quarter he sat in deep meditation; in the third quarter, without any haste, without any restlessness, without any agitation, he inspected the mouth-cloth, then his bowls and cloth wrappings, wiped them clean, took them up; then went to where Mahavira was, bowed, and said: "O Lord, with your permission I wish, on this day of breaking my fast, to walk through the high, the low, and the middling households of Kakandi, gathering alms from a community of homes." (And Mahavira said:) "As is your happy choice — do not delay."

This is a small but vivid window into the inward shape of an ideal monastic day. Even on the day of breaking a forty-eight-hour fast — a day on which any ordinary body would be desperate for food — Dhanya does not rush. He divides the day exactly as a monk must divide it. The first quarter goes to scripture-study. The second goes to meditation. Then Dhanya, with the Lord's permission, set forth from the Sahasramravan grove. Without haste, without restlessness, without agitation, with his gaze cast forward upon the path no farther than the length of a yoke before him, he came to Kakandi. He walked the alms-rounds through the high, low, and middling households, accepting only ayambil food, never non-ayambil, only what no other person desired. Sometimes if he obtained food he obtained no water; if he obtained water he obtained no food. Yet he remained un-dejected, un-disturbed in mind, un-clouded, un-saddened, untiring in his discipline. He returned to Mahavira, made the formal pratikramana for the going and the coming, declared everything correctly and incorrectly received, and showed the food and water before the Lord. This sutra carries one of the most luminous and beloved similes in the entire Jain canon: a monk eating food bilamiva paṇṇaga-bhūeṇaṃ appāṇeṇaṃ — "with the self like a snake entering its hole." When a snake enters its hole, it does not pause at the entrance to taste the air, to examine the smell, to consider whether the rim is pleasant or rough; it goes straight in for one purpose only — the protection of the body. So too the monk eats: the food touches every part of the tongue, but the tongue is not engaged with the food. Eating is reduced to a function — like breathing, like the heartbeat — without becoming an experience. The text records, with a sober single sentence, the practical outcome of the most extreme abhigraha in the canon: "if he obtained food, he did not obtain water; if he obtained water, he did not obtain food." The discarded portion that no other beggar wanted, that the householder was about to throw away, that fell within the strict bounds of ayambil and samsrishta — such a portion was rare. To find both food and water in the same alms-round was rarer still. So Dhanya often returned with only one of the two. He often returned with very little. The text uses five precise negations: not dejected, not troubled in mind, not clouded, not saddened, not weary. These five together describe the inner fortress of a true ascetic. The body may have walked many streets and received almost nothing; the mind has not registered failure, because there was no expectation. The four words for non-attachment in eating draw the limit between an eating that nourishes the soul's path and one that nourishes the soul's bondage. Most beings, even pious ones, slip somewhere along this scale. Dhanya did not slip.

The simple version: Dhanya walked through the city, gaze fixed on the ground in front of his feet, taking only food no one else wanted; sometimes he got food without water, sometimes water without food, but he was never sad or upset; he came back to the Lord, showed everything he had received, and ate the food the way a snake enters its hole — straight, without lingering, without enjoying it as taste, simply taking what the body needed and continuing the path.

Life of Tapas — General Picture of His Emaciated Body

Then the monk Dhanya, in the presence of the elder monks, studied the eleven Angas beginning with the Samayika. Having studied them, he dwelt suffusing his soul with restraint and austerity. This sutra opens the most striking section of Dhanya's chapter — and indeed one of the most carefully observed depictions of a monk's body in the entire Jain canon. Mahavira moves on to other regions; Dhanya now studies the entire eleven Angas under the elder monks of Mahavira's order. To study all eleven Angas while simultaneously living the lifelong nine-fold abhigraha is a feat of almost incomprehensible discipline. Then comes the description that has made this chapter beloved across centuries. The text piles eleven adjectives upon his austerity before naming the bodily consequence: he became dry, parched, fleshless, reduced to bone-and-skin, his joints creaking when he moved, gaunt and hollow, the great veins standing out beneath the skin like cords drawn tight on a frame. The threefold image of weariness in speech is one of the most poignant in the canon: even after he has finished speaking, he sinks; while speaking, he sinks; even at the bare thought "I am about to speak," he sinks. The body's reserves are so thin that the act of forming a sentence draws on what is barely there. And yet, the bare life-force of the soul keeps him walking, keeps him standing. The simile of the wagon is the masterstroke. A wagon piled with dry wood, leaves, castor-stalks, or charcoal — that has stood in the sun until every fibre has lost its moisture — every joint of its frame creaks against every other. So too Dhanya's body. The bones, no longer cushioned by flesh, audibly creak as he walks. But the second simile turns the picture inside out. To a casual observer, ash on top of a fire-pit looks merely grey, dead, finished. But anyone who has tended a fire knows that beneath the surface of ash lies a heat that can rekindle the world. Dhanya's body, to the eye, was ash; but within him burned the teja — the inner radiance — of austerity. The soul, strangely, strengthened in inverse proportion. The closing phrase aīva aīva uvasobhemāṇe uvasobhemāṇe — "ever more and more shining forth, ever more and more shining forth" — is repeated twice, as if no single statement of his glory could hold it. The body is the furnace; the soul is the gold being purified.

The simple version: Mahavira moved on to other regions; Dhanya stayed and studied all the scriptures while continuing his fierce austerities, until his body became so dry and thin that only bone and skin were left and his joints actually creaked when he walked, like an old wagon of dry wood; but inside, his soul shone brighter and brighter, like a fire hidden under a heap of ash.

3.A1.S18

Dhanya's Feet and Toes

धण्णस्स णं अणगारस्स पायाणं अयमेयारूवे तवरूवलावण्णे होत्था— से जहाणामए सुक्कछल्ली इ वा कट्ठपाउया इ वा जरग्ग ओवाहणा इ वा, एवामेव धण्णस्स अणगारस्स पाया सुक्का लुक्खा णिम्मंसा अट्ठिचम्मच्छिरत्ताए पण्णायंति, णो चेव णं मंससोणियत्ताए ।

From this sutra onward, the scripture begins a careful, head-to-toe inventory of Dhanya's body, member by member. The opening phrase of each comparison is striking: tava-rūva-lāvaṇya — literally "the loveliness shaped by austerity." Lāvaṇya normally means physical beauty, the kind that draws admiration in worldly life. By calling Dhanya's bony, fleshless body tapas-shaped lāvaṇya, the sutra-makers are making a quiet revolution: the new beauty of an austere monk is not the rounded fullness of well-fed limbs but the stripped clarity of bone made visible. The body has become a transparency through which something higher shines. The similes for the feet are three village objects: dried tree-bark, a wooden sandal, an old worn leather sandal. All three are flat, hardened, surface-only — no living softness inside. The similes for the toes are even more vivid: the pods of kala, mung, and urad, when plucked tender and left in the sun, shrivel into wrinkled little curves. The repeated formula at the close — "bone-and-skin only, and not at all flesh-and-blood" — drives home the central point with the patience of a litany. Each shrivelled limb is a chapter in the falling-away of karma. The body's loss is the soul's gain.

The simple version: Dhanya's feet had become as dry as old tree-bark, a wooden sandal, or a worn leather shoe — just bone and skin; his toes had shrivelled like green-gram pods left out to dry in the sun.

The march of the inventory continues upward. For the shins, the comparison is to the legs of birds — the crow, the kanka, the dheniyaliya — all known in the canon's world for the thin, dried-stick appearance of their lower limbs. The shin of a starving crow is famously bony; that is what Dhanya's shins had become. For the knees, the simile shifts to the joints of birds. The peacock's leg-joint is a familiar image, slim and dry beneath its feathers. The bird-imagery and the plant-imagery together carry a quiet teaching. The body's parts are being compared not to anything precious, not to anything the world admires, but to common bones, joints, and dried shoots — the kind of objects nobody picks up, nobody saves. The text is saying, plainly: this is no longer a body to be desired. It is a body that has stepped beyond the field of desire.

This sutra is the great mid-section of the body-description, gathering five regions of the torso. Each member is given its own simile-cluster, each cluster reaching further into household and street-life imagery. For the waist, the comparison is to the foot of a beast of burden — large-boned, leathery, hardened. For the belly, the simile-set turns to vessels: the dried-up water-skin, the iron pan used for parching gram, the wooden trough used for kneading flour. All three share one feature: emptiness held in a thin shell. For the ribs, the imagery shifts to rows: the point is countability. A normal chest has its ribs hidden beneath flesh; Dhanya's ribs stood out so clearly that they could be counted one by one, like a line of vessels arranged on a shelf. The Gujarati commentary lingers on this: gani shakatī hatī — "they could be counted." For the spine — a string of beads, of small balls, of children's round playthings. Each vertebra had become a separate, visible bead on the long line of the spine. To run a finger down such a back is to feel each knot of bone discretely, with no muscle filling the spaces between. For the chest, the comparison is gentlest of all: the underside of a bamboo basket, a fan of bamboo strips. All are open, light, with thin ribs holding a flat sheet — no thickness, no flesh, no weight. When the body is no longer being nourished, the body shrinks; but the soul of the ascetic does not shrink — the soul becomes more powerful. The very disappearance of the body's support is what allows the soul's strength to step forward.

The simple version: Dhanya's hips were hard like a camel's foot, his belly empty like a dried water-skin, his ribs so visible you could count them like cups in a row, his backbone like a string of beads, and his chest as thin and flat as a bamboo or palm-leaf fan — every part was just bone and skin, with no flesh or blood left.

The inventory now reaches the upper limbs. The arms are compared to long dried tree-pods — the shami, bahayasa, and agastya — all known for their long, narrow, hardened pods. The arm of a fed body is rounded by muscle; Dhanya's arms had become like those long, dry, woody pods. The hands are compared to three things flat, thin, and dried: a piece of cow-dung cake (the chhāṇa used as fuel in village kitchens), a withered banyan leaf, and a withered palasha-leaf. The Gujarati commentary closes this section with a memorable line: "Dhanya Anagara's arms had also become like the dried branches of a tree." The simile is not invention but observation.

This sutra brings the inventory to the head and face. The neck is compared to the necks of vessels — the karaka, the small clay kuṇḍī, and the long-spouted surāhi. All three share one shape — a long, thin, narrow neck holding the body of the pot. Dhanya's neck had become like that: a long thin column of bone bearing the head, with no flesh to round it. The chin is compared to dried fruits and stones. The mango-stone image is particularly precise — the inner kernel of a mango, when the flesh has all been eaten and the stone has dried in the sun, has a shape and a stripped-down hardness that closely matches a fleshless human chin. The lips carry a striking set of similes: a dried-up leech (alive, plump with blood; dead and dried, shrivelled into a thin, dark, curved line), a hardened pellet of plant-resin, a dried pellet of red lac. The Gujarati commentary draws out the gentleness of the contrast: "the lips that had once been red as the bimba-fruit had now, by austerity, dried out and become entirely pale." The tongue is compared to withered leaves. This image gains its full weight from the earlier mention that even speaking exhausted Dhanya — the body had so withdrawn from the act of speech that the tongue itself was now closer to dried plant-matter than to living flesh. The Vivechana adds an essential note: genuine austerity is for ātma-shuddhi — purification of the soul — not for the display of bodily endurance. Without right knowledge and right insight, even the most extreme physical austerity is bāla-tapa — "child-austerity" — which may lead to a heavenly birth, but cannot lead the soul to liberation. Dhanya's austerity is true precisely because it is rooted in his right understanding and devotion to the Tirthankara's path; it leads him not to a merely high heaven but to Sarvarthasiddha — the highest Anuttara abode.

The simple version: Dhanya's neck was as long and thin as the spout of a water-pot; his chin was as shrunken as a dried mango-stone; his lips, once red and full, had turned dark and dried like a withered leech; his tongue had become as dry as a fallen leaf — every part, only bone and skin.

3.A1.S23

Dhanya's Inner Radiance Through Severe Tapas

धण्णे णं अणगारे सुक्केणं भुक्खेणं लुक्खेणं पायजंघोरुणा, विगयतडि-करालेणं कडिकडाहेणं, पिट्ठिमवस्सिएणं उदरभायणेणं जोइज्जमाणेहिं पासुलिकडएहिं, अक्खसुत्तमाला इव गणेज्जमाणेहिं पिट्ठिकरंडगसंधीहिं, गंगातरंगभूएणं उरकडग-देसभाएणं, सुक्कसप्पसमाणाहिं बाहाहिं, सिढिल-कडाली विव लंबंतेहि य अग्गहत्थेहिं, कंपणवाइओ वेवमाणीए विव सीसघडीए, पम्माणवयणकमले, उब्भडघडमुहेव उच्छुड्डणयणकोसे जीवंजीवेणं गच्छइ ... हुयासणे इव भासरासिपलिच्छण्णे तवेणं तेएणं तवतेयसिरीए अईव अईव उवसोभेमाणे उवसोभेमाणे चिट्ठइ । ॥3.A1.S23॥

dhaṇṇe ṇaṃ aṇagāre sukkeṇaṃ bhukkheṇaṃ lukkheṇaṃ ... vigayataḍi-karāleṇaṃ kaḍikaḍāheṇaṃ ... akkhasuttamālā iva gaṇejjamāṇehiṃ piṭṭhikaraṃḍagasaṃdhīhiṃ ... gaṃgātaraṃgabhūeṇaṃ urakaḍaga-desabhāeṇaṃ ... ubbhaḍaghaḍamuheva ucchuḍḍaṇayaṇakose ... huyāsaṇe iva bhāsarāsipalicchaṇṇe ...

The venerable monk Dhanya, dried up by hunger and emaciation, had legs and thighs like sticks; his hipbones jutted out fearfully like a broken vessel; his belly clung to his backbone; his ribs could be counted one by one; the joints of his spine could be told apart like the beads of a rosary; his chest was furrowed like the waves of the Ganges; his arms were like dried serpents; his hands hung loose like the slack reins of a horse; his head trembled as if stricken with palsy; his face — once a blossomed lotus — had withered; his eyes had sunk deep into their sockets like the pits of a broken pot; he moved only by the strength of his soul, he stood only by the strength of his soul; he grew weary having spoken, weary while speaking, weary at the very thought "I shall speak." Like a cart of charcoal — and as was said of Skandaka, so here also — like a fire heaped over with ash, he stood resplendent with the lustre of his austerity, glowing more and more with every moment.

This sutra is one of the great body-descriptions of the canonical literature, written with deliberate poetic intensity. Each image is exact: the legs and thighs reduced to sticks; the hipbones thrust out as if a cauldron had cracked from inside; the belly fallen so far inward that it clings to the spine; the ribs no longer covered by flesh and so countable one by one; the spinal vertebrae visible like the beads of a rosary; the chest creased into ridges that look like waves on the Ganges; the arms shrivelled like dried snakes; the hands hanging weightless like a horse's slack reins; the head trembling as in fever; the face — once a blooming lotus — now collapsed inward; the eyes drawn so deep into their sockets that they look like the broken mouths of pots. The list does not stop until the reader has felt the weight of every joint of the body abandoned to spiritual fire. And then, in the same breath, the text turns. Despite all of this, the monk moves and stands. By what? Jīvaṃjīveṇaṃ — "by the strength of soul alone." The body is no longer holding him up; the soul is. He cannot speak without weariness; he cannot even think of speaking without weariness; yet when he does speak, the speech of the soul comes through. The closing image is the most important: the body is described as huyāsaṇa iva bhāsarāsi-palicchaṇṇe — "like a fire covered over with a heap of ash." From the outside, only the ash is visible: the wasted, fragile body. But within that ash the fire is burning hotter than ever — burning karmas in great quantity, burning the very residues of selfhood. To the eye that sees only outwardly, this is a man dying. To the eye that sees inwardly, this is the highest spiritual incandescence the human form can hold. Bodily strength and soul-strength are inversely related in the path of severe austerity; as one falls, the other rises.

The simple version: Dhanya the monk had become so thin from his austerities that you could count every rib, every joint of his spine, and his body trembled like a very sick person — yet he still glowed from within like a bright fire hidden under a heap of ash, more and more radiant with every moment.

In that age, in the city of Rajagriha, at the Gunashilaka sacred grove, there reigned King Shrenika. The Lord Mahavira arrived in holy procession; the assembly came forth, the sermon was given, the assembly returned. Then King Shrenika, having heard the dharma and reflected upon it, bowed before Mahavira and asked: "Reverend Sir! Of these fourteen thousand sramanas headed by Indrabhuti, which monk performs the most difficult austerities, and which monk effects the greatest karmic destruction?" — "Truly so, Shrenika! Of these fourteen thousand sramanas, the venerable monk Dhanya is the one who performs the most difficult austerities and who effects the greatest karmic destruction."

The scene shifts. The text moves from the body of Dhanya to the audience hall of King Shrenika. His question is precise — among the fourteen thousand monks under Mahavira's order, headed by Indrabhuti Gautama himself, which monk excels in both the outward severity of practice and the inward effectiveness of karma-destruction. He wants to know who has reached the rarest of integrations: the body that can bear the most, and the soul that thereby sheds the most. Mahavira's answer is direct, unhesitating, and singular: Dhaṇṇe aṇagāre — "the venerable monk Dhanya." Of fourteen thousand, one name. The Lord does not give the answer with qualification or caveat; he simply names him. This is a startling moment in the canon. The Tirthankara himself — the omniscient teacher whose every word is the seal of liberated knowing — picks out one monk by name and declares him supreme in austerity and karma-destruction. Among fourteen thousand serious renunciates, Dhanya stood alone at the peak. The narrative form is itself a trustworthiness-marker: this is what was said, in that age, by the Lord, in answer to the king. Nothing has been added; nothing has been editorialized.

The simple version: When King Shrenika asked Lord Mahavira "Among your fourteen thousand monks, who has the toughest practice and destroys the most karma?", Mahavira answered with a single name: Dhanya.

"For what reason, Reverend Sir, do you say so?" — "Truly so, Shrenika! In that age there was a city called Kakandi, and the venerable Dhanya was on the upper floor of his finest mansion, immersed in human pleasures. Then I, in due wandering order, going from village to village, came to Kakandi, to the Sahasramravana garden. The assembly came forth — and so on, up to — he was ordained — and so on — and he eats his food disinterestedly, like a serpent that enters its hole. The full description of Dhanya's body, beginning with his feet, is to be repeated here, up to — he stands resplendent ever more and ever more with the lustre and glory of his austerity. For this reason, Shrenika, I declare that of these fourteen thousand sramanas the venerable Dhanya is the greatest performer of difficult austerities and the greatest destroyer of karma."

Shrenika, having heard Mahavira's verdict, asks the natural follow-up: on what ground do you say so? It is the question of a king trained to weigh evidence — not a doubting question, but a respectful request for the reasoning behind the judgment. Mahavira responds by giving the ground: he tells, in compressed form, the entire arc of Dhanya's life from luxury to liberation-bound austerity. The summary moves through every stage — Dhanya in his palace, Mahavira's arrival at Kakandi, the discourse, the ordination, the daily eating "with a soul like a serpent entering its hole." Then the Lord gives the second instruction: the entire body-description from the previous sutra is to be inserted in full, not merely referenced. Mahavira himself, before King Shrenika, recounts the wasted body of Dhanya in every detail — the legs like sticks, the spine like a rosary, the eyes sunken into their sockets, the soul-strength alone keeping him upright, the fire glowing under the ash. That body is the evidence. That body is the proof of the answer. The closure — "for that reason" — completes the syllogism. The most difficult austerity has been performed; the most karma is therefore being destroyed; therefore Dhanya is the highest. What the Lord is teaching King Shrenika here is that spiritual judgments are not made in the abstract. They are made by looking at a life — what was renounced, how completely, with what discipline of body and mind.

The simple version: When the king asked "Why?", Lord Mahavira gave the full reason: he retold Dhanya's whole story — from rich young man in luxury, to ordained monk, to one who eats food without tasting it, to a body so wasted that only his soul holds it up — and then said: that is why he is the greatest.

3.A1.S26

King Shrenika's Veneration of Dhanya

तए णं से सेणिए राया ... हट्ठ तुट्ठे समणं भगवं महावीरं तिक्खुत्तो आयाहिणं पयाहिणं करेइ ... जेणेव धण्णे अणगारे तेणेव उवागच्छइ ... एवं वयासी—
धण्णे सि णं तुम देवाणुप्पिया ! सुपुण्णे सि णं तुम देवाणुप्पिया, सुकयत्थे सि णं तुम देवाणुप्पिया, कयलक्खणे सि णं तुम देवाणुप्पिया, सुलद्धे णं देवाणुप्पिया तव माणुस्सए जम्मजीवियफले त्ति कट्टु वंदइ नमंसइ ... जामेव दिसं पाउब्भूए, तामेव दिसं पडिगए । ॥3.A1.S26॥

Then King Shrenika, having heard and reflected upon this teaching from Mahavira, became delighted and overjoyed; he circumambulated Mahavira three times to the right, bowed and venerated him; then he came to where the venerable monk Dhanya was. Coming there, he circumambulated Dhanya three times to the right, bowed and venerated him, and spoke thus: "Beloved-of-the-gods! Blessed are you, full of merit are you, well-accomplished are you, marked with auspicious signs are you; well-attained, beloved-of-the-gods, is the fruit of your human birth and human life!" — and saying this he again bowed, returned to Mahavira, circumambulated him three times to the right, bowed and venerated him, and then departed in the very direction from which he had come.

tikkhutto āyāhiṇaṃ payāhiṇaṃ karei = circumambulates three times to the right (the auspicious direction, keeping the venerable on one's right shoulder) · dhaṇṇe si ṇaṃ tuma = "blessed are you" — playing on Dhanya's own name · devāṇuppiyā = O beloved-of-the-gods (the standard reverent address) · supuṇṇe si = full of merit are you · sukayatthe si = well-accomplished are you · kayalakkhaṇe si = marked with auspicious signs are you · suladdhe tava māṇussae jammajīviyaphale = well-attained is the fruit of your human birth and human life · jāmeva disaṃ pāubbhūe, tāmeva disaṃ paḍigae = he returned in the very direction from which he had come

This sutra is small in word-count but immense in implication. The reigning king of Magadha — the most powerful man in the eastern subcontinent — having received Mahavira's verdict, walks to the wasted body of a single monk and circles him three times in reverence. He does not send a messenger. He himself, in person, with his own feet, walks the distance, performs the threefold circumambulation, bows, and praises. The five-word salutation he speaks is the high formula of Jain praise. Each clause moves up a level: blessed, full of merit, of accomplished purpose, marked with the visible signs of attainment, and finally — the highest possible compliment — one for whom the entire purpose of being born human has now been grasped and won. In Jain doctrine, the human birth is the rarest of all birth-states because it is the only birth from which liberation can be reached directly. To be reborn human is to receive a chance that even the gods do not have. Therefore jamma-jīvita-phala — "the fruit of human birth, the fruit of being alive" — is the highest possible compliment: it means that the precious opportunity of this human life has been used for what it was given. Most beings squander their human births in pursuit of comfort, status, and pleasure; this one, the king is saying, has not. The detail that the king, after praising Dhanya, returns to Mahavira and bows again before leaving, is significant. The order of veneration is preserved: Tirthankara first and last, monk in the middle. The king does not let his enthusiasm for the disciple displace the reverence due to the master.

The simple version: After hearing Mahavira's words, King Shrenika himself walked over to Dhanya, circled him three times in reverence, and praised him in the highest words: "Blessed are you, your life has not been wasted; you have made the most of being born human."

3.A1.S27

Final Aradhana, Sallekhana, and Ascent to Sarvarthasiddha

तए णं तस्स धण्णस्स अणगारस्स अण्णया कयाइ पुव्वरत्तावरत्तकालसमयंसि धम्मजागरियं जागरमाणस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झत्थिए जाव समुप्पज्जित्था—
एवं खलु अहं इमेणं उरालेणं तवोकम्मेणं किसे धमणिसंतए जाए एवं जहा खंदओ तहेव चिंता । आपुच्छणं । थेरेहिं सद्धिं विउलं पव्वयं दुरुहइ । मासिया संलेहणा । णवमासा परियाओ जाव कालमासे कालं किच्चा उड्ढं चंदिम सूर-गहगण-णक्खत्त-तारारूवाणं जाव णवगेवेज्जे विमाण पत्थडे उड्ढं दूरं वीईवइत्ता सव्वट्टुसिद्धे विमाणे देवत्ताए उववण्णे ।
थेरा तहेव ओयरंति जाव इमे से आयारभंडए । ॥3.A1.S27॥

tae ṇaṃ ... puvvarattāvarattakālasamayaṃsi dhammajāgariyaṃ jāgaramāṇassa ... | jahā khaṃdao taheva ciṃtā | āpucchaṇaṃ | therehiṃ saddhiṃ viulaṃ pavvayaṃ duruhai | māsiyā saṃlehaṇā | ṇavamāsā pariyāo jāva kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā uḍḍhaṃ ... ṇavageveijje vimāṇa patthaḍe uḍḍhaṃ dūraṃ vīīvaittā savvaṭṭhasiddhe vimāṇe devattāe uvavaṇṇe |

Then once, in the late hours of the night, while the venerable monk Dhanya was keeping the dharma-vigil, this kind of inward reflection arose in him: "Indeed I, by this severe austerity, have become so wasted that my veins stand out — and so on, just as Skandaka thought, so he too thought." He took leave of his teachers; with the elder monks he ascended the great Vipula mountain. There he undertook a one-month sallekhana. With nine months of total ascetic tenure, at the time of his final hour he made his end, and rising upward past the moon, the sun, the planet-hosts, the constellations, and the star-forms, past the nine Graiveyaka heaven-tiers, having crossed upward and far, was reborn as a divine being in the Sarvarthasiddha heaven. The elder monks descended in the same manner, saying: "These are his ascetic implements."

puvvarattāvaratta-kāla-samayaṃsi = at the late-night, after-midnight hour · dhamma-jāgariyaṃ = the dharma-vigil (staying awake to reflect on the soul) · jahā khaṃdao taheva ciṃtā = the reflection is the same as Skandaka's (in the Bhagavati Sutra) · āpucchaṇaṃ = he asks permission (of Mahavira) · therehiṃ saddhiṃ viulaṃ pavvayaṃ duruhai = with the elder monks he ascends the great Vipula mountain · māsiyā saṃlehaṇā = a one-month sallekhana (final ascetic fast) · ṇavamāsā pariyāo = nine months of monastic tenure · kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā = at the appointed time, having performed the final act of dying · ṇavageveijje vimāṇa patthaḍe = past the tiers of the nine Graiveyaka heavens · savvaṭṭhasiddhe vimāṇe devattāe uvavaṇṇe = reborn as a divine being in the Sarvarthasiddha celestial mansion · ime se āyārabhaṃḍae = "these are his ascetic implements (robes, alms-bowl, water-pot)"

This is the climactic sutra, the moment toward which the entire chapter has been moving. Every detail of Dhanya's earlier life has been preparing him for this single hour. The setting is the late-night watch — the hour after midnight, when the world is most still. Dhanya is awake, keeping the dharma-jāgariyā — the spiritual vigil in which a monk reviews his own life. In that stillness, an inward reflection arises — the same reflection that arose in the great monk Skandaka in the Bhagavati Sutra: "I have practiced severe austerity; my body has become wasted; my spiritual zeal still burns; my teacher Mahavira is still present; I should make use of every advantage and complete my final practice now." Dhanya rises at dawn, asks Mahavira's permission, and with the elder monks ascends the great Vipula mountain — a known site of ascetic dying near Rajagriha — and undertakes there the one-month sallekhana. The Gujarati vivechan gives the details that the canonical compression hides: he laid out a grass-mat on a black-stone slab, sat in lotus posture, joined his palms, faced east, performed the Namotthuṇaṃ salutation, formally renounced the four kinds of food (solid, liquid, chewed, tasted), and took up pādapopagamana santhāra — the highest grade of sallekhana, the "fallen-tree" posture in which the monk lies motionless. He died in uttama samādhi-maraṇa — the supreme equipoise-death. The result of that death is described in the cosmic vertical. The soul, freed at the moment of dying, rises upward — passing the moon, sun, planets, constellations, the kalpa heavens, the nine Graiveyaka heavens, past the four sub-Anuttara heavens, and at last settling into Sarvarthasiddha-vimāna — "the celestial mansion where every desire is accomplished." Sarvarthasiddha is the highest single realm of embodied existence in the entire Jain cosmos. Its inhabitants take only one further birth — invariably as a human in Mahāvideha — and then attain final liberation. To be reborn there is therefore to be one human birth from the end of all suffering. The closing detail — the elder monks descending with his robes, alms-bowl, and water-pot — is the last earthly note. The body has been left on the mountain; the implements come back.

The simple version: In the late-night quiet, Dhanya realized his body was failing and decided to make this his final practice — he asked Mahavira's permission, climbed the great Vipula mountain with the elder monks, fasted in stillness for one month, and at the end of his nine-month monastic life he died in perfect calm and was reborn in Sarvarthasiddha, the very highest heaven of the Jain cosmos.

3.A1.S28

Gautama's Question and the Prophecy of Mahavideha Liberation

भंते ! त्ति भगवं गोयमे तहेव आपुच्छइ जहा खंदयस्स । भगवं वागरेइ जाव सव्वट्टुसिद्धे विमाणे उववण्णे ।
धण्णस्स णं भंते ! देवस्स केवइयं कालं ठिई पण्णत्ता ?
गोयमा ! तेत्तीसं सागरोवमाइं ठिई पण्णत्ता ।
से णं भंते ! ताओ देवलोगाओ कहिं गच्छिहिइ ? कहिं उववज्जिहिइ ?
गोयमा ! महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहिइ । ॥3.A1.S28॥

bhaṃte ! tti bhagavaṃ goyame taheva āpucchai jahā khaṃdayassa | ... dhaṇṇassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte ! devassa kevaiyaṃ kālaṃ ṭhiī paṇṇattā ? goyamā ! tettīsaṃ sāgarovamāiṃ ṭhiī paṇṇattā | ... goyamā ! mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihii |

"Reverend Sir!" — saying this, the venerable Gautama asked just as he had asked about Skandaka, and the Lord answered, up to "Dhanya was reborn in the Sarvarthasiddha celestial mansion." — "Reverend Sir! For how long is the lifespan of the divine Dhanya declared to be?" — "Gautama! His lifespan is declared to be thirty-three sāgaropamas." — "Reverend Sir! When that god falls from that heavenly realm, where will he go? Where will he be reborn?" — "Gautama! In the region of Mahavideha he will attain liberation."

bhagavaṃ goyame = the venerable Gautama (Indrabhuti, the chief disciple) · taheva āpucchai jahā khaṃdayassa = he asks just as he had asked concerning Skandaka · dhaṇṇassa devassa = of the divine Dhanya (now a god) · tettīsaṃ sāgarovamāiṃ ṭhiī = lifespan of thirty-three sāgaropamas (the maximum lifespan possible in any embodied state) · tāo devalogāo = from that heavenly realm · mahāvidehe vāse = in the Mahavideha region (where Tirthankaras are eternally present) · sijjhihii = he will attain liberation, become a Siddha (future definite — sealed)

This sutra completes the spiritual arc of Dhanya's soul. Gautama Swami — Mahavira's chief disciple — steps forward as the questioner. Three pieces of information are given: the station (Sarvarthasiddha), the duration (thirty-three sāgaropamas), and the destination (Mahavideha, where liberation will occur). The duration is the maximum lifespan possible in any embodied state in the entire Jain cosmos. Sāgaropama literally means "ocean-comparable," and it is an unimaginably long time-unit. When that span ends and his celestial body falls away, his soul will be reborn — by the doctrine of Sarvarthasiddha — only and exactly in Mahāvideha. Mahavideha is unique because Tirthankaras are eternally present there: in any moment of cosmic time, somewhere in the eight regions of Mahavideha, a Tirthankara is teaching. This means that a soul born there has uninterrupted access to the highest teaching, and therefore has every condition for liberation. Souls reborn from Sarvarthasiddha are by definition ripe; they will hear the teaching, undertake the practice, and reach liberation in that very Mahavideha birth. The grammatical force of sijjhihii is worth noting. It is in the future definite — not "may attain" or "is likely to attain," but he will. The Tirthankara's word here is a prophecy backed by direct knowing. There is no probabilistic margin; Dhanya's liberation is sealed. This is the doctrinal payoff of the entire chapter. From the very beginning, every detail has been pointing here: He will be liberated.

The simple version: Gautama asked: "How long will Dhanya live as a god, and what will happen after?" — and Mahavira answered: "He will live as a god in Sarvarthasiddha for thirty-three sāgaropamas, and after that he will be born once more as a human in the region of Mahavideha — where he will attain final liberation."

The historical setting reveals an important detail: Sunakshatra is also a son of Bhadra Sarthavahi. The text names her by her exact title — bhaddā ṇāmaṃ satthavāhī — using the same words used of Dhanya's mother. This means that Dhanya and Sunakshatra are biological brothers, sons of the same caravan-leader's wife in Kakandi. The Gujarati commentary makes this explicit: "Sunakshatra was the son of mother Bhadra and the (real) brother of the venerable monk Dhanya." Two sons of the same mother thus walked the same path of renunciation under the same Tirthankara, both ascending to the same supreme heaven. The compression is dense. "As with Dhanya, so" folds the upbringing by five wet-nurses, the marriage to thirty-two princesses, the awakening at Mahavira's discourse, all into Dhanya's template. "The renunciation is as that of Thavachhaputra" uses a different template for the actual ordination ceremony — Thavachhaputra's rite is recorded in the fifth chapter of the Jnatadharmakatha. The redactor's instruction is precise: for upbringing and marriage, use Dhanya's account; for the renunciation rite, use Thavachhaputra's. The deepest lesson is that the path is replicable. What Dhanya did, his real brother Sunakshatra also did, in the same household, in the same generation. The compiler does not give us a different story because, at the level of soul, the story is the same. Once a being has aligned with right vision, knowledge, and conduct, the road is one road — only the name on the cushion changes.

The simple version: In Kakandi city, mother Bhadra had another son — Sunakshatra, Dhanya's real brother — who lived the same wealthy life, heard Mahavira's same discourse, took the same renunciation, kept the same disciplines, ate his food without tasting it, studied the same scriptures, and brought his body to the same wasted, radiant condition.

Sunakshatra's Sallekhana, Ascent to Sarvarthasiddha, and Future Liberation

This sutra closes the entire arc of Sunakshatra Kumar in a single dense passage. The redactor uses every device of canonical compression: the setting in single-word notations; the assembly's coming and going in the bare formula; the night-vigil reflection by reference to Skandaka; the cosmic ascent in a single jāva; the future liberation sealed in three words. The single individuating note is the length of the ascetic tenure: bahu vāsā — "many years." Where Dhanya's tenure was nine months, Sunakshatra's was many years. The destination, however, is unambiguous. Sunakshatra reaches Sarvarthasiddha — the same supreme heaven as his elder brother. Both sons of Bhadra Sarthavahi end in the same celestial mansion, with the same lifespan, and the same prophecy of liberation in Mahavideha. It identifies this as the canonical hour of dharma-vigil for two reasons: first, because at midnight the external environment is at its most still, the mind becomes one-pointed, and meditation is most effective; second, because in the Daśavaikālika Sutra's second chūlikā, the pūrvabhāga and paścimabhāga of the night are specifically prescribed for the seeker as moments of dharma-jāgariyā. The hour of midnight is the hour of the soul's clearest seeing, and the canon places its great spiritual decisions there for that reason.

The simple version: After many years as a monk, Sunakshatra — like his brother Dhanya — had his decisive inward awakening in the late-night hour, undertook a final fast, died in equanimity, and was reborn in the highest heaven Sarvarthasiddha for thirty-three vast cosmic eras, after which he too will attain final liberation in the Mahavideha region.

Parishesha — Closing Colophon on the Structure of the Scripture

।। वर्ग-३ / ३ थी १० संपूर्ण ।।
।। अणुत्तरोववाइय सूत्र संपूर्ण ।। ॥3.A3-10.S3॥

aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ ego suyakkhaṃdho | tiṇṇi vaggā | tisu ceva divasesu uddisijjaṃti | tattha paḍhame vagge dasa uddesagā | biie vagge terasa uddesagā | taie vagge dasa uddesagā | tao suyakhaṃdho samuddisai, tao aṇuṇṇavijjai, dosu divasesu |

The Anuttaropapātika Daśā has one śruta-skandha (book-section). It has three vargas. The teaching is delivered in three days. The first Varga has ten uddeśas (recitation-units, lessons). The second has thirteen. The third has ten. After this, the entire śruta-skandha is comprehensively recited, and then permission is given (to the student to teach the next scripture); these last two acts are performed across two days.

ego suyakkhaṃdho = (it has) one śruta-skandha (composite book-section) · tiṇṇi vaggā = three vargas · tisu ceva divasesu uddisijjaṃti = (the three Vargas) are taught/recited across three days · paḍhame vagge dasa uddesagā = first Varga has ten uddeśas · biie vagge terasa = second has thirteen · taie vagge dasa = third has ten · tao suyakhaṃdho samuddisai = then the entire śruta-skandha is comprehensively recited (the samuddeśa — full recitation-review) · tao aṇuṇṇavijjai = then permission is given (the anujñā — formal authorization passing from teacher to student) · dosu divasesu = (these two activities take place) on two further days

This sutra is the parishesha — the structural colophon. It is not narrative; it is a teaching-protocol. The numbers given here add up. The Anuttaropapātika Daśā is one śruta-skandha — one unified scripture. Within it are three vargas. Each varga is taught on a single day. Within each day's varga, the material is broken into uddeśas. Varga 1 has ten (ten princes — Jali through Abhaya); Varga 2 has thirteen (thirteen princes — Dirghasena through Punyasena); Varga 3 has ten (ten monks of Kakandi and other cities — Dhanya through Vehalla). The sum is thirty-three uddeśas — the canonical count of thirty-three souls. After the three teaching-days, two more days are added. On the fourth day, the samuddeśa — the comprehensive recitation-review — is performed. On the fifth day, the anujñā — the formal authorization — is given: the teacher places his hand on the student's head and authorizes him to teach this scripture or to move on to the next. The five-day teaching-protocol is itself a discipline. It tells us that this scripture was meant to be received in three full sessions of focused listening, then reviewed in one full session, then sealed by the teacher's authorization. The reader who reads this English translation is, in effect, telescoping into a moment what the tradition asked to be unfolded across five days. The parishesha confirms beyond any further doubt the structure of the scripture: ten + thirteen + ten = thirty-three. Every soul accounted for; every Varga's unit count attested. The Gujarati editor closes with a beautiful summary line: "It must be studied with full attention to receive complete knowledge of this matter; for in this Anga the seeker hears Lord Mahavira's discourse, the arising of detachment, the festival of ordination, the supreme grade of austerity, the wasting of the body, the dharma-vigil, the spirit of the final fast, ascent to Sarvarthasiddha, future birth in Mahavideha, the attainment of Siddhi — all these subjects are taught in compressed form."

The simple version: This entire scripture has one book, three parts, and thirty-three lessons in total — taught traditionally over three days, then reviewed on a fourth, with the teacher's blessing given on the fifth — and now the whole scripture, all thirty-three lives of those who reached the highest heavens, has been fully transmitted.
