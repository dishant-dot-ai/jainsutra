The First Decad (પ્રથમ વર્ગ)

Jali Kumar's story is told in full; Adhyayanas 2–10 are extended by reference (atidesha) to nine other princes of Rajagriha.

Imparted ByLord Mahavira
Compiled BySudharma Swami
Translated ByDishant Shah

गोयमा ! महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहिइ ॥

"O Gautama! He will be born in the region of Mahavideha — and there he will attain final liberation." — Anuttarovavai 1.A1.S5

About This Chapter

First Varga of the 9th Anga

The First Varga of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā narrates the lives of ten princes of Rajagriha — sons of King Shrenika and his queens Dharini, Chellana, and Nanda. Under Lord Mahavira's teaching, each prince renounces palace, wealth, and family; observes the full discipline of monkhood — for some sixteen years, for others twelve, for others five — and at the proper hour of death ascends, by way of sallekhana upon the holy mountain Vipulagiri, into one of the five highest heavens: Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita, or Sarvarthasiddhi. From those anuttara vimānas — heavens beyond which nothing is higher — they will take a single final human birth in Mahavideha and there attain liberation.

The structural shape of this Varga is itself a teaching. Only the life of Jali Kumar — the eldest of the ten — is told in full detail. The remaining nine are presented through atidesha, a canonical compression in which the scripture says only "as in Jali's case, except…" and lists the differences. The reader is expected to lay Jali's complete narrative as the template over each subsequent prince. This is not an editorial shortcut — it is a deliberate device by which the canon honors the individuality of each soul while celebrating their common direction: from kingdom to renunciation, from outer abundance to inner discipline, from human birth to the highest heavens, and finally to liberation itself.

Adhyayana 1 — Jali Kumar
1.A1.S1

Utkshepa — The Opening Question

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं रायगिहे णयरे । अज्ज सुहम्मस्स समोसरणं । परिसा णिग्गया जाव जंबू पज्जुवासमाणे एवं वयासी— जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अट्ठमस्स अंगस्स अंतगडदसाणं अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते, णवमस्स णं भंते ! अंगस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ? ॥1.A1.S1॥

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ rāyagihe ṇayare | ajja suhammassa samosaraṇaṃ | parisā ṇiggayā jāva jambū pajjuvāsamāṇe evaṃ vayāsī— jai ṇaṃ bhante ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṭṭhamassa aṃgassa aṃtagaḍadasāṇaṃ ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte, ṇavamassa ṇaṃ bhante ! aṃgassa aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte ? || 1.A1.S1 ||

In that age, at that time, in the city of Rajagriha, the venerable Sudharma Swami arrived; the assembly came forth to hear the teaching, and after hearing it returned; then Jambu Swami, attending upon his teacher with devotion, spoke thus: "O Lord! If the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, who has attained the highest goal, has expounded the meaning of the eighth Anga — the Antagada Dasha — in this manner, then, O Lord, what meaning has the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, the attainer of liberation, expounded for the ninth Anga, the Anuttaropapātika Daśā?"

This opening sutra establishes the entire frame of the scripture. The setting is Rajagriha, the great capital of Magadha — the same city where Mahavira gave countless discourses and where his disciples continued to gather long after his liberation. The teacher here is not Mahavira himself but his chief disciple, Sudharma Swami, who carries the unbroken oral transmission. The student who asks is Jambu Swami — the last person in our current cosmic age to attain final liberation. The exchange between Sudharma and Jambu is the structural backbone of all eleven Angas of the canon. Jambu's question follows a precise logic. Having just heard the eighth Anga (the Antagada Dasha), which records ninety souls who ended all karma in that very life and crossed into final liberation, he now asks the natural next question: what does the ninth Anga teach? The answer, which this entire scripture unfolds, is that the ninth Anga records a different but equally luminous category of souls — those whose discipline was supremely pure, but whose remaining karma did not allow liberation in that birth. Such souls do not fall back into ordinary cycles of rebirth; they ascend directly to the highest five heavens, called anuttara — "beyond which nothing is higher" — and from there take one final human birth before reaching liberation. The word samosaraṇaṃ deserves note: strictly it refers to the divine pavilion that arises around an enlightened teacher, but here it indicates the holy gathering itself — the moment a teacher arrives and the community assembles. The repeated jāva ("and so on") is the canonical compression marker; scribes used it to point readers toward stock passages they would already know, keeping the sacred text concise without losing meaning. The phrase saṃpatteṇaṃ, "the attainer," is technical — it specifically denotes Mahavira as the one who has reached final liberation, distinguishing him from a still-living teacher. A historical note: a manuscript variant exists which mentions King Shrenika in this opening section. The compilers of this Gujarati edition rejected that variant, because Shrenika died while Mahavira was still alive, and Jambu Swami was ordained only after Mahavira's liberation — so the two cannot have shared a scene. The accepted reading therefore omits Shrenika here.

1.A1.S2

Three Vargas of the Ninth Anga

तए णं से सुहम्मे अणगारे जंबू अणगारं एवं वयासी— एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं णवमस्स अंगस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं तिण्णि वग्गा पण्णत्ता ।

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं णवमस्स अंगस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं तओ वग्गा पण्णत्ता, पढमस्स णं भंते ! वग्गस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं कइ अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता ?

एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं पढमस्स वग्गस्स दस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता, तं जहा— जालि-मयालि-उवयाली, पुरिससेणे य वारिसेणे य । दीहदंते य लट्टदंते य, वेहल्ले वेहायसे अभए इ य कुमारे ।।

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं पढमस्स वग्गस्स दस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता, पढमस्स णं भंते ! अज्झयणस्स समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ? ॥1.A1.S2॥

tae ṇaṃ se suhamme aṇagāre jambū aṇagāraṃ evaṃ vayāsī— evaṃ khalu jambū ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ ṇavamassa aṃgassa aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ tiṇṇi vaggā paṇṇattā | … paḍhamassa vaggassa dasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā, taṃ jahā— jāli-mayāli-uvayālī, purisaseṇe ya vāriseṇe ya | dīhadaṃte ya laṭṭadaṃte ya, vehalle vehāyase abhae i ya kumāre || … paḍhamassa ajjhayaṇassa … ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte ? || 1.A1.S2 ||

Then the venerable monk Sudharma spoke thus to the monk Jambu: "Truly, O Jambu, the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, the attainer of liberation, has expounded the ninth Anga — the Anuttaropapātika Daśā — in three sections." Jambu asked: "If, O Lord, the Ascetic Lord Mahavira has expounded three sections, then how many chapters has he expounded for the first section?" Sudharma answered: "Truly, O Jambu, in the first section the Ascetic Lord Mahavira has expounded ten chapters, namely — Jali, Mayali, Upajali, Purushasena and Varishena; Dirghadanta, Lashtadanta, Vehalla, Vehayasa, and Abhaya — these ten princes." Then Jambu asked: "If, O Lord, the first section has ten chapters, then what meaning has the Ascetic Lord Mahavira expounded for the first chapter?"

This sutra gives the architectural map of the entire scripture. The ninth Anga is divided into three sections (vargas). The first contains ten chapters covering ten princes; the next two sections (covered later in the text) contain the remaining twenty-three souls, bringing the total to thirty-three. This is why the scripture is named daśā — "the decads" — though its full count is thirty-three souls across three groupings. The list of ten princes given here is one of the few rhythmic verses (gāthā) in an otherwise prose canon. The compilers preserved this little mnemonic verse so that students could memorize all ten names at a single hearing. The names themselves carry meaning: Dirghadanta means "the long-toothed one," Lashtadanta means "the smooth-toothed one" (perhaps a reference to physical features used to distinguish them), and Abhaya means "the fearless one" — a name traditionally given to one of King Shrenika's most beloved sons, famed for his wisdom in worldly affairs before his renunciation. The dialogue form here is not decorative. In Jain tradition, sacred knowledge is always transmitted teacher-to-student, never as a monologue. Each question Jambu asks is the natural next question of a serious seeker who refuses to skip any layer. Sudharma's answers are equally measured — he tells Jambu the structure first, then the contents of each part, and only then the meaning of the first chapter. This pattern reflects the Jain conviction that depth of understanding requires orderly disclosure: first the whole, then the parts, then the substance. The repeated phrase "the attainer of liberation" applied to Mahavira is not flourish; it is theological precision. It distinguishes Mahavira's words as those of a liberated being — words that carry the authority of direct seeing, not speculation. Every time the phrase appears, it functions as a seal of authenticity attached to the teaching.

1.A1.S3

The Royal Life of Jali Kumar

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं रायगिहे णयरे, रिद्धित्थिमिय समिद्धे । गुणसिलए चेइए । सेणिए राया, धारिणी देवी । सीहो सुमिणे । जालीकुमारो । जहा मेहो अट्ठट्ठओ दाओ जाव अण्णं च विपुलं धण कणग-रयण-मणिमोत्तिय-संख-सिलप्पवाल-रत्तरयण-संतसार-सावज्ज अलाहि जाव आसत्तमाओ कुलवंसाओ पकामं दाउं, पकामं भोत्तुं, पकामं परिभाएडं ।

तए णं से जालीकुमारे एगमेगाए भारियाए एगमेगं हिरण्णकोडिं दलयइ, एगमेगं सुवण्णकोडिं दलयइ, जाव एगमेगं पेसणकारिं दलयइ, अण्णं च विउलं धणकणग जाव परिभाएडं दलयइ ।

तए णं जाली कुमारे उप्पिं पासाय वरगए जाव माणुस्सए कामभोगे पच्चणुभवमाणे विहरइ । ॥1.A1.S3॥

evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ rāyagihe ṇayare, riddhitthimiya samiddhe | guṇasilae ceie | seṇie rāyā, dhāriṇī devī | sīho sumiṇe | jālīkumāro | jahā meho aṭṭhaṭṭhao dāo jāva … āsattamāo kulavaṃsāo pakāmaṃ dāuṃ, pakāmaṃ bhottuṃ, pakāmaṃ paribhāeḍuṃ | … jālī kumāre uppiṃ pāsāya varagae jāva māṇussae kāmabhoge paccaṇubhavamāṇe viharai | || 1.A1.S3 ||

"Truly so, O Jambu! In that age, at that time, there was the city of Rajagriha — prosperous, stable, and rich in every good thing. There was the sacred grove called Gunashila. The king was Shrenika; the queen was Dharini. The queen saw a lion in her dream. A son was born — Jali Kumar. Just as in the case of Megha Kumar, the eight gifts of eight kinds were given — abundant wealth, gold, gems, jewels, pearls, conch, coral, ruby, the very best treasure, blameless and lawful — sufficient for him to give freely, to enjoy freely, and to share freely down to the seventh generation of his lineage. Then Jali Kumar gave to each of his wives one crore of silver coins, one crore of gold coins, and so on, even one personal attendant each, along with abundant wealth, gold, and the rest, for them to share freely. Thereafter Jali Kumar dwelled in his fine high palace, savoring the human pleasures of the senses."

This sutra paints the picture of Jali Kumar's worldly life in compressed but extraordinarily rich strokes. Rajagriha was the political and spiritual capital of Magadha — King Shrenika's seat, and the city where Mahavira gave many of his most important discourses. It is described as riddhi-stimita-samriddha, three precise terms: prosperous in resources, stable in governance, and abundant in every kind of well-being. The grove Gunashila on its outskirts was a sacred site frequently mentioned in Jain canonical literature. Queen Dharini's dream of a lion is one of the canonical signs that signals the conception of an extraordinary soul. In Jain tradition, the lion symbolizes fearless royal nobility and spiritual sovereignty — exactly the qualities Jali Kumar will later display in renouncing the world. The sutra deliberately invokes the parallel of Megha Kumar — another prince of Rajagriha whose detailed life-story is recorded in the Jnata Dharma Katha (the sixth Anga) — instructing the reader to fill in the details from that parallel narrative. This canonical cross-referencing is a sophisticated literary device: rather than repeat hundreds of lines, the scripture says "as in Megha's case" and trusts the reader's familiarity. Megha's story includes a five-mother system of upbringing (wet nurses for feeding, bathing, dressing, playing, and lap-holding), education in seventy-two arts, marriage to eight princesses, and a vast dowry — all of which apply to Jali as well. The eightfold-eight gifts (aṭṭhaṭṭhao dāo) is a wedding-gift formula: eight categories of wealth, eight crores in each. The Gujarati commentary clarifies that according to the parallel passage in the Jnata Dharma Katha, this dowry came from the brides' families, while the groom's father gave additional wealth. Whatever the precise mechanics, the point is unmistakable: Jali Kumar received wealth so vast it would not run out for seven generations. The phrase "give freely, enjoy freely, share freely" (pakāmaṃ dāuṃ, bhottuṃ, paribhāeḍuṃ) is a triple formula meaning the wealth was sufficient for three legitimate uses — charitable giving, personal enjoyment, and distribution among kin. Note also the ethical adjective sāvajja — "lawfully obtained, blameless." Even amid this overwhelming description of luxury, the scripture insists that the wealth was righteous wealth, not gained through violence, fraud, or oppression. This detail matters: the scripture is not glorifying riches — it is showing what Jali had, so that what he gave up will land with full weight. The closing line — Jali "experiencing fully the human pleasures of the senses" — is doubly important. First, it is honest: Jali was not a saint from infancy; he lived a complete princely life. Second, the verb paccaṇubhavamāṇa ("experiencing fully") foreshadows the exhaustion that will come. Sensory pleasure, taken to its fullness, eventually reveals its emptiness — and that revelation, in the next sutra, becomes the seed of his renunciation.

1.A1.S4

Jali's Renunciation and Spiritual Discipline

सामी समोसढे । सेणिओ णिग्गओ । जहा मेहो तहा जाली वि णिग्गओ, तहेव णिक्खंतो जहा मेहो । एक्कारस अंगाइं अहिज्जइ ।

गुणरयणं तवोकम्मं जहा खंदगस्स । एवं जा चेव खंदगस्स वत्तव्वया, सा चेव चिंतणा, आपुच्छणा, थेरेहिं सद्धिं विउलं पव्वयं तहेव दुरूहइ । णवरं सोलस वासाइं सामण्ण परियागं पाउणित्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा उड्ढं चंदिमसूर जाव णवगेवेज्जविमाणपत्थडे उड्ढं दूरं वीईवित्ता विजय विमाणे देवत्ताए उववण्णे ।

तए णं थेरा भगवंतो जालि अणगारं कालगयं जाणित्ता परिणिव्वाणवत्तियं काउस्सग्गं करेंति, करित्ता पत्तचीवराइं गेण्हंति तहेव उत्तरंति जेणेव समणे भगवं महावीरे तेणेव उवागच्छंति, समणं भगवं महावीरं वंदंति णमंसंति, वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता एवं वयासी— एवं खलु देवाणुप्पियाणं अंतेवासी जाली णामं अणगारे पगइभद्दए पगइविणीए पगइउवसंते पगइपयणुकोहमाण-माया-लोभे मिउमद्दवसंपण्णे अल्लीणे भद्दए विणीए । से णं देवाणुप्पिएहिं अब्भणुण्णाए समाणे सयमेव पंच महव्वयाइं आरोवित्ता, समणा य समणीओ य खामेत्ता, अम्हेहिं सद्धिं विउलं पव्वयं दुरूहइ, तं चेव णिरवसेसं जाव इमे य से आयारभंडए ।

भंते ! ति भगवं गोयमे समणं भगवं महावीरं वंदइ णमंसइ, वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता एवं वयासी— ॥1.A1.S4॥

sāmī samosaḍhe | seṇio ṇiggao | jahā meho tahā jālī vi ṇiggao, taheva ṇikkhaṃto jahā meho | ekkārasa aṃgāiṃ ahijjai | guṇarayaṇaṃ tavokammaṃ jahā khaṃdagassa | … solasa vāsāiṃ sāmaṇṇa pariyāgaṃ pāuṇittā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā … vijaya vimāṇe devattāe uvavaṇṇe | … pagaibhaddae pagaiviṇīe pagaiuvasaṃte pagaipayaṇukohamāṇa-māyā-lobhe miumaddavasaṃpaṇṇe allīṇe bhaddae viṇīe | … bhante ! ti bhagavaṃ goyame samaṇaṃ bhagavaṃ mahāvīraṃ vaṃdai ṇamaṃsai, vaṃdittā ṇamaṃsittā evaṃ vayāsī— || 1.A1.S4 ||

The Lord arrived. King Shrenika went forth to meet him. Just as Megha had gone forth, so did Jali; just as Megha had renounced, so did Jali — and he studied the eleven Angas. He performed the great penance called Gunaratna, just like the monk Skandaka. Whatever was said of Skandaka — the same reflection, the same final asking-of-leave from the teachers, the same ascending of the great Vipulagiri mountain together with the elders — applies here too. The only distinction is this: having lived sixteen years as a monk, when his time of death came, he passed beyond the realm of moon and sun, beyond the heavenly bodies, beyond even the platform of the Nine Graiveyaka heavens, far upward, and was reborn as a celestial in the Vijaya Vimana — the highest heaven of the Anuttara group. Then the venerable elder monks, knowing that the monk Jali had passed away, performed the standing meditation of final liberation; having done so, they took up his bowl and robes and descended (from the mountain) and went to where the Ascetic Lord Mahavira was; bowing and saluting Lord Mahavira, they said: "O Most Beloved of the gods! Your disciple, the monk named Jali — who was by nature gentle, by nature humble, by nature serene; whose anger, pride, deceit, and greed were by nature thinned almost to nothing; who was endowed with softness and yielding-grace; who was self-restrained, kind, and disciplined — that monk, with your permission, himself accepted the five great vows, asked forgiveness of all monks and nuns, and ascended the great mountain with us; everything else as told above; and these are his alms-bowl and robes." Then the venerable Gautama Swami, saying "O Lord!", bowed to the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, saluted him, and spoke thus: —

This is the heart of Jali Kumar's story — his renunciation, his austerities, and his rebirth in the highest heavens. The narrative is again told by canonical reference: "just as Megha Kumar" supplies the renunciation scene, and "just as the monk Skandaka" supplies the penance and death-meditation. To unfold these references: when Mahavira arrived at Gunashila grove, Jali (like Megha before him) heard the discourse, was moved to renounce, sought his parents' permission, gave away all his wealth, and accepted ordination from Mahavira's own hand. The eleven Angas are the eleven canonical limbs of Jain scripture — the entire body of teaching that Mahavira passed to his chief disciples. To say Jali studied all eleven is to say he received the full training of a serious monk. The Gunaratna austerity — literally "the jewel of virtues" — is a specific advanced penance involving a graduated sequence of fasts paired with intense meditation. Skandaka's story in the Bhagavati Sutra describes this penance in detail and serves as the canonical model for it. The death-meditation sequence is the spiritual climax. Sallekhana — the Jain practice of voluntary, mindful preparation for death through gradual fasting and inner reflection — is described step by step in Skandaka's story: the monk reflects on the right time, asks formal permission of his teachers, takes the elder monks as his companions, climbs the holy mountain Vipulagiri (the great peak near Rajagriha), and on its heights remains in meditation until the body releases the soul. Jali followed this same path. After sixteen years of monastic discipline, his body fell away on Vipulagiri, and the soul rose — past moon and sun, past the celestial bodies, past even the Nine Graiveyaka heavens — and entered the Vijaya Vimana. The Vijaya Vimana is the first of the five anuttara heavens — the highest dwellings short of final liberation. To be born here is to have advanced almost to the threshold of moksha; only one or two more births stand between such a soul and complete freedom. The very name of this scripture — Anuttaropapātika — means "those who attain birth in the highest heavens." Jali is the first soul named in this entire scripture, and his is the model story. The portrait of Jali's character given by the elder monks to Mahavira is remarkable. Notice the repeated word pagai — "by nature." His gentleness was not performed; it was native. His anger, pride, deceit, and greed — the four passions Jain thought identifies as the root causes of bondage — were already by nature thinned to almost nothing before he even became a monk. This detail is theologically important: Jali's high rebirth was not the accident of one fortunate life. It was the ripening of many lifetimes of inner work. His final birth was simply the harvest of long preparation. The act of asking forgiveness of all monks and nuns before death is a central Jain death-practice. Liberation requires absolute clarity in the soul; any unresolved enmity is a thread that ties one back to the world. By formally asking forgiveness from every member of the community, Jali released every karmic thread he could release. The handing over of his bowl and robes (āyāra-bhaṃḍa — "the tools of right conduct") to the elder monks is the final ritual transfer: the body's instruments of practice return to the community while the soul ascends. The closing image — Gautama Swami bowing to Mahavira to ask the next question about Jali's destination — sets up the beautifully tender exchange of the next sutra.

1.A1.S5

Jali's Future and the Path to Liberation

एवं खलु देवाणुप्पियाणं अंतेवासी जाली णामं अणगारे पगइभद्दए जाव विणीए, से णं जाली अणगारे कालगए कहिं गए, कहिं उववण्णे ?

एवं खलु गोयमा ! मम अंतेवासी जाली णामं अणगारे पगइभद्दए जाव विणीए मम अब्भणुण्णाए समाणे सयमेव पंच महव्वयाइं आरुहेत्ता, तं चेव सव्वं अवसेसियं णेयव्वं, जाव कालगए उड्ढं चंदिम सूर- गहगण-णक्खत्त- तारारूवाणं बहूइं जोयणाइं, बहूइं जोयणसयाइं, बहूइं जोयणसहस्साइं, बहूइं जोयणसयसहस्साइं, बहूइं जोयणकोडीओ, बहूइं जोयणकोडाकोडीओ उड्ढं दूरं उप्पित्ता सोहम्मीसाणसणंकुमार माहिंदब्भलंतगमहासुक्कसहस्साराणं- पाणयारणच्चुए तिण्णि य अट्ठारसुत्तरे गेविज्ज विमाणावाससए वीईवित्ता विजय महाविमाणे देवत्ताए उववण्णे ।

जालिस्स णं भंते ! देवस्स केवइयं कालं ठिई पण्णत्ता ?

गोयमा ! बत्तीसं सागरोवमाइं ठिई पण्णत्ता ।

से णं भंते ! ताओ देवलोयाओ आउक्खएणं, भवक्खएणं, ठिइक्खएणं कहिं गच्छिहिइ, कहिं उववज्जिहिइ ?

गोयमा ! महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहिइ । ॥1.A1.S5॥

evaṃ khalu devāṇuppiyāṇaṃ aṃtevāsī jālī ṇāmaṃ aṇagāre pagaibhaddae jāva viṇīe, se ṇaṃ jālī aṇagāre kālagae kahiṃ gae, kahiṃ uvavaṇṇe ? … goyamā ! battīsaṃ sāgarovamāiṃ ṭhiī paṇṇattā | … goyamā ! mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihii | || 1.A1.S5 ||

(Gautama asked:) "O Most Beloved of the gods! Your disciple, the monk named Jali — who was by nature gentle, and so on, disciplined — that monk Jali, having died, where has he gone? Where has he been reborn?" (Mahavira answered:) "Truly so, O Gautama! My disciple, the monk named Jali — who was by nature gentle and disciplined — with my permission himself took on the five great vows; everything else as told before; and at the time of his death, rising upward past the moon, the sun, the host of planets, the constellations, and the forms of the stars — past many yojanas, many hundreds of yojanas, many thousands, many hundred-thousands, many crores, many crores-of-crores of yojanas — far far upward — past the heavens of Saudharma, Ishana, Sanatkumara, Mahendra, Brahmaloka, Lantaka, Mahashukra, Sahasrara, Anata, Pranata, Arana, and Achyuta, and past the three-hundred-and-eighteen heavenly dwellings of the Graiveyakas, he has been reborn as a celestial being in the great Vijaya Vimana." (Gautama asked:) "O Lord! How long is the lifespan of the celestial being Jali?" (Mahavira:) "O Gautama! It has been declared as thirty-two sagaropama." (Gautama:) "O Lord! When his life there is exhausted, when that mode of being is exhausted, when his celestial duration is exhausted, where will he go? Where will he be reborn?" (Mahavira:) "O Gautama! He will be born in the region of Mahavideha — and there he will attain final liberation."

This sutra delivers the spiritual payload of the Anuttaropapātika scripture in its most concentrated form. Gautama Swami — Mahavira's beloved chief disciple — asks the only question that ultimately matters: where has this soul gone? Mahavira's answer is a journey across the Jain cosmos. The Jain universe is hierarchically structured. Above the human realm rise the celestial heavens, in tiers of increasing subtlety. The first twelve are the Kalpa heavens — Saudharma through Achyuta — where divine beings still experience pleasure-bound existence, however refined. Above these rise the nine Graiveyaka heavens (the "neck" heavens, so called because they are imagined as adorning the neck of the cosmic person), housed in three hundred and eighteen specific celestial dwellings. Above even these are the five anuttara heavens — Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita, and Sarvarthasiddhi — the supreme dwellings, beyond which only liberation itself remains. Jali's soul rose past every layer below — past the Kalpa heavens, past the Graiveyaka heavens, all the way up to the Vijaya Vimana, the first of the five highest heavens. The cumulative listing of distances — yojanas, hundreds, thousands, hundred-thousands, crores, crores-of-crores — is not literal arithmetic but a poetic device to communicate the unimaginable scale of the journey. The mind cannot grasp it; the language reaches for the upper edge of language and stops there. Jali's lifespan in this heaven is thirty-two sagaropama. The sagaropama ("ocean-comparable") is one of the largest units of time in Jain cosmology — a single one is unimaginably long, computed by analogies involving filling and emptying vast oceans grain by grain. Thirty-two of these is the standard duration assigned to the Vijaya heaven by Jain cosmography. Then Gautama asks the question that closes the arc: when this celestial life ends, where next? Mahavira's answer is brief and luminous: Mahavideha — and there he will attain liberation. Mahavideha is the central continent in Jain geography where, unlike our continent, the conditions for full liberation are continuously available. There, in his next human birth, Jali's soul will end the cycle once and for all. The spiritual logic is now complete. Jali was supremely disciplined, but the karmic momentum of his soul was not yet exhausted enough for liberation in his immediate human life. So he was given the highest possible non-final destination — the Vijaya Vimana — where he experiences the most refined heavenly bliss for thirty-two oceans of time, and from there a single human birth in Mahavideha will complete his journey. This is what anuttara — "beyond which nothing is higher" — means in lived form: the highest stop short of the goal, with the goal now within one breath's reach. The sutra is also a teaching about the relationship between conduct and outcome. Pure conduct does not always immediately produce liberation — but it never fails to bring the soul to the closest possible position to liberation. There is no wasted discipline. There is no spiritual effort that is forgotten by the universe.

Nikshepa — Closing of Adhyayana 1

एवं खलु जंबू समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं पढमस्स वग्गस्स पढमस्स अज्झयणस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते । ॥1.A1.S6॥

evaṃ khalu jambū samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ paḍhamassa vaggassa paḍhamassa ajjhayaṇassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte | || 1.A1.S6 ||

"Truly so, O Jambu! This is the meaning the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, the attainer of liberation, has expounded for the first chapter of the first section of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā."

This brief closing — called the nikṣepa in canonical analysis — is the formal seal that closes a chapter and matches the utkṣepa (the formal opening) of Sutra 1. The structure is mirrored: the chapter opened with Jambu asking "what meaning has been expounded for the first chapter?" and it now closes with Sudharma answering "this is the meaning that has been expounded." The frame is now complete. This pattern of opening-question and closing-answer is consistent across all the Angas. It serves several functions: it defines the boundaries of each unit of teaching so they can be memorized as discrete wholes; it preserves the dialogical character of Jain transmission; and it embeds humility into the form itself — every teaching is presented as the answer to a question, never as an unprompted assertion. The closing also returns the entire chapter to its proper authority. The story of Jali Kumar is not Sudharma's story; Sudharma is only the transmitter. The story is what Mahavira himself taught — and Mahavira taught it not as theory but as the lived reality of one of the souls he himself ordained. In this way, every chapter of the Anuttaropapātika is not a fable but a witness account: a record of someone Mahavira knew, taught, watched walk the path, and saw cross over.

Adhyayanas 2–10 — Mayali through Abhaya
1.A2-10.S1

The Nine Remaining Princes

एवं सेसाणं वि णवण्हं भाणियव्वं । णवरं सत्त धारिणीसुआ, वेहल्ल-वेहायसा चेल्लणाए । अभओ णंदाए ।

आइल्लाणं पंचण्हं सोलस वासाइं सामण्णपरियाओ । तिण्हं बारस वासाइं । दोण्हं पंच वासाइं ।

आइल्लाणं पंचण्हं आणुपुव्वीए उववाओ विजए वेजयंते जयंते अपराजिए सव्वट्ठसिद्धे ।

दीहदंते सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । उक्कमेणं सेसा । अभओ विजए । सेसं जहा पढमे । ॥1.A2-10.S1॥

evaṃ sesāṇaṃ vi ṇavaṇhaṃ bhāṇiyavvaṃ | ṇavaraṃ satta dhāriṇīsuā, vehalla-vehāyasā cellaṇāe | abhao ṇaṃdāe | āillāṇaṃ paṃcaṇhaṃ solasa vāsāiṃ sāmaṇṇapariyāo | tiṇhaṃ bārasa vāsāiṃ | doṇhaṃ paṃca vāsāiṃ | āillāṇaṃ paṃcaṇhaṃ āṇupuvvīe uvavāo vijae vejayaṃte jayaṃte aparājie savvaṭṭhasiddhe | dīhadaṃte savvaṭṭhasiddhe | ukkameṇaṃ sesā | abhao vijae | sesaṃ jahā paḍhame | || 1.A2-10.S1 ||

The same description applies to the remaining nine. The only differences are these: seven of them were sons of Queen Dharini; Vehalla and Vehayasa were sons of Queen Chellana; Abhaya was the son of Queen Nanda. The first five lived sixteen years as monks; the next three lived twelve years; the last two lived five years. The first five were reborn, in order, in the heavens Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita, and Sarvarthasiddhi. Dirghadanta (the sixth) was reborn in Sarvarthasiddhi; the remaining four (Lashtadanta, Vehalla, Vehayasa, and Abhaya) were reborn in the higher heavens in reverse order — and Abhaya was reborn in Vijaya. The rest of the description is the same as in the first chapter.

This sutra is one of the great compressions of the Jain canon. Rather than retell nine more lives in full — each a near-copy of Jali's — the scripture gives only the points of difference. The reader is expected to apply Jali's full narrative to each prince and only update the named details. The differences are themselves rich. The ten princes are sons of three different queens of King Shrenika: seven by Queen Dharini (including Jali himself, as already told), two — Vehalla and Vehayasa — by Queen Chellana, and one — Abhaya — by Queen Nanda. The mention of Chellana is significant in canonical history: she was a deeply devoted Jain queen, daughter of King Chetaka of Vaishali, and her sons inherited her piety. Abhaya, son of Nanda, is one of the most famous figures of Magadhan history — celebrated in many Jain narratives as the wise minister who advised his father Shrenika before himself renouncing the world. The lengths of monastic life differ across the ten: five lived as monks for sixteen years, three for twelve years, and two for only five years. This single detail tells us something about the variety of paths to the same destination. Sixteen years is a long, mature renunciation; five years is short and intense. Yet both can lead to the highest heavens — because what determines the rebirth is not duration but inner purity and the karmic state at the moment of death. The scripture is implicitly teaching that there is no single template for spiritual maturity. Some souls need long discipline; others arrive faster. The five anuttara heavens are named here in order: Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita, Sarvarthasiddhi — meaning roughly "the victorious," "the well-victorious," "the conquering," "the unconquerable," and "the all-purpose-fulfilled." Sarvarthasiddhi, the fifth and highest, is special: souls reborn there always attain liberation in their very next human birth, with no further detours. To be reborn in Sarvarthasiddhi is to be one human life away from final freedom. Two of the ten princes — Dirghadanta (the sixth) and Varishena (the fifth) — reach this supreme dwelling. The pattern of rebirth shows a beautiful symmetry. The first five princes ascend in order through the five heavens: Jali in Vijaya, Mayali in Vaijayanta, Upajali in Jayanta, Purushasena in Aparajita, Varishena in Sarvarthasiddhi. The sixth, Dirghadanta, also reaches Sarvarthasiddhi. Then the remaining four return through the heavens in reverse: Lashtadanta in Aparajita, Vehalla in Jayanta, Vehayasa in Vaijayanta, Abhaya in Vijaya. The structure is almost like a wave that rises, peaks twice at the top, and descends — covering every one of the five highest heavens with at least two princes each. The commentary in the Gujarati edition adds a meditative note: although the highest fruit of pure conduct is liberation itself, here the residual karma kept these ten souls in heavenly form rather than freed form. They will all attain liberation in time. The scripture's teaching here is that human birth — however privileged — is the only platform for final freedom; even the highest heavens are way-stations, not destinations.

⚠️The order of rebirth for the last four princes (Lashtadanta, Vehalla, Vehayasa, Abhaya) is given by the marker ukkameṇaṃ sesā ("the rest in reverse order") together with abhao vijae ("Abhaya in Vijaya"). The Gujarati commentary states that the first five were reborn in ascending order Vijaya→Sarvarthasiddhi, the sixth (Dirghadanta) in Sarvarthasiddhi, and "the rest" in reverse order with Abhaya (the tenth) in Vijaya. Working backward: Abhaya in Vijaya, Vehayasa in Vaijayanta, Vehalla in Jayanta, Lashtadanta in Aparajita. This reading is consistent with the commentary but the precise sequence for the middle two is inferred from "reverse order" rather than stated explicitly in the Prakrit.

The simple version: The other nine princes lived lives just like Jali's, with just a few differences in their mothers, the length of their monkhood, and which of the five highest heavens they were reborn into; together the ten princes covered every one of those heavens.

1.A2-10.S2

Closing of the First Varga

एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइय दसाणं पढमस्स वग्गस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते । ॥1.A2-10.S2॥

evaṃ khalu jambū ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarovavāiya dasāṇaṃ paḍhamassa vaggassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte | || 1.A2-10.S2 ||

"Truly so, O Jambu! This is the meaning the Ascetic Lord Mahavira, the attainer of liberation, has expounded for the first section of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā."

This sutra closes the entire First Varga. It mirrors the opening question of Sutra 2 — where Jambu asked, "What meaning has been expounded for the first section?" — and now seals the answer. The First Varga, with its ten princes, is now formally complete. The deeper teaching of the First Varga emerges from looking back across all ten princes together. They were brothers — sons of the same king, raised in the same palace, surrounded by the same wealth, taught by the same scriptures. Yet each walked his own path, each at his own pace, each reborn in his own appointed dwelling. The scripture does not flatten them into a single archetype. It honors their individuality even as it celebrates their common destination. What unites them is not the sameness of their lives but the direction of their lives. Each one took the same fundamental turn — from kingdom to monkhood, from possession to renunciation, from outer abundance to inner discipline. After that one decisive turn, the variations no longer mattered: long life or short, this heaven or that — all of it was simply the natural shape of the soul's homeward journey. The Gujarati commentary closes the chapter with a beautiful synthesis: pure conduct alone may not always burn through every karma in this life, but it ensures that the soul will rise through layers of luminous existence and return for one final human birth in which liberation will be inevitable. The body the seeker now wears, the house they now inhabit, the discipline they now practice — none of it is wasted. All of it is moving them, directly or by long arc, toward the freedom that is their birthright.
