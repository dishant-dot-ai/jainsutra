The Second Group (દ્વિતીય વર્ગ)

Thirteen princes of Rajagriha, headed by Dirghasena, who renounce under Mahavira and ascend to the four sub-Anuttara heavens

About This Chapter

Second Varga of the 9th Anga

The Second Varga of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā presents thirteen princes of Rajagriha — Dirghasena, Mahasena, Lashtadanta, Gudhadanta, Shuddhadanta, Halla, Druma, Drumasena, Mahadrumasena, Simha, Simhasena, Mahasimhasena, and Punyasena — sons of King Shrenika by various queens, who renounced the world under Lord Mahavira, observed sixteen years of monastic discipline, undertook a one-month sallekhana in the highest pādapopagamana grade, and ascended in pairs to the four sub-Anuttara heavens (Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita), with the last five — beginning with Mahadrumasena — rising to Sarvarthasiddha, the supreme heaven.

The chapter is told in the canonical shorthand of the daśā form: only the utkshepa (opening question), the body of the account (Dirghasena's biography by reference to Jali Kumar of Varga 1), and the nikshepa (closing seal) are given as full Prakrit. The individual accounts of the remaining twelve princes are extended by atidesha — formula reference — using the phrase jahā dīhaseṇe tahā [name] vi ("as with Dirghasena, so also with [name]"), folding all thirteen lives into one shared biography that differs only in the prince's name and final destination.

Adhyayana 1 — Dirghasena Kumar
2.A1.S1

"Reverend Sir! If the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared this meaning of the first chapter of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā, then, Reverend Sir, what meaning has He declared of its second chapter?" — "Truly so, Jambu! Then what meaning has He declared of its first sub-chapter?"

This opening sutra is the utkshepa — the formal invitation that begins every chapter of the daśā literature. Jambu Swami, the disciple, asks his teacher Sudharma Swami: "You have told me what is in the first chapter — now what is in the second?" The teacher answers by first listing the thirteen names that the chapter will cover. This pattern — list first, narrate second — is the standard scaffolding of the daśā texts and serves a memorial purpose. In an oral tradition, names had to be fixed at the outset so the listener knew the shape of what was coming and could later test their recall against the list. Most names end in -sena ("army"), a typical feature of Kshatriya naming in the Magadhan era; three are formed with -danta ("tooth"), likely descriptive of the prince's dental beauty, a noted feature in classical Indian portraiture of nobility. The repetition of root-names with intensifiers — Sena/Mahasena, Druma/Drumasena/Mahadrumasena, Simha/Simhasena/Mahasimhasena — suggests these were brothers or near-brothers in the same lineage, named in graded series. The fact that thirteen princes from the same household chose ordination in a single generation is itself the silent claim of this chapter: Mahavira's teaching, when it entered Shrenika's palace, did not draw one or two disciples but emptied entire wings of it.

Simply put: Jambu asks his teacher what the second chapter of the scripture says, and the teacher answers by first naming the thirteen princes the chapter is about, and then asking which one to begin with — the first, Dirghasena.

2.A1.S2

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं रायगिहे नयरे, गुणसिलए चेइए । सेणिए राया । धारिणी देवी । सीहो सुमिणे । जहा जाली तहा जम्मं, बालत्तणं, कलाओ । नवरं दीहसेणे णामं कुमारे ।

सच्चेव वत्तव्वया जहा जालिस्स जाव अंतं काहिइ ।

एवं तेरस वि रायगिहे । सेणिओ पिया । धारिणी माया । तेरसण्हं वि सोलस वासा परियाओ । आणुपुव्वीए विजए दोण्णि, वेजयंते दोण्णि, जयंते दोण्णि, अपराजिए दोण्णि, सेसा महादुमसेणमाई पंच सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । ॥2.A1.S2॥

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ rāyagihe nayare, guṇasilae ceie | seṇie rāyā | dhāriṇī devī | sīho sumiṇe | jahā jālī tahā jammaṃ, bālattaṇaṃ, kalāo | navaraṃ dīhaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre | saccceva vattavvayā jahā jālissa jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii | evaṃ terasa vi rāyagihe | seṇio piyā | dhāriṇī māyā | terasaṇhaṃ vi solasa vāsā pariyāo | āṇupuvvīe vijae doṇṇi, vejayaṃte doṇṇi, jayaṃte doṇṇi, aparājie doṇṇi, sesā mahādumaseṇamāī paṃca savvaṭṭhasiddhe |

"Truly so, Jambu! In that age, at that time, there was a city called Rajagriha and a sacred grove called Gunashilaka. The king was Shrenika; the queen was Dharini. She saw a lion in her dream. As with Jali, so the birth, the childhood, and the training in the arts are to be understood — only the prince's name was Dirghasena. The same account as Jali's is to be applied to him, up to 'he will make an end of all suffering.' In the same way, all thirteen were of Rajagriha; Shrenika was the father; Dharini was the mother; the ascetic life of all thirteen lasted sixteen years. In due order: two were reborn in the Vijaya heaven, two in the Vaijayanta, two in the Jayanta, two in the Aparajita; the remaining five, beginning with Mahadrumasena, in Sarvarthasiddha."

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū = thus indeed, O Jambu | teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ = in that age, at that time | rāyagihe nayare = in the city of Rajagriha | guṇasilae ceie = the Gunashilaka sacred grove | seṇie rāyā = King Shrenika | dhāriṇī devī = Queen Dharini | sīho sumiṇe = a lion (was seen) in (her) dream | jahā jālī tahā jammaṃ = as with Jali, so the birth | bālattaṇaṃ = childhood | kalāo = the seventy-two arts (mastered) | navaraṃ = the only difference | dīhaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince's name was Dirghasena | saccceva vattavvayā = the same account is to be told | jahā jālissa = as in Jali's case | jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii = up to "he will make an end (of all suffering)" | evaṃ terasa vi = in this way, all thirteen as well | solasa vāsā pariyāo = sixteen years was their ascetic tenure | āṇupuvvīe = in due sequence | vijae doṇṇi = in Vijaya, two | vejayaṃte doṇṇi = in Vaijayanta, two | jayaṃte doṇṇi = in Jayanta, two | aparājie doṇṇi = in Aparajita, two | sesā = the remaining ones | mahādumaseṇamāī paṃca = the five beginning with Mahadrumasena | savvaṭṭhasiddhe = in Sarvarthasiddha (the supreme heaven).

This is the heart of the Second Varga, told in canonical shorthand. The opening sentence — "in that age, at that time, in Rajagriha, at the Gunashilaka grove, King Shrenika, Queen Dharini, a lion in her dream" — is not new information but a stamp every prince in this chapter shares. The phrase jahā jālī — "as with Jali" — is the textual hinge: the entire detailed biography of Jali Kumar from the First Varga is to be transposed onto Dirghasena, with only the prince's name changed. This includes Jali's lion-dream conception, his upbringing by five wet-nurses, his mastery of the seventy-two arts, his marriage to eight princesses each bringing eight crores of silver and eight crores of gold, his life of pleasure in the upper apartments, his awakening when Mahavira arrived in Rajagriha, his ordination after his parents' permission, his study of the eleven Angas under the elders, his Gunaratna fast, his sallekhana, his ascent to an Anuttara heaven, and finally his single rebirth in Mahavideha where he will attain liberation. The phrase jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii — "up to 'he will make an end'" — abbreviates the entire ending of the Jali account into three syllables. The collective summary then groups all thirteen princes by destination: eight (in pairs) to the four sub-Anuttara heavens, and the remaining five to Sarvarthasiddha — the supreme fifth Anuttara heaven, the highest single station any embodied soul can reach short of liberation. The detail that all thirteen kept exactly sixteen years of ascetic tenure tells us they functioned as one phenomenon viewed thirteenfold: a single household's mass renunciation. The economy of the writing is itself a teaching — the text refuses to entertain us with thirteen separate stories because, at the level of soul, the story is one.

Simply put: Prince Dirghasena's whole life, from birth to liberation, is the same story as Jali's from the first chapter — and the same story applies to all thirteen princes named here, who lived sixteen years as monks and were reborn in the highest heavens, with the last five reaching Sarvarthasiddha, the very top.

2.A1.S3

एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोव-वाइयदसाणं दोच्चस्स वग्गस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते ।

मासियाए संलेहणाए दोसु वि वग्गेसु । ॥2.A1.S3॥

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarova-vāiyadasāṇaṃ doccassa vaggassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte | māsiyāe saṃlehaṇāe dosu vi vaggesu |

"Truly so, Jambu! This is the meaning of the second chapter of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā that the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared. In both chapters, the sallekhana of every monk lasted one month."

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū = thus indeed, O Jambu | samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ = by the Sramana Lord Mahavira | jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ = up to "the one who has attained (liberation)" | aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ = of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā | doccassa vaggassa = of the second chapter | ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte = this meaning has been declared | māsiyāe saṃlehaṇāe = with a one-month sallekhana (final fasting and self-purification) | dosu vi vaggesu = in both chapters (i.e., for every monk in both Vargas).

This is the nikshepa — the formal sealing of the chapter. Sudharma Swami declares the second chapter complete and adds a single technical note of immense importance: in both the first and second chapters, every monk's sallekhana lasted exactly one month. Sallekhana is the final spiritual practice of the Jain renunciate — a deliberate, gradual reduction of food and water leading to a peaceful, conscious death held in deep equanimity. It is not suicide; it is the opposite. Suicide is performed in revulsion against life; sallekhana is performed in detachment from both life and death, when the body has become incapable of sustaining its religious duties and the soul is ready to leave with its attention undisturbed. The discipline requires that the dying soul hold no clinging to life, no fear of death, no attachment to past pleasures, no aversion to past pains, no anxiety about the next birth — a state called samādhi-maraṇa, equipoise-death. The note here specifies that all twenty-three princes — ten in Varga 1 and thirteen in Varga 2 — undertook a one-month sallekhana, and the editor's vivechan further explains that all twenty-three undertook pādapopagamana santhāra — the highest grade, in which the monk lies down motionless like a fallen tree (pādapa, "tree") and remains so until death, neither moving the limbs nor accepting any service. Twenty-three princes of one royal house, born to luxury, raised in palaces, married to dozens of princesses, owning crores of gold — all of them ended their lives lying immobile in a forest, eating nothing, watching their last breath leave with the same equanimity with which they had once watched dancers perform.

Simply put: The teacher closes the second chapter and adds one critical detail: every single one of the twenty-three princes from the first and second chapters spent the last month of their lives in a deliberate, peaceful fast, ending their bodies in full awareness.

Adhyayana 2 — Mahasena Kumar
2.A2.S1

नवरं महासेणे णामं कुमारे । ॥2.A2.S1॥

navaraṃ mahāseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre |

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

navaraṃ = the only difference | mahāseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Mahasena ("great-armied") | (the rest of the account — Rajagriha, Gunashilaka, Shrenika, Dharini, lion dream, birth, childhood, arts, marriage, awakening, ordination, sixteen years of ascetic life, ascent to one of the four sub-Anuttara heavens — vijae / vejayaṃte / jayaṃte / aparājie — and the final rebirth in Mahavideha for liberation, is the same as for Dirghasena and ultimately as for Jali Kumar.)

Mahasena's name — "great army" — pairs naturally with his elder Dirghasena's "long army"; together the names suggest two princes intended for military leadership in their father Shrenika's empire. The text gives us no individuating story. Instead it asks the reader to take the entire Jali template — the lion-dream conception, the birth as one of the most beloved sons of the Magadhan throne, the upbringing by five royal nurses, the mastery of the seventy-two arts, the marriage to eight princesses, the awakening when Mahavira arrived at Gunashilaka, the parents' grief and final consent, the renunciation, the eleven Anga studies under the elders, the long silent fasts, the climb up Vipulachala, and the final ascent — and apply it without subtraction. The deep point of this kind of telling is theological: the text refuses to make Mahasena's life feel different from Dirghasena's because, in the logic of liberation, the differences do not matter. What matters is the path — the alignment of right vision, knowledge, and conduct — and once two souls have aligned themselves with that path, their biographies become functionally identical even if their birth-names and palace memories differ. The sameness is not a literary shortcut; it is a metaphysical statement.

Whether Mahasena ascended specifically to Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, or Aparajita is not individually specified; the text only tells us that of the first eight princes in the list, two each went to those four heavens in due order. The plain pair-reading places Dirghasena and Mahasena together in Vijaya, but the source does not state this assignment in so many words.

Simply put: The second prince, Mahasena, lived the same life as Dirghasena and Jali — born to King Shrenika and Queen Dharini, raised in Rajagriha, ordained under Mahavira, sixteen years a monk, and reborn in one of the highest heavens.

Adhyayana 3 — Lashtadanta Kumar
2.A3.S1

जहा दीहसेणे तहा लट्ठदंते वि । नवरं लट्ठदंते णामं कुमारे । ॥2.A3.S1॥

jahā dīhaseṇe tahā laṭṭhadaṃte vi | navaraṃ laṭṭhadaṃte ṇāmaṃ kumāre |

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

jahā dīhaseṇe = as with Dirghasena | tahā laṭṭhadaṃte vi = so also with Lashtadanta | navaraṃ = the only difference | laṭṭhadaṃte ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Lashtadanta (literally "of graceful / well-formed teeth"; laṣṭa in Prakrit/Sanskrit denotes elegance or beauty) | (rest as Dirghasena, ultimately as Jali; the rebirth was in one of the four sub-Anuttara heavens within the chapter's collective allocation of two-each in due order.)

Lashtadanta's name draws attention to a feature of classical Indian portraiture: well-shaped teeth were among the thirty-two physical signs of a great being (mahāpuruṣa-lakṣaṇa). To be named "graceful-toothed" is to carry one of those signs as a daily badge. The chapter's economy says nothing about whether Lashtadanta lived up to the implication, only that his life followed the Jali pattern. Names like this matter because they reveal how the world saw these princes before their renunciation. They were the most beautiful, most accomplished, most physically auspicious men of their generation — and they walked away from their bodies as easily as from their robes. The text places the name and the renunciation side by side without comment, trusting the reader to feel the contrast.

The PDF does not specify Lashtadanta's exact heavenly destination; he is one of the eight princes paired into the four sub-Anuttara heavens in order.

Simply put: The third prince, Lashtadanta — named for his beautiful teeth — lived the same life of luxury and renunciation as his brothers, ending in one of the highest heavens.

The name Gudhadanta — "concealed-toothed" — describes a prince whose teeth did not show prominently when he smiled, considered an aristocratic feature in classical India. He is paired in the listing-verse with Lashtadanta and Shuddhadanta, the three "tooth-named" princes appearing together. Like his brothers, his life is folded into Dirghasena's. The sixteen years of ascetic discipline, the eleven Angas studied, the long silent fasts, the final sallekhana — all are his too. Whether his Anuttara destination was Vaijayanta or one of the others is not individually specified.

The interpretation of gūḍha-danta as "concealed-toothed" follows standard scholarly readings; alternatives such as "deep-set teeth" or "even-toothed" are possible but less standard.

Simply put: The fourth prince, Gudhadanta, named for the way his teeth were set, walked the same path as his brothers — palace to forest to heaven.

Shuddhadanta — "pure-toothed" — completes the trio of dental-named princes. The name means more than dental hygiene; in the classical idiom śuddha carries a sense of brightness, clarity, and untaintedness. To carry such a name was to carry a piece of physical perfection. The text gives us no individual story, only the formulaic placement: he too was a son of Shrenika and Dharini, ordained at the standard age, kept sixteen years of ascetic life, and was reborn in one of the sub-Anuttara heavens. He is, by the order in the listing-verse, the fifth of the eight who were paired into Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, and Aparajita.

Reading the listing-verse strictly in pairs would place Shuddhadanta and Halla together as the third pair (probably in Jayanta), but the text only specifies "two each in due order" without naming the pairs explicitly. The Gujarati edition's vivechan notes that some readings differ between manuscripts (kyāṃka pāṭhabheda jovā maḷe chhe).

Simply put: The fifth prince, Shuddhadanta — named for his pure white teeth — followed the same path of renunciation as his brothers and reached one of the highest heavens.

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

jahā dīhaseṇe = as with Dirghasena | tahā halle vi = so also with Halla | navaraṃ = the only difference | halle ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Halla | (rest as Dirghasena.)

Halla is one of the better-known names from the Magadhan canonical cycle. The text places Halla among the eight who were reborn in the sub-Anuttara heavens (Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, or Aparajita), with the standard sixteen-year ascetic tenure. He is the sixth in the listing-verse — by strict pair-reading he would be in Jayanta; but again the text does not assign pairs explicitly. The very plainness of the entry tells us something important: even a prince whose worldly story may have been dramatic (war, rivalry, loss of empire) is, in this scripture, reduced to a single line — not because his story did not matter, but because once he reached the path, his story collapsed into the path. The drama of the world becomes a footnote when the soul finds its road.

A prince named Halla appears in the Bhagavati Sutra and elsewhere as a son of Shrenika who, with his half-brother Vihalla, became famous for an episode involving the divine elephant Sechanaka and the necklace called Aṭṭhāra-saraga, leading to a war with their elder half-brother Kuṇika (Ajātaśatru). Whether the Halla of this Anuttarovavai chapter is the same prince is not stated in the source PDF and is debated in the broader tradition.

Simply put: The sixth prince, Halla — perhaps the same Halla famous in other Jain texts for a war over an elephant — walked the same path of renunciation as his brothers and reached one of the highest heavens.

Druma — "tree" — is the seventh of the listing. He is the last of the eight princes assigned to the sub-Anuttara heavens. The name "tree" is not random. In Jain symbolism a tree often carries the meanings of stability, rootedness, and finally the pādapopagamana form of sallekhana — the "fallen-tree" posture in which the dying monk lies motionless. A prince named Druma whose life ends in the tree-posture sallekhana embodies his name in his death.

By strict pair-reading Druma would be in Aparajita with Drumasena, but the text does not assign pairs explicitly. The connection between his name and the pādapopagamana sallekhana is interpretive; the text itself does not draw it.

Simply put: The seventh prince, Druma — whose name means "tree" — followed the same renunciation as his brothers and reached one of the highest heavens.

Drumasena's name pairs the steadiness of "tree" with the strength of "army": a prince of a forest-strong battalion. He is the eighth in the listing-verse, occupying the borderline position between the eight sub-Anuttara princes and the five Sarvarthasiddha princes that follow. The source PDF locates his rebirth in the collective ("two each in Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita; the rest, beginning with Mahadrumasena, in Sarvarthasiddha"). Reading "the rest beginning with Mahadrumasena" strictly excludes Drumasena from Sarvarthasiddha — meaning he is the last of the eight in Aparajita. The Gujarati bhāvārtha on PDF p. 12 confirms this reading: "the remaining five Mahadrumasena and the others ascended to Sarvarthasiddha."

Drumasena is the boundary case: by strict pair-reading he is the last of the eight assigned to the four sub-Anuttara heavens, but the source text does not state this individually.

Simply put: The eighth prince, Drumasena, was the last of the eight brothers reborn in the four sub-Anuttara heavens after sixteen years of monastic life.

Adhyayana 9 — Mahadrumasena Kumar
2.A9.S1

जहा दीहसेणे तहा महादुमसेणे वि । नवरं महादुमसेणे णामं कुमारे । उववाओ सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । ॥2.A9.S1॥

jahā dīhaseṇe tahā mahādumaseṇe vi | navaraṃ mahādumaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre | uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe |

Individual Adhyayana Prakrit not given in the PDF; reconstructed by chapter-internal formula.

As with Dirghasena, so also with Mahadrumasena. The only difference is that the prince's name was Mahadrumasena, and his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha.

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

jahā dīhaseṇe = as with Dirghasena | tahā mahādumaseṇe vi = so also with Mahadrumasena | navaraṃ = the difference (here is twofold) | mahādumaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Mahadrumasena ("great tree-armied") | uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe = his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha (the supreme Anuttara heaven) | (rest of the life — birth, upbringing, ordination, sixteen years of monastic life, sallekhana — as Dirghasena.)

With Mahadrumasena the chapter shifts. He is the first of the five princes reborn in Sarvarthasiddha — the supreme heaven of the Jain cosmos, the highest single station any embodied soul can occupy. From Sarvarthasiddha, only one more birth in Mahavideha is required, and then liberation. The four sub-Anuttara heavens are also one-life-from-liberation realms, but Sarvarthasiddha is the peak: its inhabitants are the spiritual cream of the cosmos. The text does not tell us why Mahadrumasena was reborn higher than his predecessors. There is no narrative of him fasting longer, meditating deeper, or studying more thoroughly. The implication, in Jain doctrine, is that the difference lay in the subtler quality of his right-vision-knowledge-conduct — the inwardness of his practice rather than its outward form. Two monks may keep identical fasts; one rises higher because his attention is purer. This is a sobering point. The chapter does not give the reader a checklist by which to climb. It tells us that climbing happens, that some climb higher than others, and that the difference is invisible to outside observation. What is visible is the path; what determines the elevation is the quiet inward fire.

Simply put: The ninth prince, Mahadrumasena, was the first of five brothers reborn in Sarvarthasiddha — the very highest heaven, only one more human life away from final liberation.

Simha — "lion" — carries one of the most charged names in the Indian spiritual lexicon. The lion is the king of beasts; in Jain symbolism it stands for fearless solitude, royal bearing, and the uncompromising spirit of the renunciate. Mahavira himself is repeatedly compared to a lion in his independence and his refusal to be moved by approval or fear. To name a prince "Simha" was to wish on him exactly the qualities he later showed when he walked away from his throne. His mother Dharini is also said in Varga 1 (and by inference here) to have seen a lion in her dream when she conceived. The recurrence of the lion-image — in the dream, in the prince's name, and in the Tirthankara whose teaching he followed — is one of the quiet structural beauties of the chapter. Simha is the second of the five princes reborn in Sarvarthasiddha, the supreme heaven.

Simply put: The tenth prince, Simha — "lion" — was the second of five brothers to reach Sarvarthasiddha, the very highest heaven.

2.A11.S1

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

jahā dīhaseṇe = as with Dirghasena | tahā sīhaseṇe vi = so also with Simhasena | navaraṃ = the difference | sīhaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Simhasena ("lion-armied") | uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe = his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha | (rest as Dirghasena.)

Simhasena is the third of the Sarvarthasiddha five and the second of the lion-named trio. The graded series — Simha, Simhasena, Mahasimhasena — mirrors the earlier graded series — Druma, Drumasena, Mahadrumasena — and shows the typical Magadhan custom of naming successive sons in intensifying degrees. His ascent to Sarvarthasiddha is identical in form to his elder brother Simha's: sixteen years of strict ascetic life, the eleven Angas mastered, the one-month pādapopagamana sallekhana, and then the soul's rise past every lower heavenly realm — the Saudharma, the Iśāna, the Sanat-kumāra, the Māhendra, the Brahma-loka, the Lāntaka, the Mahāśukra, the Sahasrāra, the Ānata, the Prāṇata, the Āraṇa, and the Acyuta; then past the nine Graiveyaka layers above those; and finally past the four sub-Anuttara heavens — Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita — to settle in the highest Anuttara: Sarvarthasiddha. From there, a single human birth in Mahavideha brings final liberation.

Simply put: The eleventh prince, Simhasena, was the third of five brothers to reach Sarvarthasiddha, the highest heaven, after sixteen years as a monk.

Mahasimhasena — "great lion-army" — completes the lion trio. He is the fourth of the five princes who reached Sarvarthasiddha. The intensifier mahā ("great") added to his older brothers' names suggests he was the youngest of the three, named in the established custom of giving younger sons grander forms of the elder son's name. What is striking, when one steps back to view the chapter as a whole, is that the youngest in each grouping seems to have reached the highest destination. Mahadrumasena is reborn higher than Druma and Drumasena. Mahasimhasena is reborn higher than Simha and Simhasena.

This pattern is an interpretive observation; the text does not draw the comparison, and the cause-and-effect cannot be extracted from the source. A doctrinal reading would note that the spiritual destination depends on the soul's inner state at the moment of death, not on age or birth-order. The pattern, if it is a pattern, may be coincidental.

Simply put: The twelfth prince, Mahasimhasena — "great lion-army" — was the fourth of five brothers to reach the highest heaven, Sarvarthasiddha.

2.A13.S1

जहा दीहसेणे तहा पुण्णसेणे वि । नवरं पुण्णसेणे णामं कुमारे । उववाओ सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । ॥2.A13.S1॥

Individual Adhyayana Prakrit not given in the PDF; reconstructed by chapter-internal formula.

Anvayartha · Word-by-Word

jahā dīhaseṇe = as with Dirghasena | tahā puṇṇaseṇe vi = so also with Punyasena | navaraṃ = the difference | puṇṇaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre = the prince was named Punyasena (literally "army of merit"; puṇya = merit, virtue, auspicious karma) | uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe = his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha | (rest as Dirghasena.)

Punyasena closes the chapter — the thirteenth and last of the princes, the fifth and last of the Sarvarthasiddha-bound. His name means "army of merit" or "merit-armied," a beautifully apt closing name for a chapter whose entire arc is about the conversion of inherited royal merit into the merit that lifts a soul to the highest heaven. Among the thirteen, he stands at the end of the list as a kind of summation. The chapter began with Dirghasena, "long-armied" — the prince of long worldly reach — and ends with Punyasena, "merit-armied" — the prince whose army was inward, made not of spears but of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct. The scripture has, by listing them in this order, traced an arc: from worldly army to spiritual army, from kingdom to renunciation, from palace to forest to highest heaven. With Punyasena's ascent, the chapter closes its full circle. All thirteen are now installed in heaven, all twenty-three (including the ten of Varga 1) have completed their one-month sallekhana, and the path has been demonstrated to be available — visibly, repeatedly, in one royal generation alone. The reader who has read carefully is meant to notice that the path has not been kept secret, that it was not reserved for a single hero. It was walked thirteen times in a single household, and twenty-three times in two adjacent chapters.

The Gujarati vivechan (PDF p. 13) makes the chapter's closing point explicit: "In this Varga, the auspicious fruit of the integrated practice of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct has been displayed. It is well known that only the action performed in conjunction with right faith and right knowledge has the power to destroy karma."

Simply put: The thirteenth and final prince, Punyasena — "merit-armied" — was the last of five brothers to reach Sarvarthasiddha, completing the chapter's account of all thirteen princes who walked from palace to highest heaven.
