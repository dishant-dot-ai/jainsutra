# Anuttarovavai Sutra — Chapter 2 (Varga 2): Dirghasena and the Twelve Other Princes

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Shri Anuttarovavai Sutra, Gujarati edition (Guru Pran Prakashan)
**Section:** Second Varga — Thirteen Princes of Rajagriha
**Total Adhyayanas:** 13 (delivered in three condensed sutras: Utkshepa, Body, Nikshepa)
**Overview:** The Second Varga of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā lists thirteen sons of King Shrenika and Queen Dharini who renounced the world, kept sramana discipline for sixteen years, and were reborn — eight of them — in the four uppermost Anuttara heavens (Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, Aparajita), and the remaining five — beginning with Mahadrumasena — in Sarvarthasiddha, the highest Anuttara abode. The Varga is told in extreme abbreviation: the full life-account of the first prince, Dirghasena, is given by reference to Jali Kumar of Varga 1, and the other twelve are folded into him by the formula "all the rest is to be understood as in the Jali chapter." This is the canonical method of the *daśā* texts — the first life is told in full, the remaining are gathered into it. From the seekers' viewpoint, all thirteen princes share one essential biography: born to royal parents in Rajagriha, raised in luxury, married into wealth, awakened by Mahavira's discourse, ordained, mastered the eleven Anga scriptures, fasted to a one-month sallekhana, and ascended to an Anuttara heaven, from which a single human birth in Mahavideha will complete their liberation.

## Adhyayana 1 — Dirghasena Kumar

### SUTRA 2.A1.S1 — Utkshepa (Opening Question and the Listing of Thirteen Names)

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं पढमस्स वग्गस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते, दोच्चस्स णं भंते ! वग्गस्स अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ?

एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइय-दसाणं दोच्चस्स वग्गस्स तेरस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता । तं जहा—

दीहसेणे महासेणे, लट्टदंते य गूढदंते य सुद्धदंते य ।

हल्ले दुमे दुमसेणे, महादुमसेणे य आहिए ।।

सीहे य सीहसेणे य, महासीहसेणे य आहिए ।

पुण्णसेणे य बोधव्वे, तेरसमे होइ अज्झयणे ।।

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोववाइयदसाणं दोच्चस्स वग्गस्स तेरस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता, पढमस्स णं भंते ! अज्झयणस्स समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते । ॥2.A1.S1॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dīhaseṇe mahāseṇe, laṭṭadaṃte ya gūḍhadaṃte ya suddhadaṃte ya |

sīhe ya sīhaseṇe ya, mahāsīhaseṇe ya āhie |

puṇṇaseṇe ya bodhavve, terasame hoi ajjhayaṇe ||

jai ṇaṃ bhante ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ doccassa vaggassa terasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā, paḍhamassa ṇaṃ bhante ! ajjhayaṇassa samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte | || 2.A1.S1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Reverend Sir! If the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared this meaning of the first chapter of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā, then, Reverend Sir, what meaning has the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira declared of its second chapter?" — "Truly so, Jambu! The liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared thirteen sub-chapters of the second chapter of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā, namely: Dirghasena, Mahasena, Lashtadanta, Gudhadanta, and Shuddhadanta; Halla, Druma, Drumasena, and Mahadrumasena are stated; Simha and Simhasena, and Mahasimhasena are stated; and Punyasena is to be known as the thirteenth sub-chapter." — "Reverend Sir! If the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared thirteen sub-chapters of the second chapter, then, Reverend Sir, what meaning has the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira declared of its first sub-chapter?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jai ṇaṃ bhante* = if, Reverend Sir | *samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ* = by the Sramana Lord Mahavira | *jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ* = up to "the one who has attained" (i.e., who has reached liberation/nirvāṇa) | *aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ* = of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā (the Ninth Anga) | *paḍhamassa vaggassa* = of the first chapter | *ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte* = this meaning has been declared | *doccassa vaggassa* = of the second chapter | *ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte* = what meaning has been declared? | *evaṃ khalu jaṃbū* = thus indeed, O Jambu | *terasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā* = thirteen sub-chapters have been declared | *taṃ jahā* = namely, as follows | *dīhaseṇe* = Dirghasena (long-armied / of long army) | *mahāseṇe* = Mahasena (great-armied) | *laṭṭadaṃte* = Lashtadanta (graceful-toothed) | *gūḍhadaṃte* = Gudhadanta (concealed-toothed) | *suddhadaṃte* = Shuddhadanta (pure-toothed) | *halle* = Halla | *dume* = Druma (tree) | *dumaseṇe* = Drumasena | *mahādumaseṇe* = Mahadrumasena | *āhie* = is/are stated, named | *sīhe* = Simha (lion) | *sīhaseṇe* = Simhasena | *mahāsīhaseṇe* = Mahasimhasena | *puṇṇaseṇe* = Punyasena (army-of-merit) | *bodhavve* = is to be understood, to be known | *terasame ajjhayaṇe* = as the thirteenth sub-chapter | *paḍhamassa ajjhayaṇassa* = of the first sub-chapter | *ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte* = what meaning has been declared?

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra is the *utkshepa* — the formal invitation that begins every chapter of the *daśā* literature. Jambu Swami, the disciple, asks his teacher Sudharma Swami: "You have told me what is in the first chapter — now what is in the second?" The teacher answers by first listing the thirteen names that the chapter will cover. This pattern — list first, narrate second — is the standard scaffolding of the *daśā* texts and serves a memorial purpose. In an oral tradition, names had to be fixed at the outset so the listener knew the shape of what was coming and could later test their recall against the list.

The names themselves are worth pausing over. Most are dynastic compound names ending in *-sena* ("army"), a typical feature of Kshatriya naming in the Magadhan era — Dirghasena ("long army"), Mahasena ("great army"), Drumasena ("tree-army"), Simhasena ("lion-army"), Punyasena ("merit-army"). Three are formed with *-danta* ("tooth"): Lashtadanta, Gudhadanta, Shuddhadanta — likely descriptive of the prince's dental beauty, a noted feature in classical Indian portraiture of nobility. Halla and Druma stand alone and are well-known names from the Magadha cycle; Halla in particular is named in the Bhagavati and Antakrtdaśā as a son of Shrenika who features in famous palace episodes. The repetition of root-names with intensifiers — Sena, Mahasena; Druma, Drumasena, Mahadrumasena; Simha, Simhasena, Mahasimhasena — suggests these were brothers or near-brothers in the same lineage, named in graded series, a custom seen elsewhere in the canon when a single mother bore many sons.

The fact that thirteen princes from the same household chose ordination in a single generation is itself the silent claim of this chapter. It tells us that Mahavira's teaching, when it entered Shrenika's palace, did not draw one or two disciples but emptied entire wings of it. The chapter shows that renunciation can move not merely individuals but whole houses, and that great worldly fortune is no protection against the awakening that recognizes its emptiness. After listing the names, Jambu asks the natural follow-up: tell me about the first one. That single biography — Dirghasena's — will then stand as the template for all thirteen, in the canonical economy of the *daśā* form.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Jambu asks his teacher what the second chapter of the scripture says, and the teacher answers by first naming the thirteen princes the chapter is about, and then asking which one to begin with — the first, Dirghasena.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you hear thirteen names of people who walked the same path, do you hear them as a list — or as a quiet proof that this path is walkable?

### SUTRA 2.A1.S2 — Dirghasena Kumar's Life and the Collective Account of All Thirteen

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं रायगिहे नयरे, गुणसिलए चेइए । सेणिए राया । धारिणी देवी । सीहो सुमिणे । जहा जाली तहा जम्मं, बालत्तणं, कलाओ । नवरं दीहसेणे णामं कुमारे ।

सच्चेव वत्तव्वया जहा जालिस्स जाव अंतं काहिइ ।

एवं तेरस वि रायगिहे । सेणिओ पिया । धारिणी माया । तेरसण्हं वि सोलस वासा परियाओ । आणुपुव्वीए विजए दोण्णि, वेजयंते दोण्णि, जयंते दोण्णि, अपराजिए दोण्णि, सेसा महादुमसेणमाई पंच सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । ॥2.A1.S2॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ rāyagihe nayare, guṇasilae ceie | seṇie rāyā | dhāriṇī devī | sīho sumiṇe | jahā jālī tahā jammaṃ, bālattaṇaṃ, kalāo | navaraṃ dīhaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre |

saccceva vattavvayā jahā jālissa jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii |

evaṃ terasa vi rāyagihe | seṇio piyā | dhāriṇī māyā | terasaṇhaṃ vi solasa vāsā pariyāo | āṇupuvvīe vijae doṇṇi, vejayaṃte doṇṇi, jayaṃte doṇṇi, aparājie doṇṇi, sesā mahādumaseṇamāī paṃca savvaṭṭhasiddhe | || 2.A1.S2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Truly so, Jambu! In that age, at that time, there was a city called Rajagriha and a sacred grove called Gunashilaka. The king was Shrenika; the queen was Dharini. She saw a lion in her dream. As with Jali, so the birth, the childhood, and the training in the arts are to be understood — only the prince's name was Dirghasena. The same account as Jali's is to be applied to him, up to 'he will make an end of all suffering.' In the same way, all thirteen were of Rajagriha; Shrenika was the father; Dharini was the mother; the ascetic life of all thirteen lasted sixteen years. In due order: two were reborn in the Vijaya heaven, two in the Vaijayanta, two in the Jayanta, two in the Aparajita; the remaining five, beginning with Mahadrumasena, in Sarvarthasiddha."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evaṃ khalu jaṃbū* = thus indeed, O Jambu | *teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ* = in that age, at that time | *rāyagihe nayare* = in the city of Rajagriha | *guṇasilae ceie* = the Gunashilaka sacred grove (chaitya) | *seṇie rāyā* = King Shrenika | *dhāriṇī devī* = Queen Dharini | *sīho sumiṇe* = a lion (was seen) in (her) dream | *jahā jālī tahā jammaṃ* = as with Jali, so the birth | *bālattaṇaṃ* = childhood | *kalāo* = the seventy-two arts (mastered) | *navaraṃ* = the only difference (is) | *dīhaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre* = the prince's name was Dirghasena | *saccceva vattavvayā* = the same account is to be told | *jahā jālissa* = as in Jali's case | *jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii* = up to "he will make an end (of all suffering)" | *evaṃ terasa vi* = in this way, all thirteen as well | *rāyagihe* = were of Rajagriha | *seṇio piyā* = Shrenika was the father | *dhāriṇī māyā* = Dharini was the mother | *terasaṇhaṃ vi* = of all thirteen | *solasa vāsā pariyāo* = sixteen years was their ascetic tenure (paryaya) | *āṇupuvvīe* = in due sequence, in order | *vijae doṇṇi* = in Vijaya, two | *vejayaṃte doṇṇi* = in Vaijayanta, two | *jayaṃte doṇṇi* = in Jayanta, two | *aparājie doṇṇi* = in Aparajita, two | *sesā* = the remaining ones | *mahādumaseṇamāī paṃca* = the five beginning with Mahadrumasena | *savvaṭṭhasiddhe* = in Sarvarthasiddha (the supreme heaven)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the heart of the Second Varga, told in the canonical shorthand. The opening sentence — "in that age, at that time, in the city of Rajagriha, at the Gunashilaka sacred grove, King Shrenika, Queen Dharini, a lion in her dream" — is not new information; it is a stamp. Every prince in this chapter shares this opening. The phrase *jahā jālī* — "as with Jali" — is the textual hinge: the entire detailed biography of Jali Kumar from the First Varga is to be transposed onto Dirghasena, with only the prince's name changed. This includes Jali's birth after a lion-dream (auspicious sign of a great soul), his upbringing by five wet-nurses, his mastery of the seventy-two arts, his marriage to eight princesses each bringing eight crores of silver and eight crores of gold, his life of pleasure in the upper apartments, his awakening when Mahavira arrived in Rajagriha, his ordination after his parents' permission, his study of the eleven Angas under the elders, his Gunaratna fast, his sallekhana, his ascent to an Anuttara heaven, and finally his single rebirth in Mahavideha where he will attain liberation.

The phrase *jāva aṃtaṃ kāhii* — "up to 'he will make an end'" — is the technical closure: it abbreviates the entire ending of the Jali account, where Mahavira tells Gautama that the soul, after one further human birth, will end all suffering. The whole arc of the spiritual life is folded into three syllables.

The collective summary that follows then groups all thirteen princes by destination. Eight of them — pairs of two — were reborn in the four named Anuttara heavens: Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, and Aparajita. These four are the sub-Anuttara heavens, where great souls reside until their final human birth. The remaining five — Mahadrumasena and the four after him in the list (Simha, Simhasena, Mahasimhasena, Punyasena) — were reborn in Sarvarthasiddha, the supreme fifth Anuttara heaven, the highest single station any embodied soul can reach short of liberation itself. In the Jain cosmology, the soul born in Sarvarthasiddha takes only one more birth — in the Mahavideha region, where Tirthankaras are eternally present — and then attains nirvana. So all thirteen princes are, in this chapter, declared to be one-life-from-liberation beings.

The detail that all thirteen kept sixteen years of ascetic tenure is striking. It tells us they were ordained at roughly the same time, lived through the same length of monastic life, and reached their respective sallekhana ends together. They are functionally one phenomenon viewed thirteenfold: a single household's mass renunciation that swept brothers and half-brothers from the palace to the forest in one generational wave.

The economy of the writing is itself a teaching. The text refuses to entertain us with thirteen separate stories because, at the level of soul, the story is one. Once a being commits to right vision, right knowledge, and right conduct, the path is the same path — only the name on the cushion changes.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Prince Dirghasena's whole life, from birth to liberation, is the same story as Jali's from the first chapter — and the same story applies to all thirteen princes named here, who lived sixteen years as monks and were reborn in the highest heavens, with the last five reaching Sarvarthasiddha, the very top.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If thirteen brothers from the same palace can each walk the same path to the highest heaven, what excuse remains for me — that my life is too unique to follow it?

### SUTRA 2.A1.S3 — Nikshepa (Closing Statement on the Sallekhana of All Twenty-Three)

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं अणुत्तरोव-वाइयदसाणं दोच्चस्स वग्गस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते ।

मासियाए संलेहणाए दोसु वि वग्गेसु । ॥2.A1.S3॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ aṇuttarova-vāiyadasāṇaṃ doccassa vaggassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte |

māsiyāe saṃlehaṇāe dosu vi vaggesu | || 2.A1.S3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Truly so, Jambu! This is the meaning of the second chapter of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā that the liberated Sramana Lord Mahavira has declared. In both chapters, the sallekhana of every monk lasted one month."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evaṃ khalu jaṃbū* = thus indeed, O Jambu | *samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ* = by the Sramana Lord Mahavira | *jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ* = up to "the one who has attained (liberation)" | *aṇuttarovavāiyadasāṇaṃ* = of the Anuttaropapātika Daśā | *doccassa vaggassa* = of the second chapter | *ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte* = this meaning has been declared | *māsiyāe saṃlehaṇāe* = with a one-month sallekhana (final fasting and self-purification) | *dosu vi vaggesu* = in both chapters (i.e., for every monk in both Vargas)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the *nikshepa* — the formal sealing of the chapter. Sudharma Swami declares the second chapter complete and adds a single technical note that the editor of the Sutra placed here as a footnote of immense importance: in both the first and second chapters, every monk's sallekhana lasted exactly one month.

Sallekhana is the final spiritual practice of the Jain renunciate — a deliberate, gradual reduction of food and water leading to a peaceful, conscious death held in deep equanimity. It is not suicide; it is the opposite. Suicide is performed in revulsion against life; sallekhana is performed in detachment from both life and death, when the body has become incapable of sustaining its religious duties and the soul is ready to leave with its attention undisturbed by the body's complaints. The discipline requires that the dying soul hold no clinging to life, no fear of death, no attachment to past pleasures, no aversion to past pains, no anxiety about the next birth — a state called *samādhi-maraṇa*, equipoise-death.

The note here specifies that all twenty-three of these princes — ten in Varga 1 and thirteen in Varga 2 — undertook a one-month sallekhana. The text immediately following this sutra (in the editor's *vivechan*) explains further that all twenty-three undertook *pādapopagamana santhāra* — the highest grade of sallekhana, in which the monk lies down motionless like a fallen tree (*pādapa*, "tree") and remains so until death, neither moving the limbs nor accepting service of any kind. This is the most demanding form of the practice and is reserved for monks of the highest spiritual capacity, those whose bodies are still functional enough to be motionless without involuntary stirring and whose minds are firm enough to remain in pure awareness without external support.

The mention is brief but the implication is enormous. Twenty-three princes of one royal house, born to luxury, raised in palaces, married to dozens of princesses, owning crores of gold — all of them ended their lives lying immobile in a forest, eating nothing, watching their last breath leave with the same equanimity with which they had once watched dancers perform. The chapter does not describe this in narrative; it states it as a fact. The reader is meant to feel the weight of the fact rather than be moved by its drama.

The Vivechan note that follows the sutra in the Gujarati edition adds a final theological observation: this Varga has displayed the auspicious fruit of the integrated practice of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct — for it is well known that only right action carried out together with right faith and right knowledge has the power to destroy karma. The mass ascent of these thirteen princes to the Anuttara heavens is the proof in evidence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The teacher closes the second chapter and adds one critical detail: every single one of the twenty-three princes from the first and second chapters spent the last month of their lives in a deliberate, peaceful fast, ending their bodies in full awareness.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you knew you had one month to leave this body — could you let go of it the way these princes did, without fear, without grasping, without one last bargain?

## Adhyayana 2 — Mahasena Kumar

### SUTRA 2.A2.S1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

navaraṃ mahāseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre | || 2.A2.S1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The only difference is that the prince's name was Mahasena.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*navaraṃ* = the only difference (is) | *mahāseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre* = the prince was named Mahasena ("great-armied") | (the rest of the account — Rajagriha, Gunashilaka, Shrenika, Dharini, lion dream, birth, childhood, arts, marriage, awakening, ordination, sixteen years of ascetic life, ascent to one of the four sub-Anuttara heavens — *vijae / vejayaṃte / jayaṃte / aparājie* — and the final rebirth in Mahavideha for liberation, is the same as for Dirghasena and ultimately as for Jali Kumar.)

**5. COMMENTARY**

Mahasena's name — "great army" — pairs naturally with his elder Dirghasena's "long army"; together the names suggest two princes intended for military leadership in their father Shrenika's empire. The text gives us no individuating story. Instead it asks the reader to take the entire Jali template — the lion-dream conception, the birth as one of the most beloved sons of the Magadhan throne, the upbringing by five royal nurses, the mastery of the seventy-two arts of a Kshatriya prince, the marriage to eight princesses, the awakening when Mahavira arrived at Gunashilaka, the parents' grief and final consent, the renunciation, the eleven Anga studies under the elders, the long silent fasts, the climb up Vipulachala, and the final ascent — and apply it without subtraction. ⚠️ Whether Mahasena ascended specifically to Vijaya, Vaijayanta, Jayanta, or Aparajita is not individually specified; the text only tells us that of the first eight princes in the list, two each went to those four heavens in due order. The plain reading is that Dirghasena and Mahasena went to Vijaya, the next pair (Lashtadanta, Gudhadanta) to Vaijayanta, and so on — but the source does not state this assignment in so many words.

The deep point of this kind of telling is theological. The text refuses to make Mahasena's life feel different from Dirghasena's because, in the logic of liberation, the differences do not matter. What matters is the path — the alignment of right vision, knowledge, and conduct — and once two souls have aligned themselves with that path, their biographies become functionally identical even if their birth-names and palace memories differ. The sameness is not a literary shortcut; it is a metaphysical statement.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The second prince, Mahasena, lived the same life as Dirghasena and Jali — born to King Shrenika and Queen Dharini, raised in Rajagriha, ordained under Mahavira, sixteen years a monk, and reborn in one of the highest heavens.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If two brothers can walk the identical path to liberation, where in your life do you still insist that your circumstances are uniquely difficult?

With Mahadrumasena the chapter shifts. He is the first of the five princes reborn in Sarvarthasiddha — the supreme heaven of the Jain cosmos, the highest single station any embodied soul can occupy. The four sub-Anuttara heavens are also one-life-from-liberation realms, but Sarvarthasiddha is *the* peak: its inhabitants are the spiritual cream of the cosmos.

The text does not tell us why Mahadrumasena was reborn higher than his predecessors. There is no narrative of him fasting longer, meditating deeper, or studying more thoroughly. The implication, in Jain doctrine, is that the difference lay in the subtler quality of his right-vision-knowledge-conduct — the inwardness of his practice rather than its outward form. Two monks may keep identical fasts; one rises higher because his attention is purer.

This is a sobering point. The chapter does not give the reader a checklist by which to climb. It tells us that climbing happens, that some climb higher than others, and that the difference is invisible to outside observation. What is visible is the path; what determines the elevation is the quiet inward fire.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The ninth prince, Mahadrumasena, was the first of five brothers reborn in Sarvarthasiddha — the very highest heaven, only one more human life away from final liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Two people can do the same practice for the same number of years and end at different heights — what is the inner quality you bring that no one else can see?

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The twelfth prince, Mahasimhasena — "great lion-army" — was the fourth of five brothers to reach the highest heaven, Sarvarthasiddha.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The youngest in your family, the latest in your line — does that position carry hidden gifts that the elders' shadow makes hard to see?

## Adhyayana 13 — Punyasena Kumar

### SUTRA 2.A13.S1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(Prakrit reconstructed by chapter-internal formula. ⚠️ Individual Adhyayana Prakrit not given in the PDF.)

जहा दीहसेणे तहा पुण्णसेणे वि । नवरं पुण्णसेणे णामं कुमारे । उववाओ सव्वट्ठसिद्धे । ॥2.A13.S1॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jahā dīhaseṇe tahā puṇṇaseṇe vi | navaraṃ puṇṇaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre | uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe | || 2.A13.S1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

As with Dirghasena, so also with Punyasena. The only difference is that the prince's name was Punyasena, and his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā dīhaseṇe* = as with Dirghasena | *tahā puṇṇaseṇe vi* = so also with Punyasena | *navaraṃ* = the difference | *puṇṇaseṇe ṇāmaṃ kumāre* = the prince was named Punyasena (literally "army of merit"; *puṇya* = merit, virtue, auspicious karma) | *uvavāo savvaṭṭhasiddhe* = his rebirth was in Sarvarthasiddha | (rest as Dirghasena.)

**5. COMMENTARY**

Punyasena closes the chapter — the thirteenth and last of the princes, the fifth and last of the Sarvarthasiddha-bound. His name means "army of merit" or "merit-armied," a beautifully apt closing name for a chapter whose entire arc is about the conversion of inherited royal merit into the merit that lifts a soul to the highest heaven.

Among the thirteen, he stands at the end of the list as a kind of summation. The chapter began with Dirghasena, "long-armied" — the prince of long worldly reach — and ends with Punyasena, "merit-armied" — the prince whose army was inward, made not of spears but of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct. The scripture has, by listing them in this order, traced an arc: from worldly army to spiritual army, from kingdom to renunciation, from palace to forest to highest heaven.

⚠️ The Gujarati *vivechan* (PDF p. 13) makes the chapter's closing point explicit: "In this Varga, the auspicious fruit of the integrated practice of right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct has been displayed. It is well known that only the action performed in conjunction with right faith and right knowledge has the power to destroy karma." With Punyasena's ascent, the chapter closes its full circle. All thirteen are now installed in heaven, all twenty-three (including the ten of Varga 1) have completed their one-month sallekhana, and the path has been demonstrated to be available — visibly, repeatedly, in one royal generation alone.

The reader who has read carefully is meant to notice that the path has not been kept secret, that it was not reserved for a single hero. It was walked thirteen times in a single household, and twenty-three times in two adjacent chapters.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The thirteenth and final prince, Punyasena — "merit-armied" — was the last of five brothers to reach Sarvarthasiddha, completing the chapter's account of all thirteen princes who walked from palace to highest heaven.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When your life is finally listed in a single line at the end of a chapter — what would the name beside that line truly mean? "Long-armied"? Or "merit-armied"?

## Closing Note from the Editor's Vivechan (Gujarati commentary)

Two observations are added by the editor of the Gujarati edition at the close of this Varga, and both are important for the reader:

1. **The twenty-three monks of Vargas 1 and 2 each undertook *pādapopagamana* santhāra** — the highest grade of sallekhana, in which the dying monk lies motionless like a fallen tree, accepting no service, until death — and were thereby reborn in the Anuttara heavens. This detail unifies both Vargas under a single ascetic discipline and elevates the entire group of twenty-three to the highest grade of practitioners.

2. **The auspicious fruit of integrated right faith, knowledge, and conduct** is the doctrinal teaching of this Varga. Right action alone, without right vision and right knowledge, cannot destroy karma; only when all three are integrated does the practice become liberating.

3. **Manuscript variation** — the Gujarati editor notes that different handwritten copies of this Sutra show some textual variation in this Varga because of the choice between abbreviated and expanded readings (*saṃkṣipta ane vistṛta paddhati*). This is one reason the text feels uneven in detail and why scholarly editions sometimes differ on particulars.
