# Sukha Vipaak Sutra - Chapter 8: Bhadranandi - Second (ભદ્રનંદી)

**Source:** Vipaak Sutra, Second Shrutaskandha (Sukha Vipaak), Chapter 8
**Subject:** The story of Prince Bhadranandi (second) and the fruits of good karma
**Total Sutras:** 2
**Form:** Narrative prose (Ardhamagadhi Prakrit)
**Narrator:** Sudharmaswami to Jambukumar

## SUTRA [8.1]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
अट्ठमस्स उक्खेवो ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
aṭṭhamassa ukkhevo |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The introduction of the eighth chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**अट्ठमस्स** = of the eighth; **उक्खेवो** = introduction / commencement

### 5. COMMENTARY
This is the standard opening marker that announces the beginning of the eighth chapter. Each chapter in the Sukha Vipaak opens with this brief formula identifying its number in the sequence. This marker serves as a structural signpost for both the reader and the reciter. It signals that the previous story has concluded and a new narrative is about to begin. Notably, this chapter shares its protagonist's name — Bhadranandi — with Chapter 2, but the two stories involve entirely different individuals in different cities with different families and past lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
This line simply marks the start of the eighth chapter.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When we encounter a familiar name in a new context, do we bring old assumptions — or do we allow the new story to speak for itself?

## SUTRA [8.2]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
सुघोसं णयरं। देवरमणं उज्जाणं। वीरसेणो जक्खो। अज्जुणो राया। तत्तवई देवी। सिरीदेवी पामोक्खाणं पंचसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं पाणिग्गहणं। तित्थयरागमणं। पुव्वभव पुच्छा। महाघोसे णयरे। धम्मघोसे गाहावई। धम्मसीहे अणगारे पडिलाभिए जाव सिद्धे। णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स। ।। अट्ठमं अज्झयणं समत्तं ।।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
sughosaṃ ṇayaraṃ | devaramaṇaṃ ujjāṇaṃ | vīraseṇo jakkho | ajjuṇo rāyā | tattavaī devī | sirīdevī pāmokkhāṇaṃ paṃcasayāṇaṃ rāyavarakaṇṇagāṇaṃ pāṇiggahaṇaṃ | titthayarāgamaṇaṃ | puvvabhava pucchā | mahāghose ṇayare | dhammaghose gāhāvaī | dhammasīhe aṇagāre paḍilābhie jāva siddhe | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa | || aṭṭhamaṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ samattaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
"In the city of Sughosa. The Devramana garden. The guardian spirit was Virasena. The king was Arjuna. The queen was Tattavai Devi. He married Siridevi, chief among five hundred princesses. The Tirthankara arrived. The question of previous births was asked. In the city of Mahaghosha. The householder Dharmghosha. The monk Dharmasimha was encountered, leading all the way to liberation. The closing is as in the first chapter. The eighth chapter is complete."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**सुघोसं णयरं** = in the city of Sughosa; **देवरमणं उज्जाणं** = the Devramana garden; **वीरसेणो जक्खो** = the guardian spirit Virasena; **अज्जुणो राया** = King Arjuna; **तत्तवई देवी** = Queen Tattavai Devi; **सिरीदेवी पामोक्खाणं** = Siridevi, chief among; **पंचसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं** = five hundred princesses; **पाणिग्गहणं** = marriage; **तित्थयरागमणं** = the arrival of the Tirthankara; **पुव्वभव पुच्छा** = the question about previous births; **महाघोसे णयरे** = in the city of Mahaghosha; **धम्मघोसे गाहावई** = the householder Dharmghosha; **धम्मसीहे अणगारे** = the monk Dharmasimha; **पडिलाभिए** = was encountered / attained; **जाव सिद्धे** = all the way to liberation; **णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स** = the closing is as in the first (chapter); **अट्ठमं अज्झयणं समत्तं** = the eighth chapter is complete

### 5. COMMENTARY
This sutra tells the complete story of a second Prince Bhadranandi — entirely distinct from the Bhadranandi of Chapter 2. This Bhadranandi was born in the city of Sughosa (Sudhosa), where the garden was called Devramana and the guardian spirit was Virasena. His father was King Arjuna (Ajjuno) and his mother was Queen Tattavai Devi. The prince married Siridevi, who was chief among five hundred royal maidens. When Lord Mahavira arrived in the city, Bhadranandi sought teachings and asked about previous births. The Lord revealed that in a former life, Bhadranandi had been born in the city of Mahaghosha as a householder named Dharmghosha. In that life, he had encountered a monk named Dharmasimha, heard the teachings of truth, practiced generosity and moral conduct, and through the merit accumulated across lives, ultimately attained liberation. The text states the closing follows the same pattern as Chapter 1, confirming that Bhadranandi will make an end of all suffering. This repetition of the name Bhadranandi across two chapters carries a deliberate message: the path to liberation is not reserved for a unique individual. Different souls, in different cities, with different families and different past lives, all arrive at the same destination through the same practice of right conduct and renunciation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Prince Bhadranandi of Sughosa city — a different person from the Bhadranandi in Chapter 2 — was born to King Arjuna and Queen Tattavai. In his past life as the householder Dharmghosha in Mahaghosha city, he had met the monk Dharmasimha and practiced righteousness. He followed Lord Mahavira's path and was assured of liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Two souls share the same name yet walk entirely different paths to the same destination. Does this suggest that liberation depends not on who you are, but on what you practice?
