# Vipaak Sutra — Chapter 1: Mrigaputra (મૃગાપુત્ર)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary — Shri Vipaak Sutra
**Volume:** Pratham Shrutaskandha — Duhkhavipak (First Volume — Suffering Fruition)
**Chapter:** First Adhyayana — Mrigaputra
**Total Sutras:** 31
**Story:** The story of Mrigaputra, a child born utterly blind, deaf, mute, limbless, and deformed — kept hidden underground by his mother in Mrigagram city. Gautam Swami visits and, moved by compassion, asks Mahavir about the child's past. Mahavir reveals the child's previous birth as Ikaai Rathod — a cruel tax collector who tormented thousands — and traces how those evil deeds produced this life of suffering. The chapter concludes with the child's entire future: cycles of hellish births, animal existences, and eventual liberation as a monk.

### SUTRA 1.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं चंपा णामं णयरी होत्था । वण्णओ । पुण्णभद्दे चेइए । वण्णओ ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ campā nāmaṃ ṇagarī hotthā | vaṇṇao | puṇṇabhadde ceie | vaṇṇao |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time, at that period, there was a city called Champa; [its description is to be known as in the Aupapatika Sutra]. There was a garden called Purnabhadra; [its description follows likewise].

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ = at that time (the fourth period of the descending cycle); teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ = at that very period (when Mahavir was alive and teaching); campā nāmaṃ = named Champa; ṇagarī = city; hotthā = there was; vaṇṇao = description to be understood from the Aupapatika Sutra; puṇṇabhadde = Purnabhadra (name of the garden-shrine); ceie = garden with a deity's shrine; vaṇṇao = its description similarly follows

**5. COMMENTARY**

The canonical opening formula "at that time, at that period" is not mere repetition — the commentator Abhayadeva Suri explains that "time" (kāla) refers to the general era of the fourth descending cycle, and "period" (samaya) refers specifically to the span of Mahavir's earthly presence when these teachings were given. Champa was one of the great cities of ancient India, celebrated for its wealth and culture. The Aupapatika Sutra contains a full poetic description of such cities and their surrounding gardens — rather than repeat it, the text abbreviates with "vaṇṇao," trusting that the reader knows or will seek it. Purnabhadra was a famous garden outside Champa — a place with a yaksha shrine, lush with trees, flowers, and fruits of every season. Such gardens were the natural gathering places for Mahavir's religious discourses, being open, pleasant, and accessible to all.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Long ago, in a rich city called Champa, there was a beautiful garden where the story we are about to hear was first told.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do I tend to rush past the setting of a sacred story, eager for the action — or do I pause to feel the world in which it unfolds?

### SUTRA 1.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स अंतेवासी अज्जसुहम्मे णामं अणगारे जाइसंपण्णे जाव पंचहिं अणगारसईहिं सड्ढिं संपरिवुढे पुढवीं चरमाणे गामाणुगामं दूइज्जमाणे सुहंसुहेणं विहरमाणे जेणेव चंपाणयरी जेणेव पुण्णभद्दे चेइए तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता अहापडिरूवं उग्गहं उग्गिणिहइ, अहापडिरूवं उग्गहं उग्गिणिहित्ता संजमेणं तवसा अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरइ । परिसा णिग्गया । धम्मं सोच्चा णिसम्म जामेव दिसिं पाउब्भूया तामेव दिसिं पडिगया ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇassa bhagavao Mahāvīrassa aṃtevāsī Ajjasuhamme nāmaṃ aṇagāre jāisaṃpaṇṇe jāva pañcahiṃ aṇagārasaīhiṃ saḍḍhiṃ saṃparivaḍhe puḍhavīṃ caramāṇe gāmāṇugāmaṃ dūijjamāṇe suhaṃsuhēṇaṃ viharamāṇe jeṇeva Campānagarī jeṇeva Puṇṇabhadde ceie teṇeva uvāgacchhai, uvāgacchhittā ahāpaḍirūvaṃ uggahaṃ uggaṇihai, ahāpaḍirūvaṃ uggahaṃ uggaṇihittā saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe viharai | parisā ṇiggayā | dhammaṃ socchā ṇisammaṃ jāmeva disiṃ pāubbhūyā tāmeva disiṃ paḍigayā |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time, at that period, the disciple of the venerable ascetic Mahavir — the monk named Arya Sudharmashvami, endowed with noble birth, along with five hundred monks, wandering from village to village across the earth with ease and contentment — arrived at the city of Champa, at the garden of Purnabhadra; having arrived, he accepted a lodging suitable to his practice, and dwelling thus — cultivating his soul through restraint and austerity — the assembly came out, listened to the teaching, and after hearing and absorbing the dharma, each returned to whichever direction they had come from.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

samaṇassa bhagavao Mahāvīrassa aṃtevāsī = disciple of the venerable ascetic Mahavir; Ajjasuhamme = Arya Sudharmashvami (the fifth and final Ganadhar of Mahavir); jāisaṃpaṇṇe = endowed with noble birth; jāva = [qualities enumerated in the Aupapatika Sutra]; pañcahiṃ aṇagārasaīhiṃ saḍḍhiṃ = accompanied by five hundred monks; gāmāṇugāmaṃ = from village to village; suhaṃsuhēṇaṃ = with ease and contentment; viharamāṇe = wandering, sojourning; uvāgacchhai = arrived; ahāpaḍirūvaṃ uggahaṃ = lodging suitable to one's practice; saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā = through restraint and austerity; appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe = cultivating the soul; parisā ṇiggayā = the assembly came out; dhammaṃ socchā ṇisammaṃ = having heard and absorbed the teaching; paḍigayā = returned

**5. COMMENTARY**

Arya Sudharmashvami was the last surviving Ganadhar (chief disciple) of Mahavir and the living chain through which Mahavir's teachings passed into the canonical Agamas. He is the transmitter of the Vipaak Sutra. The phrase "wandering from village to village with ease and contentment" describes the ideal life of a Jain monk — never staying in one place for more than a few days (to avoid attachment), moving continuously, dependent on nothing but the natural world, practicing both restraint (refraining from all harm) and austerity (fasting, meditation, hardship). The image of the assembly streaming out of the city to hear the teaching, then quietly dispersing home after — each returning to their own direction — captures the organic, unhurried rhythm of how dharma was transmitted in ancient India.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

One of Mahavir's top disciples, Sudharmashvami, arrived at a city with five hundred monks, settled in, gave teachings, and the crowd that gathered listened and then went home.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What does it mean to truly "hear and absorb" a teaching — not just receive words, but let them change the direction I move in?

### SUTRA 1.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं अज्जसुहम्मस्स अंतेवासी अज्जजंबू णामं अणगारे सतुस्सहे जाव संजमेणं तवसा अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरइ ।
तए णं अज्जजंबू णामं अणगारे जायसड्ढे जाव जेणेव अज्जसुहम्मे अणगारे तेणेव उवागए, तिक्खुत्तो आयाहिणं पयाहिणं करेइ, करेत्ता वंदइ णमंसइ, वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता जाव पज्जुवासमाणे एवं वयासी —

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ Ajjasuhamme aṃtevāsī Ajjajaṃbū nāmaṃ aṇagāre satussahe jāva saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe viharai |
tae ṇaṃ Ajjajaṃbū nāmaṃ aṇagāre jāyasaḍḍhe jāva jeṇeva Ajjasuhamme aṇagāre teṇeva uvāgae, tikkhuṭṭo āyāhiṇaṃ payāhiṇaṃ karei, karettā vaṃdai ṇamasai, vaṃdittā ṇamasittā jāva pajjuvāsamāṇe evaṃ vayāsī —

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time, at that period, the disciple of Arya Sudharmashvami — the monk named Arya Jambushvami, full of great energy, [with all qualities enumerated], dwelling by cultivating the soul through restraint and austerity — he, filled with faith and longing, approached where the monk Arya Sudharmashvami was staying; having approached, he circumambulated three times clockwise, paid obeisance with folded hands, bowed and saluted, and — while attentively serving him — spoke thus:

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

Ajjasuhamme aṃtevāsī = disciple of Arya Sudharmashvami; Ajjajaṃbū = Arya Jambushvami (the direct disciple who preserved and transmitted the Agamas); satussahe = full of great energy and enthusiasm; jāyasaḍḍhe = having arisen with faith and desire to know; tikkhuṭṭo = three times; āyāhiṇaṃ payāhiṇaṃ = clockwise circumambulation (pradakshina); vaṃdai ṇamasai = bows and salutes with reverence; pajjuvāsamāṇe = while serving attentively, sitting in service

**5. COMMENTARY**

Arya Jambushvami was the direct disciple of Sudharmashvami and the last human being in this era to attain liberation (moksha) through the path of knowledge and renunciation. He represents the final link in the direct chain of transmission from Mahavir himself. The triple clockwise circumambulation before asking a question is not empty ritual — it embodies the Jain virtue of vinaya (humility before the teacher), which is itself considered a prerequisite for true learning. One does not approach a Ganadhar with curiosity alone; one prepares the inner vessel through reverence. The phrase "filled with faith and longing" (jāyasaḍḍhe) describes the psychological state ideal for receiving wisdom — not skepticism, not passivity, but awakened, faithful yearning.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Sudharmashvami's student Jambushvami came to his teacher with great respect — circling him three times, bowing deeply — and prepared to ask a question.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Before I seek knowledge, do I prepare myself — clearing pride, slowing down, cultivating the inner quietness that makes real understanding possible?

### SUTRA 1.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं दसमस्स अंगस्स पण्हावागरणस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते, एक्कारसमस्स णं भंते ! अंगस्स विवागसुयस्स समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jai ṇaṃ bhaṃte! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ dasamassa aṃgassa paṇhāvāgaraṇassa ayamatthe paṇṇatte, ekkārasamassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte! aṃgassa vivāgasuyassa samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ ke atthe paṇṇatte?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Venerable sir, if the venerable ascetic Mahavir — who has attained liberation — has taught this meaning in the tenth scripture called the Prashnavyakarana, then what meaning, venerable sir, has the venerable ascetic Mahavir — who has attained liberation — taught in the eleventh scripture called the Vipaak Sutra?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

jai ṇaṃ bhaṃte = venerable sir, if it is so; samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ = by the venerable ascetic Mahavir; jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ = who has attained [liberation] (a reverential abbreviation covering Mahavir's full titles and attainments); dasamassa aṃgassa = tenth scripture (Anga); paṇhāvāgaraṇassa = Prashnavyakarana (Questions and Explanations); ayamatthe paṇṇatte = this meaning has been taught; ekkārasamassa = the eleventh; vivāgasuyassa = Vipaak Sutra (Scripture of Karmic Fruition); ke atthe paṇṇatte = what meaning has been taught?

**5. COMMENTARY**

Jambushvami's question is elegantly framed — acknowledging what the tenth Anga (Prashnavyakarana) teaches before asking about the eleventh (Vipaak Sutra). The Jain canonical corpus of twelve Angas represents a systematic body of knowledge; the question invites Sudharmashvami to summarize the purpose and substance of the entire Vipaak Sutra. The title "Vipaak Sutra" itself means "scripture of karmic ripening" — vipaak means the fruition or maturation of previously accumulated karma. This question is the frame through which the entire scripture unfolds; every story that follows is Sudharmashvami's answer.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Jambushvami asked his teacher: "The tenth scripture has been explained — now what is the eleventh scripture, the Vipaak Sutra, about?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I curious about why my life looks the way it does — could my circumstances today be the "answer" to choices I once made without fully understanding their weight?

### SUTRA 1.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं अज्जसुहम्मे अणगारे जंबुं अणगारं एवं वयासी— एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं एक्कारसमस्स अंगस्स विवागसुयस्स दो सुयक्खंधा पण्णत्ता, तं जहा— दुहिव्वागा य सुहिव्वागा य ।
जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं एक्कारसमस्स अंगस्स विवागसुयस्स दो सुयक्खंधा पण्णत्ता, तं जहा— दुहिव्वागा य सुहिव्वागा य । पढमस्स णं भंते ! सुयक्खंधस्स दुहिव्वागाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं कइ अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता ?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ Ajjasuhamme aṇagāre Jaṃbuṃ aṇagāraṃ evaṃ vayāsī— evaṃ khalu Jaṃbū! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ ekkārasamassa aṃgassa vivāgasuyassa do suyakkhaṃdhā paṇṇattā, taṃ jahā— duhivvāgā ya suhivvāgā ya |
jai ṇaṃ bhaṃte! ... do suyakkhaṃdhā paṇṇattā, taṃ jahā— duhivvāgā ya suhivvāgā ya | padhamassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte! suyakkhaṃdhassa duhivvāgāṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ kai ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then the monk Arya Sudharmashvami spoke to the monk Jambushvami thus: "Know this well, Jambushvami! The venerable ascetic Mahavir has taught that the eleventh scripture, the Vipaak Sutra, has two volumes — namely, the Volume of Suffering Fruition and the Volume of Happiness Fruition." [Jambushvami then asks:] "If the Vipaak Sutra has two volumes — Suffering Fruition and Happiness Fruition — then, venerable sir, how many chapters has the venerable ascetic Mahavir taught in the first volume, the Volume of Suffering Fruition?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

evaṃ khalu Jaṃbū = know this truly, Jambushvami; do suyakkhaṃdhā = two volumes (Shrutaskandhas); duhivvāgā = Duhkhavipak (fruition of suffering, i.e., bad karma bearing painful results); suhivvāgā = Sukhavipak (fruition of happiness, i.e., good karma bearing joyful results); padhamassa suyakkhaṃdhassa = first volume; kai ajjhayaṇā = how many chapters (Adhyayanas)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Vipaak Sutra's division into two equal volumes is itself a profound philosophical statement: karma ripens in both directions. The first volume (Duhkhavipak) presents ten stories of beings experiencing severe suffering as the direct fruit of evil deeds performed in past lives. The second volume (Sukhavipak) presents ten stories of beings experiencing great happiness and prosperity as the fruit of virtuous deeds. Together they demonstrate that karma is not punishment or reward handed out by a deity — it is a natural, impersonal law of causation. What we experience today is the fruition of choices already made; what we choose today is the seed of what we will experience in lives to come. This law is not pessimistic — it is empowering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Sudharmashvami explained that the Vipaak Sutra has two parts: stories of people suffering because of past bad deeds, and stories of people thriving because of past good deeds.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If both suffering and happiness are fruits of past choices, what does that mean for how I relate to difficulty — as punishment, or as information?

### SUTRA 1.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं अज्जसुहम्मे अणगारे जंबुं अणगारं एवं वयासी— एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं दुहिव्वागाणं दस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता, तं जहा—
मियापुत्ते य उज्झियए, अभग्ग सगडे बहुस्सई णंदी ।
ऊंबर सोरियदत्ते य, देवदत्ता य अंजू य ।।१।।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ Ajjasuhamme aṇagāre Jaṃbuṃ aṇagāraṃ evaṃ vayāsī— evaṃ khalu Jaṃbū! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ duhivvāgāṇaṃ dasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā, taṃ jahā—
miyāputte ya ujjhiyae, abhagga sagaḍe bahussaī ṇaṃdī |
ūṃbara soriadatte ya, devadattā ya aṃjū ya || 1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then the monk Arya Sudharmashvami spoke to the monk Jambushvami thus: "Know this, Jambushvami! The venerable ascetic Mahavir has taught ten chapters in the Volume of Suffering Fruition, namely: Mrigaputra, Ujjhitaka, Abhagna, Shakata, Brihaspati, Nandi, Umbara, Shoriadatta, Devadatta, and Anju."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

duhivvāgāṇaṃ dasa ajjhayaṇā = ten chapters in the Suffering Fruition volume; miyāputte = Mrigaputra (the born-blind son); ujjhiyae = Ujjhitaka (the abandoned one); abhagga = Abhagna (the unbroken); sagaḍe = Shakata (the cart-maker's son); bahussaī = Brihaspati (the learned one); ṇaṃdī = Nandi; ūṃbara = Umbara; soriadatte = Shoriadatta (gift of the sun); devadattā = Devadatta (gift of the gods); aṃjū = Anju

**5. COMMENTARY**

The ten names listed here are the ten protagonists of the Duhkhavipak volume — each one a being enduring extraordinary suffering in their present life as the direct consequence of terrible deeds performed in a previous birth. The names are listed in a single mnemonic verse, which was how ancient students memorized the structure of large scriptures before writing was in common use. Each story functions as a case study in the mechanics of karma: not karma as abstract philosophy, but karma made visible, tangible, and emotionally real through narrative. These are not parables — the Jain tradition holds them as actual accounts of real beings, spoken by an omniscient being who perceived their histories directly.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are ten stories of suffering in this volume — Mrigaputra is first; the others are Ujjhitaka, Abhagna, Shakata, Brihaspati, Nandi, Umbara, Shoriadatta, Devadatta, and Anju.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Each of these names represents a real life of suffering with a real cause behind it — can I extend the same compassion to suffering people around me today, knowing that every condition has a history I cannot see?

### SUTRA 1.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं दुहिव्वागाणं दस अज्झयणा पण्णत्ता, तं जहा— मियापुत्ते य जाव अंजू य । पढमस्स णं भंते ! अज्झयणस्स दुहिव्वागाणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ?
तए णं से सुहम्मे अणगारे जंबुं अणगारं एवं वयासी— एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं मियग्गामे णामं णयरे होत्था । वण्णओ । तस्स णं मियग्गामस्स णयरस्स बहिया उत्तरपुरत्थिमे दिसीभाए चंदणपायवे णामं उज्जाणे होत्था । सव्वोउय पुप्फ फल समिद्धे, वण्णओ । तत्थ णं सुधम्मस्स जक्खस्स जक्खायतणे होत्था । चिराईए जहा पुण्णभद्दे ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jai ṇaṃ bhaṃte! ... dasa ajjhayaṇā paṇṇattā... padhamassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte! ajjhayaṇassa duhivvāgāṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpattēṇaṃ ke atthe paṇṇatte?

Then the monk Sudharmashvami spoke to the monk Jambushvami thus: "Know this, Jambushvami! At that time, at that period, there was a city called Mrigagram; [description follows]. Outside that city, to the north-east, there was a garden called Chandanapādapa — rich with flowers and fruits of every season; [description follows]. **5. COMMENTARY**

With this sutra, the inner narrative begins — the actual story of Chapter 1 opens within the outer frame of the Jambushvami-Sudharmashvami dialogue. Mrigagram (literally "deer-village") was a real city, and its description follows standard canonical patterns for a prosperous ancient Indian settlement. The north-east direction (Ishana corner) was considered auspicious in both Jain and Vedic traditions — gardens, shrines, and sacred spaces were typically situated there. The Chandanapādapa garden, fragrant with sandalwood trees, blooming in all seasons, represents the world into which Mahavir is about to come — a beautiful outer setting that will contrast sharply with the underground suffering about to be revealed within the same city.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The story of Mrigaputra takes place in a city called Mrigagram, which had a lovely garden to the north-east — and this is where everything happens.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Beauty and suffering often exist in the same place, in the same city — am I aware of both, or do I notice only what is visible on the surface?

### SUTRA 1.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In that city of Mrigagram, there lived a Kshatriya king named Vijay; [his description follows]. His queen was named Mriga — complete and flawless [in beauty and quality]; [description follows]. This sutra delivers the story's central image with devastating directness: a child who is blind, deaf, mute, limbless, and deformed — hidden underground by a mother too ashamed or frightened to let the world know he exists. The phrase "only the faintest outlines of where limbs should be" conveys a body that is human in form but barely so — a kind of living sketch of a person. From the Jain philosophical perspective, this is precisely the point: this being is a human soul — an immortal, conscious entity — experiencing the specific fruits of specific evil karmas, housed in a body that reflects those karmas directly. The mother's act of hiding him in an underground chamber (bhūmighar, literally "earth-house") while secretly feeding him is portrayed not as cruelty but as a desperate, loving concealment. She keeps him alive — but invisibly, below the world.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

King Vijay's son, Mrigaputra, was born with no working limbs or senses — completely helpless — and his mother kept him hidden in a secret underground room.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there something in me that I keep hidden underground — some part of my inner life I am too afraid or ashamed to bring into the light?

At that time, at that period, Mahavir's senior-most disciple, the monk named Indrabhuti (Gautam Swami), was also present. Gautam Swami saw the born-blind man and — stirred with faithful curiosity — asked Mahavir: "Venerable sir, is there anyone who is blind from birth and also of born-blind form [i.e., completely without any limbs]?" "Yes, there is." "Venerable sir, how so — where is such a being who is born-blind and of born-blind form?" "Know this, Gautam! In this very city of Mrigagram, there is a child named Mrigaputra — son of King Vijay the Kshatriya and Queen Mrigadevi — who is blind from birth and of born-blind form: he has no hands, no feet, no ears, no eyes, no nose — only the faintest outlines of where those limbs and organs should be — and Queen Mrigadevi is secretly nourishing and tending to him." Then Gautam Swami bowed to Mahavir and said: "Venerable sir, with your permission I wish to go and see the child Mrigaputra." Mahavir replied: "As you please, beloved of the gods."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

jeṭṭhe aṃtevāsī = senior-most disciple; Iṃdabhūī nāmaṃ aṇagāre = the monk named Indrabhuti, known as Gautam Swami; jāiaṃdhārūve = of a body that is entirely without limbs (the form of a born-blind being); haṃtā atthi = yes, there is; abbhaṇuṇṇāe samāṇe = with your permission; pāssittae = to see, to visit; ahāsuhaṃ devāṇuppiyā = as you please, beloved of the gods (Mahavir's gracious permission)

**5. COMMENTARY**

Gautam Swami sees the blind beggar moving through the crowd and asks Mahavir a question that goes deeper than curiosity about that man — it opens up to reveal a hidden reality within the same city. Mahavir's answer is omniscient knowledge made specific: there is a child, right here, in this very city, whose condition is even more extreme than the blind beggar's. The phrase "born-blind form" (jāiaṃdhārūve) refers not just to blindness but to a body so deformed that it has only the trace-outline of human form — a being in human birth who is effectively entirely helpless. Gautam's response — "with your permission, I wish to go and see him" — is the compassionate impulse of a great soul: he is not satisfied by the description; he wants to be present with the suffering itself. Mahavir's simple "as you please" is full of trust.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Gautam Swami noticed the blind beggar and asked Mahavir about him — and Mahavir revealed there was actually a child in this very city in a far worse condition, hidden underground, and Gautam immediately asked permission to go see him.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I hear about someone suffering, is my instinct to keep a safe distance and feel sympathetic — or to actually go, to be present, to witness?

### SUTRA 1.13

tae ṇaṃ se bhagavaṃ Goyame samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā Mahāvīreṇaṃ abbhaṇuṇṇāe samāṇe haṭṭuṭṭhe samaṇassa bhagavao Mahāvīrassa aṃtiyāo paḍiṇikkhamai, paḍiṇikkhamittā aturiyaṃ jāva riyaṃ sohemāṇe jeṇeva Miyaggāme ṇayare teṇeva uvāgacchhai, uvāgacchhittā Miyaggāmaṃ ṇayaraṃ aṇupivisai, aṇuppivisittā Miyaggāmassa ṇayarassa majjhamajjheṇaṃ ṇigacchai ṇigacchhittā jeṇeva Miyādevīe gihe teṇeva uvāgacchhai |

Then Gautam Swami — having received Mahavir's permission, joyful and pleased — departed from Mahavir's presence; departing, he moved unhurriedly, carefully observing the path of proper conduct, and arrived at the city of Mrigagram; having entered, he passed through the middle of Mrigagram, and arrived at Queen Mrigadevi's house.

abbhaṇuṇṇāe samāṇe = having received permission; haṭṭuṭṭhe = joyful and delighted (lit. "with hair standing on end in joy"); paḍiṇikkhamai = departed from; aturiyaṃ = without haste, unhurriedly; riyaṃ sohemāṇe = carefully cleaning/observing the path (a monk's care not to harm small creatures underfoot); aṇupivisai = entered; majjhamajjheṇaṃ = through the very middle of the city; Miyādevīe gihe = Mrigadevi's house

The description of his walking — "unhurriedly, carefully observing the path" — is a technical term for the Jain monk's gait (īryāsamiti), the practice of walking with eyes cast down to avoid stepping on insects, moving slowly and mindfully. Even in moving toward a compassionate act, the monk does not abandon mindfulness. Every step is practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Gautam Swami, happy to have Mahavir's permission, walked carefully through the city and arrived at Mrigadevi's house.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I move toward an act of compassion with joy rather than heaviness — understanding that being present for suffering is itself a form of grace?

### SUTRA 1.14

tae ṇaṃ sā Miyādevī bhagavaṃ Goyamaṃ ejjamāṇaṃ pāssai, pāssittā haṭṭuṭṭhe jāva evaṃ vayāsī— saṃdisaṃtu ṇaṃ devāṇuppiyā! kimāgamaṇappoyaṇaṃ? tae ṇaṃ bhagavaṃ Goyame Miyādeviṃ evaṃ vayāsī— ahaṃ ṇaṃ devāṇuppie! tava puttaṃ pāsiuṃ havvamāgae | tae ṇaṃ sā Miyādevī Miyāputtassa dāragassa aṇumaggajāyae cattāri putte savvālaṃkāra vibhūsie karei, karettā bhagavao Goyamassa pāesu pāḍei, pāḍettā evaṃ vayāsī— ee ṇaṃ bhaṃte! mama putte, pāssaha |

Queen Mrigadevi, seeing Gautam Swami approaching, was overjoyed and said: "Please tell me, beloved of the gods — what is the purpose of your coming?" Gautam Swami replied: "Dear lady, I have come to see your son." Then Queen Mrigadevi dressed Mrigaputra's four younger brothers — born after him — in full ornaments and finery, placed them at Gautam Swami's feet, and said: "Venerable sir, these are my sons — please look at them."

She cannot bring herself to say "Mrigaputra" out loud, cannot acknowledge the hidden child underground. It is an act of denial born of shame and grief. She offers what is beautiful and acceptable in place of what is real and hidden. Gautam Swami sees through it instantly — but his response in the next sutra is not rebuke; it is gentle, precise, and compassionate.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When Gautam said he'd come to see her son, Mrigadevi dressed up her four other sons and showed them off — she couldn't bring herself to mention the child hidden underground.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When asked to show what is real and hidden within me, do I present only the polished, acceptable versions of myself — the "four dressed-up sons" — while keeping the truth locked underground?

### SUTRA 1.15

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se bhagavaṃ Goyame Miyādeviṃ evaṃ vayāsī— ṇo khalu devāṇuppie! ahaṃ ee tava putte pāsiuṃ havvamāgae | tattha ṇaṃ je se tava jeṭṭhe Miyāputte dārae jāiaṃdhe jāiaṃdhārūve, jaṃ ṇaṃ tumaṃ rahassiyaṃsi bhūmigharaṃsi rahassieṇaṃ bhattapāṇeṇaṃ piṃḍajāgaramāṇī piṃḍajāgaramāṇī viharasi, taṃ ṇaṃ ahaṃ pāsiuṃ havvamāgae | tae ṇaṃ sā Miyādevī bhagavaṃ Goyamaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— se ke ṇaṃ Goyamā! se tahārūve ṇāṇī vā tavassī vā, jeṇaṃ esamatthe mama tāva rahassīkae tubbhaṃ havvamakkhāe, jāo ṇaṃ tubbhe jāṇaha? tae ṇaṃ bhagavaṃ Goyame Miyādeviṃ evaṃ vayāsī— evaṃ khalu devāṇuppie! mama dhammāyarie samaṇe bhagavaṃ Mahāvīre tahārūve ṇāṇī vā tavassī vā, jeṇaṃ esamatthe tava tāva rahassīkae mama havvamakkhāe tao ṇaṃ ahaṃ jāṇāmi |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Gautam Swami said to Mrigadevi: "No, dear lady — it is not these sons I have come to see. It is your eldest son Mrigaputra — the one who is blind from birth and of born-blind form, whom you secretly tend in a hidden underground chamber, secretly nourishing him with food and water — it is him I have come to see." Mrigadevi said: "Gautam! Who could be such a knower or ascetic that he has told you what I have kept so deeply secret? How do you know?" Gautam Swami replied: "Dear lady — my spiritual preceptor, the venerable ascetic Mahavir, is exactly such a knower and ascetic; it is he who has revealed to me what you had kept secret, and that is how I know."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

ṇo khalu = no, certainly not; jeṭṭhe = eldest; rahassīkae = kept secret; tubbhaṃ havvamakkhāe = told to you directly; tahārūve ṇāṇī = such a knower (one with omniscient knowledge); tavassī = ascetic (one who has attained knowledge through austerity); dhammāyarie = spiritual preceptor (dharma-teacher); mama havvamakkhāe = has told me directly; tao ṇaṃ ahaṃ jāṇāmi = and that is how I know

**5. COMMENTARY**

Gautam's precision is stunning — he names the child, describes his exact condition, and mentions the underground chamber — details no ordinary person could know. Mrigadevi's reaction is not anger but astonishment: "Who could be such a knower?" Her question is itself an opening — the first crack in the wall of secrecy and shame. Gautam's answer is the pivot of the sutra: he attributes his knowledge entirely to Mahavir, calling him "my spiritual preceptor, the venerable ascetic Mahavir — exactly such a knower." In this one exchange, the omniscience of the Tirthankara is demonstrated not through abstract argument but through lived reality: he knew what no one else knew, and that knowledge has led a compassionate disciple to the door of a suffering child. Omniscience in Jain thought is not a divine power — it is the natural state of a soul entirely freed from karmic obscuration.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Gautam said, "No — I came to see your hidden son Mrigaputra, the one underground." Mrigadevi was shocked: "How do you know?!" And Gautam said: "My teacher Mahavir told me — he knows everything."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there something in my life I've kept so hidden, so deep underground, that I've started to believe no one could ever know — and what would it mean if wisdom could see it clearly?

Just then, it was time to feed Mrigaputra, so Mrigadevi packed a cart full of food, changed her clothes, and said "follow me" — and Gautam walked quietly behind her.

When I love someone whose condition causes me shame or grief, do I still show up faithfully — still prepare, still nourish, still care — even when no one is watching?

Mrigadevi, pulling the cart along, arrived at the underground chamber; having arrived, she bound her face with a four-layered cloth and then said to Gautam Swami: "Venerable sir, you too must cover your nose and mouth with your mouth-cloth." And Gautam Swami, hearing this from Mrigadevi, covered his mouth with his mouth-cloth (muhapatti).

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

kaṭṭusagaḍiyaṃ aṇukaḍḍumāṇī = pulling the wooden cart along; bhūmaighare = at the underground chamber; cauppuḍeṇaṃ vattheṇaṃ = with a four-layered cloth; muhaṃ baṃdhei = covered her mouth/nose; muhapottiyāe = with the mouth-cloth (muhapatti — the cloth monks carry for the same purpose); muhaṃ baṃdhaha = please cover your mouth/nose

**5. COMMENTARY**

The four-layered cloth Mrigadevi wraps around her face before opening the underground chamber is a direct indication of the terrible stench that will emanate from within. Jain monks carry a muhapatti (small mouth-cloth) as part of their standard equipment — originally to prevent accidentally inhaling small insects, but here used to shield against an overpowering smell. The fact that even a monk trained in austerity needs to cover his nose to enter this space tells us something about the severity of what is behind that door. Mrigadevi has done this countless times, alone, in secret — covering her face, opening the chamber, feeding her child in stench and darkness. The enormity of her daily act of love becomes visible here.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When they reached the underground room, Mrigadevi covered her nose with a thick cloth and told Gautam to cover his too — because the smell inside was overwhelming.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there places in my own inner life I approach only with a kind of protective covering — afraid to open them fully and breathe what is actually there?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Mrigadevi, turning her face away, opened the door of the underground chamber. A smell then came out — and what a smell: was it like the stench of a dead snake, a dead cow, a dead dog, a dead cat, a dead person, a dead buffalo, a dead rat, a dead horse, a dead elephant, a dead lion, a dead tiger, a dead leopard, a dead panther — putrefied, rotted, indescribably foul, swarming with maggots, filthy, dissolved, revolting, fit to horrify even the sight? No — the text declares: the smell was far worse than any of those — more repulsive, more unbearable, more unpleasant, more disagreeable, more abhorrent than any of those. Then the child Mrigaputra — overwhelmed by the smell of the abundant food — became unconsciously drawn to it; he consumed the abundant solid food, liquids, snacks, and delicacies from that state of near-unconscious craving; and as soon as he ate it, the food immediately transformed and decayed — and then converted into pus and blood; and he consumed even that — the pus and blood — as his food.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

paramuhi = turning the face away (because of the anticipated stench); bhūmigharassa duvāraṃ vihāḍei = opened the door of the underground chamber; gaṃdhe ṇiggacchai = a smell came out; ahimaḍe i vā = like a dead snake; gomaḍe = dead cow; sīhamaḍe = dead lion; vaggamaḍe = dead tiger; kimijālāula = swarming with maggots; asuhi-vilīṇa = filthy, dissolved; bībhaccha darisaṇijje = revolting even to look at; aṇiṭṭatarāe = even more repulsive than those; amaṇāmatarāe = even more abhorrent; gaṃdheṇaṃ abhihūe = overwhelmed by the smell of food; mucchie = drawn in a near-unconscious state of craving; āsaeṇaṃ āhārei = consumed reflexively; khippāmeva viddhaṃsei = immediately disintegrated/decayed; pūyatāe ya soṇiyatāe ya pariṇamai = transformed into pus and blood; pūyaṃ ca soṇiyaṃ ca āhārei = and he ate even the pus and blood

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is perhaps the most viscerally confronting passage in the entire chapter — and it is written that way deliberately. The Vipaak Sutra does not flinch. The elaborate list of dead animals — snake, cow, dog, cat, human, buffalo, rat, horse, elephant, lion, tiger, leopard, panther — is built up precisely to then demolish it: no, none of those, not even close. This is worse. The stench of Mrigaputra's chamber is beyond any ordinary category of decay. The food turning instantly to pus and blood in his body — the disease within him (called "bhasmark" in the commentary, a condition that causes everything consumed to immediately decompose) — is the karmic body made fully visible. He eats, but what he receives is rot. This is the fruit of past deeds, rendered in flesh and stone. And yet — in the midst of this horror — the child is alive. He feels. He eats. He exists. The soul within is still present, still experiencing. That is the Jain teaching: no condition, no matter how terrible, extinguishes the soul.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When the underground room opened, the smell was so horrifying it was beyond anything imaginable — and inside, Mrigaputra ate the food that was brought, but it turned to pus and blood in his body the moment he swallowed it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a way in which the "nourishment" I seek — the comfort, approval, or pleasure I consume — is itself transforming into something that does not truly sustain me?

I have never before seen a hell-being directly — but truly, this person is experiencing pain identical to that of a hell-being, right here before my eyes." He sees a being experiencing the fruition of accumulated karma — and his first instinct is not pity that maintains distance, but inquiry that seeks understanding. His comparison to a hell-being is precise: in Jain cosmology, hell-beings (nāraki) experience the most intense suffering possible without dying; Mrigaputra's pain — the stench, the bodily disintegration, the consumption of pus and blood — mirrors that infernal experience within a human birth. The series of questions Gautam asks — "Who was he? What was his name? Where did he live? What did he do?" — are not mere curiosity. They are the questions that karmic understanding demands: every effect has a traceable cause. Mahavir's omniscience can trace that cause across births.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I see suffering that seems extreme or "unfair," do I ask deeper questions — or do I stop at the surface, without seeking understanding?

### SUTRA 1.20

In that city ruled a king named Dhanavati; [description follows]. Ikaai Rathod was not a demon or a monster by appearance — he was an administrative official, a rashtrakuta: a government tax-collector and regional administrator. His evil lay entirely in how he used that position. The cascade of compound words — irreligious, following irreligion, speaking irreligion, looking through irreligion, delighting in irreligion, behaving against all righteousness — is the Agama's way of showing a soul entirely inverted from the good. He was not merely indifferent to dharma; he was actively, comprehensively opposed to it in every dimension of his life. And he held power over five hundred villages — which means five hundred communities lay under his authority. What he chose to do with that power is what created Mrigaputra.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Mahavir began to tell the story: in a past life, Mrigaputra was a powerful government official named Ikaai Rathod who ran five hundred villages — and he was completely, thoroughly evil in every way.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Power and position are morally neutral — it is what I do with authority that plants seeds of future experience. What am I planting today?

### SUTRA 1.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से इक्काई रटुकूडे विजयवद्धमाणस्स खेडस्स पंच गामसयाइं बहूहिं करेहि य भरेहि य विद्धीहि य उक्कोडाहि य पराभवेहि य देज्जेहि य भंजेहि य कुंतेहि य लंछपोसेहि य आलीवणेहि य पंथकोट्टिहि य ओवीलेमाणे ओवीलेमाणे, विहम्मेमाणे विहम्मेमाणे, तज्जेमाणे तज्जेमाणे, तालेमाणे तालेमाणे, णिठुणे करेमाणे करेमाणे विहरइ ।
तए णं से इक्काई रटुकूडे विजयवद्धमाणस्स खेडस्स बहूणं राईसर-तलवर-माडंबिय-कोडुंबिय-इभ-सेट्ठि-सेणावइ-सत्थवाहाणं अण्णेसिं च बहूणं गामेल्लग-पुरिसाणं बहुसु कज्जेसु य कारणेसु य मंतेसु य गुज्झेसु य णिच्छएसु य ववहारेसु य सुणमाणे भणइ ण सुणेमि, असुणमाणे भणइ सुणेमि एवं पस्समाणे, भासमाणे, गिण्हमाणे जाणेमाणे । तए णं से इक्काई रटुकूडे एयकम्मे एयप्पहाणे एयविज्जे एयसमायारे सुबहुं पावकम्मं किलकलुसं समज्जिणमाणे विहरइ ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se Ikkāī raṭukūḍe Vijayavaddhamāṇassa kheḍassa paṃca gāmasayāiṃ bahūhiṃ karehiṃ ya bharehiṃ ya viḍḍhīhiṃ ya ukkōḍāhiṃ ya parābhavehiṃ ya dejjehiṃ ya bhaṃjehiṃ ya kuṃtehiṃ ya laṃchposehiṃ ya ālivvaṇehiṃ ya paṃthakōṭṭihiṃ ya ovīlemāṇe ovīlemāṇe, vihamemāṇe vihamemāṇe, tajjemāṇe tajjemāṇe, tālemāṇe tālemāṇe, ṇiṭhuṇe karemāṇe karemāṇe viharai | tae ṇaṃ se Ikkāī raṭukūḍe ... bahūṇaṃ rāīsara-talavara-māḍaṃbiya-koḍuṃbiya-ibha-seṭṭhi-seṇāvai-satthavāhāṇaṃ ... suṇamāṇe bhaṇai ṇa suṇemi, asuṇamāṇe bhaṇai suṇemi evaṃ passamāṇe, bhāsamāṇe, giṇhamāṇe jāṇemāṇe | tae ṇaṃ se Ikkāī raṭukūḍe eyakamme eyapphāṇe eyavijje eyasamāyāre subāhuṃ pāvakammaṃ kilakalusaṃ samajjiṇamāṇe viharai |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That Ikaai Rathod oppressed the five hundred villages of the Vijayavardhaman township in many ways — through heavy taxation, unjust levies, illegal surcharges, corruption, humiliation, extortion, destruction of livelihoods, false branding, slandering, and harassment of travelers — oppressing and oppressing, violating and violating, threatening and threatening, beating and beating, being merciless and merciless. And in matters of many noblemen, chiefs, landowners, wealthy families, merchants, generals, and caravan leaders, as well as village common-folk — in affairs, causes, councils, secrets, decisions, and business dealings — when he had heard something, he said "I did not hear"; when he had not heard, he said "I heard"; similarly with seeing, speaking, taking, and knowing — he lied in every dimension. Thus, with this single pursuit, this single goal, this single practice, this single conduct — accumulating vast amounts of sinful, defiling karma — he passed his days.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

karehiṃ = through taxation (kara); bharehiṃ = forced levies; viḍḍhīhiṃ = illegal surcharges/interest; ukkōḍāhiṃ = bribery and corruption; parābhavehiṃ = humiliation and subjugation; dejjehiṃ = through payments demanded by force; bhaṃjehiṃ = destruction; kuṃtehiṃ = branding with false marks; laṃchposehiṃ = branding cattle and goods unfairly; ālivvaṇehiṃ = slander; paṃthakōṭṭihiṃ = robbing travelers on roads; ovīlemāṇe = oppressing; tajjemāṇe = threatening; ṭālemāṇe = beating; ṇiṭhuṇe = cruelly, mercilessly; suṇamāṇe bhaṇai ṇa suṇemi = "I heard [it]" says "I didn't hear"; pāvakammaṃ kilakalusaṃ = sinful, defiling karma; samajjiṇamāṇe = accumulating

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra presents an extraordinarily detailed list of the specific methods by which Ikaai Rathod oppressed his subjects — taxation, illegal levies, bribery, extortion, humiliation, slandering, highway robbery, and more. What is most striking is the systematic nature of his lying: in every possible dimension of knowing and speaking — hearing, seeing, speaking, receiving, deciding — he said the opposite of truth. This is not casual dishonesty; it is the total inversion of truth as a way of life. In Jain ethics, truthfulness (satya) is one of the five great vows precisely because untruth, when practiced systematically, corrupts not just relationships but the very soul. Ikaai Rathod's accumulated karma from this comprehensive pattern of cruelty and deception is what produces Mrigaputra — a being in whom every sense organ and limb is absent or non-functional; a being who cannot see, hear, speak, or move. The punishment is, in the most precise sense, the fruit of the crime.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Ikaai Rathod tortured the five hundred villages under him in every way imaginable — taking bribes, beating people, lying constantly about everything — and all this evil built up into the karma that became Mrigaputra's suffering.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Every lie I tell, every person I exploit, every act of cruelty I justify — these are not separate events; they are accumulating into the shape of my future. What shape am I building?

### SUTRA 1.22

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं तस्स रटुकूडस्स अण्णया कयाइ सरीरगंसि जमगसमगमेव सोलस रोगायंका पाउब्भूया । तं जहा—
सासे कासे जरे दाहे, कुच्छिसूले भगंदरे ।
अरिसे अजीरए दिट्ठी, मद्धसूले अकारए ।।
अच्छिवेयणा कण्णवेयणा कंडू उयरे कोढे ।।
तए णं से इक्काई रटुकूडे सोलसहिं रोगायंकेहिं अभिभूए समाणे कोडुंबिय पुरिसे सद्दावेइ, सद्दावित्ता एवं वयासी— गच्छह णं तुब्भे देवाणुप्पिया ! विजय-वद्धमाणे खेडे सिंगाडग-तिग-चउक्क-चच्चर-महापह-पहेसु महया महया सद्देणं उग्घोसेमाणा उग्घोसेमाणा एवं वयह— इह खलु देवाणुप्पिया ! इक्काई रटुकूडस्स सरीरगंसि सोलस रोगायंका पाउब्भूया, तं जहा— सासे कासे जाव कोढे, तं जो णं इच्छइ देवाणुप्पिया ! वेज्जो वा वेज्जपुत्तो वा जाणओ वा जाणयपुत्तो वा तेगिच्छिओ वा तेगिच्छियपुत्तो वा इक्काई रटुकूडस्स तेसिं सोलसण्हं रोगायंकाणं एगमवि रोगायंकं उवसामितए, तस्स णं इक्काई रटुकूडे विउलं अत्थसंपयाणं दलयइ । दोच्चं पि तच्चं पि उग्घोसेह, उग्घोसित्ता मम एयमाणत्तियं पच्चप्पिणह ।
तए णं ते कोडुंबियपुरिसा जाव तमाणत्तियं पच्चप्पिणंति !

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ tassa raṭukūḍassa aṇṇayā kayāi sarīragaṃsi jamagasamagameva solas rogāyaṃkā pāubbhūyā | taṃ jahā—
sāse kāse jare dāhe, kucchisūle bhagaṃdare |
arise ajīrae diṭṭhī, maddhasūle akārae ||
acchiveyaṇā kaṇṇaveyaṇā kaṃḍū uyare koḍhe ||
tae ṇaṃ se Ikkāī raṭukūḍe solasahiṃ rogāyaṃkehiṃ abhibhūe samāṇe koḍuṃbiya puruse saddāvei, saddāvittā evaṃ vayāsī— gacchaha ṇaṃ tubbhe devāṇuppiyā! Vijayavaddhamāṇe kheḍe siṃgāḍaga-tiga-caukkka-caccara-mahāpaha-pahēsu mahayā mahayā saddēṇaṃ ugghōsemāṇā ugghōsemāṇā evaṃ vayaha— iha khalu devāṇuppiyā! Ikkāī raṭukūḍassa sarīragaṃsi solas rogāyaṃkā pāubbhūyā... taṃ jo ṇaṃ icchhai devāṇuppiyā! vejjo vā vejjaputto vā jāṇao vā jāṇayaputto vā tegicchio vā tegicchiyaputto vā Ikkāī raṭukūḍassa tesinṃ solasaṇhaṃ rogāyaṃkāṇaṃ egamavi rogāyaṃkaṃ uvasāmitae, tassa ṇaṃ Ikkāī raṭukūḍe viulaṃ atthasaṃpayāṇaṃ dalayai | doccaṃ pi taccaṃ pi ugghōseha... |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then, suddenly one day, sixteen incurable diseases arose simultaneously in Ikaai Rathod's body — namely: (1) shortness of breath, (2) cough, (3) fever, (4) burning sensation, (5) abdominal colic, (6) fistula, (7) piles, (8) indigestion, (9) eye-pain/disease, (10) headache, (11) loss of appetite, (12) eye-nerve pain, (13) ear pain, (14) itching/rash, (15) stomach disorder, (16) leprosy. Overcome by these sixteen diseases, Ikaai Rathod summoned his household servants and said: "Go, beloved friends, to the township of Vijayavardhaman — to the crossroads, three-way intersections, four-way crossings, open squares, highways, and side-roads — and announce loudly, again and again: 'In the body of Ikaai Rathod, sixteen diseases have arisen — [lists them] — whoever wishes — any physician, physician's son, scholar, scholar's son, healer, or healer's son — to cure even one of these sixteen diseases in Ikaai Rathod, that person shall receive vast wealth from Ikaai Rathod.' Announce this two times, three times, and report back to me."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

aṇṇayā kayāi = once, at some time; jamagasamagameva = simultaneously, all at once; solas rogāyaṃkā = sixteen incurable diseases; sāse kāse = shortness of breath and cough; jare = fever; dāhe = burning sensation; kucchisūle = abdominal colic; bhagaṃdare = fistula; arise = piles/hemorrhoids; ajīrae = indigestion; diṭṭhī = eye disease; maddhasūle = headache; akārae = loss of appetite; acchiveyaṇā = eye-nerve pain; kaṇṇaveyaṇā = ear pain; kaṃḍū = itching/skin rash; uyare = stomach disorder; koḍhe = leprosy; siṃgāḍaga-tiga-caukkka-caccara = T-junctions, three-roads, four-roads, open squares (all public gathering points); ugghōsemāṇā = announcing loudly; vejjo vā vejjaputto vā = physician or physician's son; viulaṃ atthasaṃpayāṇaṃ = vast wealth; dalayai = shall give

**5. COMMENTARY**

Sixteen diseases arriving simultaneously is medically impossible in any ordinary sense — and the text intends it to be understood as the precise karmic fruition of systematic cruelty. In Jain understanding, the karma of violence, oppression, and harm binds itself to the subtle body (karma-sharira) in specific forms — each type of evil producing specific forms of physical suffering at the time of fruition. The sixteen diseases listed here are a classical Ayurvedic enumeration of major categories of illness; their simultaneous appearance signals that the fruition is total, not partial. Ikaai Rathod's response — immediately sending servants to announce a reward for anyone who can cure even one disease — is revealing: even in agony, he reaches for power and wealth as the solution. He never looks inward. He never considers what he has done. The announcement is broadcast from every crossroads, in every public space — a grand, humiliating public spectacle for a man who once commanded five hundred villages.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

All of a sudden, sixteen terrible diseases attacked Ikaai Rathod's body at the same time — and he sent servants to announce throughout the city that anyone who could cure even one disease would be richly rewarded.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When suffering arrives in my life, is my first instinct to look outward for someone to fix it — or to look inward for what it might be asking me to see?

### SUTRA 1.23

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से विजयवद्धमाणे खेडे इमं एयारूवं उग्घोसणं सोच्चा णिसम्म बहवे वेज्जा य जाव सत्थकोसहत्थगया सईहिं सईहिं गिहेहिंतो पडिणिक्खमंति पडिणिक्खमित्ता विजयवद्धमाणस्स खेडस्स मज्झमज्झेणं जेणेव इक्काई रटुकूडस्स गिहे तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता इक्काई रटुकूडस्स सरीरगं परामुसंति, परामुसित्ता तेसिं रोगाणं णिदाणं पुच्छंति, पुच्छित्ता, इक्काई रटुकूडस्स बहूहिं अभंगेहि य उवट्टणेहि य सिणेहपाणेहि य वमणेहि य विरेयणेहि य सेयणाहि य अवद्दहणाहि य अणुवासणाहि य वत्थिकम्मेहि य णिरूहेहि य सिरावेहेहि य तच्छणेहि य पच्छणेहि य सिरोवत्थीहि य तप्पणाहि य पुडपागेहि य छल्लीहि य मूलेहि य कंदेहि य फलेहि य बीएहि य सिलियाहि य गुलियाहि य ओसहेहि य भेसज्जेहि य इच्छंति तेसिं सोलसण्हं रोगायंकाणं एगमवि रोगायंकं उवसामितए, णो चेव णं संचाएंति उवसामितए । तए णं ते बहवे वेज्जा य वेज्जपुत्ता य जाणया य जाणयपुत्ता य तेगिच्छिया य तेगिच्छियपुत्ता य जाहे णो संचाएंति तेसिं सोलसण्हं रोगायंकाणं एगमवि रोगायंकं उवसामितए, ताहे संता तंता परितंता जामेव दिसिं पाउब्भूया तामेव दिसिं पडिगया ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se Vijayavaddhamāṇe kheḍe imaṃ eyārūvaṃ ugghōsaṇaṃ socchā ṇisamm bahave vejjā ya jāva satthakosahathagayā saīhiṃ saīhiṃ gihehiṃto paḍiṇikkhmaṃti... jeṇeva Ikkāī raṭukūḍassa gihe teṇeva uvāgacchhai, uvāgacchhittā Ikkāī raṭukūḍassa sarīragaṃ parāmusaṃti... tesinṃ rogāṇaṃ ṇidāṇaṃ pucchaṃti... Ikkāī raṭukūḍassa bahūhiṃ abhaṃgehiṃ ya uvaṭṭaṇehiṃ ya siṇehapāṇehiṃ ya vamaṇehiṃ ya vireyaṇehiṃ ya seyaṇāhiṃ ya avaddahaṇāhiṃ ya aṇuvāsaṇāhiṃ ya vatthikammehiṃ ya ṇirūhehiṃ ya sirāvehehiṃ ya tacchaṇehiṃ ya pacchaṇehiṃ ya sirovattīhiṃ ya tappaṇāhiṃ ya puḍapāgehiṃ ya chalīhiṃ ya mūlehiṃ ya kaṃdehiṃ ya phalehiṃ ya bīehiṃ ya siliyāhiṃ ya guliyāhiṃ ya osahehiṃ ya bhesajjehiṃ ya icchaṃti tesinṃ solasaṇhaṃ rogāyaṃkāṇaṃ egamavi rogāyaṃkaṃ uvasāmitae, ṇo ceva ṇaṃ saṃcāeṃti uvasāmitae |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Hearing that announcement throughout the Vijayavardhaman township, many physicians, physicians' sons, scholars, scholar's sons, healers, and healer's sons — each carrying their own medicine-boxes and instruments — came from their homes and arrived at Ikaai Rathod's house. They felt his body, inquired into the origins of the diseases, and then attempted to cure even one of his sixteen diseases using every possible treatment: massage, ointments, oily drinks, emetics, purgatives, steam treatments, hot-iron applications, enemas, bladder treatments, decoctions, vein-openings (bloodletting), scraping, scarification, head-oil treatments, irrigations, heated poultice treatments, bark preparations, roots, bulbs, fruits, seeds, saps, pills, medicines, and medicinal compounds — despite all of these efforts, they could not cure even one of the sixteen diseases. And those many physicians, scholars, and healers — exhausted, mentally worn, and physically tired — departed in the direction from which they had come.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

satthakosahathagayā = carrying their instrument-boxes; sarīragaṃ parāmusaṃti = examined his body; ṇidāṇaṃ pucchaṃti = inquired into the cause/origin of the diseases; abhaṃgehiṃ = massage (abhyanga); uvaṭṭaṇehiṃ = ointments/rubbing treatments; siṇehapāṇehiṃ = oily drinks (sneha-pana); vamaṇehiṃ = emetics; vireyaṇehiṃ = purgatives; seyaṇāhiṃ = steam treatments; avaddahaṇāhiṃ = hot-iron application; aṇuvāsaṇāhiṃ = enema; vatthikammehiṃ = bladder treatment; ṇirūhehiṃ = decoction enema; sirāvehehiṃ = bloodletting; tacchaṇehiṃ = scraping; pacchaṇehiṃ = scarification/fine cutting; sirovattīhiṃ = head-oil application; puḍapāgehiṃ = hot-poultice treatment; ṇo ceva ṇaṃ saṃcāeṃti = they were simply unable; saṃtā taṃtā parītantā = exhausted in body and mind

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra presents an extensive catalog of ancient Ayurvedic medical treatments — from massage to bloodletting, from herbal roots to heated poultices — all deployed and all failing. The text is not anti-medicine; it is making a deeper point: karma-generated suffering cannot be addressed by physical means alone. The disease did not arise from physical causes; it arose from the accumulated weight of moral choices. Medicine addresses the body; karma operates at the level of the soul. The parade of exhausted physicians departing — "tired in body, tired in mind, depleted in spirit" — is a powerful image. All their skill, all their instruments, all their knowledge: unable to touch even one of sixteen diseases. This was not because medicine is useless — it is because these particular diseases were karmic fruition, not physical malfunction. They could not be cured; they could only be experienced and exhausted.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every doctor and healer in the region came and tried every possible treatment — massage, medicines, surgeries, herbs — but not a single one of the sixteen diseases could be cured. They all left defeated.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there pains in my life that I keep trying to fix from the outside — while the real work asks me to go deeper within?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then that Ikaai Rathod — abandoned by physicians, attendants, and healers; abandoned by medicines and compounds; overwhelmed by sixteen incurable diseases; lost in attachment and delusion toward his kingdom, state, inner chambers, and all possessions; deeply craving, intensely desiring, longing for his kingdom, wealth, and inner quarters — suffering intolerably, enslaved by pain, bound by craving — having lived out his full lifespan of two hundred and fifty years, died at the appointed time and was born in this Ratnaprabha earth — the first hell — at the worst possible station among the hell-beings, as a hell-being. Then, immediately upon emerging from there, he was born as a son in the womb of Queen Mrigadevi, wife of the Kshatriya King Vijay, right here in the city of Mrigagram.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

vejja-paḍiyāicikkhae pariyāragaparicchatte = abandoned by physicians, attendants, and all caregivers; ṇiṭṭhivaṇṇosaha = abandoned by all medicines; mucchie = lost in delusion and attachment; ajjhovavaṇṇe = deeply drowned in craving; aṭṭaduhāṭṭavasaṭṭe = tormented by pain, enslaved by suffering; paramāuyaṃ pālittā = having lived out the full lifespan; kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā = dying at the appointed death-time; rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe = in the Ratnaprabha earth (the first of seven hells); ukkōseṇaṃ = at the highest (worst) degree of suffering; ṇeraiyatāe = as a hell-being; uvavaṇṇe = was born; aṇaṃtaraṃ uvvaṭṭittā = immediately upon emerging; kucchhisi puttāe = as a son in the womb

**5. COMMENTARY**

The death scene of Ikaai Rathod is heartbreaking in its completeness: abandoned by medicine, abandoned by attendants, overwhelmed by sixteen diseases — and yet, even in those final moments, his mind is clutching at his kingdom, his wealth, his inner chambers. He is dying and still craving the world he oppressed. The phrase "enslaved by pain, bound by craving" captures the double trap: his body suffers the karma he created, and his mind still clings to the very things he was willing to harm others for. He dies without letting go, without remorse, without a single thought of the thousands he hurt. The result: birth in the first hell at the worst possible station. And then, immediately upon completing his hellish existence, he is reborn — not in freedom, but directly into Mrigagram, into Mrigadevi's womb, as Mrigaputra. The karma has not been exhausted; only transformed into a new form of suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Ikaai Rathod died after 250 years, still clinging to his wealth and power, and was born in the first hell — and then, coming out of there, was born again right here as Mrigaputra.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If I were to die right now, would my mind be free — or would it be clutching something? What am I unable to let go of?
