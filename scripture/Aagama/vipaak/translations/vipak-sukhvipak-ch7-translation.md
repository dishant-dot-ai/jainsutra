# Sukha Vipaak Sutra - Chapter 7: Mahabal (મહાબળ)

**Source:** Vipaak Sutra, Second Shrutaskandha (Sukha Vipaak), Chapter 7
**Subject:** The story of Prince Mahabal and the fruits of good karma
**Total Sutras:** 2
**Form:** Narrative prose (Ardhamagadhi Prakrit)
**Narrator:** Sudharmaswami to Jambukumar
**Theme:** Prince Mahabal, son of King Bale in the city of Mahapura, encounters Lord Mahavira's teachings and attains liberation. His spiritual merit traces back to a previous life as Nagadatta, a generous householder in Manipur.

## SUTRA [7.2]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
महापुरं णयरं। रत्तासोगं उज्जाणं। रत्तपाओ जक्खो। बले राया। सुभद्दा देवी। महब्बले कुमारे। रत्तवईपामोक्खाणं पंचसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं पाणिग्गहणं। तित्थयरागमणं। पुव्वभव पुच्छा। मणिपुरं णयरे। णागदत्ते गाहावई। इंददत्ते अणगारे पडिलाभिए जाव सिद्धे। णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स। ।। सत्तमं अज्झयणं समत्तं ।।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
mahāpuraṃ ṇayaraṃ | rattāsogaṃ ujjāṇaṃ | rattapāo jakkho | bale rāyā | subhaddā devī | mahabbale kumāre | rattavaī-pāmokkhaṇaṃ paṃcasayāṇaṃ rāyavarakaṇṇagāṇaṃ pāṇiggahaṇaṃ | titthayarāgamaṇaṃ | puvvabhava pucchā | maṇipuraṃ ṇayare | ṇāgadatte gāhāvaī | iṃdadatte aṇagāre paḍilābhie jāva siddhe | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa || sattamaṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ samattaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
In the city of Mahapura (the Great City), there was a garden called Rattashoka (Rattasog — the Red Ashoka Garden). The guardian spirit was Yaksha Rattapao (Red-Footed One). King Bale ruled there. His queen was Subhadda Devi. Prince Mahabal (Mahabbale — the Greatly Powerful) was born. He married Rattavai, chief among five hundred royal maidens. The coming of the Tirthankara is described. When asked about his previous birth: in the city of Manipur, there was a householder named Nagadatta. Through the monk Indadatta, he attained spiritual progress, up to final liberation. The closing follows the pattern of the first chapter. The seventh chapter is completed.

⚠️ This sutra is heavily abbreviated. The full narrative — including the city's description, garden details, Mahabal's birth dreams, Lord Mahavira's arrival and teachings, the detailed past-life story of Nagadatta, and the stages of renunciation leading to liberation — follows the template established in Chapter 1 (Subahukumar).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**महापुरं णयरं** = the city of Mahapura (the Great City); **रत्तासोगं उज्जाणं** = Rattashoka garden (Red Ashoka Garden); **रत्तपाओ जक्खो** = Yaksha Rattapao (guardian spirit, the Red-Footed One); **बले राया** = King Bale (the Powerful); **सुभद्दा देवी** = Queen Subhadda; **महब्बले कुमारे** = Prince Mahabal (the Greatly Powerful); **रत्तवई-पामोक्खाणं** = Rattavai as chief; **पंचसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं** = among five hundred royal maidens; **पाणिग्गहणं** = marriage (literally: taking of the hand); **तित्थयरागमणं** = the coming of the Tirthankara; **पुव्वभव पुच्छा** = inquiry about previous birth; **मणिपुरं णयरे** = in the city of Manipur; **णागदत्ते गाहावई** = householder Nagadatta; **इंददत्ते अणगारे** = the monk Indadatta; **पडिलाभिए** = having attained; **जाव सिद्धे** = up to liberation; **णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स** = the closing follows the first chapter; **सत्तमं अज्झयणं समत्तं** = the seventh chapter is completed

### 5. COMMENTARY
The seventh chapter tells the story of Prince Mahabal in the city of Mahapura. The names in this chapter carry a theme of strength and power: Mahapura means "the Great City," King Bale means "the Powerful," and Mahabal means "the Greatly Powerful." Even the guardian spirit Rattapao and the garden Rattashoka share the color red, traditionally associated with energy and vitality. Yet as with every chapter in this section, worldly power is not the point — spiritual transformation is.

King Bale and Queen Subhadda's son, Prince Mahabal, marries Rattavai, described as chief among five hundred royal maidens. When Lord Mahavira arrives and teaches, Mahabal is moved to accept the spiritual path.

The past-life inquiry reveals a notable difference from the preceding chapters. While Chapters 5 and 6 traced the previous birth to a king's court, Mahabal's previous incarnation was as Nagadatta, described as a "gahavai" — a householder, not a king. This is significant. It shows that spiritual merit sufficient to produce a royal birth and eventual liberation does not require royal status in the past life. An ordinary householder who practices generosity and virtue with sincerity can accumulate merit equal to or greater than that of a king.

The guiding monk in the past life was Indadatta, a different teacher from the Sambhutivijaya mentioned in Chapters 5 and 6. This variation shows that the path to liberation is not dependent on a single teacher or lineage but is accessible through any genuine spiritual guide.

The chapter reinforces the core teaching: the merit from past-life charity and spiritual practice bears fruit as a noble birth, spiritual awakening, and ultimate freedom from the cycle of rebirth.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
In the powerful city of Mahapura, Prince Mahabal heard Lord Mahavira's teachings and chose the spiritual path. His good fortune came from a past life as Nagadatta, an ordinary but generous householder in Manipur who practiced virtue under the monk Indadatta's guidance. He eventually attained complete liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Mahabal's past life was not as a king but as an ordinary householder — yet that life of quiet virtue produced a prince who attained liberation. What does this say about the hidden power of simple, sincere goodness practiced without fanfare?
