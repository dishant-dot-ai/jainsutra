# Vipaak Sutra — Chapter 10: Anjushri
## Dukha Vipaak Sutra (Suffering from Evil Deeds)
### Scripture Pages 146–152 | PDF Pages 198–204

---

## Parichay (Introduction)

This tenth and final chapter of the Dukha Vipaak Sutra is named "Anjushri." It narrates the life of a courtesan and the terrible consequences she experiences in her next birth.

In the ancient city of Indrapur in Bharata, there lived a courtesan named Prithvishri. Using intoxicating powders, love potions, mantra-compounds, and bewitching agents, she captivated the hearts of wealthy men — kings, merchants, and officials — and spent her life in limitless sensual pleasure, considering this her calling and her joy. After a life of 3,500 years in this manner, she was reborn in the sixth hell, where she suffered for twenty-two sagaropam.

From that hell she emerged and was born as the daughter of a wealthy merchant in Vardhamanpur, named Anjushri. She grew up beautiful and was taken as a consort by King Vijayamitra. After some time, a severe and incurable vaginal disease overtook her. Despite the king proclaiming an open reward throughout the city, no physician could cure her. She lay emaciated, hollow, skin-and-bone, weeping in distress.

It was while Gautam Swami was passing by the king's Ashoka grove that he saw this wretched woman and asked Bhagavan Mahavir about her former birth. After hearing the full account, he asked about her future. Bhagavan declared: after enduring suffering for ninety years and dying, she will pass through more rebirths — including as a peacock — before finally, in a merchant family, attaining spiritual awakening, taking monastic vows, and eventually reaching liberation in the Mahavideh region.

With this chapter, the first canon — the Dukha Vipaak Sutra — comes to its close.

## Adhyayan Prarambh (Chapter Opening)

---

### SUTRA 10.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दसमस्स उक्खेवो ।
॥१०.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Dasamassa ukkheyo |
|| 10.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The introduction of the tenth chapter — understand it as the same as the first chapter's opening.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Dasamassa* — of the tenth (chapter)
- *ukkheyo* — the opening, the upakrama (introduction)

**5. COMMENTARY**

As with each preceding chapter, this single line marks the formal commencement of the final teaching of the Dukha Vipaak Sutra. By now the reader has encountered nine souls across nine chapters — each with a past life steeped in specific violence or moral corruption, each suffering the precise karmic consequence in their current existence, each destined for eventual liberation. This tenth sutra, pointing back to the first as its structural mirror, also carries a deeper resonance: we have come full circle. The first chapter opened the Dukha Vipaak; the tenth closes it. What began with Mrigaputra now ends with Anjushri. The suffering is different in form, but identical in cause: lives lived without dharma, without restraint, in pursuit of sensory gratification at the cost of other beings' wellbeing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Chapter Ten begins just like Chapter One — the formal opening tells us another story of suffering caused by past evil actions is about to be told.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What cycle am I coming full circle on? What pattern in my life is asking to be completed — and released?

## Setting and Characters

---

### SUTRA 10.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं वद्धमाणपुरे णामं णयरे होत्था । विजयवद्धमाणे उज्जाणे । मणिभद्दे जक्खे । विजयमित्ते राया । तत्थ णं धणदेवे णामं सत्थवाहे होत्था, अड्ढे जाव अपरिभूए । पियंगु णामं भारिया । अंजू दारिया जाव उक्किट्टु सरीरा । समोसरणं जाव परिसा पडिगया ।
॥१०.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Evaṃ khalu Jambū! Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ Vaḍḍhamāṇapure nāmaṃ nayare hotthā | Vijayavaḍḍhamāṇe ujjāṇe | Maṇibhadde jakkhe | Vijayamitte rāyā | Tattha ṇaṃ Dhaṇadeve nāmaṃ satthāvahe hotthā, aḍḍhe jāva aparibhūe | Piyaṃgu nāmaṃ bhāriyā | Aṃjū dāriyā jāva ukkiṭṭu sarīrā | Samosaraṇaṃ jāva parisā paḍigayā |
|| 10.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Indeed, O Jambu! At that time and in that era, there was a city named Vardhamanpur; a garden called Vijayavardhana; a yaksha shrine named Manibhadra; a king named Vijayamitra; and in that city a merchant caravan-leader named Dhanadeva — wealthy and of undiminished standing — whose wife was named Priyengu, and whose daughter Anju had a supremely beautiful body; and Bhagavan Mahavir arrived, his divine assembly convened, and the assembly returned.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Vaḍḍhamāṇapure* — Vardhamanpur, the city of prosperity (named after the growth/increase of wealth)
- *Vijayavaḍḍhamāṇe ujjāṇe* — the garden named Vijayavardhana (Victory-Prosperity)
- *Maṇibhadde jakkhe* — the yaksha (local deity) named Manibhadra (Jewel-Good-Fortune)
- *Vijayamitte rāyā* — King Vijayamitra (Victory-Friend)
- *Dhaṇadeve nāmaṃ satthāvahe* — a merchant caravan-leader named Dhanadeva (Wealth-Divine)
- *aḍḍhe jāva aparibhūe* — wealthy, prosperous, and of undiminished honour (standard Agamic description)
- *Piyaṃgu nāmaṃ bhāriyā* — wife named Priyengu (a type of fragrant grass/creeper — used as a beautiful feminine name)
- *Aṃjū dāriyā ukkiṭṭu sarīrā* — their daughter Anju, of supremely excellent physical form
- *samosaraṇaṃ jāva parisā paḍigayā* — Bhagavan arrived in his divine assembly hall (samavasaraṇa); the congregation attended; and the assembly returned — abbreviated by "jāva"

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra efficiently establishes the world of Chapter 10: a prosperous city, a royal presence, a wealthy merchant family, and a daughter of exceptional beauty. The name Anju — later formally known as Anjushri — carries aesthetic associations with something delicate and graceful, and this contrast with her later condition of emaciated suffering is central to the chapter's teaching. Priyengu, her mother's name, refers to a fragrant and delicate climbing plant — suggesting a family of aesthetic refinement and material comfort. The sutra also establishes that Bhagavan Mahavir is present in Vardhamanpur: this is what will give Gautam Swami the opportunity to witness Anjushri's suffering and receive Bhagavan's explanation of its karmic origin. The entire story unfolds within the sphere of the Lord's active teaching presence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In the prosperous city of Vardhamanpur lived King Vijayamitra and a wealthy merchant named Dhanadeva, whose beautiful daughter Anju lived there — and Bhagavan Mahavir was also present in the city at this time.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Beauty and wealth are the most common reasons we forget the soul. What have I taken as permanent that is, in truth, entirely temporary?

## Anjushri no Parichay (Introduction of Anjushri)

---

### SUTRA 10.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स जेट्ठे अंतेवासी जाव अडमाणे विजयमित्तस्स रण्णो गिहस्स असोगवणियाए अदूरसामंतेणं वीईयमाणे पासइ एगं इत्थियं– सुक्कं, भुक्खं, णिम्मंसं, किडिकिडियाभूयं, अड्ढुच्चमावणद्धं णीलसाडगिणियत्थं कट्टाइं कलुणाइं विस्सराइं कूवमाणिं पासइ, पासित्ता चिंता तहेव जाव एवं वयासी– सा णं भंते ! इत्थिया पुव्वभवे का आसी ? वागरणं ।
॥१०.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇassa bhagavao Mahāvīrassa jeṭṭhe aṃtevāsī jāva aḍamāṇe Vijayamittassa raṇṇo gihassa asogavaṇiyāe adūrasāmantenaṃ vīīyamāṇe pāsai egaṃ itthiyaṃ – sukkaṃ, bhujjhaṃ, ṇimmansaṃ, kiḍikiḍiyābhūyaṃ, aḍḍhuccamāvaṇaddhaṃ ṇīlasāḍagiṇiyatthaṃ kaṭṭāiṃ kaluṇāiṃ vissarāiṃ kūvamāṇiṃ pāsai, pāsittā ciṃtā taheva jāva evaṃ vayāsī – sā ṇaṃ bhaṃte! itthiyā pubbhave kā āsī? vāgaraṇaṃ |
|| 10.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time and in that era, the chief disciple of Bhagavan Mahavir, while walking on his begging rounds, passing near the Ashoka grove of King Vijayamitra's palace, saw a woman — dried up, starving, flesh-less, her bones crackling, wrapped in half-dried skin, wearing a blue sari, crying out pitiful, tearful, and distressing sounds; seeing her, the familiar reflection arose within him and he addressed Bhagavan: "O Bhagavan! Who was this woman in her previous life?" Bhagavan gave the answer.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *asogavaṇiyāe adūrasāmantenaṃ* — near the Ashoka grove (a pleasure garden attached to the palace, sacred to the goddess of good fortune)
- *vīīyamāṇe* — while passing / while proceeding on his way
- *sukkaṃ* — dried up / parched / desiccated (body with no moisture)
- *bhujjhaṃ* (bhukkaṃ) — starving / famished / deeply hungry
- *ṇimmansaṃ* — flesh-less / without flesh on the body (only bones)
- *kiḍikiḍiyābhūyaṃ* — having become like a crackling thing / making kiḍikiḍā sounds (the sound of bones creaking and cracking as there is no flesh to muffle them)
- *aḍḍhuccamāvaṇaddhaṃ* — bound/wrapped in skin that is half-dried (skin adhered to bones)
- *ṇīlasāḍagiṇiyatthaṃ* — wearing a blue-coloured sari (indicating poverty and abandonment — blue/dark-hued garments were associated with low status)
- *kaṭṭāiṃ kaluṇāiṃ vissarāiṃ kūvamāṇiṃ* — crying out in pitiful, heart-rending, and distressing sounds (a triple description of the intensity of lamentation)
- *ciṃtā taheva* — the familiar reflection arose (referring to Gautam's standard meditative recognition that this person is experiencing karmic consequences)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sight that stops Gautam Swami is devastating in its clinical precision. The word *kiḍikiḍiyābhūya* — "having become something that makes crackling sounds" — is particularly striking: this is not a metaphor but a physiological description of a body so depleted of flesh and fluid that its very bones creak audibly with each movement. Anjushri has been reduced to a living skeleton wrapped in drying skin, sitting near the royal Ashoka grove — a place of pleasure and beauty — wearing the dark blue sari of the destitute, crying out in sounds the text describes as pitiful, tearful, and distressing. These three adjectives build on each other: *kaṭṭā* (hard, harsh), *kaluṇā* (compassion-inducing, heart-wrenching), *vissarā* (resonant with distress, far-carrying). Her cries are not quiet — they carry. And yet she sits alone. No physician has been able to help her. The king's proclamation has failed. She is abandoned to her pain. The contrast with the Ashoka grove — where blossoming trees and fragrant flowers surround her — makes the image unbearable. This is the world the Vipaak Sutra never lets us look away from: beauty and suffering, side by side, both rooted in karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Gautam Swami, walking near the king's garden, saw a woman reduced to skin and bones — starving, flesh-less, her bones crackling — wailing in grief. He asked Bhagavan who she had been in a past life.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The sounds of another's suffering — do I hear them? Or do I pass by the Ashoka grove with my eyes forward and my ears closed?

## Anjushri no Purvabhav (Past Life of Anjushri)

---

### SUTRA 10.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु गोयमा ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं इहेव जंबुद्दीवे दीवे भारहेवासे इंदपुरे णामं णयरे होत्था । तत्थ णं इंददत्ते राया । पुढविसिरी णामं गणिया होत्था । वण्णओ । तए णं सा पुढविसिरी गणिया इंदपुरे णयरे बहवे राईसर जाव पभिओ बहूइहिं विज्जापओगेहिं य मंतपाओगेहिं य चुण्णपाओगेहिं य हियड्ढावणेहिं य णिण्हवणेहिं य पण्हवणेहिं य वसीकरणेहिं य आभिओगिएहिं य अभिओगेत्ता उरालाइं माणुस्सगाइं भोगभोगाइं भुंजमाणी विहरइ ।
॥१०.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Evaṃ khalu Goyamā! Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ iheva Jambudīve dīve Bhārahevāse Iṃdapure nāmaṃ nayare hotthā | Tattha ṇaṃ Iṃdadatte rāyā | Puḍhavisirī nāmaṃ gaṇiyā hotthā | Vaṇṇao | Tae ṇaṃ sā Puḍhavisirī gaṇiyā Iṃdapure ṇayare bahave rāīsara jāva pabhio bahūihiṃ vijjāpāogaihiṃ ya mantapāogaihiṃ ya cuṇṇapāogaihiṃ ya hiyaḍḍhāvaṇehiṃ ya ṇiṇhavaṇehiṃ ya paṇhavaṇehiṃ ya vasīkaraṇehiṃ ya ābhiogiaihiṃ ya abhiogettā urālāiṃ māṇussagāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇī viharai |
|| 10.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus, O Gautam! At that time and in that era, here on this very continent of Jambudvipa, in the land of Bharata, there was a city named Indrapur; there King Indradatta ruled; and in that city there was a courtesan named Prithvishri — of renowned beauty — who used various forms of intoxicating knowledge-applications, mantra-applications, powder-applications, heart-captivating agents, binding substances, questioning compounds, bewitching preparations, and subjugation-medicines to captivate the minds of numerous kings, lords, and wealthy men — and thus lived consuming abundant human pleasures and comforts.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Iṃdapure* — Indrapur (City of Indra — a name suggesting royal magnificence)
- *Iṃdadatte rāyā* — King Indradatta (Gift of Indra)
- *Puḍhavisirī nāmaṃ gaṇiyā* — a courtesan/prostitute named Prithvishri (Earth-Beauty); *gaṇiyā* = gaṇikā, a high-status professional courtesan in ancient India
- *vaṇṇao* — of described/renowned beauty (standard Agamic phrase indicating the physical description is to be understood)
- *vijjāpāoga* — applications of knowledge/science (medicinal or occult knowledge used for manipulation)
- *mantapāoga* — mantra applications (verbal/vibrational spells)
- *cuṇṇapāoga* — powder applications (herbal or chemical compounds administered to induce states)
- *hiyaḍḍhāvaṇa* — heart-attracting agents (substances or words that create attraction)
- *ṇiṇhavana* — binding substances (agents that keep a person emotionally bound)
- *paṇhavana* — questioning/probing compounds (various)
- *vasīkaraṇa* — bewitching/subjugation (making others submit to one's will)
- *ābhiogiya* — compelling agents (compulsion-inducing substances or practices)
- *urālāiṃ māṇussagāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇī* — consuming/enjoying abundant human pleasures and comforts

**5. COMMENTARY**

Prithvishri's story introduces the only female protagonist in the Dukha Vipaak Sutra, and she is presented with complete moral complexity. The text does not describe her as merely promiscuous — it describes a sophisticated professional who deployed an entire pharmacopoeia of psychological, chemical, and ritualistic tools to make men captive to her. The eight categories of instruments she used range from scientific knowledge to mantra-based influence to physical powders and potions — a complete spectrum of methods, both material and subtle. The gaṇikā in ancient Indian society occupied a complex social position: educated, skilled in arts, often wealthy, patronized by kings, but existing outside the moral framework of dharmic life. What the Agama identifies as Prithvishri's specific karma is not simply that she enjoyed pleasure — it is that she deliberately manipulated other beings' minds and desires, making them instruments of her gratification without regard for their spiritual welfare or her own. The word *abhiogettā* — "having subjugated" — captures this: she did not merely attract men, she conquered their will. This weaponized use of desire, repeated across 3,500 years of a lifespan, accumulated an enormous weight of binding karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In a past life, Anjushri was a wealthy and clever courtesan named Prithvishri who used potions, spells, and tricks to trap the hearts of kings and rich men and live a life of total indulgence — for 3,500 years.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In how many subtle ways do I use others as instruments of my own gratification — not with potions, but with words, status, charm, or emotional leverage?

### SUTRA 10.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं सा पुढविसिरी गणिया एयकम्मा एयप्पहाणा एयविज्जा एयसमायारा सुबहुं पावं कम्मं समिज्जिणित्ता पणतीसं वाससयाइं परमाउयं पालित्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा छट्टीए पुढवीए उक्कोसेणं बावीसं सागरोवमं ट्ठितिएसु णेरइएसु णेरइयत्ताए उवण्णा ।
॥१०.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Tae ṇaṃ sā Puḍhavisirī gaṇiyā eyakammā eyapphāṇā eyavijjā eyasamāyārā subahuṃ pāvaṃ kammaṃ samijjiṇittā paṇatīsaṃ vāsasayāiṃ paramāuyaṃ pālittā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe ukkosenaaṃ bāvīsaṃ sāgarovamaṃ ṭhitiesu ṇeraiesu ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇā |
|| 10.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then the courtesan Prithvishri, having made this very activity her sole karma, her supreme purpose, her only expertise, and her constant practice — accumulating an enormous mass of evil karma — after living out her maximum lifespan of three thousand and five hundred years, died at the proper time of death, and was born as a hell-being among the hell-beings of the sixth hell, with the maximum duration of twenty-two ocean-measure periods.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *eyakammā eyapphāṇā eyavijjā eyasamāyārā* — the fourfold formula: sole karma, supreme purpose, only expertise, constant conduct (same as previous chapters — indicating complete immersion in this way of life)
- *paṇatīsaṃ vāsasayāiṃ* — 3,500 years (35 × 100 = 3500 years of lifespan — longest lifespan seen in the Dukha Vipaak so far)
- *paramāuyaṃ pālittā* — having lived out the maximum lifespan
- *chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe* — in the sixth hell-realm (Tamaḥprabha — the realm of profound darkness)
- *ukkosenaaṃ bāvīsaṃ sāgarovamaṃ* — with the maximum term of twenty-two sagaropam
- *ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇā* — born as a hell-being

**5. COMMENTARY**

The mathematical symmetry in this sutra is striking: Prithvishri lived for 3,500 years — the longest lifespan among all the protagonists of the Dukha Vipaak — and descended to the sixth hell with the same maximum term of twenty-two sagaropam as others who practiced far more overtly violent crimes. This equivalence is the Agama's statement about the nature of harm. Violence of the body — torture, murder, abuse — is not the only form of karma-accumulation that leads to the deepest hells. Violence of the soul — the systematic corruption of others' consciousness, the weaponization of desire, the turning of beings into objects of gratification — accumulates equally severe karma. Prithvishri did not kill anyone. She did not torture anyone physically. But she spent 3,500 years deliberately enthralling the minds and desires of countless beings, leading them away from dharma and into bondage, while she herself was completely enslaved to sensory pleasure. The fourfold formula — sole karma, supreme purpose, only expertise, constant practice — applies as devastatingly to her as it did to Durjodhan the prison warden.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Because Prithvishri spent her entire 3,500-year life completely consumed by the pursuit of pleasure and manipulation of others, she was reborn in the sixth and near-deepest level of hell, where she suffered for twenty-two unimaginably long time periods.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Harm does not require a weapon. What ways of relating to others in my life — however refined and socially accepted — might still be causing harm to their souls?

## Anjushri no Vartaman Bhav (Present Life of Anjushri)

---

### SUTRA 10.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सा णं तओ अणंतरं उव्वट्टित्ता इहेव वद्धमाणपुरे णयरे धणदेवस्स सत्थवाहस्स पियंगु भारियाए कुंछिसि दारियत्ताए उववण्णा । तए णं सा पियंगु भारिया णवण्हं मासाणं बहुपडिपुण्णाणं दारियं पयाया । णामं अंजूसिरी । सेसं जहा देवदत्ताए ।
॥१०.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Sā ṇaṃ tao aṇantaraṃ uvvaṭṭittā iheva Vaḍḍhamāṇapure ṇayare Dhaṇadevassa satthāvāhassa Piyaṃgu bhāriyāe kuṃchisi dāriyattāe uvavaṇṇā | Tae ṇaṃ sā Piyaṃgu bhāriyā ṇavaṇhaṃ māsāṇaṃ bahuppaḍipuṇṇāṇaṃ dāriyaṃ payāyā | Nāmaṃ Aṃjūsirī | Sesaṃ jahā Devadattāe |
|| 10.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Immediately after passing from that existence, she was reborn in the womb of Priyengu, the wife of merchant Dhanadeva, right here in the city of Vardhamanpur, as a daughter; after nine months were fully complete, Priyengu gave birth to a daughter; she was named Anjushri; the rest is as described in the chapter of Devadatta.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *uvvaṭṭittā* — having passed out of / emerged from the hell-existence
- *dāriyattāe uvavaṇṇā* — entered the womb as a daughter (female birth)
- *Aṃjūsirī* — "Anju-shri" — Anju (a diminutive/affectionate name, associated with grace) + shri (auspiciousness, beauty, good fortune)
- *sesaṃ jahā Devadattāe* — "the rest is as in the case of Devadatta" — the abbreviated reference to the ninth chapter, indicating the same standard description of birth, naming ceremony, childhood, physical description applies

**5. COMMENTARY**

The soul that was Prithvishri — who spent millennia expertly enthralling others with beauty, substances, and the arts of seduction — is now born as a daughter. The irony is precise: Prithvishri's entire life had been organized around physical beauty as a tool of power. She is reborn as a girl named Anjushri — "beautiful Anju" — into a prosperous household. The wealth and beauty continue; they are the residual impressions of a life entirely oriented around them. But the karma carried beneath the surface is the karmic debt for all those centuries of exploitation. The soul does not begin its new life in obvious suffering — the suffering comes later, slowly, as the residual karma ripens. The reference to Chapter Nine (Devadatta) for the standard descriptions indicates that Anjushri's early life followed the same pattern as other noble daughters in these chapters: named at twelve days, raised with care, growing to youth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

After leaving the sixth hell, Prithvishri's soul was born again — this time as the beautiful daughter of a wealthy merchant in Vardhamanpur, and was named Anjushri.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The soul carries its patterns into each new life. What patterns from my own past — even this lifetime — am I still replaying without realizing it?

The Agama's teaching here is not that medicine is useless — it is that certain conditions are karmically sealed. When the ripening of karma is the root cause, material remedies can alleviate symptoms but cannot dissolve the underlying karmic seed. The physicians depart "exhausted, discouraged, and worn out" — and the three words are carefully graduated: exhausted (*saṃtā*) from effort, discouraged (*taṃtā*) from failure, and worn out (*paritaṃtā*) from both. The scene closes with the return to the image of Anjushri that opened the chapter: the same three-word description — dry, starving, flesh-less — now confirmed not as a momentary state but as her settled condition. And Bhagavan's final statement seals the meaning: "She is experiencing the specific fruit of ancient evil deeds."

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra of the Dukha Vipaak Sutra is layered with significance. Anjushri's post-death journey is: first hell (short term, as the current life's karma is less intense than the sixth-hell level of the past life) → extensive wandering through lower life forms → peacock in Sarvarthobhadra → killed by hunters → merchant's son in the same city → spiritual awakening → renunciation → Saudharma heaven → Mahavideh → liberation. The peacock birth is notable: the peacock is among the most visually spectacular of birds, famous for its display of beauty. A soul that spent its foundational life as Prithvishri — whose entire existence revolved around beauty as a tool and spectacle — is given one final animal birth in the form of beauty itself. But the peacock is also prey. It is killed by hunters. The beauty that was once power becomes vulnerability. And from that death comes the merchant's son, the quiet householder life, the encounter with a great teacher, the awakening, and the long arc toward liberation. The formal closing — Sudharma's statement to Jambu, and Jambu's double affirmation "Sevaṃ bhaṃte, Sevaṃ bhaṃte" — is the traditional seal of the oral Agamic transmission, affirming that the teaching received is true, complete, and accepted.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bhagavan tells Gautam that Anjushri will suffer for ninety years, then die and be reborn in the first hell, then wander through many rebirths, eventually becoming a peacock that is killed, then a merchant's son who finally awakens spiritually, takes monastic vows, and after more lives, reaches complete liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Every story of suffering in this text ends in liberation. What does it mean that no matter how far I fall or how long I wander, the destination is always the same? Does that truth give me peace — or do I resist receiving it?

## Vivechan (Commentary and Reflection)

Six teachings emerge from the closing of the Dukha Vipaak Sutra through this chapter:

**1. The ripening of deeply accumulated karma admits no exception.** No intensity of suffering, however severe, is permanent — but when deeply embedded karma (niyachit udaya) ripens, it produces conditions like Anjushri's that resist all external remedy. The physicians failed not from incompetence but because the root was karmic, not physical.

**2. Sensory pleasures are sweet poison.** The Agamic teaching says clearly: *"saṃsāra mokkhassa vipakkhabḥūyā, khāṇī aṇatthāṇa hu kāmabhogā"* — worldly pleasures are the enemy of liberation, a mine of misfortunes. What Prithvishri called her calling, her art, and her joy was in truth a slow accumulation of chains.

**3. Intense karma without dharmic foundation leaves no refuge.** When crisis comes — as it does for all beings — those without dharma find themselves truly alone. Anjushri's wealth, royal patronage, and access to every physician failed her. Only the awakening that no external condition can give — the turning of the soul inward — could have helped her.

**4. Dharma internalized transforms suffering into liberation.** Those who have genuinely practiced dharma — even partially — can meet the worst circumstances with equanimity, experiencing the karmic ripening without creating new karma. Suffering need not generate more suffering if it is met with awareness rather than reaction.

**5. The Dukha Vipaak as a complete canon.** This tenth chapter completes the first canon of the Vipaak Sutra. The ten protagonists — Mrigaputra, Ujjhitak, Abhagrasena, Shakatkumar, Bruhaspatidat, Nandivardhana, Umbaradatt, Shaurikadatt, Devadatta, and Anjushri — represent a full spectrum of human failures: violence, cruelty, addiction, betrayal, lust, greed, and exploitation. Each one ends in liberation. The Dukha Vipaak is not a scripture of despair — it is a scripture of unflinching honesty about the cost of unconscious living, paired with the guarantee that no soul is permanently lost.

**6. The purpose of witnessing.** The Agama invites us not merely to read these accounts but to witness them — to let the suffering of these ten souls genuinely land in our awareness, to feel the weight of karma in a way that motivates not fear but transformation. The closing words of Jambu — "So it is, O Bhagavan. So it is, O Bhagavan. Thus I say" — are words of complete, unresisting acceptance of the truth. This is the posture the Dukha Vipaak asks of every reader.
