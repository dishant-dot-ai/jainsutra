# Shri Vipaak Sutra — Chapter 9: Devadatta
## Dukha Vipaak (The Fruit of Evil Deeds)

**Chapter Theme:** King Simhasena burns 499 mothers alive to protect his favorite queen — reborn as Devadatta, who murders her mother-in-law and is publicly impaled as punishment. The law of karma operates with perfect symmetry: those who gave pain receive pain; those who took life have life taken by cruelty.

**Source:** Shri Vipaak Sutra, Gujarati PDF, book pages 127–145 (PDF pages 179–197)
**Total Sutras:** 29

## SUTRA 9.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उक्खेवो णवमस्स ।
॥९.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ukkhevaṇavamassa.
|| 9.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Here begins the introduction of the ninth chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ukkhevaṇa** = introduction / opening / uplifting narration
- **navamassa** = of the ninth (chapter)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This single-line sutra serves as the formal opening declaration of Chapter 9. In the Vipaak Sutra's structure, each chapter begins with such a brief titular marker, signaling that a new account of karmic fruition is about to unfold. The brevity is intentional — the weight is carried by all that follows. This chapter presents one of the most psychologically complex narratives in Dukha Vipaak, centered on a woman whose beauty was earned through ancient merit, but whose jealousy and cruelty propel her toward devastating consequences. The opening also invites the listener to settle into a state of attentive receptivity before the story commences.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This marks the beginning of the ninth story in the chapter on suffering caused by bad deeds.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What kind of inner stillness do I bring when I sit to receive wisdom — am I truly present, or is my mind already elsewhere?

## SUTRA 9.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं रोहिडए नामं नयरे होत्था। पुढविअवंसए नामं उज्जाणे। धारणे नामं जक्खे। वेसमणदत्ते नामं राया। सिरिदेवी देवी। पुप्फणंदी कुमारे ।
॥९.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ rohiḍae nāmaṃ nayare hotthā. puḍhaviaṃsae nāmaṃ ujjāṇe. dhāraṇe nāmaṃ jakkhe. vesamaṇadatte nāmaṃ rāyā. siridevi devī. pupphaNaṃdī kumāre.
|| 9.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that time and in that era, there was a city called Rohitak, a garden called Prithviavtansak, a deity called Dharana, a king named Vaishramandatt, his queen named Shridevi, and a crown prince named Pushpnandi.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ** = at that time, in that era (standard temporal formula)
- **rohiḍae nāmaṃ nayare** = city named Rohitak
- **puḍhaviaṃsae nāmaṃ ujjāṇe** = garden named Prithviavtansak (meaning "ornament/garland of the earth")
- **dhāraṇe nāmaṃ jakkhe** = yaksha (attendant deity) named Dharana
- **vesamaṇadatte nāmaṃ rāyā** = king named Vaishramandatt
- **siridevi devī** = queen named Shridevi ("goddess of auspiciousness")
- **pupphaNaṃdī kumāre** = crown prince named Pushpnandi ("joy of flowers")

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra establishes the geographical and social setting of Chapter 9. Rohitak was a significant city in the ancient Jain narrative world, and the presence of a named garden, yaksha deity, and royal family immediately signals a prosperous and well-ordered civilization — one that will become the backdrop for a terrible act of maternal vengeance. The naming of Shridevi as queen and Pushpnandi as crown prince is important: both will later be central to Devadatta's crime. The yaksha Dharana's mention is standard in these openings, connecting the earthly city to the protective divine order. Jain narratives consistently show that sin erupts not in the margins of civilization but at its very center.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The story takes place in a prosperous city called Rohitak, ruled by King Vaishramandatt, with his queen Shridevi and their son Prince Pushpnandi.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I carry the assumption that comfort and prosperity protect me from spiritual danger — or do I see that the greatest temptations arise precisely when life seems settled and secure?

## SUTRA 9.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थ णं रोहिडए नयरे दत्ते नामं गाहावई होत्था। कण्हसिरी भारिया। देवदत्ता धूया — सुरूवा, सोम्मदंसणिज्जा, पंचेंदिय-संपन्ना ।
॥९.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tattha ṇaṃ rohiḍae nayare datte nāmaṃ gāhāvaī hotthā. kaṇhasirī bhāriyā. devadattā dhūyā — surūvā, sommadaṃsaṇijjā, paṃceṃdiya-saṃpannā.
|| 9.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In that city of Rohitak there lived a merchant householder named Datt, whose wife was named Krishnashri, and whose daughter Devadatta was beautiful, pleasant to behold, and complete in all five senses.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **datte nāmaṃ gāhāvaī** = merchant householder named Datt (gāhāvaī = head of household, caravan-leader class)
- **kaṇhasirī bhāriyā** = wife named Krishnashri (dark-auspicious; kaṇha = dark/Krishna + sirī = Shri/auspiciousness)
- **devadattā dhūyā** = daughter named Devadatta ("given by the gods")
- **surūvā** = beautiful in form
- **sommadaṃsaṇijjā** = pleasant, gentle, lovely to see
- **paṃceṃdiya-saṃpannā** = endowed with all five senses (fully formed, no deficiency)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Devadatta's description as "given by the gods" and "complete in all five senses" is not accidental — it is the narrative's way of showing that her present human body reflects genuine merit from past lives. Yet in Jain thought, physical beauty and sensory completeness are the fruits of past virtue, not the guarantee of present virtue. The same soul that previously burned hundreds of mothers alive now arrives in an exquisitely beautiful form, a paradox that is central to this chapter's teaching. Datt is introduced as a gāhāvaī — a respected merchant householder — which places Devadatta in a comfortable, socially respectable family. Her beauty will attract the attention of royalty, setting the fatal chain in motion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A merchant named Datt had a daughter called Devadatta — she was extraordinarily beautiful and healthy in every way.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I see someone blessed with beauty or talent, do I pause to consider what inner qualities — in this life or previous ones — might have given rise to such gifts?

## SUTRA 9.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणे भगवं महावीरे… जाव पज्जुवासइ। तए णं से गोयमे समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स अंतियाओ छट्ठभत्त-परियागेणं पडिक्कमिय, रायमग्गे एगं इत्थियं पासइ — लोहसंखलेहिं बद्धं, कण्णनासिया छिण्णाओ, गेरूएण विलित्तं, सवण-पुष्फमाला गले, मरण-वत्थं पहरियं, सूले आरोविज्जमाणं ।
॥९.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre… jāva pajjuvasai. tae ṇaṃ se goyame samaṇassa bhagavaō mahāvīrassa aṃtiyāo chaṭṭhabhatta-pariyāgeṇaṃ paḍikkamiya, rāyamagge egaṃ itthiyaṃ pāsai — lohasaṃkhalehi baddhaṃ, kaṇṇanāsiyā chiṇṇāo, gerūeṇa vilittaṃ, savaṇa-puṣphamālā gale, maraṇa-vatthaṃ phariyaṃ, sūle ārovijjamāṇaṃ.
|| 9.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that time, the venerable ascetic Mahavir arrived... and was being attended upon; then Gautam, completing a sixth-fast alms round in departure from the presence of Mahavir, saw on the royal road a woman — bound with iron shackles, ears and nose cut off, body smeared with red ocher, a red flower garland around her neck, dressed in death-garments, and being impaled upon a spike.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **chaṭṭhabhatta-pariyāgeṇaṃ** = having completed a sixth-fast (chaṭṭhabhatta = fast of six days; pariyāga = completion/departure)
- **paḍikkamiya** = having turned back (from Mahavir's presence, going on alms rounds)
- **rāyamagge** = on the royal road / main highway
- **lohasaṃkhalehi baddhaṃ** = bound with iron shackles/chains
- **kaṇṇanāsiyā chiṇṇāo** = ears and nose cut off
- **gerūeṇa vilittaṃ** = body smeared with red ocher (gerū = reddish-ochre mineral)
- **savaṇa-puṣphamālā gale** = red flower garland placed around the neck (mark of a condemned person)
- **maraṇa-vatthaṃ phariyaṃ** = dressed in death-garments (white cloth of the condemned)
- **sūle ārovijjamāṇaṃ** = being raised/impaled upon a spike

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the scene-setting sutra that frames the entire chapter — Gautam witnesses a condemned woman being publicly executed by impalement, and the subsequent narrative explains how karma brought her to this moment. The specific details of punishment are vivid and deliberate: iron shackles (loss of freedom), ears and nose cut (disfigurement mirroring what she did to others), red ocher and death garlands (public humiliation and death-marking), impalement (the most extreme civic punishment). The sixth-fast practice of Gautam — going without food for six days — is mentioned to establish his spiritual purity and alertness in this moment of witnessing. Jain scripture uses such scenes of visible suffering as teaching moments: what the eye sees externally, the mind is invited to trace back to its invisible karmic roots.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gautam was out on his alms round when he saw a woman on the main road being tortured and executed — chained, mutilated, smeared in red, and impaled on a spike.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I witness suffering in another — do I pass it by, or do I pause long enough to ask what deep causes and choices might have led to this moment?

## SUTRA 9.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से गोयमे भगवं महावीरं पुच्छइ: "कस्स णं भंते! एसा इत्थी पुव्वभवे?" भगवं उत्तरइ: "तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं सुप्पइट्ठे नामं नयरे। महासेणे नामं राया। सहस्सं देवीओ।" ।
॥९.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se goyame bhagavaṃ mahāvīraṃ pucchai: "kassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte! esā itthī pubbhave?" bhagavaṃ uttarai: "teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ suppaïṭṭhe nāmaṃ nayare. mahāseṇe nāmaṃ rāyā. sahasaṃ devīo."
|| 9.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Gautam asked the venerable Mahavir: "Lord, who was this woman in her past life?" Mahavir replied: "At that time and in that era, there was a city named Supratishtha; there was a king named Mahasena; he had one thousand queens."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte esā itthī pubbhave** = Lord, who was this woman in a previous birth?
- **suppaïṭṭhe nāmaṃ nayare** = city named Supratishtha ("well-established/firmly founded")
- **mahāseṇe nāmaṃ rāyā** = king named Mahasena ("great army")
- **sahasaṃ devīo** = one thousand queens (devīo = queens, divine consorts)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gautam's question is the pivot point on which the entire narrative turns. In Jain pedagogical style, present suffering is always explained through past causation — the question "who was she before?" is the Jain question par excellence. Mahavir's answer begins not with Devadatta herself, but with a king who had a thousand queens — already signaling a story of favoritism, jealousy, and violence among women in a royal household. The city name Supratishtha ("well-founded") and the king's name Mahasena ("great army") suggest a powerful civilization, one in which the worst human impulses — possessiveness, rage, revenge — were about to find catastrophic expression. The soul who would become Devadatta was, in this life, a man of extraordinary power.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gautam asked Mahavir who this woman was in her past life, and Mahavir began: "There was once a powerful king named Mahasena who had a thousand wives."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can I sit with the possibility that who I am today — my circumstances, my body, my relationships — carries the full weight of choices made long before I remember?

## SUTRA 9.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स णं महासेणस्स रण्णो पुत्ते सीहसेणे नामं कुमारे। तस्स माता-पिऊ पंचसया-घरए पासाए करापेंति — पंचसयाणं रायवरकन्नाणं एगदिवसंसि पाणिग्गहणं करेंति — पंच-पंच वत्थु ददंति ।
॥९.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tassa ṇaṃ mahāseṇassa raṇṇo putte sīhaseṇe nāmaṃ kumāre. tassa māta-piū paṃcasayā-gharae pāsāe karāpeṃti — paṃcasayāṇaṃ rāyavarakaṇṇāṇaṃ egadivassaṃsi pāṇiggahaṇaṃ kareṃti — paṃca-paṃca vatthu dadaṃti.
|| 9.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

King Mahasena's son was a prince named Simhasena; his parents had a palace of five hundred sections built, arranged the marriage of five hundred royal daughters in a single day, and gave five gifts each.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sīhaseṇe nāmaṃ kumāre** = prince named Simhasena ("lion-army")
- **paṃcasayā-gharae pāsāe** = palace of five hundred sections/chambers (one for each bride)
- **paṃcasayāṇaṃ rāyavarakaṇṇāṇaṃ** = five hundred daughters of noble/royal families
- **egadivassaṃsi pāṇiggahaṇaṃ** = hand-taking (marriage ceremony) on a single day
- **paṃca-paṃca vatthu dadaṃti** = gave five (items/categories) each as dowry

### 5. COMMENTARY

The scale of Simhasena's wedding — five hundred brides in a single day, each given a palace chamber and dowry gifts — reflects the extraordinary power and wealth of this royal house. In ancient India, plural marriage among royalty was a marker of political alliances, not merely personal desire. Simhasena's parents oversaw this entire arrangement, indicating that the five hundred marriages were a matter of statecraft and family honor. Jain narratives use such scenes not to glorify royal grandeur but to establish the karmic weight of the situation: when five hundred families give their daughters to one man, five hundred sets of expectations and emotions are created. The seeds of catastrophe are planted in the very act of celebration.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Prince Simhasena married five hundred royal brides in a single day, and his parents gave each bride her own chamber and gifts.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I accumulate — whether possessions, relationships, or status — do I pause to consider the responsibilities and expectations I am creating for myself and others?

## SUTRA 9.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से महासेणे राया कालमासे कालं किच्चा। तए णं सीहसेणे कुमारे रज्जे पइट्ठिए ।
॥९.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se mahāseṇe rāyā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā. tae ṇaṃ sīhaseṇe kumāre rajje paiṭṭhie.
|| 9.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Mahasena, in due course of time, passed away; and prince Simhasena was established on the throne.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā** = having done (made) his time at the time of death (= died at the natural end of his time)
- **rajje paiṭṭhie** = established in the kingdom / ascended the throne

### 5. COMMENTARY

This brief transitional sutra marks the generational shift: the old order ends, the new king begins. In Jain narrative construction, such transitions are rarely arbitrary — the father's death always signals that the son is now fully accountable for his own karma. Simhasena can no longer act under the umbrella of his parents' wisdom or restraint. The brevity of this sutra contrasts with the detailed catastrophe that follows: in just two short lines, an entire era ends and a new one — one that will end in mass murder — begins. Jain scripture teaches that the moment power fully transfers to a person is the moment their inner character is truly tested.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

King Mahasena died, and his son Simhasena became the new king.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I step into a role of greater responsibility or freedom — do I use it to grow inward, or does unchecked power begin to erode my better judgment?

## SUTRA 9.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सीहसेणे राया सामाओ देवीए मुच्चिए — सेसाओ चउण्हं सयाओ देवीओ नो जाणइ, नो मण्णइ ।
॥९.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sīhaseṇe rāyā sāmāo devīe mucciae — sesāo cauṇhaṃ sayāo devīo no jāṇai, no maṇṇai.
|| 9.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Simhasena became completely infatuated with queen Shyama, and no longer acknowledged or honored the remaining four hundred ninety-nine queens.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sāmāo devīe mucciae** = became infatuated/absorbed with/in queen Shyama (mucciae = entangled, lost, consumed by attachment)
- **sesāo cauṇhaṃ sayāo devīo** = the remaining four hundred ninety-nine queens (lit. four hundred — the text abbreviates)
- **no jāṇai** = does not recognize / does not acknowledge
- **no maṇṇai** = does not respect / does not honor

### 5. COMMENTARY

The word *mucciae* — infatuation, entanglement — is one of Jain philosophy's key terms for the mechanism by which souls destroy themselves. Simhasena had five hundred wives, each with her own chamber and dowry, each the daughter of a noble family. His total absorption in one and complete neglect of all others is not merely a personal failing — it is a political and moral catastrophe. Four hundred ninety-nine families had given their daughters in good faith; those daughters are now invisible to him. Jain thought identifies this kind of obsessive favoritism as a form of violence — it extinguishes the dignity of others through complete inattention. The inequality of his love is the first sin that will compound into murder.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simhasena became so obsessed with one queen, Shyama, that he completely ignored and disrespected all his other 499 wives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In my own life, am I guilty of a similar narrowness — giving all my care to one person or thing while those equally deserving of my attention fade into invisibility?

## SUTRA 9.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तासिं चउण्हं सयाणं देवीणं माया-पिऊ जाणंति — सीहसेणे राया सामाए मुच्चिए, सेसाओ नो जाणइ, नो मण्णइ — ता सामायं अग्गिणा वा, विसेणं वा, सत्थेणं वा घाएमो। सामा जाणइ, उव्विग्गा कोवभवणे णिसिण्णा ।
॥९.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tāsiṃ cauṇhaṃ sayāṇaṃ devīṇaṃ māyā-piū jāṇaṃti — sīhaseṇe rāyā sāmāe mucciae, sesāo no jāṇai, no maṇṇai — tā sāmāyaṃ aggiṇā vā, viseṇaṃ vā, sattheṇaṃ vā ghāemo. sāmā jāṇai, uvvigā kovabhavaṇe ṇisiṇṇā.
|| 9.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the parents of those four hundred ninety-nine queens learned that Simhasena was absorbed in Shyama and no longer acknowledged or honored their daughters, and collectively resolved to kill Shyama — by fire, or poison, or weapon; Shyama learned of this, became frightened, and sat in the pouting chamber.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **māyā-piū** = mothers and fathers (parents of the 499 queens)
- **sāmāyaṃ ghāemo** = let us kill Shyama
- **aggiṇā vā, viseṇaṃ vā, sattheṇaṃ vā** = by fire, or by poison, or by weapon (three modes of killing)
- **uvvigā** = frightened, agitated, alarmed
- **kovabhavaṇe ṇisiṇṇā** = sat in the pouting chamber (lit. anger-room; the chamber where an upset wife withdraws)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The collective rage of four hundred ninety-nine sets of parents is entirely human and entirely comprehensible — their daughters have been dishonored, their alliances rendered meaningless, their family pride humiliated. Yet the resolution to commit murder, even in response to genuine injustice, is a choice that plants catastrophic karmic seeds. The three methods considered — fire, poison, weapon — are precisely the three instruments by which violence is typically carried out, and Jain scripture is deliberate in naming them: the thought of murder is itself a sinful act. Shyama's retreat to the pouting chamber is a courtly convention — a wife withdraws there to signal distress and summon the king's attention. Her fear, though understandable, sets in motion the sequence that will result in mass killing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The families of the 499 neglected wives were furious and plotted to kill Shyama. When Shyama found out, she was terrified and hid in a private room.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I feel threatened or wronged, do I examine whether my reaction — however justified it seems — might create greater harm than the original offense?

## SUTRA 9.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सीहसेणे राया सामायं कोवभवणे जाणइ, पच्चासण्णे जाइ: "किण्णु णं तुमं एत्थ निसण्णा?" सामा देवी सव्वं णिवेदेइ। सीहसेणे राया समस्सेइ, उवाए चिंतेइ ।
॥९.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

sīhaseṇe rāyā sāmāyaṃ kovabhavaṇe jāṇai, paccāsaṇṇe jāi: "kiṇṇu ṇaṃ tumaṃ ettha ṇisaṇṇā?" sāmā devī savvaṃ ṇivedei. sīhaseṇe rāyā sammassei, uvāe ciṃtei.
|| 9.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

King Simhasena learned that Shyama was in the pouting chamber, went near her, and asked: "Why are you sitting here?" Queen Shyama told him everything; King Simhasena comforted her and began to think of a plan.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kovabhavaṇe** = pouting chamber / anger-room (royal convention; the private room where a wife goes when upset)
- **paccāsaṇṇe jāi** = went nearby / approached
- **kiṇṇu ṇaṃ tumaṃ ettha ṇisaṇṇā** = why are you seated here?
- **savvaṃ ṇivedei** = told him everything / disclosed the full situation
- **sammassei** = comforted her / reassured her
- **uvāe ciṃtei** = thought of/planned a scheme/strategy (uvāe = upāya = means, plan, strategy)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Simhasena's immediate response to Shyama's disclosure is not reflection on his own role in creating this crisis — it is strategic planning. He does not consider that his own neglect of four hundred ninety-nine wives caused this situation. He does not ask whether reconciliation or fairness might resolve the conflict. He simply asks: how do I protect Shyama? This is the king's fundamental moral failure — his infatuation has so completely narrowed his vision that he cannot see the full scope of what he has done. The word *uvāe* (strategy, scheme) is significant: it signals that the king is about to take deliberate, premeditated action, not act in passion. What follows is therefore not impulsive violence but planned murder — which carries heavier karmic weight.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king went to Shyama, asked why she was hiding, heard the whole story, comforted her, and started plotting a response.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone I love is in danger, do I first examine whether my own choices created the danger — or do I rush straight to "fixing" it without honest self-reflection?

## SUTRA 9.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सीहसेणे राया अम्मच्च-पुरिसे सद्दावेइ: "खिप्पामेव सुप्पइट्ठाए नयरीए पच्चत्थिमेणं एगं महं कूडागारसालं करेह — बहु-खंभ-जुत्तं, महत्ति-सभं" — देसि-देसि घोसावेइ: "चउण्हं सयाणं देवीणं मायरो आगच्छंतु" ।
॥९.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sīhaseṇe rāyā ammacca-purise saddāvei: "khippāmeva suppaïṭṭhāe nayarīe paccatthimeṇaṃ egaṃ mahaṃ kūḍāgārasālaṃ kareh — bahu-khaṃbha-juttaṃ, mahatti-sabhaṃ" — desi-desi ghosāvei: "cauṇhaṃ sayāṇaṃ devīṇaṃ māyaro āgacchaṃtu."
|| 9.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Simhasena summoned his ministers and attendants: "Quickly build a great festival hall to the west of Supratishtha city — fitted with many pillars, a magnificent assembly-hall" — and had it announced in every direction: "Let the mothers of the four hundred ninety-nine queens come."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ammacca-purise saddāvei** = summoned ministers and attendants
- **paccatthimeṇaṃ** = to the west
- **kūḍāgārasālaṃ** = festival hall / gathering hall (kūḍāgāra = peaked structure, grand hall; sāla = hall)
- **bahu-khaṃbha-juttaṃ** = fitted with many pillars (grand, impressive construction)
- **mahatti-sabhaṃ** = magnificent assembly hall
- **desi-desi ghosāvei** = had it announced/proclaimed in every direction
- **māyaro āgacchaṃtu** = let the mothers come

### 5. COMMENTARY

Simhasena's plan is chilling in its calculated deception. He builds a grand hall and publicly invites the mothers — not to address their grievances, not to negotiate peace, but to lure them into a trap. The hall built "outside the city to the west" is significant: west in Indian spatial symbolism is associated with endings and death. The grandeur of the invitation — a magnificent pillared hall — is designed to disarm suspicion. The mothers who plotted murder are being murdered themselves, not in hot anger but through cold premeditation. Jain ethics make no exception for "justified" revenge: deliberate, planned killing is the heaviest karma, regardless of the perceived righteousness of the motive. Simhasena is about to destroy hundreds of lives to protect one person.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simhasena secretly planned a trap: he had a beautiful grand hall built outside the city and invited all 499 mothers to attend a celebration.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I plan a response to someone who has wronged me, am I guided by justice and truth — or by the desire to win and make them suffer?

## SUTRA 9.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं चउण्हं सयाणं देवीणं माया-पिऊ अलंकिया-विभूसिया पुप्फ-वत्थ-गंध-माल्ल-विलेवणाइं गेण्हित्ता, महय-महेण सुप्पइट्ठं नयरिं मज्झंमज्झेणं हत्थिखंधवरगया जाव आगच्छंति। सीहसेणे राया कूडागारसालाए उवस्सयं देइ ।
॥९.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ cauṇhaṃ sayāṇaṃ devīṇaṃ māyā-piū alaṃkiyā-vibhūsiyā puppha-vattha-gaṃdha-mālla-vilevaNāïṃ geṇhittā, mahaya-maheṇa suppaïṭṭhaṃ nayariṃ majjhaṃmajjheṇaṃ hatthikhaṃdhavaragayā jāva āgacchaṃti. sīhaseṇe rāyā kūḍāgārasālāe uvassayaṃ dei.
|| 9.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the mothers of the four hundred ninety-nine queens, adorned and decorated, taking flowers, garments, perfumes, garlands, and unguents, arrived through the middle of Supratishtha city with great pomp riding on fine elephant backs; King Simhasena gave them lodging in the festival hall.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **alaṃkiyā-vibhūsiyā** = adorned and decorated (dressed in their finest)
- **puppha-vattha-gaṃdha-mālla-vilevaNāïṃ** = flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, unguents (the five gifts of honor)
- **mahaya-maheṇa** = with great pomp / festivity (mahotsava)
- **hatthikhaṃdhavaragayā** = riding on the backs of fine/choice elephants
- **majjhaṃmajjheṇaṃ** = through the very middle (of the city — publicly, ceremonially)
- **uvassayaṃ dei** = gave them lodging / quarters (assigned them rooms)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The mothers' arrival is described in celebratory terms — they came adorned, riding elephants, bearing gifts of honor, parading through the city center. This is deliberately contrasted with what is about to happen to them. They came in their finest garments; they will leave as ash. They arrived on elephant-back in honor; they will die in fire in dishonor. Jain narrative art uses such vivid contrasts to underscore the fragility of worldly grandeur — celebration and destruction are not separated by years or lifetimes, but by a single night. The mothers who plotted murder through fire, poison, and weapon are themselves about to be killed by fire. The karma they generated toward Shyama now falls upon them with perfect precision.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

All 499 mothers came dressed in their finest clothes, riding elephants, thinking they were going to a grand celebration. The king welcomed them and gave them rooms in his new hall.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have I ever walked into a situation full of confidence and celebration, unaware that my past actions had already determined the outcome?

## SUTRA 9.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सीहसेणे राया अम्मच्चे सद्दावेइ: "खिप्पामेव कूडागारसालं अन्नपाण-विभवेणं, चउव्विहेणं आहारेणं, छव्विहेण सुराए — मेरगाए, जाइयाए, सीहुणा, पसण्णाए, सुराए, मज्जेणं — पुप्फ-वत्थ-गंध-माल्ल-विलेवणाइं संपादेह"। ताए रायं पज्जुवासंति ।
॥९.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sīhaseṇe rāyā ammacce saddāvei: "khippāmeva kūḍāgārasālaṃ annapāṇa-vibhaveṇaṃ, cauvviheṇaṃ āhāreṇaṃ, chavviheṇa surāe — meragāe, jāiyāe, sīhuṇā, pasaṇṇāe, surāe, majjeṇaṃ — puppha-vattha-gaṃdha-mālla-vilevaNāïṃ saṃpādeh". tāe rāyaṃ pajjuvasaṃti.
|| 9.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Simhasena summoned his ministers: "Immediately arrange the festival hall with abundance of food and drink — four kinds of food, six kinds of liquor: merag, jai, sidhu, pasanna, sura, and madhu — along with flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, and unguents"; and they attended upon the king in that (celebration).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **annapāṇa-vibhaveṇaṃ** = with abundance of food and drink
- **cauvviheṇaṃ āhāreṇaṃ** = four kinds of food (eatable, drinkable, lickable, tasteable — the Jain classification of food)
- **chavviheṇa surāe** = six kinds of liquor/fermented drinks
- **meragāe** = merag (a fermented grain drink)
- **jāiyāe** = jai (a type of distilled spirit)
- **sīhuṇā** = sidhu (fermented sugarcane/date spirit)
- **pasaṇṇāe** = pasanna (clarified/settled ferment)
- **surāe** = sura (general grain liquor)
- **majjeṇaṃ** = madhu (honey-wine/mead)
- **puppha-vattha-gaṃdha-mālla-vilevaNāïṃ** = flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, unguents

### 5. COMMENTARY

The feast is lavish and specific — four categories of food, six named varieties of liquor, the full five gifts of celebration. This level of detail serves a purpose: the mothers are being given every earthly pleasure before being destroyed. In Jain thought, intoxication itself (all six liquors are named) is already a sinful act — it dulls the awareness of the soul. The mothers are being welcomed with the very things that dim consciousness, making them more vulnerable and less capable of perceiving danger. The irony is profound: they came to celebrate their daughters' marriages to a king, and the king now feeds them the feast of the condemned. Every cup they raise in celebration compounds the tragedy of their final night.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king arranged a massive feast in the hall — all kinds of food and six types of wine — and the mothers enjoyed the celebration through the night.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I ever let pleasure, comfort, or festivity lull me into a false sense of safety — missing the signs of what is actually happening around me?

## SUTRA 9.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से सीहसेणे राया अद्धरत्ताकालसमयंसि बहूहिं पुरिसेहिं सद्धिं कूडागारसालं पडिबंधइ, चउद्दिसिं अग्गिं दलयइ। चउण्हं सयाणं देवीणं माया-पिऊ — अणाहा, असरणा — रुवंता-कंदंता-विलवंता कालमासे कालं करेंति ।
॥९.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se sīhaseṇe rāyā addhara-ttākālasamayaṃsi bahūhi purisehiṃ saddhiṃ kūḍāgārasālaṃ paḍibandhai, catuddisiṃ aggiṃ dalayai. cauṇhaṃ sayāṇaṃ devīṇaṃ māyā-piū — aṇāhā, asaraṇā — ruvaṃtā-kaṃdaṃtā-vilavasṃtā kālamāse kālaṃ kareṃti.
|| 9.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Simhasena, at the time of midnight, along with many men, had all the gates of the festival hall locked, and set fire on all four sides; the mothers of the four hundred ninety-nine queens — helpless, with no refuge — died crying, wailing, and lamenting.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **addhara-ttākālasamayaṃsi** = at the time of midnight
- **paḍibandhai** = had locked / sealed / blocked (the gates)
- **catuddisiṃ aggiṃ dalayai** = set fire on all four sides/directions
- **aṇāhā** = helpless / without protector (an + nātha = without lord/protector)
- **asaraṇā** = without refuge / without shelter
- **ruvaṃtā-kaṃdaṃtā-vilavasṃtā** = crying, wailing, lamenting (three intensifying terms for distress)
- **kālamāse kālaṃ kareṃti** = died at the time of death (= met their end)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra contains one of the most horrifying acts in the entire Vipaak Sutra. At midnight — when the victims are asleep or drunk — the king locks the hall and sets fire on all four sides. The image of four hundred ninety-nine mothers dying in fire, "helpless and without refuge," is almost unbearable. The two words *aṇāhā* and *asaraṇā* — "without protector, without refuge" — are the Jain meditation on the existential condition of the soul bound in wrong views: utterly alone in its suffering, with nowhere to turn. These words also echo the Jain teaching that the only true refuge is the liberated soul, the teaching, and the community of practitioners — and that worldly power, royal protection, and kinship bonds dissolve in the moment of death. Simhasena, at the moment of this act, accumulates the heaviest possible karma — the deliberate, premeditated killing of hundreds of living beings.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

At midnight, Simhasena locked the hall and set fire to all four sides. All 499 mothers died trapped inside, crying and helpless.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a "fire" I have set — a decision, a word, a withdrawal of care — that has left someone else helpless and without refuge?

## SUTRA 9.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से सीहसेणे राया तेहिं कम्मेहिं तित्तालीससयवाससहस्साउयं छट्ठीए पुढवीए नेरइयत्ताए उवविण्णे — बावीसं सागरोवमाणि ठिई। तओ उव्वट्टिय रोहिडए नयरे दत्तस्स गाहावइस्स कण्हसिरीए भारियाए कुच्छिंसि देवदत्ता णाम धूया उप्पण्णा ।
॥९.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se sīhaseṇe rāyā tehiṃ kammehiṃ tittālīsasayavāsasahassāuyaṃ chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe nera-iyattāe uvavīṇṇe — bāvīsaṃ sāgarovamāṇi ṭhiī. tao uvvaṭṭiya rohiḍae nayare dattassa gāhavais-sa kaṇhasirīe bhāriyāe kucchiṃsi devadattā nāma dhūyā uppanṇā.
|| 9.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Simhasena, through those deeds, with a lifespan of thirty-four thousand years, was reborn as a hellish being in the sixth hell — a duration of twenty-two ocean-measure periods; emerging from the sixth hell, he was reborn in Rohitak city as a daughter named Devadatta in the womb of Krishnashri, wife of merchant Datt.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tittālīssaya-vāsasahassāuyaṃ** = lifespan of thirty-four hundred thousand years (tittālīsa = 34; saya = hundred; vāsa = years; sahassa = thousand — 3,400,000 years in hell life)
- **chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe** = sixth layer/earth (6th hell; the second deepest in Jain cosmology)
- **nera-iyattāe uvavīṇṇe** = entered/was born as a hellish being
- **bāvīsaṃ sāgarovamāṇi** = twenty-two ocean-measure periods (sāgaropama = one of the largest Jain time units, a near-infinite ocean of time)
- **ṭhiī** = duration / lifespan (in hell)
- **tao uvvaṭṭiya** = having emerged / after ascending from there
- **kucchiṃsi** = in the womb

### 5. COMMENTARY

The karmic arithmetic of Simhasena's hell-sentence is precise: 3,400,000 years in the sixth hell, enduring 22 sagaropam of suffering. The sixth hell is one of the most intense realms of suffering in Jain cosmology — the pain there is barely describable, inflicted by the natural conditions of darkness, cold, heat, and the attacks of other hellish beings. This is the proportional response of the universe to the deliberate incineration of nearly five hundred human beings. When the sentence is complete, the soul does not instantly become free — it is reborn in a human body still carrying the residual karma, which must now ripen in human form. The birth as Devadatta — beautiful, in a merchant's family, in a comfortable city — represents the exhausted positive karma from prior lives, while the remaining negative karma prepares to bear its next fruit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Because of what he did, Simhasena was reborn in the deepest hell and suffered there for an unimaginable length of time. When he finally came out, his soul was reborn as Devadatta — the beautiful girl in the merchant's house.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I truly believe that no action — however hidden, however powerful the one who commits it — escapes the accounting of karma?

## SUTRA 9.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं कण्हसिरी देवी नव-मास-परिपुण्णे देवदत्तं धूयं पसूया — सुकुमालपाणिपाया, सुहुम-सुजाय-सव्वंग-संपन्ना। देवदत्ता णामं काहामो। पंचहिं धाईहिं परिवुडा ।
॥९.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ kaṇhasirī devī nava-māsa-paripuṇṇe devadattaṃ dhūyaṃ pasūyā — sukumālapāṇipāyā, suhuma-sujāya-savvaṃga-saṃpannā. devadattā nāmaṃ kāhāmo. paṃcahi dhāīhi parivuḍā.
|| 9.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Krishnashri, after nine complete months, gave birth to a daughter — soft-handed and soft-footed, with fine, well-formed, complete limbs; they named her Devadatta; she grew up attended by five nurses.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **nava-māsa-paripuṇṇe** = after nine months fully complete (standard gestation formula)
- **pasūyā** = gave birth
- **sukumālapāṇipāyā** = with soft/delicate hands and feet (sign of nobility and refinement)
- **suhuma-sujāya-savvaṃga-saṃpannā** = with fine, well-formed, all-limbs-complete body
- **devadattā nāmaṃ kāhāmo** = we will call her Devadatta (naming declaration)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The birth description emphasizes physical perfection: soft hands and feet, fine and complete limbs. This is the outward expression of accumulated positive karma from lives of virtue — even after a sojourn in the sixth hell, residual merit can produce a beautiful body. The five nurses attending Devadatta's infancy signal that she is born into comfort, protected, cherished. Yet the Jain listener knows exactly what this beautiful child carries inside: the karmic residue of burning five hundred mothers alive. The contrast between the tender image of a baby in the care of nurses and the catastrophic karma hidden within her consciousness is one of the most powerful teachings of Jain karmic philosophy — that appearances reveal nothing of the soul's true history.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Krishnashri gave birth to Devadatta — a perfectly formed, beautiful baby — and she was raised with love and care by five nurses.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I meet someone born into comfort and beauty, can I hold the awareness that every soul carries layers of history I cannot see — layers that will shape what unfolds?

## SUTRA 9.17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

॥९.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sā devadattā aṇukkameṇa vaḍḍhamāṇī jovvanagaṃ pattā — abhirūvā. egayā sā devadattā nhāyā-majjiyā-suvibhūsiyā agāsatale bahūhi dāsīhi saddhiṃ kīlai.
|| 9.17 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Devadatta, growing gradually, reached the age of youth — she was exquisitely beautiful; one day, bathed, anointed, and adorned, she was playing on the rooftop with many maidservants.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jovvanagaṃ pattā** = reached the stage of youth
- **abhirūvā** = exquisitely beautiful / having surpassing form
- **nhāyā-majjiyā-suvibhūsiyā** = bathed, anointed (with oils), and beautifully adorned/decorated

### 5. COMMENTARY

The scene of Devadatta on the rooftop — bathed, adorned, playing with servants — is one of elevated social grace. Jain scripture often places these pivotal moments in beautiful, everyday settings to underscore how karma does not arrive dramatically — it arrives through the ordinary unfolding of life, appearance, desire, and consequence. The root cause is not the rooftop but the karma from 3,400,000 years ago.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Devadatta grew into a strikingly beautiful young woman, and one day she was playing on her rooftop with her maids when everything changed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there an ordinary moment in my life — a door I once opened casually — whose full consequences I am still living through?

King Vaishramandatt was out for a horse ride when he happened to pass Datt's house and spotted Devadatta on the rooftop.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When desire arises in me — for a person, a thing, a situation — can I pause to ask whether I am seeing clearly, or whether I am simply reacting to surface beauty?

tae ṇaṃ se vesamaṇadatte rāyā purise pucchai: "kā ṇaṃ esā?" purisā bhaṇaṃti: "dattassa gāhavais-sa dhūyā, kaṇhasirīe puttī, devadattā nāma — abhirūvā, paḍirūvā, manoramā."

- **devadattā nāma** = named Devadatta
- **abhirūvā** = exquisitely beautiful
- **paḍirūvā** = well-formed / matching beauty (form suited to her name)

The attendants' description of Devadatta uses three progressive terms of beauty — *abhirūvā* (surpassing in form), *paḍirūvā* (perfectly proportioned), *manoramā* (captivating to the mind) — building from the physical outward to the inner impression. This cascade of descriptors mirrors the king's escalating attention. The inquiry "who is she?" is one of the most consequential questions in this chapter: it triggers the social machinery that will lift Devadatta from merchant's daughter to queen — and from queen to condemned prisoner. The attendants' answer is accurate, complete, and innocent; they could not have known that they were providing the information that would eventually lead to her public execution.

## SUTRA 9.25

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सा देवदत्ता देवी अद्धरत्ताए जागरमाणी एवं चिंतेइ: "पुप्फणंदी राया सिरिदेवी माउए मुच्चिए — ण सक्केमि पुप्फणंदिणा सद्धिं रयणिभोगे वा दिवसभोगे वा भुंजित्तए। सिरिदेवी यदि अग्गिणा वा, विसेणं वा, सत्थेणं वा, मंतेणं वा मरिज्जा, तया णं अहं पुप्फणंदिणा सद्धिं सुहभोगे भुंजिस्सामि।" छिद्दं विवरं पेहेइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sā devadattā devī addhara-ttāe jāgaramāṇī evaṃ ciṃtei: "pupphaNaṃdī rāyā siridevi māue mucciae — ṇa sakkemi pupphaNaṃdiṇā saddhiṃ rayaṇibhoge vā divasabhoge vā bhuṃjittae. siridevi yadi aggiṇā vā, viseṇaṃ vā, satthenāṃ vā, maṃteṇaṃ vā marijjā, tayā ṇaṃ ahaṃ pupphaNaṃdiṇā saddhiṃ subhabhoge bhuṃjissāmi." chiddaṃ vivaraṃ pehei.

- **addhara-ttāe jāgaramāṇī** = lying awake at midnight

- **rayaṇibhoge vā divasabhoge vā** = pleasures of the night or pleasures of the day (full marital enjoyment)
- **aggiṇā vā, viseṇaṃ vā, satthenāṃ vā, maṃteṇaṃ vā** = by fire, or poison, or weapon, or mantra (four modes of killing)

The karma has come full circle. The four methods of murder she contemplates — fire, poison, weapon, mantra — also echo the four methods the 499 mothers planned against Shyama. What was plotted against Shyama, what was executed against those mothers, now ripens in Devadatta's own midnight mind. Karma does not just transfer from one person to another; it mirrors itself with astonishing precision.

In the middle of the night, Devadatta lay awake thinking: if only Pushpnandi's mother were dead, she could have him all to herself. She began looking for a chance to kill her.

## SUTRA 9.28

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से पुप्फणंदी राया थोवंपि विसण्णे। अणेगेहिं राय-महासेट्ठि-सत्थवाह-मित्त-नाइहिं सद्धिं सिरिदेवीए उडुच्चारं-पडिच्चारं-मच्छरीयकम्मं काऊण, देवदत्तं गाहावेइ। देवदत्ताए कण्णनासिया छिणिज्जइ, गेरूएणं विलिप्पइ, मरण-वत्थं परिहिज्जइ, हत्थेहिं बज्झइ, सवण-पुप्फ-माला गले दिज्जइ, सूलम्मि आरोविज्जइ — रायमग्गेणं हिंडिज्जइ। भगवं: "एत्तो देवदत्ताए पुव्वकम्मविवागे" ।
॥९.२८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se pupphaNaṃdī rāyā thōvaṃpi visaṇṇe. aṇegěhiṃ rāya-mahāseṭṭhi-satthavāha-mitta-nāihiṃ saddhiṃ siridevīe uḍuccāraṃ-paḍiccāraṃ-maccharīyakammaṃ kāūṇa, devadattaṃ gāhāvei. devadattāe kaṇṇanāsiyā chiṇijjai, gerūeṇaṃ vilippai, maraṇa-vatthaṃ parihijjai, hatthehiṃ bajjhai, savaṇa-pupphamālā gale dijjai, sūlammi ārovijjai — rāyamaggeṇaṃ hiṃḍijjai. bhagavaṃ: "etto devadattāe pubbakammavivaāge."
|| 9.28 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Pushpnandi recovered from his grief after a short while, and with many kings, great merchants, caravan leaders, friends, and relatives, performed the funeral rites for Shridevi; then had Devadatta seized; Devadatta's ears and nose were cut, her body smeared with red ocher, dressed in death-garments, hands bound, a red flower garland placed around her neck, impaled on a spike, and paraded on the royal road. The Blessed One said: "This is the fruition of Devadatta's past karma."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **thōvaṃpi visaṇṇe** = recovered after a little while (from grief and shock)
- **uḍuccāraṃ-paḍiccāraṃ-maccharīyakammaṃ** = funeral rites (lifting, cremation, mourning — same three-stage formula as sutra 23)
- **gāhāvei** = had (her) seized / arrested
- **kaṇṇanāsiyā chiṇijjai** = ears and nose are cut off
- **gerūeṇaṃ vilippai** = body smeared with red ocher
- **maraṇa-vatthaṃ parihijjai** = dressed in death-garments
- **hatthehiṃ bajjhai** = hands bound (with iron shackles)
- **savaṇa-pupphamālā gale** = red flower garland placed around the neck (mark of the condemned)
- **sūlammi ārovijjai** = raised/impaled on a spike
- **rāyamaggeṇaṃ hiṃḍijjai** = paraded on the royal road
- **pubbakammavivaāge** = the fruition/ripening of past karma (pūrva-karma-vipāka)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The punishment Devadatta receives mirrors with cruel precision what she did to others. She burned mothers: she is now publicly destroyed. She mutilated sleeping Shridevi from within: she now has her face mutilated externally for all to see. She stole Shridevi's life in secret darkness: she is now paraded in the full light of the royal road. Mahavir's concluding statement — "this is the fruition of Devadatta's past karma" — is not a judgment but a teaching: the Blessed One is not condemning Devadatta, he is completing the explanation that Gautam asked for at the beginning. The phrase *pūrva-karma-vipāka* (fruition of past karma) is the central teaching term of the entire Vipaak Sutra. Every detail of Devadatta's punishment corresponds to a past act — ears and nose cut because she mutilated Shridevi; public death because she ended lives in secret; fire of cruelty returns as fire of shame.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Pushpnandi, after grieving, had Devadatta arrested, mutilated, dressed for death, and publicly impaled — that horrifying scene Gautam saw on the road. Mahavir said: this is exactly what her past karma had ripened into.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can I trace a present difficulty in my life — a consequence I am living — back to a choice I made, and can I accept it with understanding rather than resentment?

## SUTRA 9.29

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सा देवदत्ता देवी असीतिं वाससयं परमाउयं पालेइत्ता, कालमासे कालं किच्चा, रयणप्पभाए पुढवीए नेरइयत्ताए उवविण्णा। तओ उव्वट्टिय वणस्साइ-काइयत्ताए, अण्णेसु य योणीसु, पुणो गंगापुरे हंसत्ताए उवविण्णा — लुद्धेहिं हया। पुणो गंगापुरे सेट्ठिपुत्तत्ताए उवविण्णा — धम्मं सोच्चा, संजमं पडिवज्जिय, सोहम्मे कप्पे देवत्ताए उवविण्णा। तओ मज्झिमे महाविदेहे वासे मणुस्सत्ताए उवविण्णा — णाण-दंसण-चरित्त-आराहणाए, सव्वकम्मे खवेत्ता, सिज्झिस्सइ जाव सव्वदुक्खाणमंतं करेहिति ।
॥९.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sā devadattā devī asītiṃ vāssayaṃ paramāuyaṃ pāleittā, kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā, rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe nera-iyattāe uvavīṇṇā. tao uvvaṭṭiya vaṇassāi-kāiyattāe, aṇṇesu ya yoṇīsu, puṇo gaṃgāpure haṃsattāe uvavīṇṇā — luddehiṃ hayā. puṇo gaṃgāpure seṭṭhiputt-attāe uvavīṇṇā — dhammaṃ soccā, saṃjamaṃ paḍivajjiya, sohamme kappe devattāe uvavīṇṇā. tao majjhime mahāvidehe vāse maNussattāe uvavīṇṇā — ṇāṇa-daṃsaṇa-caritta-ārāhaṇāe, savvakamme khavettā, sijjhissai jāva savvadukkkhāṇamaṃtaṃ karehiti.
|| 9.29 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then queen Devadatta, having lived out a remaining lifespan of eighty years, died in due course, and was reborn as a hellish being in the first hell (Ratnaprabha); emerging from there, she passed through plant-body existences and other life-forms, then was reborn as a goose in Gangapur city — killed by hunters; then reborn as a merchant's son in Gangapur — hearing the teaching, taking up restraint, born in Saudhama heaven; then taking human birth in the middle Mahavideha realm — through the practice of knowledge, perception, and right conduct, having extinguished all karma, she will attain liberation, ending all suffering forever.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **asītiṃ vāssayaṃ paramāuyaṃ** = eighty years of remaining lifespan (the total life minus the time already suffered on the spike)
- **rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe** = in Ratnaprabha, the first/top layer of hell (the mildest of the seven hells)
- **vaṇassāi-kāiyattāe** = as plant-body beings (one-sensed living entities in vegetation)
- **gaṃgāpure haṃsattāe** = as a goose in Gangapur city (haṃsa = goose/swan; Gangapur = city by the Ganges)
- **luddehiṃ hayā** = killed by hunters
- **seṭṭhiputt-attāe** = as a merchant's son (seṭṭhi = wealthy merchant, financier)
- **dhammaṃ soccā** = having heard the teaching (dharma)
- **saṃjamaṃ paḍivajjiya** = having taken up restraint (monastic or ethical discipline)
- **sohamme kappe** = in Saudhama heaven (first of the divine realms)
- **majjhime mahāvidehe** = in the middle Mahavideha realm (Mahavideha = realm where liberation is possible in the current cosmic cycle)
- **ṇāṇa-daṃsaṇa-caritta-ārāhaṇāe** = through the practice/worship of knowledge, perception, and right conduct (the three jewels of Jain practice)
- **savvakamme khavettā** = having extinguished/destroyed all karma
- **sijjhissai** = will attain liberation
- **savvadukkkhāṇamaṃtaṃ karehiti** = will make an end of all suffering

### 5. COMMENTARY

The final sutra of Chapter 9 is simultaneously a map of suffering and a declaration of ultimate hope. Devadatta's path forward passes through the first hell (lighter than Simhasena's sixth), through plant-body existences, then through an animal birth as a goose (killed again — karma still ripening), then finally to a human birth as a merchant's son who hears the teaching, takes up the path, and rises to heaven and ultimately to the Mahavideha realm where liberation becomes possible. The three jewels — knowledge, perception, right conduct — are named as the vehicle of final liberation. Jain theology insists that no soul, no matter how deep its fall, is permanently lost. The path back is always open; it is merely longer for some souls than for others. Devadatta's liberation is assured — not because she was good in this life, but because karma, once fully exhausted, leaves the soul in its pure, radiant, liberated state. This is the ultimate teaching of the Vipaak Sutra: even the most terrible karma eventually ends, and the soul's nature is eternally free.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After living out the rest of her life, Devadatta went to hell, then wandered through many lower life-forms, and eventually — after hearing the teaching and practicing it sincerely — she will be liberated from all suffering forever.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If I truly believed that liberation is the guaranteed destination of every soul — including mine — would I walk this path with more trust, more patience, and more compassion for those who seem furthest from it?
