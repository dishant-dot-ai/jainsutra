# Sukha Vipaak Sutra - Chapter 5: Jindas (જિનદાસ)

**Source:** Vipaak Sutra, Second Shrutaskandha (Sukha Vipaak), Chapter 5
**Subject:** The story of Jindas (Jinadasa) and the fruits of good karma
**Total Sutras:** 2
**Form:** Narrative prose (Ardhamagadhi Prakrit)
**Narrator:** Sudharmaswami to Jambukumar
**Theme:** Jindas, the grandson of King Apratihata, hears Lord Mahavira's teachings, accepts the householder's dharma, and eventually attains liberation — his spiritual merit traced back to a virtuous previous life in Manipur city.

---

## SUTRA [5.1]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
पंचमस्स उक्खेवो ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
paṃcamassa ukkhevo |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The opening of the fifth chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**पंचमस्स** = of the fifth; **उक्खेवो** = opening / commencement

### 5. COMMENTARY
This is the standard opening formula that marks the beginning of the fifth chapter of the Sukha Vipaak section. The term "ukkhevo" literally means "lifting up" or "commencement," indicating that a new narrative is about to begin. Each of the ten chapters in this section follows the same structural convention of opening with this brief declaration before proceeding to the story itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
This line simply announces: "The fifth chapter begins now."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Every new beginning carries the possibility of transformation — are you ready to receive what this story offers?

## SUTRA [5.2]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
सोगंधिया णगरी। णीलासोये उज्जाणं। सुकालो जक्खो। अप्पडिहयो राया। सुकण्हा देवी। महाचंदे कुमारे। तस्स अरहदत्ता भारिया। जिणदासो पुत्तो। तित्थयरागमणं। सावगधम्मं। पुव्वभव पुच्छा। मणिपुरं णयरे। मेहरहो राया। संभूतिविजए अणगारे पडिलाभिए जाव सिद्धे। णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स।।। पंचमं अज्झयणं समत्तं ।।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
sogaṃdhiyā ṇagarī | ṇīlāsoye ujjāṇaṃ | sukālo jakkho | appaḍihayo rāyā | sukaṇhā devī | mahācaṃde kumāre | tassa arahadattā bhāriyā | jiṇadāso putto | titthayarāgamaṇaṃ | sāvagadhammaṃ | puvvabhava pucchā | maṇipuraṃ ṇayare | meharaho rāyā | saṃbhūtivijae aṇagāre paḍilābhie jāva siddhe | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa ||| paṃcamaṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ samattaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
In the city of Saugandhika (Sogandiya), there was a garden called Nilashoka (Nilasoy). The guardian spirit was Yaksha Sukala. King Apratihata ruled there. His queen was Sukanha Devi. Their son was Prince Mahachandra (Mahachande). Mahachandra's wife was Arahadatta. Their son was Jindas (Jinadasa). The coming of the Tirthankara is described. Jindas accepted the householder's dharma. When asked about his previous birth: in the city of Manipur, there was King Meharaha. Through the monk Sambhutivijaya, Jindas attained spiritual progress, up to final liberation. The closing follows the pattern of the first chapter. The fifth chapter is completed.

⚠️ This sutra is heavily abbreviated using "jāva" (जाव) and cross-references to Chapter 1. The full narrative — including descriptions of the city's prosperity, the garden's beauty, the royal household, Lord Mahavira's arrival, the detailed past-life account, and the stages of renunciation — is meant to be expanded following the template established in the Subahukumar story.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**सोगंधिया णगरी** = the city of Saugandhika; **णीलासोये उज्जाणं** = Nilashoka garden; **सुकालो जक्खो** = Yaksha Sukala (guardian spirit); **अप्पडिहयो राया** = King Apratihata (the unobstructed one); **सुकण्हा देवी** = Queen Sukanha; **महाचंदे कुमारे** = Prince Mahachandra; **तस्स अरहदत्ता भारिया** = his wife Arahadatta; **जिणदासो पुत्तो** = son named Jinadasa (servant of the Jina); **तित्थयरागमणं** = the coming of the Tirthankara; **सावगधम्मं** = householder's dharma (shravaka-dharma); **पुव्वभव पुच्छा** = inquiry about previous birth; **मणिपुरं णयरे** = in the city of Manipur; **मेहरहो राया** = King Meharaha; **संभूतिविजए अणगारे** = the monk Sambhutivijaya; **पडिलाभिए** = having attained; **जाव सिद्धे** = up to liberation; **णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स** = the closing follows the first chapter; **पंचमं अज्झयणं समत्तं** = the fifth chapter is completed

### 5. COMMENTARY
This compressed sutra contains the entire fifth chapter in summary form, following the well-established narrative pattern of the Sukha Vipaak section. What makes this chapter distinct is the generational structure: Jindas is not the son of the king but the grandson. King Apratihata and Queen Sukanha have a son, Prince Mahachandra, who marries Arahadatta. Their son is Jindas. This adds an extra generational layer not seen in the earlier chapters, showing that spiritual merit can manifest not just in the immediate next generation but can skip or flow through family lines in unexpected ways.

The city of Saugandhika, meaning "the fragrant one," and the Nilashoka garden establish the setting. Yaksha Sukala serves as the city's guardian spirit. When Lord Mahavira arrives and teaches, Jindas — whose very name means "servant of the Jina" — accepts the householder's dharma, the twelve-fold discipline of ethical living for laypeople.

The past-life inquiry reveals that Jindas's present good fortune traces back to a previous existence in the city of Manipur, during the reign of King Meharaha. There, through the guidance of the monk Sambhutivijaya, he accumulated the spiritual merit that bore fruit as his noble birth and eventual liberation. The narrative reinforces the central teaching of the entire Sukha Vipaak section: virtuous conduct in one life creates the conditions for spiritual success in future lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
In the city of Saugandhika, King Apratihata's grandson Jindas heard Lord Mahavira's teachings and chose the spiritual path. His good fortune came from merit earned in a past life in Manipur, where he had practiced virtue under a wise monk's guidance. He eventually attained complete liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Jindas's name means "servant of the Jina" — long before he met the teacher, his very identity pointed toward his destiny. How much of what we call "choice" is actually the flowering of seeds planted long before we were aware of them?
