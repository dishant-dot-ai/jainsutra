## SETTING THE SCENE

The seventh chapter of the Dukha Vipaak Sutra unfolds in the city of Patlikhanda, where Bhagavan Mahavir is dwelling in the Vanakhanda garden. This chapter tells the story of a soul who possessed extraordinary intelligence — the eight branches of Ayurvedic knowledge were at his command — and who used that intelligence not to heal but to harm. Dhanvantari the royal physician of Vijaypur prescribed the flesh of every species of animal and bird to his patients and consumed it himself with relish and wine, considering this the pinnacle of medical science. A soul with such capacity for good, choosing such a path of harm, accumulates sinful karma of proportional intensity. The consequences play out across the sixth hell, a long karmic journey, and ultimately the present miserable life of Umbaradatt — afflicted by sixteen simultaneous diseases, wandering from door to door begging for alms, encountered by Gautam Swami at each of the four city gates on four successive alms rounds.

## SUTRA 7.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**उक्खेवो सत्तमस्स ।**

॥७.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**ukkhevaṃ sattamassa ।**

|| 7.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The opening invocation of the seventh chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sattamassa** = of the seventh (adhyayan / chapter)
- **ukkhevaṃ** = the beginning, the lifting up, the commencement

### 5. COMMENTARY

The seventh chapter opens with the same canonical invocation that begins each chapter of the Vipaak Sutra — the single word "commencement" that signals the beginning of a new sacred account. Each such invocation is a reminder that Jain scripture is not a collection of moral fables but a living record of karmic reality — accounts of actual souls whose journeys have been witnessed by the omniscient awareness of a Tirthankara. The teacher being quoted is Bhagavan Mahavir himself, and the questioner is Gautam Swami. Together they form the channel through which these teachings reach us across two and a half millennia. The brevity of the opening sutra is itself a teaching: what follows is immeasurably larger than the word that introduces it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This is the formal opening that marks the beginning of Chapter Seven.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

As I open to this teaching, am I truly ready to receive what it has to show me about my own life and choices?

## SUTRA 7.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं पाडलिसंडे णयरे होत्था । वणखंडे णामं उज्जाणे । उंबरदत्ते जक्खे । तत्थ णं पाडलिसंडे णयरे सिद्धत्थे राया । तत्थ णं पाडलिसंडे णयरे सागरदत्ते सत्थवाहे होत्था । अड्डे जाव अपरिभूए । गंगदत्ता भारिया । तस्स सागरदत्तस्स पुत्ते गंगदत्ताए भारियाए अत्तए उंबरदत्ते णामं दारए होत्था । अहीणपडिपुण्णपंचिंदियसरीरे, वण्णओ ।**

॥७.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ pāḍalisaṃḍe ṇayare hotthā । vaṇakhaṃḍe nāmaṃ ujjāṇe । uṃbaradatte jakkhe । tattha ṇaṃ pāḍalisaṃḍe ṇayare siddhatthe rāyā । tattha ṇaṃ pāḍalisaṃḍe ṇayare sāgaradatte satthavāhe hotthā । aḍḍhe jāva aparibhūe । gaṃgadattā bhāriyā । tassa sāgaradattassa putte gaṃgadattāe bhāriyāe attae uṃbaradatte nāmaṃ dāraye hotthā । ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapaṃciṃdiyasarīre, vaṇṇao ।**

|| 7.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Thus indeed, Jambu! At that time, in that era, there was a city named Patlikhanda; a garden named Vanakhanda; a yaksha named Umbaradatt; in that city of Patlikhanda there was a king named Siddhartha; in that city of Patlikhanda there was a caravan leader named Sagaradatt — wealthy and so on, up to not to be disrespected; his wife was Gangadatta; and the son of that Sagaradatt, born from the womb of his wife Gangadatta, was a child named Umbaradatt — with a body complete in all five senses, as described elsewhere."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jaṃbū** = Jambu (the disciple to whom Sudharma Swami is narrating — the canonical frame of the Vipaak Sutra)
- **pāḍalisaṃḍe ṇayare** = in the city of Patlikhanda (*pāṭalī* = Patali/trumpet-flower tree; *khaṇḍa* = section/grove — possibly "the grove of Patali trees")
- **vaṇakhaṃḍe ujjāṇe** = the garden named Vanakhanda (*vana* = forest/garden; *khaṇḍa* = section)
- **uṃbaradatte jakkhe** = the yaksha named Umbaradatt (*umbarada* = fig tree — one associated with the fig tree; *yakṣa* = a class of powerful semi-divine beings)
- **sāgaradatte satthavāhe** = Sagaradatt the caravan leader (*sāgara* = ocean; *datta* = given — "ocean-given"; *sārthavāha* = caravan leader)
- **aḍḍhe** = wealthy, prosperous
- **jāva aparibhūe** = and so on up to "not to be disrespected/dishonored" (canonical abbreviation for the full list of qualities of a respected merchant)
- **gaṃgadattā** = Gangadatta, the wife's name (*gaṃgā* = the Ganga river; *datta* = given)
- **uṃbaradatte** = Umbaradatt, the son's name (named after the yaksha through whose grace he was born)
- **ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapaṃciṃdiyasarīre** = with a body that is complete and not deficient in the five senses (*ahīna* = not deficient; *paḍipuṇṇa* = complete, full; *paṃcindriya* = five senses; *sarīra* = body)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The opening of Chapter Seven introduces a city named Patlikhanda — a prosperous merchant city with a well-known yaksha shrine in its garden. The yaksha Umbaradatt (associated with the fig tree, considered an auspicious tree) is a powerful local deity whose shrine plays a central role in the chapter's narrative. The caravan leader Sagaradatt — wealthy, respected, beyond disrespect — represents the pinnacle of mercantile success and social standing. His son Umbaradatt, born beautiful and complete in all senses, would seem to embody the reward of such prosperity. Yet the chapter will reveal that this prosperous birth is simply the outward shell in which a soul burdened with tremendous sinful karma has arrived — and the karmic debt will manifest with devastating force within this very lifetime, reducing this apparently fortunate birth to one of extreme suffering. The naming of the child after the yaksha already signals that his birth was not straightforward — a detail explained in sutras 11–15.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The chapter is set in Patlikhanda city, where a wealthy merchant named Sagaradatt and his wife Gangadatta had a son named Umbaradatt — born beautiful and healthy.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of my current comfort, health, and social standing do I mistake for permanent security, when in fact it may be the shell of a life whose deeper karmic story is still unfolding?

## SUTRA 7.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स समोसरणं जाव परिसा पडिगया ।**

॥७.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa samoṣaraṇaṃ jāva parisā paḍigayā ।**

|| 7.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that time, in that era, the Venerable Ascetic Bhagavan Mahavir arrived and settled — and so on, up to — the assembly dispersed and returned.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **samoṣaraṇaṃ** = arrived and settled, descended (*samavasaraṇa* = the formal arrival and settling of a Tirthankara)
- **jāva** = up to and including (canonical abbreviation for the full description of Mahavir's arrival, his divine assembly hall, the sermon, and the assembly's reception)
- **parisā paḍigayā** = the assembly dispersed, went back (*parisā* = assembly, congregation; *paḍigayā* = returned, dispersed)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Bhagavan Mahavir's arrival at Patlikhanda is stated with characteristic economy — the full canonical description of his arrival, the divine assembly (samavasarana), the four-fold congregation of monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen, and the sermon — all abbreviated by *jāva* ("up to and including"). The assembly's return after receiving the teaching is the prelude to Gautam Swami's alms rounds, during which the pivotal encounter will occur. This sutra performs a structural function: it establishes that the omniscient Mahavir is present and accessible, so that when Gautam sees the suffering man and brings his question, the answer can come from the most authoritative source in the universe. Without Mahavir's presence, the karmic story of Umbaradatt would remain invisible — just another suffering man on the road.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Bhagavan Mahavir arrived at Patlikhanda, gave his teachings to the assembled people, and the assembly returned to their homes.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a source of deep wisdom in my life that I have access to but do not fully utilize?

## SUTRA 7.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणेणं भगवओ गोयमे तहेव जाव पाडलिसंडं णयरं पुरित्थिमिल्लेणं दुवारेणं अणुप्पविसइ, अणुप्पविसित्ता तत्थ णं पासइ एगं पुरिसं कच्छुल्लं कोइयं दओयरियं भगंदिरियं अरिसिल्लं कासिल्लं सासिल्लं सोइयलं सुयमूहं सुयहत्थं सुयपायं, सडियहत्थंगुलियं सडियपायंगुलियं सडियकण्णणासियं रिसयाए य पूएण य थिविथिवियं वणमुहिकिमउत्तयंतं पगलंतपूयरुहिरं लालपगलंतकण्णणासं अभिक्खणं अभिक्खणं पूयकवले य रुहिरकवले य किमिकवले य वममाणं कट्टाइं कलुणाइं विसराइं कूयमाणं मच्छियाचडगरपहकरेणं अणिण्ण जमाणमग्गं फुट्टहडाहडसीसं डंडिखंडवसणं खंडमल्ल-खंडघड-हत्थगयं, गेहे गेहे देहंबलियाए वित्ति कप्पेमाणं पासइ ।**

॥७.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavao goyame taheva jāva pāḍalisaṃḍaṃ ṇayaraṃ puritthimileṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ aṇuppivisai, aṇuppivisittā tattha ṇaṃ pāsai egaṃ purisaṃ kachullaṃ koiyaṃ daoyariyaṃ bhaṃgadiriyaṃ arisillaṃ kāsillaṃ sāsillaṃ soiyalaṃ suyamūhaṃ suyahattha ṃ suyapāyaṃ, saḍiyahatthāṃguliyaṃ saḍiyapāyāṃguliyaṃ saḍiyakaṇṇaṇāsiyaṃ risayāe ya pūeṇa ya thivithiviyaṃ vaṇamuhikimautti antaṃ pagalaṃtapūyaruhiraṃ lālapagalaṃtakaṇṇaṇāsaṃ abhikkhaṇaṃ abhikkhaṇaṃ pūyakavale ya ruhirakavale ya kimikavale ya vamamāṇaṃ kaṭṭāiṃ kaluṇāiṃ visarāiṃ kūyamāṇaṃ macchiyācaḍagarapahakareṇaṃ aṇiṇṇajamāṇamagge phuṭṭahaḍāhaḍasīsaṃ daṃḍikhaṃḍavasaṇaṃ khaṃḍamalla-khaṃḍaghaḍa-hatthagayaṃ, gehe gehe dehaṃbaliyāe vitti kappeṃmaṇaṃ pāsai ।**

|| 7.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that time, in that era, the revered Gautam — a disciple of the Venerable Ascetic — and so on just as before — entering the city of Patlikhanda through the eastern gate, and having entered, saw there a man afflicted with leprosy, jaundice, fistula, rectal disease, hemorrhoids, cough, asthma, foul odor, dog-like face, dog-like hands, dog-like feet; with rotting fingers on the hands, rotting toes on the feet, rotting ears and nose; oozing and dripping with pus and blood continually; with wounds full of worms crawling out from the wound openings; with pus, blood, and worms continuously vomiting out; crying out harsh, pitiful, miserable sounds again and again; unable to move freely on the road because of swarms of flies clustered front and back; head split and utterly wrecked; wearing torn, shredded clothing; holding broken and cracked begging bowls in his hands; going from house to house, maintaining his livelihood through the offerings of his body.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kachullaṃ** = afflicted with leprosy or severe skin disease (*kacchu* = itch, mange, severe skin disease)
- **koiyaṃ** = afflicted with jaundice (*koḍiya* = jaundice, yellowing disease)
- **daoyariyaṃ** = afflicted with fistula, a deep disease of internal passages (*daovariya* = bhagandara = anal fistula)
- **bhaṃgadiriyaṃ** = afflicted with rectal/fistular disease (*bhagaṃdira* = fistula-in-ano)
- **arisillaṃ** = afflicted with hemorrhoids (*arśas* = piles/hemorrhoids)
- **kāsillaṃ** = afflicted with cough (*kāsa* = cough)
- **sāsillaṃ** = afflicted with asthma or respiratory disease (*śvāsa* = breath/asthma)
- **soiyalaṃ** = with foul body odor, putrid smell (*saucilyam* = foulness)
- **suyamūhaṃ** = with a face like a dog (distorted, animal-like face due to disease)
- **suyahattha ṃ / suyapāyaṃ** = with hands and feet like a dog's (contracted, deformed)
- **saḍiyahatthāṃguliyaṃ** = with rotting/fallen-off fingers (*saḍita* = decayed; *aṃguli* = finger)
- **saḍiyapāyāṃguliyaṃ** = with rotting toes
- **saḍiyakaṇṇaṇāsiyaṃ** = with rotting/collapsed ears and nose
- **risayāe ya pūeṇa** = oozing pus and reddish serum (*rasa* = fluid; *pūya* = pus)
- **thivithiviyaṃ** = dripping, oozing continuously
- **vaṇamuhikimautti antaṃ** = with worms crawling out from the wound openings
- **pagalaṃtapūyaruhiraṃ** = with pus and blood flowing out
- **lālapagalaṃtakaṇṇaṇāsaṃ** = with drool and ooze coming from ears and nose
- **pūyakavale ya ruhirakavale ya kimikavale ya vamamāṇaṃ** = vomiting mouthfuls of pus, blood, and worms
- **kaluṇāiṃ visarāiṃ kūyamāṇaṃ** = crying pitiful, miserable, heart-rending sounds
- **macchiyācaḍagarapahakareṇaṃ** = because of swarms of flies thick as a blanket clustered around him
- **aṇiṇṇajamāṇamagge** = unable to move freely on the path
- **phuṭṭahaḍāhaḍasīsaṃ** = with head cracked and utterly wrecked
- **daṃḍikhaṃḍavasaṇaṃ** = wearing shredded, tattered clothing
- **khaṃḍamalla-khaṃḍaghaḍa-hatthagayaṃ** = holding broken and cracked bowls in his hands
- **dehaṃbaliyāe vitti kappeṃmaṇaṃ** = maintaining livelihood through offerings given to his suffering body (going from door to door begging)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is among the most graphically detailed descriptions of physical suffering in the Vipaak Sutra. Sixteen different diseases afflict Umbaradatt simultaneously — leprosy, jaundice, fistula, rectal diseases, hemorrhoids, cough, asthma, and more, combined with the degeneration of fingers, toes, ears, and nose, continuous discharge of pus and blood, infestations of worms, and the misery of being unable to move freely because of the flies that cluster around his body. The imagery is precise and unflinching — this is not a metaphor but a literal description of what the text tells us this man's physical condition actually was. The Vipaak Sutra does not spare us from the reality of extreme suffering, because its purpose is not comfort but understanding. The man holding broken bowls, wearing shredded cloth, going from door to door receiving whatever people throw into his broken vessel — this is the complete reversal of Dhanvantari's former life, in which he served at the table of kings and ate the finest preparations of every species. Now he subsists on scraps given in pity to his ruined body.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gautam entered the city through the eastern gate and saw a man in an almost unimaginably terrible condition — covered in diseases, rotting, oozing pus and blood, worms crawling on him, unable to even walk properly because of swarms of flies, crying pitifully and begging from door to door.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I encounter suffering — in others or in myself — can I hold the awareness that every condition has a cause, without using that awareness as an excuse to avoid compassion?

## SUTRA 7.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं से भगवं गोयमे दोच्चं पि छट्टुखमणपारणगंसि पढमाए पोरिसीए सज्झायं करेइ जाव पाडलिसंडं णयरं दाहिणिल्लेणं दुवारेणं अणुप्पविसइ, तं चेव पुरिसं पासइ — कच्छुल्लं तहेव जाव संजमेणं तवसा अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरइ ।**

॥७.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ se bhagavaṃ goyame doccaṃ pi chaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi paḍhamāe porīsīe sajjhāyaṃ karei jāva pāḍalisaṃḍaṃ ṇayaraṃ dāhiṇilleṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ aṇuppivisai, taṃ ceva purisaṃ pāsai — kachullaṃ taheva jāva saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe viharai ।**

|| 7.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that revered Gautam, on the second occasion of the alms-round after a six-day fast, having performed morning study in the first quarter of the day — and so on, up to — entered the city of Patlikhanda through the southern gate, and saw that same man — afflicted with leprosy, as described before — and so on, up to — went about cultivating the self with restraint and austerity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **doccaṃ pi** = for the second time also
- **chaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi** = on the occasion of the alms-round after a six-day fast (*chaṭṭha* = sixth; *khamaṇa* = fasting; *pāraṇa* = breaking the fast by going for alms)
- **paḍhamāe porīsīe** = in the first quarter of the day (*pauruṣī* = a quarter of the day, approximately three hours)
- **sajjhāyaṃ** = study, recitation (*svādhyāya* = self-study, study of scripture)
- **dāhiṇilleṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ** = through the southern gate
- **taṃ ceva purisaṃ** = that very same man
- **saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe** = cultivating the self with restraint and austerity (what Gautam returns to doing after collecting alms and seeing the man)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The repetition of Gautam's encounter is structurally deliberate: he sees the same man from four different directions on four different alms rounds. This is not coincidence within the narrative but a teaching device. Gautam's life as an ascetic monk involves systematic alms rounds through the city — first quarter of the day, second quarter, third quarter — and through different gates in turn. That the same man appears at each gate, in the same terrible condition, creates an unavoidable encounter. By the fourth sighting, Gautam's spiritual perception is fully engaged: this is not random suffering but a soul broadcasting its karmic story. The practice of six-day fasting (*chaṭṭha khamaṇa* — six days without food or water) followed by an alms round is a rigorous ascetic discipline, and the fact that Gautam sees this suffering man while engaged in his own spiritual practice creates a profound counterpoint: two souls at opposite ends of the karmic spectrum passing each other on the city roads.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

On his second alms round (after a six-day fast), Gautam entered from the south gate — and saw the same suffering man again.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When the same situation or person appears before me repeatedly, am I paying attention — or do I walk past as if it were coincidence?

## SUTRA 7.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं से गोयमे तच्चं पि छट्टुखमणपारणगंसि तहेव जाव पच्चत्थिमेलं दुवारेणं अणुप्पविसमाणे तं चेव पुरिसं पासइ कच्छुल्लं जाव संजमेणं तवसा अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरइ ।**

॥७.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ se goyame taccaṃ pi chaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi taheva jāva paccatthimelaṃ duvāreṇaṃ aṇuppivisamāṇe taṃ ceva purisaṃ pāsai kachullaṃ jāva saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe viharai ।**

|| 7.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that Gautam, on the third occasion of the alms-round after a six-day fast, likewise — and so on, up to — entering through the western gate, saw that very same man afflicted with leprosy — and so on, up to — went about cultivating the self with restraint and austerity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taccaṃ pi** = for the third time also
- **paccatthimelaṃ duvāreṇaṃ** = through the western gate (*pratyakṣima* = western)
- **aṇuppivisamāṇe** = while entering
- **taṃ ceva purisaṃ** = that very same man

### 5. COMMENTARY

For the third time, at the western gate, Gautam encounters the same suffering figure. The three-fold repetition before the fourth and final sighting mirrors the traditional narrative pattern of the Agamas, where things are often presented in triads before the climactic fourth occurrence. Each sighting deepens the mystery for Gautam: Who is this man? How is he everywhere? What is the story behind this magnitude of suffering? The western direction in Jain cosmological symbolism is associated with the realm of the setting sun — endings, conclusions, the dusk of one phase before another begins. That Gautam's third encounter is through the western gate subtly signals that understanding is approaching — the conclusion of the mystery is near.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A third time, entering from the western gate, Gautam saw the same terribly suffering man.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it take for me to finally stop and truly inquire into something I have walked past before?

## SUTRA 7.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तएणं भगवं गोयमे चउत्थं पि छट्टुखमणपारणगंसि उत्तरेण दुवारेण जाव तं चेव पुरिसं पासित्ता इमेयारूवे अज्झत्थिए जाव समुप्पण्णे — अहो णं इमे पुरिसे पुरापोराणाणं जाव वंदित्ता नमंसित्ता एवं वयासी — एवं खलु अहं भंते ! छट्टुखमणपारणगंसि तुभेहिं अब्भणुण्णाए जाव पाडलिसंडे पुरित्थिमिल्लेणं दुवारेणं अणुपविट्टे । तत्थ णं एगं पुरिसं पासामि कच्छुल्लं जाव वित्ति कप्पेमाणं । तए णं अहं दोच्चछट्टुखमण पारणगंसि दाहिणिल्लेणं दुवारेणं, तहेव । तए णं अहं तच्चछट्टुखमणपारणगंसि पच्चत्थिमेणं दुवारेणं, तहेव । तए णं अहं चउत्थछट्टुखमणपारणगंसि उत्तरदुवारेणं अणुप्पविसामि, तं चेव पुरिसं पासामि कच्छुल्लं जाव वित्ति कप्पेमाणं विहरइ । चिंता मम । से णं भंते ! पुरिसे पुव्वभवे के आसी ? जाव पच्चणुभवमाणे विहरइ ? गोयमा ! इति समणे भगवं महावीरे भगवं गोयमं एवं वयासी —**

॥७.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**taeṇaṃ bhagavaṃ goyame cauththaṃ pi chaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi uttareṇa duvāreṇa jāva taṃ ceva purisaṃ pāsittā imeyārūve ajjhatthie jāva samupphaṇṇe — aho ṇaṃ ime purise purāporāṇāṇaṃ jāva vaṃdittā namaṃsittā evaṃ vayāsī — evaṃ khalu ahaṃ bhaṃte! chaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi tubhehiṃ abbhaṇuṇṇāe jāva pāḍalisaṃḍe puritthimileṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ aṇupavitthe । tattha ṇaṃ egaṃ purisaṃ pāsāmi kachullaṃ jāva vitti kappeṃmāṇaṃ । tae ṇaṃ ahaṃ doccachaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi dāhiṇilleṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ, taheva । tae ṇaṃ ahaṃ taccachaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi paccatthimeṇaṃ duvāreṇaṃ, taheva । tae ṇaṃ ahaṃ cauththachaṭṭukhamaṇapāraṇaṃgasi uttaraduvāreṇaṃ aṇuppivisāmi, taṃ ceva purisaṃ pāsāmi kachullaṃ jāva vitti kappeṃmāṇaṃ viharai । ciṃtā mama । se ṇaṃ bhaṃte! purise puvvabhave ke āsī? jāva paccaṇubhavamāṇe viharai? goyamā! iti samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre bhagavaṃ goyamaṃ evaṃ vayāsī —**

|| 7.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then revered Gautam, on the fourth occasion of the alms-round after a six-day fast, through the northern gate — and so on, up to — having seen that very same man, this kind of inward thought arose — and so on — "Ah! This man is experiencing the fruit of past karma!" — and so on; having paid homage and offered obeisance, he spoke thus: "Venerable One, indeed, on my alms-round after the six-day fast, having received your permission, I entered Patlikhanda through the eastern gate; there I saw a man afflicted with leprosy and so on, maintaining his livelihood. Then on the second alms-round I entered through the southern gate — likewise. Then on the third alms-round through the western gate — likewise. Then on the fourth alms-round, entering through the northern gate, I see that very same man, afflicted with leprosy and so on, maintaining his livelihood. This troubles my mind. Venerable One, who was that man in his past life? — and so on — what karma is he experiencing?" Then the Venerable Ascetic Bhagavan Mahavir spoke thus to revered Gautam:

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **cauththaṃ pi** = for the fourth time also
- **uttareṇa duvāreṇa** = through the northern gate
- **ajjhatthie samupphaṇṇe** = an inward thought arose (*adhyātmika* = inward, spiritual; *samutpanna* = arose)
- **purāporāṇāṇaṃ** = of past karma (*pūrva-paurāṇa* = previous/ancient — past actions)
- **abbhaṇuṇṇāe** = having received permission (*abhy-anuṃjñāta* = given approval/permission)
- **ciṃtā mama** = this troubles my mind; I am concerned (*cintā* = concern, thought, preoccupation)
- **puvvabhave ke āsī** = who was he in his past life?

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth encounter triggers Gautam's full inquiry. The phrase "this troubles my mind" (*cintā mama*) is significant: Gautam's mind is not troubled by mere curiosity but by the deeper stirring of spiritual concern — the recognition that what he is seeing is not random. A mind trained in careful observation, encounter after encounter at each city gate, has finally moved to the point of formal inquiry. His report to Mahavir is precise and structured: first encounter (east gate), second (south), third (west), fourth (north) — covering all four directions, the complete compass of the city. The suffering man has appeared at every entrance. In Jain narrative symbolism, the four directions represent totality and completeness — Gautam's four-fold encounter with this suffering figure is a complete, unavoidable confrontation with the reality of karmic consequence. He cannot leave the city without seeing it. He cannot unsee it. He must understand.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

On the fourth alms round, entering from the north gate, Gautam saw the same man yet again. His heart was deeply troubled. He went to Mahavir and said: "I saw this suffering man at all four gates. Who was he? What karma is he living through?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I have seen something troubling multiple times and can no longer ignore it, what is the quality of my inquiry — do I approach it with genuine desire to understand, or with a desire to simply have my discomfort resolved?

## SUTRA 7.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**एवं खलु गोयमा ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं इहेव जंबूदीवे दीवे भारहेवासे विजयपुरे णामं णयरे होत्था । रिद्धित्थिमयसमिद्धे, वण्णओ । तत्थ णं विजयपुरे णयरे कणगरहस्स रण्णो धण्णंतरी णामं वेज्जे होत्था । अटुंगाउव्वेयपाढे, तंजहा — कुमारभिच्चं सालगे सल्लहत्ते कायितसा— गिंचा जंगोले भूयविज्जा रसायणे वाजीकरणे । सिवहत्थे सुहहत्थे लहुहत्थे ।**

॥७.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**evaṃ khalu goyamā! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ iheva jaṃbūdīve dīve bhārahevāse vijayapure nāmaṃ ṇayare hotthā । riddhitthimayasamiddhe, vaṇṇao । tattha ṇaṃ vijayapure ṇayare kaṇagarahassa raṇṇo dhaṇṇaṃtarī nāmaṃ vejje hotthā । aṭuṃgāuvveyapāḍhe, taṃjahā — kumārabhiccaṃ sālage sallahatte kāyitasā — giṃcā jaṃgole bhūyavijjā rasāyaṇe vājīkaraṇe । sivahatte suhahatte lahuhatte ।**

|| 7.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Thus indeed, Gautam! At that time, in that era, here in Jambudvipa, in the Bharata region, there was a city named Vijaypur — prosperous and well-established as described. In that city of Vijaypur, under King Kanagarha, there was a physician named Dhanvantari. He was versed in the eight branches of Ayurveda — namely: Kaumarabhritya (pediatrics), Shalakya (diseases of the upper body — eyes, ears, nose), Shalya (surgery — extraction of foreign objects), Kayachikitsa (internal medicine — bodily diseases), Jangul (toxicology — treatment of poison), Bhutavidya (mental and spiritual disorders), Rasayana (rejuvenation therapy), and Vajikaran (reproductive and vitality therapy). He was Shivahasta — one whose hand brings welfare to all; Suhahasta — one with a pleasant, beneficial hand; and Lahuhasta — one with a light, skilled, delicate hand."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vijayapure** = in the city of Vijaypur ("city of victory")
- **kaṇagarahassa raṇṇo** = under King Kanagarha (*kaṇaka* = gold; *raha* = chariot — "golden chariot" — king's name)
- **dhaṇṇaṃtarī** = Dhanvantari — the name of the mythic divine physician of the gods in Hindu tradition, used here as a proper name for this royal physician (*dhana* = wealth; *antara* = within — or the divine physician)
- **vejje** = physician (*vaidya* = one trained in Ayurveda)
- **aṭuṃgāuvveyapāḍhe** = versed in the eight branches of Ayurveda (*aṣṭāṃga* = eight-limbed/branched; *āyurveda* = the science of life)
- **kumārabhiccaṃ** = Kaumarabhritya — care and diseases of infants and children
- **sālage** = Shalakya — diseases of the eyes, ears, nose, throat (upper regions treated with thin instruments/shalaka)
- **sallahatte** = Shalya — surgery, extraction of thorns/foreign objects (*śalya* = thorn, foreign body)
- **kāyitasā** = Kayachikitsa — internal medicine, treatment of bodily diseases
- **giṃcā** = Jangul — toxicology, treatment of poisons from animals and plants (*jaṃgula* = jungle — wild poisons)
- **jaṃgole** ⚠️ = (this may be a variant transcription — possibly part of Jangul or a separate listing)
- **bhūyavijjā** = Bhutavidya — treatment of mental and spiritual disorders, psychiatric conditions (*bhūta* = spirit/being; *vidyā* = knowledge)
- **rasāyaṇe** = Rasayana — rejuvenation therapy, the science of restoring youth and vitality
- **vājīkaraṇe** = Vajikaran — reproductive medicine, treatments to enhance fertility and vitality
- **sivahatte** = Shivahasta — "auspicious hand," one whose treatment always brings welfare
- **suhahatte** = Suhahasta — "pleasant hand," one with a gentle, beneficent healing touch
- **lahuhatte** = Lahuhasta — "light hand," one with delicate surgical and therapeutic skill

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra introduces Dhanvantari with a resume of extraordinary distinction. He mastered all eight branches of Ayurveda — the complete system of ancient Indian medicine — and possessed three qualities of healing skill described by three epithets of "hand": the auspicious hand (Shivahasta), the pleasant hand (Suhahasta), and the light hand (Lahuhasta). In Ayurvedic tradition, these three qualities of the physician's hand represent the highest level of clinical excellence — the ability to diagnose through touch, to operate with precision, and to treat with a quality that is itself therapeutic. Dhanvantari was no ordinary doctor; he was, by every measure, a brilliant and accomplished healer. This makes the revelation that follows all the more striking: a soul with such capacity for healing, for reducing suffering, for benefiting living beings — chose instead to use that very knowledge to direct and sanction the killing of animals. In Jain ethics, the magnitude of sinful karma is amplified when those with greater capacity for good choose harm instead. The Vipaak Sutra is not teaching that medical knowledge is harmful; it is teaching that knowledge is powerful — and power used for violence creates correspondingly powerful karma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Mahavir began to explain: In a past life, this suffering man was a brilliant royal physician named Dhanvantari, who had mastered all eight branches of medicine and was famous for his healing skill.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What gifts, skills, or knowledge do I possess — and am I using them in ways that reduce suffering or in ways that, however subtly, contribute to it?

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the most comprehensive catalogue of prescribed meat-eating in the Vipaak Sutra. Dhanvantari's practice covers every class of living being: aquatic creatures (fish, tortoise, crocodile, gharial, monitor lizard), terrestrial mammals (goat, sheep, buffalo, pig, deer, rabbit, cow), and aerial creatures (quail, partridge, pigeon, chicken, peacock). His patients span the entire social spectrum — from queens in the royal palace to skull-bowl ascetics, from merchants to beggars. No one is exempt from his prescriptions; no species is exempt from his recommendations. Most significantly, he himself eats everything he prescribes — he is not a detached physician recommending treatments he personally abstains from. He consumes meat in every form of preparation (broth, roasted, fried, cooked) with every kind of fermented drink (six kinds named). In Jain understanding, this is the maximum possible accumulation of sinful karma related to the violence against five-sensed beings: recommending it systematically, profiting from it professionally, and consuming it personally with evident relish. The six verbs at the end — tasting, savoring, distributing, consuming — convey not just action but attitude: he does not eat reluctantly; he delights in it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Dhanvantari prescribed the meat of every kind of animal and bird — fish, tortoise, crocodile, goat, deer, peacock, and many more — to all his patients, from kings to beggars; and he himself ate all of these meats with six different kinds of liquor, relishing every bite and every drink.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have I ever used expertise, authority, or professional role to normalize or recommend something harmful — and called it "the right treatment" or "good practice"?

॥७.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ se dhaṇṇaṃtarī vejje eyakamme eyapphāṇe eyavijje eyasamāyāre subahu ṃ pāvaṃ kammaṃ samajjiṇittā battīsaṃ vassasayāiṃ paramāuyaṃ pālaittvā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā chaṭṭīe puḍhavīe ukkoseṇaṃ bāvīsasāgarovamaṃ ṭhiīe su ṇeraiesu ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇe ।**

|| 7.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that physician Dhanvantari — considering these sinful acts as the one practice, as the one excellence, as the one science, as the one way of life — having accumulated immense sinful karma, having lived out 3,200 years of the maximum lifespan, died at the appointed time and was reborn as a hellish being in the sixth hell, with a maximum lifespan of twenty-two ocean-measured time units.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **eyakamme** = considering these (sinful acts) as the one practice (*eka karma* = one action/practice — "this is my way")
- **eyapphāṇe** = considering them as the one excellence, the one standard (*eka pradhāna* = the one primary thing)
- **eyavijje** = considering them as the one science, the one expertise (*eka vidyā* = the one knowledge/science)
- **eyasamāyāre** = considering them as the one way of life (*eka samācāra* = the one conduct/custom)
- **subahu ṃ pāvaṃ kammaṃ samajjiṇittā** = having accumulated immense sinful karma (*subahula* = very much, immense; *pāpa karma* = sinful action)
- **battīsaṃ vassasayāiṃ** = 3,200 years (*battīsa* = 32; *vasasaya* = hundred years — 32 × 100 = 3,200 years)
- **paramāuyaṃ** = maximum lifespan (*parama* = maximum; *āyus* = lifespan)
- **chaṭṭīe puḍhavīe** = in the sixth hell (*ṣaṣṭha pṛthivī* = the sixth of the seven hells — called Tamahprabha or "dark light")
- **ukkoseṇaṃ bāvīsasāgarovamaṃ** = with a maximum lifespan of twenty-two ocean-measured time units (*uttarṣa* = maximum; *bāvīsa* = twenty-two; *sāgaropama* = ocean-measured unit)
- **ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇe** = was reborn as a hellish being

### 5. COMMENTARY

The four-fold description of Dhanvantari's attitude — "the one practice, the one excellence, the one science, the one way of life" — is perhaps the most psychologically penetrating phrase in this chapter. He did not reluctantly participate in meat-prescribing and meat-eating; he genuinely believed it was the right, the scientific, the optimal approach. He considered his entire medical system — built on animal flesh as medicine — to be the pinnacle of knowledge. This total conviction is itself a form of spiritual blindness (ajñāna), and in Jain thought, sinful karma accumulated with this kind of wholehearted identification is among the most severe. Having accumulated 3,200 years of such karma and lived the full extent of his lifespan, he is reborn in the sixth hell with twenty-two sagaropam of suffering — the second deepest of the seven hells, with one of the longest lifespans. The scale is staggering: twenty-two sagaropam in the near-dark of the sixth hell. The chapter then traces his emergence from that hell and rebirth as Umbaradatt.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Dhanvantari considered his meat-prescribing lifestyle to be perfect medicine and the height of his science — never questioning it. After 3,200 years of this karma, he died and was reborn in the sixth level of hell, where he suffered for an almost unimaginably long time.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are there areas of my life where I have so thoroughly convinced myself that my way is right and good, that I am no longer able to see its potential for harm?

## THE BIRTH OF UMBARADATT: GANGADATTA'S LONGING

## SUTRA 7.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं सा गंगदत्ता भारिया जायिणिदुया याविं होत्था । जाया जाया दारगा विणिहायमावज्जंति । तए णं तीसे गंगदत्ताए सत्थवाहीए अण्णया कयाइ पुव्व-रत्तावरत्तकालसमयंसि कुटुंबजागरियं जागरमाणीए अयं अज्झत्थिए जाव समुप्पण्णे — एवं खलु अहं सागरदत्तेण सत्थवाहेणं सद्धिं बहूणं वासाइं उरालाइं माणुस्सगाइं भोगभोगाइं भुंजमाणी विहरामि, णो चेव णं अहं दारगं वा दारियं वा पयामि ।**

॥७.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ sā gaṃgadattā bhāriyā jāyiṇiduyā yāviṃ hotthā । jāyā jāyā dāragā viṇihāyamāvajjaṃti । tae ṇaṃ tīse gaṃgadattāe satthavāhīe aṇṇayā kayāi puvva-rattāvarattakālasamayaṃsi kuṭuṃbajāgariyaṃ jāgaramāṇīe ayaṃ ajjhatthie jāva samupphaṇṇe — evaṃ khalu ahaṃ sāgaradatteṇa satthavāheṇaṃ saddhiṃ bahūṇaṃ vāsāiṃ urālāiṃ māṇussagāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇī viharāmi, ṇo ceva ṇaṃ ahaṃ dāragaṃ vā dāriyaṃ vā payāmi ।**

|| 7.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now that wife Gangadatta was what is called *jātindhukā* — every child born to her died at birth. Then, one night, while Gangadatta the wife of the caravan leader was lying awake in the late watches of the night thinking about her household, this inward thought arose in her: "Indeed, for so many years I have been living with and enjoying lavish, elevated human pleasures together with caravan leader Sagaradatt — and yet I have not given birth to a living son or daughter."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jāyiṇiduyā** = one whose children are *jātindhuka* — children born dead or dying immediately at birth (*jāti* = birth; *indhuka* = related to the perishing of newborns; a specific term for a condition where every child born to a woman dies)
- **jāyā jāyā dāragā viṇihāyamāvajjaṃti** = every child born fell into destruction, died (*viṇihāya* = destruction, death)
- **puvva-rattāvarattakālasamayaṃsi** = at the time of the late night watches, at the pre-dawn hour (*pūrvarātra-aparārātra* = the early part and later part of the night)
- **kuṭuṃbajāgariyaṃ jāgaramāṇīe** = while awake with concerns/thoughts about the household (*kuṭuṃba* = family; *jāgarita* = awake, waking state)
- **urālāiṃ māṇussagāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ** = lavish, elevated human pleasures (*udāra* = elevated, generous, great; *mānuṣaka* = human; *bhoga* = enjoyment, pleasure)
- **ṇo ceva ṇaṃ ahaṃ dāragaṃ vā dāriyaṃ vā payāmi** = and yet I have not given birth to a son or a daughter

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gangadatta's condition — *jātindhukā* — is one of the most poignant circumstances described in the Vipaak Sutra. Every child she has carried to term has been born dead, or has died immediately at birth. The Gujarati commentary describes her lying awake in the small hours of the night, her mind turning over the grief she carries. The phrase "for so many years I have enjoyed abundant pleasures with my husband" is not boastful — it is the prelude to the contrast: all this prosperity, all this comfort, and yet the one thing she longs for most remains beyond her reach. This is the Jain understanding of the mixed nature of all worldly existence: even the most fortunate circumstances contain a specific emptiness that no worldly wealth can fill. Gangadatta's sorrow is deeply human and universally recognizable. Her response — to turn toward spiritual petition — is the beginning of the chain of events that brings the soul of Dhanvantari back into human birth.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gangadatta had a heartbreaking condition: every baby she carried was born dead. Lying awake in the night, she thought: "Despite all our prosperity, I have never had even one living child."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the specific emptiness in my life that no amount of comfort or success can fill — and what does that emptiness ask of me?

## SUTRA 7.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तं धणाओ णं ताओ अम्मयाओ, सपुण्णाओ, कयत्थाओ, कयलक्खणाओ णं ताओ अम्मयाओ, सुलद्धे णं तासिं अम्मयाणं माणुस्सए जम्मजीवियफले, जाओ णं विउलं असणं पाणं खाइमं सांइमं उवक्खडावेंति, उवक्खडावेत्ता बहूहिं मित्त णाइ जाव परिवुडाओ तं विउलं असणं पाणं खाइमं सांइमं, सुरं च महुं च मेरगं च जाइं च सीधुं च पसण्णं च पुष्पवत्थगंधमल्लालंकारं गहाय पाडिलिसंड णयरं मज्झंमज्झेणं पडिणिक्खमइ, पडिणिक्खमित्ता जेणेव पुक्खरिणी तेणेव उवागच्छइ ।**

॥७.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**taṃ dhaṇāo ṇaṃ tāo ammayāo, sapuṇṇāo, kayatthāo, kayalakkhaṇāo ṇaṃ tāo ammayāo, suladdhe ṇaṃ tāsiṃ ammayāṇaṃ māṇussae jammajīviyaphale, jāo ṇaṃ viulaṃ asaṇaṃ pāṇaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ uvakkhaḍāveṃti, uvakkhaḍāvettā bahūhiṃ mitta ṇāi jāva parivuḍāo taṃ viulaṃ asaṇaṃ pāṇaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ, suraṃ ca mahuṃ ca meragaṃ ca jāiṃ ca sīdhuṃ ca pasaṇṇaṃ ca puṣpavathagaṃdhamallālaṃkāraṃ gahāya pāḍilisaṃḍa ṇayaraṃ majjhṃmajjheṇaṃ paḍiṇikkhamai, paḍiṇikkhaminttā jeṇeva pukkhaṃriṇī teṇeva uvāgacchai ।**

|| 7.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Blessed are those mothers, those who are fortunate, those who have achieved their purpose, those who are auspiciously marked — how fortunate is the fruit of human birth and life for those mothers! They who prepare abundant solid food, drink, snacks, and delicacies, and surrounded by many friends and relatives, taking that abundant food, drink, snacks, and delicacies, along with sura, honey-wine, grape-wine, coconut toddy, fermented rice drink, clarified drink, flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, and ornaments — leave through the middle of Patlikhanda city, and proceeding thus, arrive at the lake..."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **dhaṇāo ammayāo** = blessed mothers, fortunate mothers
- **sapuṇṇāo** = those who are fortunate, those full of merit (*sa* = with; *puṇya* = merit)
- **kayatthāo** = those who have achieved their purpose (*kṛtārtha* = one whose purpose has been fulfilled)
- **kayalakkhaṇāo** = those who are auspiciously marked, who have achieved auspicious signs
- **suladdhe māṇussae jammajīviyaphale** = how well-obtained is the fruit of human birth and life for them (*sulabdha* = well-gained; *mānuṣa* = human; *janma* = birth; *jīvita* = life; *phala* = fruit)
- **viulaṃ asaṇaṃ pāṇaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ** = abundant solid food, drink, snacks, and delicacies (the four types of food in Jain categorization)
- **uvakkhaḍāveṃti** = they prepare, they cause to be prepared (*upaskarati* = prepares)
- **pukkhaṃriṇī** = the lake, the lotus pond (*puṣkariṇī* = a lotus pond or lake)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gangadatta's midnight meditation takes a poignant turn: she imagines the women who have children, who can fulfill the special *dohad* (pregnancy craving) that comes in the middle months of pregnancy. She pictures them going to the lake with abundant food, drink, flowers, and adornments — and this image of fulfilled motherhood, of the *dohad* satisfied, becomes the object of her deepest longing. The Gujarati commentary elaborates her thoughts at length: she thinks of mothers who enjoy the warmth of their infants suckling, whose babies call them "mama" in sweet voices, who hold their children in their laps. This meditation is both deeply human and karmically significant: she will petition the yaksha Umbaradatt for a child, and the soul that answers her prayer will be the soul of Dhanvantari, descending from the sixth hell and taking its next birth in her womb.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gangadatta thought longingly about mothers who have living children — imagining them going to the lake with food and friends to celebrate a pregnancy craving — and she longed to be one of them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What blessings do I take for granted that others are quietly, desperately wishing for?

## SUTRA 7.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं सा गंगदत्ता सत्थवाहीए तिण्हं मासाणं बहुपिडुपुण्णाणं अयमेयारूवे दोहले पाउभूए — धणाओ णं ताओ अम्मयाओ जाव सुलद्धे णं तासिं अम्मयाणं माणुस्सए जम्मजीवियफले, जाओ णं विउलं असणं पाणं खाइमं साइमं उवक्खडावेंति, उवक्खडावेत्ता तं विउलं असणं पाणं खाइमं साइमं, सुरं च महुं च मेरगं च जाइं च सीधुं च पसण्णं च आसाएमाणा दोहलं विणेइ, विणेत्ता जामेव दिसिं पाउभूया तामेव दिसिं पडिगया ।**

॥७.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ sā gaṃgadattā satthavāhīe tiṇhaṃ māsāṇaṃ bahupidupuṇṇāṇaṃ ayameyārūve dohale pāubhūe — dhaṇāo ṇaṃ tāo ammayāo jāva suladdhe ṇaṃ tāsiṃ ammayāṇaṃ māṇussae jammajīviyaphale, jāo ṇaṃ viulaṃ asaṇaṃ pāṇaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ uvakkhaḍāveṃti, uvakkhaḍāvettā taṃ viulaṃ asaṇaṃ pāṇaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ, suraṃ ca mahuṃ ca meragaṃ ca jāiṃ ca sīdhuṃ ca pasaṇṇaṃ ca āsāemāṇā dohalaṃ viṇei, viṇettā jāmeva disiṃ pāubhūyā tāmeva disiṃ paḍigayā ।**

|| 7.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, in the third month of Gangadatta the caravan leader's wife's pregnancy, when the pregnancy was well-developed and full, this kind of craving (*dohad*) arose: "Blessed are those mothers — and so on, up to — how fortunate is the fruit of human birth and life for those mothers who prepare abundant solid food, drink, snacks, and delicacies, and taking that abundant food, drink, snacks, delicacies, along with sura, honey-wine, grape-wine, coconut toddy, fermented rice drink, and clarified drink — savoring it thus — fulfill the craving, and having fulfilled it, returned to whichever direction they had come from."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tiṇhaṃ māsāṇaṃ bahupidupuṇṇāṇaṃ** = in the third month, when the pregnancy was very fully developed (*pṛṣṭha-pūrṇa* = full, rounded — the fetus well-established)
- **dohale pāubhūe** = a pregnancy craving arose (*dohada* = pregnancy craving)
- **āsāemāṇā** = savoring, tasting, enjoying (*āsvādana*)
- **dohalaṃ viṇei** = fulfilled the craving (*viṇeti* = fulfilled, resolved, satisfied)
- **jāmeva disiṃ pāubhūyā tāmeva disiṃ paḍigayā** = returned to whichever direction she had come from (she went and came back — completed the ritual circuit)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The pregnancy craving (*dohad*) that arises in Gangadatta's third month is intimately connected to the meditations of her midnight wakefulness. The craving she experiences is precisely the scene she had been longing for: to go to the lake with abundant food, the six kinds of drink, flowers and adornments, with friends — and to enjoy the fulfilment of a mother's craving. In Jain tradition, the *dohad* is understood as the fetus itself communicating its karmic tendencies through the mother's body. The soul of Dhanvantari, arriving from the sixth hell into Gangadatta's womb, brings with it the conditioned desires of many past lives — desires for rich food and fermented drink — and these surface as the mother's craving. This is not a negative sign alone; the very specificity of the craving gives it the meaning that will name the child. The ritual of fulfillment — going to the lake, eating, drinking, returning — will complete the sacred circuit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In the third month of her pregnancy, Gangadatta developed a craving: she wanted to go to the lake with her friends and enjoy a great feast with six kinds of drinks.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What cravings arise in me that I have not yet traced to their deeper source — not the craving itself, but what it is really asking for?

|| 7.14 ||

Then Gangadatta, having received this permission from caravan leader Sagaradatt, had prepared abundant food of four types and six kinds of sura, and many flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, and ornaments; and surrounded by many friends, relatives, kinswomen, family, and associated people, proceeded with them — and so on, up to — at the bank of the lake she placed the many flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, and ornaments; entered the lake; bathed in the water; played in the water; having bathed — and so on — came out of the lake with her wet garments; took up the flowers, garments, fragrances, garlands, and ornaments; went to the Umbaradatt yaksha shrine; arrived at the yaksha shrine; paid obeisance at the sight of the yaksha; stroked the yaksha with a soft brush; massaged the yaksha statue with the soft brush; anointed the yaksha statue with a stream of water; rubbed the body of the yaksha with a body-stick; dressed the yaksha in white garments; performed the flower-offering, garland-offering, fragrance-offering, and powder-offering; burned incense; and kneeling at the feet made this vow: "Beloved One! If I give birth to a living son or daughter, I will increase your yagna, your gift-portion, your share, and your inexhaustible treasury." Having made this vow and prayer, she returned in whichever direction she had come from.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **viulaṃ asaṇaṃ 4** = abundant food of four types (solid food, drink, light food, delicacies — the four Jain food categories)
- **suraṃ ca 6** = six types of sura (fermented drinks — the six types: sura, madhu, meraga, jai, sidhu, pasanna)
- **lomahatthaṇaṃ** = with a soft brush, a whisk of fur (*loma* = hair/fur; *hasta* = hand — a soft brush used for ritual cleaning of idols)
- **pamajjai** = massaged, gently rubbed
- **dagadhārāe abhukkhai** = anointed/bathed with a stream of water
- **gāyalaṭṭī olūhai** = rubbed/cleaned with a body-stick (a ritual instrument for cleaning the idol's body)
- **puppāruhaṇaṃ, mallāruhaṇaṃ, gaṃdhāruhaṇaṃ, cuṇṇāruhaṇaṃ** = flower-offering, garland-offering, fragrance-offering, powder-offering (four ritual offerings)
- **jāyaṃ ca dāyaṃ ca bhāyaṃ ca akkhayaṇihiṃ ca** = yagna (sacrifice offering), dana (charitable portion), bhag (share/distribution), and inexhaustible treasury (*akṣaya-nidhi* = inexhaustible storehouse — promising to continuously replenish the yaksha's resources)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The elaborate ritual of lake bathing followed by yaksha worship is described with exceptional detail — each step of the worship ritual is named: stroking with the brush, massaging, anointing with water, rubbing with the body-stick, dressing in white garments, the four types of offering (flower, garland, fragrance, powder), and the burning of incense. This is not just ritual observance but desperate petition: every action is performed with full attention by a woman who has wanted a living child for her entire married life. The vow she makes — to increase the yaksha's offerings in four ways — is a formal transaction: conditional on receiving a child, she promises perpetual increase of the yaksha's honor. The soul that will respond to this petition is the soul of Dhanvantari, completing its tenure in the sixth hell and seeking a new birth. The earnestness of Gangadatta's devotion becomes the vessel that receives this incoming soul.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

With Sagaradatt's permission, Gangadatta went to the lake with her friends, bathed and played in the water, then went to the Umbaradatt yaksha shrine and performed a complete ritual worship, making a vow: "If I have a living child, I will increase all your offerings."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What am I willing to commit to — what promises am I willing to make and keep — in pursuit of what I truly long for?

## SUTRA 7.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं सा गंगदत्ता भारिया जवणहं मासाणं बहुपिडुपुण्णाणं दारगं पयाया । ठिवडिया जाव णामधेज्जं करेंति — जम्हा णं अम्हे इमे दारए उंबरदत्तस्स जक्खस्स ओवाइयलद्धे, तम्हा णं होउ णं दारए उंबरदत्ते णामेणं । तए णं से उंबरदत्ते दारए पंचधाईपरिग्गहिए परिवडुइ ।**

॥७.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ sā gaṃgadattā bhāriyā javaṇahaṃ māsāṇaṃ bahupidupuṇṇāṇaṃ dāragaṃ payāyā । ṭhivaḍiyā jāva nāmadhejaṃ kaṃreṃti — jamhā ṇaṃ amhe ime dārāe uṃbaradattassa jakkhassa ovāiyaladdhe, tamhā ṇaṃ hoū ṇaṃ dārāe uṃbaradatte nāmeṇaṃ । tae ṇaṃ se uṃbaradatte dārāe paṃcadhāīpariggahie parivadu i ।**

|| 7.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that wife Gangadatta, when nine months were complete and the pregnancy had fully matured, gave birth to a son. The naming ceremony was performed — and so on, up to — they named him: "Since we received this child through the petition to the Umbaradatt yaksha, let the child's name be Umbaradatt." Then that child Umbaradatt was placed in the care of five nurse-mothers and grew up.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **javaṇahaṃ māsāṇaṃ bahupidupuṇṇāṇaṃ** = nine months fully matured (*nava māsa* = nine months; *pūrṇa* = full, complete)
- **ṭhivaḍiyā** = the formal placement/laying-down ceremony at birth
- **nāmadhejaṃ kaṃreṃti** = they performed the naming ceremony (*nāmadheya* = name-giving)
- **ovāiyaladdhe** = received through petition/vow (*ovāita* = obtained through a vow or supplication)
- **paṃcadhāīpariggahie** = placed in the care of five nurses (*pañca dhātrī* = five nurse-mothers: birth nurse, milk nurse, washing nurse, play nurse, and carrying nurse — the traditional five)
- **parivadu i** = grew up, matured

### 5. COMMENTARY

The birth of Umbaradatt after Gangadatta's long years of childlessness is the pivot of the chapter's human narrative. His name — taken from the yaksha through whose grace he was obtained — is a perpetual reminder of his unusual origin story. The soul of Dhanvantari, having completed twenty-two sagaropam in the sixth hell, has now arrived in human form — the son of a prosperous caravan leader, beautiful, healthy, complete in all senses. The five nurses represent the traditional Indian system of child care: one for the birth and cleansing, one for nursing, one for bathing, one for playing, one for carrying. Umbaradatt grows up in comfort, care, and privilege — the beloved son his mother had prayed for through years of grief. Yet the karmic debt he carries from the sixth hell and from the physician's lifetimes before it is immense, and it will manifest.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After nine months, Gangadatta gave birth to a healthy son. They named him Umbaradatt after the yaksha who had granted their prayer, and he grew up cared for by five nurses.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a long-awaited blessing finally arrives in my life, do I remember the prayer and the patience that preceded it — or do I quickly take the blessing for granted?

## SUTRA 7.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**तए णं से सागरदत्ते सत्थवाहे जहा विजयमित्ते कालधम्मुणा संजुत्ते । गंगदत्ता वि । उंबरदत्ते णिच्छूढे जहा उज्झियए । तए णं से उंबरदत्ते दारए अण्णया कयाइ सरीरगंसि जमगसमगमेव सोलस रोगायंका पाउभूया । तंजहा — सासे, कासे जाव कोढे । तए णं से उंबरदत्ते दारए सोलसहिं रोगायकेहिं अभिभूए समाणे कच्छुल्ले जाव देहं बलियाए वित्तिं कप्पेमाणे विहरइ । एवं खलु गोयमा ! उंबरदत्ते दारए पुरापोराणाणं जाव पच्चणुभवमाणे विहरइ ।**

॥७.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**tae ṇaṃ se sāgaradatte satthavāhe jahā vijayamitte kāladhammūṇā sañjutte । gaṃgadattā vi । uṃbaradatte nicchūḍhe jahā ujjhiyae । tae ṇaṃ se uṃbaradatte dārāe aṇṇayā kayāi sarīragaṃsi jamagasamagameva solasa rogāyaṃkā pāubhūyā । taṃjahā — sāse, kāse jāva koḍhe । tae ṇaṃ se uṃbaradatte dārāe solasahiṃ rogāyakehiṃ abhibhūe samāṇe kachulle jāva dehaṃ baliyāe vittiṃ kappeṃmāṇe viharai । evaṃ khalu goyamā! uṃbaradatte dārāe purāporāṇāṇaṃ jāva paccaṇubhavamāṇe viharai ।**

|| 7.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then caravan leader Sagaradatt died as Vijayamitra died — attaining the death that comes in due course. And so did Gangadatta. Umbaradatt was expelled as Ujjhitak was expelled. Then at some point, sixteen types of diseases arose simultaneously in Umbaradatt's body — namely: asthma, cough — and so on, up to — leprosy. Afflicted by these sixteen diseases, Umbaradatt — afflicted with leprosy and so on — maintained his livelihood through offerings given to his suffering body. Thus indeed, Gautam, Umbaradatt is experiencing and living through the fruit of his past karma.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kāladhammūṇā sañjutte** = attained the law of death, passed away naturally
- **jahā vijayamitte** = as Vijayamitra (a canonical cross-reference to a character in the Vipaak Sutra whose death is described in standard form)
- **nicchūḍhe jahā ujjhiyae** = expelled as Ujjhitak was expelled (reference to Chapter 2's protagonist Ujjhitak, who was also expelled from his home after his parents' death)
- **jamagasamagameva** = simultaneously, all at once (*yugapat* = simultaneous; *samam* = together — all sixteen at the same moment)
- **solasa rogāyaṃkā** = sixteen types of diseases (*ṣoḍaśa* = sixteen; *rogāyaṃka* = disease condition)
- **sāse, kāse jāva koḍhe** = asthma, cough, and so on up to leprosy (the *jāva* abbreviation indicating a canonical list of all sixteen)
- **dehaṃ baliyāe vittiṃ kappeṃmāṇe** = maintaining livelihood through offerings made to the body (*deha* = body; *baliyā* = offering — alms given as if to honor the body; *vitti* = livelihood)
- **purāporāṇāṇaṃ paccaṇubhavamāṇe** = experiencing and living through the fruit of past karma

### 5. COMMENTARY

The collapse of Umbaradatt's fortunate circumstances is total and swift. His father dies (as all caravan leaders die, following the canonical formula — the same death described in other chapters). His mother dies too — presumably from grief. And then, following the pattern of Chapter Two's Ujjhitak, he is expelled from his own home and his wealth is taken by others. The final blow is the simultaneous eruption of sixteen diseases in his body — not one by one, as misfortunes sometimes accumulate, but all at once, like a dam bursting. The canonical list begins with asthma and cough and ends with leprosy (*koḍha*) — the full range of severe, disfiguring, and debilitating conditions that make Umbaradatt the figure Gautam encountered at all four city gates. Bhagavan Mahavir's conclusion is the chapter's central teaching, stated simply: "Gautam, this is Umbaradatt experiencing the fruit of his past karma." Not punishment. Not curse. Fruit — the natural ripening of what was sown.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Umbaradatt's parents both died, he was thrown out of his own home penniless, and then sixteen terrible diseases struck his body all at once — turning him into the suffering beggar Gautam had seen at all four city gates.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "simultaneous eruption" of consequences in my life might have a single, deeper cause that I have not yet looked at honestly?

## SUTRA 7.17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

**से णं उंबरदत्ते दारए बावत्तिरं वासाइं पालइत्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा इमीसे रयणप्पभाए पुढवीए णेरइएसु णेरइयत्ताए उवव्ज्जिहिइ । संसारो तहेव जाव पुढवी । तओ हत्थिणाउरे णयरे कुक्कुडत्ताए पच्चायाहिइ । जायमेत्ते चेव गोठिल्लवलिहेए तत्थेव हत्थिणाउरे णयरे सेट्टिकुलंसि उवव्ज्जिहिइ । बोहिं, सोहम्मे कप्पे, महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहिइ । णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स ।**

॥७.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

**se ṇaṃ uṃbaradatte dārāe bāvattirim vāsāiṃ pālaittvā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā imīse rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe ṇeraiesu ṇeraiyattāe uvavjjihii । saṃsāro taheva jāva puḍhavī । tao hatthinaūre ṇayare kukkuḍattāe paccāyāhii । jāyamette ceva goṭhillavalihea tattheva hatthinaūre ṇayare seṭṭhikulaṃsi uvavjjihii । bohiṃ, sohamme kappe, mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihii । ṇikkhevaṃ jahā paḍhamassa ।**

|| 7.17 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That Umbaradatt, having lived out seventy-two years of remaining lifespan, at the time of death will die and be reborn as a hellish being in this first hell of Ratnaprabha. Through samsara — likewise — and so on, through the earth-body existences. Then he will be reborn as a crow in the city of Hastinapur. Having died there by others' hands, he will be immediately reborn in a merchant family in that very city of Hastinapur. Awakening, in the Saudhama heaven, in the Mahavideha realm — he will attain liberation. The concluding statement is as the first chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **bāvattirim vāsāiṃ** = seventy-two years (*dvāsaptati* = 72) of remaining lifespan
- **rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe** = in the first hell, Ratnaprabha ("jewel-light")
- **saṃsāro taheva jāva puḍhavī** = wandering through samsara as described — through earth-body lives (*pṛthvīkāya* = earth-body existences — rebirths as microscopic earth-body organisms)
- **hatthinaūre ṇayare** = in the city of Hastinapur (*hasti* = elephant; *napur* = city — "elephant city," the ancient Kuru capital)
- **kukkuḍattāe** = as a crow (*kukkuṭa* = rooster/crow — here the Gujarati commentary confirms crow)
- **paccāyāhii** = will be reborn (*pratyāyāsyati* = will return, will be reborn)
- **jāyamette ceva goṭhillavalihea** = having died there immediately at the hands of others (*jāyamātra* = as soon as born/arrived; *goṭhila* = a type of killing — here meaning killed by others right there)
- **seṭṭhikulaṃsi** = in a merchant family (*śreṣṭhi* = leading merchant)
- **bohiṃ** = awakening, enlightenment (*bodhi* = enlightenment, spiritual awakening)
- **sohamme kappe** = in the Saudhama heaven, first celestial realm
- **mahāvidehe vāse** = dwelling in the Mahavideha realm
- **sijjhihii** = will attain liberation (*sidhyati* = achieves liberation)
- **ṇikkhevaṃ jahā paḍhamassa** = the concluding statement is as the first chapter (standard canonical closure)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The future trajectory of Umbaradatt's soul traces a path that is long but ultimately luminous. Seventy-two years of remaining lifespan in his disease-ravaged body — the karma working itself out through physical suffering. Then first hell (Ratnaprabha), the mildest of the seven hells — far less severe than the sixth hell where Dhanvantari spent twenty-two sagaropam. The severity has dramatically decreased. Then samsara through earth-body existences — microscopic rebirths that are part of the soul's karmic reduction process. Then a crow in Hastinapur — killed by others, experiencing the echo of having prescribed and organized the killing of countless creatures. Then the decisive turn: a merchant's son in Hastinapur who receives dharma, takes the path of awakening, is born in the Saudhama heaven, and finally in the Mahavideha realm completes the journey to liberation. The teaching is written in the arc itself: no matter how far a soul has fallen — sixth hell, twenty-two sagaropam, disease-ravaged human life — the path to liberation remains, and its end is certain.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Mahavir revealed Umbaradatt's future: after living out seventy-two more years, his soul would go to the first hell, then through many rebirths — including as a crow in Hastinapur, killed by others — and eventually, as a merchant's son who embraces a spiritual path, would reach complete liberation from all suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even in my most difficult moments, can I hold the awareness that the soul's journey has a direction — that no period of suffering, however long, is the final word?

**Vivechan Summary:**

The commentary tradition on Chapter Seven draws from the Acharanga Sutra (4th chapter): *"je āsava te parissava, je parissava te āsava"* — what flows into the soul as sin flows out as suffering; what flows out as austerity flows back in as purification. Dhanvantari possessed extraordinary intelligence and medical mastery that could have been an instrument of immense healing and merit. Instead, he used it to normalize, prescribe, and personally indulge in the killing and consumption of animals across eight species and every class of living being. He considered this his highest science, his supreme practice, his definitive way of life. This wholehearted identification with sinful activity as "expertise" is what makes his karma so severe. The lesson is not that medical knowledge is harmful, but that knowledge without ethical grounding is dangerous precisely because of its power. The Vipaak Sutra holds a mirror to any life — in any era — where intelligence is used to rationalize harm, where professional authority is used to sanction violence, where the one who should heal instead directs destruction. The liberation promised at the end of Umbaradatt's long journey is the teaching's final word: even this soul will be free.
