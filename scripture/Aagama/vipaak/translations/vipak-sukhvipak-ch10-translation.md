### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
दसमस्स उक्खेवो ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
dasamassa ukkhevo |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The introduction of the tenth chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**दसमस्स** = of the tenth; **उक्खेवो** = introduction / commencement

### 5. COMMENTARY
This is the standard opening marker that announces the beginning of the tenth and final chapter of the Sukha Vipaak Sutra. It signals that the story of Mahachandrakumar has concluded and the last narrative — that of Vardatta — is about to begin. This is the final opening formula in the entire Sukha Vipaak section, marking the approach toward the conclusion of the second half of the Vipaak Sutra. After ten chapters of stories demonstrating how suffering follows from bad karma (Dukha Vipaak) and ten chapters showing how good fortune follows from good karma (Sukha Vipaak), this final chapter will close the entire scriptural text.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
This line marks the start of the tenth and final chapter of the Sukha Vipaak.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Every journey has a final step. Does knowing you are near the end change how you walk?

## SUTRA [10.2]

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं साएयं णामं णयरं होत्था। उत्तरकुरू उज्जाणे। पासामिओ जक्खो। मित्तणंदी राया। सिरिकंता देवी। वरसेणापामोक्खाणं पंचचेवीसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं पाणिग्गहणं। तित्थयरागमणं। सावगधम्मं। पुव्वभवपुच्छा। सयदुवारे णयरे। धम्मरुई णामं अणगारे एज्जमाणं पासाइ, पासित्ता पडिलाभिए जाव सिद्धे। सुबाहुस्स णवरं भद्दणंदी कुमारे... जहा सुबाहुस्स... जाव सव्वदुक्खाणमंतं काहिइ। णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ sāeyaṃ ṇāmaṃ ṇayaraṃ hotthā | uttarakurū ujjāṇe | pāsāmio jakkho | mittaṇaṃdī rāyā | sirikaṃtā devī | varaseṇāpāmokkhāṇaṃ paṃcacevīsayāṇaṃ rāyavarakaṇṇagāṇaṃ pāṇiggahaṇaṃ | titthayarāgamaṇaṃ | sāvagadhammaṃ | puvvabhavapucchā | sayaduvāre ṇayare | dhammaruī ṇāmaṃ aṇagāre ejjamāṇaṃ pāsāi, pāsittā paḍilābhie jāva siddhe | subāhussa ṇavaraṃ bhaddaṇaṃdī kumāre... jahā subāhussa... jāva savvadukkhāṇamaṃtaṃ kāhii | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
"Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that period, there was a city named Sayeya. In the Uttarakuru garden. The guardian spirit was Pasamiao. The king was Mittanandi. The queen was Sirikanta Devi. He married Varasena, chief among five hundred and twenty-four princesses. The Tirthankara arrived. He accepted the layman's dharma. The question of previous births was asked. In the city of Sayaduvara. He saw the monk named Dhammarui walking, and having seen him, he attained — all the way to liberation. Just as for Subahukumar, so too for Prince Bhadranandi... just as for Subahukumar... all the way to making an end of all suffering. The closing is as in the first chapter."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**एवं खलु जंबू** = thus indeed, O Jambu; **तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं** = at that time, in that period; **साएयं णामं णयरं होत्था** = there was a city named Sayeya (Shatadwar); **उत्तरकुरू उज्जाणे** = in the Uttarakuru garden; **पासामिओ जक्खो** = the guardian spirit Pasamiao; **मित्तणंदी राया** = King Mittanandi (Mitranandi); **सिरिकंता देवी** = Queen Sirikanta Devi; **वरसेणापामोक्खाणं** = Varasena, chief among; **पंचचेवीसयाणं रायवरकण्णगाणं** = five hundred and twenty-four princesses; **पाणिग्गहणं** = marriage; **तित्थयरागमणं** = the arrival of the Tirthankara; **सावगधम्मं** = the layman's dharma (shravaka dharma); **पुव्वभवपुच्छा** = the question about previous births; **सयदुवारे णयरे** = in the city of Sayaduvara (Shatadwar); **धम्मरुई णामं अणगारे** = the monk named Dhammarui (Dharmaruci); **एज्जमाणं पासाइ** = saw him walking; **पासित्ता पडिलाभिए** = having seen, he attained; **जाव सिद्धे** = all the way to liberation; **सुबाहुस्स णवरं** = just as for Subahukumar, but (with this difference); **भद्दणंदी कुमारे** = Prince Bhadranandi; **जाव सव्वदुक्खाणमंतं काहिइ** = all the way to making an end of all suffering; **णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स** = the closing is as in the first (chapter)

### 5. COMMENTARY
This sutra presents the final narrative of the Sukha Vipaak: the story of Prince Vardatta. He was born in the city of Sayeya (identified as Shatadwar), where the garden was called Uttarakuru and the guardian spirit was Pasamiao. His father was King Mittanandi (Mitranandi) and his mother was Queen Sirikanta Devi. Vardatta married Varasena, who was chief among five hundred and twenty-four princesses — a slightly larger number than the five hundred mentioned in earlier chapters. When Lord Mahavira arrived, Vardatta accepted the layman's dharma (the discipline of a devout householder) and asked about his previous births. The Lord revealed that in a former life, Vardatta had lived in the city of Sayaduvara (Shatadwar). There he had seen a monk named Dhammarui (Dharmaruci) walking past. That single encounter — simply seeing a monk engaged in practice — planted the seed of spiritual awakening. Through the merit accumulated across multiple lives of charity, moral conduct, and devotion, Vardatta was born into his current royal fortune. He practiced under Lord Mahavira and, through continued effort across future births, will eventually attain liberation at Mahavidehakshetra. The text explicitly links his story back to the pattern of Subahukumar from Chapter 1, confirming that the same spiritual law governs every soul: good actions accumulate into good fortune, and persistent practice leads to the end of all suffering.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Prince Vardatta of Sayeya (Shatadwar) city was born to King Mittanandi and Queen Sirikanta. In a past life, he encountered the monk Dharmaruci and was moved toward the spiritual path. He practiced great charity, followed Lord Mahavira's teachings, and through continued effort across multiple births, will eventually attain liberation at Mahavidehakshetra.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
A single glimpse of a monk walking past was enough to set a soul on the path to liberation. What seemingly small encounter in your own life might be the seed of your deepest transformation?

## SUTRA [10.3] — Closing Formula of the Sukha Vipaak Sutra

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
एवं खलु जंबू ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं सुहविवागाणं दसमं अज्झयणं समत्तं । ति बेमि ।
।। दसमं अज्झयणं समत्तं ।।
।। सुहविवागं समत्तं ।।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
evaṃ khalu jambū ! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ suhavivāgāṇaṃ dasamaṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ samattaṃ | ti bemi |
|| dasamaṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ samattaṃ ||
|| suhavivāgaṃ samattaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
"Thus indeed, O Jambu! By the blessed ascetic Lord Mahavira, the tenth chapter of the Sukha Vipaak has been completed. Thus I say."
"The tenth chapter is complete."
"The Sukha Vipaak is complete."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
**एवं खलु जंबू** = thus indeed, O Jambu; **समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं** = by the blessed ascetic Lord Mahavira; **जाव संपत्तेणं** = having attained / arrived at; **सुहविवागाणं** = of the Sukha Vipaak (good fruition); **दसमं अज्झयणं** = the tenth chapter; **समत्तं** = is complete; **ति बेमि** = thus I say; **सुहविवागं समत्तं** = the Sukha Vipaak is complete

### 5. COMMENTARY
This is the closing formula for the entire Sukha Vipaak Sutra. It carries three layers of closure. First, Sudharmaswami declares to his disciple Jambukumar that Lord Mahavira has completed the tenth chapter. Second, the text confirms that the tenth chapter is complete. Third — and most significantly — it declares that the entire Sukha Vipaak section is complete. This triple closure marks the end of the second half of the Vipaak Sutra. The complete Vipaak Sutra consists of twenty chapters in total: the first ten form the Dukha Vipaak (stories of suffering as the fruit of bad karma), and the second ten form the Sukha Vipaak (stories of good fortune as the fruit of good karma). With this closing formula, both halves are now complete. The phrase "ti bemi" ("thus I say") is the traditional Jain formula used to seal a teaching — it signals that the speaker has fully delivered the teaching and stands by its truth. The entire Vipaak Sutra thus presents a complete moral framework: actions have consequences, suffering follows from harmful conduct, and good fortune follows from righteous conduct — and in both cases, the ultimate destination is liberation from all suffering.

> **Note on the complete Vipaak Sutra structure:**
> The Vipaak Sutra is the seventh Anga of the Jain Agama canon. It consists of two Shrutaskandhas:
> - **First Shrutaskandha — Dukha Vipaak (10 chapters):** Stories showing how bad karma leads to suffering
> - **Second Shrutaskandha — Sukha Vipaak (10 chapters):** Stories showing how good karma leads to good fortune
> Together, the 20 chapters present a complete picture of karmic law through narrative.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Sudharmaswami closes by declaring: "Lord Mahavira has completed the tenth chapter of the Sukha Vipaak — thus I say." With this, the entire Sukha Vipaak section ends. Combined with the Dukha Vipaak's ten chapters, all twenty chapters of the Vipaak Sutra are now complete.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Twenty stories — ten of suffering, ten of good fortune — all pointing to the same truth: actions have consequences, and liberation is the final destination. Having heard them all, what will you do differently starting now?
