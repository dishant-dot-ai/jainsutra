# Vipaak Sutra — Chapter 6: Nandivardhana
## Dukha Vipaak Sutra (Suffering from Evil Deeds)
### Scripture Pages 89–101 | PDF Pages 141–153

---

## Parichay (Introduction)

This sixth chapter recounts the life story of Prince Nandivardhana. The chapter takes its name from this prince.

In the city of Mathura, King Shreedama ruled. His son Nandivardhana was endowed with all physical perfections and good qualities, and had been appointed as crown prince in due time. Yet because King Shreedama lived to an old age, Nandivardhana could not attain the throne even after reaching sixty years.

Growing impatient after many long years without kingship, Nandivardhana's desire for the throne turned into a consuming ambition. Finding no suitable opportunity to seize power naturally, he decided to have his own father killed. Finding no one suitable for the deed, he approached the royal barber Chitra, who had unrestricted access to all royal chambers, and promised him half the kingdom in exchange for slitting the king's throat while performing his duties.

The barber agreed initially out of greed, but then became fearful. He disclosed everything to King Shreedama. The king, enraged, had his own son Nandivardhana arrested and sentenced to death. The royal soldiers bound him in chains, paraded him through the city, and then seated him on a scorching iron throne and performed a mock royal coronation using boiling metals and caustic liquids — a grotesque punishment.

At that moment, Gautam Swami was passing through on his begging rounds and witnessed this horrifying spectacle. Recognizing the man's hellish agony, he asked Bhagavan Mahavir about the man's previous life. This chapter records the Lord's answer and the full account of Nandivardhana's spiritual journey.

### SUTRA 6.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

छट्टुस्स उक्खेवो ।
॥६.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Chaṭṭussa ukkheyo |
|| 6.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The introduction of the sixth chapter — understand it as the same as the first chapter's opening.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Chaṭṭussa* — of the sixth (chapter)
- *ukkheyo* — the opening, the upakrama (introduction/commencement)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This brief sutra functions as a formal chapter marker, indicating that the opening of the sixth teaching follows the same structural pattern established in Chapter One. In the Vipaak Sutra's oral tradition, each chapter maintains a standardized beginning — the formal invocation, the narrative context, and the entry of Bhagavan Mahavir and Gautam Swami. Rather than repeating the full formula, the text refers back to the first chapter as the model. This brevity is itself an expression of the Agamic literary style: economy without loss of completeness. The word *ukkheya* (upakrama) means both "beginning" and "lifting up" — an elevation into the sacred teaching that follows.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

This is simply saying: "Chapter Six begins the same way Chapter One did — let that be understood."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How often do I rush past beginnings, treating them as mere formality? What would change if I brought the same reverence to every start as I do to the heart of a practice?

## Setting and Characters

### SUTRA 6.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं महुरा णामं णयरी होत्था । भंडीरे उज्जाणे । सुदंसणे जक्खे । सिरिदामे राया । बंधुसिरी भारिया । पुत्ते णंदिवद्धणे कुमारे, अहीण पडिपुण्ण पंचिंदियसरीरे जाव जुवराया ।
॥६.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Evaṃ khalu Jambū! Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ mahurā nāmaṃ nayarī hotthā | Bhaṇḍīre ujjāṇe | Sudaṃsaṇe jakkhe | Siridāme rāyā | Baṃdhusirī bhāriyā | putte Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre, ahīṇa paḍipuṇṇa paṃciṃdiyasarīre jāva juvarāyā |
|| 6.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Indeed, O Jambu! At that time and in that era, there was a city called Mathura, with a garden named Bhandira, a yaksha shrine called Sudarshan, a king named Shreedama, a queen named Bandhushri, and their son Prince Nandivardhana — endowed with complete and perfect fivefold sense faculties — who had been appointed crown prince.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *evaṃ khalu* — verily, indeed (affirmative opening)
- *Jambū* — addressing the disciple Jambu Swami
- *teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ* — at that time and in that era
- *mahurā nāmaṃ nayarī* — a city by the name of Mathura
- *Bhaṇḍīre ujjāṇe* — the garden named Bhandira
- *Sudaṃsaṇe jakkhe* — the yaksha deity named Sudarshan (a local protective deity)
- *Siridāme rāyā* — King Shreedama (ruling there)
- *Baṃdhusirī bhāriyā* — queen named Bandhushri
- *Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre* — Prince Nandivardhana (their son)
- *ahīṇa paḍipuṇṇa paṃciṃdiyasarīre* — having complete, undeficient, perfectly formed fivefold sense faculties
- *juvarāyā* — appointed as crown prince / yuvaraja

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra opens by establishing Mathura — one of the great ancient cities of India and a place frequently named in the Agamas as a center of dharmic activity. The Bhandira garden outside the city was a well-known assembly ground. The mention of the Sudarshan yaksha shrine locates us in the religious geography of the time. King Shreedama is described as a righteous ruler, and his queen Bandhushri ("friend-beauty") befits the royal household. Their son Nandivardhana — "one who increases joy and prosperity" — is described in the standard Agamic phrase as having a body with complete and flawless sense faculties: seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting, and touching — all fully developed. That he had already been crowned crown prince signals that the kingdom was rightfully his destiny. Yet the narrative that follows shows how impatience and greed could make even a destined prince commit the worst of crimes.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In the ancient city of Mathura, King Shreedama ruled with his queen Bandhushri; their son Nandivardhana — healthy, handsome, and fully capable — had already been named as the next king.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If everything I desire is already on its way to me, what violence do I commit — inward or outward — by refusing to wait?

### SUTRA 6.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तस्स सिरिदामस्स सुबंधु णामं अमच्चे होत्था । साम-दंड-भेय उवप्पयाण णीइकुसले, सुपउत्त णयविहण्णू । तस्स णं सुबंधुस्स अमच्चस्स बहुमित्तापुत्ते णामं दारए होत्था, अहीणपडिपुण्णपंचिंदियसरीरे, वण्णओ । तस्स णं सिरिदामस्स रण्णो चित्ते णामं अलंकारिए होत्था । सिरिदामस्स रण्णो चित्ते बहुविहं अलंकारियकम्मं करेमाणे सव्वट्टाणेसु य सव्वभूमियासु य, अंतेउरे य, दिण्णियारे य याविं होत्था ।
॥६.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Tassa Siridāmassa Subaṃdhu nāmaṃ amacce hotthā | Sāma-daṃḍa-bhea uvappayāṇa nīikusale, supautte nayavihāṇṇū | Tassa ṇaṃ Subaṃdhussa amacchassa bahumittāpute nāmaṃ dāraye hotthā, ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapaṃciṃdiyasarīre, vaṇṇao | Tassa ṇaṃ Siridāmassa raṇṇo Citte nāmaṃ alaṃkārie hotthā | Siridāmassa raṇṇo Citte bahu-vihaṃ alaṃkāriyakammaṃ karemāṇe savvaṭṭāṇesu ya savvabhūmiyāsu ya, aṃteuire ya, diṇṇiyāre ya yāviṃ hotthā |
|| 6.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

King Shreedama had a minister named Subandhu, skilled in the four royal policies of conciliation, punishment, division, and gift-giving, and expert in statecraft; Subandhu's minister-son was named Bahumittraputra, of perfect physical form. King Shreedama also had a royal barber named Chitra, who performed diverse grooming services and had unrestricted access to all royal chambers, quarters, and the inner palace at all times.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Subaṃdhu nāmaṃ amacce* — a minister named Subandhu
- *Sāma-daṃḍa-bhea uvappayāṇa nīikusale* — skilled in the four statecraft policies: conciliation (sāma), punishment (daṇḍa), division/splitting alliances (bheda), and gifting (upapradāna)
- *nayavihāṇṇū* — knower/expert in political science and governance
- *bahumittāpute nāmaṃ dāraye* — a son named Bahumittraputra (son of many friends)
- *Citte nāmaṃ alaṃkārie* — a barber/beautician named Chitra
- *alaṃkāriyakammaṃ* — beautification and grooming work
- *sav-vaṭṭāṇesu* — in all places/locations (of the palace)
- *aṃteure* — in the inner palace / women's quarters
- *diṇṇiyāre* — given free passage / unrestricted access

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra introduces two key figures whose roles will determine the narrative's outcome: the minister Subandhu and the royal barber Chitra. Subandhu is described with a classic fourfold political framework derived from ancient Indian statecraft — the use of gentle words (sāma), force (daṇḍa), creating divisions among enemies (bheda), and offering gifts (upapradāna). This characterization marks him as a sophisticated political operative. More significant for the story is Chitra the barber. In ancient royal courts, the royal barber held a uniquely intimate position — performing grooming services for the king's person, attending to his beard and hair, and therefore having physical proximity to the king that almost no one else had. Chitra's unrestricted access to every part of the palace — including the inner women's quarters — made him the perfect instrument for a conspiracy. It is precisely this trust that Nandivardhana sought to exploit, and that Chitra ultimately could not bear to betray.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

King Shreedama had a wise minister named Subandhu and a trusted royal barber named Chitra — who could go anywhere in the palace, even the private royal chambers.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Trusted access is sacred. When someone trusts me completely, how do I honor that trust rather than exploit it?

### SUTRA 6.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं सामी समोसढे । परिसा णिग्गया, राया णिग्गओ जाव परिसा पडिगया ।
॥६.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ sāmī samosaḍhe | Parisā niggayā, rāyā niggao jāva parisā paḍigayā |
|| 6.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time and in that era, the Lord arrived and descended there; the assembly went forth, the king went forth, and — up to — the assembly returned.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *sāmī samosaḍhe* — the Lord (Bhagavan Mahavir) arrived and took his station (samavasaraṇa — divine assembly)
- *parisā niggayā* — the assembly went out (to receive him)
- *rāyā niggao* — the king went forth (to pay homage)
- *jāva parisā paḍigayā* — up to: the assembly returned (after receiving teaching)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is a condensed sutra using the Agamic convention of "jāva" (up to / yāvat) to abbreviate a well-known sequence. The full scene — Bhagavan Mahavir's divine assembly (samavasaraṇa) descending in or near Mathura, the announcement going through the city, the great congregation of monks, nuns, laypeople, and royalty going out to hear the teaching, the king paying his respects — is understood by the reader familiar with the Agamic format. The text does not repeat what is standard; it signals it with shorthand. The fact that Bhagavan was in Mathura at precisely the moment of Nandivardhana's punishment is the narrative's turning point: it is the Lord's presence that makes visible, through Gautam's questioning, the karmic story behind a scene of brutal punishment.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

At that very time, Bhagavan Mahavir arrived in Mathura and gave a teaching — people came to hear him, and then returned home.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The divine arrives at the exact moment we need to see the truth. Am I present enough to recognize that arrival in my own life?

## Nandivardhano no Parichay (Introduction of Nandivardhana)

---

### SUTRA 6.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स जेट्ठे अंतेवासी जाव रायमग्गमोगाढे तहेव हत्थी, आसे, पुरिसे पासइ । तेसिं च पुरिसाणं मज्झगयं एगं पुरिसं पासइ जाव णरणारिसंपरिवुडं । तए णं तं पुरिसं रायपुरिसा चच्चरंसि तत्तसि अयोमयंसि समजोइभूयंसि सिंहासणंसि णिवेसावंति । तयाणंतरं च णं पुरिसाणं मज्झगयं पुरिसं बहुविह अयकलसेहिं तत्तेहिं समजोएभूएहिं, अप्पेगइया तंबरिएहिं, अप्पेगइया तउयरिएहिं, अप्पेगइया सीसगरिएहिं, अप्पेगइया कलकलरिएहिं, अप्पेगइया खारतेल्लरिएहिं, महया महया रायाभिसेएणं अभिसिंचंति । तयाणंतरं च णं ततं अयोमयं समजोइभूयं अयोमयसंडासएणं गहाय हारं पिणद्धंति । तयाणंतरं च णं अद्धहारं पिणद्धंति, तिसिरियं पिणद्धंति, पालंबं पिणद्धंति, कडिसुत्तयं पिणद्धंति, पट्टुं पिणद्धंति, मउडं पिणद्धंति ।
चिंता तहेव जाव वागरेइ ।
॥६.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇassa bhagavao Mahāvīrassa jeṭṭhe aṃtevāsī jāva rāyamaggamogāḍhe taheva hatthī, āse, purise pāsai | Tesīṃ ca purisāṇaṃ majjhagayaṃ egaṃ purisaṃ pāsai jāva naraṇārisaṃparivuḍaṃ | Tae ṇaṃ taṃ purisaṃ rāyapurisā caccaraṃsi tattasi ayomayaṃsi samajoi-bhūyaṃsi siṃhāsaṇaṃsi ṇivesāvaṃti | Tayāṇantaraṃ ca ṇaṃ purisāṇaṃ majjhagayaṃ purisaṃ bahuoviha ayakalesehiṃ tattehiṃ samajoe-bhūehiṃ, appegaiyā taṃbariyehiṃ, appegaiyā tauariyehiṃ, appegaiyā sīsagariyehiṃ, appegaiyā kalakalriyehiṃ, appegaiyā khāratellriyehiṃ, mahayā mahayā rāyābhiseeṇaṃ abhisiṃcaṃti | Tayāṇantaraṃ ca ṇaṃ tataṃ ayomayaṃ samajoi-bhūyaṃ ayomayasaṃḍāsaeṇaṃ gahāya hāraṃ piṇaddhanti | Tayāṇantaraṃ ca ṇaṃ addhahāraṃ piṇaddhanti, tisiriyaṃ piṇaddhanti, pālaṃbaṃ piṇaddhanti, kaḍisuttayaṃ piṇaddhanti, paṭṭuṃ piṇaddhanti, mauḍaṃ piṇaddhanti |
Ciṃtā taheva jāva vāgarei |
|| 6.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time and in that era, the chief disciple of Bhagavan Mahavir, while traveling along the royal road for alms, saw elephants, horses, and people; among the crowd of people he saw one man surrounded by men and women; royal soldiers then seated that man on a blazing iron throne glowing red like fire in the city square; and thereafter they poured over him, in the manner of a great royal coronation, iron vessels filled with various heated fluids — some filled with molten copper, some with tin, some with lead, some with boiling water, some with caustic hot oil — and then, using iron tongs, placed on him a red-hot iron necklace, a half-necklace, a three-strand ornament, a long pendant garland, a waist-cord, a chest-plate, and a crown.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *jeṭṭhe aṃtevāsī* — the chief disciple (Gautam Swami, eldest and foremost among Mahavir's disciples)
- *rāyamaggamogāḍhe* — having entered/proceeded onto the royal road
- *caccaraṃsi* — in the city square / crossroads / public area
- *ayomayaṃsi siṃhāsaṇaṃsi* — on the iron throne / iron seat
- *samajoi-bhūyaṃsi* — blazing like fire / incandescent (fully heated red-hot)
- *taṃbariye* — filled with molten copper
- *tauariye* — filled with molten tin/zinc
- *sīsagariye* — filled with molten lead
- *kalakalriye* — filled with boiling turbulent water
- *khāratellriye* — filled with caustic/alkaline hot oil
- *rāyābhisee* — royal coronation / royal anointing ceremony
- *ayomayasaṃḍāsae* — iron tongs/pincers
- *hāraṃ* — full necklace; *addhahāraṃ* — half necklace; *tisiriyaṃ* — three-strand necklace
- *pālaṃbaṃ* — long hanging garland; *kaḍisuttayaṃ* — waist-thread/cord; *paṭṭuṃ* — chest-plate ornament; *mauḍaṃ* — crown
- *ciṃtā taheva jāva vāgarei* — Gautam's reflection arises as before (as in chapter one), and Bhagavan answers

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra depicts one of the most haunting scenes in the entire Vipaak Sutra: a man being "crowned" not with jewels and sandalwood paste, but with boiling metals and caustic oil, while seated on a throne of white-hot iron. The grotesque parody of a royal coronation — where every liquid poured is a burning punishment and every ornament placed is a brand of scorching iron — is a deliberate and calculated execution method. The punishment fits the crime in a dark symbolic way: Nandivardhana wanted the throne so desperately that he was willing to commit patricide, so he is given the throne — but made of fire. This scene is what stops Gautam Swami in his tracks. Having seen the visions of hell repeatedly through the Lord's teachings, Gautam recognizes the pattern: this man is suffering the fruit of past wickedness. The "reflection arises as before" (ciṃtā taheva) refers to the standard moment in each chapter where Gautam, moved by what he sees, resolves to ask Bhagavan the truth behind the spectacle.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Gautam Swami, while walking through Mathura, saw a man being tortured in a horrifying mock-coronation — seated on a red-hot iron throne and "crowned" with burning metal ornaments — and decided to ask Bhagavan Mahavir why this was happening to him.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Every throne sought through harm becomes a throne of fire. What do I desire so intensely that I might justify wrong action to obtain it?

## Nandivardhano no Purvabhav (Past Life of Nandivardhana)

---

### SUTRA 6.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु गोयमा ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं इहेव जंबुद्दीवे दीवे भारहे वासे सीहपुरे णामं णयरे होत्था । रिद्धिथिमियसमिद्धे, वण्णओ । तत्थ णं सीहपुरे णयरे सीहरहे णामं राया होत्था । तस्स णं सीहरहस्स रण्णो दुज्जोहणे णामं चारगपालए होत्था, अहम्मिए जाव दुप्पडियाणंदे ।
॥६.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Evaṃ khalu Goyamā! Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ iheva Jambudīve dīve Bhārahe vāse Sīhapure nāmaṃ nayare hotthā | Riddhi-thimiya-samiḍḍhe, vaṇṇao | Tattha ṇaṃ Sīhapure nayare Sīharahe nāmaṃ rāyā hotthā | Tassa ṇaṃ Sīharahassa raṇṇo Dujjohane nāmaṃ cāragapālae hotthā, ahammie jāva duppraḍiyānaṃde |
|| 6.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus, O Gautam! At that time and in that era, here on this very continent of Jambudvipa, in the land of Bharata, there was a city named Sinhapur — prosperous, stable, and affluent; in that city of Sinhapur, a king named Sinharatha ruled; and that king Sinharatha had a prison warden named Durjodhan — who was irreligious, and who took pleasure in causing others' misery.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *Goyamā* — O Gautam (Bhagavan addressing his chief disciple)
- *Jambudīve dīve Bhārahe vāse* — on the continent called Jambudvipa, in the region called Bharata (the known world in Jain cosmology)
- *Sīhapure nāmaṃ nayare* — a city called Sinhapur (Lion City)
- *riddhi-thimiya-samiḍḍhe* — prosperous, stable, and well-stocked (a standard Agamic description of a flourishing city)
- *Sīharahe nāmaṃ rāyā* — a king named Sinharatha (Lion-chariot)
- *Dujjohane nāmaṃ cāragapālae* — a prison warden named Durjodhan (evil-fighter / difficult to defeat in wickedness)
- *ahammie* — irreligious, without dharma
- *duppraḍiyānaṃde* — one who finds delight in others' suffering / one who is pleased by cruelty

**5. COMMENTARY**

Bhagavan now reveals the past life that set the karmic trajectory in motion. The soul now manifested as Nandivardhana was previously born as Durjodhan — a prison warden in the wealthy city of Sinhapur. The character of Durjodhan is sketched in two devastating words: *ahammie* (without dharma, without moral order) and *duppraḍiyānaṃde* (one who takes pleasure in the suffering of others). This last quality is among the most spiritually destructive tendencies a being can harbor — not merely causing harm from greed or fear, but actively delighting in cruelty. A prison warden who enjoys the pain of prisoners is not simply doing a job; he is feeding his soul on violence. This is the karmic seed that Jain philosophy would identify as the binding of intense negative karma — particularly the karma associated with causing extreme pain to sentient beings who are helpless before you.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The man being tortured was previously born as Durjodhan, a prison warden in the city of Sinhapur, who had no religion in his heart and actually enjoyed making prisoners suffer.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I hold power over anyone — even small power, even briefly — what do I feel? Do I feel the weight of responsibility, or something else?

## Karagruh Adhipatino Ghor Atyachar (Atrocities of the Prison Chief)

---

### SUTRA 6.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तस्स णं दुज्जोहणस्स चारगपालगस्स इमेयारूवे चारगभंडे होत्था– बहवे अयकुंडीओ अप्पेगइयाओ तंबरिएओ, अप्पेगइयाओ तउयरिएओ, अप्पेगइयाओ सीसगरिएओ, अप्पेगइयाओ कलकलरिएओ, अप्पेगइयाओ खारतेल्लरिएओ– अणिगिकायंसि अद्धियाओ चिट्टुंति ।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The prison warden Durjodhan possessed the following prison equipment: many iron cauldrons — some filled with molten copper, some with molten tin, some with molten lead, some with violently boiling water, some with caustic scalding oil — all placed constantly on fire; large clay pots filled with the urine of horses, elephants, cows, buffaloes, camels, goats, and sheep; heaps and stocks of hand shackles, foot shackles, wooden stocks, iron fetters, and iron chains; heaps of bamboo rods, rattan rods, jamboline-wood rods, leather whips, ordinary whips, and bark whips; heaps of stones, wooden clubs, iron hammers, and stone mallets; heaps of leather ropes, ordinary ropes, bark-fiber ropes, and hair ropes; heaps of sword-leaf blades, saw-blades, razor-blades, and kadambachira cutting weapons; heaps of iron nails, iron thorns, dried leather strips, and fresh leather strips; heaps of needles, pricking instruments, and hooked pointed tools; heaps of spears, axes, hatchets, nail-cutters, and sharp-tipped grass-knife instruments.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra presents an exhaustive inventory of Durjodhan's prison arsenal, and its very exhaustiveness is the point. The Agama does not list all these implements to be gratuitous — it does so to establish the sheer systematic comprehensiveness of Durjodhan's cruelty. He did not merely have a stick and a chain; he had an entire apparatus of suffering, organized by category: metals for burning, animal wastes for degradation, restraints for immobilization, rods and whips for beating, stones and hammers for crushing, ropes for binding and suspending, blades for cutting, nails and needles for piercing, and axes and spears for more extreme mutilations. The cauldrons kept on constant fire — always ready, never cooling — speak to a warden who did not wait for crimes to be brought to him. He maintained a permanent infrastructure of pain. From a karmic standpoint, the construction, maintenance, and active use of each of these instruments constituted a continuous act of violence — binding layer upon layer of suffering karma onto the soul of Durjodhan with every passing day.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Durjodhan's prison was stocked with every possible tool of torture — burning vats, urine pots, chains, whips, knives, needles, and axes — all kept ready at all times, like a factory of suffering.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What instruments of harm — subtle or obvious — do I keep "ready" in my own heart? What do I maintain that I tell myself I will never actually use?

### SUTRA 6.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से दुज्जोहणे चारगपाले सीहरहस्स रण्णो बहवे चोरे य पारदारिए य गंठिभेए य रायावकारी य अणहारए य बालघायए य विस्संभघायए य जूयगरे य खंडपट्टे य पुरिसेहिं गिण्हावेइ, गिण्हावित्ता उत्ताणए पाडेइ, पाडेत्ता लोहदंडेणं मुहं विहाडेइ, विहाडित्ता अप्पेगइए तत्ततंबं पज्जेइ, अप्पेगइए तउयं पज्जेइ, अप्पेगइए सीसगं पज्जेइ, अप्पेगइए कलकलं पज्जेइ, अप्पेगइए खारतेल्लं पज्जेइ, अप्पेगइयाणं तेणं चेव अभिसेयगं करेइ ।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then the prison warden Durjodhan would have seized by royal soldiers: thieves, adulterers, burglars, rebels against the king, tax-evaders, child-killers, traitors, gamblers, and corrupt persons; after seizing them he would throw them face up, then pry open their mouths with an iron rod, and pour into some molten copper, into some molten tin, into some molten lead, into some violently boiling water, into some caustic oil, and bathe some with these same fluids; he would throw some face up and force-feed them horse urine — and likewise elephant, cow, buffalo, camel, goat, and sheep urine; he would throw some face down and cause violent vomiting, then crush them with the same fluid; he would shackle some with hand restraints, some with foot restraints, some with wooden stocks, some with iron fetters, some contorted and twisted, some in iron chains; he would cut off some's hands, and cause others to be struck with bamboo rods through to bark whips; he would lay some face up and place stones and wood on their chests, then thrust clubs beneath them and have soldiers shake them causing bones to break; he would bind some's hands and feet with leather ropes through to cotton ropes and hurl them into wells; he would flay some with sword-leaf blades through to kadambachira weapons and then rub caustic oil into their wounds; he would drive iron nails and iron thorns into foreheads, shoulders, elbows, knees, and ankles, and break the body's joints; he would drive needles and pointed instruments into the fingers and toes, then dig the earth with hooked tools; he would skin some with swords and nail-cutters, wrap them in sharp grass and wet leather, then place them in the sun — and when the leather dried and tightened, it would crack them open with a cracking sound.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *core* — thieves; *pāradārie* — adulterers; *gaṃṭhibhee* — burglars (those who break locks/nodes); *rāyāvakārī* — rebels against the king; *aṇahārae* — those who do not pay taxes/tribute; *bālaghāyae* — child-killers; *vissaṃbhaghāyae* — traitors (those who betray trust); *jūyagare* — gamblers; *khaṃdapaṭṭa* — corrupt/fraudulent persons
- *uttāṇae pāḍei* — throws face-up / on the back
- *lohadaṃḍeṇaṃ muhaṃ vihāḍei* — pries open the mouth with an iron rod
- *pajjei* — causes to drink / pours into
- *abhiseyagaṃ karei* — performs a bathing/anointing (with burning fluids)
- *heṭṭāmuhae pāḍei* — throws face-down; *vammāvei* — causes violent vomiting
- *saṃkoḍiyamoḍiyaṃ* — contorted and twisted (extreme restraint position)
- *satthovāḍiyaṃ* — cutting/flaying with sword
- *ukkampāvei* — causes to shake/tremble (shaking stones placed on body)
- *āyavaṃsi* — in sunlight; *dalaittā sukkhe samāṇe caḍacaḍassa uppāvei* — when dried, tears open with a cracking sound (leather shrinkage flaying)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is among the most disturbing passages in the Vipaak Sutra, and its inclusion in sacred scripture carries a deliberate pedagogical purpose. The Jain tradition does not look away from the consequences of evil. By cataloguing each method of Durjodhan's torture in such clinical detail, the text forces the reader to confront what cruelty truly looks like when systematized by one who has chosen it as a way of life. Among the most disturbing elements: the victims include not only criminals but people who failed to pay taxes — revealing that the judicial system of the time could deliver anyone to this warden's hands. The methods escalate in sophistication: from pouring burning metal to humiliation through animal waste, to the gruesome sunlight-and-leather technique at the end, where wet leather is wrapped around the prisoner and when it dries and contracts in the sun, it tears the skin with an audible cracking sound. Each act Durjodhan committed was a fresh accumulation of karma. And yet the sutra makes clear: he did this as his "duty," finding in it not reluctance but pleasure.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Durjodhan carried out unimaginably brutal torture on prisoners — burning them, choking them, crushing them, flaying them alive — and did all of this day after day as his normal job.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Harm that becomes routine loses none of its weight karmically. What have I normalized in my own life that once would have troubled me?

### SUTRA 6.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से दुज्जोहणे चारगपाले एयकम्मे एयप्पहाणे एयविज्जे एयसमायारे सुबहुं पावकम्मं समज्जिणित्ता एगतीसं वाससयाइं परमाउयं पालित्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा छट्टीए पुढवीए उक्कोसेणं बावीससागरोवमं ट्ठितिएसु णेरइएसु णेरइयत्ताए उवण्णे ।
॥६.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Tae ṇaṃ se Dujjohāṇe cāragapāle eyakamme eyapphāṇe eyavijje eyasamāyāre subahuṃ pāvakammaṃ samajjiṇittā egatīsaṃ vāsasayāiṃ paramāuyaṃ pālittā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe ukkosenaaṃ bāvīsasāgarovaṃ ṭhitiesu ṇeraiesu ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇe |
|| 6.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then the prison warden Durjodhan, having made this very activity his sole occupation, his supreme purpose, his only expertise, and his constant practice — and having accumulated an enormous mass of evil karma — after completing his maximum lifespan of three hundred and ten years, died at the time of death and was born as a hell-being among the hell-beings of the sixth hell, with a maximum duration of twenty-two ocean-measure periods.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *eyakamme* — having this (torture/cruelty) as his sole karma/work
- *eyapphāṇe* — having this as his primary/supreme purpose
- *eyavijje* — having this as his only knowledge/expertise
- *eyasamāyāre* — having this as his constant practice/conduct
- *subahuṃ pāvakammaṃ samajjiṇittā* — having accumulated an enormous quantity of evil karma
- *egatīsaṃ vāsasayāiṃ* — three hundred and ten years (31 × 10 = 310 years lifespan)
- *paramāuyaṃ pālittā* — having completed his maximum lifespan
- *kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā* — having died at the proper time of death
- *chaṭṭhīe puḍhavīe* — in the sixth hell-realm (Tamaḥprabha — the realm of deep darkness)
- *ukkosenaaṃ bāvīsasāgarovaṃ* — with the maximum lifespan of twenty-two sagaropam (ocean-measure units of time — inconceivably vast)
- *ṇeraiyattāe uvaṇṇe* — born as a hell-being

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra's four parallel phrases — his sole work, his supreme purpose, his only expertise, his constant conduct — form a devastating portrait of complete spiritual captivity. Durjodhan did not torture occasionally, or reluctantly, or even professionally while maintaining some inner distance. He *was* this work. His entire identity, skill, attention, and daily life were consumed by the act of causing pain. In Jain karmic philosophy, the intensity of karma bound to the soul depends not only on what one does, but on the degree of intentionality, delight, and repetition. Durjodhan combined all three: he chose this, he enjoyed it, and he did it every day for 310 years. The result is the sixth hell — the fifth-deepest of the seven hells — with a maximum term of twenty-two sagaropam. A sagaropam is a time-unit so vast that it can only be conceived through the image of an ocean being emptied one drop per century. Twenty-two such units in the sixth hell means suffering of a scope the human mind cannot hold. And yet the mathematics of karma are exact: what was done to others is now experienced in the self.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Because Durjodhan spent his entire life of 310 years torturing people and delighting in their pain, he was reborn into the sixth level of hell — one of the deepest — where he suffered for twenty-two unimaginably long cosmic time periods.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What we practice becomes who we are. What am I becoming through my repeated daily choices?

### SUTRA 6.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

से णं तओ अणंतरं उव्वट्टित्ता इहेव महुराए णगरीए सिरिदामस्स रण्णो बंधुसिरीए देवीए कुंछिसि पुत्ताए उववण्णे । तए णं बंधुसिरि णवण्हं मासाणं बहुपडिपुण्णाणं जाव दारगं पयाया । तए णं तस्स दारगस्स अम्मापियरो णिच्छत्ते बारसाहे इमं एयारूवं णामधेज्जं करेंति-होउ णं अम्हं दारगे णंदिवद्धणे णामेणं ।

Tae ṇaṃ se Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre paṃcadhāīparivuḍe jāva parivuḍui | Tae ṇaṃ se Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre ummukkaabālabhāve viṇṇāyapariṇayamette jovvaṇagamanuppe viharai jāva juvarāyā jāe yāviṃ hotthā |

|| 6.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Immediately after passing from that existence, he was reborn in the womb of Queen Bandhushri, the wife of King Shreedama, right here in the city of Mathura, as a son; after nine months were fully complete, Bandhushri gave birth to the child; on the twelfth day of the birth ceremony, the parents gave the child the name Nandivardhana; Prince Nandivardhana grew up cared for by five wet nurses; he passed through childhood, attained full education, and reached youth — and was appointed as crown prince; then Nandivardhana, obsessed with the kingdom and the inner palace, desired to kill King Shreedama and rule the kingdom himself; and he began constantly watching for opportunities, weaknesses, and moments when the king was alone and vulnerable.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *uvvaṭṭittā* — having passed out of / having emerged from (the hell existence)
- *kuṃchisi puttāe uvavaṇṇe* — entered the womb as a son
- *ṇicchatte bārasāhe* — on the twelfth day after birth (the naming ceremony)
- *ṇāmadhejjaṃ kareṃti* — give the name
- *Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe* — "one who increases joy and prosperity" (the name's meaning)
- *paṃcadhāīparivuḍe* — surrounded/cared for by five wet nurses
- *ummukkaabālabhāva* — having passed beyond the stage of childhood
- *viṇṇāyapariṇayamette* — having reached the fullness of education and understanding
- *jovvaṇagamanuppe* — having arrived at the attainment of youth
- *rajje ya aṃteure ya mucchhie* — deeply attached/obsessed with the kingdom and the inner palace (throne-room and women's quarters — symbols of total royal power)
- *jīviyāo vaoroveettā* — taking away the life / killing
- *aṃtarāṇi chiddhāṇi virāhāṇi* — opportunities, gaps/vulnerabilities, moments of aloneness
- *piṃḍajāgaremāṇe* — constantly watching, perpetually alert (like a spider waiting)

**5. COMMENTARY**

After the incomprehensible suffering of the sixth hell, the soul emerges and takes birth — not in some distant land, but right back in Mathura, in the very royal household as the king's own son. This is the Agamic teaching on the continuity of karmic consequence: the soul carries its unresolved desires and impressions from life to life. The soul that was Durjodhan — who wielded absolute power over helpless prisoners — is now reborn with the compulsion toward power still burning. As Nandivardhana, he grows up in comfort, receives education, is given the title of crown prince, and yet cannot wait. The deeper tragedy is embedded in the name itself: "Nandivardhana" means "one who increases joy and prosperity" — and yet this very person was working to destroy the source of his family's joy. The description of him "watching like a hunter" for moments when his father would be alone and vulnerable is chilling. The soul that once ran a prison, watching for prisoners to punish, now watches for a parent to destroy.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

After his punishment in hell, Durjodhan's soul was reborn as Nandivardhana, the prince of Mathura — but the same inner greed for power drove him to secretly plot his own father's murder.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What inner hungers persist in me — beneath all my circumstances, beneath all my education — quietly driving my choices? What am I watching for?

## Pitruvdhano Duhsankalp (The Terrible Plot to Kill His Father)

---

### SUTRA 6.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से णंदिवद्धणे कुमारे सिरिदामस्स रण्णो अंतरं अलभमाणे अण्णया कयाइ चित्तं अलंकारियं सद्धावेइ, सद्धावेत्ता एवं वयासी– तुम्हे णं देवाणुप्पिया ! सिरिदामस्स रण्णो सव्वट्टाणेसु य सव्वभूमिया य अंतेउरे य दिण्णियारे य सिरिदामस्स रण्णो अभिकखणं अभिकखणं अलंकारियं कम्मं करेमाणे विहरसि । तं णं तुमं देवाणुप्पिया ! सिरिदामस्स रण्णो अलंकारियं कम्मं करेमाणे गीवाए खुरं णिवेसेहि ।
तो णं अहं तुम्हं अद्धरज्जयं करिस्सामि । तुमं अम्हेहिं सद्धिं उरालाइं भोगभोगाइं भुंजमाणे विहरिस्ससि ।
तए णं से चित्ते अलंकारिए णंदिवद्धणस्स कुमारस्स एयमट्टुं पडिसुणेइ ।
॥६.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Tae ṇaṃ se Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre Siridāmassa raṇṇo aṃtaraṃ alabhamāṇe aṇṇayā kayāi Cittaṃ alaṃkāriyaṃ saddhāvei, saddhāvettā evaṃ vayāsī – tumhe ṇaṃ devāṇuppiyā! Siridāmassa raṇṇo savvaṭṭāṇesu ya savvabhūmiyā ya aṃteure ya diṇṇiyāre ya Siridāmassa raṇṇo abhikkhaṇaṃ abhikkhaṇaṃ alaṃkāriyaṃ kammaṃ karemāṇe viharasi | Taṃ ṇaṃ tumaṃ devāṇuppiyā! Siridāmassa raṇṇo alaṃkāriyaṃ kammaṃ karemāṇe gīvāe khuraṃ ṇivesehī |
To ṇaṃ ahaṃ tumhaṃ addhārajjaṃ karissāmi | Tumaṃ amhehiṃ saddhiṃ urālāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇe viharissasi |
Tae ṇaṃ se Citte alaṃkāriye Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇassa kumārassa eyamaṭṭhuṃ paḍisuṇei |
|| 6.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then Prince Nandivardhana, finding no opportunity to reach the king alone, one day summoned the royal barber Chitra and spoke to him thus: "O dear Chitra! You have been given free access to all of King Shreedama's rooms, chambers, inner quarters, and grounds, and you attend on the king continuously and repeatedly in your barber's duties. So, O dear one! While performing your barber's service on King Shreedama, place the razor upon his throat." "And then I shall give you half the kingdom. You shall live with us enjoying the finest pleasures." And Chitra the barber heard this proposal from Prince Nandivardhana and agreed to it.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *aṃtaraṃ alabhamāṇe* — not finding an opportunity / gap
- *Cittaṃ alaṃkāriyaṃ saddhāvei* — summons Chitra the barber/beautician
- *devāṇuppiyā* — "O beloved of the gods" / a polite honorific address
- *abhikkhaṇaṃ abhikkhaṇaṃ* — repeatedly, again and again (constant attendance)
- *gīvāe khuraṃ ṇivesehī* — place the razor on his throat (command to cut the king's throat while shaving)
- *addhārajjaṃ* — half the kingdom
- *urālāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ* — the finest, most abundant pleasures and comforts
- *paḍisuṇei* — heard and agreed / accepted the proposal

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra reveals the moral abyss into which Nandivardhana had descended. Unable to find a natural opportunity to harm his own father, he turned to the one person with the most intimate access to the king's body: the royal barber. The barber's razor, used in a moment of trust — when the king's throat is exposed and his eyes are closed — would be the perfect murder weapon. The prince offered half the kingdom for this single act, clothing his conspiracy in generous terms. The barber Chitra's agreement is understandable from a human perspective — the temptation of half a kingdom was immense. Yet what the story highlights is that Chitra was being asked to convert the deepest royal trust into an instrument of murder. The razor that served the king's beauty was to become the knife of his death. In Jain ethics, those who arrange for others to commit harm accumulate karma just as directly as those who act themselves — the instigator and the agent share the karmic weight of the deed.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Unable to get to his father alone, Nandivardhana secretly bribed the royal barber Chitra — who shaved the king every day — to cut the king's throat during a shaving session, promising him half the kingdom in return.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What have I asked someone else to do that I knew was wrong — using their access, their position, their trust — to carry out something I was unwilling to do myself?

## Shadyantrini Nishfalta (The Conspiracy Fails)

---

### SUTRA 6.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं तस्स चित्तस्स अलंकारियस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झत्थिए जाव समुप्पज्जित्था जइ णं मम सिरिदामे राया एयमट्टुं आगमेइ, तए णं मम णं णज्जइ केणइ असुभेणं कुमारेणं मारिस्सइ त्ति कट्टु भीए जाव जेणेव सिरिदामे राया तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता सिरिदामं रायं रहस्सियगं करयलपरिग्गहियं सिरसावत्तं मत्थए अंजलिं कट्टु एवं वयासी–
एवं खलु सामी ! णंदिवद्धणे कुमारे रज्जे य जाव अंतेउरे य मुच्छिए जाव अज्झोव्वण्णे इच्छइ तुब्भे जीवियाओ वओरोवित्ता सयमेव रज्जसिरिं कारेमाणे पालेमाणे विहरित्तए ।
तए णं से सिरिदामे राया चित्तस्स अलंकारियस्स एयमट्टुं सोच्चा णिसम्म आसुरुत्ते जाव णंदिसेणं कुमारं पुरिसेहिं गिण्हावेइ, गिण्हावित्ता ए एणं विहाणेणं वज्झं आणवेइ ।
तं एवं खलु गोयमा ! णंदिवद्धणे कुमारे पुरापोराणाणं जाव कम्माणं पावगं फलविट्टिविसेसं पच्चणुभवमाणे विहरइ ।
॥६.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Tae ṇaṃ tassa Cittassa alaṃkāriyassa imeyārūve ajjhitthie jāva samuppajjitthā jai ṇaṃ mama Siridāme rāyā eyamaṭṭhuṃ āgamei, tae ṇaṃ mama ṇaṃ ṇajjai keṇai asubheṇaṃ kumāreṇaṃ mārissai tti kattu bhīe jāva jeṇeva Siridāme rāyā teṇeva uvāgacchai, uvāgacchittā Siridāmaṃ rāyaṃ rahassiyagaṃ karayalaparggaihiyaṃ sirasāvattaṃ matthae aṃjaliṃ kattu evaṃ vayāsī –
Evaṃ khalu sāmī! Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre rajje ya jāva aṃteure ya mucchhie jāva ajjhohavaṇṇe icchai tubbhe jīviyāo vaoroveittā sayameva rajjasiriṃ kāremāṇe pālemāṇe viharittae |
Tae ṇaṃ se Siridāme rāyā Cittassa alaṃkāriyassa eyamaṭṭhuṃ soccā ṇisammai āsurute jāva Naṃdisenaṃ kumāraṃ purisehiṃ giṇhāvei, giṇhāvittā ae eṇaṃ vihāṇeṇaṃ vajjhaṃ āṇavei |
Taṃ evaṃ khalu Goyamā! Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre purāporāṇāṇaṃ jāva kammāṇaṃ pāvagaṃ phalaviṭṭhivisesaṃ paccaṇubhavamāṇe viharai |
|| 6.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then within the barber Chitra's mind arose this thought: "If King Shreedama were to come to know of this matter, he might have me killed by some cruel means on account of the prince" — and out of this fear, he went directly to where King Shreedama was; approaching the king, he took his hands together respectfully with his palms joined, bowed his head, and spoke thus in private: "O my Lord! Prince Nandivardhana, intoxicated with desire for the kingdom and the inner palace, wishes to take away your life and rule the kingdom himself." Upon hearing and fully grasping this report from the barber Chitra, King Shreedama was consumed with fury, and had Prince Nandivardhana seized by soldiers — and commanded that he be executed by the prescribed method. Thus, O Gautam! Prince Nandivardhana is experiencing the particular bitter fruit of ancient evil deeds.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *ajjhitthie samuppajjitthā* — a thought/reflection arose within him
- *rahassiyagaṃ* — privately, in secret
- *karayalaparggaihiyaṃ sirasāvattaṃ matthae aṃjaliṃ* — with hands joined in reverence, palms together raised to the head (the formal gesture of submission and respect)
- *mucchhie ajjhohavaṇṇe* — deeply intoxicated, completely overwhelmed (with desire for the throne)
- *āsurute* — filled with fury / enraged
- *vajjhaṃ āṇavei* — commands the death sentence / orders execution
- *purāporāṇāṇaṃ kammāṇaṃ pāvagaṃ phalaviṭṭhivisesaṃ paccaṇubhavamāṇe* — experiencing the specific and particular bitter fruit-ripening of ancient evil deeds

**5. COMMENTARY**

The conspiracy's collapse comes not from a divine intervention, but from an ordinary human calculation: Chitra, who had agreed to murder out of greed, became afraid of the consequences. When he imagined the king discovering the plot and punishing *him* for it, his self-preservation overrode his greed. This is not dharma saving the king — it is one criminal being afraid of another. Yet through this imperfect chain of human fear and self-interest, justice arrives. The king's fury at the report is total: he does not hold a trial or summon witnesses. He acts immediately and harshly. The "prescribed method" of execution — the mock coronation with boiling metals that Gautam witnessed in the public square — is thus the sentence carried out, fulfilling the scene with which the chapter opened. Bhagavan's final statement to Gautam seals the theological meaning: this is not arbitrary royal cruelty. This is the precise ripening of ancient karma — the karma of Durjodhan, who once presided over burning iron thrones and boiling vats, now experiencing them himself.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The barber Chitra got scared and told the king everything. King Shreedama, furious, had his own son Nandivardhana arrested and condemned to the very kind of torture that Nandivardhana's past-life self had inflicted on others.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Karma does not simply punish — it mirrors. What mirror is my current suffering holding up to me?

## Nandivardhano nu Bhavishya ane Bhavbhraman (Nandivardhana's Future and Wandering Through Existences)

---

### SUTRA 6.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णंदिवद्धणे णं भंते ! कुमारे इओ चुए कालमासे कालं किच्चा किहं गच्छिहिइ ? किहं उवव्वजिहिइ ?
गोयमा ! णंदिवद्धणे कुमारे सट्ठिवासाइं परमाउयं पालित्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा इमीसे रयणप्पभाए पुढवीए जाव संसारो तहेव ।
तओ हत्थिणाउरे णयरे मच्छत्ताए उवव्वजिहिइ । से णं तत्थ मच्छिएहिं विहिए समाणे तत्थेव सेट्टिकुले पुत्ताए पच्चायाहिइ । बोहिं, सोहम्मे कप्पे, महाविदेहवासे सिज्झिहिइ जाव सव्वदुक्खाणमंतं करेहिइ ।
णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स ।
॥६.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe ṇaṃ bhaṃte! kumāre io cue kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā kihaṃ gacchihii? Kihaṃ uvavvajihii?
Goyamā! Naṃdivaḍḍhaṇe kumāre saṭṭhivāsāiṃ paramāuyaṃ pālittā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā imīse Rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe jāva saṃsāro taheva |
Tao Hatthiṇāure ṇayare macchattāe uvavvajihii | Se ṇaṃ tattha macchiyehiṃ vihie samāṇe tattheva seṭṭhikule puttāe paccāyāhii | Bohiṃ, Sohamme kappe, Mahāvidehavāse sijjhihii jāva savvadukkkhāṇamaṃtaṃ karehii |
Ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa |
|| 6.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"O Bhagavan! When Prince Nandivardhana dies here and passes at the time of death, where will he go? Where will he be reborn?" "O Gautam! Prince Nandivardhana, having completed his maximum lifespan of sixty years, will die at the time of death and be born in the first hell-realm of Ratnaprabha" — and the cycle of wandering is as described before; thereafter he will be reborn as a fish in the city of Hastinapur; after being killed by fishermen there, he will be reborn as a son in a merchant's family in that same city; there he will attain awakening, be born in the Saudharma heaven, and will in the Mahavideh region attain liberation and bring all suffering to an end. The closing formula is as in the first chapter.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *bhaṃte* — O Bhagavan / Reverend Lord (form of respectful address to Mahavir)
- *io cue* — passing away from here / dying from this existence
- *kihaṃ gacchihii? Kihaṃ uvavvajihii?* — where will he go? where will he be reborn?
- *saṭṭhivāsāiṃ paramāuyaṃ* — sixty years of maximum lifespan
- *Rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe* — in the Ratnaprabha (Jewel-Light) hell — the first (shallowest) of the seven hells
- *saṃsāro taheva* — the cycle of wandering (through the various body-forms) is as described in the first chapter
- *Hatthiṇāure ṇayare macchattāe* — reborn as a fish in the city of Hastinapur
- *macchiyehiṃ vihie* — having been killed by fishermen
- *seṭṭhikule puttāe* — born as the son of a merchant family (śreṣṭhi-kula)
- *paccāyāhii* — will be reborn
- *Bohiṃ* — will attain bodhi (spiritual awakening, right understanding)
- *Sohamme kappe* — in the Saudharma heaven (the first of the celestial realms)
- *Mahāvidehavāse* — in the region called Mahavideh (where a living Tirthankara is always present)
- *sijjhihii* — will be liberated / will achieve moksha
- *savvadukkkhāṇamaṃtaṃ karehii* — will bring all suffering to an end
- *Ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa* — the closing section is the same as in the first chapter

**5. COMMENTARY**

Gautam's question about the future is the final turning of the wheel in each chapter's narrative. The answer reveals that even for Nandivardhana — a patricide who plotted to murder his own father and who carried the karma of Durjodhan's 310 years of systematic torture — liberation is not permanently out of reach. The path is long: first the first hell (a lesser term than Durjodhan's sixth-hell sentence, reflecting the lesser accumulated karma of this life), then the wandering through countless forms, then rebirth as a fish in Hastinapur, then violent death, then a merchant family in which the opportunity for spiritual awakening finally arrives. The fish-birth in Hastinapur is significant: Hastinapur (the city of elephants) appears repeatedly in the Agamas as a place where souls begin their final ascent. The merchant family provides the stability, resources, and dharmic exposure needed for bodhi. After the awakening, the soul rises through the Saudharma heaven, takes a final human birth in Mahavideh — where a living liberated teacher is always present — and achieves final liberation. Every soul's story ends in liberation. The question is only how long and how many turns of the wheel it takes to get there.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bhagavan tells Gautam that after his current punishment is over, Nandivardhana will go through more difficult rebirths — including as a fish — but eventually, in a future human life, he will wake up spiritually, reach liberation, and be free from all suffering forever.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

No matter how far I have fallen, liberation is still my destination. What one step, however small, could I take today toward waking up?

## Vivechan (Commentary and Reflection)

The relationship between parent and child is said to be among the deepest bonds of love in existence. And yet, when the accumulated weight of past-life karma rises to the surface, even this bond can be severed by hatred and greed. This chapter shows a prince — loved, educated, privileged, and already named as heir — plotting the murder of his own father out of impatience. The relationship between karma and circumstance is illustrated precisely here: Nandivardhana's soul carried the deep impressions of Durjodhan, who had wielded absolute power over helpless beings. That same craving for control and power — unable to be contained by the mere promise of a crown — drove him to the darkest possible act.

The chapter's justice is both karmic and symbolic: the man who operated burning iron thrones and boiling metal vats as instruments of others' punishment now experiences the exact same apparatus applied to his own body in a public square. The Agama does not present this as mere poetic vengeance — it presents it as the natural operation of a precise universal law.

From some manuscript traditions of the Vipaak Sutra, this chapter's protagonist appears under the name **Nandisena** rather than Nandivardhana. The Tika (commentary) of Acharya Abhaydeva Suri, when explaining the term "Nandisena" in the Sthananga Sutra, also references the name Nandivardhana as an alternative. The present edition follows the name Nandivardhana as it is accepted in this recension.

*The true poverty is not the poverty of wealth but the poverty of patience — for it is impatience that turns inheritance into crime, and a crown into a throne of fire.*
