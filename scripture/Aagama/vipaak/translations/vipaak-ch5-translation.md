# Shri Vipaak Sutra — Chapter 5 Translation

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary (Shri Vipaak Sutra, Shri Guru Prana Prakashan, Parasdham, Ghatkopar)
**Chapter:** Fifth Adhyayan — Bruhaspatidat (પાંચમું અધ્યયન — બૃહસ્પતિદત્ત)
**Section:** Pratham Shrutaskandh — Dukha Vipaak Sutra (First Canon — Scripture of the Fruits of Evil Deeds)
**Scripture Pages:** 82–88
**PDF Pages:** 134–140
**Total Sutras:** 12
**Theme:** A royal priest named Bruhaspatidat suffers devastating consequences — in this very life and beyond — for the terrible violence and betrayal committed across his lifetimes. The chapter illustrates with precision how even the most deeply buried karma surfaces in its own time, without fail.

---

## ADHYAYAN PRARAMBH (Chapter Opening)

---

### SUTRA 5.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पञ्चमस्स उक्खेवो ।
॥५.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

pañcamassa ukkhevo |
|| 5.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The invocation-opening of the fifth chapter commences.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- pañcamassa = of the fifth (chapter)
- ukkhevo = the uplift/opening/invocation (utkṣepa — formal commencement of a chapter)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This brief opening sutra formally inaugurates the fifth chapter. It signals continuity with the preceding chapters and instructs that the opening of this chapter follows the same form as the first chapter. In the tradition of Jain canonical literature, each new chapter opens with an utkṣepa — a formal announcement that honors the chapter's place within the whole body of scripture. The word ukkhevo literally means "that which is lifted up," indicating this sutra elevates what follows into a sacred space. Like the formal opening of a temple gate before worship, this single word acknowledges that what we are about to enter is not ordinary — it is the living record of a soul's journey across many existences.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The fifth chapter now begins, following the same formal opening as the first chapter.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you begin something new — a day, a conversation, a task — do you enter it consciously and with awareness, or do you simply rush in without acknowledging the space you are stepping into?

## SETTING THE SCENE

---

### SUTRA 5.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं कोसंबी णामं णयरी होत्था । रिद्धित्थिमयसमिद्धा, वण्णओ । बाहिं चंदोतरणे उज्जाणे । सेयभद्दे जक्खे ।
तत्थ णं कोसंबीए णयरीए सयाणीए णामं राया होत्था । वण्णओ । मियावई देवी, वण्णओ । तस्स णं सयाणीयस्स पुत्ते मियादेवीए अत्तए उदायणे णामं कुमारे होत्था, अहीणपडिपुण्णपंचिंदियसरीरे जाव जुवराया । तस्स णं उदायणस्स कुमारस्स पउमावई णामं देवी होत्था ।
॥५.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ kosambī nāmaṃ ṇayarī hotthā | riddhitthimayasamiddhā, vaṇṇao | bāhiṃ caṃdotaraṇe ujjāṇe | seybhadde jakhe |
tattha ṇaṃ kosambīe ṇayarīe sayāṇīe nāmaṃ rāyā hotthā | vaṇṇao | miyāvaī devī, vaṇṇao | tassa ṇaṃ sayāṇīyassa putte miyādevīe attae udāyaṇe nāmaṃ kumāre hotthā, ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapañciṃdiyasarīre jāva juvārāyā | tassa ṇaṃ udāyaṇassa kumārassa paumāvaī nāmaṃ devī hotthā |
|| 5.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus it is, O Jambu! In that time and in that season, there was a city called Kaushambi — prosperous, wealthy, and abundant, as described. Outside the city was a garden called Chandravataran, with the yaksha shrine of Shvetabhad. In that city of Kaushambi reigned a king named Shatanick, as described. His queen was Mrigavati, as described. Shatanick's son — born of Mrigavati — was a prince named Udayan, whose body was perfectly complete in all five senses, right up to being crown prince. Prince Udayan's wife was the queen named Padmavati.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- evaṃ khalu jaṃbū = Thus indeed, O Jambu (Gautam Swami is narrating to his disciple Jambu Swami)
- teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ = in that time, in that season
- kosambī nāmaṃ ṇayarī = a city named Kaushambi
- riddhitthimayasamiddhā = rich with prosperity, people, and material abundance
- vaṇṇao = as described (a standard canonical abbreviation referring to the full description given elsewhere)
- caṃdotaraṇe ujjāṇe = a pleasure garden named Chandravataran (outside the city)
- seybhadde jakhe = the yaksha (protective demigod) named Shvetabhad
- sayāṇīe nāmaṃ rāyā = a king named Shatanick
- miyāvaī devī = Queen Mrigavati
- ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapañciṃdiyasarīre = whose body is perfect and complete in all five senses — nothing lacking, nothing excessive
- jāva juvārāyā = up to and including the position of crown prince (yuvarāja)
- paumāvaī nāmaṃ devī = a queen named Padmavati

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra establishes the historical and geographical setting of the fifth chapter. Kaushambi was one of the most prominent cities of Bhagavan Mahavir's time, capital of the Vatsa kingdom, known for its wealth and refined culture. King Shatanick was a powerful ruler, and his son Udayan would become a celebrated king of the region. The introduction of Queen Padmavati is significant — she will become the unwitting instrument through whom karmic consequences play out. The phrase "perfectly complete in all five senses" (ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapañciṃdiya) is a standard royal description indicating that Udayan was blessed with full physical vitality — a mark of accumulated merit from past lives. The mention of the garden and yaksha shrine locates the scene within the living spiritual geography of the time, where such guardians of place were considered real presences, not merely symbols.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

In the wealthy city of Kaushambi lived a powerful king named Shatanick, his queen Mrigavati, and their son Prince Udayan — perfectly healthy and destined to become a great king. Udayan's wife was the beautiful queen Padmavati.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Every significant person in our lives may carry a karmic connection stretching across many lifetimes. Have you ever felt an unexplained depth to a relationship — as though you had known someone far longer than the years suggest?

## PUROHIT BRUHASPATIDAT (The Royal Priest)

---

### SUTRA 5.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तस्स णं सयाणीयस्स सोमदत्ते णामं पुरोहिए होत्था, रिउव्वेय यजुव्वेय सामवेय अथव्वेयकुसले ।
तस्स णं सोमदत्तस्स पुरोहियस्स वसुदत्ता णामं भारिया होत्था । तस्स णं सोमदत्तस्स पुत्ते वसुदत्ताए अत्तए बहस्सइदत्ते णामं दारए होत्था । अहीणपडिपुण्णपंचिंदियसरीरे जाव सुरूवे ।
॥५.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tassa ṇaṃ sayāṇīyassa somadatte nāmaṃ purohie hotthā, riuvvea yajuvvea sāmavea athavveyakusale |
tassa ṇaṃ somadattassa purohiyassa vasudattā nāmaṃ bhāriyā hotthā | tassa ṇaṃ somadattassa putte vasudattāe attae bahassaidatte nāmaṃ dārae hotthā | ahīṇapaḍipuṇṇapañciṃdiyasarīre jāva surūve |
|| 5.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

King Shatanick had a court priest named Somadat, who was proficient in all four Vedas — the Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, and Atharvaveda. Somadat's wife was named Vasudatta. Somadat's son, born of Vasudatta, was a boy named Bruhaspatidat — whose body was perfectly complete in all five senses, right up to being handsome and well-formed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- somadatte nāmaṃ purohie = a court priest (purohita) named Somadat
- riuvvea yajuvvea sāmavea athavveyakusale = proficient in the Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, and Atharvaveda — the four Vedas (kusala = skillful, well-versed)
- vasudattā nāmaṃ bhāriyā = a wife named Vasudatta (bhāriyā = wife)
- bahassaidatte nāmaṃ dārae = a son (dāraka = boy child) named Bruhaspatidat (the name honors both the father's lineage — Somadat — and the mother's — Vasudatta; Bṛhaspati being the divine teacher of the gods)
- jāva surūve = up to and including being handsome and well-formed (surūpa)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The introduction of Somadat the purohit (royal priest) lays the ground for the chapter's central story. He was deeply learned in all four Vedas — the highest Brahminical qualification of that time. This is not, however, presented as an unambiguous virtue. In the Jain tradition, knowledge of the Vedas alone does not constitute spiritual wisdom. The Vedic ritual system — particularly its use of sacrifice, and as we will see, its most extreme forms — is viewed as a path that binds rather than liberates. The naming of Bruhaspatidat is charged with meaning: Bṛhaspati is the divine teacher of the gods in Vedic tradition — the name carries an aura of priestly grandeur. Yet this child, born perfect in body and into an honored family, will live out the precise karmic consequences of a most terrible previous life. Beautiful births and learned lineages cannot shield a soul from what it has created.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

King Shatanick's chief priest was a learned man named Somadat, whose wife was Vasudatta. Their son — born perfectly healthy and handsome — was named Bruhaspatidat.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

A beautiful body and a learned family provide a comfortable beginning — but do they reveal anything true about the soul that inhabits them? What do you actually know about yourself that is not given by birth?

### SUTRA 5.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणे भगवं महावीरे समोसरिए । तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं भगवं गोयमे तहेव जाव रायमग्गमोगाढे । तहेव पासइ हत्थी, आसे, पुरिसमज्झे पुरिसं । चिंता । तहेव पुच्छइ पुव्वभवं । भगवं वागरेइ ।
॥५.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ māhāvīre samosarie | teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ bhagavaṃ goyame taheva jāva rāyamaggamogāḍhe | taheva pāsai hatthī, āse, purisamajjhe purisaṃ | ciṃtā | taheva pucchhai puvvabhavaṃ | bhagavaṃ vāgarei |
|| 5.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

At that time and in that season, the ascetic Bhagavan Mahavir came to Kaushambi. At that same time, Bhagavan Gautam entered the royal road of the city while seeking alms. There, just as before, he saw elephants, horses, and amidst a group of men, one single man being led away. He was moved to compassion and reflection. Just as before, he asked Bhagavan about the man's previous life. Bhagavan narrated.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- samaṇe bhagavaṃ māhāvīre = Shraman (the great renunciate) Bhagavan Mahavir
- samosarie = arrived, came to stay (samavaśṛta = descended, consecrated the ground with his presence)
- rāyamaggamogāḍhe = entered deep into the royal road (for alms-collection)
- hatthī, āse = elephants, horses (part of an execution or punishment procession)
- purisamajjhe purisaṃ = amidst a group of men, one particular man (a prisoner being led to execution)
- ciṃtā = thought, compassionate reflection
- puvvabhavaṃ = the previous birth (Gautam asked what past life led this man to such suffering)
- bhagavaṃ vāgarei = Bhagavan spoke, narrated

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is the narrative pivot — the moment when the eternal and the temporal intersect. Bhagavan Mahavir's arrival in Kaushambi is not incidental; the Omniscient One arrives precisely when a karmic drama is reaching its visible climax. Gautam Swami, walking the streets for alms, witnesses a man — Bruhaspatidat — being led to execution. The phrase "just as before" (taheva) is deliberate: this chapter follows the same structural pattern as the first chapter, where a similar scene of visible suffering prompted an inquiry into past lives. This repetition is a teaching device. Each time we witness suffering, the question must arise: What past action brought this soul to this moment? And the answer, always, reveals the working of karma across lifetimes. The curiosity Gautam expresses here is not idle — it is the desire to understand, so that all who hear may choose wisely.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bhagavan Mahavir arrived in Kaushambi, and his disciple Gautam — seeing a man being led to his execution — asked Bhagavan: what past actions brought this man to such a terrible end?

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you witness suffering around you, do you ask why? And if the answer were given to you clearly — would it change something about how you live?

## PURVABHAV — PUROHIT MAHESHRADATT (The Previous Life of the Priest)

---

### SUTRA 5.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं इहेव जंबूद्वीवे दीवे भारहे वासे सव्वओभद्धे णामं णयरे होत्था । रिद्धित्थिमयसमिद्धे, वण्णओ । तत्थ णं सव्वओभद्धे णयरे जियसत्तू राया । तस्स णं जियसत्तुस्स रण्णो महेसरदत्ते णामं पुरोहिए होत्था, रिउव्वेय-यजुव्वेय-सामवेय-अथव्वेयकुसले याविं होत्था ।
॥५.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ iheva jaṃbūdvīve dīve bhārahe vāse savvāobhadde nāmaṃ ṇayare hotthā | riddhitthimayasamiddhe, vaṇṇao | tattha ṇaṃ savvāobhadde ṇayare jiyasattū rāyā | tassa ṇaṃ jiyasattūssa raṇṇo mahesaradatte nāmaṃ purohie hotthā, riuvvea-yajuvvea-sāmavea-athavveyakusale yāviṃ hotthā |
|| 5.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus indeed, O Jambu! In that time and in that season, here in this very continent of Jambudvipa, in the Bharatakshetra region, there was a city named Sarvabhad — prosperous, wealthy, and abundant, as described. In that city of Sarvabhad, a king named Jitashatru reigned. His royal priest was named Maheshradatt, who was also proficient in all four Vedas — the Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, and Atharvaveda.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- iheva jaṃbūdvīve dīve = here in this very continent called Jambudvipa
- bhārahe vāse = in the Bharata region (the southernmost region of Jambudvipa, corresponding to India)
- savvāobhadde nāmaṃ ṇayare = a city named Sarvabhad (Sarvaśubha = completely auspicious; the irony of this name is sharp, given what will occur within it)
- jiyasattū rāyā = King Jitashatru (literally: "one who has conquered enemies")
- mahesaradatte nāmaṃ purohie = a court priest named Maheshradatt (bearing the name of the great god Maheshvara — Shiva)
- riuvvea-yajuvvea-sāmavea-athavveyakusale = proficient in all four Vedas

**5. COMMENTARY**

Bhagavan opens the window of past lives. The man Gautam witnessed being led to execution — that suffering soul was, in a prior life, the royal priest Maheshradatt of a city called Sarvabhad. The irony of the city's name is deliberate and sharp: Sarvabhad means "completely auspicious," yet what takes place there will be among the most horrifying acts described in this scripture. Maheshradatt bears the same Vedic credentials as Somadat in the present birth — proficiency in all four Vedas. This parallelism is intentional. Knowledge of scripture does not equal righteousness. A person can be deeply learned and simultaneously commit acts of profound darkness. This is the Jain critique of ritual knowledge divorced from non-violence and genuine compassion: learning without ahimsa is not wisdom, it is a sharper instrument for harm.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bhagavan explains that the suffering man Gautam saw was, in a previous life, a learned court priest named Maheshradatt who served King Jitashatru in a prosperous city called Sarvabhad.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Has there been a moment in your life when you used knowledge, position, or a convincing story to justify something that — in the quiet of your heart — you knew was wrong?

## MAHESHRADATT DVARA GHOR PANCHENDRIY HIMSA (Maheshradatt's Terrible Violence Against Five-Sensed Beings)

---

### SUTRA 5.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से महेसरदत्ते पुरोहिए जितसत्तुस्स रण्णो रज्जबलिविवड्ढणट्ठाए कल्लाकिल्लिं एगमेगं माहणदारयं, एगमेगं खित्तियदारयं एगमेगं वइस्सदारयं, एगमेगं सुद्दारयं गिण्हावेइ, गिण्हावेत्ता तेसिं जीवंतगाणं चेव हिययदंडाए गिण्हावेइ गिण्हावेत्ता जियसत्तुस्स रण्णो संतिहोमं करेइ ।
तए णं से महेसरदत्ते पुरोहिए अट्ठमी-चउद्दसीसु दुवे-दुवे माहणखित्तिय-वइस्स-सुद्दारगे, चउण्हं मासाणं चत्तारि-चत्तारि, छण्हं मासाणं अट्टु-अट्टु, संवच्छरस्स सोलस-सोलस ।
जाहे जाहे वि य णं जियसत्तू राया परबलेण अभिजुंजइ, ताहे ताहे वि य णं से महेसरदत्ते पुरोहिए अट्टुसयं माहणदारगाणं, अट्टुसयं खित्तियदारगाणं, अट्टुसयं वइस्सदारगाणं, अट्टुसयं सुद्दारगाणं पुरिसेहिं गिण्हावेइ, गिण्हावेत्ता जियसत्तुस्स रण्णो संतिहोमं करेइ । तए णं से परबले खिप्पामेव विद्धिसज्जइ वा पडिसेहिज्जइ वा ।
॥५.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se mahesaradatte purohie jitasattūssa raṇṇo rajjabalivīvaḍḍhaṇaṭṭhāe kallākilliṃ egamegaraṃ māhaṇadārayaṃ, egamegaraṃ khittīyadārayaṃ egamegaraṃ vaissadārayaṃ, egamegaraṃ suddārayaṃ giṇhāvei, giṇhāvettā tesiṃ jīvaṃtagāṇaṃ ceva hīyadaṃḍāe giṇhāvei giṇhāvettā jiyasattūssa raṇṇo saṃtīhomaṃ karei |
tae ṇaṃ se mahesaradatte purohie aṭṭhamī-cauddusīsu duve-duve māhaṇakhittiya-vaissa-suddārage, cauṇhaṃ māsāṇaṃ cattāri-cattāri, chaṇhaṃ māsāṇaṃ aṭṭu-aṭṭu, saṃvaccharassa solasa-solasa |
jāhe jāhe vi ya ṇaṃ jiyasattū rāyā parabelaṇa abhijuṃjai, tāhe tāhe vi ya ṇaṃ se mahesaradatte purohie aṭṭusayaṃ māhaṇadāragāṇaṃ, aṭṭusayaṃ khittīyadāragāṇaṃ, aṭṭusayaṃ vaissadāragāṇaṃ, aṭṭusayaṃ suddāragāṇaṃ purisehi giṇhāvei, giṇhāvettā jiyasattūssa raṇṇo saṃtīhomaṃ karei | tae ṇaṃ se parabale khippāmeva viddhisajjai vā paḍisehijjai vā |
|| 5.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That priest Maheshradatt, for the purpose of increasing the kingdom and military power of King Jitashatru, would daily seize one Brahmin child, one Kshatriya child, one Vaishya child, and one Shudra child — and while they were still alive, he would extract their hearts, and perform a fire-ritual (shanti-homa) for King Jitashatru's victory. On the eighth and fourteenth days of the lunar fortnight, he would take two children of each caste; every four months, four children of each; every six months, eight children of each; every year, sixteen of each. And whenever King Jitashatru went to war against an enemy, Maheshradatt would seize 108 Brahmin children, 108 Kshatriya children, 108 Vaishya children, and 108 Shudra children — extract their hearts while they were still alive — and perform a fire-ritual for the king's victory. And by this, the enemy force would either be quickly routed or repelled.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- rajjabalivīvaḍḍhaṇaṭṭhāe = for the purpose of increasing (vivardhaṇa) the kingdom (rajja) and its power/tribute (bali)
- kallākilliṃ = every day (kālyakālīna = daily, from morning to morning)
- egamegaraṃ = one each
- māhaṇadārayaṃ = a Brahmin child (māhaṇa = Brahmin; dāraka = child)
- khittīyadārayaṃ = a Kshatriya child
- vaissadārayaṃ = a Vaishya child
- suddārayaṃ = a Shudra child
- jīvaṃtagāṇaṃ ceva = while still alive (jīvanta = living)
- hīyadaṃḍāe = striking at the heart; tearing out the heart (hṛdaya-daṇḍā)
- saṃtīhomaṃ = a shanti-homa — a fire offering for peace and victory (śānti = peace)
- aṭṭhamī-cauddusīsu = on the eighth and fourteenth days (of the lunar fortnight)
- saṃvaccharassa = yearly (saṃvatsara)
- aṭṭusayaṃ = 108 (aṣṭaśata)
- parabale = enemy force (para-bala)
- viddhisajjai = is quickly routed/crushed
- paḍisehijjai = is repelled/turned back

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra describes what is, without qualification, one of the most disturbing passages in the entire Vipaak Sutra. Maheshradatt was not an ignorant man — he was highly educated, a master of the four Vedas, a holder of the highest ritual office in the kingdom. And yet, in the name of royal power and military victory, he committed organized mass murder of children — from every social class — ripping out their hearts while they were still alive, as a regular, scheduled religious practice. The scripture states this in methodical detail: daily four children, on special lunar days eight, every four months sixteen, every six months thirty-two, every year sixty-four — and at every time of war, 432 at once (108 × 4). The text wants us to feel the magnitude. This is not a description of war or accident. It is cold, deliberate, institutionalized violence performed by a learned man who had convinced himself it was righteous service to his king and his gods. This is perhaps the deepest warning of the Vipaak Sutra: profound knowledge and high position, when divorced from compassion and non-violence, do not prevent evil — they become its most sophisticated instruments.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The priest Maheshradatt did something terrible: every single day he would tear out the hearts of living children — from all four classes of society — as a sacrifice to give his king victory in battle. The number of children he killed grew larger with every festival and every war.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can any "good reason" — for the king, for the religion, for the greater good — ever justify causing suffering to an innocent being? Have you ever told yourself a story that made it acceptable to harm something or someone?

## PAPISHTH MAHESHRADATT PANCHAMI NARAKA MAM (The Wicked Maheshradatt Falls to the Fifth Hell)

---

### SUTRA 5.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से महेसरदत्ते पुरोहिए एयकम्मे एयप्पाहणे एयिवज्जे एयसमायारे सुबहुं पावकम्मं समज्जिणित्ता तीसं वाससयं परमाउयं पालित्ता कालमासे कालं किच्चा पंचमीए पुढवीए उक्कोसेणं सत्तरस सागरोवम टिइए णरगे उवण्णे ।
॥५.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se mahesaradatte purohie eayakamme eayappāhaṇe eayivajje eayasamāyāre subhahuṃ pāvakammaṃ samajjiṇittā tīsaṃ vāsasayaṃ paramāuyaṃ pālittā kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā pañcamīe puḍhavīe ukkoseṇaṃ sattarasa sāgarovama ṭiie ṇarage uvaṇṇe |
|| 5.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That priest Maheshradatt — engaged in this one type of action, this one resolve, this one sin, this one conduct — having accumulated vast quantities of evil karma, and having lived out his full lifespan of three hundred years, died at the appointed hour of death, and was born in the fifth earth (hell) at the highest possible duration of seventeen sagaropam in the hellish realm.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- eayakamme = engaged in this one type of action (ekakarmī = defined by a single repeated act)
- eayappāhaṇe = with this one resolve/primary intent (ekaprādhānya = one predominant purpose)
- eayivajje = with this one sin/transgression
- eayasamāyāre = with this one conduct/way of life
- subhahuṃ pāvakammaṃ = a very great quantity (subahu) of evil karma (pāpakarmā)
- samajjiṇittā = having accumulated, having earned
- tīsaṃ vāsasayaṃ = three hundred years (triṃśat-varṣa-śata)
- paramāuyaṃ pālittā = having lived out the full maximum lifespan
- kālamāse kālaṃ kiccā = having done the act of dying at the time of death
- pañcamīe puḍhavīe = in the fifth earth — the fifth level of hell
- ukkoseṇaṃ = at the highest/maximum duration
- sattarasa sāgarovama = seventeen sagaropam (a sagaropam is an almost incomprehensibly vast measure of time)
- ṭiie ṇarage = duration in the hellish realm

**5. COMMENTARY**

The result of Maheshradatt's lifetime of systematic murder arrives with the mathematical precision of karma. Three hundred years of a seemingly powerful, successful life end in a single moment, and the soul falls to the fifth hell — among the deepest levels — where suffering is most intense and the duration longest. Seventeen sagaropam is a period beyond any human conception of time. In the Jain cosmological understanding, hell is not an external punishment imposed by a judge — it is the natural destination that a soul of this kind of accumulated weight gravitates toward, as inevitably as a stone falls to the earth when released. The phrase "this one action, this one resolve, this one sin, this one conduct" (eayakamme, eayappāhaṇe, eayivajje, eayasamāyāre) is philosophically precise: a soul that dedicates its entire life — every day, every festival, every war — to a single act of destruction creates a karma of concentrated, focused intensity. The soul becomes defined by what it does habitually, with full conscious intent, over a lifetime.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Because Maheshradatt spent his entire three-hundred-year life doing nothing but accumulating terrible karma through these killings, when he died he was reborn in the fifth hell — one of the deepest — where he would suffer for seventeen sagaropam, a duration beyond imagination.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Your habits, repeated over a lifetime, are quietly building the shape of your soul. What is the accumulated direction of your daily choices pointing toward?

## BRUHASPATIDAT NO VARTAMAN BHAV (Bruhaspatidat's Present Life)

---

### SUTRA 5.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

से णं तओ अणंतरं उवट्टित्ता इहेव कोसंबीए णयरीए सोमदत्तस्स पुरोहियस्स वसुदत्ताए भारियाए पुत्ताए उवण्णे । तए णं तस्स दारगस्स अम्मापियरो णिव्वत्तबारसाहस्स इमं एयारूवं णामधेज्जं करेंति–जम्हा णं अम्हं इमे दारए सोमदत्तस्स पुरोहियस्स पुत्ते, वसुदत्ताए अत्तए, तम्हा णं होउ अम्हं दारए बहस्सइदत्ते णामेणं । तए णं से बहस्सइदत्ते दारए पंचधाइपरिग्गहिए जाव परिवड्डुइ । तए णं से बहस्सइदत्ते उम्मुकबालभावे जोव्वणगमणुप्पत्ते विण्णायपरिणयमेत्ते होत्था । से णं उदायणस्स कुमारस्स पियबालवयस्साए याविं होत्था । सहजायाए, सहवड्डियाए, सहपंसुकीलियाए ।
॥५.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

se ṇaṃ tao aṇaṃtaraṃ uvaṭṭittā iheva kosambīe ṇayarīe somadattassa purohiyassa vasudattāe bhāriyāe puttāe uvaṇṇe | tae ṇaṃ tassa dāragassa ammāpiyaro ṇivvattabārasāhassa imaṃ eayārūvaṃ nāmadhejjaṃ karaṃti – jamhā ṇaṃ amhaṃ ime dārae somadattassa purohiyassa putte, vasudattāe attae, tamhā ṇaṃ hōu amhaṃ dārae bahassaidatte nāmeṇaṃ | tae ṇaṃ se bahassaidatte dārae paṃcadhāiparigghie jāva parivaddui | tae ṇaṃ se bahassaidatte ummukabālabhāve jovvaṇagamanuṃpatte viṇṇāyaparīṇayamette hotthā | se ṇaṃ udāyaṇassa kumārassa piyabālavayassāe yāviṃ hotthā | sahajāyāe, sahavaḍḍiyāe, sahapaṃsukīliyāe |
|| 5.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That soul — immediately after its emergence from the fifth hell — was born in the city of Kaushambi as the son of priest Somadat and his wife Vasudatta. On the twelfth day after birth, the parents performed the naming ceremony and gave him this name: "Since this boy is the son of priest Somadat and the son of Vasudatta, let our child bear the name Bruhaspatidat." Then Bruhaspatidat, cared for by five wet nurses, grew and developed. Having left behind childhood and reached youth, he became endowed with mature wisdom and understanding. He was the beloved childhood friend of Prince Udayan — born together, grown together, playing together in the dust.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- tao aṇaṃtaraṃ uvaṭṭittā = immediately after emerging from that (hell) — not after a long interval
- puttāe uvaṇṇe = born as a son
- ṇivvattabārasāhassa = on the completion of the twelfth day after birth (the traditional nāmakaraṇa — naming ceremony)
- nāmadhejjaṃ = the naming (nāmadheyam)
- paṃcadhāiparigghie = cared for/surrounded by five wet nurses (pañca-dhātrī)
- jāva parivaddui = grew, flourished, and developed
- ummukabālabhāve = having shed/left behind childhood (unmukta-bāla-bhāva)
- jovvaṇagamanuṃpatte = having attained the stage of youth (yauvana)
- viṇṇāyaparīṇayamette = having fully matured in knowledge and discrimination
- piyabālavayassāe = a beloved childhood friend (priya-bāla-vayasya)
- sahajāyāe = born together
- sahavaḍḍiyāe = grown together
- sahapaṃsukīliyāe = played together in the dust (the most intimate image of inseparable childhood friendship)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is a masterpiece of karmic poetry and irony. The soul that was Maheshradatt — who spent his entire life ripping out the hearts of living children — now takes birth as a child himself. He emerges from seventeen sagaropam of hellish suffering and is born into a life of beauty, warmth, and privilege. He is given an honored name, raised with every care by five nurses, and grows up as the inseparable best friend of the future king. The tenderness of "born together, grown together, played together in the dust" is deliberately placed here. It is meant to show the full humanity of this soul — and then to show how that humanity is betrayed by its own unresolved tendencies. Karma does not end with suffering; after the hell is exhausted, the soul re-enters the world — and unless the root impulses have been truly transformed, the pattern reasserts itself. The soul carries its inclinations forward, even across the gap of incomprehensible suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

After his time in hell finally ended, the soul of Maheshradatt was reborn in Kaushambi as Bruhaspatidat, the son of priest Somadat and Vasudatta. He grew up to be a smart, handsome young man and the closest childhood friend of Prince Udayan — they were born the same day, raised together, and played together as boys.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

A soul carries its tendencies forward even after enormous consequences. What deep-seated patterns in yourself seem to persist no matter what — and what does that tell you about what truly needs to change?

### SUTRA 5.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से सयाणीए राया अण्णया कयाइ कालधम्मुणा संजुत्ते । तए णं से उदायणे कुमारे बहूहिं राईसर-तलवर-माडंबिय-कोडुंबिय-इब्भ-सेट्टी-सेणावइ सत्थवाहप्पभिइहिं सद्धिं संपरिवुडे रोयमाणे, कंदमाणे, विलवमाणे सयाणीयस्स रण्णो महया इड्डि-सक्कारसमुदएणं णीहरणं करेइ, करेत्ता बहूहिं लोइयाइं मयिकिच्चाइं करेइ । तए णं ते बहवे राईसर जाव सत्थवाहा उदायणं कुमारं महया-महया रायाभिसेएणं अभिसिंचंति । तए णं से उदायणकुमारे राया जाए महया हिमवंत जाव रज्जं पसासेमाणे विहरइ ।
तए णं से बहस्सइदत्ते दारए उदायणस्स रण्णो पुरोहियकम्मं करेमाणे सव्वट्टाणेसु, सव्वभूमियासु, अंतेउरे य दिण्णवियारे जाए याविं होत्था ।
॥५.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se sayāṇīe rāyā aṇṇayā kayāi kāladhammuṇā saṃjutte | tae ṇaṃ se udāyaṇe kumāre bahūhiṃ rāīsara-talavara-māḍaṃbiya-koḍuṃbiya-ibbha-seṭṭī-seṇāvai satthavāhappabhiīhiṃ saddhiṃ saṃparivuḍe royamāṇe, kaṃdamāṇe, vilavamāṇe sayāṇīyassa raṇṇo mahayā iḍḍi-sakkārasamudaeṇaṃ ṇīharaṇaṃ karei, karettā bahūhiṃ loiyāiṃ mayikicchāiṃ karei | tae ṇaṃ te bahave rāīsara jāva satthavāhā udāyaṇaṃ kumāraṃ mahayā-mahayā rāyābhiseeṇaṃ abhisiṃcaṃti | tae ṇaṃ se udāyaṇakumāre rāyā jāe mahayā himavaṃta jāva rajjaṃ pasāsemāṇe viharai |
tae ṇaṃ se bahassaidatte dārae udāyaṇassa raṇṇo purohiyakammaṃ karemāṇe savvaṭṭhāṇesu, savvabhūmiyāsu, aṃteūre ya diṇṇaviyāre jāe yāviṃ hotthā |
|| 5.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then, one day, King Shatanick was united with the law of death. Prince Udayan — surrounded by many royal officers, military chiefs, nobles, householder-leaders, wealthy merchants, generals, and caravan-masters — weeping, wailing, and lamenting, conducted the great funeral rites for King Shatanick with full royal honors. Having done so, he performed all the traditional worldly death-rites. Then those many royal officers and merchants anointed Prince Udayan with a great, magnificent royal coronation. Prince Udayan became king and began ruling his kingdom, great as the Himalaya. After this, Bruhaspatidat took on the duties of royal purohit for King Udayan and was granted free passage everywhere — in all places, all territories, and even in the inner royal chambers.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- kāladhammuṇā saṃjutte = united with the law of time/death (kāla-dharma = the natural law of death; a respectful euphemism)
- rāīsara = royal lords/officers (rājeśvara)
- talavara = sword-bearers, cavalry commanders
- māḍaṃbiya = feudal nobles
- koḍuṃbiya = householder leaders, family heads
- ibbha = wealthy elephant-keepers, distinguished families
- seṭṭī = merchant-bankers
- seṇāvai = generals (senāpati)
- satthavāhā = caravan masters
- iḍḍi-sakkārasamudaeṇaṃ = with a grand gathering of royal pomp and ceremonial honor
- ṇīharaṇaṃ = the funeral procession and rites (nihāraṇa)
- loiyāiṃ mayikicchāiṃ = worldly death-rites (laukika-mṛtaka-kṛtya)
- rāyābhiseeṇaṃ = with a royal coronation (rājābhiṣeka)
- savvaṭṭhāṇesu, savvabhūmiyāsu = in all places, all territories
- aṃteūre = the inner chambers (antaḥpura) — the private residential quarters of the queen
- diṇṇaviyāre = granted free passage and access (datta-vicāra)

**5. COMMENTARY**

With King Shatanick's death, Bruhaspatidat's position is elevated — he becomes the royal priest himself, inheriting his father's office, and his access to the palace becomes entirely unrestricted, including the antahpura — the queen's most private chambers. In the royal and Vedic tradition of that era, the purohit enjoyed extraordinary trust; the king's success was seen as flowing from the priest's blessings and rituals. This total access was a mark of the deepest honor. The phrase "free passage everywhere" (diṇṇaviyāre) is the pivot of the chapter's tragedy. Unrestricted access, given in trust, becomes the instrument of betrayal. The Vipaak Sutra does not condemn the position of priest or the institution of royal service — it shows how unchecked desire turns a privileged position into a path of ruin. The very freedom given in trust becomes the opening through which temptation enters.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

King Shatanick died, and Udayan was crowned the new king in a grand ceremony. Bruhaspatidat became the royal priest and was trusted completely — given free access to every part of the palace, including the private quarters of the queens.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone places complete trust in you, do you use it to serve them — or is there a part of you that sees the openness it creates as an opportunity for yourself?

### SUTRA 5.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तए णं से बहस्सइदत्ते पुरोहिए उदायणस्स रण्णो अंतेउरसि वेलासु य अवेलासु य, काले य अकाले य, राओ य वियाले य पविसमाणे अण्णया कयाइ पउमावईए देवीए सद्धिं सपल्लंगे जाए याविं होत्था । पउमावईए देवीए सद्धिं उरालाइं भोगभोगाइं भुंजमाणे विहरइ ।
॥५.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tae ṇaṃ se bahassaidatte purohie udāyaṇassa raṇṇo aṃteūrasi velāsu ya avelāsu ya, kāle ya akāle ya, rāo ya viyāle ya pavisamāṇe aṇṇayā kayāi paumāvaīe devīe saddhiṃ sapallage jāe yāviṃ hotthā | paumāvaīe devīe saddhiṃ urālāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇe viharai |
|| 5.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then, that priest Bruhaspatidat — entering King Udayan's inner chambers at fitting and unfitting times, at the right hour and the wrong hour, by night and in the darkest hours — one day found himself sharing the same couch as Queen Padmavati. He began indulging in gross sensual pleasures with Queen Padmavati.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- aṃteūrasi = in the inner royal chambers (antaḥpura)
- velāsu ya avelāsu ya = at fitting times and unfitting times
- kāle ya akāle ya = at the right time and the wrong time
- rāo ya viyāle ya = by night and in the later hours of darkness
- sapallage jāe = came to be on the same bed/couch (sa-pallanka = sharing the same seat or bed)
- urālāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ = gross, lavish, unrestrained sensual pleasures (udāra-bhoga = abundant enjoyment)
- bhuṃjamāṇe viharai = indulging in, experiencing, living in this

**5. COMMENTARY**

The scripture states this event without dramatization or moral commentary — the facts speak with devastating plainness. Bruhaspatidat, given unlimited access to the royal palace in absolute trust by his closest friend, used that access to betray him in the most intimate way. The phrase "at fitting times and unfitting times, right hour and wrong hour, night and darkest hours" is significant — it shows this was not a moment of sudden weakness, but a sustained pattern of deliberate deception carried out over time. The word "urālāiṃ" (udāra = lavish, gross, abundant) qualifies the indulgence as utterly unrestrained — total surrender to desire with no inner check. This is the re-emergence of the old pattern: the soul that once extracted the hearts of innocent children with no restraint now indulges in sensory betrayal with no restraint. The vessel has changed; the tendency has not.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bruhaspatidat, who was trusted to come and go freely in the palace, began entering the queen's private chambers at all hours — day and night — and started a secret relationship with Queen Padmavati, King Udayan's own wife.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you are given freedom and trust, what is the inner force that prevents you from misusing them — and is that force truly internal, or does it depend on the fear of being discovered?

### SUTRA 5.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

इमं च णं से उदायणे राया णहाए जाव विभूसिए जेणेव पउमावई देवी तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता बहस्सइदत्तं पुरोहियं पउमावईए देवीए सद्धिं उरालाइं भोगभोगाइं भुंजमाणं पासइ, पासित्ता आसुरुत्ते जाव तिवलियं भिउडिं णिडाले साहट्टु बहस्सइदत्तं पुरोहियं पुरिसेहिं गिण्हावेइ गिण्हावेत्ता अट्टि-मुट्ठि-जाणु-कोप्परपहार-संभग-महियगत्तं करेइ, करेत्ता अवओदय-बंधणं करेइ, करेत्ता एएणं विहाणेणं वज्झं आणवेइ । एवं खलु गोयमा ! बहस्सइदत्ते पुरोहिए पुरा पुराणाणं जाव विहरइ ।

imaṃ ca ṇaṃ se udāyaṇe rāyā ṇahāe jāva vibhūsie jeṇeva paumāvaī devī teṇeva uvāgaccha, uvāgacchittā bahassaidattaṃ purohiyaṃ paumāvaīe devīe saddhiṃ urālāiṃ bhogabhogāiṃ bhuṃjamāṇaṃ pāsai, pāsittā āsurute jāva tivaliyaṃ bhiuḍiṃ ṇiḍāle sāhaṭṭu bahassaidattaṃ purohiyaṃ purisehi giṇhāvei giṇhāvettā aṭṭi-muṭṭhi-jāṇu-kopparapahāra-saṃbhaga-mahiyagattaṃ karei, karettā avaodaya-baṃdhaṇaṃ karei, karettā eaeṇaṃ vihāṇeṇaṃ vajjhaṃ āṇavei | evaṃ khalu goyamā! bahassaidatte purohie purā purāṇāṇaṃ jāva viharai |

Then King Udayan — having bathed and adorned himself — came to exactly where Queen Padmavati was. Coming there, he saw priest Bruhaspatidat indulging in gross sensual pleasures with Queen Padmavati. He then had him beaten with bone-crushing blows of fists, knees, and elbows, his body broken and hurled to the ground. The moment of discovery arrives with the force of a thunderbolt. The image of the three-lined furrow on Udayan's forehead (tivaliyaṃ bhiuḍiṃ) is one of the most vivid expressions in Jain canonical literature for absolute, undisguised rage. What follows — the beating, the breaking of bones, the chaining, the death sentence — is not merely the wrath of a betrayed husband. It is the precise, impartial mechanism of karmic return made visible. Maheshradatt once had the hearts of living children torn from their bodies for "the king's benefit." Now Bruhaspatidat's own body is crushed, his own bones broken, by the king's command. The positions are reversed; the principle is unchanged. The scripture points this out explicitly and without sentiment: "Bruhaspatidat was experiencing the fruit of his own ancient karma." This is the Vipaak Sutra's central affirmation: karma is not a metaphor. It is precise, impartial, and inevitable.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

King Udayan walked in and found Bruhaspatidat with Queen Padmavati. Furious, he ordered his men to seize and beat Bruhaspatidat — breaking his bones — and then had him chained and sentenced to death. Bhagavan tells Gautam: this was the ripening of Bruhaspatidat's own past karma.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you witness someone experiencing a devastating consequence for their actions, how do you respond — with judgment, with pity, or with the sobering recognition that all karma returns, and that your own actions too are building their fruit right now?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"O Bhante! After Bruhaspatidat the priest dies from here, where will he go? Where will he be reborn?"
"O Gautam! Bruhaspatidat the priest, having lived to his full lifespan of sixty-four years — today itself, when one-third of the day still remains, being impaled on the execution stake — will die at the hour of death, and will be reborn among the hell-beings of this Ratnaprabha earth at the highest duration. He will wander through the cycle of existence — as described in the first chapter — through air, fire, water, and earth existences. After that, in the city of Hastinapur, he will be reborn as a deer. Being killed there by hunters, he will be reborn as a son in the family of a wealthy merchant in that same city of Hastinapur. He will attain right understanding, be born in the Saudharma heaven, and in the Mahavideh Kshetra he will attain liberation. The concluding statement is as in the first chapter."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- bhaṃte = O revered one (a term of respectful address to Bhagavan)
- kālagae = having departed at death's time (kālagata = gone at the time)
- causaṭṭhiṃ vāsāiṃ = sixty-four years (caturṣaṣṭi varṣa)
- paramāuyaṃ pālittā = having lived to the full maximum lifespan
- ajjeva = today itself (adyaiva — he will die this very day)
- tibhāgāvase dīvase = when one-third of the day still remains (tribhāgāvaśeṣa-dina)
- sūli-bhiṇṇe = pierced/impaled by the sūli (an execution stake — death by impalement)
- rayaṇappabhāe puḍhavīe = the Ratnaprabha earth — the first hell (literally: "earth of jewel-light")
- ukkosaṃ sāgarovama = the highest possible duration measured in sagaropam
- saṃsāro jahā paḍhame = the wandering of the soul is as described in the first chapter
- vāu teu āu puḍhavīsu = in air (vāyu), fire (tejas), water (āpas), and earth (pṛthvī) existences
- migattāe paccāyāissai = will be reborn as a deer (mṛga)
- vāuriehi vīhae = killed by hunters who set snares (vyādha = hunter)
- seṭṭikulaṃsi puttāe = as a son in the family of a wealthy merchant (śreṣṭhin)
- bohiṃ = bodhi — right understanding, the spiritual awakening that opens the door to liberation
- sohamm kappe = the Saudharma heaven (the first of the Jain heavens)
- mahāvīdaehe vāse = in the Mahavideh Kshetra (the liberated cosmic realm where moksha is always accessible)
- sijjhihii = will attain liberation (siddhi — to become a Siddha, a liberated soul)
- ṇikkheyo jahā paḍhamassa = the concluding statement is as in the first chapter

**5. COMMENTARY**

Bhagavan's answer reveals the full arc of Bruhaspatidat's future soul-journey with the precision of one who sees beyond all veils of time. He will die today — by impalement — at the precise hour when one-third of the day remains. He will enter the first hell at the maximum duration, and then wander through elemental existences — fire, water, air, earth — as described in the first chapter. The mention of rebirth as a deer in Hastinapur carries its own quiet poetry: the priest who once had children killed for "shanti" (peace rituals) will take birth as a hunted animal — knowing firsthand what it is to be prey. But then the path turns. Born as a merchant's son in that same city, he will attain bodhi — the spiritual awakening that changes everything. The arc ends not in annihilation but in siddhi — final, complete liberation — attained through the Mahavideh Kshetra, a realm where liberation is always available. This is the deepest teaching of the Vipaak Sutra: every accumulated karma is finite. Even the most terrible karmic weight, after it is fully exhausted, releases the soul to begin moving toward its own true nature — which is infinite freedom, infinite knowledge, and infinite peace.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Bhagavan tells Gautam: Bruhaspatidat will die today, impaled on a stake. He will then suffer in the first hell for a long time, wander as various lower beings through many cycles, be reborn as a deer in Hastinapur and be killed by hunters, then be born as a merchant's son, have a spiritual awakening, go to the first heaven, and finally reach the Mahavideh Kshetra — where he will attain complete and permanent liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Even after the most terrible consequences, the soul's journey does not end in permanent darkness — it ends in liberation. What does this tell you about the true nature of your own soul, regardless of what you may have done or experienced?

## VIVECHAN (Reflection)

This chapter places before us the devastating consequences of violence rooted in self-justification, and the betrayal of the deepest trust. The story of Bruhaspatidat makes clear that no matter how clever a person is, no matter how honored a position they hold, no matter how convincing the reasoning they construct — sin will always bring forth its fruit. Concealed karma does not decay; it waits.

The deeper pattern this chapter reveals is the soul's repetition. Maheshradatt was a learned priest who spent an entire lifetime harming others without remorse. He endured the fifth hell for an almost incomprehensible duration. And yet, when the soul emerged and was born as Bruhaspatidat — with a beautiful body, a privileged priestly family, the friendship of a king, and complete access to the palace — the same unrestricted impulse returned. The tendency toward self-gratification without regard for others, the indulgence of desire at any cost, the absence of inner restraint — all of this re-emerged as though the aeons of suffering had not occurred. This is the most sobering insight of this chapter: suffering alone does not purify. Pain, even tremendous pain, does not automatically produce wisdom. What transforms a soul is right understanding, self-discipline, and the decision to live in accordance with ahimsa — non-harm — in thought, word, and action.

Yet the chapter does not end in despair. After many wanderings — hells, animal births, elemental existences — the soul attains bodhi. The same soul that once ripped out the hearts of children will, in its own time, find a merchant's body, hear the right teaching, make the right choice, and begin the final journey home to liberation. This is the Jain teaching on the soul: no soul is ever permanently condemned. Every soul contains, at its core, the same infinite potential for knowledge, peace, and freedom. The path may be long — lifetimes, ages, immeasurable time — but the destination is always the same. Liberation awaits every soul that chooses the direction of non-harm, restraint, and wakefulness.
