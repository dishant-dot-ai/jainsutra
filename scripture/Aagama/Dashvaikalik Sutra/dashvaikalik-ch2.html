The Second Adhyayan — the Foundation of Monkhood. Includes the celebrated dialogue of Rajemati and Rathnemi: the noble lady who, at her wedding-eve renunciation, rebukes the wavering Rathnemi and restores him to the path.

Adhyayan 2: Śrāmaṇyapūrvaka

About This Adhyayan

When the Body Wavers, the Vow Holds

The second adhyayan addresses the deepest test of the renunciate's life: the resurgence of desire after the vow has been taken. It teaches that the foundation of monkhood (śrāmaṇya) is the conquest of kāmarāga — sensual longing. The chapter unfolds with metaphors of the steady mind, the suppressed flame, the well-rooted tree.

At its centre is one of the most moving episodes in all Jain narrative: Rajemati, the bride-to-be of Lord Neminath who became a nun on the day he renounced, encounters her brother-in-law Rathnemi who is wavering at the sight of her wet robes. Her four verses of rebuke — invoking the noble Agandhana serpents who refuse to swallow back what they have spat out, the disgrace (dhiratthu) of falling, the lineage of the Yadavas, and the rootless hada — restore Rathnemi instantly. The chapter closes with Acharya Shyambhava naming Rathnemi puruṣottama, the supreme among men.

2.1

The Foundation of Monkhood: Renouncing Desire

कहं नु कुज्जा सामण्णं, जो कामे ण निवारए । पए पए विसीयंतो, संकप्पस्स वसं गओ

How indeed could one practise monkhood, who does not turn away from desires — sinking down at every step, fallen under the sway of his own conflicting plans?

The chapter opens with a question, and the question is rhetorical. Acharya Shyambhava is not really asking how — he is showing that the answer is "he cannot at all." A person who does not subdue desire cannot maintain monkhood. The two are mutually exclusive. Desire and monkhood are like light and darkness: where one is, the other is not. They cannot coexist in the same room. To understand the verse one has to understand what kama means in this context. The word is often narrowly translated as "lust," but the Jain tradition gives it a wider meaning. Kama is of two kinds. Dravya-kama is the outer object — the beautiful body, the laughter and grace, the fine bedding, the costly ornaments, the sweet food — anything that excites the senses. Bhava-kama is the inner movement — the wanting itself, which has two sub-types: iccha-kama (the wish to have things one does not have) and madana-kama (the sexual longing for bodily union). The verse refers to all of these together. The monk who does not turn from any of them — who keeps even one door of desire open — has not yet become a monk in any meaningful sense. The image in the second half of the verse is striking. Pae pae visīyanto — "sinking at every step." Imagine a person crossing a marsh. He plants his foot and the foot sinks. He pulls it out and plants the other, and that one sinks. Every single step is a struggle and a small drowning. This is the inner experience of a monk who has not subdued desire. Every encounter with a sense-object — a sight, a sound, a smell, a memory — pulls him under, and he must claw his way back up only to be pulled under again at the next encounter. There is no firm ground underfoot. The whole journey becomes one slow exhausting series of sinkings. The cause is identified as sankalpassa vasam gao — "fallen under the sway of conflicting plans." Desire never produces a single clear intention. It produces a swarm. Should I take this? Should I leave it? What if I just have a little? What if no one is watching? But what if it leads to more? Maybe I should — no, I should not. This endless sankalpa-vikalpa, this flicker of competing intentions, is the signature of a mind ruled by desire. The mind cannot rest because the desires never agree among themselves; one desire wants one thing, another wants its opposite, and the practitioner is caught in the cross-current. The text behind this verse offers a piercing illustration. There was an old monk in the Konkan region who had renounced together with his son. The son loved the old monk dearly and could not bear to see him uncomfortable. One day the son said, "Guruji, your bare feet must be hurting on these stones — let me get you sandals." The old monk, who had gradually begun to soften, accepted. Some days later: "The cold is harming your feet — let me get you socks." The old monk accepted. Then: "The heat of the day must be hurting your bald head — let me bring a cloth to cover it." Accepted. Then bedding too soft to lie on the bare ground, then food brought to him because alms were too tiring, then a razor and a bath because shaving and ascetic dirt were unpleasant. One day the son finally said, "Guruji, I cannot bear it any more — I cannot live without a wife." The old monk, who had quietly nodded yes to every previous request, looked at his son and understood that this had been coming all along. Each small concession to desire had not pacified desire but fed it. The son was no longer fit to be a monk. The lesson is exact: desire does not diminish by being satisfied; it grows by being satisfied. The only way to "turn away" from desire is to refuse it at the very first step, before it gets the chance to make a second request. This is what Acharya Shyambhava means when he asks, with rhetorical force, kahaṃ nu kujjā sāmaṇṇaṃ: how indeed will the monk practise monkhood, if he allows even the first request of desire to be granted? The verse therefore stands at the head of the chapter as both a warning and a definition. The very prerequisite of monkhood is the willingness to say no to desire — completely, immediately, every time — because desire compounds and the monk who tolerates a little will soon be ruled by a lot.

Simply Put: You cannot really live the life of a monk if you have not first learned to say no to your own wants — because every time you give in, even a little, the wanting grows stronger, and pretty soon you will not be able to take a single step without it pulling you down.

2.2

Outward Renunciation Is Not Real Renunciation

वत्थगंधमलंकारं, इत्थीओ सयणाणि य । अच्छंदा जे ण भुंजंति, ण से चाइ त्ति वुच्चइ

Those who, only because they have no choice in the matter, do not enjoy clothing, perfumes, ornaments, women, and beds — such a person is not called a renunciant.

This verse and the next form a pair. Together they draw the line — sharply and unforgivingly — between two kinds of people who from the outside look identical but inwardly are opposites: the person who does not enjoy because he cannot, and the person who does not enjoy because he chooses not to. Only the second deserves the title of renunciant. The five things listed — clothing, perfumes, ornaments, women, beds — are a deliberately complete catalogue of the sense-pleasures of the body. Fine clothing pleases the sense of touch and the eye; perfumes please the nose; ornaments please the eye and the touch; the company of women (or, by extension, of any object of sexual longing) provides the deepest sense-pleasure of all; soft beds please the body's longing for ease. To turn away from all five is the formal outer mark of renunciation. But the verse insists: the outer turning-away is not what counts. The decisive word is acchandā — "without choice." It points to a person who would gladly enjoy these things if only he could but cannot, for one reason or another. He may be poor and unable to afford fine clothing; he may be sick and unable to wear perfume; he may be old and unable to enjoy a soft bed; he may have lost the chance for marriage and so live alone; he may have been disfigured or disabled and so finds the company of others unavailable. Such a person, the verse declares, is not a renunciant — even though, externally, his life looks identical to a renunciant's. He has no fine clothes, no perfume, no spouse, no soft bed. But the absence is imposed, not chosen. Inside he still wants. He has merely been forbidden by circumstance. The renouncing of desire is what constitutes renunciation; the absence of objects does not. The text behind this verse offers a sharp story to make the point. In the Mauryan court, when Chandragupta had taken the throne from the Nanda king, the wise minister Chanakya saw that the new king's loyal attendant Subandhu had become hostile and was plotting against him. Chanakya, knowing that he himself was the obstacle, withdrew from the court, gave away all his possessions, went into the forest, and undertook the fast unto death — the supreme act of Jain renunciation. He was, by every external mark, a renouncer. But before doing so, he had set a trap: he had hidden a sealed letter inside layers of perfumed paper, inside a box, inside other boxes, inside a special chamber, with instructions that anyone who opened it and inhaled the fragrance and then bathed, ate well, lay on a soft bed, and indulged in sense-pleasures would die. Subandhu, finding the letter, did exactly what was forbidden — and died. The story's point is brutal: even Chanakya, in his last act, was not free of the will to harm. His outer renunciation was complete; his inner renunciation was not. The verse is asking the monk: which kind are you? A second story makes the point from the other direction. A poor labourer in Rajagriha had taken initiation under the great teacher Sudharma Swami. As the monk went on his alms round, the people of his old neighbourhood mocked him: "He renounced because he couldn't carry a labourer's load, not because of religion." The monks brought this to Abhaya Kumar, the wise prince. Abhaya devised a test. He set up three mounds of priceless jewels outside the city — three crore worth in each — and announced that anyone willing to renounce, for life, three things (uncooked water, fire, and the company of women) could take them all. Not a single citizen accepted. Abhaya then said: "See? The labourer who already renounced these three things has done something that all the wealth of three crore jewels cannot buy from any of you. His renunciation, even if circumstance played a part, has reached a depth that you cannot reach for any price." The teaching is the inverse of the first story. A person who outwardly looks like a half-renunciant may inwardly be more a renunciant than the wealthy person who would never give it up. Read together, the two stories show what the verse is really asking. The question is not "do you have these things or not." The question is "if you could have them, would you?" If the answer is yes — if the only thing keeping you from sense-pleasure is circumstance — then no matter how renounced you look, you are not a renunciant. Renunciation is an inner state, not an outer condition.

Simply Put: If you do not enjoy fine clothes, perfumes, jewellery, romance, and a comfortable bed only because you cannot get hold of them — that does not make you a renunciant. Real renunciation is when you could have all of it and still walk away.

2.3

The True Renunciant Walks Away from What He Could Have

जे य कंते पिए भोए, लद्धे वि पिट्ठि कुव्वइ । साहीणे चयइ भोए, से हु चाइ त्ति वुच्चइ

The one who, even when beautiful and beloved enjoyments lie within reach and are his own to take, turns his back on them and renounces those enjoyments that are entirely under his command — he, truly, is called a renunciant.

This is the matching verse to the previous one. The previous verse defined who is not a renunciant; this verse defines who is. Side by side they form a small definition of unmistakable clarity. The two key words are kante (pleasing) and pie (beloved). The text behind the verse takes care to distinguish them. Kante refers to the inherent loveliness of an object — its natural beauty as it stands. A flower is kante; a piece of fine art is kante; a healthy young body is kante. Pie refers to the personal preciousness an object holds for a particular person. The same flower may be kante to everyone but pie only to the one who received it as a gift from a loved one. Pie, in other words, includes the personal history that has knit itself around the object. Four combinations follow: some things are kante but not pie (lovely but personally unimportant); some are pie but not kante (personally precious but not objectively lovely, like a worn old letter); some are both; some are neither. The verse uses both words to make the renunciation total: the renunciant turns away from what is objectively beautiful and from what is personally precious — both at once. The crucial qualifier is sāhīṇe — "under one's own command." This is the verse's whole point. The renunciation that counts is the renunciation of what was actually within reach. To give up what one never had is no renunciation at all. To give up what one cannot have is no renunciation at all. Only to give up what one could right now enjoy, freely and without obstacle — that is renunciation. The previous verse set the lower bound; this verse sets the upper. The text behind the verse pauses on the word piṭṭhi kuvvai — "turns the back upon" — and gives it two meanings. The first is the literal one: physically, the renunciant turns his back, walks the other way, refuses the encounter. The second is deeper: he turns his back inwardly. Even as the object remains in his sight, his inner state turns away from it; he sees it but is no longer drawn by it. He has understood, by direct seeing, that the object's pleasure is short-lived and produces longer suffering. With that understanding the inner attraction simply ceases. He has become vimukha — averted — not by force of will but by clarity of seeing. There is a particular emphasis in this verse on the word cayai — "renounces" — which carries the sense of letting drop, releasing. The renunciation here is not a struggle against a still-burning desire; it is the cool, willing release of something that one has truly seen through. The picture is of a person holding a hot coal who finally understands it is burning him and lets it fall. He does not fight to drop it; he simply lets it go. This is the texture of true renunciation. It is light, not laboured. The forced renunciation that grits its teeth and white-knuckles its way through abstinence is still a kind of bondage. The true renunciation has the quality of relief. The two verses together — 2.2 and 2.3 — form one of the sharpest definitions of renunciation in all of Indian religious literature. The contrast cannot be missed. On one side: the person who has nothing but who would gladly have it if he could. On the other side: the person who has everything within reach and who lets it go. The first is not a renunciant though he looks like one. The second is a renunciant though sometimes he may not look like one at all. This second verse closes the opening movement of the chapter. The principle has been stated: monkhood requires the renunciation of desire (verse 1), and that renunciation must be the inner turning-away from what one could enjoy (verses 2–3), not merely the outer absence of objects one cannot obtain. With this established, the chapter now turns to the question: what does a monk do when desire, despite his sincere renunciation, suddenly returns?

Simply Put: The real renunciant is the person who has all the beautiful and precious things he could ever want sitting right within his reach, and who quietly turns away and walks past them — not because he could not have them, but because he has truly seen through them.

2.4

When the Mind Drifts: The Anyatva Contemplation

समाइ पेहाइ परिव्वयंतो, सिया मणो णिस्सरई बहिद्धा । ण सा महं णो वि अहं पि तीसे, इच्चेव ताओ विणएज्ज रागं

While the monk wanders forth, watching with right awareness and equanimity, his mind may yet on occasion slip outward beyond the boundaries of restraint and chastity. Thinking, "She is not mine, nor am I hers" — by just this contemplation he should remove his attraction toward her.

The chapter has so far set the principle: a monk must renounce desire totally. But Acharya Shyambhava is a realist, and he knows what the disciple needs to hear next. What if, despite the sincere vow, despite years of discipline, despite the alms-round and the meditation and the scripture-study — desire suddenly returns? This verse is the answer. It does not pretend that this never happens. It accepts that it sometimes does, and then it gives the practitioner a precise tool for handling it. The opening phrase paints the picture. Samāi pehāi parivvayanto — "wandering forth with right awareness and equanimity." This is the monk doing everything right. He is alert, he is balanced, he is on his alms-round or his journey, his discipline is intact. And yet — siyā maṇo ṇissarai bahiddhā — "sometimes the mind slips outward beyond the boundary." A glance, a memory, a chance encounter, and suddenly the mind is no longer where it was. The seed of desire is, the text observes, very subtle. It may seem completely uprooted for many years and then, in the presence of the right trigger, sprout again as if it had been waiting patiently all along. The text behind the verse offers a beautiful image. A maidservant came past a school carrying a clay pot of water on her head. A schoolboy, restless, threw a small pebble that struck the pot and pierced a tiny hole. The water began to leak. The maidservant immediately took a piece of wet clay and pressed it over the hole, sealing it. She did not go back to scold the boy or weep over the pierced pot — she simply sealed the leak before the water all ran out. The Acharya says: this is exactly what a monk must do. When the mind, the inner pot of character, is suddenly pierced by a small pebble of desire and a leak begins to form, the monk should not drown in shame or attempt to argue his way back to peace. He should immediately apply the right contemplation, like wet clay, and seal the leak before the inner water of restraint runs out. The contemplation prescribed in this verse is one of the twelve great Jain contemplations: anyatva-bhavana, the contemplation of separateness. The formula is precise: ṇa sā mahaṃ ṇo vi ahaṃ pi tīse — "she is not mine, nor am I hers." Said about a particular person who has just stirred desire, the formula has the form of a meditative whisper. But its meaning is not a denial of the person's existence; it is an honest recognition of the separateness of two souls. The other person is a separate soul with her own karma, her own journey, her own destination; she is not in any real sense "mine." And I, as a soul, am not in any real sense "hers." The temporary feeling of belonging that desire produces is precisely that — temporary, produced by a passing arrangement of bodies and minds. The two souls have their own separate trajectories. The text behind the verse offers another small story to show this contemplation at work. A merchant's son had taken renunciation but could not stop missing his former wife. He took up the practice of repeating "she is not mine, I am not hers" — but it remained mere words. One day, troubled by the longing, he wandered into the village where she still lived, hoping to see her. She did not recognise him in his monk's robes. He stayed near her house and asked the neighbours, "is the wife of so-and-so still alive?" — secretly hoping that if she were dead, he might give up his vows in peace. The neighbours, sensing the truth, told him: "Yes, she is alive — but she has gone off with another man." Hearing this, his attachment instantly dissolved. He thought: "The very person I was longing for, the one I imagined was somehow still mine — she has already moved on with someone else without a thought of me. So she truly is not mine, and I am truly not hers. The contemplation I had been mouthing is exactly correct. To love anyone in this way is futile." The mantra, which had been lifeless on his lips, became living truth in his heart, and he returned to his vows steady. The verse therefore prescribes a remarkably practical method. When attraction stirs, do not panic, do not argue, do not try to push the desire away by force — simply contemplate honestly: I do not belong to her; she does not belong to me. She is a separate soul, and so am I. The bond I am imagining is not real; it is only desire's projection. This contemplation, repeated quietly and seriously, dissolves the projection. The other person remains, the world remains, the body remains — but the false sense of "mine and hers" simply lifts, and with it, the desire. The verse uses the word viṇaejja — "should remove" — in a particular way. It does not say "should suppress" or "should hide" or "should pretend not to feel." It says "should remove" — should genuinely take away. The Jain method is not the suppression of desire but the dissolving of the misperception that produces desire. When the misperception (this is mine, I am hers) is gone, the desire is gone with it. There is no left-over to suppress.

Simply Put: Even a careful monk's mind sometimes slips and is suddenly pulled toward someone — and the way to handle it is not to panic or fight, but to quietly think the truth: she is not mine, and I am not hers; we are two separate lives. That honest seeing is enough to lift the pull.

2.5

Six Methods for Subduing the Senses

आयावयाही चय सोगमल्लं, कामे कमाही कमियं खु दुक्खं । छिंदाहि दोसं विणएज्ज रागं, एवं सुही होहिसि संपराए

Practise the heat of austerity; abandon delicacy of body; cross over desires — for once desires are crossed, suffering itself is surely crossed. Cut off aversion; remove attraction. By doing so you will be happy in this world and the next.

The previous verse gave one method — the anyatva contemplation — for the moment when the mind suddenly slips. This verse expands the kit. It gives six related methods, all addressed to the same problem: how to keep monkhood unshaken when desire returns. Read together, the two verses form a complete protocol for handling the inner enemy. The first method is āyāvayāhī — the practice of ātāpana, the deliberate exposure of the body to heat (and, by extension, to cold). In summer the monk sits in the open sun; in winter he removes upper garments and bears the cold. This is not asceticism for its own sake. It has a specific function: when the body has been pampered with comfort, the senses grow plump and demanding. By voluntarily exposing the body to discomfort, the monk reduces the body's capacity to entertain desire. A body that is steadily wrestling with heat or cold has little energy left for the imaginings of pleasure. The second method is caya sogamallaṃ — "abandon delicacy." This is the inner counterpart of the first. Saukumārya is the love of softness — soft beds, soft cloths, soft food, soft conditions in general. The text behind the verse warns that a person addicted to softness, who hates inconvenience and demands ease, will be utterly defenceless when desire stirs, because his whole organism has been trained to follow the easier path. The monk who has trained himself to welcome difficulty will, when desire knocks, find it natural to refuse the easy yes. The third method is kāme kamāhī — "cross over desires." The verb kamāhī (kram-) means literally "to step beyond." The image is of a river: the desires are the river, and the monk steps across to the further bank. The clause that follows — kamiyaṃ khu dukkhaṃ — declares the result: once desires are crossed, suffering is crossed. The two are inseparable. Every suffering, the verse implies, is rooted in some desire — the desire to have something one does not, or the desire to be rid of something one has. Remove the desire and the suffering rooted in it cannot remain. This is one of the most condensed statements of the entire Jain (and indeed Indian) understanding of suffering: desire is the root, and uprooting the root removes the tree. The fourth method is chiṃdāhi dosaṃ — "cut off aversion." Here a subtle point is made. The Jain tradition treats dvesha (aversion) and raga (attraction) as the twin roots of all karmic bondage. They look like opposites — one pushes away, one pulls toward — but in fact they are mirror images of the same disease, namely the inability to remain equanimous in the presence of any object. Dvesha has two faces: dislike for the path of restraint itself (aversion to the discipline of monkhood, finding it tiresome or distasteful) and dislike for unwanted experiences (irritation, anger, distaste at things one would rather not encounter). The monk must cut off both. The word chiṃdāhi — "cut" — is sharp; aversion is to be severed cleanly, not merely managed. The fifth method is viṇaejja rāgaṃ — "remove attraction." This is the mirror practice. Raga too has two faces: pleasure in unrestraint (the pull back toward the indulgent life one renounced) and pleasure in beloved objects (the fondness for particular things or people). The verse uses a softer verb here than for aversion — viṇaejja (subdue, dispel) rather than chiṃdāhi (cut). The reason, the text suggests, is that raga tends to be more subtle and pervasive than dvesha; it does not always present itself as a clear blow that can be cut, but as a soft warmth that must be gradually dispelled. The sixth method is the result: evaṃ suhī hohisi saṃparāe — "thus you will be happy in samparaya." The word samparaya has four meanings, all of which the text behind the verse unfolds. (1) Samsara — the cycle of birth and death; you will be happy even within the wheel of rebirth, because by uprooting desire you have already destroyed suffering's seed. (2) Paraloka — the next world; in your future births you will land in pleasant states (heavens or noble human births) until liberation comes. (3) Future time — in any time to come, the practice's fruit will follow. (4) Battle — in the battle of life, especially the battle against the inner enemies of passion, you will emerge victorious. All four senses apply. Through these six practices, happiness becomes the practitioner's natural state in every dimension of his existence. The verse therefore lays out a complete inner regimen: harden the body so the senses cannot run rampant (austerity, abandonment of delicacy); transcend desire itself (cross it); and in the daily fluctuation of mind, cut off the aversive movements and dispel the attractive ones (cut aversion, remove attraction). Practise these six together, the verse says, and happiness in every world is yours.

Simply Put: Make your body strong by enduring heat and cold; stop demanding comfort; step right over your wants — and once you do, your suffering crosses over with them. Cut off the dislikes that pull you one way and the likes that pull you the other, and you will be happy now, later, and forever.

2.6

The Snake Will Not Drink Its Vomited Poison

पक्खंदे जलियं जोई, धूमकेउं दुरासयं । णेच्छंति वंतयं भोत्तुं, कुले जाया अगंधणे

The serpents born in the Agandhana family enter into the blazing fire — into the unbearable, smoke-bannered flame — but do not wish to swallow back the poison they have once vomited out.

This verse is the first of six that contain the most famous narrative of the Dashvaikalik Sutra: Sadhvi Rajemati's rebuke of Muni Rathnemi. To understand the verse, the story behind it must be told. In the city of Dwarka in the Sorath region — twelve yojanas long and nine wide — the ninth Vasudeva, Sri Krishna Maharaja, ruled. His father's elder brother Samudra Vijaya had a queen named Shiva, from whose womb was born Aristanemi, the twenty-second Tirthankara. When Aristanemi reached youth, Krishna Vasudeva arranged his marriage to Princess Rajemati, daughter of King Ugrasena. The wedding day arrived. The chariot of Aristanemi was passing through the streets of Junagadh when the prince heard the cries of countless animals penned in enclosures — beasts to be slaughtered for the wedding feast. He asked the charioteer why they were bound. The charioteer answered, "For the meat-eating guests of your wedding, prince." Hearing this, Aristanemi's heart filled with detached compassion. He realised: it is not right that for my marriage so many lives must be killed. He turned the chariot around, freed the animals, returned home, gave away gold for a year, and took initiation along with a thousand others. Princess Rajemati, learning that her bridegroom had renounced and that she would never be a bride, herself entered the path of renunciation. She accepted initiation along with seven hundred companion sadhvis and became, in time, a deeply wise and accomplished nun. One day, Sadhvi Rajemati and her group were going for darshan of Bhagwan Aristanemi atop Mount Raivatagiri (Girnar). On the way a sudden storm with high winds broke. Rajemati was separated from the group. Soaked through, she sought shelter and entered a cave. Finding the place deserted, she removed her wet upper garments and laid them on the ground to dry. What she did not know was that Muni Rathnemi — Aristanemi's younger brother, who had also taken initiation — was already meditating in the dark cave. In a flash of lightning, the radiant body of the unclothed sadhvi became visible to Rathnemi. Whose mind, in that instant, slipped outward. Old desire — long buried under years of monastic discipline — sprang back to life. He approached the sadhvi and proposed that they leave the order and live together in worldly life. Rajemati at that moment, instead of fleeing or weeping, did three things together: she covered herself with care, she became inwardly steady in her discipline, and she struck Rathnemi with words sharper than the keenest sword. Verses 6 through 9 contain those words. This sixth verse — about the serpent — is her opening blow. There are, the texts on serpentology recognise, two great families of serpents. The Gandhana clan — when bitten, the serpent's poison can be drawn back into its own body by mantras and antidotes if the serpent is willing to suck it back. The Agandhana clan — these noble serpents will refuse. Once they have bitten and the poison has left their fangs, they will sooner die than draw it back. Even if a roaring fire is built, even if they are commanded by the sorcerer to enter the fire and burn alive, even into the unbearable smoke-bannered flame they will leap — but they will not, will not, swallow back what they have once vomited out. The vomited poison is, for them, beneath their dignity to consume. Rajemati uses this image as a mirror. She is saying to Rathnemi, without yet saying his name: there are two kinds of beings in this world. There is the lower kind, who can be persuaded to take back what they have once renounced. And there is the higher kind, the noble kind, who would sooner die than reclaim what they have once cast out. To which kind do you belong, Rathnemi? The image is brilliantly chosen. It does not insult Rathnemi directly. It puts him to a question he must answer himself. It also draws on a piece of natural lore that any listener would recognise as worthy of admiration — the noble serpent who would rather burn than disgrace itself by taking back its poison. Even an animal, the image implies, has this much honour. And you, Rathnemi, born in the noblest of human families, having taken the noblest of vows — will you have less? The verse makes a clear point about renunciation that completes what the chapter has been building. To take back what one has renounced is to declare that the renunciation was a lie. It is to say to the world and to oneself, "I gave it up, but I did not really mean it; I would still take it if I could." This is the deepest possible self-betrayal. It dishonours not just the present moment but every previous moment of the practice. The Agandhana serpent's choice — death over reclamation — is the only choice consistent with honour. And by extension, it is the only choice consistent with true renunciation.

Simply Put: There is a noble kind of snake that, once it has spit out its poison, will rather walk into a roaring fire and die than take that poison back into its own body — and the question being asked is: are you that kind of being, or the lower kind?

2.7

Shame on You for Wanting What You Renounced

धिरत्थु तेऽजसोकामी, जो तं जीवियकारणा । वंतं इच्छसि आवेउं, सेयं ते मरणं भवे

Shame upon you, O lover of disgrace! You who, for the mere sake of this restraint-less life, wish to drink back the very poison you have already vomited out — better for you to die than to do such a thing.

The serpent image of the previous verse was indirect; it laid out a comparison and let the listener draw the inference. This verse is direct. Rajemati now turns the comparison upon Rathnemi himself, by name in spirit if not by syllable, and pronounces the verdict. The opening word, dhiratthu — "shame, fie, disgust" — is one of the strongest expressions in the language. It is not merely disapproval; it is the kind of word a noble person uses when faced with something so contrary to honour that ordinary words will not do. Rajemati uses it not to wound Rathnemi but to wake him. A monk fallen into desire will not be moved by gentle words; gentle words will only let him slide further. Only a sharp blow to his sense of self-respect can stop him in his tracks. Rajemati administers that blow. The address — ajasokāmī — is bitterly ironic. The text behind the verse explains that the word can be parsed three ways. (1) Apayasha-kāmī — "lover of infamy"; you are seeking exactly the disgrace you most fear. (2) Ayaśas-kāmin — taking yasha to mean samyama (restraint) and ayasha its opposite, "lover of unrestraint"; you are now wanting precisely the unrestraint that you renounced. (3) Yashas-kāmin — "lover of fame"; the verse is then ironic in another way: you wanted fame and yet now act in a way that destroys all your fame. All three readings sting because all three are true. Rathnemi, who at his initiation desired the spiritual fame of monkhood, is now desiring the worldly indulgence of unrestraint. He cannot have both. Wanting both makes him a thing fit only for shame. The phrase jīviyakāraṇā — "for the sake of life" — carries its own bite. It means: for the sake of mere physical survival in the worldly sense, for the sake of an embodied life with sense-pleasure in it. Rajemati is saying: this is what you are willing to throw away your monkhood for? Mere survival? A few more years of sensation in this short life? You are trading the eternal goal of liberation for a handful of brief sensations. The image of the previous verse is now made absolutely literal. The monk's sense-pleasures, having been vomited out at the moment of initiation, are vantam — vomited matter. To desire to take them back is to desire to consume one's own vomit. There is no softer way to say this. The image is intentionally repulsive because the action it describes is, spiritually speaking, exactly that repulsive. A person who would not, under any circumstance, eat his own physical vomit — should not, under any circumstance, take back the inner vomit of renounced desires. The closing line is the most striking of all: seyaṃ te maraṇaṃ bhave — "better for you to die." This is not a curse. It is a precise spiritual judgement. The Jain texts recognise three principles that the verse's commentary draws out. (1) Breaking a great vow inflicts spiritual damage greater than physical death; the soul falls back into bondage that may take many lives to unwind. To die while keeping the vow is therefore better than to live while breaking it. (2) Even if a person should suffer greatly while keeping his vow, the suffering is not blameworthy; what is blameworthy is the breaking. (3) A person who chooses death over the breaking of character is not a suicide but a vow-protector, and is honoured by the tradition. By all three standards, Rathnemi's death would be a better outcome than his contemplated indulgence. The harshness of Rajemati's words must be understood in context. She is not unkind. She is, in fact, doing the kindest thing possible — she is administering exactly the medicine that Rathnemi needs in order to live. A gentler word would have let him fall further; a softer rebuke would have given him room to negotiate. By drawing the line so absolutely — by saying, in effect, "die rather than do this" — she gives him no room to manoeuvre. She forces him to choose between his shame and his recovery, and by making the shame so undeniable, she forces the recovery. The narrative records that Rathnemi, hearing these words, did exactly that — he chose recovery. The blow landed. The pride of his lineage, the truth of his vow, the disgust at his own intended action, all rose at once in his mind. He returned to his vows that very day, and through continued practice eventually reached the state of liberation. The medicine worked because the dose was large enough. This verse therefore sits at the heart of the chapter as a teaching about how to help a fallen practitioner. The teaching is uncomfortable but precise: when desire has overtaken a sincere monk, half-measures do not help. Only the sharpest possible naming of what is being attempted — only the most direct shaming of the action — can recall the practitioner to himself. Rajemati's words have echoed through Jain tradition for two and a half thousand years because they show what right speech looks like when right speech is hard.

Simply Put: She tells him directly: "Shame on you — you would actually trade your whole spiritual life just to swallow back what you already spit out? It would be better for you to die than to do such a thing."

2.8

Born of a Noble Lineage, Be Like the Noble Serpent

अहं च भोगरायस्स, तं चऽसि अंधगवण्हिणो । मा कुले गंधणा होमो, संजमं णिहुओ चर

I am the daughter of King Ugrasena, the Bhoja-king; you are the son of King Samudra Vijaya, of the Andhakavrishni clan. Let us not become like serpents of the gandhana lineage — born into noble families. Stand firm in your mind, and walk the path of restraint.

After two verses of sharp rebuke, the third verse softens — but only into a different form of pressure. Rajemati now appeals to Rathnemi's noble lineage. The technique is exactly right. Having struck him with shame in the previous verse, she now offers him a way back to dignity by reminding him of what he is. She names the houses. She is the daughter of King Ugrasena, the bhoga-raja — the great Bhoja sovereign. He is the son of King Samudra Vijaya, of the noble Andhaka-Vrishni lineage — the same Yadava clan that produced Krishna and his brothers. Both of them come from the highest possible birth. Both of them carry the honour of those lines. The unspoken implication is: whatever ordinary persons of obscure birth might be expected to do under the pressure of desire, you and I cannot do. We carry too much. The Jain tradition does not normally make much of caste or birth in the worldly sense; in matters of spiritual realisation, all souls are equal. But in this passage Rajemati is using nobility of birth in a particular and skillful way — not as a claim of inherent superiority, but as a reminder of inherited expectation. A person born into a noble line has been raised with certain standards, has been told from childhood that certain actions are beneath him, has internalised a certain self-image. To act against that internalised self-image produces a deep psychological disruption. Rajemati uses this knowingly. She is not flattering Rathnemi; she is reminding him that his own self-respect, formed by the family that raised him, will not let him do what he is now contemplating. The line mā kule gandhaṇā homo — "let us not become gandhana ones in our family" — is exact in its construction. The earlier verse had set up two kinds of serpents: the gandhana, who can be made to take back their vomit, and the agandhana, who would sooner die. Rajemati now says: in our noble human families, let us not become the gandhana type. The two highest human houses of the time should produce only agandhana-style beings — beings whose word, once given, cannot be unsaid. To break our vows would be to lower ourselves below even the noble serpent. It would be a double disgrace: a disgrace to our renunciation, and a disgrace to the families that produced us. Notice that Rajemati uses the plural — homo, "let us not become." She does not stand outside the situation as an accuser; she places herself alongside Rathnemi inside the question. This is significant. She is not saying, "I am pure and you are fallen, therefore I rebuke you." She is saying, "We two are in this together, and the question is whether either of us will dishonour the families and the vows we share. Let us both refuse to become that lower kind." The shared "us" is a hand offered. It says: I have not given up on you; I am still standing alongside you; and together we will rise above this moment. The closing command is precise: saṃjamaṃ ṇihuo cara — "walk the path of restraint with a steady mind." The word ṇihuo (nibhṛta) means inwardly motionless, uncomplicated by inner fluctuation. The image is of a flame in a windless place — straight up, unflickering. Rajemati is telling Rathnemi: do not just return to monkhood with a still-wavering mind; return to it with a mind that has become inwardly motionless. The previous flickering — toward her, away from her, toward desire, away from it — must end. From this moment on, walk steady. The text behind the verse adds an observation about why this appeal worked. Persons born of noble lineage, the commentator notes, are generally unwilling to commit unworthy acts; the trained sense of inherited honour serves as an inner brake. Rathnemi was such a person. The reminder of his lineage activated exactly that brake. He recovered his self-image, recovered his sense of who he was supposed to be, and used that recovered self-image to step back into his vows. This verse therefore demonstrates a deep principle in spiritual recovery: shame alone does not heal. Shame opens the wound, but pride in something higher must close it. Verse 7 administered the shame; verse 8 administers the inheritance of nobility that allows Rathnemi to choose differently. The two are inseparable. A teacher who only shames produces despair; a teacher who only encourages without first naming the wrong produces complacency. Rajemati does both, in the right order, and that is why her words succeed where lesser words would have failed.

Simply Put: She reminds him: "I come from a great king's house and so do you — both of us were raised in lineages that do not break their word. Let us not become the kind of beings who would dishonour our families. Steady your mind, and walk the path you took."

2.9

Like a Tree Uprooted by the Wind

जइ तं काहिसि भावं, जा जा दिच्छसि णारिओ । वायाविद्धोव्व हडो, अट्ठियप्पा भविस्ससि

If you keep forming such desire toward whichever and whichever woman you happen to see — you will become like the rootless hada plant, tossed about by the wind, a soul without any inner standing.

This is the fourth verse of Rajemati's rebuke and the last of her direct words to Rathnemi. Having shamed him (verse 7), having appealed to his lineage (verse 8), she now shows him what his future will look like if he yields. The image she chooses is exactly suited to the warning. The hada is a kind of rootless waterweed. It floats on the surface of a pond, with no anchor to the bottom. When the breeze blows from the east, the plant drifts west; when it shifts to the north, the plant drifts south. It has no will of its own, no direction, no destination. Wherever the wind decides, the hada goes. It is the perfect picture of a being without inner standing. Rajemati is saying: this is what you will become if you yield to desire. Not just toward me, in this single moment — but toward whichever woman you happen to see thereafter. Jā jā dicchasi ṇārio — "whichever and whichever you see." The doubling of jā is meaningful. It signals not one fall but a continuing pattern. Once a monk has yielded to desire once, the inner barrier is breached. The next encounter, with the next woman, will not face the same resistance, because the will to resist has been weakened. And the encounter after that even less. Soon the practitioner is simply blown — by every chance sight, every passing impression, every random stimulus — toward whatever pull is strongest at that moment. The image of the rootless plant is contrasted, in the listener's mind, with what a monk is supposed to be — a tree with deep roots. A rooted tree may be shaken by storms; its branches may sway; its leaves may be blown sideways; but the tree itself stays in place because the roots hold. A monk's roots are his vows, his discipline, his understanding. With these roots in place, even strong storms of desire can shake the surface without uprooting the being. But once the roots are cut — once the monk has agreed, even once, to act on desire instead of against it — there are no roots left. The being becomes the hada. The next breeze, however gentle, takes it. The phrase aṭṭhiyappā bhavissasi — "you will become a soul without standing" — names the ultimate consequence. The Jain tradition treats atma-sthairya (the soul's standing in itself) as the great achievement of the spiritual life. To be sthita (standing) in oneself is to be free of the wind of circumstance, free of the pull of objects, free of the thousand small movements that keep ordinary beings in constant agitation. To be aṭṭhiya (un-standing) is the opposite — it is to have lost one's centre, to have nothing of one's own to stand on, to be entirely a function of whatever is happening around one. A monk who falls into this condition has, in a sense, lost the very thing his monkhood was supposed to produce. There is also a quiet warning here about the mechanism of attachment. Notice the structure: jā jā dicchasi — "whichever you see." The trigger is described as visual perception. The verse is reminding Rathnemi that even the simple act of looking is dangerous for one who has not subdued desire. A look becomes a thought; a thought becomes a wish; a wish becomes an action; an action becomes a habit; a habit becomes a destiny. The whole chain begins with the eye. Rajemati's warning is therefore practical at the very root: guard the senses, especially the eye, because the senses are the doorway through which the wind enters. The text behind the verse pulls together what the four verses of Rajemati have done. (1) Verse 6: the noble serpent will not take back its vomit — what kind of being are you? (2) Verse 7: shame on you for wanting to do what no honourable being would do. (3) Verse 8: remember the noble families we both come from and stand steady. (4) Verse 9: if you do not stand steady, you will become a rootless thing, blown about by the wind of every passing sight. Together these four form a complete intervention: the principle, the rebuke, the appeal, and the warning of the future. The narrative records that this fourth verse, with its picture of a soul becoming a rudderless waterweed, was the final blow that broke through. Rathnemi saw, with sudden clarity, what he was about to make of himself. And he stopped. This verse closes Rajemati's portion of the chapter. The next two verses describe the result of her words and the meaning Acharya Shyambhava draws from the whole episode.

Simply Put: She warns him: if you start desiring every woman you happen to lay eyes on, you will end up like a rootless waterweed — blown around by every little breeze, with no inner ground of your own left to stand on.

2.10

Like the Elephant Subdued by the Goad

तीसे सो वयणं सोच्चा, संजयाइ सुभासियं । अंकुसेण जहा णागो, धम्मे संपडिवाइओ

Hearing those well-spoken words of the restrained nun, he — just as an elephant returns to obedience under the mahout's goad — was firmly re-established in dharma.

After four verses of Rajemati's piercing words, this verse describes the result. Rathnemi has heard. Rathnemi has been moved. Rathnemi has returned. The verse uses one perfectly chosen image to show how the return happened: the elephant under the goad. A wild or intoxicated elephant is dangerous. In the heat of his rut he is uncontrollable, deaf to ordinary commands, capable of crushing whatever stands in his way. The mahout, however, carries a small instrument — the ankusha, a metal goad with a sharp hook. With one well-placed touch of the goad to the right spot behind the ear, the elephant's intoxication breaks. He comes back to himself. He responds to the mahout. He resumes his trained obedience. The contrast between the elephant's overwhelming power and the goad's tiny size is part of the image's force. It is not the goad's weight that subdues the elephant; it is the goad's precision, applied to exactly the right place at exactly the right moment. Rajemati's words, the verse says, were the goad. Rathnemi was the elephant in rut — overcome by the intoxication of desire, deaf to the ordinary reminders of his vows, dangerous to himself and to the spiritual order. Her four sharp verses, applied to exactly the right inner spot — his pride of lineage, his fear of disgrace, his understanding of what he was about to throw away, his foresight of what he would become — broke the intoxication. He returned to himself. He resumed his trained discipline. The verse uses the word saṃpaḍivāio — "firmly re-established" — to indicate that this was not a temporary recovery but a real return. From this moment on, Rathnemi was steady in his vows, and the tradition records that in time he attained liberation. The phrase subhāsiyaṃ — "well-spoken" — deserves attention. The text behind the verse explains its meaning. Subhashita literally means "well-spoken," but in this context it has a specific sense: words spoken from a good intention, words that are beneficial to the listener, words that are sharp without being malicious. Rajemati's words were certainly sharp. Dhiratthu — "shame on you" — is not a mild expression. Vantam icchasi — "you wish to swallow your vomit" — is not a polite phrase. Yet these words are called subhasita, well-spoken, because their intent was the listener's good. They were sharp the way a surgeon's knife is sharp — for healing, not for harm. The Jain texts make a strong point of this: words that wound but heal are well-spoken; words that soothe but corrupt are not. The criterion for "well-spoken" is the welfare of the hearer, not the comfort of the moment. A second observation the text makes is about the success of the teaching. A teacher's words succeed only when the listener has the inner capacity to receive them. Rathnemi was, despite his moment of weakness, a sincere monk. He had truly intended his renunciation. He had simply been overtaken by an old subterranean current of desire that he had not finished uprooting. When Rajemati's words struck, they struck a substrate that was still essentially good. The elephant in this image was not a permanently wild elephant; he was a trained elephant who had momentarily become intoxicated. The goad therefore worked. If Rathnemi had been an entirely undisciplined being with no foundation of practice, no goad however sharp could have brought him back. The verse therefore teaches a second lesson: the success of right speech depends on both the speaker and the hearer. Rajemati's wisdom was complete; Rathnemi's foundation was sound; the meeting of the two produced the recovery. The text also draws out the gentleness implicit in the image, hidden behind its surface harshness. The mahout does not kill the elephant. The mahout does not drive the elephant from the herd. The mahout simply touches the elephant with the goad and waits. The elephant comes back, trembling perhaps, ashamed perhaps, but whole. Rajemati did exactly this. She did not abandon Rathnemi. She did not report him. She did not treat him as ruined. She struck the precise spot, waited for the response, and when the response came, she received him back into the order without humiliation. This is the deeper compassion of her sharpness — it was administered for his return, and the moment the return happened, the sharpness was complete and over. The narrative also tells us, the texts add, that Rathnemi after this incident practised with great steadiness. The intoxication never returned. He completed his monkhood with full discipline, and the karmic destruction that followed brought him to liberation. This whole arc — from initiation, through the moment of fall, through Rajemati's intervention, to recovery and final liberation — has stood for two and a half thousand years as one of the most beloved narratives in Jain tradition. It is loved because it is honest. It does not pretend that monks do not fall. It does not pretend that the path is easy. It shows the real shape of human practice: there will be moments of slipping, and what matters is whether someone will speak the goad-words at the right moment, and whether the listener will have the foundation to receive them.

Simply Put: When Rathnemi heard those carefully chosen, well-meant, cutting words from the disciplined nun, his intoxication broke — just like a wild elephant settles down the instant the mahout's hook touches the right spot — and he returned, fully and steadily, to the path he had taken.

2.11

Thus the Awakened Withdraw from Pleasure

एवं करंति संबुद्धा, पंडिया पवियक्खणा । विणियट्टंति भोगेसु, जहा से पुरिसुत्तमो ॥त्ति बेमि

In just this way do the truly awakened, the wise, and the discerning act — they turn back from sense-enjoyments — just as that highest among men did. So I declare.

This is the closing verse of the chapter. Acharya Shyambhava himself now speaks, drawing the universal teaching out of the particular story he has just told. Rathnemi's narrative was the example; this verse is the rule the example illustrates. The verse names three kinds of practitioners — sambuddha, pandiya, paviyakkhana — and at first glance these three words seem to mean the same thing. The text behind the verse shows that they are different. The three correspond to the three jewels (ratna-traya) of Jain practice: right perception, right knowledge, and right conduct. Sambuddha refers to the practitioner endowed with samyak-darshana, right perception — the one who has truly seen the nature of reality and become awakened to what matters. Pandiya (pandit) refers to the practitioner endowed with samyak-jnana, right knowledge — the one who, having known the nature of objects (their impermanence, their incapacity to give lasting joy), turns away from sinful conduct that pursues them. Paviyakkhana refers to the practitioner endowed with samyak-charitra, right conduct — the one who, fearing the increase of bondage, makes every action with discernment. To list all three together is to say that the one who acts as Rathnemi acted (after his recovery) is a person in whom all three jewels are alive. It is not enough to have only right perception, or only right knowledge, or only right conduct; the three must work together. Rathnemi's recovery shows this. He had right perception (he understood the path); he had right knowledge (he knew the nature of sense-pleasure); but at the moment of weakness his right conduct slipped. Rajemati's words restored the conduct, and with all three jewels back in alignment, his withdrawal from sense-pleasures became complete and final. The phrase viṇiyaṭṭanti bhogesu — "they turn back from sense-enjoyments" — is the operative description. The verb viniyat (vi + ni + yat) means to turn back, to withdraw, to retreat from. The image is of someone who has gone forward toward something, paused, and then turned around and walked the other way. This is precisely what Rathnemi did. He had moved toward Rajemati in desire; on hearing her words, he stopped, reversed, and walked the other way. This reversal is the action the verse names as the mark of the truly awakened, wise, and discerning. The closing comparison — jahā se purisuttamo — "just as that highest of men" — refers to Rathnemi himself, dignified by Acharya Shyambhava with the title purisuttamo, the supreme person. The text behind the verse explains why this title is used. A purushottama (highest person) is not necessarily one who has never fallen. A purushottama is one who, after falling under the influence of delusion (moha-udaya) and committing some wrong, accepts the wrong as a wrong, receives the teaching of the truthful, becomes afraid of further wrongdoing, returns to the path with steadiness, and from then on practises with full discipline. Rathnemi's title is given to him not despite his moment of fall but in part because of it — because his recovery from that fall was so complete and his subsequent practice so unwavering. This is one of the deepest teachings of the Jain tradition about human nature: the highest person is not the one who never stumbles but the one who, having stumbled, returns to the path so completely that the stumble becomes the very moment of his deepest commitment. The text closes with a final reflection on the chapter as a whole. The whole arc — from the impossibility of monkhood without renouncing desire (verse 1), through the distinction between false and true renunciation (verses 2–3), through the methods for handling desire when it returns (verses 4–5), through the great example of Rathnemi (verses 6–10), to this concluding rule (verse 11) — is the complete teaching on the foundation of monkhood. Without renouncing the desire-bondage produced from beginningless time, monkhood cannot be established. With it renounced, even a person who has slipped can recover, and recovering, become the highest of persons. The chapter therefore ends not in defeat but in hope: every practitioner has access to this same recovery, if only the goad of right speech reaches him at the right moment, and if only he has the foundation to receive it. The closing formula ti bemi — "thus I declare" — is the same that closed the first chapter. It is the seal of authority. What Acharya Shyambhava has just said is not his invention; it is the transmitted word of Mahavira, faithfully passed down. Sudharma Swami spoke this to Jambu Swami, and Jambu to the next, and so the line came down to Shyambhava and through him to his young son Manak. With this seal, the chapter closes.

Simply Put: This is what the truly awakened, the wise, and the keenly discerning all do — they turn around and walk away from sense-pleasures, exactly the way that finest of men, Rathnemi, did once his vision cleared.
