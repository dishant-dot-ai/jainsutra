The Second Chulika — Solitary Conduct. The principles of solitary residence, careful conduct, and the qualities of the truly liberated monk.

Chulika 2: Vivikta-Caryā

About This Chulika

The Solitary Path

The second Chulika, Vivikta-Caryā ('solitary conduct'), addresses the monk who has progressed beyond the need for community guidance and is ready for the deepest stage of practice — solitary residence and complete inner withdrawal. It teaches the principles of choosing the right place, maintaining unbroken mindfulness, and the qualities of the truly liberated monk.

Where the main ten chapters of the Dashvaikalik form a complete teaching for the new monk, the two Chulikas serve as supplements: the first to restore the wavering, the second to refine the advanced. Together they bring the Dashvaikalik to its final shape — a complete training manual for every stage of the monastic life, from initiation to mastery.

The Sutras of Chulika 2

2.1

The Opening Vow (Uthanika)

चूलियं तु पवक्खामि, सुयं केवलिभासियं । जं सुणित्तु सुपुण्णाणं, धम्मे उप्पज्जए मई

I shall now declare the chulika — that which has been heard, which was spoken by the Kevali (the omniscient one) — by the hearing of which, in those of (great) merit, faith arises in the dharma.

This opening gatha is the uthanika — the formal threshold-verse of the chulika. In it the compiler-arranger does two things at once: he asserts the source of what follows, and he names its fruit. The source is named with two precise words. Suyaṃ — "heard" — affirms that what follows is part of the śruta tradition, the heard-and-handed-down scripture. Kevali-bhāsiyaṃ — "spoken by the Kevali" — affirms that the original source was an omniscient Tirthankara. Together these words assert the chulika's full scriptural authority. The fruit is named with equal care. The chulika is not a source of magical power, scholarly distinction, or worldly benefit. Its fruit is dhamme uppajjae maī — "right understanding arises in the dharma." That is to say: the meritorious listener, on hearing this chulika, finds that his mind takes a turn — toward the discipline, toward the practice, toward the soul. Yet the gatha is honest: this fruit arises only in supuṇṇāṇaṃ, in those of merit. Not every hearer will be receptive. The teaching is offered freely; the soil that receives it must already have been prepared. In Jain tradition, the original sutras (mūla-sūtras) were composed by the Ganadharas. Over time, ācāryas of profound learning gathered, edited, and arranged these teachings into the various extant āgamas. Because the foundation of all āgamas is the Dvādaśāṅgī (the twelve-fold body of teaching) revealed by the Kevali through the Ganadharas, every āgama-passage may rightly be called kevali-bhāsita. This gatha is therefore not making a literal claim that the compiler personally heard the Tirthankara, but the deeper claim that the lineage of transmission goes back, unbroken, to the omniscient one.

Simply Put: The compiler opens the chulika by saying: "I will now speak the crest-teaching that was heard from the omniscient ones. By hearing this, the merit-blessed soul finds his mind turning to the dharma."

2.2

The One Who Has Found the Goal of the Counter-Stream

अणुसोयपट्ठिए बहुजणम्मि, पडिसोयलद्धलक्खेणं । पडिसोयमेव अप्पा, दायव्वो होउकामेणं

While the multitude is set out flowing with the stream, by the one who has found the goal of (going against) the counter-stream — by the one who desires to become (truly liberated) — the soul (self) is to be set, indeed, against the (counter-)stream itself.

This gatha sets out the chulika's central image: two streams, two directions. The world's mass — bahu-jana — is "set out" (paṭṭhie) flowing along the stream of sense-pleasure, attachment, and worldly habit. This is the natural current; almost everyone in the universe is caught in it. The monk who has woken up to the truth — paḍisoya-laddha-lakkheṇaṃ, "one who has found the goal of the counter-stream" — recognises that liberation lies the other way. He is named here also by his deepest motivation: hou-kāmeṇaṃ, "by one who desires to become." Not "to have," not "to acquire," but to become — to come into the true being of a liberated self. The instruction is precise. Paḍisoyam-eva appā dāyavvo — "the soul itself is to be set against the (counter-)stream itself." The verb dāyavvo (literally, "to be given") here carries the sense of commitment: the soul is to be handed over to the counter-stream, given to it without reservation. Not partly. Not occasionally. Not only when it is convenient. The whole soul, set against the whole current. The gatha thus marks the transition from the wavering monk (whom the first chulika addressed) to the committed monk (whom the second chulika addresses). Whoever has found the goal — whoever has tasted, even briefly, the direction of liberation — is being asked here to commit fully.

Simply Put: Most of the world is moving with the current of sense-pleasure. But the one who has woken up and seen that liberation lies the other way — the one who actually wants to become free — must hand his whole self over to the counter-current.

2.3

The Counter-Stream is the Refuge of the Well-Practiced

अणुसोयसुहो लोगो, पडिसोओ आसवो (आसमो) सुविहियाणं । अणुसोओ संसारो, पडिसोओ तस्स उत्तारो

For ordinary people the (sense-)stream is pleasant; the counter-stream is the refuge (or: the resting-place) of the well-practiced (monks). The (sense-)stream is saṃsāra (the cycle of birth-and-death); the counter-stream is the crossing-over from it.

The image of two streams is now sharpened with a sober honesty. The gatha admits, without sentimentality, that the sense-stream is pleasant — aṇusoya-suho. Not just pleasant in seeming, but pleasant in fact, to the ordinary person. The Jina-teaching does not pretend that worldly pleasure is illusory or non-existent; it acknowledges that it gives real satisfaction in the moment. What it questions is the result. Aṇusoo saṃsāro — that pleasant stream is precisely saṃsāra; it is the very thing that keeps a soul cycling through birth and death. The counter-stream, by contrast, is named āsavo (refuge / proper resort) or, by alternate reading, āsamo (dwelling-place, āśrama) for suvihiyāṇaṃ — for the well-practiced. The discipline is not pleasant, not in the worldly sense; but for those who have begun to taste it, it becomes a home. It becomes the place where the soul actually belongs. And it is, the gatha says, tassa uttāro — the crossing-over from saṃsāra. Where the sense-stream takes the soul deeper into birth-and-death, the counter-stream lifts the soul out of it altogether. The gatha's parallel structure makes the choice stark: the same word paḍisoo appears twice — once as "refuge," once as "crossing-over" — and the same word aṇusoo appears twice — once as "pleasant," once as "saṃsāra." The paired contrast reveals the deeper truth: the very thing that feels easy is the thing that binds; the very thing that feels hard is the thing that frees.

Simply Put: For ordinary people, going with the current of the senses feels pleasant — but that current is exactly what keeps them trapped in the cycle of birth and death. For the well-practiced monks, going against the current is the true home; it is also the way out of the cycle.

2.4

The Charge to the Conduct-Brave Monk

तम्हा आयारपरक्कमेणं, संवरसमाहिबहुलेणं । चरिया गुणा य णियमा य, हुंति साहूणं दट्ठव्वा

Therefore, by the (monk) who is brave-strong in (right) conduct, who is rich in saṃvara (restraint) and samādhi (composure), the (monk's daily) conduct, the qualities (vinaya etc.), and the (various) rules and sub-rules — these are to be seen / kept in view / observed by the monks.

The gatha begins with a "therefore" (tamhā) that ties it directly to the two previous gathas. Since the worldly stream leads to saṃsāra and the counter-stream is the crossing-over, therefore the monk has work to do — and that work is not vague. It has three precise dimensions: cariyā (his daily conduct), guṇā (his qualities of character — humility and the rest), and ṇiyamā (his particular rules, vows, and self-imposed restrictions). The agent who is to do this work is named with two adjectives. Āyāra-parakkameṇaṃ — "brave-strong in conduct." The word parākrama (here in instrumental) means heroic vigour, the kind of strength that strives forward. The monk is to be heroic in his conduct — not just heroic in renunciation, not just heroic in fasting, but heroic in the daily, granular, walking, eating, speaking, sleeping practice of conduct. This is the harder heroism. Saṃvara-samāhi-bahuleṇaṃ — "rich in saṃvara (the closing of the doors of the senses and the mind to the inflow of karma) and samādhi (the steady, composed mind)." The monk is to be heavy with both: full of restraint, full of composure. What such a monk sees (or keeps in view — daṭṭhavvā) is the threefold practice: his cariyā, his guṇā, his ṇiyamā. The verse is therefore both a description of what a true monk is, and a charge to the practitioner: be this; and keep these three in view as your work. The commentators add a luminous observation: each of these three dimensions feeds the others. The qualities (vinaya, etc.) make the conduct possible; the conduct makes the rules possible; the rules in turn make the qualities deeper. The gatha is therefore not three separate items but one organic discipline.

Simply Put: So: the monk who has bravely committed to conduct, and who is rich in restraint and composure, must keep three things steadily in view — his daily conduct, his inner qualities, and the particular rules he has taken on.

2.5

The Six Praiseworthy Qualities of the Wandering Sage

अणिएयवासो समुयाण चरिया, अण्णायउंछं पइरिक्कया य । अप्पोवही कलहविवज्जणा य, विहारचरिया इसिणं पसत्था

Dwelling without a fixed abode, begging from many houses (samudāna), accepting unfamiliar / unprepared morsels (ajñāta-uñcha), staying in solitary places, keeping few possessions, and the giving up of quarrels — this is the praiseworthy wandering-conduct of the sages (ṛṣis).

This gatha is the chulika's compact list of the six praiseworthy qualities of the wandering sage. Each one deserves its own attention. Aṇieyavāso — "no fixed abode." The general rule (utsarga-mārga) for the monk is perpetual movement. He should not become rooted in any one place. This is the rāja-mārga, the highway, of monastic life. The commentary admits an exception: in old age, in physical infirmity, or in special circumstances, with proper discrimination and discernment, a monk may stay in one place — and this is the apavāda-mārga, the scripturally permitted exception. Samuyāṇa-cariyā — "many-house begging." The monk does not single out any one house as his food-provider. He goes naturally from house to house, rich and poor, without distinction; he takes only a little from each, like a cow grazing. This protects him from forming attachments, from being a burden on any single household, and from the corruption of being "patronised." Aṇṇāya-uṃcha — "from unknown houses." He does not arrange his food in advance. He does not have favourite donors. He does not even necessarily know the household into which he steps. This is the highest form of non-attachment in food. Pairikkayā — "in solitary places." The monk lives in a place suitable for the discipline — quiet, free of crowd-disturbance, free of the immediate presence of women, animals, eunuchs (the categories the discipline names as risks to brahmacarya). He does not live in dense, busy, urban places. Appovahī — "few possessions." The monk who has renounced parigraha keeps only the limited monastic equipment (clothes, alms-bowl, a few items) authorised by the āgama. There is a practical reason as well as a spiritual one: the monk must do pratilekhana (mindful examination) of all his belongings every morning and evening, and must carry them all on his own back as he walks from village to village. Excess possessions are simply impossible to maintain in such a life. Kalaha-vivajjaṇā — "giving up of quarrels." Quarrelling destroys peace, magnifies attachment-and-aversion, multiplies karma-bondage, and damages the discipline itself. Worse, it gives observers a pretext for contempt of the dharma. The monk who has set his soul against the worldly stream cannot afford to be seen brawling. These six together are the vihāra-cariyā — the comprehensive wandering-life — which the sages have called pasatthā, praiseworthy. The monk who actually keeps all six is, the commentary notes, walking the discipline as it was meant to be walked.

Simply Put: The praiseworthy life of the wandering monk has six features: he never settles in one place, he begs from many houses without playing favourites, he takes only what comes from unknown houses without prior arrangement, he stays in solitary places, he keeps very few possessions, and he gives up quarrelling.

2.6

The Acceptable and Unacceptable in Begging

आइण्णओमाणविवज्जणा य, ओसण्णदिट्ठाहडभत्तपाणे । संसट्ठकप्पेण चरिज्ज भिक्खू, तज्जायसंसट्ठो जई जएज्जा

(The monk) shall give up (going to) the over-crowded feast (ākīrṇa) and the under-supplied feast (avamāna); shall accept food and drink (only when) generally seen-being-brought (osanna-diṭṭha-āhaḍa); the bhikṣu shall practice begging by the rule of (taking from) the saṃsṛṣṭa (vessel-with-residue); the yati shall make particular effort with the taj-jāta-saṃsṛṣṭa (vessel with residue of the very same food being given).

This gatha names two technical Jain terms — ākīrṇa and avamāna — and two more — saṃsṛṣṭa and taj-jāta-saṃsṛṣṭa — that govern the proper begging-practice of the monk. Ākīrṇa-avamāna-vivajjaṇā — the monk avoids both the over-crowded feast and the under-supplied feast. Why? In an over-crowded feast (a wedding, a great public meal with hundreds or thousands of guests), the monk cannot maintain his disciplinary care: he gets jostled by the crowd, has body-contact (saṃghaṭṭa) with men and women he should not touch, and risks being knocked over. In an under-supplied feast, the food is already short of the gathering, and to take from it is to leave the householders hungrier. Both forms of public feast are therefore avoided. Osaṇṇa-diṭṭhāhaḍa-bhatta-pāṇe — "generally, food and drink seen-as-brought." The standard rule is that the monk only accepts food and drink whose source he has been able to observe; this protects against the offering of contaminated, prepared-for-monks, or improperly stored items. The word osaṇṇa — "generally, for the most part" — is doing important work here. The compiler-arranger is signalling that there are principled exceptions. The commentary names them: in some highly-restrictive religious households, monks are not permitted into the kitchen area, so the food must be brought from out of sight; in such cases, with discrimination, the monk may accept what was brought from unseen places. The exception is allowed; the rule remains. Saṃsaṭṭha-kappa and taj-jāta-saṃsaṭṭha are technical resolutions (abhigraha) the monk may take. Saṃsṛṣṭa literally means "smeared / having residue." The first form: the monk resolves to accept food only when given by a hand, ladle, or vessel already smeared with some non-living food residue. The second, more advanced form: he resolves to accept the food only when the smearing residue is of the very same item being given (so curd from a curd-smeared ladle, milk from a milk-smeared vessel, etc.). The point of these resolutions is twofold: (a) they reduce the risk of paścāt-karma — the householder having to wash the vessel afterwards on the monk's account, which would itself be a sin; and (b) they cultivate the monk's ability to take what is being given, not what is being prepared specially for him. The advanced form is the more demanding because it requires precise correspondence. The verse uses jaī jaejjā for the second clause — "the yati shall make effort" — signalling that this practice is for the more developed monk who has chosen the taj-jāta-saṃsṛṣṭa resolution as his discipline.

Simply Put: The monk should not go to giant public feasts (where he gets jostled and the discipline breaks) or to feasts that are short on food. He should normally take only food he has actually seen being brought to him. He should accept food given from a hand or ladle already smeared with food residue (so the householder has no extra cleaning to do); a more advanced monk takes the resolution that the smearing must be from the very same item being given.

2.7

The Forbidden Foods and the Practices That Replace Them

अमज्जमंसासी अमच्छरीआ, अभिक्खणं णिव्विगइगया य । अभिक्खणं काउसग्गकारी, सज्झायजोगे पयओ हविज्जा

Not eating wine and meat (and fish), free of matsya (or: free of mātsarya — envy), repeatedly going to the nirvikṛti state (food without enriching ingredients), repeatedly performing kāyotsarga — (the monk) shall be diligent in the practice of svādhyāya (scripture-study) and yoga (meditative practices).

This gatha gathers four positive instructions for the monk: what he should not eat, what he should regularly drop from his diet, what he should regularly stand in (kāyotsarga), and what he should diligently engage in (svādhyāya). A-majja-maṃsa-āsī — "not eating wine and meat." The Jain monk is unconditionally forbidden these. The commentary on amaccharīyā notes that the word can be read two ways. In the Sanskrit shadow amātsarya, it means "free of envy, free of pride" — the standard ethical reading. In the alternative shadow amatsya, it means "not eating fish." The compiler-arranger seems to note that both readings are appropriate: the first half of the gatha thus addresses both the mahā-vigaya (the "great forbidden" — wine, meat, fish, butter) and the inner counterparts (envy, pride) which are their psychological correlates. ⚠️ Note: Both readings are present in the tradition; the commentary explicitly preserves the ambiguity. Abhikkhaṇaṃ ṇivvigai-gayā — "repeatedly going to the nirvikṛti state." The Jain dietary system distinguishes the vigayas — six categories of "enriching" food (milk, curd, ghee, oil, jaggery, fried items) — which are not forbidden but are recognised as taste-enhancing and attachment-producing. The monk is asked to frequently abstain from them: 10, 20, 25 days a month, as ability allows, he should eat only the most basic food without the enriching ingredients. This loosens attachment to taste, supports control of the senses and mind, and counts as the rasa-parityāga austerity which produces great karmic burning. Abhikkhaṇaṃ kāusagga-kārī — "repeatedly performing kāyotsarga." Kāyotsarga literally means "abandonment of the body" — standing in stillness, releasing identification with the physical form, fixing the mind on the soul. The commentary notes that the monk's day is structured around it: after every major activity — begging, examining belongings, study, walking — the monk performs a kāyotsarga to purify the activity just completed. The whole arc of the monk's discipline aims at this: to release identification with the body and rest in identification with the soul. Sajjhāya-joge payao havijjā — "diligent in the practice of svādhyāya." The monk's other principal occupation is the study of scripture. He should be payao — diligent, vigilant, ever-engaged — in this. Time not consumed by other practices is to be filled with study and meditation, not with idle conversation (vikathā). The four together — abstaining from forbidden foods, regularly fasting from enriching foods, regularly entering kāyotsarga, regularly engaging in study — form the daily backbone of the chulika-prescribed monk's life. The commentary observes that the gatha can be read either as descriptive ("the monk is one who...") or as imperative ("the monk should..."), and both readings are valid.

Simply Put: The monk does not eat wine, meat, or fish (and is also free of envy and pride); he often eats food without the rich ingredients (milk, curd, ghee, oil, jaggery, fried items); he often stands in body-releasing meditation; and he is always diligent in studying scripture.

2.8

No Attachment to Things or Places

ण पडिण्णवेज्जा सयणासणाइं, सेज्जं णिसेज्जं तह भत्तपाणं । गामे कुले वा णगरे व देसे, ममत्तभावं ण कहिंपि कुज्जा

(The monk) shall not bind himself by (attachment-based) resolution to (particular) bedding or seat, lodging, study-place, or food and drink; in any village, family, city, or country — let him not anywhere form the feeling of "mine."

This gatha addresses a subtle danger: the monk has renounced his household, but he can still re-form attachments to the replacements — to "my" bedding, "my" lodging, "my" donor, "my" preferred study-spot, "my" particular food. The chulika sees this and forbids it. The technical word paḍiṇṇa (Sanskrit pratijñā) means a binding determination. The monk is told: do not make any attachment-based resolution that "such-and-such bedding alone is suitable for me" or "I will only study in this particular spot." Whatever bedding, whatever vessel, whatever lodging he encounters in his wanderings — let him be content with what is found, remain in equanimity, hold to the discipline. Once he says "only this kind of equipment will do for me," he has betrayed the very non-possession great vow he has taken; many further faults arise from there. The second half of the gatha extends the principle from things to places. Gāme kule vā ṇagare va dese — in no village, no family, no city, no country — should the monk form the feeling of "mine." Not "my city," not "my householder-supporters," not "my region." The monk is to wander as the wind wanders: rooted nowhere, owned by nowhere. The text adds an important practical implication: as long as the monk has the strength, he should keep moving across many regions — not letting any one place lay claim to him.

Simply Put: The monk should not become attached to any specific bedding, seat, lodging, study-spot, or kind of food. He should not form a feeling of "this is mine" toward any village, family, city, or country.

2.9

Reverence Not for Householders, Company Only with the Stainless

गिहिणो वेयावडियं ण कुज्जा, अभिवायणं वंदणपूयणं वा । असंकिलिट्ठेहिं समं वसेज्जा, मुणी चरित्तस्स जओ ण हाणी

(The monk) shall not perform service (vaiyāvṛtya) for a householder, nor offer to him the formal greeting, salutation, or worship-honour. The muni shall dwell only with the stainless ones (asaṃkliṣṭa) — those by association with whom there is no loss of conduct (cāritra).

This gatha sets two important boundaries for the monk's social life. The first concerns his relationship with householders — even respectable, wealthy, powerful, devoted householders. The monk does not perform vaiyāvṛtya (the deferential service of foot-rubbing, fetching food, attending to bodily needs) for any householder. The reason is precise: a king, a minister, a wealthy merchant, however respected in the world, is still a householder — that is, still in the avirati (non-renounced) condition. The monastic state is a higher state. For the monk to render the service that disciples render to gurus toward someone who is not a guru is to praise and approve the very non-renunciation he has himself renounced. The seemingly humble act of serving a householder is, the commentary says, actually a betrayal of the great vow. Equally, the monk does not offer a householder the formal abhivādana (deferential greeting), vandana (bowing), or pūjana (worship-honours). These are reserved for those who are themselves on the path. To offer them to a householder is to confuse the discipline. The second half of the gatha turns to the monk's positive companionship. Asaṃkliṣṭa literally means "without kleśa" — without contention, without inner stain, without distress. The monk should live with monks of stainless conduct. If he finds himself in a place where there is contention, he should first try to subdue his own reactivity and remain at peace with the situation; if that proves impossible, he should leave for a stainless place. The principle is simple and ancient: a person is shaped, consciously or unconsciously, by the company he keeps. The wise have therefore always urged dwelling among the good.

Simply Put: The monk does not serve, formally greet, bow to, or honour a householder — even a powerful or generous one — because the monk's status is higher than that of the householder. He stays in the company of monks whose conduct is stainless, so that his own conduct is not damaged by association.

2.10

Solitary Wandering When No Worthy Companion is Found

ण वा लभेज्जा णिउणं सहायं, गुणाहियं वा गुणओ समं वा । एक्को वि पावाइं विवज्जयंतो, विहरेज्ज कामेसु असज्जमाणो

If (the monk) does not find a skilled companion — one greater than himself in qualities or his equal in qualities — let him, even alone, while completely giving up sinful (acts), wander, not getting attached to (any) sense-objects.

This is one of the most carefully balanced gathas in the entire chulika. It both permits and constrains solitary wandering. The Jain tradition recognises certain advanced practitioners — the jinakalpī, the pratimā-dhārī, the heroic ascetic — who, by the leave of the ācārya, may take up either short-term or lifelong solitary wandering as a deliberate discipline. These are the rare practitioners. The gatha here is not addressing them. The gatha addresses the ordinary monk. The general principle for him is to live in community: it is in community that his discipline is protected, that his observance is supported, that his wavering is steadied. To leave the community and go alone is, the commentary frankly admits, "bahu jokham bharelu" — full of risk. The solitary monk, with no one to check him, may quietly slip in his conduct, slip in his celibacy, slip in his right view. The risk is real. But — and this is the carefully balanced concession — circumstances may arise in which the monk simply cannot find a companion who is either superior to him or his equal in qualities. Perhaps his community has scattered. Perhaps the only available companions are those whose conduct would actively damage him. In such case, two paths lie before him: stay in bad company and lose his discipline, or wander alone with full vigilance and protect his discipline. The chulika instructs him to choose the second. The conditions on solitary wandering are stringent: pāvāiṃ vivajjayaṃto — completely giving up sinful actions; and kāmesu asajjamāṇo — not getting attached anywhere to sense-objects. The solitary monk, having lost the external scaffolding of the community, must therefore become his own community: his own discipline, his own watchman, his own corrector. The first chulika exhorted the monk not to abandon the discipline; this gatha extends the same exhortation: even if the company is gone, the discipline must remain. The relationship between the two chulikas is illuminating here. The first chulika (Rativakya) spoke against giving up the discipline. This gatha says: even if you cannot find a companion, do not give up the discipline; wander alone. The two chulikas thus speak with one voice from two angles.

Simply Put: If the monk cannot find a fellow-monk who is his superior or his equal in conduct, let him wander alone — but only while completely giving up all sinful acts and refusing to be entangled in any sense-pleasure. Solitude is permitted as a last resort, and only on those strict conditions.

2.11

The Rule of Stay-Length and the Following of Scripture

संवच्छरं वावि परं पमाणं, बीयं च वासं ण तहिं वसेज्जा । सुत्तस्स मग्गेण चरेज्ज भिक्खू, सुत्तस्स अत्थो जह आणवेइ

For up to one full year (after the cāturmāsa stay) the upper limit (of avoidance) holds; (the monk) shall not return to dwell in that same place a second rainy-season. The bhikṣu shall walk by the path of the sutra; just as the meaning of the sutra commands.

This gatha gives the kalpa-maryādā — the technical rule on length of stay. The Jain monk has two recognised "stays": the māsa-kalpa (one-month stay, observed in summer and winter — 29 days for monks, 58 days for nuns) and the cāturmāsa-kalpa (four-month rainy-season stay). The Bṛhatkalpa Sūtra establishes the principle: after either stay, the monk may not return to dwell in that same place until at least double the elapsed time has passed. So after a 29-day stay, two months must pass; after a four-month rainy-season stay, eight months must pass — and since the monk cannot wander during the next rainy season anyway, this effectively means a full year before he can return for another rainy-season stay. This is the "one year" the gatha references. The reasoning is twofold and beautiful. First, on the spiritual side, the rule prevents the monk from forming attachments — to a place, to a household, to particular devotees. The discipline is meant to remain unrooted; a monk who returns again and again to the same village inevitably grows roots there. Second, on the propagation side, the rule ensures that the Jain teaching spreads to many regions: the monk is forced, by his own discipline, to keep moving into new villages, new towns, new districts, where new souls may encounter the dharma and find their own way to the path. The second half of the gatha gives a more general principle. Suttassa maggeṇa carejja bhikkhū — "the bhikṣu shall walk by the path of the sutra." Not by personal preference, not by current fashion, not by what is convenient — but by what scripture sets out. Suttassa attho jaha āṇavei — "as the meaning of the sutra commands." This is more demanding still: the monk is not to follow only the literal letter of the sutra but its meaning — the sutra's spirit — which often requires understanding, discrimination, and serious reflection. The verse thereby installs the sutra (the recorded teaching) as the absolute reference point for the monk's daily life.

Simply Put: After a four-month rainy-season stay, the monk may not return to that same place for at least a year. The monk should walk by the path that the sutra lays out — not by the literal words alone, but by what the sutra's deeper meaning instructs.

2.12

The Six Self-Examination Questions (Part One)

जो पुव्वरत्तावररत्तकाले, संपेहए अप्पगमप्पएणं । किं मे कडं किं च मे किच्चसेसं, किं सक्कणिज्जं ण समायरामि

(The monk) who, at the first watch and the last watch of the night, examines the soul (himself) by the soul (himself) — (asking): "What has been done by me? What of my work-yet-to-do remains? What that is achievable am I (still) not undertaking?" —

This and the next gatha together set out the chulika's striking self-examination practice. The framing is precise. The monk is to perform this examination puvva-rattāvara-ratta-kāle — at the first watch and the last watch of the night. That is, just before sleeping and just after rising. These are the two thresholds of the night: the moment of laying-down and the moment of taking-up. They are also the moments when the mind is most quiet, least burdened by the day's events. The examination is appagam appaeṇaṃ — "the soul by the soul." The monk does not examine himself by reading another text; he does not consult a teacher; he does not poll a peer. He sets the soul as the object and the soul as the agent and looks. This is among the most pure formulations of self-inquiry in any tradition. This gatha lists the first three of six questions. They are oriented toward gain — toward what is being added to the discipline. (1) Kiṃ me kaḍaṃ? "What has been done by me today?" — a survey of what was actually accomplished, not what was intended. (2) Kiṃ ca me kicca-sesaṃ? "What of my work-yet-to-do remains?" — a survey of what was planned but not yet completed. (3) Kiṃ sakkaṇijjaṃ ṇa samāyarāmi? "What that is well within my power am I still not undertaking?" — the most uncomfortable of the three, because it strips away the easy excuse of inability. The monk asks: what could I do, and have not done, only out of laziness or inattention? The commentary notes that for the monk the two principal areas of "what could I do?" are śruta-jñāna (study of scripture — receiving teaching, reflecting on it, memorising it) and tapasyā (austerity — fasting, reduced food, kāyotsarga, etc.). The first three self-examination questions therefore drive directly at: am I adding to my discipline what I am capable of adding?

Simply Put: The monk should sit, just before sleep and just after waking, and examine his soul by his soul, asking: (1) What did I actually do today? (2) What did I plan to do that I haven't yet finished? (3) What could I be doing that is within my power, and yet I'm not doing?

2.13

The Six Self-Examination Questions (Part Two)

किं मे परो पासइ किं च अप्पा, किं वाहं खलियं ण विवज्जयामि । इच्चेव सम्मं अणुपासमाणो, अणागयं णो पडिबंधं कुज्जा

"What faults of mine does another see? What faults do I see in myself? Which fault of mine, even (when) I see (it), do I (still) not give up?" — Thus rightly examining (himself), (the monk) shall not, regarding the future, hold on to (a fixed bondage / a continuation of those faults).

This gatha completes the six questions begun in the previous gatha. Where the first three questions were oriented toward gain (what am I adding?), these last three are oriented toward loss (what am I leaking?). And they grow progressively more uncomfortable. (4) Kiṃ me paro pāsai? — "What faults of mine does another see?" This is the first uncomfortable question, because it admits that others see things in me that I do not. The monk is being asked to look at himself the way others look at him. (5) Kiṃ ca appā? — "And what (faults) do I (myself) see in me?" This is the second-level question: those few faults that I, even with my own preferred lens, cannot escape seeing. This question forces the monk to be a witness to himself. (6) Kiṃ vāhaṃ khaliyaṃ ṇa vivajjayāmi? — "Which fault, even (when) I see (it), do I (still) not give up?" This is the third and most painful question, because it admits the seen fault that has not been corrected. The fault that the monk sees clearly, knows clearly, and yet, for reasons of habit, of subtle attachment, of laziness, has not yet released. It is one thing to fail through ignorance; it is far harder to face the failure that persists after the seeing. The closing line is the gatha's instruction: aṇāgayaṃ ṇo paḍibaṃdhaṃ kujjā — "regarding the future, he should not hold on to (the same bondage)." The Sanskrit shadow renders paḍibaṃdha as "bondage" or "obstruction"; the commentary glosses it as "the fixed determination to keep doing the same thing tomorrow that I did today." That is, the monk is not to defer the correction. He is not to say "I will give it up next time." He is to identify it tonight, and start tomorrow already free of it. These two gathas together constitute, in the commentary's view, one of the most direct ātma-anuśāsana (self-discipline) instructions in the entire scripture. They are a complete daily practice in eight lines.

Simply Put: The monk also asks himself: (4) What faults of mine do others see? (5) What faults do I see in myself? (6) Which fault, even though I clearly see it, am I still not giving up? Having examined himself this way, he must not let the same faults continue into tomorrow — he must not put off the correction.

2.14

The Instant Recall, Like a Horse to the Rein

जत्थेव पासे कइ दुप्पउत्तं, काएण वाया अदु माणसेणं । तत्थेव धीरो पडिसाहरेज्जा, आइण्णओ खिप्पमिव क्खलीणं

Wherever (the monk) sees, in any matter, (himself to be) ill-engaged — by the body, by speech, or by the mind — there itself, the steady one shall withdraw (the wrong-engaged self) — like a thoroughbred (horse) (yielding) instantly to the bit.

This gatha is the practical conclusion of the self-examination gathas. The previous two gathas instructed the monk to examine himself at the night-thresholds. But what about the moments during the day, when an unwholesome engagement actually arises? This gatha addresses that. The instruction is clean: jattheva ... tattheva — "wherever ... there itself." The very moment the monk catches himself ill-engaged — by body (a wrong action), by speech (wrong words), or by mind (wrong thoughts) — at that very moment, in that very place, he is to withdraw. He does not wait until evening. He does not promise himself "later." He does not finish the wrong action and then regret. He withdraws immediately. The simile is the gatha's gift: āiṇṇao khippam iva kkhalīṇaṃ — "like a thoroughbred to the bit." The image is precise. An ordinary horse, when the rein is pulled, fights, resists, must be physically forced. A thoroughbred — a horse of noble breed, well-trained — yields to the slightest pull on the bit. He does not need to be jerked; he does not even need a hard pull; the lightest touch on the rein turns him completely. The chulika's vision of the mature monk is exactly this: at the slightest registering of wrong-engagement, the monk turns. The pull on the rein need not be painful or dramatic; it need only be felt; and the response is instant. The qualification dhīro — "the steady one" — is doing important work here. The instant turning is not the work of a beginner; it is the work of one whose mind has been steadied through long discipline. The beginner must rely on regret-and-correction; the dhīra monk has internalised the rein, and turns the moment he feels its touch. The commentary observes that this self-disciplining without external prompting is the heart of ātma-anuśāsana. The monk does not need a guru to point out his lapse; he does not need a community to correct him; his own awareness has become, through long practice, the rein on his own conduct.

Simply Put: The moment the monk notices himself doing, saying, or thinking something wrong, he immediately pulls himself back from it — not later, not after the act, but right then. He is like a fine horse that turns the instant it feels the slightest pull on the bit.

2.15

The Awakened-Living Monk

जस्सेरिसा जोगा जिइंदियस्स, धिईमओ सप्पुरिसस्स णिच्चं । तमाहु लोए पडिबुद्धजीवी, सो जीवइ संजमजीविएणं

He whose engagements (yogas — of mind, speech, body) are always thus, who is conqueror of the senses, full of steadfastness, a true person — him, in this world, (the wise) call the paḍibuddha-jīvī (one who lives awakened); he lives by the life of self-discipline (saṃyama).

This gatha is the chulika's title-of-honour, the conferring of a name. The monk who has actually realised what the previous gathas have taught — perpetual mindfulness, instant self-correction, the conquest of the senses, steadfastness in the discipline — is now to be called by a precise title: paḍibuddha-jīvī, "the one who lives awakened." The commentary glosses the title in several ways. It means one who lives the discipline with both faith (śraddhā) and knowledge (jñāna) — not merely as ritual but as understood truth. It means the jñānī ātmā — the soul become wise. It means the jāgṛta ātmā — the soul ever-awake. The qualifications the gatha lists are exact: (1) jitendriya — conqueror of the senses; (2) dhṛti-mat — full of steadfastness; (3) sat-puruṣa — true person, of noble disposition and right conduct; (4) jasserisā jogā — whose engagements (of mind, speech, and body) are always thus — that is, always rightly directed. And the result is named: saṃjama-jīvieṇaṃ jīvai — "he lives by the life of saṃyama." His ordinary biological life has become inseparable from the discipline; his discipline is no longer a thing he does but the very fabric of his living. The gatha's deepest claim is therefore not about technique but about identity. There is a kind of person whose every action expresses the discipline; whose breathing, speaking, walking, eating, sleeping all bear the mark of saṃyama; for whom the discipline is not external but is now his life itself. That person, the gatha says, has earned a name. He is paḍibuddha-jīvī. The implicit corollary is sobering: anyone who has not reached this stage is still merely practising the discipline — still doing it from the outside, still managing it with effort. To pass from practitioner to paḍibuddha-jīvī is a real transition, and the chulika has just given the formula for it.

Simply Put: The monk who is always like this — having conquered his senses, full of steadiness, of noble character, with mind, speech, and body always rightly engaged — is called by the wise an awakened-living one. His ordinary daily life is the life of self-discipline.

2.16

The Soul is to be Continuously Guarded

अप्पा खलु सययं रक्खियव्वो, सव्विंदिएहिं सुसमाहिएहिं । अरक्खिओ जाइपहं उवेइ, सुरक्खिओ सव्वदुहाण मुच्चइ ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥

The soul, indeed, is to be continuously guarded — by (the monk who is) well-composed in all the senses. The unguarded (soul) goes to the path of (low) (re-)birth; the well-guarded (soul) is liberated from all sorrows. — Thus I declare.

This is the final gatha of the chulika and the final gatha of the entire Dashvaikalik Sutra. The compiler has given it the weight of a parting gift, and it carries that weight gracefully. The instruction is single and total: appā khalu sayayaṃ rakkhiyavvo — "the soul, indeed, is to be continuously guarded." Three words deserve attention. Khalu — "indeed" — is the emphatic particle; the compiler is asserting this with full force. Sayayaṃ — "continuously" — is the key word; not at certain hours, not on certain days, not in certain circumstances, but continuously. The guarding is to be unceasing. The agency by which the guarding happens is named: savviṃdiehiṃ susamāhiehiṃ — "by all the senses well-composed." The soul is guarded through the disciplining of the senses. Each sense, brought into mastery, becomes a guardian of the soul; each sense let loose becomes a thief that breaks the soul's house. Samādhi here is therefore not abstract meditation but the steady composure that has the senses in hand. The two-fold consequence is stated with stark symmetry. Arakkhio jāi-pahaṃ uvei — the unguarded soul goes onto the path of (re-)birth, falling into low life-forms (one-sensed beings, two-sensed beings, etc.) and there suffering unbearable pain. Surakkhio savva-duhāṇa muccai — the well-guarded soul is liberated from all sorrows. The commentary's final observation is striking. Many people, it notes, treat the body as the supreme thing to be guarded — caring for its health, comfort, beauty. The chulika here sets the priority straight: the soul is the supreme thing to be guarded. Not the body, but the soul. The body is a tool for the soul's discipline; once the body fails to support the discipline, the wise practitioner releases it (through the final fast, santhārā) and continues into the next moment with the discipline intact. The soul is what crosses; the body is what is shed. The closing formula tti bemi — "thus I declare" — is the standard closing of an āgama section, putting the seal of the speaker on the teaching just given. It marks the end of this chulika; and since this chulika is the last section of the entire Dashvaikalik Sutra, tti bemi here also marks the end of the whole sutra. Ten chapters of monastic conduct, two chulikas of crowning instruction — all are sealed with this single phrase. The entire teaching is laid down with the parting word: tti bemi — "thus I declare."

Simply Put: The soul must be guarded continuously — by senses brought into well-composed mastery. An unguarded soul falls into low rebirths; a well-guarded soul is freed from every sorrow. Thus I declare.
