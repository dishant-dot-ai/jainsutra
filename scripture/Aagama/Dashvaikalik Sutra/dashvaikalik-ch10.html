The Tenth and Culminating Adhyayan — Such a One Is a True Monk. Twenty-one verses each ending with the refrain 'sa bhikkhū' — 'such a one is a (true) monk.'

Adhyayan 10: Sa-Bhikṣu

About This Adhyayan

The Definition by Living Example

The tenth adhyayan is one of the most beautiful in all Jain literature. It defines the true monk not by abstract characteristics but by twenty-one concrete examples — each verse naming a specific quality, a specific discipline, a specific relationship to the world, and concluding with the unforgettable refrain sa bhikkhū — 'such a one is a (true) monk.'

Each verse paints a portrait: the one steady in celibacy is a true monk; the one restrained in alms is a true monk; the one equanimous in suffering is a true monk; the one who has set down anger, pride, deceit, and greed is a true monk. The chapter culminates the entire Dashvaikalik by gathering, in twenty-one images, every quality the previous nine chapters have built — and showing them all alive in a single human being. To read the chapter is to receive a living mirror of what the path produces.

The Sutras of Adhyayan 10

10.1

Steadfast in Celibacy

णिक्खम्म माणाइ य बुद्धवयणे, णिच्चं चित्तसमाहिओ हविज्जा । इत्थीण वसं ण याऽवि गच्छे, वंतं णो पडियाइ जे स भिक्खू ॥१॥

The one who, having renounced (the household) by the command (of the Jina) and (taken refuge) in the words of the Awakened Ones, remains always with a mind settled in equanimity; who never falls under the sway of women; who does not return to vomited (renounced) sense-pleasures — such a one is a (true) monk.

The chapter opens with the foundation of monastic life — brahmacharya, the great vow of celibacy and inner restraint. The verse stitches together three clauses, each one a test. First: the monk has gone forth by the command (āṇā) of the Bhagavan, not from worldly disappointment or escape — the renunciation must be rooted in the Jina's instruction, not in sorrow or pique. Second: the mind must be constantly settled in equanimity — not occasionally, not under favourable conditions, but as a permanent disposition. Third: the renounced is not to be re-tasted. The image of vāntaṃ — vomited matter — is deliberately strong. What has been thrown up is not to be swallowed back. The monk who returns, even in thought, to the pleasures left behind has betrayed the very meaning of going forth. The placement of this verse first is significant. The chapter could have opened with non-violence or non-possession; it opens instead with celibacy and steadfastness of mind. The commentary explains that brahmacharya is the very life-breath of monastic conduct (saṃyama-prāṇa-samā brahmacharyam). Without it, the other vows cannot stand. A mind unsettled by sense-craving cannot hold non-violence, cannot hold truthfulness, cannot hold non-attachment. So the chapter begins where the practice begins — at the root of the will.

Simply Put: The true monk is one who left home not on a whim but because the Jina's words drew him out; who keeps his mind steady all the time, not just when life is easy; who never lets a woman become the centre of his attention; and who refuses to go back, even in his thoughts, to the pleasures he has already renounced. Such a one is a true monk.

10.2

Protector of the Six Classes of Beings

पुढविं ण खणे ण खणावए, सीओदगं ण पिए ण पियावए । अगणिसत्थं जहा सुणिसियं, तं ण जले ण जलावए जे स भिक्खू ॥२॥

The one who does not himself dig the earth, nor cause others to dig it; who does not himself drink raw (unboiled, sentient) water, nor cause others to drink it; who, knowing fire to be like a sharply-honed weapon, neither himself kindles it nor causes another to kindle it — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse and the next together cover the six kāyas (classes of beings) — earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and the mobile (trasa) beings. Here three are taken: earth, water, fire. The structure is navakoti in spirit — for each, the monk neither does it nor causes it. The third koti, anumodana (approval), is implied by the framing of the chapter and is to be understood throughout. The image of fire as a sunisiyaṃ sattha — a "sharply-honed weapon" — is a Jain technical phrase. The Jains uniquely teach that fire is itself a sentient body (tejaskāya), and at the same time it kills every other body it touches. So in a single act of kindling, the practitioner injures the fire-beings themselves and any earth, water, air, and vegetation beings caught in its path. To kindle fire is to wield a blade against five categories of life at once. This is why the Jain monk neither cooks nor lights lamps nor warms himself — every fire is a weapon.

Simply Put: The true monk does not himself dig the earth, drink raw water, or kindle fire — and he does not get someone else to do these things for him either. He understands that fire in particular is like a sharpened knife: every flame cuts through five kinds of living bodies at once.

10.3

Protector of Air, Plants, and Seeds

अणिलेण ण वीए ण वीयावए, हरियाणि ण छिंदे ण छिंदावए । बीयाणि सया विवज्जयंतो, सचित्तं णाहारए जे स भिक्खू ॥३॥

The one who does not himself fan air (with a fan or the like), nor cause others to fan it; who does not himself cut green (vegetation), nor cause others to cut it; who, always avoiding (treading on) seeds (and sprouted things), does not consume sentient (uncooked, living) food — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse completes the protection of the six kāyas. With the previous verse on earth, water and fire, and this verse on air (vāyu), vegetation (vanaspati), and the mobile (trasa) beings implicit in sacittaṃ (sentient food which includes mobile-bodied creatures), the monk who passes these two tests is one who has truly internalised the first great vow. The detail of "fanning the air" is striking. To stir the air with a fan is, in Jain reckoning, to disturb the bodies of air-beings. This is why monks do not use fans even in summer — the relief is bought at the price of injury. The detail of avoiding seeds is also pointed: a sprout is the embodied life of a vegetation-being beginning its course. To crush it underfoot is to extinguish that life. The monk walks watching the ground (īryā-samiti), choosing each step. The phrase sacittaṃ ṇāhārae — "does not eat sentient food" — is the dietary corollary. All Jain monastic food must be acchitta (not-living): boiled water, not raw; cooked vegetables, not raw; food prepared by householders for themselves, not specially made for the monk. The verse seals the picture: the true monk's body, every step and every meal, is held within the discipline of ahimsā.

Simply Put: The true monk does not stir the air with a fan, does not cut or have anything cut from a living plant, walks watching where seeds may have fallen so as not to crush them, and does not eat any food that is still living — that is, raw, unboiled, or uncooked. Such a one is a true monk.

10.4

Refuses Food Made For Him

वहणं तस-थावराण होइ, पुढवीतणकट्ठणिस्सियाणं । तम्हा उद्देसियं ण भुंजे, णो वि पए ण पयावए जे स भिक्खू ॥४॥

(Cooking) becomes the killing of mobile and immobile beings — those that depend on earth, grass, and wood. Therefore the monk who does not eat food specially prepared (uddiṣṭa) for him, who himself neither cooks nor causes another to cook — such a one is a (true) monk.

This gatha gives the reason the monk eats only what was already prepared for the householder's own family. Cooking — the lighting of fire, the use of water, the cutting of vegetables, the heating of grain — destroys earth-beings, water-beings, fire-beings, air-beings, vegetation-beings, and any tiny mobile beings that have taken shelter in the grain or the wood. So when food is cooked uddiṣṭa (specifically with the monk in mind), the monk is the moral cause of all that killing — even though it was the householder's hands that did it. This is the heart of the Jain bhikshachari (alms-rounds) discipline. The monk goes only to homes where food was being made anyway, takes only a little from many homes, and never accepts what was made for him. His begging is meant to leave the household's karma untouched. The verse states the principle plainly: cooking is killing of the six kāyas; therefore the monk neither does it, asks for it, nor accepts what was done on his account.

Simply Put: When food is cooked, many tiny beings are killed — beings in the earth, grass, wood, water, and fire that are used. So the true monk does not accept any food that was made specifically for him; he does not cook himself, and he does not ask anyone else to cook for him. Such a one is a true monk.

10.5

Treats Six Classes Like Himself

रोइय णायपुत्तवयणे, अत्तसमे मण्णेज्ज छप्पि काए । पंच य फासे महव्वयाइं, पंचासवसंवरे जे स भिक्खू ॥५॥

The one who, having taken delight in the words of the Son of the Jnāta clan (Mahavira), regards the six classes of living beings as equal to himself; who fully observes the five great vows; who shuts down the five inflows of karma (āśrava) — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse is the doctrinal core of the chapter. It joins three pillars: (1) faith in the Jina's word (roiya Nāyaputta-vayaṇe), (2) the foundational ethical attitude — to see every other living being as oneself (atmavat sarvabhūteṣu), and (3) the formal monastic vows (the five mahāvratas) and the formal stopping of karmic inflow (saṃvara). Faith without ethics is empty; ethics without vow-discipline is unstable; vow-discipline without saṃvara does not stop new karma. All three must hold together. The phrase attasame maṇṇejja chappi kāe — "consider the six classes of beings as equal to one's own self" — is the philosophical foundation of Jain ahimsā. The reason not to harm an ant or a leaf is not sentiment but identity: the consciousness in the ant is the same consciousness in me. To see this is to make non-violence not a rule but a recognition. The five mahāvratas are then not arbitrary prohibitions; they are the natural conduct of one who has truly seen.

Simply Put: The true monk has wholeheartedly accepted Lord Mahavira's teaching, treats every one of the six kinds of living beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile creatures) as he treats himself, fully observes the five great vows, and stops the five doors through which karma flows in. Such a one is a true monk.

10.6

Vomits the Passions, Refuses Gold

चत्तारि वमे सया कसाए, धुवजोगी हवेज्ज बुद्धवयणे । अहणे णिज्जायरूवरयए, गिहिजोगं परिवज्जए जे स भिक्खू ॥६॥

The one who always vomits out the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed); who is steadfast in the yoga of the Awakened Ones' words; who is wealthless and free of (desire for) silver and gold; who avoids the activities and company of householders — such a one is a (true) monk.

The first half names the inner renunciation — the four kashayas (passions). The image is again vāntaṃ / vamati — to vomit, to throw up. The passions are not merely to be controlled or suppressed; they are to be ejected like poison from the body. And not once; sayā — "always." Each fresh stir of anger, each fresh pulse of pride, each fresh whisper of deceit or greed must be vomited at the moment it arises. The second half names the outer renunciation. Ahaṇe — "without wealth." Ṇijjāyarūva-rayae — "free from (taste for) silver and gold." A Jain monk does not own, does not handle, does not even desire to handle gold or silver. Gihijogaṃ parivajjae — avoids the activities of householders: cooking, buying, selling, owning a home, accumulating things. The two halves match: outer renunciation without inner is hypocrisy; inner without outer is fantasy. Both must be present together. This is the rare combined picture.

Simply Put: The true monk vomits out the four poisons of anger, pride, deceit, and greed every time they arise — not just once but always. He stays steady in the Jina's teaching, owns nothing, has no taste for silver or gold, and stays clear of household activities and householder company.

10.7

Right-Seeing, Always Alert

सम्मद्दिट्ठी सया अमूढे, अत्थि हु णाणे तवे संजमे य । तवसा धुणइ पुराणपावगं, मणवयकायसुसंवुडे जे स भिक्खू ॥७॥

The one who is right-sighted (samyag-dṛṣṭi); who is always alert (un-deluded); who steadfastly abides in knowledge, austerity, and self-restraint; who shakes off (destroys) accumulated past sin by austerity; who is well-restrained in mind, speech, and body — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse names the four pillars of liberation as the Jain tradition counts them: right faith (samyag-darśana), right knowledge (samyag-jñāna), right conduct (samyag-cāritra) — here split into austerity (tapa) and self-restraint (saṃyama). Right faith is the eye that sees correctly; right knowledge is what is seen through that eye; right conduct is the action that follows from what is seen. Amūḍhe — "un-deluded" — is the technical name of one of the eight requirements (aṅga) of right belief: not to be deluded by displays of false miracles, not to be deluded by the persuasions of false teachers, and not to be deluded into ignoring the scriptures. The verse couples this with the active discipline: austerity that burns past karma, restraint of mind, speech, and body that prevents new karma. The picture is of a soul that both wipes the old slate clean and stops new chalk from being added.

Simply Put: The true monk holds the right view of reality, stays alert and is not fooled, and stands firm in knowledge, austerity, and self-control. By his austerities he burns away the karma of past wrongs, and by carefully restraining his mind, speech, and body he prevents new karma from forming.

10.8

Stores Nothing for Tomorrow

तहेव असणं पाणगं वा, विविहं खाइमं साइमं लभित्ता । होही अट्ठो सुए परे वा, तं ण णिहे ण णिहावए जे स भिक्खू ॥८॥

Likewise, having obtained food, drink, various chewables, and various savouries — the one who does not (think) "this will be of use tomorrow or the day after" and store it, nor cause another to store it (for him) — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse defines the Jain monk's daily food discipline: alms collected today must be eaten today. Nothing is set aside for tomorrow. The reason is aparigraha — non-possession — applied even to food. The very act of storing food for the morrow is an assertion of attachment to one's own continuity, an attempt to secure the future, a quiet possessiveness that the monk has formally renounced. The traditional rule extends further: even within the same day, food brought in the first watch (morning) is not to be kept for the fourth watch (evening). The bird image is given in the commentary — birds eat what they need when hungry, store nothing. The monk's life is to follow that pattern. The result is twofold: the monk is forced to depend daily on the spontaneous generosity of the householders (which keeps humility alive), and the monk's mind is freed from the calculation, "How will I eat tomorrow?" — which is itself a great burden.

Simply Put: When the monk gets food, water, snacks, or savouries on his alms-rounds, he does not think "I'll save some for tomorrow or the day after" — and he does not let anyone else save it for him either. He eats today what he received today.

10.9

Shares With Fellow Monks, Then Studies

तहेव असणं पाणगं वा, विविहं खाइमं साइमं लभित्ता । छंदिय साहम्मियाण भुंजे, भुच्चा सज्झायरए जे स भिक्खू ॥९॥

Likewise, having obtained food, drink, various chewables, and various savouries — the one who, having invited his fellow monks (to share), then eats; and having eaten, becomes absorbed in self-study — such a one is a (true) monk.

The previous verse barred the monk from storing food for tomorrow; this verse bars him from a subtler vice — eating his alms alone. The monk who returns from his rounds with good food must first invite his fellow monks to share. The technical word is chaṃdiya — to give them the choice ("please take what you wish"). Only after the offer has been made does the monk himself eat. This destroys the silent possessiveness that says "this is mine because I begged it." The verse closes with the second discipline: after eating, the monk does not lounge, does not nap, does not chat. He becomes sajjhāya-rae — absorbed in self-study. The post-meal hour is dangerous to a renunciant: full belly, contented mind, the quiet pull toward indolence. The verse insists that this very hour be turned into svādhyāya — the recitation, contemplation, questioning, and pondering of scripture. Food is taken as fuel, and the fuel is immediately put to use.

Simply Put: When the monk gets good food, water, snacks, or savouries from his alms-rounds, he first invites his fellow monks to share, then eats — and after eating, he goes straight to scriptural study and stays absorbed in it. Such a one is a true monk.

10.10

Free of Quarrelling Speech, Calm in Sense and Conduct

ण य वुग्गहियं कहं कहेज्जा, ण य कुप्पे णिहुइंदिए पसंते । संजमे धुवजोगजुत्ते, उवसंते अविहेडए जे स भिक्खू ॥१०॥

The one who does not engage in quarrel-producing talk; who, even given cause, does not become angry; who has restrained sense-organs and is calm; who is steadfast-yoked in self-restraint; who is utterly tranquil and does not show contempt (for anyone) — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse moves from food to speech and disposition. Four marks are named. Na vuggahiyaṃ kahaṃ — does not engage in talk that produces dispute. The Jain monastic tradition lists vikathā (distracting / harmful talk) into categories: talk about kings, kingdoms, and politics; talk about food; talk about women; talk about countries. To these the technical vyāgrahikī kathā is added — talk whose nature itself breeds quarrel ("X is a bad king," "Y is a bad community," "Z is a bad person"). The monk avoids the talk itself, not just the temper that follows. Na kuppe — does not get angry even when given cause. The provocation may come, but the response does not. Nihui-indie pasaṃte — senses gathered in, mind tranquil. Dhuvajogajutte — steadfastly engaged in the disciplines (study, contemplation, alms, scripture). Avihedae — does not look down on or scorn others, even those who scorn him. The cumulative picture is of a man whom no provocation can ruffle, no insult can goad, no idle word can pull off course. The verse is a portrait of upaśama — the cooled-down, tempered, steady soul.

Simply Put: The true monk does not engage in talk that starts quarrels, does not lose his temper even when provoked, keeps his senses calm and his mind peaceful, stays steadily yoked to his discipline, remains utterly tranquil, and does not look down on anyone.

10.11

Bears Insult, Beating, and Terror Equally

जो सहइ हु गामकंटए, अक्कोसपहारतज्जणाओ य । भयभेरवसद्दसप्पहासे, समसुहदुक्खसहे य जे स भिक्खू ॥११॥

The one who endures the village-thorns (the painful sense-stimuli — harsh words and the like); who endures abuse, beating, and threats; who, on hearing terrifying sounds and demonic laughter, (does not flinch); who bears pleasure and pain with equal mind — such a one is a (true) monk.

The "village-thorns" (grāma-kaṇṭaka) is a Jain technical phrase for the unpleasant sensory stimuli that a monk meets in inhabited places — the rough words of children, the contemptuous looks, the foul smells, the harsh sounds. They are called "thorns" because, like thorns in the foot, they prick the inner stillness of the practitioner. The verse names four levels of disturbance: ordinary sense-thorns; deliberate abuse, blows, threats; supernatural terrors (demonic sounds in lonely meditation places); and the alternation of pleasure and pain. The monk is to endure all four with equanimity — samasuha-dukkha-sahe. Pleasure is to be borne; pain is to be borne. The verse does not say "endure pain bravely while enjoying pleasure" — it says bear them equally, treat them equally. This is the sign of one who has internalised that both are passing and neither defines the soul. The classic example given by the commentary is the muni Khandhakṛṣi, whose body was being skinned by the king's order while his mind remained settled in equanimity — an extreme case, but the verse demands the same orientation in every encounter.

Simply Put: The true monk bears the small thorns of village life — harsh words, ugly sights, rude noises — without complaint. He bears abuse, beating, and threats. Even when terrifying sounds or demonic laughter come at him in lonely places, he does not flinch. And he holds pleasure and pain in the same hand.

10.12

Stands in Cremation-Ground Meditation, Free of Bodily Craving

पडिमं पडिवज्जिया मसाणे, णो भीयए भयभेरवाइं दिस्स । विविहगुणतवोरए य णिच्चं, ण सरीरं चाभिकंखए जे स भिक्खू ॥१२॥

The one who, having undertaken the standing-meditation (pratimā) in a cremation ground, does not become afraid even on seeing terrifying things; who is always devoted to the various virtues and austerities; and who has no longing (even) for the body itself — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse moves from passive endurance (previous gatha) to active austerity. The monk who, for the great wearing-away of karma, deliberately takes himself to a masāṇa — a cremation ground, a desolate forest, a haunted ruin — and there stands or sits in kāyotsarga meditation for a fixed time, neither moving nor speaking, no matter what appears, embodies the full freedom from bodily attachment. The image of bhaya-bheravāiṃ — fear-inspiring forms, demon-howls, sudden touches of jackals — is the test. Most beings would flee. The monk who has truly let go of the body does not flee, because there is nothing left to protect. The closing line — ṇa sarīraṃ cābhikaṃkhae — "does not crave / desire the body" — is the deeper teaching. The monk does not even crave to keep his body intact. He is willing to lose it. The body is the instrument of the soul's discipline; once the soul has used it for liberation, it is not to be clung to. This is what makes the meditation in the cremation-ground possible: the practitioner has already, in mind, let the body go.

Simply Put: The true monk goes to a cremation ground, takes a vow of standing meditation, and stays there motionless. Even when terrifying things appear — strange sounds, frightening shapes — he does not get scared. He is constantly devoted to virtue and austerity, and he does not even cling to his own body.

10.13

Bears Beating Like the Earth Bears the Footstep

असइ वोसट्ट चत्तदेहे, अक्कुट्ठे व हए व लूसिए वा । पुढवि समे मुणी हवेज्जा, अणियाणे अकोउहल्ले जे स भिक्खू ॥१३॥

The one who has, again and again, given up his body (in kāyotsarga); whether abused, struck, or wounded — the one who remains like the earth (bearing all without recoil); who is without (binding) wishes (nidāna) and without curiosity (about the cause of his suffering) — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse pushes the picture further than the previous one. There the monk endured terrifying sounds in a graveyard. Here he endures abuse, blows, even being cut. The image of pṛthvī-sama — like the earth — is one of the great Jain similes. The earth bears every footstep without complaint; the earth bears every kind of treatment — flowers planted, refuse dumped, blood spilled — without preference. The monk under blow and insult is to be like that. The earth does not retaliate; the earth does not feel injured; the earth simply continues to be earth. Two further qualifications guard against subtle distortions. Aṇiyāṇe — without nidāna — means he is not enduring the suffering with a hidden wish: "May this earn me wealth or pleasure in some future life." Such a wish would chain the austerity to a future trap. Akouhalle — without curiosity — means he does not even wonder, "Why am I being beaten? What past karma caused this?" The wondering itself would be a subtle attachment to one's own story. The monk simply bears, without protest and without inquiry. The earth bears the footstep without asking whose foot it was.

Simply Put: Again and again the monk gives up his body in motionless meditation. Even when he is abused, hit, or wounded with weapons, he remains as steady as the earth — which bears every footstep without complaint. He has no hidden wish that his suffering should bring him some future reward, and he is not even curious about why this is happening to him.

10.14

Crosses the Ocean of Birth and Death

अभिभूय कायेण परीसहाइं, समुद्धरे जाइपहाओ अप्पयं । विइत्तु जाईमरणं महब्भयं, तवे रए सामणिए जे स भिक्खू ॥१४॥

The one who, having conquered the (twenty-two) hardships through the body; who lifts his own self up out of the path of (re-)birth; who, knowing birth-and-death to be a great fear, is absorbed in austerity and in monastic life — such a one is a (true) monk.

This gatha names the goal toward which all the previous gathas point — samuddhare jāipahāo appayaṃ: lifting the self out of the path of (repeated) birth. The twenty-two parīṣahas (hardships) listed in the Jain tradition — hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect-bites, nakedness, displeasure with monastic life, women, walking, posture, sleeping-place, abuse, beating, begging, lack of alms, illness, grass-bed, dirt, honour, knowledge, ignorance, doubt — are not random sufferings but the recognised tests of monastic life. To bear them all, kāyena (with the body itself, not just with intellectual acceptance), is to use the body as the very instrument of liberation. The phrase jāī-maraṇaṃ mahabbhayaṃ — "birth and death is a great fear" — is the proper Jain reorientation of fear. The worldly being fears death; the monk has learned that the cycle of births and deaths is what is to be feared, not death within it. To be absorbed in austerity (tave rae) and in monastic discipline (sāmaṇie) is not penance for its own sake but the active dismantling of the cycle. Each parīṣaha endured wears down the karma that would otherwise bring another birth.

Simply Put: The true monk has used his very body to conquer the hardships of monastic life — hunger, thirst, cold, heat, and the rest. Knowing that the endless cycle of birth and death is the greatest fear, he lifts his own soul out of that cycle and stays absorbed in austerity and the disciplined life of a monk.

10.15

Restrained in Hands, Feet, Speech, Senses

हत्थसंजए पायसंजए, वायसंजए संजइंदिए । अज्झप्परए सुसमाहियप्पा, सुत्तत्थं च वियाणइ जे स भिक्खू ॥१५॥

The one who is restrained in hands, restrained in feet, restrained in speech, restrained in the senses; who is absorbed in the inner self (adhyātma); who is well-composed in mind; who knows the meaning of the scriptures — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse turns from the great battles (parīṣahas, cremation-ground meditation) to the small daily disciplines on which they rest. Hattha-saṃjae — careful with the hands. The monk does not wave his hands carelessly; whatever is to be set down is set down with full awareness; whatever is to be picked up is picked up after the spot has been examined for tiny living beings (pratilekhana). Pāya-saṃjae — careful with the feet. He walks watching the ground (īryā-samiti) so as not to crush life. Vāya-saṃjae — careful with speech. He speaks only what is true and beneficial. Saṃjaiṃdie — careful with the senses, not letting them wander toward objects. To these four bodily disciplines is added the inner discipline: ajjhapparae — absorbed in adhyātma, the science and meditation of the soul; susamāhiyappā — well-composed; and suttatthaṃ ca viyāṇai — knowing the meaning of the scriptures, not just their words. The verse therefore pictures the monk as a being whose every limb, sense, mind, and study is gathered into a single focused practice. Restraint of the body and absorption in the soul together complete the picture.

Simply Put: The true monk is careful with his hands, careful with his feet, careful with his speech, careful with his senses. He is absorbed in the study and meditation of the soul, has a calm and composed mind, and truly understands the meaning of the scriptures.

10.16

Unattached to Equipment, Free of All Holdings

उवहिम्मि अमुच्छिए अगिद्धे, अण्णायउंछं पुलणिप्पुलाए । कयविक्कयसण्णिहिओ विरए, सव्वसंगावगए य जे स भिक्खू ॥१६॥

The one who is un-infatuated and ungreedy with respect to monastic equipment; who collects (his alms) a little at a time from unknown houses; who takes only the most ordinary (uninviting) things; who is abstinent from buying, selling, and storing; who has gone beyond every kind of attachment — such a one is a (true) monk.

This verse turns to aparigraha — non-possession — applied even to the few items the monk is permitted to hold. The Jain monk's upadhi is small: a set of robes, a bowl, a broom, a face-cloth, books. But the verse warns that even these few items can become objects of attachment. Amucchie — un-infatuated; agiddhe — ungreedy. The monk who fusses over his bowl, who is partial to his particular robe, who hoards books — has fallen back into possession even within renunciation. The food clauses sharpen the picture. Aṇṇāya-uṃchaṃ — a little (uñcha) at a time, from unknown (ajñāta) houses, where no preparation has been made for the monk and where the monk is not personally known. Pulaṇippulāe — taking only what is most insubstantial — not the rich choice items but the lightest, most ordinary, most easily-spared offerings. The monk is not to take anything that the householder might prize. Finally, kaya-vikkaya-saṇṇihio virae — utterly abstinent from any commercial activity (buying, selling) and from accumulation. The closing line — savva-saṃgāvagae — "gone beyond every attachment" — gathers the verse: the true monk is one who is free even from the few things he holds.

Simply Put: The true monk is not infatuated with his few possessions and is not greedy for more. He goes for alms to houses where he is unknown, taking only a little from each, and only the most ordinary items — never the choice things. He never buys, sells, or stores anything. He has gone beyond every kind of attachment.

10.17

Free of Greed, Honour, and Worldly Affection

अलोल भिक्खू ण रसेसु गिज्झे, उंछं चरे जीवियं णाभिकंखे । इड्ढिं च सक्कारण पूयणं च, चए ठियप्पा अणिहे जे स भिक्खू ॥१७॥

The unagitated monk who is not greedy for tasty foods (rasas); who lives by uñcha (a-little-at-a-time) alms-rounds; who does not crave (dishonest) life; who renounces (lust for) wealth, honour, and worship; who is established in himself; who is free of (worldly) affection — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse names two parallel renunciations: of rasa (taste) and of yaśas (honour). The monk is alola — unagitated — toward the six tastes. He eats what comes; he does not seek out the sweet or the savoury; he does not refuse what is bland. Uṃchaṃ care — he wanders taking only small portions from many houses, never burdening any one home, never being able to predict in advance what his meal will be. Jīviyaṃ ṇābhikaṃkhe is the deeper test: he would not stay alive at the cost of his discipline. If unrighteous food were the only food available, he would refuse, even at the cost of life. The second renunciation is from the spiritual hazard of success. Iḍḍhi — material prosperity (donations, large gatherings); sakkāra — public honour and titles; pūyaṇa — ritual worship by laypeople. These come naturally to a successful monk and are exactly what destroy a successful monk. The Jain tradition recognises them as one of the most dangerous tests precisely because they come dressed as encouragements. The Sūyagaḍāṃga sūtra warns: "Take all the worship of the world as a great thorn in the foot — handle it with the utmost care, lest the subtle spike lodges itself within and is impossible to remove." The monk who is aṇihe — free of worldly affection — and ṭhiyappā — established in his own true self — is not pulled out of his discipline by either taste or honour.

Simply Put: The true monk has no longing for tasty food and is not greedy for any of the six tastes. He goes from house to house taking only a little, and would rather not stay alive at all than stay alive by breaking his discipline. He gives up the desire for wealth, honour, and worship. He stays anchored in himself and is free of worldly affections.

10.18

Speaks No Ill, Is Not Provoked, Knows Each Soul's Karma

ण परं वइज्जासि अयं कुसीले, जेण च कुप्पिज्ज ण तं वइज्जा । जाणिय पत्तेयं पुण्णपावं, अत्ताणं ण समुक्कसे जे स भिक्खू ॥१८॥

The one who does not say of another, "this one is of bad conduct"; who does not speak that which would make another angry; who, knowing that each (being's) merit and demerit (karma) is its own, does not exalt himself — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse turns to two of the most subtle traps of advancing practice — para-ninda (criticising others) and atma-praśaṃsā (self-exaltation). They are paired because they are the same defect from two angles. The monk who has begun to develop real discipline naturally notices the laxity of others; the temptation rises to label them "kuśīla" — slack, corrupt. The verse forbids this not only because it injures the other but because it fattens the speaker's own ego. Ṇa taṃ vaijjā — say nothing that would make another angry — extends the rule beyond direct criticism to any provoking speech. The remedy given is profound: jāṇiya patteyaṃ puṇṇa-pāvaṃ — "knowing that each soul has its own particular merit and demerit." Each being acts according to the rise (udaya) of its own karma. The lapse you see in another is not for you to judge — it is the working out of karma you cannot see. To label is to assume a knowledge you do not have. And once this is understood, attāṇaṃ ṇa samukkase — one does not raise oneself up. For if I am keeping the discipline well, that too is the rise of my own particular karma; it is not a personal achievement to be boasted about. The Sūyagaḍāṃga is paraphrased: "The contempt and rejection of others is utterly fruitless." The monk steady in equanimity neither casts down others nor raises up himself.

Simply Put: The true monk does not call another person bad, and does not say anything that would provoke another to anger. He understands that every soul has its own particular karma rising — what looks like another's fault is really their karma working out — so he never lifts himself up by putting others down.

10.19

Free of Pride in Birth, Looks, Gain, or Knowledge

ण जाइमत्ते ण य रूवमत्ते, ण लाभमत्ते ण सुएण मत्ते । मयाणि सव्वाणि विवज्जइत्ता, धम्मज्झाणरए जे स भिक्खू ॥१९॥

The one who is not intoxicated by (high) birth, nor by beauty, nor by gain, nor by scriptural learning — having abandoned every kind of pride (mada), is absorbed in righteous meditation (dharma-dhyāna) — such a one is a (true) monk.

The Jain tradition lists eight madas (pride-intoxications): pride of birth (jāti), pride of family (kula), pride of physical strength (bala), pride of wealth (aiśvarya), pride of beauty (rūpa), pride of austerities (tapa), pride of learning (śruta), and pride of gains/success (lābha). This verse names four — birth, beauty, gain, learning — and abbreviates the rest with mayāṇi savvāṇi — "all the prides." Even one of these intoxicants is enough to derail spiritual progress; the verse demands the renunciation of every one. The list is striking. Pride of beauty is recognisably worldly. But pride of learning — the monk who knows the scriptures and is silently pleased with his knowledge — and pride of austerity (named in the larger list) — the monk who fasts severely and is silently pleased with his fasting — are the dangers of advanced practice. They look like merit but are themselves karma-binding. The verse closes with the positive: dhamma-jjhāṇa-rae — absorbed in dharma-dhyāna. The mind that is full of dharma-meditation has no room for the swelling of pride; the antidote is occupation. The contemplative gathered in the contemplation of dharma does not have time to admire himself.

Simply Put: The true monk is not intoxicated by high birth, by his good looks, by his successful alms-rounds, or by his scriptural learning. He has given up every kind of pride and is absorbed in the meditation on dharma. Such a one is a true monk.

10.20

Teaches the Pure Doctrine, Avoids Bad Conduct, Refuses Buffoonery

पवेयए अज्जपयं महामुणी, धम्मे ठिओ ठावयइ परं पि । णिक्खम्म वज्जेज्ज कुसीललिंगं, ण याऽवि हास कुहए जे स भिक्खू ॥२०॥

The great muni who proclaims the noble word (the pure dharma); who, himself established in dharma, establishes others also (in it); who, having gone forth, avoids the marks of slack conduct; who does not engage in joking and clowning — such a one is a (true) monk.

The verse complements verse 18 (which forbade criticising others). There the monk was told not to put others down. Here he is told to actively raise others up — paveyae ajjapayaṃ, proclaiming the noble teaching. Having stood himself in dharma (dhamme ṭhio), he sets others also in it (ṭhāvayai paraṃ pi). This is the positive teaching mission: a true monk does not merely cultivate his own liberation; he becomes an instrument by which others are drawn to the path. But this teaching mission has dangers, and the verse names two. First, the monk who teaches becomes admired, gathers followers, and is tempted to relax his discipline a little — what is called śithilācāra or kusīla-liṅga (the visible marks of laxity). The verse insists: in proclaiming the dharma, the monk's own conduct must remain tight. Second, the public teacher is tempted to become entertaining — to tell jokes, to perform "wonders," to amuse the crowd. Hāsa — laughter-talk; kuhae — clowning, magical tricks (the kuhaka tradition that uses sleight-of-hand or claimed miracles to attract crowds). The verse forbids both. The monk teaches the dharma, not himself; and he teaches by the gravity of truth, not by the lightness of entertainment.

Simply Put: The great monk preaches the noble teaching. Standing firm in dharma himself, he establishes others in dharma too. Once he has gone forth, he avoids the visible signs of slack conduct, and he does not engage in joking or clowning around for entertainment.

10.21

Conclusion: Reaches the State of No-Return

तं देहवासं असुइं असासयं, सया चए णिच्चहियट्ठियप्पा । छिंदित्तु जाईमरणस्स बंधणं, उवेइ भिक्खू अपुणागमं गई ॥२१॥ ॥त्ति बेमि॥

That dwelling-in-the-body — impure and impermanent — the (monk), established in his own eternal welfare (the soul), always renounces (attachment to it). Having severed the bondage of birth and death, the monk attains the goal of no-return (the liberated state). — So I say.

The chapter closes with a single comprehensive verse that gathers everything before it into the goal. The body (dehavāsa) — described frankly as asui (impure: full of bone, sinew, fat, pus, sweat, excretions) and asāsaya (impermanent: a momentary water-bubble, a flash of lightning, a dewdrop on grass-tip) — is to be relinquished. Not destroyed (the body is the instrument of practice and must be guarded as long as it serves the practice), but de-identified-with. The monk does not love his body, does not nurture it for its own sake, does not cling to its preservation when it can no longer help. Ṇiccahiyaṭṭhiyappā — the soul established in nitya-hita, in eternal welfare — is the inner condition. The soul's true welfare is not the body's comfort but the body's release. From this firm inner footing the monk severs (chiṃdittu) the bondage of birth-and-death — the long chain that has dragged the soul from one body to the next through immeasurable time. And the result is apuṇāgamaṃ gaī — the state of no-return. The Siddhakshetra. The summit of the universe. The eternal abode of pure consciousness. Once attained, never lost; once entered, never to be born again. The chapter therefore ends where the Sutra began — at liberation. Each of the twenty-one verses has been a portrait of the one who attains it. The closing tti bemi — "so I say" — is the traditional seal of the Ganadhara: this is what was heard from the Bhagavan, this is what is being faithfully passed on. The chapter proper of the Dashvaikalik ends here.

Simply Put: That dwelling in the body — which is full of impurities and bound to perish — the true monk always renounces his attachment to. Settled instead in the eternal welfare of his own soul, he cuts through the bondage of birth and death, and reaches the state from which no soul ever returns. So I say.
