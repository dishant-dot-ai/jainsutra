Rativākyā રતિવાક્યા

The First Chulika — the Speech Against Falling Back. A moving plea for steadfastness using eighteen similes for the sufferings of the householder life and the joys of liberation.

Chulika 1: Rativākyā

About This Chulika

The Plea Against Wavering

The two Chulikas (appendices) of the Dashvaikalik are not original to the sutra; tradition holds they were added later when specific spiritual needs arose. The first Chulika, Rativākyā ('the speech of restraint against falling back'), is said to have been composed when a wavering monk was on the verge of returning to householder life.

The Chulika offers eighteen vivid similes — each one a comparison between the apparent comfort of the world and its hidden suffering, or between the apparent austerity of the monastic path and its hidden joy. By the end, the trembling monk is meant to see clearly that what looked like loss in renunciation is actually gain, and what looked like gain in worldly return is actually loss. The text is short but emotionally powerful.

The Sutras of Chulika 1

Prakrit original with English translation, commentary, and the simple explanation.

1.1

The Eighteen Contemplation-Points (Prose Sutra)

इह खलु भो ! पव्वइएणं उप्पण्णदुक्खेणं संजमे अरइसमावण्णचित्तेणं, ओहाणुप्पेहिणा अणोहाइएणं चेव, हयरस्सि गयंकुस पोयपडागाभूयाइं, इमाइं अट्ठारसट्ठाणाई सम्मं संपडिलेहियव्वाई भवंति; तं जहा— हं भो ! दुस्समाए दुप्पजीवी ॥१॥ लहुस्सगा इत्तरिया गिहीणं कामभोगा ॥२॥ भुज्जो य साइबहुला मणुस्सा ॥३॥ इमे य मे दुक्खे ण चिरकालोवट्ठाई भविस्सइ ॥४॥ ओमजणपुरक्कारे ॥५॥ वंतस्स य पडियायणं ॥६॥ अहरगइवासोवसंपया ॥७॥ दुल्लहे खलु भो ! गिहीणं धम्मे, गिहीवासमज्झे वसंताणं ॥८॥ आयंके से वहाय होइ ॥९॥ संकप्पे से वहाय होइ ॥१०॥ बंधे गिहवासे, मोक्खे परियाए ॥११॥ सोवक्कसे गिहवासे, णिरुवक्कसे परियाए ॥१२॥ सावज्जे गिहवासे, अणवज्जे परियाए ॥१३॥ बहुसाहारणा गिहीणं कामभोगा ॥१४॥ पत्तेयं पुण्णपावं ॥१५॥ अणिच्चे खलु भो ! मणुयाण जीविए, कुसग्गजलबिंदुचंचले ॥१६॥ बहुं च खलु भो ! पावं कम्मं पगडं ॥१७॥ पावाणं च खलु भो कडाण कम्माणं पुव्विं दुच्चिण्णाणं दुप्पडिक्कंताणं, वेइत्ता मोक्खो, णत्थि अवेइत्ता, तवसा वा झोसइत्ता ॥१८॥ अट्ठारसमं पयं भवइ। भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो—

Here, indeed, O (disciples)! For the one who has gone forth, in whom suffering has arisen, whose mind has fallen into displeasure with the discipline, who is contemplating return (to the household) but has not yet actually returned — for such a one, like the bit for the horse, the goad for the elephant, the rudder-flag for the ship, these eighteen contemplation-points should be properly examined; namely:— (1) Ah O (soul)! In this evil age, life is hard to live. (2) The household sense-pleasures are insubstantial and short-lived. (3) Moreover, men are full of much deceit. (4) "This suffering of mine will not last forever." (5) (In the household) one must humble oneself even before lowly people. (6) (Returning) is like swallowing what was vomited. (7) (Returning) is the embrace of a low-rebirth dwelling. (8) Hard indeed, O! is dharma for householders, for those living in the midst of the household. (9) Sickness arises for the householder's destruction. (10) Painful mental constructions (anxieties) arise for the householder's destruction. (11) The household is bondage; the (monk's) life-state is liberation. (12) The household is full of distress; the (monk's) life-state is free of distress. (13) The household is sin-filled; the (monk's) life-state is sin-free. (14) Household pleasures must be shared with many. (15) Each soul's merit and demerit is its own. (16) Impermanent indeed, O! is human life — trembling like a water-drop on the tip of a kuśa-grass blade. (17) I have, indeed, done much sinful karma. (18) Of (those) sinful karmas already done, badly behaved in the past, badly atoned for — there is liberation only by enduring (their fruit), not by avoiding it; or else by burning them up through austerity. — This is the eighteenth point. And here there is a verse:—

This sutra is the heart of the chulika and addresses, with extraordinary directness, the wavering monk. The opening sentence diagnoses the condition: a monk has been initiated; suffering has arisen in his life; his mind has fallen into arati (displeasure) with the discipline; he is now contemplating return to the household but has not yet actually broken the vow. This is exactly the moment the chulika addresses. Not the monk who has fallen, but the monk who is on the brink. To him, the sutra offers the bit, the goad, the rudder. The eighteen points are then a structured contemplation. They fall into rough groups. (1)–(7) compare the state being contemplated (return to the household) to the state being held (the monastic discipline) and find the householder's life wanting on every count: hard living in this evil age; pleasures small and brief; companions deceitful; one's own suffering not actually permanent; one would have to bow to lowly people; the act of return is itself like swallowing one's own vomit; it is the embrace of a low-rebirth abode. (8)–(13) make the philosophical case: dharma is hard to practice in the household; sickness and anxieties strike the householder for his destruction; the household is bondage / sorrow / sin-stained; the monastic life-state is liberation / freedom from sorrow / sin-free. Points (14)–(18) drive deeper. (14): household pleasures are not yours alone — you share them with kings, thieves, employees, family — so the satisfaction is divided. (15): each soul faces its own karma; you cannot share that. So returning to the household to seek pleasure does not solve your karmic situation. (16): human life itself is fragile — a water-drop on a kuśa-grass tip, ready to fall at any breath of wind. (17): I have already done much sinful karma. (18): and there is no escape from karma except by enduring its fruit or by burning it through austerity. The implicit conclusion is irresistible: since karma must be faced anyway, face it where the burning can occur — in the discipline, not in the household where new karma is being added every hour. The eighteen points are not abstract doctrine but a meditation script. The chulika prescribes this script for the moment of crisis. The Sutra acknowledges, more honestly than almost any text in the tradition, that the path is hard and that the practitioner will sometimes want to leave it. Its response is not condemnation but contemplation. Sammaṃ saṃpaḍilehiyavvāiṃ — "to be examined well." Sit with these eighteen, the Sutra says; let them work on you; and when they have done their work, you will not return.

Simply Put: When a monk's heart has filled with sorrow, he has come to dislike the discipline, and he is starting to think about going back to household life — though he has not actually broken his vows yet — there are eighteen contemplation-points he should examine carefully. These are like the bit that steers a horse, the goad that steers an elephant, the rudder-flag that steers a ship. They are: (1) life in this evil age is hard; (2) household pleasures are tiny and brief; (3) people are full of deceit; (4) "this suffering of mine will not last forever"; (5) in the household one has to bow even to small people; (6) going back is like swallowing your own vomit; (7) going back is the embrace of a low-rebirth life; (8) practising dharma as a householder is very difficult; (9) sickness comes to destroy the householder; (10) anxious thoughts come to destroy him; (11) the household is bondage, monastic life is liberation; (12) the household is full of distress, monastic life is free of it; (13) the household is sin-filled, monastic life is sinless; (14) household pleasures are shared with many; (15) each soul's karma is its own; (16) human life is as fragile as a water-drop on a grass-tip; (17) "I have already done a lot of sinful karma"; (18) the only way out of karma is to endure its fruit or burn it with austerity. Sit with these eighteen.

1.2

The Ignorant One Who Trades Discipline for Pleasure

जया य चयइ धम्मं, अणज्जो भोगकारणा । से तत्थ मुच्छिए बालो, आयइं णावबुज्झइ ॥१॥

When the ignoble one (an-ārya) gives up dharma for the sake of (sense) enjoyment, he, being there infatuated, the fool — does not understand the future (consequences).

The first verse of the gatha-portion frames the entire problem in a single picture. A monk who returns to the household for the sake of sense-pleasure is, despite whatever learning he may have, aṇajja — an-ārya, ignoble. The Jain tradition's use of ārya is not racial but ethical: an Ārya is one whose conduct is governed by discriminative wisdom, an Anārya is one whose conduct is not. To give up the great vow for the small reward of sense-pleasure is by definition the work of someone whose discrimination has failed. The verse calls such a one bāla — a child, a fool. The Jain technical sense of bāla is not chronological youth but spiritual immaturity. A child puts whatever is in front of it into its mouth without thought of consequence; the bāla monk grasps at sense-pleasure without thinking of āyai — the future, his own welfare, the dignity of his soul. The verse thus diagnoses the inner state of the one who returns: it is not that he has reasoned and decided; it is that he has been infatuated (mucchie) and has lost the capacity to consider. The chulika is therefore aimed at restoring discrimination before infatuation can take hold.

Simply Put: When an ignoble person gives up the dharma for the sake of sense-pleasures, he becomes infatuated with those pleasures, and like a foolish child he simply does not see what is coming next.

1.3

Like Indra Fallen From Heaven

जया ओहाविओ होइ, इंदो वा पडिओ छमं । सव्वधम्मपरिब्भट्ठो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥२॥

When (the monk) has fled (the discipline) — like Indra fallen to the earth — that one, fallen from all dharma, afterwards burns with regret.

The next four verses give four similes for the fallen monk. The first is the most exalted: Indra, king of the gods, who lives in the celestial realms in inconceivable splendour. When his merit is exhausted and he must take rebirth on earth as a human, the contrast between what he was and what he has become tortures him. The verse uses this image for the monk who has abandoned the discipline. The fallen monk in his new life will sometimes look back and remember what he was — the great muni, the disciplined one, the bearer of the great vows — and the contrast will burn him. The verse subtly insists that there is no escape from this remorse. The decision to leave looks, in the moment, like relief. But pacchā — afterwards — the burning comes. The chulika is thus an appeal not only to the seeker's wisdom but to his self-interest: do not do this thing, because afterwards you will burn. The four similes that follow elaborate the same pattern.

Simply Put: When a monk runs away from the discipline — like Indra falling to the earth from heaven — he loses every part of his dharma, and afterwards he burns with regret.

1.4

From Worshipped to Despised

जया य वंदिमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अवंदिमो । देवया व चुया ठाणा, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥३॥

When (the monk) was once worthy of salutation, but afterwards becomes unworthy of salutation — like a deity fallen from her station — he afterwards burns with regret.

The second simile turns to social stature. As a monk, the practitioner was bowed to by the world. Lay people came for darshan, asked for blessing, regarded him with reverence. As one fallen from the discipline, the same world now turns its face. He has become an object not of reverence but of disregard — even of mild contempt — for he is the one who could not hold his vow. The verse compares this to a devī — a goddess, perhaps Indrāṇī herself — who, when her merit exhausts and she must descend to the human realm, loses the worship of all the lesser deities who once bowed to her. The fall is doubly painful: the loss of the height, and the awareness of the bowing now denied.

Simply Put: When a monk who used to be worshipped becomes someone people no longer salute — like a goddess fallen from her station in heaven — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.5

From King to Dethroned

जया य पूइमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अपूइमो । राया व रज्जपब्भट्ठो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥४॥

When (the monk) was once worthy of worship (pūjā), but afterwards becomes unworthy of worship — like a king fallen from his kingdom — he afterwards burns with regret.

The third simile sharpens the second. The previous verse spoke of the loss of vandana (salutation); this verse speaks of the loss of pūjā (formal worship — the offering of flowers, lamps, water at the feet). As a monk, even kings would come to make pūjā at his feet. As a fallen monk, even ordinary people withdraw their offerings. The simile is the king who is dethroned. He keeps the memory of the throne; he keeps the remembered taste of being bowed to; but the throne is gone. Every commoner who once approached on his knees now passes him without a glance. The pain of the dethroned king is sharper than the pain of one who never had a throne.

Simply Put: When a monk who was once formally worshipped becomes someone no one worships any more — like a king fallen from his kingdom — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.6

From Honoured Merchant to Beggar

जया य माणिमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अमाणिमो । सेट्ठिव्व कब्बडे छूढो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥५॥

When (the monk) was once held in honour (māna), but afterwards becomes unhonoured — like a great merchant (śreṣṭhin) cast into a wretched village (kabbaḍa) — he afterwards burns with regret.

The fourth simile is mercantile. A great merchant, accustomed to a wealthy city's commerce, festivals, society, and stature, is one day cast (perhaps by misfortune, perhaps by his own folly) into a poor backwater village. He still has the memory of the city. He still has the bearing of one who was great. But in this village no one knows him; no one cares for his stature; he is just one more poor man. The verse uses this image for the loss of māna — the honour formerly enjoyed. The previous three similes used celestial / royal / governmental images; this one uses the everyday image of the wealthy merchant who has lost his standing. The point is the same: what was once is no longer, and the contrast is what burns. The four similes together — Indra fallen, goddess descended, king dethroned, merchant beggared — serve to flood the wavering monk's imagination with the afterwards. Whatever the imagined pleasure of return, this is what awaits.

Simply Put: When a monk who was once held in great honour becomes one no one honours — like a great merchant who has been cast into a wretched little village — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.7

Old Age Caught on the Hook

जया य थेरओ होइ, समइक्कंतजुव्वणो । मच्छुव्व गलं गिलित्ता, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥६॥

When (the fallen monk) becomes old, his youth gone past — like a fish that has swallowed the hook — he afterwards burns with regret.

The fifth simile shifts from social fall to bodily decline. The monk who left the discipline in youth, drawn by sense-pleasure, eventually grows old. His youth has passed. His body is no longer fit for the pleasures that lured him. But he is now too far from the discipline to return to it, and the householder's life — which only made sense as a vehicle for those pleasures — has nothing left for him. The image is the fish that, attracted by the small piece of meat on the hook, swallows the hook itself. The momentary pleasure of the meat is followed by being torn from the water and dying. The momentary pleasure of the abandoned discipline is followed by old age in which the soul is impaled, unable to enjoy and unable to return. The Jain commentary notes the precision of the simile: the fish does not see the hook, only the bait. The fallen monk did not see the old age, only the immediate pleasure. By the time the hook is felt, it is too late.

Simply Put: When the fallen monk grows old and his youth is gone — like a fish that has swallowed the angler's hook (lured by a tiny bait, now caught and dying) — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.8

Bound Like an Elephant in Family Troubles

जया य कुकुडुंबस्स, कुतत्तीहिं विहम्मइ । हत्थी व बंधणे बद्धो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥७॥

When (the fallen monk) is afflicted by the wretched anxieties of a wretched family — like an elephant bound in chains — he afterwards burns with regret.

Simply Put: When the fallen monk is afflicted by the petty worries of a troubled family — like an elephant captured and bound in chains — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.9

Surrounded by Family, Like an Elephant Stuck in Mud

पुत्तदारपरिकिण्णो मोहसंताणसंततो । पंकोसण्णो जहा नागो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥८॥

Surrounded by sons and wife, continuously enmeshed in the chain of delusion — like an elephant sunk in mud — he afterwards burns with regret.

The seventh simile is the famous image of the thirsty elephant. In the burning days of summer, when the lakes are nearly dry, the great elephant comes to a shrinking pool. The water that remains is too far across the wide expanse of mud at the lake's edge. The elephant, desperate for water, steps into the mud believing he will reach the water; but with each step the mud grows deeper, and finally he is stuck — unable to reach the water, unable to climb back to the bank. There he stands, exhausted, dying of thirst within sight of the water that drew him in. This is the picture of the fallen monk surrounded by the family he came back to enjoy. The wife, the son, the household — these were the water he reached for. The mud he is sunk in is the moha-saṃtāna — the unbroken chain of delusion that comes with these attachments. He cannot enjoy them as he hoped (the water never reached); he cannot return to the discipline (the bank is no longer accessible). He stands stuck, in growing despair. The chulika's images grow steadily darker as it forces the wavering monk to see the afterwards.

Simply Put: Surrounded by his wife and children, continuously bound in the chain of delusion — like a thirsty elephant who stepped into the muddy edge of a shrinking lake and is now stuck, unable to reach the water and unable to climb back — he afterwards burns with regret.

1.10

"I Could Have Been an Acharya"

अज्जाहं गणी हुंतो, भाविअप्पा बहुस्सुओ । जइ हं रमंतो परियाए, सामण्णे जिणदेसिए ॥९॥

"Today I would have been an Acharya (gaṇī), a soul ripened by contemplation, deeply learned in scripture — if I had remained delighting in the monastic life of śramaṇa-hood as taught by the Jina."

This is one of the most heart-wrenching verses in the chulika because it places the burning regret in the mouth of the fallen monk himself. The verse is in the first person: "Today, I would have been an Acharya, I would have been a soul ripened by contemplation, I would have been deeply learned." The "if" makes it lethal: jai haṃ ramaṃto — "if I had only remained." The fallen monk imagines, in the household, what he might now be. He sees in his mind the great Acharya he could have become; the great learning he could have mastered; the great contemplative ripening he could have attained. Instead, he is here, in a household, with nothing. The verse is constructed with deliberate cruelty. It does not preach. It simply lets the fallen monk hear his own future regret. The chulika's pastoral wisdom shows itself: it is not telling the wavering monk what others have suffered; it is letting him hear himself, twenty years from now, lamenting the choice he is contemplating today.

Simply Put: "Today I would have been an Acharya — a soul deeply ripened by meditation, deeply learned in the scriptures — if I had only stayed in the monastic life that the Jina taught." This is what the fallen monk says to himself, looking back.

1.11

The Same Discipline: Heaven for Some, Hell for Others

देवलोगसमाणो उ, परियाओ महेसिणं । रयाणं अरयाणं तु, महानरयसारिसो ॥१०॥

For the great seers (mahā-ṛṣis) who delight (in it), the monastic state is like the world of the gods (devaloka); but for those who do not delight (in it), it is like the great hell (mahā-naraka).

This verse offers a profound psychological observation. The same monastic discipline — the same fasting, the same study, the same alms-rounds, the same celibacy — is devaloka (heaven) for one practitioner and mahā-naraka (great hell) for another. What makes the difference is not the discipline itself but the inner orientation: rata (delight in it) versus arata (no delight in it). The monk whose mind has truly fallen in love with the discipline experiences each austerity as joy; the monk whose mind has not — for whom the discipline is mere obligation — experiences the same austerities as torment. The teaching is both diagnostic and prescriptive. Diagnostically: if your monastic life feels like hell, the problem is not the monastic life — it is your relation to it. Prescriptively: cultivate rati (delight). When delight is restored, the same path becomes the path to bliss. The Bhagavati Sutra is paraphrased in the commentary: a monk in his first month already exceeds the bliss of certain lower deities; in his first year, exceeds the bliss of all the heavens including the Anuttara Vimanas. This is not poetic exaggeration; it is a description of the inner state of one who has found delight in the discipline.

Simply Put: For the great sages who genuinely delight in the discipline, the monastic life is like heaven itself. But for those who have lost delight in it — who keep it only as a burden — that very same monastic life feels like the great hell.

1.12

Therefore, Cultivate Delight in the Path

अमरोवमं जाणिय सुक्खमुत्तमं, रयाणं परियाए तहा अरयाणं । नरओवमं जाणिय दुक्खमुत्तमं, रमिज्ज तम्हा परियाय पंडिए ॥११॥

Knowing (the bliss of those who delight in the discipline to be) the highest joy, like that of the immortals (gods); and (knowing the suffering of) those who do not delight in the discipline to be the highest suffering, like (that of) hell — therefore the wise one (paṇḍita) should delight in the monastic state.

This verse draws the conclusion of the previous one as direct counsel. Having seen that the same monastic life produces god-like bliss for those who delight in it and hell-like suffering for those who do not, the paṇḍita (the wise one) is given the imperative: ramijja — "delight in it." The wisdom here is not abstract. The same conditions produce opposite experiences depending on inner attitude. Therefore the practitioner is to cultivate the inner attitude that produces the bliss. The verse uses the word paṇḍie — wise — to emphasize that this is not arbitrary will but considered understanding. The wise one, having seen the two outcomes clearly, simply chooses the one that leads to bliss.

Simply Put: Knowing that for those who delight in monastic life it brings the highest joy — like that of the gods — and knowing that for those who do not delight in it, it brings the highest suffering — like that of hell — the wise one should therefore cultivate delight in the monastic state.

1.13

The Fallen Monk Despised Like a Defanged Snake

धम्माउ भट्ठं सिरिओववेयं, जण्णग्गिविज्झायमिवप्पतेयं । हीलंति णं दुव्विहियं कुसीला, दाढुड्ढियं घोरविसं व नागं ॥१२॥

The one fallen from dharma, devoid of (spiritual) fortune, dim of radiance like a yajña-fire that has gone out — that misconducted (monk) is despised even by people of bad conduct (kusīla), like a venomous snake whose fangs have been pulled out.

Two parallel similes carry this verse. First: the yajña-agni — the sacred ritual fire. While it burns, even the wealthy come to feed it ghee and honey, bow before it, regard it with reverence. The moment it is extinguished, all of that ends. The cold ash on the altar is dishonoured by the same hands that worshipped the flame. Second: the nāga — the cobra — whose fangs are full of venom. Everyone fears it. But once its fangs are pulled, even children play with it; it is despised, kicked, mocked. This is the picture of the fallen monk. While he held the discipline, he was the burning fire that received the worship of all and the great cobra that even kings approached with care. Once he has fallen — once his fangs (his discipline) are gone, once his fire (his austerity) is extinguished — even the lowly, even the ill-conducted, despise him. The verse uses the cutting phrase kusīlā hīlaṃti — "even kuśīlas (people of bad conduct) despise him." The point: people who themselves have no discipline still look down on a monk who has lost his. The fall is universal in its disgrace.

Simply Put: The one who has fallen from dharma — emptied of spiritual splendour, dim like a yajña-fire that has gone out — even people of bad conduct despise that ill-conducted monk. He is like a venomous cobra whose fangs have been pulled: once feared, now mocked.

1.14

Loss of Reputation, Birth in Low Realms

इहेव धम्मो अयसो अकित्ती, दुण्णामधिज्जं च पिहुज्जणम्मि । चुयस्स धम्माउ अहम्मसेविणो, संभिण्णवित्तस्स य हेट्ठओ गई ॥१३॥

For the one fallen from dharma, given over to a-dharma, whose conduct is shattered: in this very life — un-righteousness, dis-fame, dis-repute, an evil name among the common people; and afterwards, a low rebirth (heṭṭha-gati).

The verse counts the costs of the fall in two columns. In this life: dis-fame (ayaśa), dis-repute (akīrti), an evil name (durnāma) among the common people. Lay people who pass each other on the street will say, "Did you hear? That one took the great vow and then came back. What a coward — what a hypocrite." His name itself becomes a curse-word. In the next life: heṭṭha-gati — a downward rebirth, descent into lower realms (animal, hell, lower human). The accumulated bad karma of returning to violence and greed and untruth and desire — having known better — has fruit. The verse insists on both consequences. The fall is not just internal; it is reputational here, and karmic hereafter.

Simply Put: For the one who has fallen from dharma, given himself over to a-dharma, with his conduct broken: in this very life he gets accumulated bad karma, ill-fame, ill-repute, and an evil name on every common tongue. And after this life — a rebirth in the low realms.

1.15

Pleasure Now, Suffering Later, Bodhi Lost

भुंजित्तु भोगाइं पसज्झ चेयसा, तहाविहं कट्टु असंजमं बहुं । गई च गच्छे अणहिज्झियं दुहं, बोही य से णो सुलहा पुणो पुणो ॥१४॥

Having intensely enjoyed sense-pleasures with mind given over to them, having committed many acts of un-restraint of various kinds — he goes to a destination (gati) that is undesired and full of suffering; and bodhi (right awakening) does not come easily to him, again and again (in many births).

The verse traces the karmic chain. The fallen monk, having returned to enjoy sense-pleasures with full attachment (pasajjha cetasā), commits many acts of un-restraint. These acts produce karma. The karma sends him in the next life to an undesired destination — animal birth, lower realms, painful human birth. And — most devastating — bohī ya se ṇo sulahā puṇo puṇo — "and bodhi does not come easily to him, again and again." That is, the rare opportunity of right awakening, which he had received in this life and threw away, does not return easily in subsequent lives. He may circle the wheel of samsara for many births before another opportunity arises. This is the sharpest blade of the chulika. The chance you have now — to be a human being, with the Jina's teaching available, with the discipline before you — is itself the result of immense good karma. To throw it away for momentary pleasure is to lose not just this opportunity but to make the next opportunity exceedingly hard to recover.

Simply Put: After enjoying sense-pleasures with all his attention given over to them, and committing many sinful acts of un-restraint, the fallen monk goes to an undesired destination full of suffering. And the rare gift of right awakening — which he once had — does not come easily to him again, even across many lives.

1.16

Compared to Hell, This Discipline's Suffering Is Brief

इमस्स ता णेरइयस्स जंतुणो, दुहोवणीयस्स किलेसवत्तिणो । पलिओवमं झिज्जइ सागरोवमं, किमंग पुण मज्झ इमं मणोदुहं ॥१५॥

For this hell-being (nāraka-jīva), brought into suffering, full of distress — even the palya-of-time and sāgara-of-time (immense ages) eventually run out. So what indeed (kim aṅga) is this little mental suffering of mine (in the discipline) compared to that?

This verse and the next are the chulika's direct address to the wavering monk in the moment of his suffering. The argument is mathematical and brutal. Hell-beings (nāraka) in the lower hells suffer for palyopamas and sāgaropamas — measures of time so vast they are computed by analogy (a palyopama is the time it would take to empty a vast pit of finely-cut hairs at one hair every hundred years; a sāgaropama is countless palyopamas). And yet — jhijjai — even those vast measures eventually run out. The torment of hell, however long, is not infinite; it ends. So, the verse asks: kim aṅga puṇa majjha imaṃ maṇoduhaṃ — "how much more so, how trifling, is this mental suffering of mine?" My current discomfort in the discipline is not a palyopama. It is not a sāgaropama. It is at most a few decades of mental restlessness. If the unimaginable suffering of hell ends, surely this brief inconvenience will end. The verse uses the most extreme comparison precisely to bring perspective: your present suffering, however acute it feels, is small.

Simply Put: Even the hell-being, dragged into suffering and living a life of utter distress — even his torment, measured in unimaginable ages of palyopamas and sāgaropamas, eventually ends. So how trivial, by comparison, is this little mental discomfort of mine in the discipline?

1.17

My Suffering Will Pass, My Cravings Will Pass, My Body Will Pass

ण मे चिरं दुक्खमिणं भविस्सइ, असासया भोगपिवास जंतुणो । ण चे सरीरेण इमेण अविस्सइ, अविस्सइ जीवियपज्जवेण मे ॥१६॥

This suffering of mine will not last long. The thirst for pleasures, in any (embodied) creature, is not eternal (it too will end). And if (this suffering) does not depart with this very body, then it will certainly depart at the end of (this) life.

The seventeenth verse is the chulika's logical and consoling closing. It instructs the wavering monk in the inner argument he should make to himself in the moment of his discomfort. Ṇa me ciraṃ dukkhamiṇaṃ bhavissai — "this suffering of mine will not last long." Karma matures and exhausts; suffering that arises will fade. And — asāsayā bhoga-pivāsa jaṃtuṇo — the thirst for sense-pleasures that drives me to abandon the discipline is itself impermanent. It is a fever; it will break. It will not last forever; it lasts only as long as youth, and even within youth, only when fed. The closing line is the masterstroke: ṇa ce sarīreṇa imeṇa avissai, avissai jīviya-pajjaveṇa me — "if (this suffering) does not depart with this body, it will certainly depart at the end of life." Nothing lasts beyond the body. The body itself will end. So whatever suffering, whatever craving, whatever discomfort you are now bearing, it has at most this body's lifetime. The body itself is a water-bubble (verse 16 of the prose sutra). To break a great vow for a temporary craving in a temporary body is to trade the eternal for the momentary. The chulika thus closes with the most reasonable argument it can offer: wait. The suffering will pass; the craving will pass; and even if neither passes, both will end with the body — which itself will end. The chapter ends here with this counsel. The Bije Chulika (next file) takes up a different theme — the conduct of solitary monks.

Simply Put: This suffering of mine will not last long. The thirst for sense-pleasures in any embodied creature is not permanent — it too will pass. And even if my current suffering does not leave with this very body, it will certainly leave at the end of this life. Either way, it will end.
