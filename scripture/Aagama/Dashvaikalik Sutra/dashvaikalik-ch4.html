The Fourth and Most Central Adhyayan — the Six Classes of Living Beings. The doctrinal heart of the Dashvaikalik: earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile beings — every drop, every spark, every blade alive.

Adhyayan 4: Ṣaḍ-Jīvanikāya

About This Adhyayan

The Universe Is Full of Souls

The fourth adhyayan is the longest and most central chapter of the Dashvaikalik — and arguably of all Jain ethics. It announces that the universe contains six classes of living beings: pṛthvī-kāya (earth-bodies), ap-kāya (water-bodies), tejas-kāya (fire-bodies), vāyu-kāya (air-bodies), vanaspati-kāya (plant-bodies), and trasa-kāya (mobile beings). Each handful of soil, each drop of water, each spark, each gust, each blade of grass holds aneka jīva — countless individual souls. Modern science has only recently confirmed what Jain darshan asserted twenty-five centuries ago.

From this metaphysical foundation flows the entire monastic ethic: the five mahāvrats (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession), the prohibition against night-eating, and the six yatana sutras — one per kāya — teaching the monk how to walk, eat, drink, sit, sleep, and breathe without harming the lives that fill the world. The chapter closes with verses on yatana and ayatana, the importance of knowledge, and the spiritual development arc.

The Sutras of Adhyayan 4

Prakrit original with English translation, commentary, and the simple explanation.

4.1

The Opening Declaration (Utthanika)

सुयं मे आउसं ! तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं, इह खलु छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता, सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती

I have heard, O long-lived one! Thus was it declared by that Bhagavan: in this very Jain teaching, an Adhyayan named Chh Jeevanikaya (Six Classes of Life) has been proclaimed, well-spoken, and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, of the Kashyapa lineage. Beneficial it would be for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

This is the opening sutra (utthanika) of the chapter — the formal declaration by Sudharma Swami to his disciple Jambu Swami that what follows has been heard directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. The phrasing "suyaṃ me āusaṃ" — "I have heard, O long-lived one" — is the standard opening of every Anga Agama. It establishes two things at once. First, it asserts the unbroken chain of oral transmission: Sudharma is not authoring; he is reporting. Second, it expresses Sudharma's own humility — he does not claim direct revelation, only faithful hearing. The disciple is addressed as āusaṃ — "long-lived one" — a blessing as much as a name. Of all the qualities a disciple may have (good conduct, sharp intellect, faith, devotion), longevity is singled out because only the long-lived can carry the teaching forward to the next generation. By calling Jambu āusaṃ, Sudharma is implicitly entrusting him with the future of the shasan. The chapter's two names appear in this very sutra. Chh Jeevanikaya ("Six Classes of Life") names the content — the six categories of beings that the chapter enumerates and protects. Dhammapannatti ("Proclamation of Dharma") names the function — the chapter is the formal announcement of what Jain monastic dharma actually is. To know this chapter is to know dharma; to follow it is to live dharma.

Simply Put: Sudharma tells his disciple Jambu: "Listen — I heard this directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. He taught a chapter called the Six Classes of Life, also known as the Proclamation of Dharma. It will be good for you and for me to study it."

4.2

The Disciple's Question

कयरा खलु सा छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती ?

What, then, is that Adhyayan called Chh Jeevanikaya (Six Classes of Life), proclaimed by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage, well-spoken and well-expounded — the Adhyayan named Dhammapannatti (Proclamation of Dharma) which it would be beneficial for me to study?

This sutra is the disciple's response — the request to be told what exactly the Six Jeevanikaya Adhyayan contains. Jambu does not simply say "tell me"; he repeats the entire formula, including the lineage of transmission, the name of the speaker, and the name of the chapter. This is not redundancy but reverence: the disciple's question reproduces the teacher's framing word for word, signalling that he has received the introduction with full attention and is ready for the body of the teaching. The Jain Agama style of question-and-answer mirrors a real classroom: the teacher introduces, the student requests, and the teacher then expounds. This sutra is the formal pivot between introduction and exposition.

Simply Put: Jambu replies to Sudharma: "Tell me — what exactly is this Six Classes of Life chapter that Bhagavan Mahavira taught and that you say would be good for me to study?"

4.3

The Teacher's Confirmation

इमा खलु सा छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं, समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती

This — what is now to be told — is that very Adhyayan called Chh Jeevanikaya, proclaimed and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage. It would be beneficial for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

This is Sudharma's transition sutra — his confirmation that what follows is the chapter Jambu has just asked about. The repeated full formula is again not redundancy but a teaching method: in oral transmission, the boundaries of a section must be clearly marked so that the listener knows when introduction ends and exposition begins. With this sutra, the introduction closes; the body of the chapter opens with sutra 4 (the enumeration of the six kayas).

Simply Put: Sudharma confirms: "Yes — what I am about to tell you now is exactly that chapter. It is good for both of us to study it."

4.4

The Five Sthavar (One-Sensed Beings)

तं जहा– पुढविकाइया, आउकाइया, तेउकाइया, वाउकाइया, वणस्सइकाइया, तसकाइया । पुढवी चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । आऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । तेऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वाऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वणस्सई चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । तंजहा– अग्गबीया, मूलबीया, पोरबीया, खंधबीया, बीयरुहा, संमुच्छिमा तणलया, वणस्सइकाइया, सबीया चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं

The six are these: (1) earth-bodied beings (puḍhavikāiyā), (2) water-bodied beings (āukāiyā), (3) fire-bodied beings (teukāiyā), (4) air-bodied beings (vāukāiyā), (5) plant-bodied beings (vaṇassaikāiyā), (6) mobile beings (tasakāiyā). Earth has been declared as having consciousness (cittamanta). It is many-souled, each soul having its own separate existence — except where it has been transformed by shastra (a destroying force). Water has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Fire has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Air has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Plant-life has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. These are the kinds of plants: (1) those whose seed is at the tip (aggabīyā — like coriander), (2) those whose seed is in the root (mūlabīyā — like the lotus rhizome), (3) those whose seed is in the joint or node (porabīyā — like sugarcane, bamboo), (4) those whose seed is in the trunk (khaṃdhabīyā — like the banyan, fig), (5) those that grow from a seed (bīyaruhā), (6) those that arise spontaneously (saṃmucchimā) — the various grasses and creepers (taṇalayā), the plant-bodied beings, the seed-bearing — all are declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra.

This sutra is the doctrinal foundation of the entire chapter — and arguably of all Jain ethics. It announces, on the authority of the omniscient (sarvajna) Bhagavan Mahavira, that earth, water, fire, air, and plants are not inert matter but living, conscious beings. Each is cittamanta — endowed with consciousness. Each handful of soil, each drop of water, each spark, each gust, each blade of grass holds aneka jiva — countless individual souls — and each soul has its own prithak satta, its own separate existence. They are not a collective; each is an "I." Modern science has only recently been able to confirm what Jain darshan asserted twenty-five centuries ago: a single drop of water holds thousands of microorganisms; a particle of soil teems with life. But Jain darshan goes further still — it holds that the very water itself, the very soil itself, the very fire itself is alive at the level of the substance. Each one-sensed being possesses only the sense of touch (sparshendriya); it cannot see, hear, smell, or taste, and it cannot move of its own accord. Its consciousness is dimmest. Yet it is consciousness, and therefore the soul within it is identical in essence to the soul in a human being — only more deeply veiled by karma. The phrase "aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ" — "except where transformed by shastra" — is crucial. Shastra here means a "destroying agent" — anything that has acted on the kaya to render it non-living (achit). Cooked water is no longer a living water-body; baked clay is no longer a living earth-body; extinguished fire is no longer a living fire-body. The monk may use these achit substances; what he must absolutely avoid is harming or using sachit (still-living) substances. This single phrase is the entire foundation of Jain monastic alms-rules: monks accept only prasuk (cooked, processed, lifeless) food; they drink only boiled water; they walk on already-trodden paths; they sit only where the surface has been cleaned of life. The plant section adds the eight categories of vegetation, organised by where the seed (the locus of new growth) resides. The classification is botanical-ethical: the monk who knows where life is concentrated knows where his harm could fall heaviest, and acts accordingly. The phrase "puḍhosattā" — "each separately existing" — is also doctrinally precise. The vegetable kingdom contains both pratyek (each soul in its own body, like a banana plant) and sadharan (countless souls sharing a common body, like the potato or onion). For both kinds, the souls are individual; but in the sadharan vanaspati, an enormous number of souls share a single physical body. This is why traditional Jain ethics is especially strict about avoiding root vegetables — to harm one is to harm uncountable lives.

Simply Put: There are six categories of living beings in this universe. Five of them are immobile and one-sensed (they have only touch, no sight, hearing, smell, or taste, and they cannot move): earth, water, fire, air, and plants. Each handful of earth, each drop of water, each spark of fire, each puff of wind, and each piece of vegetation contains countless individual souls — each one a separate "I." They are alive until something destroys them and turns them lifeless. The sixth category — the mobile beings — will be described in the next sutras. (Note: this sutra mentions all six in its opening list but then only describes the five sthavar in detail; the sixth, trasa, gets its own full sutra.)

4.5

The Sixth: Trasakaya (Mobile Beings)

से जे पुणे इमे अणेगे बहवे तसा पाणा, तंजहा– अंडया पोयया जराउया रसया संसेइमा संमुच्छिमा उब्भिया उववाइया जेसिं केसिं च पाणाणं अभिक्कंतं, पडिक्कंतं, संकुचियं, पसारियं, रुयं, भंतं, तसियं, पलाइयं, आगइगइविण्णाया, जे य कीडपयंगा जा य कुंथुपिपीलिया सव्वे बेइंदिया सव्वे तेइंदिया सव्वे चउरिंदिया सव्वे पंचिंदिया सव्वे तिरिक्खजोणिया सव्वे णेरइया सव्वे मणुया सव्वे देवा, सव्वे पाणा परमाहम्मिया । एसो खलु छट्ठो जीवणिकाओ तसकाउ त्ति पवुच्चइ

Now there are these many mobile beings (trasa), namely: (1) those born from eggs (aṃḍaja — birds, snakes), (2) those born without an outer covering (potaja — elephants, certain mammals born live), (3) those born wrapped in a placental membrane (jarāyuja — humans, cows, buffalo), (4) those born from spoiled liquid (rasaja — beings that arise in spoiled milk, ghee), (5) those born from sweat (saṃsedaja — lice, ticks), (6) those born from spontaneous coalescence (saṃmurchima — flies, mosquitoes), (7) those that emerge by breaking through the earth (udbhija — locusts, grasshoppers), (8) those born by sudden manifestation (aupapātika — gods and hell-beings, who manifest in their full form within a single muhurta). These are beings that move forward, move backward, contract, stretch out, make sounds, become agitated, take fright, flee, and that have knowledge of coming and going. These include worms, butterflies, ants, lice; all two-sensed beings, all three-sensed beings, all four-sensed beings, all five-sensed beings; all animal-yoni beings, all hell-beings, all humans, all gods. All these beings are paramā-dhārmika (deeply suffering / devoutly desiring of happiness). This, then, is the sixth class of life — declared to be trasakaya (the mobile-bodied beings).

This is the sixth and final category — the trasakaya, the mobile beings. While the five sthavar were grouped in a single previous sutra, the trasakaya gets its own dedicated sutra because of its extraordinary internal diversity: it includes everything from a worm to a god. The defining mark is the same throughout — the ability to move of one's own accord, to approach what one wants and avoid what one fears. The sutra lists the eight modes of birth (yoni) for trasa beings — a remarkable taxonomy. Some of these (egg-born, placenta-born) are immediately recognisable; others (sweat-born, spontaneously-coalescent) reflect the older theory of spontaneous generation, which Jain darshan held even for beings that modern biology now traces to other sources. The two extraordinary categories are udbhija (those that break out of the earth — beetles, locusts) and especially aupapātika (gods and hell-beings, who do not pass through any womb but manifest in full form on a celestial bed or in a hell-pot within a single muhurta). Then comes the sutra's emotional heart — the nine activities that distinguish the trasa being from the immobile sthavar: moving, retreating, contracting, stretching, crying out, becoming agitated, taking fright, fleeing, knowing the way back home. These are not abstract markers but visible signs of inner experience. A worm contracts when touched; a dog flees when struck; a child cries when frightened; a lost cow finds its way back to its master. Each activity is the outward sign of an inward soul. To witness these activities and still treat the being as object — to swat the fly, kick the dog, harm the cow — is the most direct possible violation of dharma. The final phrase "savve pāṇā paramāhammiyā" is theologically rich and the commentaries give it two readings. The Churni reads it as paramaduhkhita — "all beings are supremely suffering." The Tika reads it as paramadharmika — "all beings are supremely desirous of dharma / of happiness." The two readings are not in conflict: every being suffers, and precisely because every being suffers, every being yearns for happiness. This single phrase is the foundation of Jain compassion. The monk does not protect beings out of duty; he protects them out of recognition that they, like him, are simply beings trying to escape pain.

Simply Put: The sixth class of life is the mobile beings — everything that can move on its own. They are born in eight different ways: from eggs, from wombs (with or without placenta), from spoiled liquids, from sweat, from spontaneous coalescence, by emerging from the earth, or by sudden manifestation (gods and hell-beings). They move toward what they want and away from what they fear; they cry, they freeze, they flee, they find their way home. They include every kind of insect, animal, human, hell-being, and god. And every single one of them — without exception — is suffering, and longing for happiness.

4.6

The Comprehensive Vow of Six-Kaya Restraint

इच्चेसिं छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं णेव सयं दंडं समारंभिज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं दंडं समारंभाविज्जा, दंडं समारंभंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणिज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि, ण कारवेमि, करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणु- जाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि

Toward these six classes of living beings — I will not myself initiate harm; I will not have others initiate harm; nor will I approve of others who initiate harm. For as long as I live, by all three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and all three yogas (mind, speech, body) — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, I will not cause to be done, nor will I approve of another who does it. From that (past wrongdoing), O Lord, I turn back; I censure it; I confess it before my Guru; I let go of (that) sinful self.

This is the comprehensive vow — the centrepiece of the chapter and the foundational pratigya of all Jain monastic life. Here, having heard the description of all six classes of beings, the practitioner makes the formal pledge: he will not harm any of them, in any way, by any means, for the rest of his life. The structure is the navakoti — the nine-fold restraint — built from three karanas (doing, causing, approving) multiplied by three yogas (mind, speech, body). To plug all nine doors at once is to seal the soul against the entry of himsa-janya karma. Note the parallel with the Karemi Bhante of the Avashyak Sutra: the vow-formula is structurally identical — same nine-koti formula, same four-fold pratikraman ending (paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi). The difference is the content: where the Avashyak vow renounces savajja jog (sinful activity in general), the Dashvaikalik vow renounces specifically the daṃḍa (harm-causing activity) directed at the six classes of beings. This is the moment when the abstract idea of ahimsa becomes a concrete, measurable, enforceable commitment. The four-step pratikraman closing — paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi — is the same psychological progression we saw in the Avashyak chapter. Paḍikkamāmi: I turn back from the past wrongdoing. Ṇiṃdāmi: I see it as wrong before my own inner witness. Garihāmi: I confess it openly to my Guru, breaking the protective shell of pride. Appāṇaṃ vosirāmi: I release identification with the part of me that committed it. Without this fourth step, the commentators say, no purification is final — to regret a wrong while still considering it part of one's identity is to keep the karma bound. The vow is therefore not merely about future restraint but about a complete release of past harm.

Simply Put: The practitioner formally vows before the Guru: "Toward all six categories of living beings — earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile beings — I will not commit harm myself, will not ask others to commit harm, and will not approve when others commit harm. For my whole life. By mind, by speech, by body. And whatever harm I have already done, I turn from it, I see it as wrong, I confess it openly, and I release the part of me that did it."

4.7

The First Mahavrat: Pranatipata Viraman (Renunciation of Killing)

पढमे भंते ! महव्वए पाणाइवायाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! पाणाइवायं पच्चक्खामि । से सुहुमं वा बायरं वा तसं वा थावरं वा, णेव सयं पाणे अइवाएज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं पाणे अइवायावेज्जा, पाणे अइवायंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि, तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । पढमे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ पाणाइवायाओ वेरमणं

In the first Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of pranatipata (killing of living beings); O Lord, I renounce all killing of beings. Whether subtle or gross, mobile or immobile — I will neither kill beings myself, nor cause others to kill beings, nor approve of others who kill beings. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, I will not cause to be done, nor will I approve of one who does. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure it; I confess it; I let go of (that) sinful self. In the first Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all killing of beings.

This is the first and most important of the five Mahavrats. Pranatipata literally means "the destruction of pranas" — the ten life-forces by which a living being lives: the five sense-faculties, the three powers (mind, speech, body), the breath, and the lifespan itself. To kill is to separate one or more of these from a being. The vow is total: it covers the subtle and the gross (any size of being), the mobile and the immobile (any of the six kayas), and it closes all nine doors (three karanas × three yogas). Why is this vow placed first? Because himsa — violence — is the root from which all other sins grow. Lying, stealing, sexual misconduct, and possessiveness are all, in a sense, forms of violence (against truth, against property, against another's body, against the soul's freedom). Renounce killing in its full breadth, and the rest follow. The phrase "pranatipata viraman" rather than the more common "ahimsa" is itself instructive. Ahimsa is a state — non-harm. Pranatipata viraman is an act — the active turning-away from harm. The Mahavrats are formulated not as states to be admired but as acts to be performed, again and again, breath after breath. This is why the vow ends with "uvaṭṭhiomi" — "I take my stand" — present tense, active, ongoing.

Simply Put: The monk's first great vow is total non-killing: "Whether the being is tiny or large, moving or still, I will not kill it, will not have it killed, will not approve when it is killed — by my mind, my speech, or my body — for the rest of my life. Whatever harm I have caused before, I turn from it. I now stand in this vow."

4.8

The Second Mahavrat: Mrishavada Viraman (Renunciation of Falsehood)

अहावरे दोच्चे भंते ! महव्वए मुसावायाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! मुसावायं पच्चक्खामि । से कोहा वा लोहा वा भया वा हासा वा णेव सयं मुसं वइज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं मुसं वायावेज्जा, मुसं वयंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । दोच्चे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ मुसावायाओ वेरमणं

Now next, in the second Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of mrishavada (falsehood); O Lord, I renounce all falsehood. From anger, from greed, from fear, from laughter — I will neither speak falsehood myself, nor cause others to speak falsehood, nor approve of others who speak falsehood. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause to be done, nor approve of one who does. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the second Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all falsehood.

The second Mahavrat is the renunciation of all falsehood. The sutra carefully identifies the four roots from which falsehood typically arises: anger (which makes us slander), greed (which makes us deceive for gain), fear (which makes us hide), and laughter (which makes us exaggerate or mock). All other sources can be reduced to these four — or to derivatives of them (pride and deceit are forms of greed; aversion is a form of anger). To root out lying, the practitioner must root out these four passions. The commentators identify four kinds of falsehood: (1) sadbhava-nishedha — denying what exists (saying "the soul does not exist"); (2) asadbhava-udbhavana — asserting what does not exist (saying "the soul is all-pervading"); (3) arthantara — calling something by another name (calling a cow a horse); (4) garha — speech that produces hatred or hurts another's heart (calling a one-eyed person "one-eye," calling a eunuch "impotent"). The vow covers all four.

Simply Put: The monk's second vow is total truthfulness: "From anger, from greed, from fear, or from a desire to amuse — I will not lie, will not have others lie, will not approve when they lie. For my whole life, in mind, speech, and body."

4.9

The Third Mahavrat: Adattadana Viraman (Renunciation of Taking-What-Is-Not-Given)

अहावरे तच्चे भंते ! महव्वए अदिण्णादाणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! अदिण्णादाणं पच्चक्खामि । से गामे वा णगरे वा रण्णे वा अप्पं वा बहुं वा अणुं वा थूलं वा चित्तमंतं वा अचित्तमंतं वा णेव सयं अदिण्णं गिण्हिज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं अदिण्णं गिण्हाविज्जा, अदिण्णं गिण्हन्ते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । तच्चे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ अदिण्णादाणाओ वेरमणं

Now next, in the third Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of adattadana (taking-what-is-not-given); O Lord, I renounce all taking-what-is-not-given. Whether in a village, in a city, in a forest — anything of small value or great value, of small quantity or large quantity, subtle or gross, conscious (a sentient being) or non-conscious (an inanimate object) — I will neither take what is not given myself, nor cause others to take what is not given, nor approve of others who take what is not given. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the third Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all taking-what-is-not-given.

The third Mahavrat extends the principle of non-violation from beings (Mahavrat 1) and speech (Mahavrat 2) to property — but with a Jain refinement. Where ordinary morality forbids "stealing," the Jain vow forbids the broader adattadana — taking anything that has not been given. The traditional commentators identify five kinds of "what has not been given": (1) Deva-adatta — what the Tirthankara has prohibited, (2) Guru-adatta — what the Guru has not permitted, (3) Raja-adatta — what the king/state has not authorised (entering a kingdom without royal permission, etc.), (4) Grihapati-adatta — what the householder has not offered (taking even a straw without explicit permission), (5) Sadharmik-adatta — what a fellow monk has not consented to (using another monk's bowl without asking). The breadth is striking: in a village, in a city, in a forest; small thing, large thing; living being or inanimate object. Even a single blade of grass picked from a forest is adattadana if no one has given it. This is why a Jain monk asks permission before sitting in any space, before drawing water, before plucking even a leaf — and accepts only what is freely offered.

Simply Put: The monk's third vow is total non-taking: "Whether in a village, a city, or a wilderness — anything of any value, any size, living or non-living — I will not take what has not been given to me, will not have others take it, will not approve when others take it. By mind, speech, and body, for my whole life."

4.10

The Fourth Mahavrat: Maithuna Viraman (Renunciation of Sexual Activity)

अहावरे चउत्थे भंते ! महव्वए मेहुणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! मेहुणं पच्चक्खामि । से दिव्वं वा माणुसं वा तिरिक्खजोणियं वा णेव सयं मेहुणं सेवेज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं मेहुणं सेवावेज्जा, मेहुणं सेवंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । चउत्थे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ मेहुणाओ वेरमणं

Now next, in the fourth Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of maithuna (sexual activity); O Lord, I renounce all sexual activity. Whether with a divine being, a human being, or an animal-yoni being — I will neither engage in sexual activity myself, nor cause others to engage in sexual activity, nor approve of others who engage in sexual activity. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the fourth Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all sexual activity.

The fourth Mahavrat is total brahmacharya — celibacy in mind, speech, and body. The sutra explicitly names three categories: divine, human, and animal — to close any imaginable loophole. (One ancient ascetic order claimed that sexual activity with a deity in dream was permissible; this sutra forecloses that argument.) The Sthananga Sutra identifies four causes that produce sexual desire (maithuna sangya): (1) increase of flesh, blood, and semen in the body; (2) the rising of veda mohaniya karma (the karma that produces sexual identification — male-veda, female-veda, neuter-veda); (3) hearing or thinking of sexual matters; (4) reflecting on sexual experience. The Samavayanga Sutra prescribes nine brahmacharya fences (nava vaad) — restraints on dwelling place, conversation, posture, food, etc. — to keep these four causes from arising. The Uttaradhyayan adds ten brahmacharya samadhi-sthanas. To break any of these is to risk breaking the fourth Mahavrat itself. The eight subtle limbs of maithuna — remembrance, narration, play, gazing, secret conversation, resolve, intention, and consummation — are all renounced. The vow is not merely about the act; it is about the entire field of sexual mentality.

Simply Put: The monk's fourth vow is total celibacy: "With no being — divine, human, or animal — will I engage in sexual activity, cause others to, or approve when others do. In mind, speech, and body. For my whole life. Whatever I have done before, I turn from it now."

4.11

The Fifth Mahavrat: Parigraha Viraman (Renunciation of Possession)

अहावरे पंचमे भंते ! महव्वए परिग्गहाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! परिग्गहं पच्चक्खामि । से अप्पं वा बहुं वा अणुं वा थूलं वा चित्तमंतं वा अचित्तमंतं वा, णेव सयं परिग्गहं परिगिण्हेज्जा णेवण्णेहिं परिग्गहं परिगिण्हाविज्जा परिग्गहं परिगिण्हंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि, ण कारवेमि, करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । पंचमे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ परिग्गहाओ वेरमणं

Now next, in the fifth Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of parigraha (possession); O Lord, I renounce all possession. Anything of small value or great value, of small quantity or large quantity, subtle or gross, conscious (a sentient being like a disciple or animal) or non-conscious (an inanimate object) — I will neither take possession myself, nor cause others to take possession, nor approve of others who take possession. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the fifth Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all possession.

The fifth Mahavrat is the renunciation of parigraha — possession — and the sutra is careful to define possession with maximum breadth: small or large, valuable or trivial, sentient (a disciple, an animal) or inert (a coin, a cloth). The principle behind the breadth is the famous Dasvaikalik definition of parigraha (from Adhyayan 6): "muccha pariggaho vutto" — "attachment is what is called possession." It is not the thing but the attachment to the thing that constitutes parigraha. A monk who carries even his alms-bowl with attachment is bound; a monk who would carry a kingdom without attachment would (theoretically) be free. But because attachment so naturally attaches to objects, the safest renunciation is renunciation of the objects themselves. Tradition divides parigraha into nine outer kinds (land, houses, silver, gold, wealth, grain, servants, animals, household goods) and fourteen inner kinds (the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed; the nine nokashayas — laughter, attachment, dislike, fear, sorrow, disgust, three sexual identifications; and mithyatva — wrong belief). The monk renounces all outer parigraha completely, and undertakes the lifelong work of renouncing the inner. A separate three-fold classification is also given: sharira (body), karma (the karma matter clinging to the soul), and upadhi (the few permitted monastic implements). Even the body is, in this analysis, a form of parigraha that the soul will eventually shed.

Simply Put: The monk's fifth vow is total non-possession: "Anything small or large, valuable or trivial, living or non-living — I will not possess, will not have others possess, will not approve when others possess. By mind, speech, and body, for my whole life. The deepest possession is attachment itself, and from that too I now turn."

4.12

The Sixth Vow: Ratri-bhojan Viraman (Renunciation of Night-Eating)

अहावरे छट्ठे भंते ! वए राइभोयणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! राइभोयणं पच्चक्खामि । से असणं वा पाणं वा खाइमं वा साइमं वा णेव सयं राइं भुंजेज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं राइं भुंजाविज्जा, राइं भुंजंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविह तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । छट्ठे भंते ! वए उवट्ठिओमि, सव्वाओ राइभोयणाओ वेरमणं

Now next, in the sixth vow, O Lord, there is renunciation of ratri-bhojan (eating at night); O Lord, I renounce all night-eating. Of ashana (food), pana (drink), khadima (chewables — fruit, dry food), or swadima (savouries — mouth-fresheners) — I will neither eat at night myself, nor cause others to eat at night, nor approve of others who eat at night. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the sixth vow, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all night-eating.

The sixth vow — sometimes counted as a mahavrat, sometimes as an additional vow that completes the Mahavrats — is the total renunciation of all eating and drinking between sunset and sunrise. The four-fold list (ashana, pana, khadima, swadima) covers every form of intake, leaving no loophole. Why is night-eating singled out for vow-status? Several reasons interlock. First, ahimsa: at night countless small life-forms (insects, microorganisms) are invisible and inevitably enter food and water — to eat at night is to consume lives one cannot see. Second, digestion: traditional medicine and Jain physiology hold that the digestive fire (jathara-agni) wanes after sunset; food taken at night is poorly digested and produces ama (toxic residue). Third, sangrahaa — eating at night encourages stockpiling, which leads to other parigraha. Fourth, spiritual: the night is the time for swadhyaya, dhyana, and sleep — eating disturbs all three. For a lay practitioner, this is one of the few monastic vows commonly accepted in full — chauviharu (the four-fold pratyakhyana of all four kinds of food until sunrise) is widespread in observant Jain households.

Simply Put: The monk's sixth vow is total renunciation of night-eating: "Of food, drink, fruit, or savouries — I will not eat at night, will not have others eat at night, will not approve when others eat at night. By mind, speech, and body. For my whole life."

4.13

The Concluding Pratigya: Acceptance of the Six Vows

इच्चेयाइं पंच महव्वयाइं राइभोयणवेरमणं छट्ठाइं अत्तहियट्ठयाए उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरामि

These five Mahavrats together with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth — these I accept for the welfare of my own soul, and (so accepting them) I live (move forward in life).

This is the concluding pratigya — the formal acceptance verse that closes the vow-section of the chapter. Having heard each of the six vows expounded individually, the disciple now sums up his commitment in a single sentence: "All six together — I accept them, for my own welfare, and I live." The phrase "attahiyaṭṭhayāe" — "for the welfare of one's own soul" — is doctrinally precise. The vows are not undertaken for show, for social approval, for fear of hell, or even for compassion alone. They are undertaken because they are good for the one taking them. Liberation is the deepest self-interest a being can pursue, and the vows are the path. To take them is not sacrifice; it is the soul's most rational act. The phrase "uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ" is the technical term for receiving a vow from the Guru. The disciple does not invent the vows; he receives them, formally, from a teacher who has himself received them, in a chain reaching back to the Tirthankara. This is why the verse cannot be taken privately — it must be taken in the presence of a witness who carries the lineage. With this sutra, the charitra-dharma section (sutras 7–13) closes. What follows is the yatana section (sutras 14–19) — the practical instructions by which the vows are kept moment by moment.

Simply Put: The disciple closes the vow-ceremony with a single sentence: "These five great vows, together with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth — I accept all of them, for the good of my own soul, and I live by them."

4.G1–4.G6

The Six Ayatana Verses (Action Without Mindfulness Binds Karma)

|| ગ.૧ || अजयं चरमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥१॥ || ગ.૨ || अजयं चिट्ठमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥२॥ || ગ.૩ || अजयं आसमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥३॥ || ગ.૪ || अजयं सयमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥४॥ || ગ.૫ || अजयं भुंजमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥५॥ || ગ.૬ || अजयं भासमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ । बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥६॥

(1) One who walks without yatana harms the mobile and immobile beings. He binds sinful karma, and the fruit of that for him is bitter. (2) One who stands without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter. (3) One who sits without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter. (4) One who lies down (sleeps) without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter. (5) One who eats without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter. (6) One who speaks without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.

These six gathas form a single rhythmic teaching by repetition. The structure is identical in each verse; only the activity changes — walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, eating, speaking. These are the six fundamental physical activities that fill every waking hour. The teaching is precise: any one of them, performed without yatana, becomes a vehicle for harm and a binder of karma. The pivotal word is ajayaṃ — "without yatana." Yatana is not a feeling; it is upayoga — applied awareness, vigilant attention. Its opposite is pramaada — heedlessness, carelessness, the autopilot mode in which most of human life is lived. The Jain teaching is that even an apparently innocuous act (sitting on a chair, walking down a path, drinking water) becomes a karma-binding violence the moment awareness drops. The being one harms unknowingly is no less harmed for one's ignorance — and the karma binds whether one intended the harm or not. The fruit (phala) is described as kaḍuya — bitter. The metaphor is botanical: bitter fruit is the natural ripening of poisonous seed. Sin sown without awareness still ripens; the seed does not need the gardener's permission to grow.

Simply Put: Six verses, one teaching: walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, eating, and speaking — if any of these are done without careful awareness, you will harm beings, bind karma, and reap bitter fruit. There is no exemption for inattention; the harm is real even when the harmer is asleep at the wheel.

4.G7–4.G8

The Question and the Answer (Yatana Saves)

|| ગ.૭ || कहं चरे कहं चिट्ठे, कहमासे कहं सए । कहं भुंजंतो भासंतो, पावकम्मं ण बंधइ ॥७॥ || ગ.૮ || जयं चरे जयं चिट्ठे, जयमासे जयं सए । जयं भुंजंतो भासंतो, पावकम्मं ण बंधइ ॥८॥

(7) How should one walk, how should one stand, how should one sit, how should one lie down? How should one eat, how should one speak — so that one does not bind sinful karma? (8) Walk with yatana, stand with yatana, sit with yatana, lie down with yatana. Eat with yatana, speak with yatana — and one does not bind sinful karma.

These two gathas form a celebrated question-and-answer pair. The disciple asks (gatha 7): if every form of activity without yatana binds karma, then how am I to act at all? Must I cease walking, standing, sitting, eating, speaking — that is, cease living? The teacher answers (gatha 8): no — keep doing all of them, but do them with yatana. The same six activities that bind karma when done carelessly bind no karma at all when done with awareness. This is the Jain ethical pivot. The path is not renunciation of action but transformation of action. The same body that harms can also protect, if the awareness behind the body is awake. The single word jayaṃ (with yatana) is the entire difference between bondage and freedom. The two gathas are often quoted together as the practical heart of Jain ethics. They are recited in monastic morning prayers and held up as the operational guide to every monk's day.

Simply Put: The disciple asks: "How can I walk, stand, sit, sleep, eat, and speak without binding sin?" The answer comes back: "Do all of them — but do them with mindful awareness. With yatana, nothing you do will bind you."

4.13

Concluding Pratigya: The Purpose of the Six Vows

इच्चेयाइं पंच महव्वयाइं राइभोयणवेरमणछट्ठाइं अत्तहियट्ठयाए उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरामि

These five Mahavrats together with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth — having taken them upon myself for the welfare of my own soul, I now move forward (in monastic life).

The closing line of the Mahavrat-acceptance ritual. The disciple, having recited each of the five great vows plus the night-eating vow before the Guru, now declares the purpose and the commitment in a single sentence: "for the welfare of my own soul, I have accepted these and I now live in them." Note the radical interiority — attahiyaṭṭhayāe, "for the sake of my own soul's good." Not for fame, not for community approval, not for the gods. The vow is taken alone, even when taken in public. The verb viharāmi — "I move about" — is the standard Jain monastic verb for "I live the wandering homeless life." The vows are not a state; they are a practice that walks.

Simply Put: "These five Mahavrats — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession — together with the sixth vow against night-eating — I have now accepted them, for my own soul's good. And so I begin my life as a monk."

4.14

Yatana Toward Prithvi-kaya (Earth-Beings)

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा संजयविरयपडिहयपच्चक्खायपावकम्मे दिया वा राओ वा एगओ वा परिसागओ वा सुत्ते वा जागरमाणे वा; से पुढविं वा भित्तिं वा सिलं वा लेलुं वा ससरक्खं वा कायं ससरक्खं वा वत्थं; हत्थेणं वा पाएणं वा कट्ठेण वा किलिंचेण वा अंगुलियाए वा सिलागाए वा सिलागहत्थेण वा ण आलिहिज्जा ण विलिहिज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा; अण्णं ण आलिहावेज्जा ण विलिहावेज्जा ण घट्टावेज्जा ण भिंदावेज्जा; अण्णं आलिहंतं वा विलिहंतं वा घट्टंतं वा भिंदंतं वा ण समणुजाणिज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं; ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतं पि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि, तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि

That bhikshu or bhikshuni — restrained, withdrawn from sin, having destroyed past sinful karma, having renounced future sin — by day or by night, alone or in assembly, asleep or awake — shall not, with hand, foot, stick, bamboo-splinter, finger, iron-spike, or bundle of spikes, scratch, dig, scrape, or break: earth, wall, rock, clod, dust-covered body, or dust-covered cloth. Nor cause another to do so. Nor approve another doing so. Lifelong, by all three karanas and three yogas, by mind-speech-body, I do not, do not cause, do not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I release the sinful self.

This sutra opens the second half of Adhyayan-4: having vowed the six Mahavrats abstractly, the practitioner now applies them concretely — kaya by kaya. The sutra spells out, with characteristic Jain over-specificity, every kind of earth-body (six forms), every instrument of harm (seven), every grade of action (four), and the full nine-koti formula (3 karanas × 3 yogas). The pattern repeats almost identically for the next five sutras (4.15-4.19), one per kaya. The verbal precision is a kind of meditation: by naming every possible mode of harm, the practitioner installs a vigilance that operates beneath conscious choice. The body learns: do not scratch the earth, even idly. Note the five qualifying epithets of the monk himself: bhikshu, samyata, virata, pratihata-papakarma, pratyakhyata-papakarma — beggar, restrained, withdrawn, past-sin-destroyed, future-sin-renounced. This is a complete portrait of monastic identity in five compounds.

Simply Put: The monk vows: "I will not, with any tool — hand, foot, stick, splinter, finger, spike — scratch, dig, scrape, or break any form of living earth: soil, wall, rock, clod. Nor on a body or cloth caked with living dust. Nor will I have others do it. Nor approve when others do. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

4.15

Yatana Toward Ap-kaya (Water-Beings)

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से उदगं वा ओसं वा हिमं वा महियं वा करगं वा हरतणुगं वा सुद्धोदगं वा उदउल्लं वा कायं, उदउल्लं वा वत्थं, ससिणिद्धं वा कायं, ससिणिद्धं वा वत्थं, ण आमुसिज्जा ण संफुसिज्जा, ण आवीलिज्जा ण पवीलिज्जा, ण अक्खोडिज्जा ण पक्खोडिज्जा, ण आयाविज्जा ण पयाविज्जा; अण्णं ण आमुसावेज्जा ण संफुसावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain as in 4.6]

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not, regarding water — well-water, dew, ice, fog, hail, dewdrops on green grass, rainwater — nor regarding a body wet with such water, nor a wet cloth, nor a body still moist, nor a moist cloth — touch once or repeatedly, squeeze lightly or hard, shake once or vigorously, dry by friction or by the sun. Nor cause another to do so. Nor approve another doing so. [9-koti refrain]

The eight pairs — touch / repeated touch, squeeze / hard squeeze, shake / vigorous shake, dry / sun-dry — are notable for their granularity. Water-bodies are uniquely fragile in the Jain reckoning: a water-being can be killed by a single touch, because mere contact disturbs the water-body's integrity. Hence the doubling-up: amusijjā (touch once) is forbidden, saṃphusijjā (touch repeatedly) is also forbidden — neither is permitted, and naming both shuts off any rationalization. The list of seven water-types covers natural water in all its visible forms — including dew on grass, which a monk might idly brush past. The vow extends to a body or cloth wet with such water: until it is fully dry, the jiva-rasa still clings.

Simply Put: The monk vows: "I will not touch, squeeze, shake, or dry — even once, even gently — any form of living water: well-water, dew, ice, fog, hail, dewdrops, rain. Nor a body or cloth wet with such water. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

4.16

Yatana Toward Agni-kaya (Fire-Beings)

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से अगणिं वा इंगालं वा मुम्मुरं वा अच्चिं वा जालं वा अलायं वा सुद्धागणिं वा उक्कं वा; ण उज्जेज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा ण उज्जालिज्जा ण पज्जालिज्जा ण णिव्वाविज्जा; अण्णं ण उज्जावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain]

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not, regarding fire — fire, embers, smouldering coals or dung-fire, sparks, flame, half-burnt brand, pure fire (without fuel), or lightning/meteor-fire — feed (by adding fuel), stir, break (split a burning log), kindle (by fanning), inflame more, or extinguish. Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

Fire is a kaya — a body — in Jain ontology. A flame is a colony of fire-beings. Both feeding and extinguishing fire are himsa: feeding because it propagates the fire-bodies into new fuel (which itself may contain other lives); extinguishing because it kills the fire-bodies directly. The monk thus has no relationship with fire at all — he neither cooks, lights lamps, nor puts out fires. The eight forms of fire cover an exhaustive phenomenology — from the steady heat of red-hot metal to the lightning that streaks across the sky. Even a meteor is a fire-body. Even a single spark.

Simply Put: The monk vows: "I will not feed, stir, split, kindle, inflame, or extinguish any fire — coals, sparks, flame, brand, lightning. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

4.17

Yatana Toward Vayu-kaya (Air-Beings)

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से सिएणं वा विहुयणेण वा तालियंटेण वा पत्तेण वा, पत्तभंगेण वा साहाए वा साहाभंगेण वा पिहुणेण वा पिहुणहत्थेण वा चेलेण वा चेलकण्णेण वा, हत्थेण वा मुहेण वा अप्पणो वा कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं, ण फुमिज्जा, ण वीएज्जा; अण्णं ण फुमावेज्जा, ण वीयावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain]

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not — with white chowrie, fan, palmyra-fan, leaf or piece of leaf, branch or piece of branch, peacock-feather or bunch of feathers, cloth or cloth-edge, hand or mouth — blow or fan, neither one's own body nor an outer object (to cool it). Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

Air is a kaya. The wind generated by waving a fan disturbs and kills air-bodies — and the violence of intent is the same whether one fans for one's own comfort (cooling a sweaty body) or for an outer object (cooling tea). Both are renounced. This sutra is striking because it forbids the most natural thermoregulation gesture humans have — fanning oneself in heat. The Jain monk simply endures heat. He does not even blow on his hot food. The list of thirteen wind-instruments is suggestive: from royal chowrie to a piece of cloth to one's own breath, every conceivable apparatus is named.

Simply Put: The monk vows: "I will not blow with my mouth or fan with chowrie, fan, leaf, branch, peacock-feather, cloth, hand — neither to cool myself nor to cool food or water. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

4.18

Yatana Toward Vanaspati-kaya (Plant-Beings)

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से बीएसु वा बीयपइट्ठेसु वा रूढेसु वा रूढपइट्ठेसु वा जाएसु वा जायपइट्ठेसु वा हरिएसु वा हरियपइट्ठेसु वा छिण्णेसु वा छिण्णपइट्ठेसु वा सचित्तेसु वा सचित्तकोलपडिणिस्सिएसु वा, ण गच्छेज्जा ण चिट्ठेज्जा ण णिसीएज्जा ण तुयट्टेज्जा; अण्णं ण गच्छावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain]

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not — on seeds or things resting on seeds, on sprouts or things on sprouts, on grown plants or things on grown plants, on green grass or things on green grass, on cut (still-living) plants or things on cut plants, on other living vegetation or on wood that hosts wood-worms — walk, stand, sit, or lie down. Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

Where earth, water, fire, air had instrument-based harms (scratching, squeezing, fanning), vegetation has bodily-presence harms — simply walking on grass crushes the green beings beneath the foot. The sutra recognizes this and lists the four most basic body postures: walking, standing, sitting, lying. To do any of these on living vegetation is himsa. The twelve states cover the full life cycle of a plant — seed → sprout → grown plant → green grass — and add the crucial Jain observation that even cut vegetation may still host life (a freshly cut branch with leaves still green is still sachit), and that wood may host kola (wood-worms), so even old timber must be checked.

Simply Put: The monk vows: "I will not walk on, stand on, sit on, or lie down on seeds, sprouts, grown plants, green grass, cut-but-living vegetation, or wood with worms. Nor on anything resting on these. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

4.19

Yatana Toward Trasa-kaya (Mobile Beings): The Rescue Procedure

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से कीडं वा पयंगं वा कुंथुं वा पिवीलियं वा हत्थंसि वा पायंसि वा बाहुंसि वा ऊरुंसि वा उदरंसि वा सीसंसि वा वत्थंसि वा पडिग्गहंसि वा कंबलंसि वा पायपुच्छणसि वा रयहरणंसि वा गोच्छगंसि वा उंडगंसि वा दंडगंसि वा पीढगंसि वा फलगंसि वा सेज्जंसि वा संथारगंसि वा अण्णयरंसि वा तहप्पगारे उवगरणजाए; तओ संजयामेव पडिलेहिय पडिलेहिय, पमज्जिय पमज्जिय, एगंतमवणेज्जा, णो णं संघायमावज्जेज्जा

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... — when an insect, moth, kunthu (a tiny creature), or ant is on the hand, foot, arm, thigh, belly, head; on clothing, alms-bowl, blanket, foot-wiper, rajoharana (whisk-broom), gocchaka (cleaning-brush), urinal-pot, staff, stool, plank, bed, or straw-mat; or on any other such monastic implement — should, restrained, observe carefully again and again, sweep gently again and again, and place (the creature) in a safe solitary spot. Should not crowd them together. Should not crush them.

The structure of this sutra is unique among the six. For earth, water, fire, air, and plants, the formula was don't touch, don't damage, don't approve damage. But trasa beings — sentient mobile creatures — will themselves climb onto the monk. The negative formula does not work; an active rescue procedure is required. The procedure has four steps: (1) paḍilehiya paḍilehiya — look carefully, again and again (the doubling is technical: it signals deliberate, slow, mistake-free observation); (2) pamajjiya pamajjiya — sweep gently with the rajoharana, again and again, never with bare hand; (3) egantam avaṇejjā — place in a solitary spot where the creature can survive (not on the path, not in a crowd); (4) no ṇaṃ saṃghāyam āvajjejjā — do not crowd them together (which would be its own form of himsa). The eighteen named locations — six bodily, twelve in the monastic kit — are exhaustive. Every place a kunthu might wander, the monk must inspect.

Simply Put: When an insect, moth, tiny creature, or ant climbs onto the monk's body or onto any of his implements — clothing, bowl, blanket, broom, staff, bed — he must look carefully (twice), sweep gently (twice), and place the creature in a solitary safe spot. He must not crush it, and he must not crowd many together (which itself causes pain).
