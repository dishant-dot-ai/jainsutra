Adhyayan 6: Mahācārakathā

About This Adhyayan

Eighteen Pillars of the Path

The sixth adhyayan presents the complete framework of monastic conduct as eighteen sthāna — eighteen 'standing-places,' eighteen pillars on which the entire life of a monk rests. The first six are the foundation: non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession, and the prohibition against night-eating. The next six are the protections: care for the six classes of living beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants, mobile beings).

The remaining six are the boundary disciplines: avoiding forbidden food (akalpya), householder's food, beds and luxurious furniture, certain houses, bathing, and bodily ornamentation (vibhūṣā). Together these eighteen sthanas form a complete diagram of what the monk does and does not do — the architecture of the monastic life.

6.1–6.2

The Question of the Kings

णाणदंसणसंपण्णं, संजमे य तवे रयं । गणिमागमसंपण्णं, उज्जाणम्मि समोसढं रायाणो रायमच्चा य, माहणा अदुव खत्तिया । पुच्छंति निहुयप्पाणो, कहं भे आयारगोयरो

To the Acharya — endowed with knowledge and right perception, absorbed in restraint and austerity, accomplished in the scriptures, the leader of the spiritual order, seated in a garden outside the city — kings, ministers, brahmins, and warriors, with composed and steady minds, approach and ask: "What, O Reverend One, is the conduct and the begging-discipline of your monks?"

The chapter opens with a scene rather than a doctrine. Outside the city, in a garden, sits an Acharya — a teacher complete in five qualities: endowed with knowledge, endowed with perception, absorbed in restraint, absorbed in austerity, and a master of the scriptures. To him come people of every station of worldly life — kings who govern, ministers who advise, brahmins who carry the religious tradition of the land, and warriors who defend it. They do not come as adversaries or sceptics; they come with composed minds. The text uses the word nihuyappāṇo — "with steadied selves" — and the commentary stresses this is no idle curiosity. Only a settled, focused mind can receive a true answer; a restless questioner cannot hear what is given. Their question is simple but vast: "What is the conduct of your monks?" The word āyāra-goyaro contains both ācāra (the inner code — the great vows, the rules of restraint, the rules of austerity) and gocara (the outer practice, especially the begging-round and the daily activities of monastic life). The questioners want to understand not only the philosophy but the living practice — what does a monk actually do, and why? The whole of the rest of the adhyayan is the Acharya's answer. The frame is significant: the reply that follows is not addressed to monks alone, but to the world. It is conduct held up for inspection by kings and householders — meaning it must be defensible, intelligible, and worthy of universal respect. The Jain path does not hide; it sets its rules out in the open garden where any informed person may come and ask.

Simply Put: Important people from the city — kings, ministers, scholars, soldiers — come quietly and respectfully to a great spiritual teacher sitting outside the city, and ask him: "Please tell us — how do your monks actually live?"

6.3

The Acharya, Calm and Skilled, Replies

तेसिं सो णिहुओ दंतो, सव्वभूयसुहावहो । सिक्खाए सुसमाउत्तो, आयक्खइ वियक्खणो

To them, that one — calm, master of the senses, the bringer of well-being to all living beings, fully equipped with right learning, the discerning Acharya — gives his answer.

Before the answer comes, the answerer is described — and described in five qualities, exactly matching the Acharya-image of the previous gathas. The text is teaching by the very form of its sentences: a true teacher must be these five things before his words can carry weight. He is composed — free from anxiety, free from the eagerness to impress. He is master of his senses — not pulled by what he sees and hears, so that his answer comes from settled awareness rather than from reaction. He is the bringer of well-being to all beings — meaning his teaching is not for the benefit of one party, one class, or even one species; whoever hears it should be lifted up. He is equipped with right learning — both the textual learning (scriptures studied) and the practical learning (conduct lived). And he is discerning — he knows which question deserves which answer, when to speak briefly and when to expand. The deeper point is that the Jain teaching is not delivered abstractly. It is delivered through a person who has become it. The conduct described in this adhyayan is the very conduct the Acharya himself embodies — and it is because he embodies it that the kings and brahmins are willing to come and listen. Words follow life. Lifeless words cannot transmit dharma.

Simply Put: The teacher who answers is not just a knowledgeable man — he is calm, free of grasping, kind to every creature, fully trained, and wise enough to know what to say. Only such a person can really teach the path.

6.4

Listen, You Who Desire the Goal of Dharma

हंदि धम्मत्थकामाणं, निग्गंथाणं सुणेह मे । आयारगोयरं भीमं, सयलं दुरहिट्ठियं

"Listen now — hear from me the entire conduct and discipline of the unattached ones (the Nirgranthas) who desire only the goal of dharma; a conduct awe-inspiring, complete, and exceedingly difficult for ordinary people to undertake."

The Acharya now opens the answer with three loaded words: bhima (awe-inspiring), sayala (complete), and durahitthiya (very hard to undertake). He is not selling the path. He is naming it accurately. Liberation requires a way of life that, heard plainly, makes ordinary listeners shiver — pulling out one's hair by hand, walking on bare feet for life, never using a vehicle, enduring the discomforts of weather, complete celibacy, complete renunciation of night-eating, the most subtle non-violence, owning nothing, accepting no money. The text uses bhima — "terrible" — for exactly this reason. Listening to the rules can frighten an ordinary person. But notice to whom he addresses the answer: dhammatthakāmāṇaṃ niggaṃthāṇaṃ — "to the Nirgranthas who desire only the goal of dharma." This is the qualifying frame. For those whose only desire is liberation, the conduct is not terrible at all; it is the path home. For those who still want pleasures, status, and ease, the conduct will sound impossible. The same set of rules is freedom for one and prison for another, depending on what the heart actually wants. The word Nirgrantha — "without knots" — is the older Jain self-designation, predating the more familiar Shvetambara and Digambara labels. It points to the essence of monkhood: not robes, not orders, not lineages, but the dissolution of every knot — the outer knots of property and the inner knots of attachment, hatred, and ego. The conduct that follows is the technique by which those knots come undone.

Simply Put: The teacher says: "Listen carefully. I am about to describe the way of life of those who want nothing except liberation. It is severe, complete, and very hard for ordinary people to even begin — but it is the true path."

6.5

Found in No Other Tradition, Past or Future

णण्णत्थ एरिसं वुत्तं, जं लोए परम दुच्चरं । विउलट्ठाणभाइस्स, ण भूयं ण भविस्सइ

Such a conduct as this — the most difficult to walk in the whole world, belonging to those who hold the great spiritual stations — has not been taught anywhere else; it has not existed in the past in any other tradition, and it will not arise in the future in any other tradition.

This gatha makes the boldest claim of the chapter. The Acharya declares that the conduct he is about to describe is found nowhere else — not in any other tradition of the past, not in any other tradition that will arise in the future. The commentary is careful to note that this is not boastful self-promotion; it is a precise factual statement about the nature of the path. The full set of rules — the most subtle non-violence even toward earth, water, fire, air, and plant lives; perfectly truthful and harm-free speech; ownerless living; absolute celibacy; the renunciation of night-eating; the rejection of bathing and ornamentation as inner-purity practices — this complete combination is unique to the Jin-shāsana, the teaching of the Tirthankaras. The point is not sectarian pride. The point is that liberation requires an exact technology, and the Tirthankaras are the ones who have mapped it after attaining complete self-knowledge. Any path that omits even one element of the technology cannot reach the same destination. A good map for getting to a nearby village will not get you to the summit of Everest; only the right map for the right destination will work. This adhyayan is offering the right map. The phrase viula-tthana-bhai — "those who hold the vast stations" — refers to the eighteen sthanas about to be enumerated. To "hold" a sthana is to make it one's permanent inner ground, not merely to perform it from time to time. That kind of holding, says the Acharya, is what no other tradition teaches in full.

Simply Put: The teacher says: "This exact way of life — every detail of it together — is not taught in any other religion or philosophy. It never has been, and never will be, found anywhere except in the teaching of the Tirthankaras."

6.6

For Children, the Old, the Sick, and the Healthy Alike

सखुड्डगवियत्ताणं, वाहियाणं च जे गुणा । अखंडफुडिया कायव्वा, तं सुणेह जहा तहा

The qualities — the rules of conduct — that are to be observed by both the young and the mature, by the sick and the healthy alike, unbroken and uncracked, exactly as they are: hear them now, just as they truly are.

The previous gatha could have been heard as elitist — a path so exalted it must belong to special people. This gatha corrects that immediately. The conduct about to be described is meant for every mumukshu (seeker of liberation), regardless of age and regardless of physical condition. Young or old, sick or healthy — the eighteen sthanas apply. Adjustments for circumstance exist (and the chapter will detail them), but the core rules are universal. Two key words deserve attention. Akhanda means "unbroken" — observed continuously, not picked up and put down. Phudiya (Sanskrit sphuṭita) means "cracked" — and the negation akhanda-aphudiya means "not even cracked." A pot can be technically unbroken yet have hairline cracks that leak; conduct can be technically maintained yet riddled with small compromises that drain its power. The Acharya is asking for a conduct that is whole at every level — not just unbroken but uncracked. This standard sounds severe, but the commentary explains it gently: it is the destination, not the starting point. The young practitioner begins where they are, the old practitioner adjusts what they must, the sick practitioner is given allowances by the Guru — but the inner aspiration is always the same: the unbroken, uncracked vow. Conduct that is asked of all, in proportion to capacity, but never abandoned by anyone.

Simply Put: The rules of conduct apply to everyone — young and old, sick and well — and they should be kept whole, with no breaks and no hidden cracks. Now hear them exactly as they are.

6.7

The Eighteen Stations: To Break Even One Is to Fall

दस अट्ठ य ठाणाइं, जाइं बालोवरज्झइ । तत्थ अण्णयरे ठाणे, निग्गंथत्ताओ भस्सइ

There are eighteen stations of conduct; whichever immature seeker transgresses any one among them, falls from the state of being a Nirgrantha (an unattached monk).

The Acharya now names the structure of the entire chapter: there are eighteen sthanas — eighteen stations or sites of right conduct. The text uses the elegant compound dasa-attha ("ten and eight") rather than atthaarasa ("eighteen") to fit the metre, but the meaning is the same. These eighteen stations will be enumerated in the next gatha and explained one by one through the rest of the adhyayan. The crucial teaching of this gatha is the standard of integrity: to break even one of the eighteen is to fall from monkhood. The Jain monastic ideal is not "keep most of the rules and you are mostly a monk." It is wholeness. The eighteen sthanas form a single fabric; pull one thread and the whole garment unravels. A monk who is perfect in seventeen but careless in the eighteenth is not seventeen-eighteenths a monk — he has lost the state of Nirgrantha-hood entirely until that lapse is repaired. The word bala (translated as "immature seeker") is significant. It does not mean "child"; it means a practitioner who is spiritually undeveloped — one who lacks the discrimination to see why each rule matters. Such a person, taking even one rule lightly, slips. The mature practitioner (panḍita), by contrast, holds all eighteen with equal care because they understand the unity behind them. The standard is severe, but the standard is also what keeps monkhood from quietly hollowing out into appearance.

Simply Put: There are eighteen rules of conduct. A foolish or careless monk who breaks even one of them stops being a true monk in that moment.

6.8

The Names of the Eighteen Stations

वयछक्कं कायछक्कं, अकप्पो गिहिभायणं । पलियंकं णिसेज्जा य, सिणाणं सोहवज्जणं

The six vows; the six classes of life-protection; (the avoidance of) forbidden food; (the avoidance of) eating from the householder's vessels; (the avoidance of) sitting on couches; (the avoidance of) sitting in the householder's home; the renunciation of bathing; and the renunciation of bodily ornamentation.

This single gatha lists the entire structure of the rest of the adhyayan. Counted out, the eighteen are: (1–5) the five great vows of non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession; (6) renunciation of night-eating; (7–12) the six classes of life-protection — earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, air-bodies, plant-bodies, and mobile beings; (13) avoidance of forbidden food and articles; (14) avoidance of eating from the householder's plates and vessels; (15) avoidance of sitting on cushioned couches and beds; (16) avoidance of sitting in the householder's home; (17) renunciation of bathing; (18) renunciation of ornamentation, jewellery, and bodily beautification. The architecture is deliberate. The first six are the internal vows — what the monk swears not to do at the level of intent. The next six are the external applications of the first vow (non-violence) at the level of life-forms — non-violence is not merely toward humans and animals but toward every embodied being, even invisible ones. The final six are the practical disciplines of daily monastic life — what the monk does not eat, does not sit on, does not stay in, does not pour over himself, does not put on his body. Together they cover intent, ethical scope, and lived form. The chapter from this point onward will take each of the eighteen and unfold it: stating the prohibition, giving the reason, naming the harm of breaking it, naming the benefit of keeping it. This gatha is the table of contents.

Simply Put: The eighteen rules are: the six vows (no killing, no lying, no stealing, no sex, no possessions, no night-eating); the protection of six classes of life (earth, water, fire, air, plants, and moving creatures); not taking forbidden food; not eating from the householder's plates; not sitting on beds; not sitting in the householder's house; not bathing; and not decorating the body.

6.9

First Station: Non-Violence (Restraint Toward All Beings)

तत्थिमं पढमं ठाणं, महावीरेण देसियं । अहिंसा णिउणा दिट्ठा, सव्वभूएसु संजमो

Among them, this is the first station, taught by Lord Mahavira: non-violence — known and seen with subtle precision — which is restraint toward all living beings.

Of the eighteen stations, the first — and the foundation of all the rest — is ahiṃsā: non-violence. The text adds an essential qualifier: niuṇā diṭṭhā — "seen with subtle precision." This is not vague kindness or general harmlessness. The non-violence taught by Mahavira is the non-violence as seen by an omniscient eye — meaning: perceived in its complete exactness, accounting for every form of life, every level of intent, every angle of harm. Other traditions accept non-violence as a principle; only the Jain tradition develops it to the level of subtle technical precision that includes the protection of single-sense beings (earth, water, fire, air, plant) along with multi-sense beings. The gatha then equates non-violence with another word: saṃyama — restraint. The two are inseparable. Non-violence is the result; restraint of the senses, restraint of the mind, restraint of action toward all beings is the means. You cannot be non-violent without being restrained, and you cannot be truly restrained without becoming non-violent. This is why the traditional formula ahimsa parmo dharma ("non-violence is the supreme dharma") is in Jain understanding identical with samyamo parmo dharma ("restraint is the supreme dharma") — they are two faces of one practice. The phrase savva-bhuesu — "toward all living beings" — sets the scope wider than perhaps any other ethical teaching in human history. Every being, from the microbe to the human, from the earth-body to the deva, is the subject of the vow. To live with this vow is to begin reading the world differently — every step, every breath, every drop of water, every movement of speech becomes a place where the vow is either kept or broken.

Simply Put: The first and most important rule, taught by Mahavira with complete precision, is non-violence — which means holding back from harm toward every living thing.

6.10

All Beings, Knowing or Unknowing, Are Not to Be Harmed

जावंति लोए पाणा, तसा अदुव थावरा । ते जाणमजाणं वा, ण हणे णो वि घायए

Whatever living beings exist in the world, mobile or immobile, them — knowingly or unknowingly — one should not kill oneself, nor cause others to kill.

This gatha extends the first vow into its full scope. Two divisions are named: trasa (mobile beings — anything with two or more senses, capable of voluntary movement) and sthāvara (immobile beings — earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, and plant-bodied, each having only the single sense of touch). The vow covers both. The Jain analysis is unique in its insistence that the single-sense beings — the speck of soil, the drop of water, the flame, the puff of air, the blade of grass — are also living beings deserving protection. The gatha specifies jāṇaṃ ajāṇaṃ vā — "knowingly or unknowingly." This is the true test of the vow. To not kill knowingly is the easy half; to not kill unknowingly requires the cultivation of constant awareness — the discipline of looking before stepping, sweeping before sitting, seeing before drinking. The unknowing harms are the ones that sustained spiritual practice slowly eliminates, not by adding rules but by deepening attentiveness. The classical formula completes the vow with three modes: na haṇe (do not kill yourself), no vi ghāyae (do not cause others to kill), and — implicit in the larger structure of the vow — do not approve when others kill. This is the navakoti (nine-fold) frame familiar from the Karemi Bhante: three karanas (do, cause, approve) by three yogas (mind, speech, body). The full vow seals all nine doors against violence.

Simply Put: Every living thing in the world — whether it can move, like an animal, or whether it is still, like a plant or a drop of water — should not be killed by you, whether you knew it was alive or not, and you should not get others to kill it either.

6.11

Every Being Wants to Live; None Wants to Die

सव्वे जीवा वि इच्छंति, जीविउं ण मरिज्जिउं । तम्हा पाणिवहं घोरं, निग्गंथा वज्जयंति णं

All living beings wish to live; none wishes to die. Therefore the Nirgranthas renounce the terrible act of killing living beings.

This gatha gives the reason for the first vow, and in giving it Mahavira sets out one of the most radical ethical principles in human thought: the fact that every living being — without exception — wants to live and does not want to die is itself the entire foundation of non-violence. There is no need for theology, no need for command, no need for divine sanction. The will-to-live in the other being is the moral demand on you. The argument is symmetrical. I want to live and do not want to die. Every other being wants the same. To kill another is to inflict on them what I would refuse to have inflicted on me. This is the universal Jain answer to the question "why be non-violent?" — not because a god orders it, not because of consequences in another life, but because the will-to-live is the same in every soul, and recognising that sameness is the awakening of the moral self. The text uses the strong word ghora — "terrible" — for the act of killing. Not "regrettable," not "unhelpful" — terrible. The killing of a living being, however small, is a violent rupture of the universe of wills-to-live, and the practitioner who has truly seen this cannot do it. The Nirgrantha therefore renounces it — vajjayaṃti — completely, by all three karanas and all three yogas.

Simply Put: Every single living thing in the world wants to live and does not want to die. That is why the monks completely refuse to do the terrible act of killing.

6.12

Second Station: Truthfulness (No Harmful or Untrue Speech)

अप्पणट्ठा परट्ठा वा, कोहा वा जइ वा भया । हिंसगं ण मुसं बूया, णो वि अण्णं वयावए

Whether for one's own sake or for another's, whether out of anger or out of fear, one should not speak harmful or untrue words oneself, nor cause another to speak them.

The second sthana is truthfulness. This gatha names the typical motivations from which untruthful or harmful speech arises — and then forbids them all. For one's own sake (to gain something, to escape blame); for another's sake (to help a friend, to flatter a patron); out of anger (and by implication, also out of pride, deceit, and greed — the four passions); out of fear (and by implication, also for amusement, to entertain). All five classical causes of false speech are covered. Notice that the gatha does not simply say "do not speak untruth." It says "do not speak harmful untruth" — hiṃsagaṃ musaṃ. The Jain second vow is not the same as Western "honesty"; it is more refined. Speech is to be both true and non-harmful. A truth that wounds without cause is itself a violation of the second vow. The vow demands speech that is true, beneficial, kind, and timely — speech that does not become a weapon, even when factually correct. And as with all the vows, the prohibition extends not only to speaking falsely oneself but to causing another to speak falsely — and by the larger structure, to approving of another's false speech. The vow covers all three karanas. The monk who would never tell a lie himself but happily quotes another's lie has not fully kept the vow.

Simply Put: Whether to help yourself or someone else, whether out of anger or out of fear — never say anything false or hurtful, and never get someone else to say it for you.

6.13

False Speech Is Censured by All; the Liar Is Trusted by None

मुसावाओ उ लोगम्मि, सव्वसाहूहिं गरहिओ । अविस्सासो य भूयाणं, तम्हा मोसं विवज्जए

False speech is censured in the world by all good persons; (the liar becomes) untrustworthy to all living beings; therefore one should completely renounce falsehood.

The gatha gives two reasons to abandon false speech, one social and one spiritual. The social reason: false speech is condemned by all good persons everywhere. Across every culture and tradition, the liar is despised — there is no school of thought in human history that praises lying as a virtue. The Buddhist five precepts include the abstention from false speech; the Brahmanical, Christian, Islamic, and secular ethical systems all agree. The Jain teaching simply observes that on this point, all wisdom traditions converge, and the practitioner should accept that universal verdict. The spiritual reason: the liar becomes untrustworthy to all living beings. Trust, once lost, is extraordinarily hard to rebuild. A person who has been caught lying once is doubted twice; caught twice, doubted always. And this loss of trust is not just an external inconvenience — it cuts the practitioner off from the community of fellow seekers, from the Guru, from the householders who support the order. Without trust, the spiritual ecosystem cannot function. The liar isolates themselves. The commentary lists the typical failures of the second vow that arise from each of the four passions: lying out of anger (calling someone a slave when they are not); lying out of pride (claiming to be a scholar when one is not); lying out of deceit (complaining of foot pain to avoid the begging-round); lying out of greed (calling tasteless food unworthy when it is simply not what one wanted); lying out of fear (denying a fault to escape penance); lying for amusement (jokes that distort the truth). All of these the practitioner is to abandon.

Simply Put: Lying is condemned everywhere by every wise person, and a liar ends up being trusted by no one. So give up all forms of false speech, completely.

6.14–6.15

Third Station: Non-Stealing (Take Nothing Without Permission)

चित्तमंतमचित्तं वा, अप्पं वा जइ वा बहुं । दंतसोहणमित्तं पि, उग्गहंसि अजाइया तं अप्पणा ण गिण्हंति, णो वि गिण्हावए परं । अण्णं वा गिण्हमाणंपि, णाणुजाणंति संजया

Anything — sentient or non-sentient, small or large, even so much as a toothpick — that lies within (another's) holding and has not been formally requested: that, the restrained ones do not take themselves, nor cause others to take, nor approve when others take.

The third sthana is adattadana-virati — refraining from taking what has not been given. This pair of gathas defines stealing in the most refined form found in any ethical tradition. Stealing is the taking of anything — sentient or non-sentient, large or small, even a toothpick — that is within another's authority, without that authority's permission. The phrase daṃtasohaṇamittaṃ pi — "even as much as a toothpick" — is the famous Jain marker: there is no such thing as a "trivial" violation of this vow. To take a blade of grass to clean one's teeth without asking the owner is theft. The first gatha names two important categories. Sentient objects include disciples — to "ordain" someone as a monk without the permission of their parents would itself be stealing (taking a person from another's authority without consent). Non-sentient objects include the standard articles a monk uses: clothes, vessels, food, medicine. All of these come from householders; all must be received by formal asking. The second pair, small or large, removes the loophole by which the mind reasons "it was so little, it doesn't count." The second gatha then completes the vow with the navakoti (nine-fold) seal: not taken oneself, not caused to be taken by another, not approved when another takes. The restrained one — saṃyata — closes all three doors. The principle is luminous: ownership is real, and respecting another's boundary even down to a wisp of grass is the practice that dissolves the deeper grasping at the root of all human conflict. Where stealing exists, trust collapses; where this vow is kept, even the smallest objects become safe in the open.

Simply Put: The monks do not take anything — living or non-living, big or small, even a tiny toothpick — that belongs to someone else without first asking permission. They do not take it themselves, do not get others to take it, and do not approve when others take it.

6.16

Fourth Station: Celibacy (The Awe-Inspiring Vow)

अबंभचरियं घोरं, पमायं दुरहिट्ठियं । णायरंति मुणी लोए, भेयायतणवज्जिणो

The monks in the world, who avoid every site of breakage of the vow, do not practise un-celibacy — which is terrible, full of negligence, and exceedingly hard to undertake (in the sense of being a path full of suffering).

The fourth sthana is brahmacarya — celibacy, literally "wandering in Brahman, wandering in the Self." The word means much more than physical chastity; it means dwelling in one's true nature, undistracted by sensual pursuit. Un-celibacy is here described with three sharp adjectives: ghora (terrible — leading to terrible suffering), pamāya (the very embodiment of negligence — the cardinal vice that opens the door to all karmic bondage), and durahiṭṭhiya (a place exceedingly difficult to occupy — meaning, full of pain, no true refuge). Sexual indulgence is described not in terms of moral disapproval but in terms of its actual phenomenology in a spiritual life: it is a place of suffering masquerading as a place of pleasure. The phrase bheyāyatana-vajjino — "those who avoid every site of the breakage" — is the practical key to keeping this vow. The Jain analysis identifies many "sites" where the vow is at risk: living in places where the opposite sex resides, listening to talk about the opposite sex, gazing at the limbs of the opposite sex, sitting alone with the opposite sex, recalling past sexual experiences, eating rich and stimulating food, sleeping in soft beds, using ornamentation. The practitioner who would keep the vow does not merely resist temptation when it arises — they avoid the sites where temptation arises in the first place. The defence is structural, not heroic. This is one of the most psychologically realistic teachings in the chapter. The text does not say "be strong against desire"; it says "do not enter the rooms where desire is born." A monk who avoids the nine fences (nava-vāḍa) around celibacy keeps the vow without strain; a monk who steps inside them and trusts willpower to hold will sooner or later fall.

Simply Put: The monks do not engage in any sexual conduct, because it leads to terrible suffering, is the heart of carelessness, and is a place of pain disguised as pleasure. And they avoid every situation that could even bring such conduct close.

6.17

Un-Celibacy: The Root of Un-Dharma, the Storehouse of Great Faults

मूलमेयमहम्मस्स, महादोससमुस्सयं । तम्हा मेहुणसंसग्गं, निग्गंथा वज्जयंति णं

This (un-celibacy) is the root of un-dharma, the towering accumulation of great faults; therefore the Nirgranthas renounce sexual contact entirely.

This gatha gives the reason for the celibacy vow — and it is unsparing. Un-celibacy is named the root of all un-dharma — the source from which all other failures of the spiritual life flow — and a great accumulation of great faults. The commentary lists what follows from sexual indulgence: dishonesty (one lies to obtain or conceal it), deceit, theft (one steals to provide for the dependent family that follows), even violence (in jealous rage). One sin gives birth to many; sexual involvement opens the floodgate. There is also the practical reasoning of family. The moment sexual conduct produces a partner, then children, then dependents — the entire household structure follows, and the monk's renunciation is finished. The Jain order protects the renunciant's ability to remain renunciant by closing this door at the root. As long as celibacy holds, none of the entanglements of householder life can grow; once celibacy breaks, all of them follow. The text uses the strong term saṃsarga — contact, association — rather than only the act itself. The Nirgranthas renounce not only sexual conduct but the association that produces it — the company, the conversations, the gazing, the imaginative dwelling. The mature spiritual life closes the entire ecology of the vice, not just the final act. This is not prudery; it is the realistic acknowledgement that an embodied being cannot rely on willpower at the last moment. The vow is kept by structuring one's whole life so that the last moment never arises.

Simply Put: Sexual conduct is the root of everything that goes wrong in spiritual life and the source of countless other faults. So the monks completely give up not just the act, but every form of association and contact that leads to it.

6.18

Fifth Station: Non-Possession (The Storing of Anything Is Forbidden)

बिडमुब्भेइमं लोणं, तिल्लं सप्पिं च फाणियं । ण ते सण्णिहिमिच्छंति, णायपुत्तवओरया

Salt that is fire-prepared, salt that is mineral, oil, ghee, and liquid jaggery — those who are devoted to the words of the Son of the Jnata clan (Lord Mahavira) do not desire to store such things even (overnight).

The fifth sthana is aparigraha — non-possession. It begins not with grand renunciations but with a simple list of food items: prepared salt, mineral salt, oil, ghee, and liquid jaggery. Why this list? Because these are the items that, unlike fresh-cooked food, can be stored — they last overnight, they keep for days. The Jain monastic principle is that whatever can be stored is the doorway to possessiveness, and the doorway must be closed. The keyword is sannidhi — "storage." A monk receives food on each day's begging-round and consumes it on that day; nothing is kept for tomorrow. This is the practical face of non-possession: trust that tomorrow's food will come, today, fresh, by the begging-round. The moment one keeps a jar of ghee for "in case I cannot get any tomorrow," the inner stance has shifted from non-possession to possession — and with it comes the entire psychology of the householder, who plans, hoards, and protects. The phrase ṇāyaputta-vaorayā — "devoted to the words of the Son of the Jnata clan" — is a beautiful self-designation. Ñātaputra (the Son of Jnata) is one of the names of Mahavira. To be vaca-rata — "delighted in his words" — is to be a true follower. The gatha is saying: those whose hearts are tuned to Mahavira's teaching simply do not want to store. The vow is not enforced from outside; it is a natural expression of devotion to the teaching.

Simply Put: Even simple long-lasting food items — like salt, oil, ghee, or jaggery — the monks do not want to store. Those who love Mahavira's teaching simply do not desire to keep anything for later.

6.19

Even the Wish to Store Is the Touch of Greed

लोहस्सेस अणुफासो, मण्णे अण्णयरामवि । जे सिया सण्णिहीकामे, गिही पव्वइए ण से

(The Tirthankaras) declare: any storage at all of any thing whatsoever is itself a touch of greed; whoever wishes to store is a householder, not an ordained one.

This gatha pushes the vow inward. It is not enough to say the monk does not actually store; the gatha says even the wish to store — the inner inclination — is already the touch of greed. The word anusparśa is exquisite: it means a "light touching," a contact, a brush. The Tirthankaras say: any storing at all, of anything whatsoever, leaves on the soul this brush of greed. The vow is therefore not about the external act alone but about the inner movement that precedes it. Then the verse delivers the verdict: whoever wishes to store is a householder, not an ordained one. This is one of the sharpest sentences in the chapter. It cuts through the ambiguity that ordination might be confirmed by robes and rituals. Mahavira says: ordination is confirmed by the inner state. A robed person who in his heart desires to hoard is a householder, no matter what title he carries; a person without robes whose heart is free of the wish to store is closer to the monastic ideal than the storer in robes. The implication for the practitioner is profound. The vow of non-possession is to be inspected not at the level of the cupboard but at the level of the desire. Do I want to keep something for tomorrow? — the question to be asked daily, hourly. The monk's task is to cultivate the inner state in which the wish itself does not arise — and the householder's task is to recognise that, even within householder life, the slow reduction of the wish-to-hoard is the slow ascent of the spiritual path.

Simply Put: Mahavira teaches: even just wanting to store something is already a touch of greed. Anyone who wants to keep things for later is really a householder at heart — not a true monk, no matter what they wear.

6.20

But Robes, Bowls, and Tools Are Kept for Restraint, Not Possession

जंपि वत्थं व पायं वा, कंबलं पायपुंछणं । तंपि संजमलज्जट्ठा, धारंति परिहरंति य

Whatever cloth, bowl, blanket, or foot-wiper (the broom — rajoharaṇa) the monks do keep, they keep and use it solely for the sake of restraint and modesty.

This gatha addresses an obvious question. If the monk renounces possession, why do the Sthavirakalpi monks (those of the older, traditional order) carry clothes, bowls, blankets, and the rajoharaṇa? The answer: these articles are kept not for possession but for two specific purposes — saṃyama (restraint, the maintenance of the monastic discipline itself) and lajjā (modesty, the avoidance of being naked in public). The first purpose, restraint, requires explanation. The bowl is needed because the monk must accept food from the householder; without a bowl, food cannot be received. The blanket is needed for protection from cold (so that the monk does not become physically incapacitated and unable to perform the practices). The rajoharaṇa is needed for sweeping insects and dust from the place of sitting — a tool of non-violence itself. These articles serve the vows; they do not serve the self. The second purpose, modesty, addresses why clothes are worn at all. The Sthavirakalpi monk wears garments not for fashion or comfort but to avoid shocking ordinary people who would be disturbed by nakedness. (The Jinakalpi monk, of the more ancient and rigorous order, has transcended even this and wanders without robes.) The point: even the things the monk carries are not "possessions" but instruments of practice. They belong to the vow, not to the monk; they serve restraint, not self.

Simply Put: The cloth, bowl, blanket, and broom that the monks do carry are not possessions — they are kept and used only to keep the discipline going and to maintain basic modesty in society.

6.21

Possession Is Not the Object; Possession Is Attachment to It

ण सो परिग्गहो वुत्तो, णायपुत्तेण ताइणा । मुच्छा परिग्गहो वुत्तो, इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा

It (the cloth, bowl, etc.) is not called "possession" by the Son of the Jnata clan, the Protector of beings. Attachment is what is called possession — so the great seer has declared.

This is one of the most important psychological-philosophical statements in all of Jain ethics. Mahavira — the Protector of beings — declares that the object itself is not the possession. What is the possession? Mūrcchā — attachment, infatuation, the inner clinging of the mind to the object. This single insight reframes the entire discipline of non-possession. A monk holding a bowl with no attachment is not possessing it; a householder gazing at his bank statement with longing is possessing it utterly. The locus of possession is not the warehouse but the heart. This is why the gatha can declare that the monk's robes, bowl, and broom — though physically held — are not parigraha. They are held but not clung to. The vow is kept at the level where it actually matters: in the inner relation, not the outer arrangement. The implication for householders is liberating and demanding at once. Liberating: one does not have to give up everything to begin the path of non-possession; one has to begin by loosening the inner clinging. Demanding: even the householder who claims to "own little" but is consumed by attachment to that little has not begun. The work is interior. The practitioner who can hold a fortune with detachment is closer to the vow than the one who guards a single rupee with longing.

Simply Put: Mahavira said: the thing you hold is not what makes it a possession. Attachment is what makes it a possession. A monk holding a bowl with no clinging is not a "possessor" — but a person clinging to a single coin is.

6.22

The Awakened Hold Even the Body Without Possessiveness

सव्वत्थुवहिणा बुद्धा, संरक्खणपरिग्गहे । अवि अप्पणो वि देहम्मि, णायरंति ममाइयं

The awakened ones, even when they keep articles for the protection (of life and the discipline), do not practise the sense of "mine-ness" — not even toward their own body.

This gatha reaches the summit of the non-possession teaching. The awakened ones, while necessarily keeping certain articles for the protection of life and the maintenance of discipline (clothes, bowl, broom, blanket — saṃrakṣaṇa-parigraha), do not allow the sense of "mine" (mamatva) to attach even to those articles — and, the gatha goes further, not even to their own body. The body is the most natural object of "mine." Yet the awakened ones do not say "my body" in the inner clinging sense. They use the body as an instrument for the practice of dharma; they protect it as one protects a borrowed vehicle for the duration of a journey; they do not identify with it. This is the highest reach of non-possession: even the most intimate "possession" — this physical form — is held in non-attachment. When the body is held in this way, the small worldly possessions become trivially easy to hold without grasping. The body is the master test; pass it, and the rest follows. The teaching also resolves a paradox. How can the Nirgrantha keep anything at all and still be "without knots"? Because parigraha is defined by attachment, not by holding. The awakened one holds without clinging — like an open hand on which something rests but does not grip. The instruments of practice are kept, used, and one day set down, with no inner mark of possession at any point. This is the genuine aparigraha — the inner state in which even one's own body is held without "mine-ness," and the world becomes, for the first time, light.

Simply Put: The truly awakened, even when they keep necessary tools to protect life and discipline, never say "this is mine" — not even about their own body. They use the body, but they do not grasp it.

6.23

Sixth Station: Renunciation of Night-Eating (A Daily Austerity)

अहो णिच्चं तवोकम्मं, सव्वबुद्धेहिं वण्णियं । जा य लज्जासमा वित्ती, एगभत्तं च भोयणं

How wonderful — this perpetual austerity, praised by all the awakened ones (the Tirthankaras): a way of living harmonious with restraint, and eating only once (in the day, never at night).

The sixth sthana — the renunciation of night-eating — is here described as a perpetual austerity. The text uses the word aho — an exclamation of wonder — to express how remarkable it is that the Tirthankaras have praised this single discipline as a daily, lifelong tapas. Other austerities are practised on certain days; this one is practised every day. By eating only during daylight hours and never at night, the monk performs an ongoing minor fast that becomes part of the very rhythm of life. The phrase lajjāsamā vittī — a way of living "harmonious with restraint" — is the deeper teaching. Lajjā here is being used as a synonym for saṃyama (restraint), as the text explained earlier (sutra 6.20). A vṛtti (way of life, mode of subsistence) that suits restraint is one in which food serves the discipline rather than the discipline serving food. Eka-bhakta — eating once in the day — is precisely such a vṛtti. The body is sustained without becoming the centre of attention; sense-restraint is exercised at every meal; the long break between sunset and sunrise becomes an extended practice. The reasoning is twofold. First, ethical: night-eating greatly increases the chance of consuming small living beings (insects drawn to lamps, organisms invisible in the dark). Second, meditative: the discipline of fasting from sunset to sunrise quiets the body, sharpens awareness, and builds mastery over the tongue and stomach — the senses most often given to gluttony. Combined, the practice is non-violence and inner austerity at once.

Simply Put: How wonderful! All the awakened ones praise this daily small austerity: a way of life that suits restraint, and eating only in the daytime — never at night.

6.24–6.25

Why Night-Eating Is Forbidden

संतिमे सुहुमा पाणा, तसा अदुव थावरा । जाइं राओ अपासंतो, कहमेसणियं चरे उदउल्लं बीयसंसत्तं, पाणा णिवडिया महिं । दिया ताइं विवज्जिज्जा, राओ तत्थ कहं चरे

There exist these subtle living beings, both mobile and immobile, which one cannot see at night; how then would one (perform) the spotless investigation (of food)? Hands wet with water, food mixed with seeds, insects fallen on the earth — these one can avoid in daytime; how then would one walk among them at night?

These two gathas give the practical reasoning for the night-eating prohibition, which is grounded entirely in the first vow of non-violence. At night, in lamp-light or darkness, the practitioner cannot perform proper eṣaṇā — the careful inspection of food, hands, vessels, and the path. Subtle living beings of all kinds — insects, ants, eggs, microscopic organisms — cannot be seen. To eat under such conditions is to almost certainly consume living beings, to drop crumbs that attract insects to their death, and to walk a path where small lives are crushed. The second gatha gives concrete examples. A wet hand or vessel will harm water-bodied beings if used carelessly. Food that contains live seeds (bījaṃ saṃsattam) will, if taken, be a violation of vegetable-life protection. Insects — drawn to lamps or simply fallen on the ground — will be inadvertently crushed or eaten. In daytime these can be seen and avoided; at night they cannot. The argument is empirical, not dogmatic. The vow against night-eating is not a ritual taboo but a direct extension of the non-violence vow into the time of day when sight fails. This is also why even householders observing the basic six-fold vows of Jain practice are taught to avoid eating after sunset. The principle is universal: where vision cannot guarantee non-harm, the practice withdraws. The Jain dietary discipline is, at its core, the discipline of seeing.

Simply Put: There are tiny living beings, moving and still, that you simply cannot see at night. How could you possibly check your food for them? Wet hands, food with seeds in it, insects fallen on the floor — in daylight you can avoid harming them; at night you cannot.

6.26

Therefore the Nirgranthas Do Not Eat at Night

एयं च दोसं दट्ठूणं, णायपुत्तेण भासियं । सव्वाहारं ण भुंजंति, णिग्गंथा राइभोयणं

Seeing this fault and (it being thus) declared by the Son of the Jnata clan: the Nirgranthas eat no food of any kind at night.

This gatha closes the section on night-eating with the categorical statement: the Nirgranthas eat no food of any kind at night. The phrase savvāhāraṃ — "all food" — covers the four classical categories of consumables: staple food (aśana), drink (pāna), chewables (khādya), and savouries (svādya). Not even water is taken at night. The vow is total. The cited authority is again Mahavira — the Son of the Jnata clan — who, having seen the harms enumerated in the previous gathas, declared the prohibition. The wording is significant: seeing this fault, it has been declared. The teaching is grounded in observation, not arbitrary command. Anyone who observes carefully sees the same harm; the Tirthankara's authority is the authority of one who has seen completely. For monks, the rule is absolute and lifelong. For householders, it is one of the simpler practices that can be adopted as a foundational discipline — and is, in traditional Jain families, the most widely kept of all the lay vows. Eating only between sunrise and sunset (or, in stricter practice, completing the evening meal well before sunset) becomes the daily marker of a life that takes non-violence seriously even in its smallest acts.

Simply Put: Seeing all these problems clearly, Mahavira declared: the monks eat no food, of any kind, at night.

6.27–6.29

Seventh Station: Restraint Toward Earth-Bodied Beings

पुढविकायं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा । तिविहेणं करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया पुढविकायं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए । तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । पुढविकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm earth-bodied beings — by mind, by speech, or by body — through any of the three modes (doing, causing, approving). One who harms earth-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures who dwell on or within the earth — both the visible and the invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming earth-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

The seventh sthana opens the second movement of the chapter — the six classes of life-protection (kāya-ṣaṭka). These six gathas, repeated in the same triplet form for each of the six classes (earth, water, fire, air, plants, mobile beings), unfold the full scope of the first vow of non-violence. The structure is identical for all six: (1) the well-composed monk does not harm them by any of the nine doors; (2) one who harms them also harms the dependent beings living among them; (3) therefore renounce that activity for life. The first kāya is pṛthvīkāya — earth-bodied beings. According to the Jain analysis of life, even the mineral world has consciousness at its most rudimentary level; clay, soil, salt crystals, stones in their natural state are not inert matter but very small living beings with the single sense of touch. To dig, plough, build with raw clay, or mine is to inflict harm on these single-sense souls. The second gatha (6.28) extends the harm: when one digs the earth, one not only harms the earth-bodied beings themselves but also countless other beings — the worms, insects, microbes, and small creatures (visible and invisible) that depend on the soil for their existence. One act of harm always cascades. This is the deeper logic of Jain non-violence: harm is never localised; every action ripples outward through interdependent webs of life. The monk who keeps this vow does not therefore engage in any of the agricultural, mining, construction, or excavation activities that constitute ordinary worldly life. He sits, walks, and lives lightly — taking water and food prepared by others (already rendered acit by the householder for their own purposes), and never himself initiating harm.

Simply Put: The well-disciplined monks never harm earth-bodied beings — not by thinking it, saying it, or doing it; not personally, by another, or by approval. Whoever harms the earth also harms all the small creatures living on and in it, seen and unseen. So, knowing this leads to bad rebirths, the monks renounce digging and disturbing the earth for life.

6.30–6.32

Eighth Station: Restraint Toward Water-Bodied Beings

आउकायं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा । तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया आउकायं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए । तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । आउकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm water-bodied beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms water-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures dwelling within the water — both the visible and the invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming water-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

The eighth sthana applies the same triplet to apkāya — water-bodied beings. According to Jain biology, every drop of natural water is itself a single-sense living body. To draw water from a well, river, or rainfall is to inflict harm on countless of these tiny souls. To bathe in a river, to splash, to step into a pond — each act crushes their lives. And, as the second gatha emphasises, water also hosts countless other creatures — the microbes, plants, fish, and insects that dwell within it. The monk's response is total: never to draw water himself, never to wade in rivers, never to walk in the rain, never to use water as a means of cooling or cleaning that has not been rendered acit (lifeless) by the natural processes of household use (boiling, mixing with ash, etc.). When the monk needs water for drinking, he begs from a householder for water that has already been used for the householder's own purpose and is now no longer alive — the same principle by which the monk takes food. The monk does not initiate the killing; he accepts what has already been done by others for their own legitimate reasons. This vow creates the famous Jain monastic posture toward water: the avoidance of bathing (which becomes its own sthana later in the chapter), the careful sieving of drinking water, the refusal to walk in rain, the reverence with which water is handled. For Jain practice, water is not a resource but a community of small lives.

Simply Put: The disciplined monks never harm water-bodied beings — not in thought, word, or deed; not by themselves or through others. Whoever harms water harms all the tiny creatures inside it too. So, knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they renounce all such harming for life.

6.33–6.36

Ninth Station: Restraint Toward Fire-Bodied Beings

जायतेयं ण इच्छंति, पावगं जलइत्तए । तिक्खमण्णयरं सत्थं, सव्वओ वि दुरासयं पाईणं पडिणं वावि, उड्ढं अणुदिसामवि । अहे दाहिणओ वावि, दहे उत्तरओ वि य भूयाणमेसमाघाओ, हव्ववाहो ण संसओ । तं पईवपयावट्ठा, संजया किंचि णारभे तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । तेउकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

(The monks) do not wish to kindle fire — that sin-causing thing, sharp like any other weapon, an unbearable refuge from every side. (For fire) burns whatever is in the east, west, above, in the cross-directions, below, in the south, and in the north. This very fire-carrier is, without doubt, a destroyer of living beings; therefore the restrained ones undertake nothing whatsoever for the sake of (kindling fire for) lamplight or warming. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming fire-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

The ninth sthana introduces fire — teukāya / agnikāya — and the commentary's framing is striking. This four-gatha section (the only six-class entry that is more than three gathas) gives unusually full reasoning, because of all the six kāyas, fire is the most uniquely destructive. The text uses the word pāvaka — "the sin-causer" — for fire, deliberately inverting the popular religious term, which traditionally means "the purifier." For Jain analysis, fire is a uniquely powerful weapon: sharper than any blade, edged on every side, and a refuge from which no being trapped within can escape. Other weapons cut from one direction; fire burns from all directions at once. The second gatha (6.34) drives the point home with the listing of the seven directions: east, west, above, cross-directions, below, south, north — fire spreads to all of them. This is what makes it different from a weapon. A blade affects only what it touches; a fire affects everything in its zone, in every direction, and continues to spread until it consumes all available fuel. To light even a small flame is to release a destroyer that the monk cannot fully control. The third gatha then forbids even the practical uses of fire that ordinary people consider innocent: kindling it for lamplight (paīva), kindling it for warming oneself (payāva). Even these — the monks "undertake nothing whatsoever." The implication is severe but consistent. The monk does not light a lamp to read his scriptures at night (and so the night-eating prohibition becomes self-enforcing), does not light a fire to warm himself in winter (and so endures cold as an austerity), does not cook (and so depends entirely on the householder for food). The fire-vow shapes the entire texture of monastic life. Fire is the one element that the monk most resolutely refuses to touch — because once touched, it cannot be controlled, and the lives within and around it cannot be protected.

Simply Put: The monks do not even want to light a fire — because fire is sharper than any weapon, has no safe side, and burns living things in every direction. Even for lighting a lamp or warming themselves, they refuse to start a fire. So, knowing fire harms beings and leads to bad rebirths, they give up all such activity for life.

6.37–6.40

Tenth Station: Restraint Toward Air-Bodied Beings

अणिलस्स समारंभं, बुद्धा मण्णंति तारिसं । सावज्जबहुलं चेयं, णेयं ताईहिं सेवियं तालियंटेण पत्तेण, साहा विहुयणेण वा । ण ते वीइउमिच्छंति, वीयावेऊण वा परं जंपि वत्थं व पायं वा, कंबलं पायपुंछणं । ण ते वायमुईरंति, जयं परिहरंति य तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । वाउकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

The awakened ones consider the disturbing of air-bodies as severe as (the disturbing of fire-bodies); it is full of sin and is not practised by the protectors of the six classes. They do not wish to fan themselves with a palm-leaf fan, with a leaf, with a branch, or with a hand-fan — nor do they cause another to fan them. Even the cloth, bowl, blanket, or foot-wiper they keep, they do not stir into wind-creating motion; they use them with care. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming air-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

The tenth sthana addresses vāyukāya / anilakāya — air-bodied beings. The Jain teaching that even air is a community of single-sense beings is one of the most far-reaching extensions of non-violence in human ethics. Every breeze, every gust, every disturbance of the still air involves the harm of these very subtle living bodies. The Tirthankaras consider the disturbance of air to be as serious as the disturbance of fire — not because the harm is as visibly destructive, but because both are equally violations of the protection-of-life principle. The practical implications, set out in 6.38–6.39, are detailed. The monk does not use any fan: no palm-leaf, no leaf plucked from a tree, no branch waved as a fan, no hand-fan. He does not have another fan him. And even when handling his own permitted equipment — robe, bowl, blanket, broom — he handles them with such care (yatana) that he does not stir up wind in the air around him. The garments are worn slowly, the broom moved gently, the bowl carried evenly. The whole bodily life of the monk is one of slow, deliberate, wind-minimising motion. This vow shapes the very aesthetic of Jain monasticism. The famous slow, smooth, almost ceremonial movement of Jain monks and nuns is not affectation; it is the air-vow lived. To walk quickly is to disturb air. To speak loudly is to disturb air. To gesture sharply is to disturb air. The vow thereby produces a body that has become quiet at the most subtle level — an outer expression of the inner stillness the practice cultivates.

Simply Put: The Tirthankaras say disturbing the air is just as bad as disturbing fire. So the monks never fan themselves — not with a leaf, not with a branch, not with anyone's help. Even when handling their own clothes and tools, they move slowly so as not to stir up wind. They renounce all such air-disturbing activity for life.

6.41–6.43

Eleventh Station: Restraint Toward Plant-Bodied Beings

वणस्सइं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा । तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया वणस्सइं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए । तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । वणस्सइसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm plant-bodied beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms plant-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures who depend on them — both visible and invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming plant-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

The eleventh sthana applies the standard triplet to vanaspatikāya — plant-bodied beings. Plants too are single-sense beings (with the sense of touch), and to pluck, cut, uproot, or break them is to inflict harm on their living bodies. The second gatha (6.42) again emphasises the dependent ecology: plants harbour countless other lives — insects, microbes, small creatures — and the destruction of a plant always means the destruction of these dependents as well. The monk does not pluck flowers, does not pick fruits, does not gather vegetables, does not even touch growing plants if it can be avoided. The plant matter that supports his body comes entirely through the householder's vessel — vegetables that have been cooked, fruits that have been cut, all rendered acit (no longer living) by the householder's own kitchen process before reaching the monk. The monk's principle: do not initiate the harming, accept only what has already been transformed for the householder's own legitimate purpose. This is, again, the strict application of the non-violence vow. The plant-protection vow underlies the entire Jain dietary culture — including the avoidance of root vegetables (which contain whole live plants in dormant form, capable of regenerating), of fresh-picked produce by monks, and the careful timing of food preparation. The principle reaches into ordinary practice as the daily reverence for plant life that marks Jain households.

Simply Put: The well-disciplined monks never harm plants — in thought, word, or deed; alone or through others. Whoever harms plants harms all the small creatures living in them too. Knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they renounce harming plants for life.

6.44–6.46

Twelfth Station: Restraint Toward Mobile Beings

तसकायं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा । तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया तसकायं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए । तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । तसकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm mobile beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms mobile beings also harms the various other mobile and immobile beings who dwell in association with them — both visible and invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming mobile beings for as long as one lives.

The twelfth sthana — the last of the six kāya-protections — covers trasakāya, mobile beings. These are the beings with two or more senses: from the simple worm (two senses — touch and taste), through ants and insects (three senses), bees and butterflies (four senses), to fish, birds, mammals, and humans (five senses). All of them — visible and invisible, small and large — are protected by the monk's vow. The monk's task with respect to mobile beings is one of constant attentiveness. Insects appear suddenly — in food, in water, in the path. The monk inspects everything: the place where he sits is swept gently with the broom; food is examined before eating; water is filtered before drinking; the path is watched at every step. Yatana — careful attentiveness — is the inner discipline that operationalises this vow. The second gatha makes the now-familiar point: harming a mobile being also harms the dependents — the other small creatures in its vicinity, the eggs in its body, the microbes on its skin. Every act of harm is in fact many acts of harm. The monk who has internalised this teaching does not need to be told to be careful; the carefulness arises naturally from the seeing. With this twelfth sthana, the great six-fold vow of life-protection is complete. From the imperceptible single-sense earth-body to the most developed five-sense human, every form of life is brought under the canopy of non-violence. The vow has covered the entire spectrum of consciousness in the universe — and shown that the monk's life is the one in which no part of that spectrum is allowed to suffer at his hands.

Simply Put: The disciplined monks never harm any moving creature — from the tiniest worm to the largest animal — in thought, word, or deed; alone or through others. Whoever harms a moving creature harms others connected to it as well. Knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they give up such harming for life.

6.47

Thirteenth Station: Renunciation of Forbidden Articles (Akalpya)

जाइं चत्तारि अभोज्जाइं, इसिणाहारमाइणि । ताइं तु विवज्जंतो, संजमं अणुपालए

The four (categories) which are inedible for sages — beginning with the food (and including drink, chewables, savouries) — those, indeed, completely renouncing, one should preserve restraint.

The thirteenth sthana opens the third movement of the chapter — the six rules of practical monastic discipline — beginning with the renunciation of akalpya (the forbidden, the unfit-for-monks). The four categories of consumables — aśana (staple food), pāna (drink), khādya (chewables), and svādya (savouries) — are not inherently forbidden in themselves; what is forbidden is their acquisition by methods unfit for a sage. Food obtained through any of the dosha (faults) of pinda-eshana — food cooked specifically for the monk, food obtained from forbidden households, food bought, food forced upon a host, food still containing live beings, food past its proper time — all of this becomes akalpya and must be refused. This sthana refers back to the entire fifth adhyayan (Pinda-eshana), which lists in great detail the forty-six doshas of food acquisition that a monk must avoid. The thirteenth sthana of conduct is, in a sense, the application of all that previous teaching to the daily practice. The monk preserves saṃyama — restraint — precisely by refusing the forbidden food, even when hunger urges, even when the food appears appetising, even when refusing causes embarrassment. The discipline of refusal is itself the discipline of monkhood. The verse uses the verb anupālae — "should preserve / should continue protecting." Restraint is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing protection. Every meal-time is a fresh exercise of the vow. The monk who keeps his discipline at the moment of eating is the monk who keeps his monkhood at all.

Simply Put: The four categories of food — staple food, drink, chewables, and savouries — should be refused whenever they come in forms unfit for monks (such as being cooked specifically for them). By refusing such food, the monk keeps his vow of restraint alive.

6.48

Refuse the Forbidden in All Four Categories; Accept the Permissible

पिंडं सेज्जं च वत्थं च, चउत्थं पायमेव य । अकप्पियं ण इच्छिज्जा, पडिगाहिज्ज कप्पियं

Food, lodging, cloth, and fourth — the bowl: whatever is forbidden, one should not desire; whatever is permissible, one should accept.

This gatha extends the akalpya principle from food alone to the four categories of items the monk receives from householders: piṇḍa (food), śayyā (lodging), vastra (cloth), and pātra (bowl). For each of the four, what is offered may be either kalpya (fit for monks — meeting all the rules of acceptance) or akalpya (unfit — falling under any of the doshas). The discipline is uniform: refuse the forbidden, accept the permissible. The principle is one of active discrimination at every interaction with the lay community. The monk does not accept whatever is offered out of politeness; he does not refuse whatever is offered out of austerity; he discriminates. He examines what is offered against the rules he has taken, and accepts only what passes. This careful inspection is itself a form of restraint — it trains the discriminating faculty (viveka) and protects against the slow erosion of the discipline through "small acceptances." The four categories define the entire material support of the monastic life. Beyond food, lodging, cloth, and bowl, the monk needs nothing. To keep the discipline of kalpya-only at all four points is to keep the monkhood entirely. To slip even at one — to accept a fancier robe, a softer place to sleep, a special-purpose vessel — is to begin the slide.

Simply Put: For all four things a monk receives — food, place to stay, cloth, and bowl — refuse whatever is forbidden by the rules, and accept only what is permitted.

6.49–6.50

The Four Doshas of Forbidden Food: Why They Implicate the Monk in Killing

जे णियागं ममायंति, कीयमुद्देसियाहडं । वहं ते समणुजाणंति, इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा तम्हा असणपाणाइं, कीयमुद्देसियाहडं । वज्जयंति ठियप्पाणो, निग्गंथा धम्मजीविणो

Those (monks) who take as their own the perpetually-invited food, the bought food, the food prepared specifically for them, and the food brought from afar to them — they (in effect) approve of the killing (involved); thus has it been declared by the great seer. Therefore, the steady-souled Nirgranthas, who live by dharma, completely renounce food, drink, and the like that is perpetually-invited, bought, prepared specifically, or brought from afar.

These two gathas name the four most serious doshas of food acceptance and explain why even taking such food makes the monk a participant in violence. The four are: (1) nityāmantrita / niyāga — the food of a standing daily invitation, where the householder cooks every day specifically expecting the monk; (2) krīta — food purchased specifically to give to the monk; (3) audeśika — food prepared specifically with the monk in mind, even without being sold; (4) ābhṛta — food brought from a distant kitchen specifically for the monk. The reasoning is given in the third line of 6.49: vahaṃ te samaṇujāṇaṃti — "they approve of the killing." How does the monk who merely accepts the food approve of killing? Because the cooking process always involves harm — to water-bodies, fire-bodies, plant-bodies, and the small mobile beings caught up in the kitchen. When food is cooked for the householder's own purpose, the harm is already done independently of the monk; the monk neither caused nor approved it. But when the food is cooked for the monk — by standing invitation, by purchase, by specific intention, by special transport — the monk becomes the cause of the cooking, and accepting the food is the silent approval of the violence performed for his sake. The first karana is closed (the monk did not kill); but the second (causing) and third (approving) are violated. This is one of the most precise ethical analyses in Jain literature. The vow of non-violence is broken not only by direct killing but by being the reason for killing — even at second remove. The monk's discipline therefore shapes the householder's behaviour from a distance: the monk refuses any food cooked for him, and in so doing keeps the householder from initiating violence on his behalf. The whole begging-system — taking only what was already cooked for the family — is structured precisely to protect against this complicity.

Simply Put: If a monk takes food that was specially invited, bought, cooked, or brought from afar just for him, he is silently approving the killing involved in preparing it. So the steady-souled Nirgranthas who truly live by dharma refuse all such food and drink.

6.51–6.53

Fourteenth Station: Renunciation of the Householder's Vessels

कंसेसु कंसपाएसु, कुंडमोएसु वा पुणो । भुंजंतो असणपाणाइं, आयारा परिभस्सइ सीओदगसमारंभे, मत्तधोयणछड्डणे । जाइं छण्णंति भूयाइं, दिट्ठो तत्थ असंजमो पच्छाकम्मं पुरेकम्मं, सिया तत्थ ण कप्पइ । एयमट्ठं ण भुंजंति, णिग्गंथा गिहिभायणे

One who eats food, drink, and the like in the householder's bell-metal vessels, bell-metal plates, or large bowls falls from the (monastic) discipline. In the violent disturbance of cold (live) water, in the throwing-out of the rinsing-water, the living beings that get destroyed — there the lack of restraint is plainly seen. After-action (washing the vessel after the monk eats) and before-action (washing the vessel before serving the monk) might occur — these are not permissible; for this very reason, the Nirgranthas do not eat from the householder's vessels.

The fourteenth sthana addresses why the monk does not eat from the householder's plates and vessels. The reasoning, given in three gathas, has two strands: the violation of the discipline of monastic identity (saṃyama-virādhanā) and the violation of life (jīva-virādhanā). First, saṃyama-virādhanā. The monk's distinct dress, distinct equipment, and distinct manner of eating from his own permitted bowl maintain his self-identity as a monk. When he sits at the householder's table and eats from the householder's polished bell-metal plate, the visual and behavioural distinction collapses. Both the monk and any onlookers begin to lose the sense that he is a monk. The whole structure of monastic identity — kept in place by visible difference — quietly erodes. Other householders watching may also begin to doubt his monkhood. The teaching's reputation suffers. Second, jīva-virādhanā — the harm to life. The householder's vessel must be washed. If washed before serving the monk (purekarma), the washing involves the use of water-bodied beings — done specifically for the monk, which makes him the cause. If washed after the monk eats (paścātkarma), the same harm occurs, again caused by the monk's having used the vessel. Even the rinsing-water thrown out kills beings on the ground where it falls. The monk's own bowl, by contrast, is wiped clean (not water-washed) and used only by him; no harm to water-bodies is required. The discipline of using only the permitted bowl is therefore the discipline of leaving water-bodied beings undisturbed.

Simply Put: If a monk eats from the householder's bronze plates, bowls, or large vessels, he loses his monastic discipline. The water used to wash those vessels — before or after he eats — kills tiny water-creatures, and the harm is caused by the monk's act. So the monks never eat from the householder's vessels; they use only their own permitted bowls.

6.54–6.56

Fifteenth Station: Renunciation of Couches, Beds, and Cushioned Seats

आसंदीपलियंकेसु, मंचमासालएसु वा । अणायरियमज्जाणं, आसइत्तु सइत्तु वा णासंदीपलियंकेसु, ण णिसिज्जा ण पीढए । णिग्गंथा (अ)पडिलेहाए, बुद्धवुत्तमहिट्ठगा गंभीरविजया एए, पाणा दुप्पडिलेहगा । आसंदी पलियंका य, एयमट्ठं विवज्जिया

For (these) noble monks, to sit or to sleep upon small cot-stools, large couches (palaṃka), bedsteads (mañca), or cane easy-chairs (māsālaka) is unbecoming. The Nirgranthas — who follow the words of the awakened ones, who keep the discipline of (re-)inspection (pratilekhanā) — do not (sit) on these cot-stools and couches, nor on (cushioned) sitting-mats, nor on stools. These (seats) have deep cavities; the small living beings within them cannot be properly inspected; for this reason, cot-stools and large couches are renounced.

The fifteenth sthana addresses the furniture on which the monk does not sit or sleep. The reason given is precise and characteristic of Jain practice: such furniture has deep cavities, hollow joints, and unseen interiors in which small living beings (insects, mites, eggs) take shelter. These cannot be properly inspected (pratilekhanā) before use, and so sitting or sleeping on them necessarily involves the crushing of unseen lives. The first vow of non-violence is therefore the operative reason — though there is a second reason as well, hinted at in the commentary: such cushioned, comfortable furniture induces pramāda (negligence, drowsiness), which is the cardinal vice of the spiritual life. The Jain practice of pratilekhanā — the careful examination, by hand and eye, of every cloth, every place, every utensil before its use — is one of the most distinctive features of monastic life. The monk inspects his robe before putting it on, his sitting-place before sitting, his sleeping-mat before lying down, his bowl before eating from it. The purpose: to find and protect any living being that might be present. Furniture that cannot be inspected — because of its construction, its joints, its inaccessible interior — fails this requirement and is therefore avoided. The commentary notes that an exception (apavāda mārga) exists. If, for some reason of dharma — visiting a royal household, etc. — the monk must sit on such furniture, it can be done after careful inspection. But the general rule (utsarga mārga) is to avoid all such seats. The monk sits on a swept floor, on his own simple cloth (the paṭṭa), or on a wooden plank (pāṭa) with a thin sheet — all of which can be inspected fully and offer no hiding-place to small life.

Simply Put: The monks do not sit or sleep on small cots, large couches, bedsteads, or cushioned chairs — because such furniture has hollow places where tiny creatures hide and cannot be checked. Following the awakened ones, the monks who maintain careful inspection avoid all such seats.

6.57–6.60

Sixteenth Station: Not Sitting in the Householder's Home

गोयरग्गपविट्ठस्स, णिसिज्जा जस्स कप्पइ । इमेरिसमणायारं, आवज्जइ अबोहियं विवत्ती बंभचेरस्स, पाणाणं च वहे वहो । वणीमगपडिग्घाओ, पडिकोहो अगारिणं अगुत्ती बंभचेरस्स, इत्थिओ यावि संकणं । कुसीलवड्ढणं ठाणं, दूरओ परिवज्जए तिण्हमण्णयरागस्स, णिसिज्जा जस्स कप्पइ । जराए अभिभूयस्स, वाहियस्स तवस्सिणो

For one who has entered the begging-round, sitting (in the householder's home) is permissible — only (in special cases). Otherwise (the monk who sits there) attracts these unbecoming faults that bring forth ignorance: failure of celibacy; killing of living beings; the cutting-off of (other) beggars; the anger of the householders. (Sitting there causes) the un-guardedness of celibacy, the suspicion of women, a place that increases bad conduct — flee it from a far distance. Of (only) one of three (kinds of monks) is sitting there permissible: of one overcome by old age, of one diseased, and of one (great) ascetic (whose long austerity prevents normal walking).

The sixteenth sthana addresses sitting in the householder's home. The general rule (utsarga mārga): the monk enters for the begging-round, accepts the food, and immediately leaves; no sitting, no settling. The four gathas explain why with unusual specificity, naming the faults that arise from prolonged stay. Sutra 6.58 lists four direct harms. Failure of celibacy — extended exposure to the women of the household, the sights, the sounds, the conversations, weakens the celibacy vow. Killing of living beings — the householder, embarrassed by the monk's continued presence, may prepare special food, special drinks, special arrangements, all involving harm to small life. Cutting-off of other beggars — the householder, occupied with the monk, turns away other alms-seekers who would otherwise have received help. Anger of the householders — long stays disrupt the household's routine, irritate other members, and produce resentment toward the monk and the order. Sutra 6.59 names the deeper psychological harms. The vow of celibacy needs gupti — guarding — and prolonged contact with the household's women creates aguptī, the failure of that guarding. The women themselves come under suspicion (either of misbehaviour, or of inducing it). The householder's home thus becomes a kusīla-vardhana-sthāna — a "place that increases bad conduct." The instruction is unequivocal: dūrao parivajjae — flee from it from a great distance. Sutra 6.60 then introduces the exception (apavāda mārga). For three kinds of monks, sitting in the householder's home is permitted: (1) the very old, (2) the sick, (3) the great ascetic worn out by long austerity. These three may, with the householder's permission, sit briefly to rest — for them the body cannot continue without it. The general rule remains for everyone else. The presence of an exception does not weaken the rule; it shows that even the rule is held within wisdom, not rigidity.

Simply Put: When a monk goes for alms, he should not sit down in the householder's home. Sitting there leads to four problems: weakened celibacy, harm to small lives, blocking other beggars, and angering the household. It also tempts him into bad conduct. So he should avoid such places from far away. The only exceptions: very old monks, sick monks, and monks worn out by long austerity may sit briefly with permission.

6.61–6.63

Seventeenth Station: Renunciation of Bathing

वाहिओ वा अरोगी वा, सिणाणं जो उ पत्थए । वुक्कंतो होइ आयारो, जढो हवइ संजमो संतिमे सुहुमा पाणा, घसासु भिलुगासु य । जे उ भिक्खू सिणायंतो, वियडेणुप्पिलावए तम्हा ते ण सिणायंति, सीएण उसिणेण वा । जावज्जीवं वयं घोरं, असिणाणमहिट्ठगा

Whoever — diseased or healthy — desires bathing: his conduct is broken; his restraint is lost. There exist these subtle living beings — in saline soil and in the cracks of the earth; the monk who bathes (with even cleansed water) sweeps them away in the (water-)flood. Therefore they (the monks) do not bathe — neither with cold nor with hot water; for as long as life lasts, they uphold the awe-inspiring vow of non-bathing.

The seventeenth sthana — the renunciation of bathing — strikes most modern listeners as unusual, but the reasoning is twofold and entirely consistent with the chapter's logic. First, non-violence: bathing with live (un-boiled) water directly kills countless water-bodied beings; even bathing with boiled (acit) water washes that water across the ground, into cracks and porous soil where countless small mobile beings dwell, and sweeps them to their death. The second gatha (6.62) makes this exact point: the porous ground and the cracks of the earth shelter subtle lives, and the bath-water flood destroys them. Second, celibacy: bathing is a form of bodily care that easily slides into beautification — the body becomes pleasant to itself, attractive to others, and the mind is stirred. Bathing is one of the principal vibhuṣā practices (the eighteenth sthana, coming next), and the seventeenth and eighteenth sthanas together build a single defence: the monk's body is to be a means of practice, not an object of beautification. Without bathing, without ornamentation, the body becomes neutral — neither despised nor pampered, simply allowed to be the vehicle of the spiritual life. Sutra 6.61 is striking: whether diseased or healthy. Even illness is not a ground for bathing in the monastic discipline. The vow is absolute. To bathe — even from medical reason — is to break conduct (vukkanto āyāro) and to lose restraint (jaḍho saṃjamo). The text emphasises this severity to make clear that the renunciation is total. Sutra 6.63 confirms: not with cold water, not with hot water, never — jāvajjīvaṃ, for the whole of life. The monks are asiṇāṇa-ahiṭṭhagā — "the upholders of non-bathing," a noble title for those who keep this hardest of bodily disciplines. The text accepts that this is ghora — awe-inspiring, severe — but precisely as one of the marks of true monkhood. Note: in the Nishitha Sutra, partial washings of hands, feet, or mouth (when stained or unclean for some practical reason) are addressed as faults of celibacy with prescribed atonements; they are not casual permissions. The general vow stands.

Simply Put: Whoever wants to bathe — sick or healthy — breaks the conduct and loses the restraint. There are tiny living beings hiding in the porous soil and earth-cracks; even bathing with boiled water sweeps them away to their death. So the monks never bathe — not in cold water, not in hot — and they keep this severe vow of non-bathing for life.

6.64–6.65

Eighteenth Station: Renunciation of Bodily Ornamentation

सिणाणं अदुवा कक्कं, लोद्धं पउमगाणि य । गायस्सुव्वट्टणट्ठाए, णायरंति कयाइ वि णगिणस्स वावि मुंडस्स, दीहरोमणहंसिणो । मेहुणाओ उवसंतस्स, किं विभूसाए कारियं

(They use) bath-substances (perfumed cleansing pastes), or ointment-pastes (sandal-paste, fragrant pastes), or astringent-powder (loddha — a herbal cleansing-powder), or saffron-petal-powders (kuṅkuma, kesar) — for the rubbing-and-anointing of the body — never, never. Of one who is unrobed (or wears the bare minimum), shaven-headed, with long body-hair and long nails, who is utterly tranquil regarding sexual conduct — what use does he have for ornamentation?

The eighteenth and final sthana — vibhūṣā-varjana, the renunciation of bodily ornamentation — completes the chapter. The first gatha names the principal means of beautification that the monk must never use: perfumed bath-substances, fragrant ointment-pastes, herbal cleansing powders, saffron-petal pigments. These are the ordinary cosmetics of the day — the things householders apply to look attractive, smell pleasant, and adorn the skin. The monk uses none of them, ever — kayāi vi, the text emphasises with that double negative. The second gatha then asks the deeper question through a portrait. Picture the true monk: he wears either no robe (Jinakalpi) or the simplest of cloths; his head is shaven (or the hair pulled out by hand at the periodic keśa-loca); his body-hair and nails grow long because he does not trim them; he has become entirely tranquil with respect to sexual conduct. What possible use does such a person have for ornamentation? The body has become an instrument; appearance has lost meaning; the senses have stopped seeking attraction. Ornamentation serves a purpose only for one who still seeks to be seen, to be desired, to be admired. The monk has stepped outside that game entirely. This vow is therefore the natural completion of the celibacy vow. As long as the body is being decorated, the unconscious aim of the body is to attract — even to oneself, in the mirror. The monk who has taken the celibacy vow seriously cannot continue to decorate the body, because the decoration would itself stir the very inner movements that the celibacy vow is designed to still. The eighteenth sthana is not an arbitrary austerity; it is the body-discipline that allows the celibacy of the heart to actually take hold.

Simply Put: The monks never use perfumed bath powders, fragrant pastes, cleansing powders, or coloured beautifying substances on their body — never. Picture a monk: barely clothed, shaven-headed, with uncut hair and nails, completely calm about sexual matters. What possible use would such a person have for ornamentation?

6.66

Ornamentation Binds Sticky Karma That Drowns the Soul in Samsara

विभूसावत्तियं भिक्खू, कम्मं बंधइ चिक्कणं । संसारसायरे घोरे, जेणं पडइ दुरुत्तरे

A monk who acts on account of ornamentation binds sticky karma — by which he falls into the dreadful ocean of worldly existence (saṃsāra) that is exceedingly hard to cross.

This gatha gives the karmic consequence of the practice that the previous two gathas forbade. The monk who acts in any way on account of ornamentation — applying it, desiring it, even mentally entertaining it — binds cikkaṇa karma: sticky, dense karma that adheres to the soul tightly and is exceedingly hard to dissolve. By binding such karma, the monk falls into the ghora saṃsāra-sāgara — the dreadful ocean of worldly existence — that is duruttara, hard to cross. The Jain analysis of karma distinguishes between karma that adheres lightly (and dissolves quickly) and karma that adheres densely (and persists for many lifetimes). The factor that determines stickiness is principally the intensity of passion with which the act is committed. Casual, unattached acts bind light karma; passionate, ego-attached acts bind sticky karma. Bodily ornamentation is by its nature an ego-amplifying act — it is precisely the cultivation of self-image, attractiveness, vanity. So the karma it binds is heavy. For a monk, who has renounced all such cultivation, the act is doubly inappropriate: it contradicts the vow at every level, and the karma it binds is among the densest the soul can accumulate. The result is a long, hard journey through saṃsāra. The teaching is severe but logically consistent with the chapter. Each of the eighteen sthanas, when violated, multiplies durgati — bad destinies. Ornamentation, which sits at the intersection of celibacy and possession (both deep ego-investments), produces especially heavy karmic binding. Hence the strong language: ghora (dreadful), duruttara (hard to cross). The monk who genuinely understands this has no inclination toward decoration; the inclination itself has been seen for what it is.

Simply Put: A monk who decorates himself binds heavy, sticky karma — and that karma drops him into a fearful ocean of worldly rebirths that is extremely hard to cross.

6.67

Even the Mind That Dwells on Ornamentation Is Sinful

विभूसावत्तियं चेयं, बुद्धा मण्णंति तारिसं । सावज्जबहुलं चेयं, णेयं ताईहिं सेवियं

The very mind that turns toward ornamentation — the awakened ones consider that mind itself as such (as a producer of bondage). It too is full of sin; it is not to be cultivated by the protectors (of the six classes of life).

This gatha pushes the eighteenth sthana to its inward limit. It is not enough to refrain from the act of ornamentation; the very mental movement toward ornamentation — the inner wish, the inner imagining, the inner fascination — is itself a producer of sinful karma. The Tirthankaras hold that the cetas, the state of consciousness inclining toward decoration, is as binding as the act itself. There is no inner-outer split; the mind that wants to decorate is already binding karma, even if no decoration ever appears on the body. This is the inner deepening of every vow that the chapter has taught. The non-violence vow is not just about the act of killing but the mind that contemplates harming. The truth vow is not just about the speaking but the mind that drafts the lie. The non-stealing vow is not just about the taking but the mind that covets. The celibacy vow is not just about the act but the mind that dwells. The non-possession vow is not just about the holding but the mind that grasps. And now the ornamentation vow: not just about the application but the mind that desires the application. The phrase ṇeyaṃ tāīhiṃ seviyaṃ — "it is not to be cultivated by the protectors of the six classes" — closes the gatha with a beautiful identification. The monk is tāyī — protector. He protects all six classes of life from harm. And he protects his own inner life from the cultivation of states that bind heavy karma. The protection is total — outward and inward, of others and of self.

Simply Put: The Tirthankaras say: even the mind that turns toward decorating itself is full of sin. The monks who protect all life — must not let even their thoughts drift toward such ornamentation.

6.68

The Conclusion: Those Who Keep These Eighteen Burn Past Karmas, Bind No New Ones

खवेंति अप्पाणममोहदंसिणो, तवे रया संजम अज्जवे गुणे । धुणंति पावाइं पुरे कडाइं, णवाइं पावाइं ण ते कुर्वंति

Those whose vision is undeluded — absorbed in austerity, in restraint, in straightforwardness, and in (all such) qualities — wear away (the karma of) their (outer / passion-bound) self; they shake off the sinful karmas previously committed; and they do not commit new sinful karmas.

The chapter draws to its close with two beautiful gathas of summation. This first conclusion-gatha names the inner state of the practitioner who has truly kept the eighteen sthanas. Such a one is amoha-darśin — undeluded in vision; free of the fundamental confusion that mistakes the body for the self, the impermanent for the permanent, the painful for the pleasurable. They are absorbed (rata) in the qualities that constitute the spiritual life: austerity, restraint, straightforwardness, and all the supporting virtues. The fruit of this absorption is threefold. First, khaveṃti appāṇaṃ — they wear away the outer self, the karma-stained personality that has carried the soul through countless births. Second, dhuṇaṃti pāvāiṃ pure kaḍāiṃ — they shake off, like dust shaken from a cloth, the sinful karmas they have previously committed. Third, ṇavāiṃ pāvāiṃ ṇa te kurvaṃti — they do not commit new sinful karmas. These three together are the complete formula of liberation: removal of past karma (nirjarā), prevention of new karma (saṃvara), and the consequent dissolution of the karma-bound self. The eighteen sthanas of conduct that the chapter has just taught are precisely the technology by which this triple result is produced. By keeping the great vows and the protections of the six classes of life, no new karma is bound (saṃvara). By the austerities and restraints, the past karma is burned away (nirjarā). And by the absorption in the resulting qualities, the outward-turned self gradually dissolves, until the pure inner self alone remains. The chapter has therefore not only described conduct; it has described the means by which the soul becomes free. Conduct is not an end but the technique by which the goal is reached.

Simply Put: Those who see clearly — without delusion — and who give themselves to austerity, restraint, and straightforwardness, slowly burn away the bad karma they did before, and never make new bad karma. That is how the soul is set free.

6.69

Pure as the Autumn Moon: They Reach Liberation or the Highest Heavens

सओवसंता अममा अकिंचणा, सविज्जविज्जाणुगया जसंसिणो । उउप्पसण्णे विमलेव चंदिमा, सिद्धिं विमाणाइं उवेंति ताइणो ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥

Those forever calmed (sadā-upaśānta), free of mine-feeling (amama), without possession (akiṃcana), accomplished in their own (self-)knowledge (sva-vidyā-anugata), of great fame (yaśasvin), the protectors (of the six classes of life) — pure as the spotless moon in the clear autumn sky — attain liberation, or (if a residue of karma remains) the celestial palaces of the heavens. Thus do I declare.

The chapter closes with one of the most beautiful gathas in the entire Dashvaikalik. It paints the portrait of the realised monk in nine words — and ends with a celestial image. Saovasaṃtā — forever tranquil; the inner storms of passion have stilled. Amamā — free of mine-feeling; the deepest knot of the ego has loosened. Akiṃcaṇā — without any possession; the outer life mirrors the inner freedom. Savijja-vijjāṇugayā — accomplished in self-knowledge; the inner light has been kindled and is steady. Jasaṃsiṇo — of great fame; the world honours such beings, though they themselves do not seek the honour. Then comes the image: uuppasaṇṇe vimaleva caṃdimā — like the spotless full moon in the perfectly clear autumn sky. The autumn season in the Indian calendar — śarat ṛtu — is the season of clearest skies; the rains have ended, the dust has settled, and the moon shines undimmed. The realised monk is exactly that — the soul, naturally luminous, has been fully revealed because every veil of karma has been removed. There is no further obscuration. The moon shines as it truly is. Nothing more needs to be added. The destination of such a monk is then named with characteristic precision: siddhiṃ vimāṇāiṃ uveṃti — they attain siddhi (final liberation, bodiless eternal freedom) or, if any residue of karma remains, the vimāṇa (celestial palaces of the highest heavens). Either is the fruit of the conduct described in this chapter. The closing formula tti bemi — "thus do I declare" — is Sudharma Swami's seal: this entire chapter is exactly what he heard from Mahavira, transmitted faithfully, without addition or subtraction. It is the signature of authenticity that closes the great account of conduct.

Simply Put: Those who keep this conduct become forever calm, with no "mine," no possessions, full of self-knowledge, and held in the highest honour — they shine like the perfect moon in a clear autumn sky. Such monks attain final liberation; or, if some karma remains, they are reborn in the highest celestial palaces. Thus do I declare.
