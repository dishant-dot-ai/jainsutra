Ācāra-Praṇidhi આચારપ્રણિધિ

The Eighth Adhyayan — the Resolution of Conduct. The complete daily conduct of a monk: care for six classes of life, eight kinds of subtle beings, restraint of all five senses, four kashayas, and the inner discipline.

Adhyayan 8: Ācāra-Praṇidhi

About This Adhyayan

The Daily Resolution

The eighth adhyayan presents the monk's daily conduct as a single sustained praṇidhi — a resolution, a focused commitment held continuously through the day. It opens with care for the six classes of living beings, then expands to the eight kinds of subtle beings (different categories of micro-life), then to the careful use of monastic equipment through pratilekhana (visual inspection) and pramārjana (gentle cleansing).

The chapter then turns inward: restraint of the five senses, control of the four kashayas (anger, pride, deceit, greed), the discipline of speech, the avoidance of pramāda (negligence). It is essentially a daily curriculum for the monk who has taken the vows in earlier chapters and now must live them out hour by hour. The chapter has 25-64 sutras depending on the edition, ending with the famous 'अध्ययन-૮ સંપૂર્ણ.'

Sacred Text

The Sutras of Adhyayan 8

Prakrit original with English translation, commentary, and the simple explanation.

8.1

The Vow to Teach the Conduct

आयारप्पणिहिं लद्धुं, जहा कायव्व भिक्खुणा । तं भे उदाहरिस्सामि, आणुपुव्विं सुणेह मे

Having received the resolution of conduct (achar pranidhi), the manner in which a monk must conduct himself — that I shall narrate to you in due order; listen to me.

The chapter opens with a teacher's pledge to a pupil. The teacher does not begin with rules but with a frame: "you have received the discipline; now learn how to live it." The Gujarati commentary draws an important distinction here. Receiving the achar (taking ordination, accepting the great vows) is not itself liberation — it is only "stepping onto the target." Once the arrow has reached the target, the real work of penetration, of working through layer after layer, begins. This is why the very first word of the chapter is laddhuṃ — "having received" — and the next word is jahā — "as it is to be performed." The traditional commentaries explain achar as the various means that lead to liberation, and pranidhi as the storehouse, the gathering, the focused inward holding of those means. Achar-pranidhi together is therefore the concentrated resolve of a monk to live the conduct fully. It is both the discipline received from outside (from the Guru) and the discipline assumed inside (the monk's own resolve). The chapter to follow is the unfolding of what that resolve looks like in actual hour-by-hour practice.

Simply Put: "You have stepped into monastic life. Now I will tell you, step by step, exactly how to live it. Listen."

8.2

The Six Classes of Life

पुढवि दग अगणि मारुय, तणरुक्ख सबीयगा । तसा य पाणा जीवित्ति, इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा

Earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, all vegetation including grass, trees and seed-bearing plants, and the mobile (trasa) beings — all these are living beings; thus has it been declared by the great seers (the omniscient ones).

This sutra is the foundational classification of all life as the Jain tradition sees it. Earth, water, fire, and air — which the modern world treats as inert substances — are here treated as bodies of one-sensed living beings, each capable of suffering, each entitled to protection. To these four immobile (sthavara) categories the sutra adds a fifth — vegetation, which the Jain tradition further subdivides into pratyeka (single-souled plants) and sadharana (multi-souled plants like roots and tubers). The sixth category, trasa, holds all beings that can move on their own — insects, animals, humans, devas. The line "इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा" — "thus has been declared by the great seers" — anchors the classification not in observation but in the omniscient knowledge of the Tirthankaras. The monk's compassion is therefore not a sentiment but a knowledge-ethic: because the seers have seen these beings, the monk must protect them. This sutra is the philosophical backbone of every rule that follows in this chapter.

Simply Put: The omniscient ones have declared that there are six classes of living beings: earth, water, fire, air, plants, and the mobile creatures. All six are alive.

8.3

Non-Violence by Mind, Speech, and Body

तेसिं अच्छणजोएण, निच्चं होयव्वयं सिया । मणसा कायवक्केण, एवं हवइ संजए

Toward those (six classes of beings), one must always remain in the mode of non-violence — by mind, by body, and by speech. Only thus does one become a true samyata (a self-restrained monk).

The sutra defines what it means to be a samyata, a true monk. It is not a costume or an ordination certificate; it is a sustained mode of non-violence held continuously across all three doors of activity — thought, word, and body. The rare term acchaṇa-joeṇa literally means "engagement in non-injury": every act of body, every word, every thought must be screened through the question, "does this injure another being?" If even one of the three is left unscreened, the monk's status as samyata is incomplete. This is why the threefold formula recurs throughout this chapter.

Simply Put: To be a real monk is to refuse, always, by thought-word-body, to harm any of those six classes of beings.

8.4

Care of the Earth-Bodied

पुढविं भित्तिं सिलं लेलुं, नेव भिंदे न संलिहे । तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजए सुसमाहिए

Pure earth, walls, stone, lumps of clay — the well-composed restrained monk shall neither break nor scratch (any of these), by all three karanas and three yogas.

Having affirmed that earth is itself a body of life, the chapter moves to what that means in practice. The monk is forbidden to dig, scratch, scrape, or even chip away at any of the four substrates listed: untouched ground, the inner face of a wall, stone, or a clod of clay. The prohibition uses two precise Prakrit verbs — bhinde (to break, to splinter) and samlihe (to scrape, to abrade) — to cover both the dramatic act of digging and the smaller, more invisible act of idly scratching at a wall with a fingernail. The monk's relationship to the very ground he walks upon is one of restraint, not use.

Simply Put: The well-composed monk does not break, scratch, or scrape any uncultivated ground, wall, stone, or clay-lump — by any of the nine modes (three karanas times three yogas).

8.5

On Sitting and Seats

सुद्धपुढवीए न निसीए, ससरक्खम्मि य आसणे । पमज्जित्तु निसीएज्जा, जाइत्ता जस्स उग्गहं

(The monk) shall not sit upon untouched (sentient) earth, nor upon a seat covered with sentient dust. Having asked permission of the owner, and having gently brushed (the surface), only then shall he sit.

This sutra introduces two key disciplines that govern every act of sitting in the monk's life. First, the monk does not sit on still-sentient ground or on any surface dusted with sentient particles, because to do so would crush living earth-beings. Second, the monk does not occupy any place without first formally asking the owner's permission (avagraha) — a rule that protects against the subtler fault of taking what has not been freely given. Only after both conditions are satisfied — sentient surfaces ruled out and permission obtained — does the monk perform pramarjana, the gentle brushing with the woollen broom (rajoharana) to displace any tiny mobile beings that may have settled. Then, and only then, may he sit.

Simply Put: Don't sit on living earth or on a dusty seat. First ask the owner of the place. Then sweep gently with the broom. Then sit.

8.6

Care of Water-Bodied Life

सीओदगं न सेविज्जा, सिलावुट्ठं हिमाणि य । उसिणोदगं तत्तफासुयं, पडिगाहेज्ज संजए

The monk shall not consume cold (sentient) water, nor hailstones, nor snow or ice. He shall accept only hot water (which has been thoroughly boiled and rendered lifeless) — the monk shall accept (only) such achitta, prasuk water.

Water in its natural state is the body of countless ap-kaya beings. To drink it is to crush them. The Jain monastic discipline therefore restricts the monk's water entirely to prasuk water — water in which life-force has been destroyed by sufficient heating. The text uses two technical words: tatta (fully heated through) and phasuya (prasuk, lifeless). The Gujarati commentary notes carefully that water which is merely warm (e.g. naturally warm spring water) is not prasuk — only water that has been actively boiled. Even the act of avoiding cold water is, here, an act of compassion.

Simply Put: The monk does not drink cold water, hailstones, or snow. He accepts only water that has been boiled enough to be lifeless.

8.7

On a Wet Body

उदउल्लं अप्पणो कायं, नेव पुंछे न संलिहे । समुप्पेह तहाभूयं, नो णं संघट्टए मुणी

If his own body is wet (with sentient water), the monk shall neither wipe it nor rub it. Seeing it to be in that state, he shall not even touch it.

A monk on alms-rounds may be caught in unexpected rain or may have to ford a stream; his body becomes wet with sentient water. To dry the body — by wiping with a cloth or rubbing with the hand — would crush the still-living water-bodies clinging to the skin. The instruction is therefore counterintuitive: do not dry yourself. Sit, observe (samuppeha = "having looked carefully"), wait until the water has dried by itself or evaporated to harmlessness, and only then move. The discipline of compassion runs deeper than personal comfort.

Simply Put: If your body is wet with living water, don't wipe or rub it. Look carefully — wait — don't even touch it (until the water-beings are gone).

8.8

Care of Fire-Bodied Life

इंगालं अगणि अच्चिं, अलायं वा सजोइयं । न उंजेज्जा न घट्टेज्जा, णो णं णिव्वावए मुणी

The monk shall not kindle, shall not touch, and shall not extinguish — coals, fire (within iron), flame, a torch with broken flame, or any fire that bears light.

The fire-restriction is uniquely strict in Jain monasticism. Fire-beings are tejaskaya — living beings whose body is the flame itself. To kindle a fire is to bring such beings into existence in conditions that will quickly kill them; to feed a fire is to perpetuate that violence; to extinguish a fire is to crush the fire-beings outright. Even merely touching a flame is forbidden, because the touch agitates and kills them. This is the reason a Jain monk does not cook, does not warm himself by a fire on a cold night, does not blow out a lamp. The threefold negation — "do not kindle, do not touch, do not extinguish" — covers the full life-cycle of the fire-being.

Simply Put: The monk doesn't light a fire, doesn't touch one, and doesn't put one out — fire is a body of life.

8.9

Care of Air-Bodied Life

तालियंटेण पत्तेण, साहाविहुयणेण वा । न वीएज्ज अप्पणो कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं

The wise monk shall not fan — by a palmyra fan, by a leaf, by a tree-branch, or by a peacock-feather — neither his own body nor any external substance (such as hot food).

Air, like water and fire, is a body of life — vayu-kaya. To stir the air deliberately is to throw vayu-beings into violent motion and kill them. The monk therefore does not fan himself in heat, does not blow on hot food to cool it, and does not wave a feather over an offering. The list of fanning instruments is exhaustive precisely so the rule cannot be evaded by a clever substitution: not a palmyra fan, not a leaf, not a branch, not even the soft peacock-feather morchhal will do. The discipline is total.

Simply Put: The monk doesn't fan himself or his food — by leaf, branch, fan, or feather. Even moving air kills.

8.10

Care of Vegetation

तणरुक्खं न छिंदेज्जा, फलं मूलं च कस्सइ । आमगं विविहं बीयं, मणसा वि न पत्थए

The monk shall not cut grass or trees, nor anyone's fruit or root, and shall not — even by mind — desire any kind of unprocessed (still sentient) seed.

The discipline now reaches the fifth class — vanaspati-kaya, plant life. The monk does not pluck, does not cut, does not break a stem; he does not consume any unripe seed (which is still sentient and would germinate); and crucially, he does not even want to. The phrase manasa vi na patthae — "let him not even desire by mind" — is the moral hinge: the monk's restraint is not merely behavioural but goes all the way down into the level of intention. To want something is already to have begun the karma of taking it.

Simply Put: Don't cut grass, don't break a fruit or root, don't even mentally crave a sentient seed.

8.11

Where Not to Stand

गहणेसु ण चिट्ठिज्जा, बीएसु हरिएसु वा । उदगम्मि तहा निच्चं, उत्तिंगपणगेसु वा

He shall never stand within thickets of trees, nor upon seeds, nor upon green vegetation, nor upon the udaka plant (a water-borne vanaspati), nor upon ant-holes or moss-covered ground.

The monk's choice of where to stand is itself a discipline of compassion. Standing in a tree-thicket would shake the branches and disturb plant-beings; standing on seeds, green grass, or the water-plant udaka would crush them; standing on an ant-hill or moss would destroy the colonies of subtle beings beneath. The five forbidden zones cover every class of plant and every nest of mobile life. The principle is consistent: the body's mere weight is itself a potential weapon.

Simply Put: Don't stand in dense plant-clumps, on seeds, on grass, on water-plants, on ant-hills, or on moss.

8.12

The Whole Conduct of Non-Violence

तसे पाणे न हिंसेज्जा, वाया अदुव कम्मुणा । उवरओ सव्वभूएसु, पासेज्ज विविहं जगं

He shall not injure mobile (trasa) beings — neither by speech nor by deed (nor by mind). Withdrawn from harming all beings, he shall see the world (with all its variety) (with the eye of equanimity).

The sixth class — trasa beings — completes the circle. After the five immobile classes, the monk now extends his non-violence to all moving beings. The triad mind-speech-body is restated, and the verse closes with a quietly profound instruction: pasejja vivihaṃ jagaṃ — "see the variegated world." Once the monk has truly stopped harming, his vision changes: the world that was once a field of objects to be used becomes a community of beings to be witnessed. Non-violence and right perception ripen together.

Simply Put: Do not harm any moving creature — by word, deed, or mind. Stand back from all violence, and look at the world's variety with the eye of compassion.

8.13

The Eight Subtle Beings (1): The Resolve

अट्ट सुहुमाइं पेहाए, जाइं जाणित्तु संजए । दयाहिगारी भूएसु, आस चिट्ठ सएहि वा

(There are) eight (classes of) subtle beings; observing them carefully and knowing them, the monk becomes fully entitled to compassion toward all beings — and only then does he sit, stand, or sleep.

The text now opens a unique discussion: beyond the six general classes of life there are eight categories of subtle beings (sukshma) so minute that ordinary observation will not detect them. The monk's compassion is incomplete until he has learned to see these beings — until he has internalised what they are, where they live, when they appear. Only after that knowledge is his every posture (sitting, standing, lying down) a true act of dayā. The sutra-author uses the precise term dayadhikari — "one entitled to compassion" — to make the point that compassion toward what one cannot see is not a sentiment, it is a discipline trained by knowledge.

Simply Put: There are eight kinds of subtle beings. Until the monk learns to recognise them, his compassion is incomplete; only after seeing them is he fit to sit, stand, or sleep.

8.14

The Eight Subtle Beings (2): The Pupil's Question

कयराइं अट्ट सुहुमाइं, जाइं पुच्छिज्ज संजए । इमाइं ताइं मेहावी, आइक्खिज्ज वियक्खणो

"Which are those eight subtle beings?" — when the monk-pupil so asks, the wise and intelligent (teacher) shall answer: "These are they."

This sutra is structurally a bridge — it sets up the Q&A device by which the next sutra will list the eight. The dialogical form mirrors the actual life of the gurukula: the pupil asks, the teacher answers. The vocabulary is significant: the pupil is just samyata (restrained), but the teacher is medhavi (intelligent) and vichakshana (discerning) — twin qualities required to articulate what is by its very nature subtle.

Simply Put: When the pupil asks, "Which are these eight subtle beings?" — the wise teacher answers: "Here they are."

8.15

The Eight Subtle Beings (3): The List

सिणेहं पुप्फसुहुमं च, पाणुत्तिंगं तहेव य । पणगं बीयहरियं च, अंडसुहुमं च अट्ठमं

(1) Sneha-sukshma (subtle moisture), (2) pushpa-sukshma (subtle flower), (3) prani-sukshma (subtle creature), (4) uttinga-sukshma (subtle insect-burrow), (5) panaka-sukshma (subtle moss/mould), (6) bīja-sukshma (subtle seed), (7) hari-sukshma (subtle sprout / vegetation), (8) and the eighth, anda-sukshma (subtle egg).

This sutra gives the canonical list of eight sukshma jiva categories that every monk must learn to recognise. The Gujarati commentary expands each in detail: sneha covers all five forms of subtle water (dew, frost, fog, fine rain, exuded plant moisture); pushpa covers the tiny flowers of trees like banyan that scatter unnoticed across the path; prani covers the smallest mobile beings (kunthus) which are camouflaged to the colour of their surroundings and visible only when they move; uttinga covers various burrow-makers and small mushrooms; panaka is the five-coloured mould or moss that develops on damp surfaces and ripening fruits; bīja covers the tiniest of seeds (poppy, mustard); harita covers the just-emerging sprouts of the rainy season; and anda covers the eggs of bees, spiders, ants, lizards, and geckos. The classification is precise because the monk must conduct every movement — sitting, walking, sleeping, brushing, sweeping — in awareness of these. It is a knowledge-test of compassion: dayā that is informed by exact knowledge is no longer sentiment, it is yoga.

Simply Put: The eight subtle beings are: tiny moisture, tiny flowers, tiny creatures, tiny burrow-dwellers, tiny moulds, tiny seeds, tiny sprouts, and tiny eggs.

8.16

The Vigilant Monk

एवमेयाणि जाणित्ता, सव्वभावेण संजए । अप्पमत्तो जए निच्चं, सव्विंदियसमाहिए

Knowing these (eight subtle beings) thus, with all his being, the monk — vigilant, with all his senses well-composed — shall always strive (to protect them).

The sutra closes the eight-subtle-beings discussion with the disciplinary instruction: appamatto jae niccam — "unfailingly vigilant, always striving." The Gujarati commentary explains savvabhavena in three ways: (1) knowing the beings in all their forms, (2) striving with one's whole capacity, (3) understanding what one knows in fullness. The phrase savvindiya-samahie — "all senses gathered into composure" — names the inner condition without which true vigilance is impossible. The teaching is that knowledge of the eight is useless without the steady inward composure that lets that knowledge govern action moment by moment.

Simply Put: Once you know the eight subtle beings, hold all your senses in composure and stay vigilant — always — to protect them.

8.17

Pratilekhana of Equipment

धुवं च पडिलेहिज्जा, जोगसा पायकंबलं । सेज्जमुच्चारभूमिं च, संथारं अदुवासणं

Always, with concentrated attention (yogic care), the monk shall inspect (do pratilekhana of) — bowls and woollen blanket, the place of sleeping, the place of toilet, the bedding-mat, and the seat.

Pratilekhana — "looking-over" — is the ritualised inspection of every item of monastic equipment to ensure no living being has settled on or in it. The sutra mandates that this inspection be done dhuvaṃ (always, at the prescribed times — usually morning and evening) and jogasā (with focused attention, not as mechanical ritual). The list is exhaustive: the alms-bowl, the wool blanket, the place where one sleeps, the place where one relieves oneself, the mat one sits on, the seat one occupies. Every surface that the monk's body will contact is checked, because each contact is a potential act of violence.

Simply Put: Always inspect — with focused attention — your bowl, blanket, sleeping spot, toilet area, mat, and seat (before using them).

8.18

Disposal of Bodily Wastes

उच्चारं पासवणं, खेलं सिंघाण जल्लियं । फासुयं पडिलेहित्ता, परिट्ठावेज्ज संजए

The restrained monk, having inspected a prasuk (lifeless) ground, shall there dispose of — faeces, urine, phlegm, nasal mucus, and bodily perspiration / dirt.

The verse codifies the fifth samiti — paristhapanika samiti, the discipline of correct disposal. Even what is to be cast away from the body must be cast onto ground that has been inspected and found prasuk (lifeless), so that no being is harmed by the discharge. The five items listed cover everything that leaves the body. The Gujarati commentary notes that the Uttaradhyayan Sutra gives ten conditions for the qualification of the disposal-ground; the monk must select a place that fulfils them. Even the body's most ordinary functions are, here, an occasion for compassion.

Simply Put: The monk inspects an absolutely lifeless area and only there discharges body-wastes — faeces, urine, phlegm, mucus, sweat.

8.19

Conduct in a Layperson's House

पविसित्तु परागारं, पाणट्ठा भोयणस्स वा । जयं चिट्ठे मियं भासे, न य रूवेसु मणं करे

Having entered a layperson's house for water or food, the monk shall stand carefully (yatana-purvak), shall speak measuredly, and shall not turn his mind toward (any) form (i.e. shall not be attracted by the women etc. inside).

The monk on alms-rounds enters spaces he does not own. The sutra triangulates his discipline: be careful in posture, restrained in speech, and detached in perception. The third instruction — na ya rūvesu maṇaṃ kare — "do not turn the mind to forms" — names the most subtle danger: the eye sees, the mind reaches, attachment is born, and the monastic vow is internally injured even if no external misconduct has occurred. The discipline is to enter, receive, and leave without the mind ever having anchored anywhere.

Simply Put: Enter the layperson's house carefully for alms; stand alert, speak little, and don't let your mind get caught on any beautiful form.

8.20

Restraint in Reporting What One Sees and Hears

बहुं सुणेहि कण्णेहिं, बहुं अच्छीहिं पिच्छइ । न य दिट्ठं सुयं सव्वं, भिक्खू अक्खाउमरिहइ

The monk hears much with his ears, sees much with his eyes — but he is not entitled to repeat (to others) all that he has seen and heard.

A monk on alms-rounds inevitably overhears family quarrels, sees household intimacies, witnesses transactions and emotions that do not belong to him. The sutra establishes that reception is not licence to broadcast. To pass on what one has overheard would be to harm the householder, would foment quarrels, would damage the monastic order's reputation, and would chain the monk himself in pointless gossip-karma. The monk must be a sieve through which information enters but does not exit unfiltered.

Simply Put: A monk hears and sees a lot inside other people's homes — but he isn't allowed to repeat all of it.

8.21

Total Withdrawal from Householder Affairs

सुयं वा जइ वा दिट्ठं, न लवेज्जोवघाइयं । न य केणइ उवाएण, गिहिजोगं समायरे

Whatever the monk has heard or seen, if it would harm another, he shall not speak of it. And by no means whatsoever shall he engage in any householder-activity.

The sutra turns the previous principle into an absolute. Speech that harms a layperson is forbidden — even when factually true. And the monk must never, by any pretext, become useful to the householder by carrying out errands or relaying messages or running household business. This is a deep protection: once the monk becomes practically useful to lay society, his independence dissolves and his discipline begins to bend around the lay agenda. Total withdrawal from gihi-yoga (householder-engagement) is the only safeguard.

Simply Put: If something you saw or heard would harm someone, don't say it. And never, by any trick, get drawn into running householder affairs.

8.22

Restraint of the Tongue: Praise of Food

णिट्ठाणं रसणिज्जूढं, भद्दगं पावगं ति वा । पुड्ढो वावि अपुड्ढो वा, लाभालाभं न णिद्दिसे

Whether asked or unasked (by a layperson), the monk shall not declare regarding food — "this is well-spiced and savoury," "this is bland and tasteless," "this is good," "this is bad," "today I received good alms," or "today I received nothing."

The tongue's discipline is twofold — restraint of food and restraint of speech about food. Even when a householder asks the monk how the alms-round went, the monk does not answer with evaluations of taste or with reports of plenty or scarcity. To do so would betray inner attachment to food (raga and dvesha around taste), would induce the next householder to over-prepare or feel inadequate, and would tie the monk's spiritual life to the day's culinary lottery. The discipline is to receive what comes, in silence about its quality.

Simply Put: Asked or unasked, the monk does not say to a householder: "the food was great," "it was bland," "today's alms were rich," or "today I got nothing."

8.23

Begging Without Greed

न य भोयणम्मि गिद्धो, चरे उंछं अयंपिरो । अफासुयं न भुंजेज्जा, कीयमुद्देसियाहडं

Without being attached to delicious food, the monk shall (silently, without idle talk) gather alms (a little from each of many houses). He shall not eat what is aprasuk (still life-bearing), nor what was bought (with money), nor what was prepared specifically for him, nor what was brought to him (from elsewhere).

Three classical doshas (faults) of food-acceptance are formalised here: krīta (purchased for the monk), audeshika (prepared specifically for the monk), and abhyahrita (brought to the monk from elsewhere). Each of these makes the monk's food a cause of household activity — purchase, cooking, transport — and so makes the monk indirectly responsible for the himsa of those activities. The monk must instead practise uncha-vritti (gleaning a little from many houses) so that no single household is burdened. And he must do so silently, without flattery or chatter that would extract more.

Simply Put: The monk doesn't crave tasty food. He silently collects a little from many houses. He doesn't eat what's still alive, what was bought for him, what was cooked for him, or what was brought to him.

8.24

No Hoarding, No Patron-Bondage

सण्णिहिं च न कुव्वेज्जा, अणुमायं पि संजए । मुहाजीवी असंबद्धे, हवेज्ज जगणिस्सिए

The restrained monk shall not hoard / store (food) — not even an atom's worth. Free-living (without remuneration), unattached (to any patron-household), he shall live as one supported by the wide world.

Three independence-principles are stated together. Asannidhi — no hoarding, even of a grain, even overnight — keeps the monk dependent on each new day's mercy of alms and prevents the spiritual atrophy that comes with food-security. Muhajivi — taking nothing for spiritual service — protects the monk from becoming a paid functionary. Asambaddha — unbound to any one house or village — keeps the monk wandering across the wider world, "jaga-nissie," supported by no one in particular and so by everyone in general. Together they preserve the monk's spiritual mobility.

Simply Put: The monk doesn't hoard even an atom of food, doesn't take payment for spiritual help, and isn't tied to any one household — he wanders the wide world as his support.

8.25

Contentment with the Dry and the Plain

लूहवित्ती सुसंतुट्ठे, अप्पिच्छे सुहरे सिया । आसुरत्तं न गच्छेज्जा, सोच्चा णं जिणसासणं

Living on dry and tasteless food, well-content, of few wants, easily filled — having heard the Jina-teaching, he shall not yield to anger.

Four chained virtues complete the food-discipline arc: lūha-vritti (living on dry food) → santosha (contentment) → appichha (few wants) → subharata (easy satisfaction). Each ripens into the next. The verse closes with the warning against asura-tva — the disposition of anger — using the powerful image that an angry monk is no longer a saint but an asura. The remedy is not effort but remembrance: hearing the Jina's teaching and keeping it in mind. Anger dissolves not by suppression but by recall.

Simply Put: The monk lives on plain dry food, content, wanting little, easily filled. Hearing the Jina's word, he refuses to slip into anger.

8.26

Restraint of All Five Senses

कण्णसोक्खेहिं सद्देहिं, पेमं णाभिनिवेसए । दारुणं कक्कसं फासं, कायेण अहियासए

Toward sounds that are pleasing to the ear, the monk shall not let attachment establish itself. Toward dreadful and harsh touches, he shall — through the body — endure them with equanimity.

The sutra is a compressed instruction in two-sided sense-restraint. With the first sense mentioned (hearing) and the last (touch), the verse encloses all five and demands the same discipline of each: do not attach to the favourable, do not recoil from the unfavourable. The Gujarati commentary opens this out into a full doctrine — the monk must hold equal-mindedness toward the anukula (favourable) objects of all five senses (which would tempt to raga) and the pratikula (unfavourable) objects (which would tempt to dvesha), and through this equanimity he abandons both attachment and aversion. Dāruṇa and kakkasa are technical terms — the first naming what causes harm (cold, heat, sharp objects), the second naming what is rough (the wear of long walking, hard ground).

Simply Put: When sweet sounds reach you, don't let attachment take hold; when harsh painful touches come, bear them through the body with equanimity.

8.27

Endurance as the Great Fruit

खुहं पिवासं दुस्सेज्जं, सीउण्हं अरइं भयं । अहियासे अव्वहिओ, देहदुक्खं महाफलं

Hunger, thirst, an uncomfortable bed, cold, heat, mental restlessness, fear — let the unagitated monk bear all these. The pain of the body (so endured) is of great fruit.

This is one of the chapter's most quoted verses — deha-dukkhaṃ mahāphalaṃ: "the body's suffering is of great fruit." It states the foundational logic of monastic austerity. The seven parishaha (afflictions) listed cover the body's most basic distresses; instead of fleeing them, the monk bears them with an unagitated mind. The Gujarati commentary deepens this with two readings: (1) the natural reading — body-pain endured equanimously is the great fruit of liberation; (2) the deeper reading — as long as you have a body, suffering is in the body, not in you; remembering the body's transience and frangibility (kshana-bhangurata), you bear the pain by realising it does not touch the soul. Either way, the equanimous endurance of physical pain is the engine of mokshic transformation.

Simply Put: Hunger, thirst, hard beds, cold, heat, restlessness, fear — bear them all without flinching. Body-pain endured this way bears the great fruit of liberation.

8.28

Renunciation of Night-Eating

अत्थंगयम्मि आइच्चे, पुरत्था य अणुग्गए । आहारमाइयं सव्वं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए

After the sun has set, and before it has risen in the east — the monk shall not even mentally desire any kind of food, drink, or any other ingestible.

The vow of ratri-bhojan-tyaga (renunciation of night-eating) is a sixth fundamental vow for Jain monks (in addition to the five great vows) and is a core lay vow as well. The biological reason — that countless small life-forms move into food in the dark — supports the spiritual reason: that night-eating tends toward indulgence and dulls the discipline of the dawn. The remarkable feature of this sutra is the depth of its prohibition: manasa vi na patthae — "not even by mind." Where the lay vow merely forbids the act of eating, the monk's vow forbids even the desire for it. To fantasise about food at night is itself a violation of the vow. The Gujarati commentary explains: "even the wish (sankalpa, abhilasha) for night food makes the vow defective."

Simply Put: From sundown to sunrise, the monk doesn't eat — and doesn't even let his mind want to eat.

8.29

Six Qualities for Inner Peace

अतितिणे अचवले, अप्पभासी मियासणे । हवेज्ज उयरे दंते, थोवं लद्धुं न खिंसए

(The monk shall be) free of restless complaining, free of fickleness, of measured speech, of moderate eating — a tamer of the stomach; receiving little (in alms), he shall not blame (the giver).

Six chained qualities for the monk's inner peace: (1) no restless complaining (atitine), (2) no behavioural fickleness (acavale), (3) measured speech (appabhasi), (4) measured eating (miyasane), (5) tamed belly (uyare dante), (6) no blame for small alms (thovam laddhum na khimsae). Each one disciplines a specific channel of disturbance. The fifth and sixth, udar-danta and na khimsae, are particularly important: to "tame the belly" is to refuse to be enslaved by hunger, and to "not censure" the giver is to refuse to project one's own deprivation outward as blame. Together they produce an inwardly serene monk who walks lightly through the world.

Simply Put: Don't fret, don't fidget, speak little, eat little, master your stomach, and if you get only a little alms — don't blame the giver.

8.30

No Self-Praise, No Disparagement of Others

ण बाहिरं परिभवे, अत्ताणं ण समुक्कसे । सुय लाभे ण मज्जेज्जा, जच्चा तवस्सि बुद्धिए

Let him not disparage another, nor exalt himself. Having gained scriptural knowledge, let him not become intoxicated by it; nor by his birth, nor by his austerities, nor by his intellect.

The sutra targets māna-kashāya (the passion of pride) on the two faces by which it appears: ātma-praśaṃsā (self-praise) and para-paribhava (disparagement of others). Self-praise inflates the ego upward; disparagement crushes others downward. Both arise from the same root and both block spiritual progress. The text names four classical grounds on which a monk might be tempted to pride — birth (jati), austerities (tapas), intellect (buddhi), and scriptural attainment (shruta-labha) — and forbids intoxication on any of them. The Gujarati commentary cites two tight scriptural lines: je paribhavayi paraṃ jaṇaṃ saṃsāre parivattati mahaṃ — "he who disparages others wanders long in samsara" — and na paraṃ vajjijasi ayaṃ kusīle — "do not call another low or wicked." A monk who has gained much may, through self-importance, lose all.

Simply Put: Don't put others down; don't pump yourself up. Don't be drunk on your scripture, your birth, your austerity, or your intellect.

8.31

Quick Withdrawal from Wrong Conduct

से जाणमजाणं वा, कट्टु आहम्मियं पयं । संवरे खिप्पमप्पाणं, बीयं तं ण समायरे

If the monk — knowingly or unknowingly — has performed an unrighteous act, let him quickly draw himself back (in samvara), and let him never repeat that act a second time.

The verse acknowledges what so many disciplinary texts will not: the monk will err — sometimes through ignorance of the rule, sometimes through momentary capture by passion. The sutra does not despair of this; it gives a precise twofold remedy. First, immediate samvara — quick interior withdrawal from the wrong, instant alochana before the Guru, and prayashchitta. Second, total resolve never to repeat: because each repetition deepens the samskara (habit-impression) and a deepened samskara becomes an addiction. The text's deep insight is that the recovery is not in the regret but in the non-repetition.

Simply Put: If a monk does something wrong — knowing or not — he stops himself fast and never does it again.

8.32

Confession Without Concealment

अणायारं परक्कम्म, णेव गूहे ण णिण्हवे । सुई सया वियडभावे, असंसत्ते जिइंदिए

Having performed an unmonastic act, the monk shall neither hide it (slightly) nor deny it (entirely). Let him be ever pure-hearted, manifest in disposition, unattached, and master of his senses.

This is the second half of the failure-recovery teaching. Sutra 31 said: do not repeat. Sutra 32 says: do not hide. Two technical verbs are used — gūhe (to hide partially, to soften the truth) and ṇiṇhave (to deny entirely, to claim it never happened). The monk is forbidden both. The Gujarati commentary explains why: confession (alochana) before the Guru is itself a kind of penance, and accepting one's fault as a fault is the very first step that restores purity. A monk who hides is one who stays bound; a monk who confesses transparently and accepts whatever prayashchitta the Guru imposes becomes — over time — asamsakta (unattached) and jitendriya (sense-conqueror). The path to mastery passes through honesty, not concealment.

Simply Put: If the monk has done something wrong, he doesn't hide a part of it or deny all of it. He stays pure, transparent, unattached, and self-controlled.

8.33

The Discipline of the Pupil

अमोहं वयणं कुज्जा, आयरियस्स महप्पणो । तं परिगिज्झ वायाए, कम्मुणा उववायए

The (pupil-monk) shall make fruitful — never let go to waste — the words of the great-souled Acharya. Having accepted them with speech (e.g. "tahatti"), he shall fulfil them by deed.

The sutra defines the pupil's discipline as a three-stage cycle: hear, accept, fulfil. The pupil hears the Acharya's instruction, accepts it verbally with the formula tahatti ("yes, so it is"), and then enacts it with the body. Only when all three stages are complete is the Acharya's word amogha — "not in vain," fruitful. Verbal-only acceptance is mere lip-service; physical-only obedience without inner consent is also incomplete. The full cycle of mind-speech-body is required for true discipleship. This is the foundational logic of Jain vinaya (humility-discipline) — the very lifeblood of monastic learning.

Simply Put: The pupil makes the Acharya's word fruitful — accepts it aloud (with "yes, so be it") and then carries it out in deed.

8.34

The Brevity of Life

अधुवं जीवियं णच्चा, सिद्धिमग्गं वियाणिया । विणियट्टिज्ज भोगेसु, आउं परिमियमप्पणो

Knowing life to be impermanent, knowing the path to liberation, knowing one's own life-span to be brief and bounded — let him withdraw from sensual indulgences.

The teaching pivot for the whole next stretch of the chapter. The argument has three premises and one conclusion. Premise 1: life is impermanent. Premise 2: the path of liberation is now known to you. Premise 3: your own life is short. Conclusion: therefore turn away from sensual indulgence. The logic is unsparing. The human birth in the karmabhumi with access to the Jina-teaching is statistically rare; squandering it on bhoga is to throw away the rarest opportunity of countless births.

Simply Put: Life is short and uncertain, the way out is known, your time is limited — so step back from sensory pleasures.

8.35

Practise While Capable

बलं थामं च पेहाए, सद्धामारुग्गमप्पणो । खेत्तं कालं च विण्णाय, तहप्पाणं णिजुंजए

Examining one's own physical strength, mental firmness, faith, and health — and discerning the right place and the right time — let one engage oneself in spiritual practice accordingly.

A monk's practice cannot be a one-size-fits-all imposition; it must be calibrated. The sutra names six factors for self-assessment — body-strength, mental firmness, faith, health, the right place, the right time. The monk must not undertake more than capacity allows (because broken austerities damage faith and reputation), nor less than capacity permits (because wasting capacity is its own sin). The Gujarati commentary stresses that what kshetra and kala (place and time) demand differs from region to region and age to age; the wise monk reads both internal and external conditions before committing to a tap or vow.

Simply Put: Look at your strength, your firmness, your faith, your health — read the place and the time — then take up the practice that fits.

8.36

Practise Before Decline

जरा जाव ण पीडेइ, वाही जाव ण वड्ढइ । जाविंदिया ण हायंति, ताव धम्मं समायरे

So long as old age does not yet press, so long as disease does not yet grow, so long as the senses do not yet decline — practise the dharma now.

A famous urgency-verse, paired with 8.35. Practice has prerequisites — a body still functional, senses still responsive, mind still firm. All three decay with time. The sutra commands the practitioner to act before decline arrives, not after. The Gujarati commentary observes that postponement is itself a subtle form of pramada (negligence); when the body fails, even the basic mahavratas become hard to keep, let alone the higher tap. Therefore: while you can, you must.

Simply Put: Before old age pains you, before disease grows in you, before your senses fail — practise the dharma now.

8.37

Renounce the Four Kashayas

कोहं माणं च मायं च, लोभं च पाववड्ढणं । वमे चत्तारि दोसे उ, इच्छंतो हियमप्पणो

Anger, pride, deceit, and greed — the four (kashayas) which augment sin — let the one who desires his own good resolutely vomit out (renounce).

The chapter now opens its great kashaya-sequence (sutras 37-40). The four poisons are introduced together as pap-vardhana — "increasers of sin" — because every transgression in Jain karmic theory is rooted in one or more of them. The verb vame — "let him vomit out" — is unusually strong; the kashayas must not be controlled, not merely managed, but expelled from the system as the body expels what is toxic. The conditional icchanto hiyam appano — "desiring one's own good" — establishes the spiritual stake: only the person who actually wants his own welfare will undertake this difficult ejection.

Simply Put: Anger, pride, deceit, greed — these four grow sin. Whoever truly wants his own good must vomit them out.

8.38

What Each Kashaya Destroys

कोहो पीइं पणासेइ, माणो विणय णासणो । माया मित्ताणि णासेइ, लोहो सव्व विणासणो

Anger destroys love. Pride destroys humility. Deceit destroys friendships. Greed destroys all virtues entire.

The sutra is a perfect compact analysis. Each kashaya has a specific casualty: anger kills affection (the warmth between beings), pride kills humility (the basis of all learning), deceit kills friendship (the trust between souls), and greed — most damning — kills everything: integrity, dharma, peace, even the gains greed itself was after. The Gujarati commentary illustrates with the well-known parable of the son who quarrelled with his father over money (lobha), broke the relationship (priti destroyed by anger), proudly claimed his share (vinaya destroyed by mana), gained the wealth deceitfully and lied when asked (mitrata destroyed by maya), and so destroyed all virtues at once (sarva-guna destroyed by lobha). One greed cascades into all four.

Simply Put: Anger kills love. Pride kills humility. Deceit kills friendship. Greed destroys everything.

8.39

Antidote to Each Kashaya

उवसमेण हणे कोहं, माणं मद्दवया जिणे । मायं च अज्जवभावेण, लोभं संतोसओ जिणे

By forbearance (upashama), let him slay anger; by humility (mardava), let him conquer pride; by straightforwardness (arjava), (let him conquer) deceit; by contentment (santosha), let him conquer greed.

For each poison, an antidote. Krodha (anger) is dissolved by upashama — the inner pacification that does not match heat with heat. Mana (pride) is dissolved by mardava — softness, the willingness to be small. Maya (deceit) is dissolved by arjava — straightforwardness, where what is in the heart is also in the mouth. Lobha (greed) is dissolved by santosha — contentment, the active sufficiency that needs no more. The Gujarati commentary makes a key point: these four antidotes are themselves "weapons" but they are weapons one must learn to wield. They are taught in the agama and through guru-instruction, but they become effective only through repeated practice (abhyasa) and inward conviction (vairagya-buddhi). Four diseases, four medicines — but the patient must take them.

Simply Put: Slay anger with forbearance, pride with humility, deceit with straightforwardness, greed with contentment.

8.40

Unrestrained Kashayas Re-Water Samsara

कोहो य माणो य अणिग्गहीया, माया य लोभो य पवड्ढमाणा । चत्तारि एए कसिणा कसाया, सिंचंति मूलाइं पुणब्भवस्स

Anger and pride unrestrained, deceit and greed ever-growing — these four full-bodied kashayas water the very roots of the tree of rebirth.

A grand image: samsara is a tree, and its roots are watered by the four kashayas. So long as the kashayas flow unchecked, the tree of rebirth flourishes. The word kasiṇa — "full, complete, dark" (Sanskrit krtsna / krishna) — names the kashayas in their most virulent form, when they have been fed and have grown into dominance. The Gujarati commentary notes that kashaya is itself etymologised from kash (samsara) + aya (gain): "that which produces the gains of samsara." Hence to renounce them is literally to cut off samsara at its irrigation. This sutra closes the kashaya-tetrad with a vivid warning: every passion not killed becomes a root that produces another life.

Simply Put: Ungoverned anger and pride, ever-growing deceit and greed — these four dark passions water the roots of the rebirth-tree.

8.41

Vinaya, Steadfastness, and Restraint of Body

रायणिएसु विणयं पउंजे, धुवसीलयं सययं ण हावइज्जा । कुम्मुव्व अल्लीणपलीणगुत्तो, परक्कमेज्जा तवसंजमम्मि

Toward the senior monks (the ratnadhika), let him exercise humility-discipline. Let him never let his steady character (the eighteen thousand silas) decline. Like a tortoise, with all limbs well-withdrawn and well-protected, let him exert himself in austerity and restraint.

The verse braids three duties. First, to the seniors of the order (the ratnadhika — those richer in the jewels of knowledge, perception, conduct), the monk practises vinaya — humble service, deference, learning. Second, the eighteen thousand silas — the canonical enumeration of monastic conduct (10 dharma × 10 categories of beings × 5 senses × 4 sanjnas × 3 yogas × 3 karanas) — must be held without any slackening. Third, like a tortoise that pulls all limbs into its shell at the first sign of danger, the monk withdraws his sense-organs and limbs from outward engagement and places his energy into tap and samyam. The tortoise image is one of the most enduring in Indian spiritual literature; here it specifically images gupti — guarding-in.

Simply Put: Be humble before the seniors. Never let your steady character slip. Like a tortoise pulling in all its limbs, withdraw your senses and pour all your energy into austerity and restraint.

8.42

Renunciation of Pramada (Negligence)

णिद्दं च ण बहु मण्णिज्जा, सप्पहासं विवज्जए । मिहो कहाहिं ण रमे, सज्झायम्मि रओ सया

Let him not value sleep highly. Let him abandon loud laughter. Let him not delight in idle gossip with others. Let him be ever absorbed in self-study (svadhyaya).

The sutra targets the four most common forms of pramada (spiritual negligence) for a monk: excessive sleep, loud laughter, idle group-talk (vikatha), and the absence of svadhyaya. The Gujarati commentary classifies vikatha into four canonical types — stri-katha (talk about women), bhakta-katha (talk about food), raja-katha (talk about politics/kings), and desha-katha (talk about countries) — and a fifth category covering secret talk, gender-related talk, and so on. The monk's antidote is svadhyaya: absorption in scriptural reading, questioning, repetition, contemplation, and dharma-katha. The discipline is to fill the hours that pramada would empty.

Simply Put: Don't sleep too much. Don't burst into loud laughter. Don't get caught up in idle gossip. Stay always absorbed in self-study.

8.43

Energetic Engagement in Shramana-Dharma

जोगं च समणधम्मम्मि, जुंजे अणलसो धुवं । जुत्तो य समणधम्मम्मि, अट्ठं लहइ अणुत्तरं

Steadily, free of laziness, let him engage all his powers (yogas) in the shramana-dharma. The monk thus engaged in the shramana-dharma attains the supreme goal (anuttara artha — liberation).

The sutra closes the pramada-section. Where the previous verse said "do not waste yourself in sleep, laughter, gossip," this one says positively: "engage all yogas in the shramana-dharma, and the supreme goal is yours." The shramana-dharma in the standard Jain enumeration has ten components — kshama (forbearance), mardava (humility), arjava (straightforwardness), nirlobha (non-greed), tap (austerity), samyama (restraint), satya (truth), saucha (purity), akinchanata (non-possession), brahmacharya (celibacy). Engaged in these with full mind-speech-body, free of laziness and steady in continuance, the monk reaches anuttara artha — the goal beyond which there is no further goal.

Simply Put: Without laziness, steadily, engage all your faculties in the monastic dharma. Doing so, you attain the highest goal.

8.44

Service of the Learned

इह लोगपारत्तहियं, जेणं गच्छइ सोग्गइं । बहुस्सुयं पज्जुवासेज्जा, पुच्छिज्जत्थविणिच्छयं

By which one attains good in this world and the next, and reaches a good destination — let the monk serve a bahushruta (one of vast scriptural learning), and (from him) ask for the determination of meanings.

The verse establishes the monk's duty of bahushruta-paryupasana — the active service of those rich in scriptural knowledge. The verb paryupasana means more than visiting; it means dwelling in the proximity of, attending upon, learning by living-near. From such a teacher the monk does not merely receive information but obtains artha-vinishchaya — the settled determination of meaning. Doubts (shanka, jignyasa) which would otherwise corrode the monk's faith are resolved face-to-face, in the patient atmosphere of the bahushruta's company. This is the social mechanism by which the lineage of right understanding is preserved.

Simply Put: To gain good in this life and the next, the monk serves a deeply learned teacher and asks him to settle the meanings he is unsure of.

8.45

Bodily Discipline Before the Guru

हत्थं पायं च कायं च, पणिहाय जिइंदिए । अल्लीणगुत्तो णिसीए, सगासे गुरुणो मुणी

Hands, feet, the (whole) body — having well-restrained them, master of his senses, motionless and self-guarded, let the monk sit in the presence of the Guru.

The sutra describes the precise physical posture for sitting before the Guru. Hands, feet, body — every limb is to be drawn in and stilled. The senses are to be governed (jitendriya). The whole bearing should be alina-gupta — held inward and protected. This is not merely etiquette; it is a transmission discipline. The body's restlessness mirrors and produces the mind's restlessness. To sit absolutely still in the Guru's presence is to become a vessel ready to receive what the Guru transmits. Conversely, a monk who fidgets, who sprawls, who lets his eyes wander, has already declared himself spiritually closed.

Simply Put: When sitting before the Guru, the monk holds in his hands, feet, and body, masters his senses, and stays motionless — with full inward respect.

8.46

Where Not to Sit Before the Guru

ण पक्खओ ण पुरओ, णेव किच्चाण पिट्ठओ । ण य ऊरुं समासिज्जा, चिट्ठेज्जा गुरुणंतिए

Neither at the side, nor in front, nor close behind — and not so close that one's thigh touches (the Guru's) — should the monk sit when staying near the Guru.

A precise spatial discipline. To sit beside the Guru implies equality; to sit in front of the Guru implies blocking him; to sit directly behind the Guru disrespects him; to sit touching the Guru collapses the very distance that vinaya requires. The monk must therefore find the right position — close enough to learn, distant enough to honour, attentive but not crowding. The Gujarati commentary emphasises that this etiquette is not mere outward show; it expresses inward humility. The way one positions oneself reveals the disposition one brings.

Simply Put: When sitting near the Guru, don't sit beside him, don't sit in front of him, don't sit right behind him, and don't sit touching him.

8.47

Speech Discipline Before the Guru

अपुच्छिओ ण भासेज्जा, भासमाणस्स अंतरा । पिट्ठिमंसं ण खाएज्जा, मायामोसं विवज्जए

(The pupil) shall not speak unless asked. He shall not interrupt while (the Guru) is speaking. He shall not "eat back-flesh" (i.e. backbite, slander). And he shall completely renounce maya-mrisha (deceitful falsehood).

The verse extends pupil-discipline from the body (sutras 45-46) to the speech. Four prohibitions: do not speak unasked; do not interrupt; do not slander another behind their back ("eating back-flesh" — pishtimansa-bhakshana, the colourful Indian metaphor for backbiting); and do not engage in maya-mrisha (the fusion of deceit and falsehood, which the texts list as the 17th of the eighteen pap-sthanas). The commentary notes that backbiting feeds envy, hostility, and a culture of mutual suspicion that destroys monastic harmony — and that maya-mrisha breeds compounding lies that draw an entire chain of corruptions in their wake.

Simply Put: Don't speak unless asked. Don't interrupt the Guru. Don't backbite. Never speak deceitful lies.

8.48

Refrain from Hurtful Speech

अप्पत्तियं जेण सिया, आसु कुप्पिज्ज वा परो । सव्वसो तं ण भासिज्जा, भासं अहियगामिणिं

That speech by which displeasure (in another) might arise, by which the other might quickly grow angry, or which leads to harm — such speech the monk shall never, in any condition, utter.

A simple, decisive criterion: the monk applies a triple test to every utterance. Will it cause displeasure? Will it provoke anger? Will it produce harm? If any of the three is true, the speech is forbidden — savvaso, in all conditions, with no exception. This is not merely politeness; it is recognition that hurtful speech itself binds karma — both for the speaker (who has injured another) and indirectly for the listener (who is now disturbed). Speech is therefore screened before it leaves the mouth.

Simply Put: If a speech would cause hurt, anger, or harm, the monk simply does not speak it — ever, in any condition.

8.49

The Eight Qualities of Right Speech

दिट्ठं मियं असंदिद्धं, पडिपुण्णं वियं जियं । अयंपिरमणुव्विग्गं, भासं णिसिर अत्तवं

The wise monk shall utter speech that is (1) seen with one's own eyes, (2) measured, (3) free of doubt, (4) complete, (5) clear, (6) familiar (well-understood), (7) free of pretentious flourish, and (8) free of agitation.

This sutra is one of the chapter's most useful — a perfect eightfold checklist for right speech. The monk speaks only what he has directly verified (diṣṭa); only as much as is necessary (mita); only when he is certain (asandigdha); in complete sentences (paripurna); with clear pronunciation (vyakta); on subjects he knows well (jita); without rhetorical flourish or pretension (ayampira); and without agitation or anxiety (anudvigna). Each of the eight is a real-life screen against a common pathology of speech.

Simply Put: The wise monk's words are: based on what he himself has seen, measured, certain, complete, clearly pronounced, familiar, plain, and calm.

8.50

No Mockery of the Knowledgeable

आयार पण्णत्तिधरं, दिट्ठिवायमहिज्जगं । वायाविक्खलियं णच्चा, ण तं उवहसे मुणी

When the holder of (the Anga) Acharanga, of (the Anga) Prajnapti (Bhagavati), of the Drishtivada — should at some moment slip in his speech, the (other) monk shall not laugh at him.

A specific protection of the learned. Even the great scholar — the one who has memorised the Acharanga, Bhagavati, and Drishtivada (representing the beginning, middle, and end of the twelve Angas) — may, at some moment in chhadmastha (non-omniscient) state, mis-speak. The text forbids the other monk from laughing at the slip. The Gujarati commentary lists the harms: laughing at a knowledgeable person creates ashatana (disrespect to the wise) — which is one of the principal causes of jnanavaraniya karma; it diminishes one's own reputation; it generates pride; it breeds disrespect for tradition. The monk who guards his laughter guards, ultimately, his own access to knowledge.

Simply Put: When a great scholar — even one who has memorised the foundational scriptures — slips in his speech, no monk should laugh at him.

8.51

No Astrology, Spells, or Medical Prescriptions

णक्खत्तं सुमिणं जोगं, णिमित्तं मंतभेसजं । गिहिणो तं ण आइक्खे, भूयाहिगरणं पयं

Astrology of stars, dream-interpretation, occult formulae of attraction, omen-reading, mantras, and medicines — the monk shall not declare these to laypeople, for these are the locus of harm to living beings.

The sutra forbids the monk from practising as an astrologer, dream-interpreter, sorcerer, omen-reader, mantra-vendor, or doctor. The reason given is precise: bhūta-adhikarana — these are occasions of injury to living beings. Astrology induces violent decisions (a war timed by stars, a pregnancy aborted by a bad horoscope); occult attraction violates another's free will; medicines are made by killing beings and prescribing them entangles the monk in the patient's diet, lifestyle, and household economy. Even where the immediate intent is benign, the karmic chains are deep. The monk's vocation is moksha-marga; once he becomes practically useful in worldly arts, his independence dissolves.

Simply Put: Astrology, dream-reading, spells, omens, mantras, medicines — the monk doesn't tell laypeople any of these, because they all become occasions for harming beings.

8.52

Choice of Lodging

अण्णट्ठं पगडं लयणं, भएज्ज सयणासणं । उच्चारभूमिसंपण्णं, इत्थीपसुविवज्जियं

The monk shall use a lodging built for some other purpose (not for him), and seats and beds (similarly intended for others) — and only in a place that has access to a clean toilet-ground and is free of women and animals.

The verse opens the brahmacharya (celibacy) discipline that runs through sutras 52-60. Three conditions for the monk's lodging are specified: (1) it must be anyartha-prakata — built originally for someone else's purpose, never specifically for the monk (which would constitute the audeshika fault); (2) it must have access to a clean prasuk toilet-ground for the fifth samiti; (3) it must be free of women and animals (or, for nuns, free of men and male animals), because constant proximity to the opposite sex disturbs brahmacharya-samadhi and inflames the latent veda-mohaniya karma.

Simply Put: The monk uses a place built for others (not for him), with a clean toilet-area nearby, and free of women and animals.

8.53

Quiet Lodging, No Idle Talk With Women, No Householder Familiarity

विवित्ता य भवे सेज्जा, नारीणं न लवे कहं । गिहिसंथवं ण कुज्जा, कुज्जा साहूहिं संथवं

The lodging shall be solitary (free of crowd-flow). The monk shall not engage in stories with women. He shall not become familiar with householders, but he shall keep familiarity (sangati) with monks.

Three further protections of brahmacharya. First, the lodging must be vivikta — solitary, isolated, not on the busy thoroughfare where women and men pass continually. Second, the monk must not engage in conversation with women — even apparently innocent talk about women (stri-katha) excites raga and inflames the veda-mohaniya. Third, household-familiarity must be avoided; the monk does not visit a particular family often, does not develop personal favourites, does not become a "house-friend." The positive corollary is sadhu-santhava — cultivate the company of monks, where speech and behaviour are aligned with the path.

Simply Put: The monk's lodging should be quiet and out of the way. No story-talk with women. No deep familiarity with householders. Cultivate company instead with other monks.

8.54

The Chick and the Cat

जहा कुक्कुडपोयस्स, णिच्चं कुललओ भयं । एवं खु बंभयारिस्स, इत्थीविग्गहओ भयं

Just as for a chick of a hen, there is constant danger from a cat — exactly so, for the celibate (brahmachari) monk, there is danger from the body of a woman.

One of the chapter's most striking similes. The chick is harmless; it has done the cat no wrong; the cat may not even be hungry now. But the biological asymmetry is total — the cat is danger by its very nature for a chick. The sutra applies this to the brahmachari and the female body: it is not a moral judgement against women (and the parallel verse for nuns reverses the genders), it is a clear-eyed acknowledgement that as long as veda-mohaniya karma is in udaya, the very presence of an opposite-sex body in close proximity is, for the celibate, a structural danger that no good intentions on either side can remove. The monk's awareness of this danger is what keeps him safe; pretending the danger does not exist is what destroys.

Simply Put: A chick is in constant danger from a cat. In just the same way, the celibate monk is in constant danger from the body of a woman.

8.55

Eyes Withdrawn from Painted Walls and Adorned Women

चित्तभित्तिं ण णिज्झाए, नारिं वा सुअलंकियं । भक्खरं पिव दठ्ठूणं, दिट्ठिं पडिसमाहरे

The monk shall not gaze upon a wall painted with images (of women) nor upon a well-adorned woman. As one withdraws the eye instantly upon seeing the sun, so let him withdraw his gaze (from such sights).

A simple eye-discipline taught through a vivid analogy. When one accidentally looks at the sun, one does not stop to consider — the eye instantly withdraws. The monk must develop this same instantaneous reflex toward the sight of (a) painted images of women on walls or in art, and (b) actual women adorned with finery. The point is not aversion or contempt; it is the speed of withdrawal. A glance held becomes a gaze; a gaze becomes attachment; attachment becomes the breach of brahmacharya. The simile of the sun is also delicately positioned — looking at the sun damages the eye; looking at attractive forms damages the brahmacharya.

Simply Put: Don't stare at painted images of women or at well-adorned women. Just as you instantly pull your gaze back from the sun, pull it back from these.
