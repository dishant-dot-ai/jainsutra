# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 3
# Kshullakachara Katha (ક્ષુલ્લકાચાર કથા) — The Brief Account of Conduct

**Adhyayan 3 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 35–61 (PDF pages 95–122)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 15*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The third chapter of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is named **Kshullakachara Katha** — "The Brief Account of Conduct." Its subject is the **anachara** — the improper, prohibited acts that a true Jain monastic must never engage in. The sixth chapter of this same scripture, called *Mahachara Katha* ("The Great Account of Conduct"), gives an extensive treatment of monastic conduct; this third chapter, by contrast, is the condensed account — and that is exactly why it is called *kshullaka* ("small" or "brief").

In Jain practice, *achara* (right conduct) is the very heart of the spiritual path. The Tirthankaras, when they begin their teaching career, address conduct first because it is conduct that liberates the soul from bondage. There are five kinds of right conduct: conduct in knowledge, conduct in perception, conduct in character, conduct in austerity, and conduct in spiritual energy. Anything done for liberation that is supported by these five and is in line with what the scriptures permit is called *achara*. Whatever falls outside this — whatever the scriptures forbid — is *anachara*.

Anachara is unrighteousness. It is non-duty. To engage in it is to abandon the path. The chapter, therefore, is a precise inventory of fifty-two specific improper acts that the Jain monastic community has identified through centuries of careful observation. These acts are not arbitrary prohibitions; each one is forbidden because it harms living beings, weakens self-restraint, indulges the body, breaks celibacy, encourages possessiveness, or otherwise violates the great vows.

The chapter opens by establishing the qualifications of the monk who is bound by these rules — one who is firmly settled in self-restraint, free of every internal and external possession, a protector of the six classes of life, and a knot-less wanderer (*nirgrantha*). The body of the chapter then enumerates the fifty-two anacharas in groups (sutras 2–9). The chapter closes (sutras 10–15) with a glorification of the true monk who has renounced all of these — the fearless, mindful, equanimous wanderer who has conquered both the senses and the passions, who endures heat in summer and cold in winter, and who, by enduring this difficult austerity, finally burns away all karma and attains liberation.

The chapter is therefore both a code (what to avoid) and a portrait (who the true monk is). It is meant to be memorised and recited — short, sharp, and unforgettable — so that the new monastic, just stepping onto the path, knows exactly where the boundaries are.

## SUTRA 3.1 — The Qualifications of the One Bound by These Rules

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संजमे सुट्ठिअप्पाणं, विप्पमुक्काण ताइणं ।
तेसिमेयमणाइण्णं, निग्गंथाण महेसिणं ॥३.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.1 ||
saṃjame suṭṭhi-appāṇaṃ, vippamukkāṇa tāiṇaṃ |
tesim-eyam-aṇāiṇṇaṃ, niggaṃthāṇa mahesiṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For those great seers, the knot-less ones — whose own self is firmly settled in self-restraint, who are wholly liberated from every entanglement, and who are protectors of (themselves and all six classes of) living beings — the following acts are improper and not to be practised.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजमे** (saṃjame) = in self-restraint (saṃyama)
- **सुट्ठि-अप्पाणं** (suṭṭhi-appāṇaṃ) = those whose own self (ātman) is well-settled, well-established
- **विप्पमुक्काण** (vippamukkāṇa) = wholly freed — free from all worldly bondage; free, by the three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and three yogas (mind, speech, body), from all external and internal possessiveness
- **ताइणं** (tāiṇaṃ) = protectors — of the six classes of living beings and of one's own self; the word also carries the sense of "those moving toward liberation"
- **तेसिं** (tesiṃ) = for them, for those (great seers)
- **एयं** (eyaṃ) = this — the conduct enumerated below
- **अणाइण्णं** (aṇāiṇṇaṃ) = improper, not-to-be-practised, anachara
- **निग्गंथाण** (niggaṃthāṇa) = of the knot-less ones (nirgranthas) — those whose internal and external knots of possessiveness have been entirely untied
- **महेसिणं** (mahesiṇaṃ) = of the great seers (maharṣis) — the great sages who long for liberation

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening verse is the foundation on which the rest of the chapter rests. Before listing what a monk must not do, the verse first describes who the monk *is*. This is deliberate: rules without a vision of the person they apply to become mere prohibitions; but when you first see the person — the silent, fearless, knot-less wanderer who has put down the world — then the rules appear not as restrictions but as the natural shape of that life.

Four qualifying phrases are used. First, the monk is "well-settled in self-restraint." This means his own self has come to rest within the seventeen-fold practice of restraint — restraint from the five kinds of karmic inflow, restraint of the five senses, suppression of the four passions, and the keeping of the three guards (of mind, speech, body). His mind does not run out into desires; his body does not run out into harmful action; his speech does not run out into useless or wounding words. He is settled.

Second, the monk is "wholly liberated." He has cut every external possession (clothes, gold, land, kin) and every internal possession (anger, pride, deceit, greed, attachment, hatred) — and not only cut them in his own action, but also refuses to cause others to gather them and refuses to silently approve of others gathering them. The renunciation is total, by all nine modes (three karanas times three yogas).

Third, the monk is a "protector." He protects himself from sliding into a lower state by living a careful life; he protects others by treating every soul, even the smallest invisible being, as identical in essence to his own. The word also carries a beautiful second meaning — *tāyī* from the root *tay*, "to move forward" — meaning he is one who is moving toward liberation. So he protects, and he progresses.

Fourth, he is "knot-less" (*nirgrantha*). This is the oldest Jain word for a true monastic. The "knots" are the internal and external entanglements of possessiveness; one who has untied every knot is *nirgrantha*. The monk is not someone who merely wears a particular robe or recites particular words; he is someone in whom the knots themselves have come undone.

Only such a person — settled, liberated, a protector, knot-less, a great seer longing for liberation — is the one for whom the following fifty-two prohibitions are spoken. The verse, therefore, is also a quiet invitation: become this person first, and then the rules will fit you naturally.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before listing what a true monk should never do, the scripture first describes who that monk is — someone fully settled in self-discipline, completely free of all possessions inside and out, a guardian of every living being, and one whose inner knots of greed and attachment have been completely untied.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of all the "knots" — anger, pride, possessiveness, attachment — which one in you is most tightly tied today, and what would it feel like to begin loosening it?

## SUTRA 3.2 — The Nine Improper Acts (Items 1–9): Aaudeshik to Vijan

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उद्देसियं कीयगडं, णियागमभिहडाणि य ।
राइभत्ते सिणाणे य, गंधमल्ले य वीयणे ॥३.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.2 ||
uddesiyaṃ kīyagaḍaṃ, ṇiyāgam-abhihaḍāṇi ya |
rāibhatte siṇāṇe ya, gaṃdhamalle ya vīyaṇe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Improper for the monk are:) food prepared with him as the intended recipient, food bought for him, food fixed-by-prior-invitation, food brought to him from another place, eating at night, bathing, perfumes and flower-garlands, and fanning.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उद्देसियं** (uddesiyaṃ) = aaudeshik — food prepared by a householder with the monk in mind as recipient
- **कीयगडं** (kīyagaḍaṃ) = bought food — food the householder purchased specifically for the monk
- **णियाग** (ṇiyāga) = nityaag — food received by a fixed standing invitation ("come to my house every day")
- **अभिहडाणि** (abhihaḍāṇi) = brought-near food — food carried from elsewhere to the place where the monk is staying
- **य** (ya) = and
- **राइभत्ते** (rāibhatte) = ratri-bhakta — eating at night
- **सिणाणे** (siṇāṇe) = bathing — partial or full
- **गंध** (gaṃdha) = perfume — applying or wearing scented substances
- **मल्ले** (malle) = malya — wearing flower garlands
- **वीयणे** (vīyaṇe) = vijan — fanning oneself with a fan, leaf, peacock-feather, etc.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse begins the actual list of fifty-two anacharas, naming the first nine. Each one corresponds to a specific way that careless conduct slips into the monastic life and binds karma — and therefore each one is to be carefully avoided.

The first four are violations connected to **food collection**. Jain monks live by going from house to house and accepting only food that the householder cooked for the household itself, with no idea that a monk would come. The moment food is prepared or bought "for the monk," the householder has had to start a new fire, kill the small lives in the water, harm the lives in the grain — and the monk who eats such food becomes a silent partner to that violence. *Aaudeshik* is food prepared with the monk in mind. *Kitkrit* is food specifically purchased. *Nityaag* is food received under a standing invitation, where the householder keeps cooking extra every day on a "permanent reservation" — this binds both monk and householder. *Abhihrit* is food brought from another house or another place to where the monk is staying — the very act of carrying it through the world risks crushing tiny lives along the way.

The fifth — **eating at night** — is one of the most strictly enforced rules of the Jain monastic order. After sunset, countless invisible lives crowd the air, water, and food; eating then means devouring them with each mouthful. Even householders are encouraged to give it up; for a monk, it is part of one of the great vows itself. The text recognises four variants — bringing food in the day to eat at night, bringing it at night to eat in the day, and so on — and forbids all of them.

The sixth and seventh — **bathing** (full or partial) and **using perfumes** — are forbidden because they pamper and beautify the body, and because they involve killing the small lives in water and the small lives in plants from which scents are made. The monk treats his body as a vehicle for liberation, not as an object of beautification.

The eighth — **wearing flower-garlands** — combines the harm of plucking living plants with the inner harm of vanity and decoration. The ninth — **fanning** — sets the air-bodied beings into motion and harms them; it also panders to the body's desire for comfort. A true monk endures both heat and cold without artificial relief.

The pattern across all nine is consistent: each act either (a) harms a living being, (b) feeds an indulgence of the body, or (c) makes the monk dependent on the world he has renounced. The ascetic life, by contrast, is meant to leave the world untouched and to leave the body unspoiled.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never eat food that was specially made, bought, reserved, or carried-over for him; should never eat after sunset; should not bathe, wear perfume, wear flower garlands, or use a fan — because each of these either harms tiny living beings or pampers the body that is supposed to be left alone.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What small comforts do you arrange for yourself every day without ever asking what they cost the world around you?

## SUTRA 3.3 — The Eight Improper Acts (Items 10–17): Sannidhi to Dehapralokana

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संनिही गिहिमत्ते य, रायपिंडे किमिच्छए ।
संबाहणा दंतपहोयणा य, संपुच्छणा देहपलोयणा य ॥३.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.3 ||
saṃnihī gihi-matte ya, rāyapiṃḍe kimicchae |
saṃbāhaṇā daṃta-pahoyaṇā ya, saṃpucchaṇā deha-paloyaṇā ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) hoarding (food or items), eating in a householder's vessel, the king's food, "what would you like?" food, body-massage, tooth-cleaning, asking after a householder's well-being, and inspecting one's own body (in a mirror, etc.).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संनिही** (saṃnihī) = sannidhi — to store or hoard things (food, medicines)
- **गिहिमत्ते** (gihi-matte) = eating in a householder's vessel
- **य** (ya) = and
- **रायपिंडे** (rāyapiṃḍe) = rajapinda — the king's food / royal food
- **किमिच्छए** (kimicchae) = kimicchaka — food prepared after asking the monk "what would you like?"
- **संबाहणा** (saṃbāhaṇā) = sambahana — pressing/massaging the body or having it massaged
- **दंतपहोयणा** (daṃta-pahoyaṇā) = dant-pradhavan — washing/cleaning the teeth
- **संपुच्छणा** (saṃpucchaṇā) = sampuchchhana — asking a householder questions about his body, household, well-being
- **देहपलोयणा** (deha-paloyaṇā) = deha-pralokana — looking at one's own body in a mirror, water, oil, sword-blade, gem, etc.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the next eight anacharas (numbers 10–17), each of which deepens the picture of how subtly the monastic life can slide back into worldly habits.

The tenth, **hoarding**, may seem innocent, but it is utterly forbidden. The monk does not even keep medicine for tomorrow. The instinct to "save for later" is the very root of possessiveness, and possessiveness is the opposite of *nirgrantha*. The scripture makes the rule absolute: no storage of any kind — not even of a smear of ghee — even in fear of grave illness. Hoarding generates greed, generates attachment, generates the silent assumption that "tomorrow is mine."

The eleventh, **eating from a householder's vessel**, sounds harmless but creates a chain of harm: the householder must wash the vessel afterwards (killing water-bodied lives), the food may have residues of impurities, the vessel may be metal and valuable enough to be feared lost or stolen. The monk eats only from his own simple bowl.

The twelfth, **the king's food**, is a fascinating prohibition. Royal food is rich, strengthening, and stimulating to the senses — exactly the kind of food that loosens self-restraint. Royal kitchens are also crowded with ministers, soldiers, and visitors — not safe spaces for a careful monk. Rich food breeds attachment to taste; soft luxury weakens the will. So this food is to be refused.

The thirteenth, **kimicchaka food**, is food prepared after the donor specifically asks the monk what he would like. The moment a monk has stated a preference, the donor cooks accordingly — and the monk has lost his neutrality. The Jain rule is to accept whatever pure food is naturally there, never to specify a desire.

The fourteenth, **body-massage**, in any of its four forms (massaging skin, flesh, bones, or hair-roots with oil or paste), is forbidden because it pampers the body and stimulates desire. The fifteenth, **tooth-cleaning** with brushes and pastes, is similarly forbidden — Jain monks practise *adant-dhavan*, the discipline of not brushing the teeth, partly because brushing harms tiny lives in water and plant-fibre, and partly because preoccupation with appearance is itself a violation of the vow.

The sixteenth, **asking after a householder's well-being** — "How is your health? How is your son? How is business?" — sounds polite but is a slow door back into the world. To take interest in the householder's life is to silently approve of that worldly life and to weave oneself back into the social web one had left.

The seventeenth, **looking at one's own body** in a mirror or any reflective surface, is forbidden because it nourishes self-image and pride. The monk has surrendered the body as a vehicle; staring at it is to start owning it again.

Across all eight, the underlying lesson is the same: small breaches of detachment compound into great breaches of detachment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should not store food or medicine, should not eat from the householder's own dishes, should not accept a king's food or food that was made on request, should not get massaged or brush his teeth, should not chat about people's families and homes, and should not look at himself in a mirror — because each of these slowly pulls him back into the world he has left.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your day is spent silently arranging tomorrow's comfort, and how would your mind feel if you let one such arrangement go?

## SUTRA 3.4 — The Five Improper Acts (Items 18–22): Dice-Play to Fire-Lighting

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अट्ठावए य णालीए, छत्तस्स य धारणट्ठाए ।
तेगिच्छं पाहणा पाए, समारंभं च जोइणो ॥३.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.4 ||
aṭṭhāvae ya ṇālīe, chattassa ya dhāraṇaṭṭhāe |
tegicchaṃ pāhaṇā pāe, samāraṃbhaṃ ca joiṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) playing the eight-square game (chess) and dice with the dice-tube, holding a parasol, medical treatment, wearing footwear, and the lighting of fire.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अट्ठावए** (aṭṭhāvae) = ashtapada — the eight-square dice/chess game
- **य** (ya) = and
- **णालीए** (ṇālīe) = nalika — playing dice using the dice-tube
- **छत्तस्स** (chattassa) = chhatra — parasol, umbrella
- **धारणट्ठाए** (dhāraṇaṭṭhāe) = for the holding (of)
- **तेगिच्छं** (tegicchaṃ) = chikitsa — medical treatment, taking medicine, practising medicine
- **पाहणा पाए** (pāhaṇā pāe) = wearing footwear/sandals on the feet
- **समारंभं** (samāraṃbhaṃ) = the lighting / kindling
- **च** (ca) = and
- **जोइणो** (joiṇo) = jyoti — fire

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the next five anacharas (numbers 18–22).

The eighteenth, **dice-and-board games** (whether the *ashtapada* board, used for chess-like games, or the *nalika* dice-tube), are forbidden because they consume time meant for study and meditation, generate excitement and rivalry, are mostly played for stakes (which is theft of what was not given), and are major occasions for negligence — the very opposite of the careful, watchful life of the monk.

The nineteenth, **holding a parasol**, is forbidden because (a) it is a sign of pride and worldly status, (b) carrying any unnecessary item violates the principle of minimal possessions, (c) the monk is meant to bear sun and rain as part of his austerity, and (d) under a parasol one cannot keep proper watch of the path for tiny lives. The various scriptures permit a parasol only in narrow exceptions — for a very ill or aged monk, with the order's permission — but for a healthy monk, holding one is improper.

The twentieth, **medical treatment**, is the most subtle of the prohibitions. Jain monks are expected to bear bodily pain and illness as part of their austerity — to accept it as the ripening of past karma — and not to seek treatment that often involves harming further living beings (boiling water, pulping plants, killing insects for ingredients). However, the scriptures wisely permit an exception: when the pain is genuinely beyond human endurance and obstructs the practice itself, the monk may take simple permitted treatment, observe penance for that exception, and continue. The general path is endurance; the exception is mercy for the unbearable.

The twenty-first, **wearing footwear**, is forbidden for a healthy monk. Walking barefoot keeps the monk humble, slow, and intensely watchful of the ground for lives. Wearing sandals breeds pride, encourages comfort, and erodes the careful walking discipline (*irya-samiti*). An exception is permitted only for those with a foot-illness or in extreme conditions — but for the healthy, walking barefoot is the rule.

The twenty-second, **lighting fire**, is among the most strictly forbidden acts. Fire is, in Jain understanding, the sharpest of all weapons — every flame burns countless invisible lives, and the act of lighting fire has further harm: it boils, it kills the air-lives that meet it, it consumes plant-lives. A monk does not light a fire to cook (he eats only what is given), to warm himself (he endures cold as austerity), or for light (he sits in darkness). To kindle a fire — or even to ask another to do so, or silently approve of it — is forbidden across all three karanas.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never play board games or dice, hold a parasol over himself, take medical treatment except in dire need, wear footwear, or light a fire — because each of these either wastes time, breeds pride, or destroys living beings.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When pain or discomfort comes, do you instinctively reach for relief, or can you sit with it long enough to ask what it is teaching you?

## SUTRA 3.5 — The Five Improper Acts (Items 23–27): Shayyatara-Pinda to Body-Rubbing

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिज्जायरपिंडं च आसंदी पलियंकए ।
गिहंतरनिसेज्जा य, गायस्सुव्वट्टणाणि य ॥३.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.5 ||
sijjāyara-piṃḍaṃ ca āsaṃdī paliyaṃkae |
gihaṃtara-nisejjā ya, gāyas-suvvaṭṭaṇāṇi ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) the food of the lodging-host, sitting on a high cushioned seat or a couch, sitting inside a householder's home, and rubbing/scrubbing the body.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिज्जायरपिंडं** (sijjāyara-piṃḍaṃ) = shayyatara-pinda — the food of the householder who has given lodging
- **च** (ca) = and
- **आसंदी** (āsaṃdī) = aasandi — a chair, stool, palanquin-seat, sling, etc., used for sitting
- **पलियंकए** (paliyaṃkae) = paliyanka — a couch, bed, cot, sofa
- **गिहंतर** (gihaṃtara) = inside-of-another-house, the householder's home (other than one's own resting-place)
- **निसेज्जा** (nisejjā) = sitting down
- **य** (ya) = and
- **गायस्स** (gāyassa) = of the body
- **उव्वट्टणाणि** (uvvaṭṭaṇāṇi) = rubbing, scrubbing, applying scented pastes

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names anacharas 23 through 27.

The twenty-third, **food of the lodging-host** (*shayyatara*), is forbidden because the householder who has given the monk shelter is already in a position of generosity — to also accept food from him would create dependence, would generate (in the householder) pride and over-attachment, and would (in the monk) erode the discipline of going from house to house collecting alms with neutrality. The shayyatara is the host whose home (or the house he owns or supervises) the monk uses for night-rest and for the morning and evening pratikraman; from such a host, no food, no water, no clothing, no vessels — only grass, mat, plank, simple sitting boards are accepted.

The twenty-fourth, **sitting on a high or cushioned seat**, is forbidden because such seats often have hollows or weavings where insects and tiny lives hide invisibly — and sitting on them crushes them. Such seats also erode humility, breed comfort, and weaken the monk's natural discipline of sitting on the bare ground or a simple board.

The twenty-fifth, **sitting on a couch or bed**, is similarly forbidden — and not only for the same reasons, but also because beds and couches are associated with sleep and comfort. The monk does not "lie down to rest" in the householder's manner; he sits and meditates, and when he must rest, it is on a simple straw mat. Beds also threaten celibacy in subtle ways, by associating the monk's posture with householder leisure.

The twenty-sixth, **sitting inside a householder's home**, is one of the most frequently violated rules among careless monks — and one of the most spiritually dangerous. To sit down in a householder's home (other than for the brief moment of receiving alms) is to enter a zone where conversation, attachment, the gaze of women, the sound of children, and the play of household life will inevitably affect the mind. It threatens celibacy, disturbs the careful rhythm of the alms-round, and may make other alms-seekers feel obstructed. The monk pauses, receives, blesses, and moves on.

The twenty-seventh, **rubbing or scrubbing the body** with powders, pastes (*ubatans*), or fragrant substances, is forbidden because it is straightforwardly an act of beautification. The sixth chapter of this same sutra explicitly forbids body-decoration; rubbing is part of that. To rub the body for its beauty or comfort is to bind heavy karma — for the body is to be used, not adorned.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should not eat the food of the householder who has given him shelter, should not sit on chairs or beds or couches, should not sit inside a householder's home, and should not rub or scrub his body with cleansing or scented pastes — because each of these either hides hidden lives, threatens celibacy, or pampers the body.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of what you do for your body is genuine care, and how much is silent vanity dressed up as care?

## SUTRA 3.6 — The Four Improper Acts (Items 28–31): Service to Householders to Calling Family in Distress

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गिहिणो वेयावडियं, जा य आजीववत्तिया ।
तत्ताणिव्वुडभोइत्तं, आउरस्सरणाणि य ॥३.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.6 ||
gihiṇo veyāvaḍiyaṃ, jā ya ājīva-vattiyā |
tattāṇi-vvuḍa-bhoittaṃ, āurassaraṇāṇi ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) doing service for a householder, earning a living by revealing one's caste/lineage, eating food that is heated but not yet completely lifeless, and calling out to relatives when in distress.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गिहिणो** (gihiṇo) = of the householder
- **वेयावडियं** (veyāvaḍiyaṃ) = vaiyavritya — bodily service / household service / commerce
- **जा** (jā) = which
- **य** (ya) = and
- **आजीववत्तिया** (ājīva-vattiyā) = ajiva-vrittita — living by displaying one's caste, lineage, or qualifications to obtain food
- **तत्ताणिव्वुड** (tattāṇi-vvuḍa) = tapt-anivrtta — heated but not yet completely lifeless (water, food)
- **भोइत्तं** (bhoittaṃ) = the eating of (such things)
- **आउरस्सरणाणि** (āurassaraṇāṇi) = atura-smaranani / atura-sharanani — calling-to-mind or seeking-shelter-of relatives when in painful distress
- **य** (ya) = and

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names anacharas 28 through 31.

The twenty-eighth, **doing service for a householder**, has two senses, both forbidden. *Vyavahara* — engaging in household errands, business, or assistance for the householder. *Paricharya* — physical attendance, carrying water for them, fetching things from outside. The reason both are forbidden is that performing any service for a householder amounts to silent approval of the worldly life — and silent approval is one of the three modes through which sin is bound (doing, causing, approving). The monk has renounced household life by all three modes; serving the householder reverses this.

The twenty-ninth, **earning by displaying lineage** (*ajiva-vrittita*), names eight specific ways monks sometimes corrupt the alms-round: by stating their high birth caste, by stating their high lineage, by claiming agricultural skill, by claiming craftsmanship, by claiming trade-knowledge, by claiming knowledge of fighting / weaponry, by displaying austerity for fame, or by reciting one's scriptural learning to impress. All of these turn alms into payment — payment for one's identity or skill — and that turns the *bhikshu* into a vendor. The pure alms-round is one in which the monk's identity disappears entirely behind the simple act of receiving.

The thirtieth, **eating heated-but-not-fully-lifeless food**, is one of the most precise rules in the scripture. Living water and living food contain countless invisible lives. The full process of "killing" the lives is heating them properly until every life-particle has dissolved — *prasuka*, completely lifeless. *Tapt-anivrtta* is the in-between state: heated but not completely so — for example, water that was boiled but not boiled long enough, or that was boiled but has begun to come back to life because too much time has passed (in monsoon, three pahars; in winter, four; in summer, five). To consume such water or food is to consume living beings. Only completely lifeless food may be eaten.

The thirty-first, **calling out to relatives in distress**, is the most poignant of the prohibitions. When a monk falls ill, suffers great pain, or is overwhelmed by hardship, the natural human instinct is to remember one's family — to long for one's mother, to wish for the comfort of home. This longing must be set aside. To remember relatives at such moments — or to actually summon them or seek shelter at their home — is to undo the renunciation. The monk's only refuge in pain is the four refuges of Arihant, Siddha, Sadhu, and Dharma — and his own settled awareness. Otherwise the very vow he took risks unraveling.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never serve a householder, should never use his caste or skill or learning to get food, should never eat half-boiled water or food that still has even a flicker of life in it, and should never long for or call out to his family when he is suffering — because each of these unravels the renunciation he has taken.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When pain comes, who or what do you reach for first — and what does that reach reveal about where you actually take refuge?

## SUTRA 3.7 — The Seven Improper Acts (Items 32–38): Living Roots, Tubers, Fruits, Seeds

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मूलए सिंगबेरे य, उच्छुखंडे अणिव्वुडे ।
कंदे मूले य सच्चित्ते, फले बीए य आमए ॥३.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.7 ||
mūlae siṃga-bere ya, ucchu-khaṃḍe aṇivvuḍe |
kaṃde mūle ya saccitte, phale bīe ya āmae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) the radish-root, fresh ginger, sugarcane segments, all that is not fully lifeless — living tubers and roots, living fruits, and living seeds.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मूलए** (mūlae) = the radish-root (representative of all living roots)
- **सिंगबेरे** (siṃga-bere) = fresh ginger
- **य** (ya) = and
- **उच्छुखंडे** (ucchu-khaṃḍe) = segments of sugarcane
- **अणिव्वुडे** (aṇivvuḍe) = anivrtta — not fully lifeless / not fully processed
- **कंदे** (kaṃde) = tubers
- **मूले** (mūle) = roots
- **सच्चित्ते** (saccitte) = sachitta — sentient, still alive
- **फले** (phale) = fruits
- **बीए** (bīe) = seeds
- **आमए** (āmae) = aam — raw, unripe, living

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names anacharas 32 through 38, and all of them concern one principle: never eat anything still alive. In Jain biology, the plant kingdom contains many beings of vast magnitude — the *anantakaya* (infinite-bodied ones) such as roots and tubers, in which a single radish or potato contains literally infinite souls clustered together, all sharing one body. To bite into a living radish is to harm infinite souls in a single act. This is why root-vegetables are renounced not only by monks but, in many traditions, by the careful Jain laity.

The thirty-second forbids **living radish** (and by extension, all living roots). The thirty-third forbids **fresh ginger** — even though it is medicinal, in its living state it remains forbidden. The thirty-fourth forbids **living sugarcane segments** — segments cut from the cane that still hold the sap and life of the plant. The thirty-fifth forbids **living tubers** of any kind (onion, garlic, suran, potato, etc.). The thirty-sixth forbids **living roots** (the underground extensions of plant root-systems). The thirty-seventh forbids **living fruits** — mangoes, plums, and other fruits that have been picked but are still living, raw, unripe. The thirty-eighth forbids **living seeds** — wheat, barley, and other grains that, in their raw living state, are themselves capable of sprouting.

The verse uses three related terms to describe the "alive" state — *anivrtta* (not yet released from life), *sachitta* (still possessing consciousness), and *aam* (raw, unripe). These describe slightly different kinds of life: *anivrtta* is something on which a "weapon" (heat, salt, drying) has been applied but not enough to fully kill it; *sachitta* is something on which no killing process has yet acted; and *aam* is something not yet ripened to the point where the soul has naturally departed.

The principle behind all seven: **only fully lifeless food may be eaten**. This requires the food to be properly cooked, dried, salted, or otherwise rendered free of living souls before the monk consumes it. The discipline is not arbitrary — it is the practical face of *ahimsa*. The monk who refuses to eat living food embodies the truth that even his survival shall not be purchased by life. He eats only what is already fully gone from being.

This rule has a profound implication: every meal becomes a meditation on death and on the price of being alive. The food on the monk's bowl is not "ingredients" — it is the careful boundary between his life and theirs.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never eat raw radish, fresh ginger, fresh sugarcane, living tubers, living roots, living fruits, or living seeds — because each of these still contains living souls, sometimes infinitely many, and to eat them is to kill them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you put food in your mouth, do you ever pause to ask whose life paid for that bite?

## SUTRA 3.8 — The Six Improper Acts (Items 38–44): The Six Living Salts

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सोवच्चले सिंधवे लोणे, रोमालोणे य आमए ।
सामुद्दे पंसुखारे य, कालालोणे य आमए ॥३.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.8 ||
sovaccale siṃdhave loṇe, romāloṇe ya āmae |
sāmudde paṃsu-khāre ya, kālāloṇe ya āmae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) the living forms of sauvarchala-salt, sindhava-salt, romaka-salt, sea-salt, papadiyo-salt (alkali-salt), and black salt.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आमए** (āmae) = raw, living, sachitta
- **सोवच्चले** (sovaccale) = sauvarchala — a kind of mined rock-salt from the north
- **सिंधवे लोणे** (siṃdhave loṇe) = sindhava-salt — Sindhi rock-salt (Himalayan pink salt)
- **रोमालोणे** (romāloṇe) = romaka-salt — salt from the Roma region
- **सामुद्दे** (sāmudde) = sea-salt — salt from boiling sea-water
- **पंसुखारे** (paṃsu-khāre) = pansu-khara — alkali-salt that comes from upper soil layers ("papadiyo kharo")
- **कालालोणे** (kālāloṇe) = black salt
- **य** (ya) = and (joining the list)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names anacharas 38 (continuing the count from the previous list) through 44, and the topic of all six is salt — but not just any salt. The verse forbids only the *living* forms of these six salts. This is one of the most subtle teachings in the chapter, and it requires understanding the Jain biology of salt.

In Jain understanding, salt in its raw mined or freshly gathered state is itself a body of *prithvikaya* (earth-bodied) souls. The crystalline structure of salt is, biologically, a cluster of living earth-souls. Just as a radish contains infinite plant-souls, a lump of raw salt contains a vast collection of earth-souls. To grind, to crush, to dissolve raw salt is to kill these souls.

The six varieties named are: **sauvarchala**, a mined rock-salt from the northern mountains; **sindhava**, the famous pink salt of the Sindhu (Indus) region; **romaka**, salt from the Roma area (the northwestern desert); **samudra**, sea-salt obtained by boiling and crystallising sea-water; **pansu-khara**, the surface-alkali salt that crusts on certain soils after rain; and **kala-lavan**, the dark "black salt" mined from particular regions or formed naturally near the southern sea.

The rule is not that the monk cannot have any salt — it is that he cannot have salt in its *living* (sachitta) state. The lay donor who gives food to a monk is expected to have already cooked the food in such a way that the salt within it has gone through heat — and heat renders the salt-souls departed. Such "cooked salt" is *prasuka* (lifeless) and accepted. But raw salt — sprinkled, freshly mined, or freshly gathered — is forbidden until it has been rendered lifeless.

This rule reveals the depth of Jain non-violence: even the salt in one's food is treated as alive until proven dead. Even the most invisible forms of harm are accounted for. The monk's mindfulness extends not only to the obvious lives — animals and insects and plants — but even to the soul-clusters that live in the crystal structure of salt itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never use any form of salt — rock salt, pink salt, sea salt, black salt, or any other — while it is still in its living, raw state, because even a lump of salt contains countless tiny living beings until it has been properly cooked.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How far would your sense of "harm" have to extend before you started seeing life everywhere — even in the salt on your plate?

## SUTRA 3.9 — The Eight Improper Acts (Items 45–52): Body-Care Practices

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धुवणेत्ति वमणे य, वत्थीकम्म विरेयणे ।
अंजणे दंतवण्णे य, गायब्भंगविभूसणे ॥३.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.9 ||
dhuvaṇetti vamaṇe ya, vatthī-kamma vireyaṇe |
aṃjaṇe daṃta-vaṇṇe ya, gāyabbhaṃga-vibhūsaṇe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Also improper are:) the dhauti-neti cleansing practice, induced vomiting, enema treatments, taking purgatives, applying eye-makeup, colouring the teeth, oiling/anointing the body, and beautifying-decoration.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **धुवणेत्ति** (dhuvaṇetti) = dhauti-neti — the practice of inserting a soft cloth or string through nose or mouth to internally cleanse the body
- **वमणे** (vamaṇe) = vamana — induced vomiting (using salt water or other agents)
- **य** (ya) = and
- **वत्थीकम्म** (vatthī-kamma) = vasti-karma — enema therapy / lower-tract sneha-administration
- **विरेयणे** (vireyaṇe) = virechana — purgative therapy (laxative)
- **अंजणे** (aṃjaṇe) = anjana — applying collyrium (kohl) or eye-decoration
- **दंतवण्णे** (daṃta-vaṇṇe) = dant-varna — colouring the teeth (or scrubbing them for cosmetic appearance)
- **गायब्भंग** (gāyabbhaṃga) = gatra-abhyanga — anointing the body with oil
- **विभूसणे** (vibhūsaṇe) = vibhushana — adornment, beautification

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the final eight anacharas (numbers 45 through 52), all of which concern care of the body — and the underlying principle is that the body is to be tolerated, not pampered. These practices, common in the wider yogic and Ayurvedic traditions of India, are considered improper for a Jain monk because they go beyond what survival requires.

The forty-fifth, **dhauti-neti**, is the well-known yogic practice of running a soft cloth or thread through the nose or mouth to clean the inner passages. While beneficial for health in the worldly sense, it serves the body's comfort and aesthetics rather than the soul's liberation, and is therefore forbidden.

The forty-sixth, **vamana** — induced vomiting — is a purification technique used in Ayurveda. It is forbidden for the monk because (a) the Jain monk should never have eaten so much that vomiting is required, and (b) the practice itself involves harming microscopic lives in the substances used to induce it. The very need for it indicates a violation of the discipline of moderate eating (*unodari*).

The forty-seventh, **vasti-karma** (enema), and the forty-eighth, **virechana** (purgative), are therapeutic interventions to clean the lower tract. They are forbidden because (a) they pamper the body, (b) they involve harm to lives, and (c) they signal indulgence rather than restraint.

The forty-ninth, **anjana** — eye-makeup with collyrium, kohl, or similar — is forbidden because it is unambiguously cosmetic. It both decorates the body (which the monk has set aside) and indirectly involves harm to the lives in the ingredients.

The fiftieth, **dant-varna** — colouring the teeth, or even scrubbing them for cosmetic whiteness — has been discussed already (rule 15 was about cleaning the teeth). This rule extends the prohibition to any treatment of the teeth that aims at appearance.

The fifty-first, **gatra-abhyanga** — applying oil to the body or massaging in oil — is forbidden because it directly nourishes softness, beauty, and luxury of the body. The monk's body should be hardened by austerity, not oiled into smoothness.

The fifty-second and final, **vibhushana** — beautifying decoration in any form (clothing, ornaments, hair-styling, beard or moustache trimming for appearance, nail-care for show) — is forbidden as the most general principle of all. The chapter's sixth-listed exclusion (in chapter 6 of this same sutra) calls *vibhushana* "the eighteenth fruitless act"; the eighth chapter calls it "as deadly as the talaputa poison" for the celibate monastic. A monk who decorates his body weakens his celibacy because women may then feel desire toward him; he himself binds heavy karma; he opens the door to the entire range of sensual involvements that he had renounced. The Uttaradhyayana Sutra, in its sixteenth chapter on the nine fences of celibacy, names beautification as one of the most dangerous of all.

The chapter has now listed all fifty-two anacharas. The traditional summary table at the end of the chapter (provided by the editors) lays out each of the fifty-two items with its name, its meaning, and its specific reason for being improper — all converging on the same principle: any act that harms living beings, pampers the body, encourages possessiveness, breaks celibacy, or pulls the monk back into the world is anachara, and is to be entirely avoided.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk should never do internal cleansing techniques like dhauti or neti, should never make himself vomit, should never take enemas or laxatives, should never apply eye-makeup or colour his teeth, should never oil his body, and should never decorate or beautify himself in any way — because each of these is for the comfort or beauty of a body the monk has already set aside.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you take care of your body, can you tell the difference between care that supports the soul and care that quietly worships the body itself?

## SUTRA 3.10 — The Glory of the Knot-less Ones (Conclusion of Anacharas)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वमेयमणाइण्णं, णिग्गंथाण महेसिणं ।
संजमम्मि य जुत्ताणं, लहुभूयविहारिणं ॥३.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.10 ||
savvam-eyam-aṇāiṇṇaṃ, ṇiggaṃthāṇa mahesiṇaṃ |
saṃjamammi ya juttāṇaṃ, lahu-bhūya-vihāriṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

All of this is improper for the great-seer knot-less ones who are joined to self-restraint and who wander as light as the wind.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सव्वं** (savvaṃ) = all
- **एयं** (eyaṃ) = this (the entire list of fifty-two)
- **अणाइण्णं** (aṇāiṇṇaṃ) = improper, anachara
- **णिग्गंथाण** (ṇiggaṃthāṇa) = of the knot-less ones (nirgrantha)
- **महेसिणं** (mahesiṇaṃ) = of the great seers (maharṣis)
- **संजमम्मि** (saṃjamammi) = in self-restraint
- **य** (ya) = and
- **जुत्ताणं** (juttāṇaṃ) = of those joined / engaged / immersed
- **लहुभूय** (lahu-bhūya) = light-being, wind-light, becoming light as the air
- **विहारिणं** (vihāriṇaṃ) = of the wanderers — those who go from place to place

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse closes the long enumeration of the fifty-two anacharas with a quiet thunderclap of praise. It does not add any new prohibitions; it gathers all of them together and points back to the kind of person for whom they are forbidden — and in doing so, it sketches a portrait of monastic freedom.

The phrase **"joined to self-restraint"** describes the monk's inner life. He is not merely *practising* restraint as an outward discipline; he is *joined* to it — restraint has become his very rhythm. The mind moves in restraint, the speech moves in restraint, the body moves in restraint. There is no gap between the monk and his vow.

The phrase **"light as the wind"** describes his outer life and is one of the most beautiful images in Jain monastic literature. *Lahu-bhuya-vihari* — "the light-being wanderer." There are two senses of "lightness" here: outer lightness (he carries almost nothing — only the few possessions absolutely required for the spiritual life — so he is physically unencumbered like the wind) and inner lightness (he has shed the weight of the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed — so his consciousness is no longer bowed down by their burden). Like the wind, he has no fixed home, no clinging, no place where he must be; he moves wherever the path of truth carries him, harming nothing as he passes.

The chapter is therefore not a set of constraints around the monk; it is the precise outline of his freedom. Every "do not" in the list is a "you do not have to" — you do not have to maintain a body that demands beauty, you do not have to maintain relationships that demand attention, you do not have to maintain possessions that demand defence, you do not have to maintain comforts that demand replenishment. To put down all fifty-two is to rise up like the wind itself.

The verse therefore reframes the entire chapter. The fifty-two prohibitions are not a cage; they are the lifting of a weight. And once that weight is lifted, the monk moves freely through the world — knot-less, weightless, untouched by what he passes.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

All fifty-two of the things just listed are improper for the true monks — those who have completely tied their lives to self-discipline and who move through the world as light and free as the wind.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What weights are you carrying that you have never even noticed — and what would your life feel like if you set them down?

## SUTRA 3.11 — The Six Marks of the Knot-less One

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पंचासवपरिण्णाया, तिगुत्ता छसु संजया ।
पंचणिग्गहणा धीरा, णिग्गंथा उज्जुदंसिणो ॥३.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.11 ||
paṃcāsava-pariṇṇāyā, tiguttā chasu saṃjayā |
paṃca-ṇiggahaṇā dhīrā, ṇiggaṃthā ujju-daṃsiṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The knot-less ones are: knowers of the five inflows, guarded in the three guards, restrained in (the protection of) the six classes of life, suppressors of the five senses, steadfast, and seers of the straight path (to liberation).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पंचासव** (paṃcāsava) = the five karmic inflows (himsa, falsehood, theft, sexual misconduct, possessiveness)
- **परिण्णाया** (pariṇṇāyā) = knowers — those who have known both with knowledge-comprehension (jnana-parijna) and renunciation-comprehension (pratyakhyana-parijna)
- **तिगुत्ता** (tiguttā) = ones guarded in the three guards (of mind, speech, body)
- **छसु** (chasu) = in the six (classes of living beings — earth, water, fire, air, plant, mobile)
- **संजया** (saṃjayā) = restrained / careful / protective
- **पंचणिग्गहणा** (paṃca-ṇiggahaṇā) = suppressors of the five (senses — sound, form, smell, taste, touch)
- **धीरा** (dhīrā) = steadfast / fearless (also in some readings: *vīrā* — heroic)
- **णिग्गंथा** (ṇiggaṃthā) = the knot-less (nirgranthas)
- **उज्जुदंसिणो** (ujju-daṃsiṇo) = ṛju-darśin — those who see the straight path (to liberation), those who see straightly (without crookedness)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse continues the praise of the knot-less ones by giving a precise six-fold description of who they are. Each of the six qualities is a complete spiritual programme.

**Knowers of the five inflows.** The five great inflows of karma — violence, falsehood, theft, sexual misconduct, and possessiveness — are the doors through which karmic matter enters the soul. The monk has become a "knower" of them in two senses: he knows them with the knowledge of their nature (so he sees clearly what they are), and he knows them with the knowledge of renunciation (so he has set them down). To merely know intellectually but not renounce is not real knowing; that is the ignorance of one who knows the wrong is wrong yet still does it. The true knower is the renouncer.

**Guarded in the three guards.** The three guards (*gupti*) are the inner sealing of mind, speech, and body. The mind is guarded from running into impure thoughts; the speech is guarded from running into impure words; the body is guarded from running into impure actions. To be triply guarded is to have turned all three inwards toward the soul.

**Restrained in the six classes of life.** The six classes are earth-bodied beings, water-bodied beings, fire-bodied beings, air-bodied beings, plant-bodied beings, and mobile beings (insects, animals, humans). The monk is *samyata* — protective and careful — toward all six, never harming any of them. This is the practical face of *ahimsa*.

**Suppressors of the five senses.** The five senses pull the soul outward toward sound, form, smell, taste, and touch — and through these toward attachment and aversion. The monk has not destroyed his senses, but he has subdued them: he hears without binding, sees without grasping, smells without preferring, tastes without craving, touches without clinging. The senses serve the soul; the soul does not serve the senses.

**Steadfast.** The Prakrit *dhīra* means both wise and unshakable; in some readings, *vīra* — heroic, valorous. The monk is one who, having taken up the path, does not turn back regardless of difficulty.

**Seers of the straight path.** *Ṛju-darśin* has two meanings here. First: one who sees the straight (uncomplicated, unbent) path of self-restraint to liberation, neither swerving into ritualism nor into laxity. Second: one who sees straight — sees with a clear, undistorted perception of what the soul actually is and what liberation actually requires.

These six qualities together form the silhouette of the *nirgrantha* — the knot-less seer who is fit for liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monks are those who have understood and given up the five sources of karma, who guard their mind, speech, and body, who are careful never to harm any of the six kinds of living beings, who have brought their five senses under control, who are steady and unshaken, and who clearly see the straight path that leads to liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the six qualities the scripture names, which one are you closest to — and which one feels furthest from where you are right now?

## SUTRA 3.12 — The Three Seasons of the Monk

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयावयंति गिम्हेसु, हेमंतेसु अवाउडा ।
वासासु पडिसंलीणा, संजया सुसमाहिया ॥३.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.12 ||
āyāvayaṃti gimhesu, hemaṃtesu avāuḍā |
vāsāsu paḍisaṃlīṇā, saṃjayā susamāhiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In summer they expose themselves to the sun's heat; in winter they remain unclothed; in the rainy season they withdraw into a single place — these are the restrained ones, the well-meditated.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयावयंति** (āyāvayaṃti) = they undertake atapana — exposure to heat
- **गिम्हेसु** (gimhesu) = in the summer
- **हेमंतेसु** (hemaṃtesu) = in the winter
- **अवाउडा** (avāuḍā) = unclothed, uncovered, without protection
- **वासासु** (vāsāsu) = in the rainy season
- **पडिसंलीणा** (paḍisaṃlīṇā) = pratisamlina — withdrawn into one place, having minimised activity, with the senses gathered in
- **संजया** (saṃjayā) = the restrained ones
- **सुसमाहिया** (susamāhiyā) = the well-meditated, the well-collected

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse paints the portrait of the monk's year. The Jain monastic life is calibrated to the seasons of India, and the monk's discipline shifts deliberately with each one — not to seek comfort but to seek the most challenging form of practice each season permits.

**In summer**, when the rest of the world flees to shade and water, the monk practises *atapana* — deliberate exposure to the sun. He may stand on one leg facing the sun, or sit on a heated rock, or walk in the noon heat without seeking shade. This is *ushna-atapana*, the heat-austerity. The reason is twofold: it burns away karmic matter through endured discomfort, and it tests the equanimity of the soul against the body's most basic complaint — heat.

**In winter**, when the rest of the world piles on layers, the monk wears the minimum (or, in the most rigorous tradition, none at all — *avauda*, "uncovered"). He sits in open places where the cold wind reaches him, accepts the bite of the cold, and meditates through it. This is *shita-atapana*, the cold-austerity. The body's instinct to seek warmth is not satisfied; the soul observes the body's protest without acting on it.

**In the rainy season**, the monk does not wander, because the rains bring forth countless new lives — insects, worms, sprouts, water-beings — and to walk through them would be to crush them. So he enters *paryushana*, the four-month period of monsoon retreat, settles in one place, restricts movement, and turns his energy entirely inward. This is *pratisamlinata* — the deliberate gathering-in of the senses, of activity, of attention. His feet rest; his mind goes deep.

The verse also names two qualities that hold all three seasons together: **saṃyata** (the restrained) and **susamāhita** (the well-meditated). Through summer's heat, winter's cold, and monsoon's stillness, the monk's restraint never breaks and his meditation never wavers. The seasons turn around him, but inside, he is steady. That is the monk.

This verse, perhaps more than any other in the chapter, conveys what the monastic life actually *looks like* across a year. It is not glamorous, not warm, not protected. It is the deliberate stripping away of every cushion the world offers — and the discovery that, beneath the cushions, the soul is not weaker but stronger.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monks stand under the burning sun in summer, sit unclothed in the winter cold, and stay quietly in one place during the rainy season — and through all of it they remain perfectly self-controlled and deeply meditative.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What season of difficulty are you currently in — and what would it mean to stop fighting it and instead let it become your teacher?

## SUTRA 3.13 — The Aim of the Great Seers

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

परीसहरिऊदंता, धुयमोहा जिइंदिया ।
सव्वदुक्खप्पहीणट्ठा, पक्कमंति महेसिणो ॥३.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.13 ||
parīsaha-riūdaṃtā, dhuya-mohā jiiṃdiyā |
savva-dukkha-ppahīṇaṭṭhā, pakkamaṃti mahesiṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Subduers of the enemies that are the trials, shakers-off of delusion, conquerors of the senses — the great seers exert themselves with full vigour for the destruction of every suffering.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **परीसह** (parīsaha) = parisha — the trials / hardships of monastic life (twenty-two in number, listed in the second chapter of the Uttaradhyayana Sutra)
- **रिऊ** (riū) = enemies (ari)
- **दंता** (daṃtā) = subduers
- **धुयमोहा** (dhuya-mohā) = those who have shaken off delusion (moha) — destroyers of delusion-attachment
- **जिइंदिया** (jiiṃdiyā) = jitendriya — conquerors of the senses
- **सव्वदुक्ख** (savva-dukkha) = all suffering
- **प्पहीणट्ठा** (ppahīṇaṭṭhā) = for the purpose of complete destruction
- **पक्कमंति** (pakkamaṃti) = they exert themselves, they make full effort, they march forth
- **महेसिणो** (mahesiṇo) = the great seers

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the aim of the entire monastic life: the complete destruction of all suffering. Every prohibition, every austerity, every seasonal discipline of the previous verses — all of it points toward this one goal.

The verse names three things the monk subdues, and one thing he marches toward.

He **subdues the trials as enemies**. The twenty-two parishahas of the second chapter of the Uttaradhyayana — hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect-bite, nakedness, dissatisfaction, women, walking, places of rest, beds, scolding, beating, begging, refusal, illness, grass-pricks, dirt, honour, knowledge-pride, ignorance-frustration, and false-view — are not random discomforts; they are the soldiers in an army that the world sends against the monk to make him quit. He treats them as enemies precisely because they are trying to defeat his self-restraint, and he defeats them not by force but by equanimity. Each parishaha endured is a small victory; the cumulative victories destroy karma.

He **shakes off delusion**. *Moha* — delusion, infatuation, attachment — is the most fundamental binding karma. It is the soul's mistaken belief that "this is mine" and "I am this body" and "this matters." The monk has shaken it off — *dhuta*, as one shakes water off a wet cloth — and is no longer infatuated with anything outside the soul itself.

He **conquers the senses**. *Jitendriya* — having won over the senses — does not mean the senses have been destroyed but that they no longer dictate to the soul. Sound, form, scent, taste, touch arrive but pass without leaving a wake.

And then, with the trials subdued, delusion shaken off, and senses conquered, he **marches** — *pakkamati*, exerts vigorously — toward one single goal: the destruction of every suffering. The Jain understanding is that suffering, in all its forms — physical, mental, existential — is rooted in karma; karma is rooted in attachment-and-aversion; attachment-and-aversion are rooted in delusion; and delusion is destroyed by self-restraint and austerity. So when the monk walks the disciplined path, he is dismantling, link by link, the entire chain of suffering.

The image of the *great seer* marching forward is one of the most powerful in Jain literature. He is not waiting for liberation; he is *going* to liberation, step by step, each austerity another stride.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The great monks defeat every hardship as if it were an enemy, completely shake off all attachment and delusion, conquer their senses — and with all their strength they keep marching forward toward the one goal of ending every kind of suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If suffering is the symptom and attachment is the cause, what is one attachment in your life that, if released, would actually reduce your suffering?

## SUTRA 3.14 — The Two Fruits of Conduct

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुक्कराइं करित्ताणं, दुस्सहाइं सहित्तु य ।
के इत्थ देवलोएसु, केइ सिज्झंति णीरया ॥३.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.14 ||
dukkarāiṃ karittāṇaṃ, dussahāiṃ sahittu ya |
ke ittha devaloesu, kei sijjhaṃti ṇīrayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having done the difficult-to-do and having borne the difficult-to-bear, some (after death) attain the heavenly worlds, while some — having become free of all karma-dust — attain liberation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दुक्कराइं** (dukkarāiṃ) = the difficult-to-do — the rigorous practices and austerities
- **करित्ताणं** (karittāṇaṃ) = having done
- **दुस्सहाइं** (dussahāiṃ) = the difficult-to-bear — the unbearable hardships
- **सहित्तु** (sahittu) = having borne
- **य** (ya) = and
- **के** (ke) = some
- **इत्थ** (ittha) = here (in this world / in this present life)
- **देवलोएसु** (devaloesu) = in the heavenly worlds (the deva-lokas)
- **केइ** (kei) = some
- **सिज्झंति** (sijjhaṃti) = attain liberation, become Siddha
- **णीरया** (ṇīrayā) = nīraja — dust-less, free of karmic-dust

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the two possible outcomes of a life of intense Jain monastic practice. The image of "karma-dust" is an old and beautiful one in Jain thought: karma is conceived as fine particles, like dust, that cling to the soul through attachment-and-aversion. The soul that is *nīraja* — dust-less — is the soul that has shaken off every karmic particle and stands free.

The verse names two pathways. The first: *some attain the heavenly worlds*. These are monks who have practised intensely but whose karma is not yet fully exhausted at the moment of death. They have accumulated such an enormous storehouse of good karma through their austerity that, when the body falls away, they are reborn as devas in the celestial realms — radiant beings with vast lifespans, immense beauty, and tremendous spiritual power. There they enjoy the natural fruits of their practice, and from there they will eventually be reborn into a noble human birth, take up the monastic life again, and complete what was unfinished. This is the *intermediate fruit*.

The second pathway: *some become utterly free of karma-dust and attain liberation*. These are the monks whose practice has gone all the way to completion — every karma exhausted, every attachment dissolved, every binding broken. They become *Siddha* — fully liberated, eternally free, residing forever at the summit of the universe in pure consciousness. There is no return; the cycle of birth and death is finished. This is the *ultimate fruit*.

The Gujarati commentary makes a touching observation about why the verse pairs the two: it is to say that monastic effort is *never wasted*. Even if one cannot attain liberation in this life, one will at least attain a heavenly birth and continue the journey. The path is therefore safe — it cannot fail. Either you finish, or you advance further so as to finish next time.

The two phrases at the start — "having done the difficult-to-do" and "having borne the difficult-to-bear" — divide the monastic effort into its two halves. The "doing" is the active practice: meditation, study, austerity, alms-collection, careful walking, careful speech. The "bearing" is the passive endurance: heat, cold, hunger, insult, illness, hardship. Both halves are required; together they consume the karma and free the soul.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Those who have done the very difficult practices and patiently borne the very difficult hardships either go to the heavenly realms after death (if some karma still remains), or become completely free of all karma and attain final liberation — but either way, the effort is never lost.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a hardship you are facing right now were guaranteed to either liberate you fully or carry you to a higher place, would you bear it more peacefully?

## SUTRA 3.15 — The Final Goal: Liberation

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खवित्ता पुव्वकम्माइं, संजमेण तवेण य ।
सिद्धिमग्गमणुप्पत्ता, ताइणो परिणिव्वुडा ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥३.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.15 ||
khavittā puvva-kammāiṃ, saṃjameṇa taveṇa ya |
siddhi-maggam-aṇuppattā, tāiṇo pariṇivvuḍā || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having destroyed their previously-bound karmas through self-restraint and austerity, the protectors-of-life, having attained the path to liberation, reach final liberation. — Thus I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **खवित्ता** (khavittā) = having destroyed, having exhausted
- **पुव्वकम्माइं** (puvva-kammāiṃ) = previously-bound karmas (the storehouse of accumulated karma from past lives)
- **संजमेण** (saṃjameṇa) = through self-restraint (saṃyama)
- **तवेण** (taveṇa) = through austerity (tapas)
- **य** (ya) = and
- **सिद्धिमग्गं** (siddhi-maggaṃ) = the path to liberation (siddhi-marga)
- **अणुप्पत्ता** (aṇuppattā) = having attained, having entered
- **ताइणो** (tāiṇo) = the protectors — those who protect the six classes of living beings
- **परिणिव्वुडा** (pariṇivvuḍā) = pari-nirvrtta — fully liberated, completely extinguished (in the sense of all karma extinguished)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = "thus I say" — Sudharma Swami's traditional sealing-phrase, indicating that this teaching is given exactly as it was heard from Lord Mahavira

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the closing verse of the chapter, and it brings the entire teaching to its completion. The structure of the verse mirrors the structure of liberation itself: first the cause (*self-restraint and austerity*), then the action (*destroying previously-bound karmas*), then the path (*entering the road to liberation*), and then the result (*reaching final liberation*).

The Jain understanding of liberation is precise. The soul is, by its own nature, infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite energy. But this nature has been obscured, life after life, by the dust of karma — particles of subtle matter that cling to the soul through attachment-and-aversion. To liberate the soul is to remove every particle of this dust.

There are two parts to this removal. First, **stop new karma from coming in** — this is *samvara*, the closing of the doors through which karma enters. Self-restraint (*saṃyama*) is the chief means: by guarding the mind, speech, and body, by protecting the six classes of life, by refusing to engage in the fifty-two anacharas, the monk closes the inflow.

Second, **destroy the karma already stored** — this is *nirjara*, the burning-away of accumulated karma. Austerity (*tapas*) is the chief means: by enduring hardship, by undertaking fasts, by sitting in meditation, by accepting heat and cold and hunger, the monk burns away the stored karma like fire dries up a pond.

The Gujarati commentary uses a vivid simile. Imagine a great pond of water. Water keeps flowing in through a stream; meanwhile, the sun shines down and dries up the water already there. If you want to empty the pond, you must do two things: block the inflow (close the stream) *and* let the sun dry the rest. The pond's water is karma; the inflow is new sin; closing the inflow is self-restraint; the sun is austerity. Do both, and the pond will eventually be entirely dry — a clean, empty space. So too the soul, once both samvara and nirjara are accomplished, becomes pure, clean, and entirely without karma. This is *parinirvrtti* — final liberation.

The phrase **"the protectors"** at the end is significant. The monk who has reached liberation is described not by his power, not by his wisdom, not by his austerity, but by his *protection*. He has protected every living being he has encountered; he has refused to harm even the smallest soul; and through that protection he has himself been protected from karma. The chapter began with this word (*tāiṇaṃ* in sutra 1) and ends with it (*tāiṇo* in sutra 15) — the entire teaching is bracketed by the principle of protection.

The closing phrase **"thus I say"** (*tti bemi*) is the traditional seal of authenticity. Sudharma Swami, the chief disciple of Lord Mahavira, is reporting these teachings to his disciple Jambu Swami, and he ends the chapter with this affirmation: I am not telling you what I think; I am telling you exactly what I heard from the Lord. The chain of transmission is direct, unaltered, and authoritative.

The chapter closes here, and with it closes the brief account of conduct. The fifty-two anacharas have been listed. The portrait of the true monk has been drawn. The aim has been named. The path has been described. The final fruit has been declared. The student now knows what to avoid, who to become, and where the road leads.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

By practising self-discipline and austerity, the great protectors of life burn away all the karma they have collected over countless lifetimes, walk the path that leads to liberation, and finally reach the state of complete freedom. — This is what I have heard and now tell you.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If liberation is built one act of restraint and one bit of endurance at a time, what one small act today is yours to take?

The third chapter closes here. The fifty-two anacharas have been carefully named, the qualifications of the monk have been established, the seasonal discipline has been described, and the ultimate aim — the destruction of all suffering and the attainment of liberation — has been declared. The chapter is short by design (which is what makes it the *kshullaka* or "brief" account), but it is complete: it gives the new monastic everything he needs to know about what *not* to do.
