# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 10
# Sa-Bhikshu (સ ભિક્ષુ) — "Such a One is a (True) Monk"

**Adhyayan 10 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 445–471 (PDF pages 470–513)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 21*

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The tenth chapter of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is called **Sa-Bhikkhu** — "Such a one is a (true) monk." Its name comes from the refrain that closes every single one of its twenty-one verses: **जे स भिक्खू** (je sa bhikkhū) — "such a one is a monk." The chapter is a culminating portrait. Where the previous nine chapters laid out — piece by piece — the conduct, food-discipline, speech, study, and inner equanimity that define monastic life, this chapter gathers all of it into a single verbal mirror. It does not introduce new rules; it gathers the existing rules into a series of definitions of the ideal practitioner. To recite this chapter is to hold up, again and again, the question: "Am I such a one?"

The word **bhikkhū** (Skt. *bhikṣu*) literally means "one who lives by alms." But the chapter explicitly distances the *ideal* bhikkhu from a mere alms-gatherer. The chapter introduction is unusually pointed about this: a person who lives by begging but does not protect the six classes of beings, does not observe the great vows, and remains bound by gold and women is *not* a bhikkhu in this sense. The bhikkhu of this chapter is one whose entire being — mind, speech, body, food, study, equanimity — is shaped by non-violence, non-attachment, and steadfastness in the Jina's word. The chapter calls itself the marker between the two: every gatha is a touchstone on which true gold can be told from false.

Each verse follows the same architecture: a positive cluster of qualities (or sometimes a negative cluster of avoidances), and then the closing seal — *je sa bhikkhū*. Hearing it twenty-one times has the effect of a meditation. Each repetition forces the practitioner to test themselves against one more facet of the ideal: today, am I steadfast in celibacy? today, am I free from desire for gold? today, can I bear an insult as the earth bears the footstep? The chapter's pedagogical genius lies in this drumbeat — the refrain becomes the reader's own examination of conscience.

The Niryukti tradition assigns this chapter the function of *summary and definition* (*upaśama-nidarśana*). What was scattered across the earlier chapters as instruction now returns as portrait. The chapter is therefore the proper close of the Dashvaikalik proper — what follows in the two Chulikas (appended sections) is a moving plea against backsliding and a description of solitary forest-conduct, but the systematic chapters of the Sutra end here, with the soul looking into the mirror of the verses and asking, twenty-one times, *je sa bhikkhū?* — "is such a one a monk?"

## SUTRA 10.1 — Steadfast in Celibacy

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिक्खम्म माणाइ य बुद्धवयणे, णिच्चं चित्तसमाहिओ हविज्जा ।
इत्थीण वसं ण याऽवि गच्छे, वंतं णो पडियाइ जे स भिक्खू ॥१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.1 ||
ṇikkhamma māṇāi ya buddhavayaṇe, ṇiccaṃ cittasamāhio havijjā |
itthīṇa vasaṃ ṇa yā'vi gacche, vaṃtaṃ ṇo paḍiyāi je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who, having renounced (the household) by the command (of the Jina) and (taken refuge) in the words of the Awakened Ones, remains always with a mind settled in equanimity; who never falls under the sway of women; who does not return to vomited (renounced) sense-pleasures — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णिक्खम्म** (ṇikkhamma) = having gone forth, having taken initiation
- **माणाइ** (māṇāi) = by the command (of the Bhagavan / āṇā)
- **बुद्धवयणे** (buddhavayaṇe) = in the words of the Awakened Ones (the Jinas, the omniscient teachers)
- **णिच्चं** (ṇiccaṃ) = always, constantly
- **चित्तसमाहिओ** (cittasamāhio) = with mind settled in equanimity, of composed mind
- **हविज्जा** (havijjā) = should be, should remain
- **इत्थीण** (itthīṇa) = of women
- **वसं ण याऽवि गच्छे** (vasaṃ ṇa yā'vi gacche) = never even goes under the sway / control
- **वंतं** (vaṃtaṃ) = vomited (renounced) — the sense-pleasures one has already given up
- **णो पडियाइ** (ṇo paḍiyāi) = does not desire again, does not return to
- **जे स भिक्खू** (je sa bhikkhū) = such a one is a (true) monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with the foundation of monastic life — *brahmacharya*, the great vow of celibacy and inner restraint. The verse stitches together three clauses, each one a test. First: the monk has gone forth *by the command* (āṇā) of the Bhagavan, not from worldly disappointment or escape — the renunciation must be rooted in the Jina's instruction, not in sorrow or pique. Second: the mind must be *constantly* settled in equanimity — not occasionally, not under favourable conditions, but as a permanent disposition. Third: the renounced is not to be re-tasted. The image of *vāntaṃ* — vomited matter — is deliberately strong. What has been thrown up is not to be swallowed back. The monk who returns, even in thought, to the pleasures left behind has betrayed the very meaning of going forth.

The placement of this verse first is significant. The chapter could have opened with non-violence or non-possession; it opens instead with celibacy and steadfastness of mind. The commentary explains that *brahmacharya* is the very life-breath of monastic conduct (*saṃyama-prāṇa-samā brahmacharyam*). Without it, the other vows cannot stand. A mind unsettled by sense-craving cannot hold non-violence, cannot hold truthfulness, cannot hold non-attachment. So the chapter begins where the practice begins — at the root of the will.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk is one who left home not on a whim but because the Jina's words drew him out; who keeps his mind steady all the time, not just when life is easy; who never lets a woman become the centre of his attention; and who refuses to go back, even in his thoughts, to the pleasures he has already renounced. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The things you have already given up — do you ever quietly long for them again? What would it mean to refuse to swallow back what you have already vomited?

## SUTRA 10.2 — Protector of the Six Classes of Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुढविं ण खणे ण खणावए, सीओदगं ण पिए ण पियावए ।
अगणिसत्थं जहा सुणिसियं, तं ण जले ण जलावए जे स भिक्खू ॥२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.2 ||
puḍhaviṃ ṇa khaṇe ṇa khaṇāvae, sīodagaṃ ṇa pie ṇa piyāvae |
agaṇisatthaṃ jahā suṇisiyaṃ, taṃ ṇa jale ṇa jalāvae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who does not himself dig the earth, nor cause others to dig it; who does not himself drink raw (unboiled, sentient) water, nor cause others to drink it; who, knowing fire to be like a sharply-honed weapon, neither himself kindles it nor causes another to kindle it — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुढविं** (puḍhaviṃ) = the earth (earth-bodied beings)
- **ण खणे** (ṇa khaṇe) = does not himself dig
- **ण खणावए** (ṇa khaṇāvae) = does not cause another to dig
- **सीओदगं** (sīodagaṃ) = raw cold water (sentient water that has not been boiled)
- **ण पिए** (ṇa pie) = does not himself drink
- **ण पियावए** (ṇa piyāvae) = does not cause another to drink
- **अगणिसत्थं** (agaṇisatthaṃ) = fire-as-weapon (fire which is itself a weapon to all beings)
- **जहा सुणिसियं** (jahā suṇisiyaṃ) = like (a knife) sharply-honed
- **तं ण जले** (taṃ ṇa jale) = does not himself kindle it
- **ण जलावए** (ṇa jalāvae) = does not cause another to kindle it

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse and the next together cover the six *kāyas* (classes of beings) — earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, and the mobile (trasa) beings. Here three are taken: earth, water, fire. The structure is *navakoti* in spirit — for each, the monk neither does it nor causes it. The third koti, *anumodana* (approval), is implied by the framing of the chapter and is to be understood throughout.

The image of fire as a *sunisiyaṃ sattha* — a "sharply-honed weapon" — is a Jain technical phrase. The Jains uniquely teach that fire is itself a sentient body (*tejaskāya*), and at the same time it kills every other body it touches. So in a single act of kindling, the practitioner injures the fire-beings themselves and any earth, water, air, and vegetation beings caught in its path. To kindle fire is to wield a blade against five categories of life at once. This is why the Jain monk neither cooks nor lights lamps nor warms himself — every fire is a weapon.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk does not himself dig the earth, drink raw water, or kindle fire — and he does not get someone else to do these things for him either. He understands that fire in particular is like a sharpened knife: every flame cuts through five kinds of living bodies at once.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What conveniences in your life depend on the silent killing of beings you cannot see?

## SUTRA 10.3 — Protector of Air, Plants, and Seeds

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणिलेण ण वीए ण वीयावए, हरियाणि ण छिंदे ण छिंदावए ।
बीयाणि सया विवज्जयंतो, सचित्तं णाहारए जे स भिक्खू ॥३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.3 ||
aṇileṇa ṇa vīe ṇa vīyāvae, hariyāṇi ṇa chiṃde ṇa chiṃdāvae |
bīyāṇi sayā vivajjayaṃto, sacittaṃ ṇāhārae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who does not himself fan air (with a fan or the like), nor cause others to fan it; who does not himself cut green (vegetation), nor cause others to cut it; who, always avoiding (treading on) seeds (and sprouted things), does not consume sentient (uncooked, living) food — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अणिलेण** (aṇileṇa) = with the air (i.e., with a fan that stirs the air-beings)
- **ण वीए** (ṇa vīe) = does not himself fan
- **ण वीयावए** (ṇa vīyāvae) = does not cause another to fan
- **हरियाणि** (hariyāṇi) = green plants (vegetation-bodied beings)
- **ण छिंदे ण छिंदावए** (ṇa chiṃde ṇa chiṃdāvae) = does not cut, does not cause to be cut
- **बीयाणि** (bīyāṇi) = seeds, fruits with seeds, sprouted parts
- **सया विवज्जयंतो** (sayā vivajjayaṃto) = always avoiding (treading on or harming)
- **सचित्तं** (sacittaṃ) = sentient (still-living) substance — uncooked food
- **णाहारए** (ṇāhārae) = does not eat / does not take as food

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the protection of the six *kāyas*. With the previous verse on earth, water and fire, and this verse on air (vāyu), vegetation (vanaspati), and the mobile (trasa) beings implicit in *sacittaṃ* (sentient food which includes mobile-bodied creatures), the monk who passes these two tests is one who has truly internalised the first great vow.

The detail of "fanning the air" is striking. To stir the air with a fan is, in Jain reckoning, to disturb the bodies of air-beings. This is why monks do not use fans even in summer — the relief is bought at the price of injury. The detail of avoiding seeds is also pointed: a sprout is the embodied life of a vegetation-being beginning its course. To crush it underfoot is to extinguish that life. The monk walks watching the ground (*īryā-samiti*), choosing each step.

The phrase *sacittaṃ ṇāhārae* — "does not eat sentient food" — is the dietary corollary. All Jain monastic food must be *acchitta* (not-living): boiled water, not raw; cooked vegetables, not raw; food prepared by householders for themselves, not specially made for the monk. The verse seals the picture: the true monk's body, every step and every meal, is held within the discipline of *ahimsā*.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk does not stir the air with a fan, does not cut or have anything cut from a living plant, walks watching where seeds may have fallen so as not to crush them, and does not eat any food that is still living — that is, raw, unboiled, or uncooked. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you walked across a patch of ground and looked, before stepping, at what tiny life might be there?

## SUTRA 10.4 — Refuses Food Made For Him

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वहणं तस-थावराण होइ, पुढवीतणकट्ठणिस्सियाणं ।
तम्हा उद्देसियं ण भुंजे, णो वि पए ण पयावए जे स भिक्खू ॥४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.4 ||
vahaṇaṃ tasa-thāvarāṇa hoi, puḍhavī-taṇa-kaṭṭha-ṇissiyāṇaṃ |
tamhā uddesiyaṃ ṇa bhuṃje, ṇo vi pae ṇa payāvae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Cooking) becomes the killing of mobile and immobile beings — those that depend on earth, grass, and wood. Therefore the monk who does not eat food specially prepared (uddiṣṭa) for him, who himself neither cooks nor causes another to cook — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वहणं** (vahaṇaṃ) = killing, slaughter
- **तस-थावराण** (tasa-thāvarāṇa) = of mobile (trasa) and immobile (sthāvara) beings
- **होइ** (hoi) = becomes, occurs
- **पुढवीतणकट्ठणिस्सियाणं** (puḍhavī-taṇa-kaṭṭha-ṇissiyāṇaṃ) = of those (beings) sheltering in earth, grass, wood
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **उद्देसियं** (uddesiyaṃ) = made specifically for (the monk) — uddiṣṭa-doṣa food
- **ण भुंजे** (ṇa bhuṃje) = does not eat
- **णो वि पए** (ṇo vi pae) = nor himself cooks
- **ण पयावए** (ṇa payāvae) = nor causes (another) to cook

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha gives the *reason* the monk eats only what was already prepared for the householder's own family. Cooking — the lighting of fire, the use of water, the cutting of vegetables, the heating of grain — destroys earth-beings, water-beings, fire-beings, air-beings, vegetation-beings, and any tiny mobile beings that have taken shelter in the grain or the wood. So when food is cooked *uddiṣṭa* (specifically with the monk in mind), the monk is the moral cause of all that killing — even though it was the householder's hands that did it.

This is the heart of the Jain bhikshachari (alms-rounds) discipline. The monk goes only to homes where food was being made anyway, takes only a little from many homes, and never accepts what was made for him. His begging is meant to leave the household's karma untouched. The verse states the principle plainly: cooking is killing of the six *kāyas*; therefore the monk neither does it, asks for it, nor accepts what was done on his account.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When food is cooked, many tiny beings are killed — beings in the earth, grass, wood, water, and fire that are used. So the true monk does not accept any food that was made specifically for him; he does not cook himself, and he does not ask anyone else to cook for him. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If your meals were measured by the lives extinguished to make them, what would your plate look like?

## SUTRA 10.5 — Treats Six Classes Like Himself

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रोइय णायपुत्तवयणे, अत्तसमे मण्णेज्ज छप्पि काए ।
पंच य फासे महव्वयाइं, पंचासवसंवरे जे स भिक्खू ॥५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.5 ||
roiya ṇāyaputtavayaṇe, attasame maṇṇejja chappi kāe |
paṃca ya phāse mahavvayāiṃ, paṃcāsavasaṃvare je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who, having taken delight in the words of the Son of the Jnāta clan (Mahavira), regards the six classes of living beings as equal to himself; who fully observes the five great vows; who shuts down the five inflows of karma (āśrava) — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **रोइय** (roiya) = having taken delight in, having accepted with full conviction
- **णायपुत्तवयणे** (ṇāyaputtavayaṇe) = in the words of the Jnāta-putra (Mahavira, son of the Jnāta clan)
- **अत्तसमे** (attasame) = as equal to one's own self
- **मण्णेज्ज** (maṇṇejja) = should regard, should consider
- **छप्पि काए** (chappi kāe) = the six (classes of) bodies — earth, water, fire, air, vegetation, mobile
- **पंच** (paṃca) = the five
- **फासे** (phāse) = touches / observes (literally "touches" the vow — practises it intimately)
- **महव्वयाइं** (mahavvayāiṃ) = the great vows (mahāvratas — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession)
- **पंचासवसंवरे** (paṃcāsavasaṃvare) = in the stopping (saṃvara) of the five inflows (āśrava — wrong belief, non-restraint, negligence, passion, and yoga-activity)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is the doctrinal core of the chapter. It joins three pillars: (1) faith in the Jina's word (*roiya Nāyaputta-vayaṇe*), (2) the foundational ethical attitude — to see every other living being as oneself (*atmavat sarvabhūteṣu*), and (3) the formal monastic vows (the five mahāvratas) and the formal stopping of karmic inflow (*saṃvara*). Faith without ethics is empty; ethics without vow-discipline is unstable; vow-discipline without saṃvara does not stop new karma. All three must hold together.

The phrase *attasame maṇṇejja chappi kāe* — "consider the six classes of beings as equal to one's own self" — is the philosophical foundation of Jain ahimsā. The reason not to harm an ant or a leaf is not sentiment but identity: the consciousness in the ant is the same consciousness in me. To see this is to make non-violence not a rule but a recognition. The five mahāvratas are then not arbitrary prohibitions; they are the natural conduct of one who has truly seen.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk has wholeheartedly accepted Lord Mahavira's teaching, treats every one of the six kinds of living beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile creatures) as he treats himself, fully observes the five great vows, and stops the five doors through which karma flows in. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Could you, even for a single hour today, regard every living thing you encountered as identical in essence to yourself?

## SUTRA 10.6 — Vomits the Passions, Refuses Gold

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चत्तारि वमे सया कसाए, धुवजोगी हवेज्ज बुद्धवयणे ।
अहणे णिज्जायरूवरयए, गिहिजोगं परिवज्जए जे स भिक्खू ॥६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.6 ||
cattāri vame sayā kasāe, dhuvajogī havejja buddhavayaṇe |
ahaṇe ṇijjāyarūvarayae, gihijogaṃ parivajjae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who always vomits out the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed); who is steadfast in the yoga of the Awakened Ones' words; who is wealthless and free of (desire for) silver and gold; who avoids the activities and company of householders — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चत्तारि** (cattāri) = the four
- **कसाए** (kasāe) = passions — anger (krodha), pride (māna), deceit (māyā), greed (lobha)
- **वमे सया** (vame sayā) = always vomits out, ejects
- **धुवजोगी** (dhuvajogī) = steadfast (dhruva-yogī) — firm in the yoga (of mind, speech, body) of the Jina's word
- **हवेज्ज** (havejja) = should be / becomes
- **बुद्धवयणे** (buddhavayaṇe) = in the words of the Awakened
- **अहणे** (ahaṇe) = wealthless (a-dhana — without possessions)
- **णिज्जायरूवरयए** (ṇijjāyarūvarayae) = free from (desire for) silver and gold (rūpya = silver, jāta-rūpa = gold)
- **गिहिजोगं** (gihijogaṃ) = the activities of householders (cooking, buying, selling, household connections)
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = utterly avoids

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first half names the inner renunciation — the four *kashayas* (passions). The image is again *vāntaṃ* / *vamati* — to vomit, to throw up. The passions are not merely to be controlled or suppressed; they are to be ejected like poison from the body. And not once; *sayā* — "always." Each fresh stir of anger, each fresh pulse of pride, each fresh whisper of deceit or greed must be vomited at the moment it arises.

The second half names the outer renunciation. *Ahaṇe* — "without wealth." *Ṇijjāyarūva-rayae* — "free from (taste for) silver and gold." A Jain monk does not own, does not handle, does not even desire to handle gold or silver. *Gihijogaṃ parivajjae* — avoids the activities of householders: cooking, buying, selling, owning a home, accumulating things. The two halves match: outer renunciation without inner is hypocrisy; inner without outer is fantasy. Both must be present together. This is the rare combined picture.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk vomits out the four poisons of anger, pride, deceit, and greed every time they arise — not just once but always. He stays steady in the Jina's teaching, owns nothing, has no taste for silver or gold, and stays clear of household activities and householder company.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Anger has just risen in you. Can you eject it the way the body ejects spoiled food — not analyze it, not justify it, just throw it up?

## SUTRA 10.7 — Right-Seeing, Always Alert

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सम्मद्दिट्ठी सया अमूढे, अत्थि हु णाणे तवे संजमे य ।
तवसा धुणइ पुराणपावगं, मणवयकायसुसंवुडे जे स भिक्खू ॥७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.7 ||
sammaddiṭṭhī sayā amūḍhe, atthi hu ṇāṇe tave saṃjame ya |
tavasā dhuṇai purāṇapāvagaṃ, maṇavayakāyasusaṃvuḍe je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who is right-sighted (samyag-dṛṣṭi); who is always alert (un-deluded); who steadfastly abides in knowledge, austerity, and self-restraint; who shakes off (destroys) accumulated past sin by austerity; who is well-restrained in mind, speech, and body — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सम्मद्दिट्ठी** (sammaddiṭṭhī) = right-believer, right-sighted
- **सया अमूढे** (sayā amūḍhe) = always un-deluded, always alert
- **अत्थि हु** (atthi hu) = is indeed (firm, present)
- **णाणे तवे संजमे य** (ṇāṇe tave saṃjame ya) = in knowledge, in austerity, and in self-restraint
- **तवसा** (tavasā) = by austerity
- **धुणइ** (dhuṇai) = shakes off, destroys
- **पुराणपावगं** (purāṇapāvagaṃ) = ancient (accumulated) sin-karma
- **मणवयकायसुसंवुडे** (maṇavayakāyasusaṃvuḍe) = well-restrained in mind, speech, and body

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the four pillars of liberation as the Jain tradition counts them: right faith (*samyag-darśana*), right knowledge (*samyag-jñāna*), right conduct (*samyag-cāritra*) — here split into austerity (*tapa*) and self-restraint (*saṃyama*). Right faith is the eye that sees correctly; right knowledge is what is seen through that eye; right conduct is the action that follows from what is seen.

*Amūḍhe* — "un-deluded" — is the technical name of one of the eight requirements (aṅga) of right belief: not to be deluded by displays of false miracles, not to be deluded by the persuasions of false teachers, and not to be deluded into ignoring the scriptures. The verse couples this with the *active* discipline: austerity that burns past karma, restraint of mind, speech, and body that prevents new karma. The picture is of a soul that both wipes the old slate clean and stops new chalk from being added.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk holds the right view of reality, stays alert and is not fooled, and stands firm in knowledge, austerity, and self-control. By his austerities he burns away the karma of past wrongs, and by carefully restraining his mind, speech, and body he prevents new karma from forming.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The two motions of the spiritual life — burning away the old, preventing the new — are you doing both, or only one?

## SUTRA 10.8 — Stores Nothing for Tomorrow

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव असणं पाणगं वा, विविहं खाइमं साइमं लभित्ता ।
होही अट्ठो सुए परे वा, तं ण णिहे ण णिहावए जे स भिक्खू ॥८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.8 ||
taheva asaṇaṃ pāṇagaṃ vā, vivihaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ labhittā |
hohī aṭṭho sue pare vā, taṃ ṇa ṇihe ṇa ṇihāvae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, having obtained food, drink, various chewables, and various savouries — the one who does not (think) "this will be of use tomorrow or the day after" and store it, nor cause another to store it (for him) — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise
- **असणं** (asaṇaṃ) = food (cooked grains)
- **पाणगं** (pāṇagaṃ) = drink
- **विविहं** (vivihaṃ) = of various kinds
- **खाइमं** (khāimaṃ) = chewables (fruits, dry items)
- **साइमं** (sāimaṃ) = savouries (relishes, condiments)
- **लभित्ता** (labhittā) = having obtained
- **होही अट्ठो** (hohī aṭṭho) = "(this) will be of need / use"
- **सुए परे वा** (sue pare vā) = tomorrow or the day after
- **तं ण णिहे** (taṃ ṇa ṇihe) = does not store it
- **ण णिहावए** (ṇa ṇihāvae) = does not have (another) store it

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse defines the Jain monk's daily food discipline: alms collected today must be eaten today. Nothing is set aside for tomorrow. The reason is *aparigraha* — non-possession — applied even to food. The very act of storing food for the morrow is an assertion of attachment to one's own continuity, an attempt to secure the future, a quiet possessiveness that the monk has formally renounced.

The traditional rule extends further: even within the same day, food brought in the first watch (morning) is not to be kept for the fourth watch (evening). The bird image is given in the commentary — birds eat what they need when hungry, store nothing. The monk's life is to follow that pattern. The result is twofold: the monk is forced to depend daily on the spontaneous generosity of the householders (which keeps humility alive), and the monk's mind is freed from the calculation, "How will I eat tomorrow?" — which is itself a great burden.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk gets food, water, snacks, or savouries on his alms-rounds, he does not think "I'll save some for tomorrow or the day after" — and he does not let anyone else save it for him either. He eats today what he received today.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How many of your daily decisions are not about today at all but about securing tomorrow?

## SUTRA 10.9 — Shares With Fellow Monks, Then Studies

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव असणं पाणगं वा, विविहं खाइमं साइमं लभित्ता ।
छंदिय साहम्मियाण भुंजे, भुच्चा सज्झायरए जे स भिक्खू ॥९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.9 ||
taheva asaṇaṃ pāṇagaṃ vā, vivihaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ labhittā |
chaṃdiya sāhammiyāṇa bhuṃje, bhuccā sajjhāyarae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, having obtained food, drink, various chewables, and various savouries — the one who, having invited his fellow monks (to share), then eats; and having eaten, becomes absorbed in self-study — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise (continuing the previous picture)
- **असणं पाणगं** (asaṇaṃ pāṇagaṃ) = food and drink
- **विविहं खाइमं साइमं** (vivihaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ) = various chewables and savouries
- **लभित्ता** (labhittā) = having obtained
- **छंदिय** (chaṃdiya) = having invited (chanda — to offer the choice)
- **साहम्मियाण** (sāhammiyāṇa) = (his) co-religionists, fellow monks of the same conduct
- **भुंजे** (bhuṃje) = eats
- **भुच्चा** (bhuccā) = having eaten
- **सज्झायरए** (sajjhāyarae) = absorbed in self-study (svādhyāya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verse barred the monk from storing food for tomorrow; this verse bars him from a subtler vice — eating his alms alone. The monk who returns from his rounds with good food must first invite his fellow monks to share. The technical word is *chaṃdiya* — to give them the choice ("please take what you wish"). Only after the offer has been made does the monk himself eat. This destroys the silent possessiveness that says "this is *mine* because I begged it."

The verse closes with the second discipline: after eating, the monk does not lounge, does not nap, does not chat. He becomes *sajjhāya-rae* — absorbed in self-study. The post-meal hour is dangerous to a renunciant: full belly, contented mind, the quiet pull toward indolence. The verse insists that this very hour be turned into svādhyāya — the recitation, contemplation, questioning, and pondering of scripture. Food is taken as fuel, and the fuel is immediately put to use.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk gets good food, water, snacks, or savouries from his alms-rounds, he first invites his fellow monks to share, then eats — and after eating, he goes straight to scriptural study and stays absorbed in it. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something good comes to you, is your first thought to share it, or to keep it?

## SUTRA 10.10 — Free of Quarrelling Speech, Calm in Sense and Conduct

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण य वुग्गहियं कहं कहेज्जा, ण य कुप्पे णिहुइंदिए पसंते ।
संजमे धुवजोगजुत्ते, उवसंते अविहेडए जे स भिक्खू ॥१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.10 ||
ṇa ya vuggahiyaṃ kahaṃ kahejjā, ṇa ya kuppe ṇihuiṃdie pasaṃte |
saṃjame dhuvajogajutte, uvasaṃte avihedae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who does not engage in quarrel-producing talk; who, even given cause, does not become angry; who has restrained sense-organs and is calm; who is steadfast-yoked in self-restraint; who is utterly tranquil and does not show contempt (for anyone) — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण य वुग्गहियं कहं कहेज्जा** (ṇa ya vuggahiyaṃ kahaṃ kahejjā) = does not at all speak quarrel-producing talk (vyāgrahikī kathā — talk that breeds dispute)
- **ण य कुप्पे** (ṇa ya kuppe) = does not become angry (even when provoked)
- **णिहुइंदिए** (ṇihuiṃdie) = with restrained / disciplined senses (nibhṛta-indriya)
- **पसंते** (pasaṃte) = calm, tranquil
- **संजमे** (saṃjame) = in self-restraint
- **धुवजोगजुत्ते** (dhuvajogajutte) = firmly yoked (in the threefold yoga of mind, speech, body)
- **उवसंते** (uvasaṃte) = utterly pacified (upaśānta — passions calmed)
- **अविहेडए** (avihedae) = not contemptuous, does not despise / scorn others, does not neglect proper duties

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse moves from food to speech and disposition. Four marks are named. *Na vuggahiyaṃ kahaṃ* — does not engage in talk that produces dispute. The Jain monastic tradition lists *vikathā* (distracting / harmful talk) into categories: talk about kings, kingdoms, and politics; talk about food; talk about women; talk about countries. To these the technical *vyāgrahikī kathā* is added — talk whose nature itself breeds quarrel ("X is a bad king," "Y is a bad community," "Z is a bad person"). The monk avoids the talk itself, not just the temper that follows.

*Na kuppe* — does not get angry even when given cause. The provocation may come, but the response does not. *Nihui-indie pasaṃte* — senses gathered in, mind tranquil. *Dhuvajogajutte* — steadfastly engaged in the disciplines (study, contemplation, alms, scripture). *Avihedae* — does not look down on or scorn others, even those who scorn him. The cumulative picture is of a man whom no provocation can ruffle, no insult can goad, no idle word can pull off course. The verse is a portrait of *upaśama* — the cooled-down, tempered, steady soul.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk does not engage in talk that starts quarrels, does not lose his temper even when provoked, keeps his senses calm and his mind peaceful, stays steadily yoked to his discipline, remains utterly tranquil, and does not look down on anyone.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

There is a kind of conversation you know breeds dispute. Could you simply not enter it today?

## SUTRA 10.11 — Bears Insult, Beating, and Terror Equally

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो सहइ हु गामकंटए, अक्कोसपहारतज्जणाओ य ।
भयभेरवसद्दसप्पहासे, समसुहदुक्खसहे य जे स भिक्खू ॥११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.11 ||
jo sahai hu gāmakaṃṭae, akkosa-pahāra-tajjaṇāo ya |
bhaya-bherava-sadda-sappahāse, samasuha-dukkha-sahe ya je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who endures the village-thorns (the painful sense-stimuli — harsh words and the like); who endures abuse, beating, and threats; who, on hearing terrifying sounds and demonic laughter, (does not flinch); who bears pleasure and pain with equal mind — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जो सहइ हु** (jo sahai hu) = the one who indeed endures
- **गामकंटए** (gāmakaṃṭae) = the "village-thorns" — sense-stimuli (śabda etc.) that prick the senses like thorns; the unpleasant sounds, sights, smells of village/town life
- **अक्कोसपहारतज्जणाओ** (akkosa-pahāra-tajjaṇāo) = abusive speech, beating, threats
- **भयभेरवसद्दसप्पहासे** (bhaya-bherava-sadda-sappahāse) = terrifying sounds, demonic laughter (the sounds made by demons / vetālas in solitary places)
- **समसुहदुक्खसहे** (samasuha-dukkha-sahe) = bearing pleasure and pain equally (with equanimity)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The "village-thorns" (*grāma-kaṇṭaka*) is a Jain technical phrase for the unpleasant sensory stimuli that a monk meets in inhabited places — the rough words of children, the contemptuous looks, the foul smells, the harsh sounds. They are called "thorns" because, like thorns in the foot, they prick the inner stillness of the practitioner. The verse names four levels of disturbance: ordinary sense-thorns; deliberate abuse, blows, threats; supernatural terrors (demonic sounds in lonely meditation places); and the alternation of pleasure and pain.

The monk is to endure all four with equanimity — *samasuha-dukkha-sahe*. Pleasure is to be borne; pain is to be borne. The verse does not say "endure pain bravely while enjoying pleasure" — it says bear them equally, treat them equally. This is the sign of one who has internalised that both are passing and neither defines the soul. The classic example given by the commentary is the muni Khandhakṛṣi, whose body was being skinned by the king's order while his mind remained settled in equanimity — an extreme case, but the verse demands the same orientation in every encounter.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk bears the small thorns of village life — harsh words, ugly sights, rude noises — without complaint. He bears abuse, beating, and threats. Even when terrifying sounds or demonic laughter come at him in lonely places, he does not flinch. And he holds pleasure and pain in the same hand.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Today, when something pleasant comes and when something unpleasant comes, can you receive them with the same face?

## SUTRA 10.12 — Stands in Cremation-Ground Meditation, Free of Bodily Craving

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिमं पडिवज्जिया मसाणे, णो भीयए भयभेरवाइं दिस्स ।
विविहगुणतवोरए य णिच्चं, ण सरीरं चाभिकंखए जे स भिक्खू ॥१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.12 ||
paḍimaṃ paḍivajjiyā masāṇe, ṇo bhīyae bhaya-bheravāiṃ dissa |
vivihaguṇa-tavorae ya ṇiccaṃ, ṇa sarīraṃ cābhikaṃkhae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who, having undertaken the standing-meditation (pratimā) in a cremation ground, does not become afraid even on seeing terrifying things; who is always devoted to the various virtues and austerities; and who has no longing (even) for the body itself — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पडिमं** (paḍimaṃ) = the (kāyotsarga) standing-meditation, pratimā — a vow of motionless meditation for a fixed duration
- **पडिवज्जिया** (paḍivajjiyā) = having accepted, having undertaken
- **मसाणे** (masāṇe) = in a cremation ground
- **णो भीयए** (ṇo bhīyae) = does not become afraid
- **भयभेरवाइं दिस्स** (bhaya-bheravāiṃ dissa) = on seeing fear-inspiring, terrifying things (vetāla-spirits, animals, etc.)
- **विविहगुण तवोरए** (vivihaguṇa tavorae) = devoted to manifold virtues and austerities
- **णिच्चं** (ṇiccaṃ) = always
- **ण सरीरं चाभिकंखए** (ṇa sarīraṃ cābhikaṃkhae) = does not crave (even) the body — does not desire the body's preservation

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse moves from passive endurance (previous gatha) to active austerity. The monk who, for the great wearing-away of karma, deliberately takes himself to a *masāṇa* — a cremation ground, a desolate forest, a haunted ruin — and there stands or sits in *kāyotsarga* meditation for a fixed time, neither moving nor speaking, no matter what appears, embodies the full freedom from bodily attachment. The image of *bhaya-bheravāiṃ* — fear-inspiring forms, demon-howls, sudden touches of jackals — is the test. Most beings would flee. The monk who has truly let go of the body does not flee, because there is nothing left to protect.

The closing line — *ṇa sarīraṃ cābhikaṃkhae* — "does not crave / desire the body" — is the deeper teaching. The monk does not even crave to keep his body intact. He is willing to lose it. The body is the instrument of the soul's discipline; once the soul has used it for liberation, it is not to be clung to. This is what makes the meditation in the cremation-ground possible: the practitioner has already, in mind, let the body go.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk goes to a cremation ground, takes a vow of standing meditation, and stays there motionless. Even when terrifying things appear — strange sounds, frightening shapes — he does not get scared. He is constantly devoted to virtue and austerity, and he does not even cling to his own body.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you let go of clinging to the body, what would you no longer be afraid of?

## SUTRA 10.13 — Bears Beating Like the Earth Bears the Footstep

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असइ वोसट्ट चत्तदेहे, अक्कुट्ठे व हए व लूसिए वा ।
पुढवि समे मुणी हवेज्जा, अणियाणे अकोउहल्ले जे स भिक्खू ॥१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.13 ||
asai vosaṭṭa cattadehe, akkuṭṭhe va hae va lūsie vā |
puḍhavi same muṇī havejjā, aṇiyāṇe akouhalle je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who has, again and again, given up his body (in kāyotsarga); whether abused, struck, or wounded — the one who remains like the earth (bearing all without recoil); who is without (binding) wishes (nidāna) and without curiosity (about the cause of his suffering) — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **असइ** (asai) = repeatedly, again and again
- **वोसट्ट चत्तदेहे** (vosaṭṭa cattadehe) = with body laid down (vyutsṛṣṭa) and given up (tyakta) — having undertaken kāyotsarga
- **अक्कुट्ठे** (akkuṭṭhe) = abused (with harsh words)
- **हए** (hae) = struck (with stick or fist)
- **लूसिए** (lūsie) = wounded (with sword or sharp instrument)
- **पुढवि समे** (puḍhavi same) = like the earth (which bears every footstep without protest)
- **मुणी हवेज्जा** (muṇī havejjā) = the muni should be / should remain
- **अणियाणे** (aṇiyāṇe) = without nidāna — without binding desires for future fruit (no wish like "may my austerity bring me wealth in next life")
- **अकोउहल्ले** (akouhalle) = without curiosity, without astonishment — does not even ask "why is this happening to me?"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse pushes the picture further than the previous one. There the monk endured terrifying sounds in a graveyard. Here he endures abuse, blows, even being cut. The image of *pṛthvī-sama* — like the earth — is one of the great Jain similes. The earth bears every footstep without complaint; the earth bears every kind of treatment — flowers planted, refuse dumped, blood spilled — without preference. The monk under blow and insult is to be like that. The earth does not retaliate; the earth does not feel injured; the earth simply continues to be earth.

Two further qualifications guard against subtle distortions. *Aṇiyāṇe* — without *nidāna* — means he is not enduring the suffering with a hidden wish: "May this earn me wealth or pleasure in some future life." Such a wish would chain the austerity to a future trap. *Akouhalle* — without curiosity — means he does not even wonder, "Why am I being beaten? What past karma caused this?" The wondering itself would be a subtle attachment to one's own story. The monk simply bears, without protest and without inquiry. The earth bears the footstep without asking whose foot it was.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Again and again the monk gives up his body in motionless meditation. Even when he is abused, hit, or wounded with weapons, he remains as steady as the earth — which bears every footstep without complaint. He has no hidden wish that his suffering should bring him some future reward, and he is not even curious about why this is happening to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When suffering comes, can you be the earth that bears the footstep — neither resisting, nor explaining it to yourself, nor asking why?

## SUTRA 10.14 — Crosses the Ocean of Birth and Death

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अभिभूय कायेण परीसहाइं, समुद्धरे जाइपहाओ अप्पयं ।
विइत्तु जाईमरणं महब्भयं, तवे रए सामणिए जे स भिक्खू ॥१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.14 ||
abhibhūya kāeṇa parīsahāiṃ, samuddhare jāipahāo appayaṃ |
viittu jāī-maraṇaṃ mahabbhayaṃ, tave rae sāmaṇie je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who, having conquered the (twenty-two) hardships through the body; who lifts his own self up out of the path of (re-)birth; who, knowing birth-and-death to be a great fear, is absorbed in austerity and in monastic life — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभिभूय** (abhibhūya) = having overcome, conquered
- **कायेण** (kāeṇa) = through the body (i.e., bodily endurance)
- **परीसहाइं** (parīsahāiṃ) = the (twenty-two) parīṣahas — hardships of monastic life: hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect-bites, etc.
- **समुद्धरे** (samuddhare) = lifts up, rescues
- **जाइपहाओ** (jāipahāo) = from the path of (re-)birth (jāti-patha — the cycle of births)
- **अप्पयं** (appayaṃ) = (his own) self
- **विइत्तु** (viittu) = having known, having understood
- **जाईमरणं** (jāī-maraṇaṃ) = birth and death
- **महब्भयं** (mahabbhayaṃ) = great fear (mahā-bhaya)
- **तवे रए** (tave rae) = absorbed in austerity
- **सामणिए** (sāmaṇie) = in śramaṇa-life, monastic discipline

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha names the goal toward which all the previous gathas point — *samuddhare jāipahāo appayaṃ*: lifting the self out of the path of (repeated) birth. The twenty-two *parīṣahas* (hardships) listed in the Jain tradition — hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect-bites, nakedness, displeasure with monastic life, women, walking, posture, sleeping-place, abuse, beating, begging, lack of alms, illness, grass-bed, dirt, honour, knowledge, ignorance, doubt — are not random sufferings but the recognised tests of monastic life. To bear them all, *kāyena* (with the body itself, not just with intellectual acceptance), is to use the body as the very instrument of liberation.

The phrase *jāī-maraṇaṃ mahabbhayaṃ* — "birth and death is a great fear" — is the proper Jain reorientation of fear. The worldly being fears death; the monk has learned that the *cycle* of births and deaths is what is to be feared, not death within it. To be absorbed in austerity (*tave rae*) and in monastic discipline (*sāmaṇie*) is not penance for its own sake but the active dismantling of the cycle. Each parīṣaha endured wears down the karma that would otherwise bring another birth.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk has used his very body to conquer the hardships of monastic life — hunger, thirst, cold, heat, and the rest. Knowing that the endless cycle of birth and death is the greatest fear, he lifts his own soul out of that cycle and stays absorbed in austerity and the disciplined life of a monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What if the thing to be feared is not death, but being born again into another body that will die again?

## SUTRA 10.15 — Restrained in Hands, Feet, Speech, Senses

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

हत्थसंजए पायसंजए, वायसंजए संजइंदिए ।
अज्झप्परए सुसमाहियप्पा, सुत्तत्थं च वियाणइ जे स भिक्खू ॥१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.15 ||
hattha-saṃjae pāya-saṃjae, vāya-saṃjae saṃjaiṃdie |
ajjhapparae susamāhiyappā, suttatthaṃ ca viyāṇai je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who is restrained in hands, restrained in feet, restrained in speech, restrained in the senses; who is absorbed in the inner self (adhyātma); who is well-composed in mind; who knows the meaning of the scriptures — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **हत्थसंजए** (hattha-saṃjae) = restrained in hand (movements careful, deliberate, non-injurious)
- **पायसंजए** (pāya-saṃjae) = restrained in foot (walking carefully, watching the path — īryā-samiti)
- **वायसंजए** (vāya-saṃjae) = restrained in speech (speaking only what is true, kind, necessary)
- **संजइंदिए** (saṃjaiṃdie) = with restrained sense-organs (gathered in, not chasing objects)
- **अज्झप्परए** (ajjhapparae) = absorbed in the inner self, in adhyātma (the science of the soul)
- **सुसमाहियप्पा** (susamāhiyappā) = with a well-composed self (susamāhita-ātmā)
- **सुत्तत्थं च वियाणइ** (suttatthaṃ ca viyāṇai) = and knows the meaning of the sutras (scripture)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse turns from the great battles (parīṣahas, cremation-ground meditation) to the small daily disciplines on which they rest. *Hattha-saṃjae* — careful with the hands. The monk does not wave his hands carelessly; whatever is to be set down is set down with full awareness; whatever is to be picked up is picked up after the spot has been examined for tiny living beings (*pratilekhana*). *Pāya-saṃjae* — careful with the feet. He walks watching the ground (*īryā-samiti*) so as not to crush life. *Vāya-saṃjae* — careful with speech. He speaks only what is true and beneficial. *Saṃjaiṃdie* — careful with the senses, not letting them wander toward objects.

To these four bodily disciplines is added the inner discipline: *ajjhapparae* — absorbed in adhyātma, the science and meditation of the soul; *susamāhiyappā* — well-composed; and *suttatthaṃ ca viyāṇai* — knowing the meaning of the scriptures, not just their words. The verse therefore pictures the monk as a being whose every limb, sense, mind, and study is gathered into a single focused practice. Restraint of the body and absorption in the soul together complete the picture.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk is careful with his hands, careful with his feet, careful with his speech, careful with his senses. He is absorbed in the study and meditation of the soul, has a calm and composed mind, and truly understands the meaning of the scriptures.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

For just one hour today, could you watch your hands, watch your feet, watch your tongue, and watch your senses?

## SUTRA 10.16 — Unattached to Equipment, Free of All Holdings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उवहिम्मि अमुच्छिए अगिद्धे, अण्णायउंछं पुलणिप्पुलाए ।
कयविक्कयसण्णिहिओ विरए, सव्वसंगावगए य जे स भिक्खू ॥१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.16 ||
uvahimmi amucchie agiddhe, aṇṇāya-uṃchaṃ pulaṇippulāe |
kaya-vikkaya-saṇṇihio virae, savva-saṃgāvagae ya je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who is un-infatuated and ungreedy with respect to monastic equipment; who collects (his alms) a little at a time from unknown houses; who takes only the most ordinary (uninviting) things; who is abstinent from buying, selling, and storing; who has gone beyond every kind of attachment — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उवहिम्मि** (uvahimmi) = with respect to upadhi (monastic equipment — robes, bowl, broom, etc.)
- **अमुच्छिए** (amucchie) = un-infatuated, not deeply attached
- **अगिद्धे** (agiddhe) = ungreedy
- **अण्णायउंछं** (aṇṇāya-uṃchaṃ) = (food taken) from unknown houses, a little at a time (uñcha)
- **पुलणिप्पुलाए** (pulaṇippulāe) = (taking) the most ordinary, uninviting (lit. "from-husk husk-less" — the lightest, most disregardable items)
- **कयविक्कयसण्णिहिओ विरए** (kaya-vikkaya-saṇṇihio virae) = abstaining from buying, selling, and storing-up (saṃnidhi)
- **सव्वसंगावगए** (savva-saṃgāvagae) = gone beyond all attachments (saṅga)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse turns to *aparigraha* — non-possession — applied even to the few items the monk is permitted to hold. The Jain monk's *upadhi* is small: a set of robes, a bowl, a broom, a face-cloth, books. But the verse warns that even these few items can become objects of attachment. *Amucchie* — un-infatuated; *agiddhe* — ungreedy. The monk who fusses over his bowl, who is partial to his particular robe, who hoards books — has fallen back into possession even within renunciation.

The food clauses sharpen the picture. *Aṇṇāya-uṃchaṃ* — a little (*uñcha*) at a time, from unknown (*ajñāta*) houses, where no preparation has been made for the monk and where the monk is not personally known. *Pulaṇippulāe* — taking only what is most insubstantial — not the rich choice items but the lightest, most ordinary, most easily-spared offerings. The monk is not to take anything that the householder might prize. Finally, *kaya-vikkaya-saṇṇihio virae* — utterly abstinent from any commercial activity (buying, selling) and from accumulation. The closing line — *savva-saṃgāvagae* — "gone beyond every attachment" — gathers the verse: the true monk is one who is free even from the few things he holds.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk is not infatuated with his few possessions and is not greedy for more. He goes for alms to houses where he is unknown, taking only a little from each, and only the most ordinary items — never the choice things. He never buys, sells, or stores anything. He has gone beyond every kind of attachment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even in your simplest belongings, is there one item you would be quietly upset to lose? What does that tell you?

## SUTRA 10.17 — Free of Greed, Honour, and Worldly Affection

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अलोल भिक्खू ण रसेसु गिज्झे, उंछं चरे जीवियं णाभिकंखे ।
इड्ढिं च सक्कारण पूयणं च, चए ठियप्पा अणिहे जे स भिक्खू ॥१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.17 ||
alola bhikkhū ṇa rasesu gijjhe, uṃchaṃ care jīviyaṃ ṇābhikaṃkhe |
iḍḍhiṃ ca sakkāraṇa pūyaṇaṃ ca, cae ṭhiyappā aṇihe je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The unagitated monk who is not greedy for tasty foods (rasas); who lives by uñcha (a-little-at-a-time) alms-rounds; who does not crave (dishonest) life; who renounces (lust for) wealth, honour, and worship; who is established in himself; who is free of (worldly) affection — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अलोल** (alola) = unagitated, not given to longing (a-lola — without lust)
- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **ण रसेसु गिज्झे** (ṇa rasesu gijjhe) = not greedy in (the six) tastes — sweet, sour, salty, bitter, pungent, astringent
- **उंछं चरे** (uṃchaṃ care) = goes (for alms) by uñcha — taking small amounts from many houses
- **जीवियं णाभिकंखे** (jīviyaṃ ṇābhikaṃkhe) = does not crave (an unworthy / unrestrained) life — i.e., would not stay alive at the cost of his discipline
- **इड्ढिं** (iḍḍhiṃ) = wealth, prosperity, pomp
- **सक्कारण** (sakkāraṇa) = honour (sat-kāra)
- **पूयणं** (pūyaṇaṃ) = ritual worship (pūjā)
- **चए** (cae) = renounces, gives up
- **ठियप्पा** (ṭhiyappā) = with self established (in soul, knowledge, restraint — sthitātmā)
- **अणिहे** (aṇihe) = without (worldly) affection (a-sneha)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse names two parallel renunciations: of *rasa* (taste) and of *yaśas* (honour). The monk is *alola* — unagitated — toward the six tastes. He eats what comes; he does not seek out the sweet or the savoury; he does not refuse what is bland. *Uṃchaṃ care* — he wanders taking only small portions from many houses, never burdening any one home, never being able to predict in advance what his meal will be. *Jīviyaṃ ṇābhikaṃkhe* is the deeper test: he would not stay alive at the cost of his discipline. If unrighteous food were the only food available, he would refuse, even at the cost of life.

The second renunciation is from the spiritual hazard of success. *Iḍḍhi* — material prosperity (donations, large gatherings); *sakkāra* — public honour and titles; *pūyaṇa* — ritual worship by laypeople. These come naturally to a successful monk and are exactly what destroy a successful monk. The Jain tradition recognises them as one of the most dangerous tests precisely because they come dressed as encouragements. The Sūyagaḍāṃga sūtra warns: "Take all the worship of the world as a great thorn in the foot — handle it with the utmost care, lest the subtle spike lodges itself within and is impossible to remove." The monk who is *aṇihe* — free of worldly affection — and *ṭhiyappā* — established in his own true self — is not pulled out of his discipline by either taste or honour.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk has no longing for tasty food and is not greedy for any of the six tastes. He goes from house to house taking only a little, and would rather not stay alive at all than stay alive by breaking his discipline. He gives up the desire for wealth, honour, and worship. He stays anchored in himself and is free of worldly affections.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Praise just came your way. Did your mind reach out for it the way the tongue reaches for something sweet? Could you simply not reach?

## SUTRA 10.18 — Speaks No Ill, Is Not Provoked, Knows Each Soul's Karma

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण परं वइज्जासि अयं कुसीले, जेण च कुप्पिज्ज ण तं वइज्जा ।
जाणिय पत्तेयं पुण्णपावं, अत्ताणं ण समुक्कसे जे स भिक्खू ॥१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.18 ||
ṇa paraṃ vaijjāsi ayaṃ kusīle, jeṇa ca kuppijja ṇa taṃ vaijjā |
jāṇiya patteyaṃ puṇṇa-pāvaṃ, attāṇaṃ ṇa samukkase je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who does not say of another, "this one is of bad conduct"; who does not speak that which would make another angry; who, knowing that each (being's) merit and demerit (karma) is its own, does not exalt himself — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण परं वइज्जासि** (ṇa paraṃ vaijjāsi) = does not speak of another (so as) to say
- **अयं कुसीले** (ayaṃ kusīle) = "this one is of bad conduct" (kuśīla — corrupt in conduct)
- **जेण च कुप्पिज्ज** (jeṇa ca kuppijja) = by which (speech) another would become angry
- **ण तं वइज्जा** (ṇa taṃ vaijjā) = does not speak that
- **जाणिय** (jāṇiya) = knowing
- **पत्तेयं** (patteyaṃ) = each (one's) own (separately)
- **पुण्णपावं** (puṇṇa-pāvaṃ) = merit and demerit (the karmic fruition)
- **अत्ताणं** (attāṇaṃ) = (his own) self
- **ण समुक्कसे** (ṇa samukkase) = does not exalt, does not raise up (in pride)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse turns to two of the most subtle traps of advancing practice — *para-ninda* (criticising others) and *atma-praśaṃsā* (self-exaltation). They are paired because they are the same defect from two angles. The monk who has begun to develop real discipline naturally notices the laxity of others; the temptation rises to label them "kuśīla" — slack, corrupt. The verse forbids this not only because it injures the other but because it fattens the speaker's own ego. *Ṇa taṃ vaijjā* — say nothing that would make another angry — extends the rule beyond direct criticism to any provoking speech.

The remedy given is profound: *jāṇiya patteyaṃ puṇṇa-pāvaṃ* — "knowing that each soul has its own particular merit and demerit." Each being acts according to the rise (udaya) of its own karma. The lapse you see in another is not for you to judge — it is the working out of karma you cannot see. To label is to assume a knowledge you do not have. And once this is understood, *attāṇaṃ ṇa samukkase* — one does not raise oneself up. For if I am keeping the discipline well, that too is the rise of my own particular karma; it is not a personal achievement to be boasted about. The Sūyagaḍāṃga is paraphrased: "The contempt and rejection of others is utterly fruitless." The monk steady in equanimity neither casts down others nor raises up himself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk does not call another person bad, and does not say anything that would provoke another to anger. He understands that every soul has its own particular karma rising — what looks like another's fault is really their karma working out — so he never lifts himself up by putting others down.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The next time you notice a flaw in someone, can you remember that it is their karma rising, not their character — and therefore not for you to judge?

## SUTRA 10.19 — Free of Pride in Birth, Looks, Gain, or Knowledge

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण जाइमत्ते ण य रूवमत्ते, ण लाभमत्ते ण सुएण मत्ते ।
मयाणि सव्वाणि विवज्जइत्ता, धम्मज्झाणरए जे स भिक्खू ॥१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.19 ||
ṇa jāi-matte ṇa ya rūva-matte, ṇa lābha-matte ṇa sueṇa matte |
mayāṇi savvāṇi vivajjaittā, dhamma-jjhāṇa-rae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who is not intoxicated by (high) birth, nor by beauty, nor by gain, nor by scriptural learning — having abandoned every kind of pride (mada), is absorbed in righteous meditation (dharma-dhyāna) — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण जाइमत्ते** (ṇa jāi-matte) = not intoxicated by jāti (birth, family, caste)
- **ण य रूवमत्ते** (ṇa ya rūva-matte) = not intoxicated by rūpa (beauty, form)
- **ण लाभमत्ते** (ṇa lābha-matte) = not intoxicated by lābha (gain, success in begging, donations)
- **ण सुएण मत्ते** (ṇa sueṇa matte) = not intoxicated by śruta (scriptural learning)
- **मयाणि** (mayāṇi) = pride-intoxications (madas)
- **सव्वाणि विवज्जइत्ता** (savvāṇi vivajjaittā) = having renounced all
- **धम्मज्झाणरए** (dhamma-jjhāṇa-rae) = absorbed in righteous meditation (dharma-dhyāna)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Jain tradition lists eight *madas* (pride-intoxications): pride of birth (jāti), pride of family (kula), pride of physical strength (bala), pride of wealth (aiśvarya), pride of beauty (rūpa), pride of austerities (tapa), pride of learning (śruta), and pride of gains/success (lābha). This verse names four — birth, beauty, gain, learning — and abbreviates the rest with *mayāṇi savvāṇi* — "all the prides." Even *one* of these intoxicants is enough to derail spiritual progress; the verse demands the renunciation of every one.

The list is striking. Pride of beauty is recognisably worldly. But pride of *learning* — the monk who knows the scriptures and is silently pleased with his knowledge — and pride of *austerity* (named in the larger list) — the monk who fasts severely and is silently pleased with his fasting — are the dangers of advanced practice. They look like merit but are themselves karma-binding. The verse closes with the positive: *dhamma-jjhāṇa-rae* — absorbed in *dharma-dhyāna*. The mind that is full of dharma-meditation has no room for the swelling of pride; the antidote is occupation. The contemplative gathered in the contemplation of dharma does not have time to admire himself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The true monk is not intoxicated by high birth, by his good looks, by his successful alms-rounds, or by his scriptural learning. He has given up every kind of pride and is absorbed in the meditation on dharma. Such a one is a true monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the eight prides — birth, family, strength, wealth, beauty, austerity, learning, success — which one quietly intoxicates you? Could you set down even one of them today?

## SUTRA 10.20 — Teaches the Pure Doctrine, Avoids Bad Conduct, Refuses Buffoonery

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पवेयए अज्जपयं महामुणी, धम्मे ठिओ ठावयइ परं पि ।
णिक्खम्म वज्जेज्ज कुसीललिंगं, ण याऽवि हास कुहए जे स भिक्खू ॥२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.20 ||
paveyae ajjapayaṃ mahāmuṇī, dhamme ṭhio ṭhāvayai paraṃ pi |
ṇikkhamma vajjejja kusīla-liṅgaṃ, ṇa yā'vi hāsa kuhae je sa bhikkhū ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The great muni who proclaims the noble word (the pure dharma); who, himself established in dharma, establishes others also (in it); who, having gone forth, avoids the marks of slack conduct; who does not engage in joking and clowning — such a one is a (true) monk.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पवेयए** (paveyae) = proclaims, expounds
- **अज्जपयं** (ajjapayaṃ) = the noble word (ārya-pada — the pure teaching of the Jina)
- **महामुणी** (mahāmuṇī) = the great muni
- **धम्मे ठिओ** (dhamme ṭhio) = (himself) established in dharma
- **ठावयइ परं पि** (ṭhāvayai paraṃ pi) = establishes others also (in dharma)
- **णिक्खम्म** (ṇikkhamma) = having gone forth (taken initiation, left the household)
- **वज्जेज्ज** (vajjejja) = avoids
- **कुसीललिंगं** (kusīla-liṅgaṃ) = the marks (signs, behaviours) of slack conduct
- **ण याऽवि हास कुहए** (ṇa yā'vi hāsa kuhae) = nor (engages in) joking, clowning, magical-tricks for entertainment

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse complements verse 18 (which forbade criticising others). There the monk was told not to put others down. Here he is told to actively *raise* others up — *paveyae ajjapayaṃ*, proclaiming the noble teaching. Having stood himself in dharma (*dhamme ṭhio*), he sets others also in it (*ṭhāvayai paraṃ pi*). This is the positive teaching mission: a true monk does not merely cultivate his own liberation; he becomes an instrument by which others are drawn to the path.

But this teaching mission has dangers, and the verse names two. First, the monk who teaches becomes admired, gathers followers, and is tempted to relax his discipline a little — what is called *śithilācāra* or *kusīla-liṅga* (the visible marks of laxity). The verse insists: in proclaiming the dharma, the monk's own conduct must remain tight. Second, the public teacher is tempted to become entertaining — to tell jokes, to perform "wonders," to amuse the crowd. *Hāsa* — laughter-talk; *kuhae* — clowning, magical tricks (the *kuhaka* tradition that uses sleight-of-hand or claimed miracles to attract crowds). The verse forbids both. The monk teaches the dharma, not himself; and he teaches by the gravity of truth, not by the lightness of entertainment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The great monk preaches the noble teaching. Standing firm in dharma himself, he establishes others in dharma too. Once he has gone forth, he avoids the visible signs of slack conduct, and he does not engage in joking or clowning around for entertainment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you teach or share what is good, do you teach the truth — or do you teach yourself, dressed up in the costume of the truth?

## SUTRA 10.21 — Conclusion: Reaches the State of No-Return

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं देहवासं असुइं असासयं, सया चए णिच्चहियट्ठियप्पा ।
छिंदित्तु जाईमरणस्स बंधणं, उवेइ भिक्खू अपुणागमं गई ॥२१॥ ॥त्ति बेमि॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 10.21 ||
taṃ dehavāsaṃ asuiṃ asāsayaṃ, sayā cae ṇiccahiyaṭṭhiyappā |
chiṃdittu jāī-maraṇassa baṃdhaṇaṃ, uvei bhikkhū apuṇāgamaṃ gaī || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That dwelling-in-the-body — impure and impermanent — the (monk), established in his own eternal welfare (the soul), always renounces (attachment to it). Having severed the bondage of birth and death, the monk attains the goal of no-return (the liberated state). — So I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तं देहवासं** (taṃ dehavāsaṃ) = that dwelling-in-the-body
- **असुइं** (asuiṃ) = impure (a-śuci — full of impure substances)
- **असासयं** (asāsayaṃ) = impermanent, perishable (a-śāśvataṃ)
- **सया चए** (sayā cae) = always renounces (attachment to)
- **णिच्चहियट्ठियप्पा** (ṇiccahiyaṭṭhiyappā) = (he whose) self is established in eternal welfare (nitya-hita-sthita-ātmā)
- **छिंदित्तु** (chiṃdittu) = having cut, having severed
- **जाईमरणस्स बंधणं** (jāī-maraṇassa baṃdhaṇaṃ) = the bondage of birth-and-death
- **उवेइ** (uvei) = attains, reaches
- **अपुणागमं गई** (apuṇāgamaṃ gaī) = the state of no-return (apunarāgamana — the liberated state from which there is no rebirth)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = so I say (the traditional closing of a chapter)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter closes with a single comprehensive verse that gathers everything before it into the goal. The body (*dehavāsa*) — described frankly as *asui* (impure: full of bone, sinew, fat, pus, sweat, excretions) and *asāsaya* (impermanent: a momentary water-bubble, a flash of lightning, a dewdrop on grass-tip) — is to be relinquished. Not destroyed (the body is the instrument of practice and must be guarded as long as it serves the practice), but de-identified-with. The monk does not love his body, does not nurture it for its own sake, does not cling to its preservation when it can no longer help.

*Ṇiccahiyaṭṭhiyappā* — the soul established in *nitya-hita*, in eternal welfare — is the inner condition. The soul's true welfare is not the body's comfort but the body's release. From this firm inner footing the monk severs (*chiṃdittu*) the bondage of birth-and-death — the long chain that has dragged the soul from one body to the next through immeasurable time. And the result is *apuṇāgamaṃ gaī* — the state of no-return. The Siddhakshetra. The summit of the universe. The eternal abode of pure consciousness. Once attained, never lost; once entered, never to be born again.

The chapter therefore ends where the Sutra began — at liberation. Each of the twenty-one verses has been a portrait of the one who attains it. The closing *tti bemi* — "so I say" — is the traditional seal of the Ganadhara: this is what was heard from the Bhagavan, this is what is being faithfully passed on. The chapter proper of the Dashvaikalik ends here.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

That dwelling in the body — which is full of impurities and bound to perish — the true monk always renounces his attachment to. Settled instead in the eternal welfare of his own soul, he cuts through the bondage of birth and death, and reaches the state from which no soul ever returns. So I say.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you stopped identifying with the body that will die, what would be left of you that does not die?

## END OF ADHYAYAN 10

॥ અધ્યયન-૧૦ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Adhyayan-10 sampurna — "Adhyayan 10 is complete.")*

The chapter that began with the question "who is a true monk?" closes with the answer: the one who, by every facet enumerated — celibacy, protection of the six classes, austerity, equanimity, freedom from possession, freedom from passion, freedom from pride, faithful teaching, and final non-identification with the body — severs the long chain of birth and death and reaches the unreturning home of the soul. The twenty-one verses have not added new doctrine; they have given the practitioner a mirror in which to keep looking, and a refrain — *je sa bhikkhū* — to keep asking. The mirror remains for any seeker who wishes to use it.

What follows in the two Chulikas — appended sections — is no longer the systematic Sutra but a moving plea against giving up (Pratham Chulika: Rativakya) and a portrait of solitary forest-conduct (Bije Chulika: Vivikta-Charya). The Dashvaikalik proper is complete here.

Jai Jinendra.
