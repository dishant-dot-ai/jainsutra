# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 4
# Chh Jeevanikaya (છ જીવનિકાય) — The Six Classes of Life

**Adhyayan 4 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 64–115 (PDF pages 124–187)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 19 prose sutras + 29 gathas = 48 sutras*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fourth Adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik — *Chh Jeevanikaya*, "The Six Classes of Life" — is the longest, the most systematic, and the most foundational chapter of the entire sutra. It is the chapter in which Jain monastic life crystallises from intuition into formal vow. A novice (*nava-dikshit*) at his Vadi Diksha (greater initiation) is given exactly this chapter to internalise; from it he learns the six categories of beings he must protect, the five great vows by which he protects them, the renunciation of night-eating that completes the protection, and the careful restraint (*yatana*) by which the protection is maintained moment by moment.

The chapter is structured in three clear divisions, all bound together by the single unifying purpose of *ahimsa*:

1. **Sutras 1–6 (Jeevanikaya division):** Sudharma Swami opens the chapter by reciting to his disciple Jambu Swami what he himself heard from Bhagavan Mahavira — the full ennumeration of the six classes of living beings. Five are *sthavar* (immobile, one-sensed): earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, and plant-bodied beings. The sixth is *trasa* (mobile, two- to five-sensed): worms, insects, animals, humans, hell-beings, and gods. Sutra 6 is the comprehensive vow — the practitioner pledges, before the Guru, to abstain from harming any of the six categories by all three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and three yogas (mind, speech, body).

2. **Sutras 7–13 (Charitra-dharma division):** The chapter then formalises the Five Mahavrats (great vows) of Jain monasticism — non-violence (*pranatipata viraman*), truthfulness (*mrishavada viraman*), non-stealing (*adattadana viraman*), celibacy (*maithuna viraman*), and non-possession (*parigraha viraman*) — and adds the sixth practice of *ratri-bhojan viraman* (renunciation of night-eating). Sutra 13 is the concluding pratigya in which the monk takes upon himself the full discipline.

3. **Sutras 14–19 (Yatana division):** The chapter then turns to *yatana* — the careful, mindful restraint by which the monk avoids harming each of the six kayas. Six parallel sutras describe how the monk must walk, sit, sleep, eat, drink, accept alms, and use his belongings so that no being of any of the six classes is harmed.

4. **Gathas 1–29 (Verse division):** The chapter closes with twenty-nine verses in metre. The earlier gathas (1–12) repeat the *yatana-ayatana* teaching in poetic form and stress the central importance of *jnana* (knowledge) — for ignorance, however well-intentioned, cannot protect beings. The middle gathas (13–25) trace the spiritual development arc: from knowing the six categories, to renouncing the world, to taking the vows, to attaining samvara, then nirjara, then kevalajnana, and finally moksha. The closing gathas (26–29) seal the teaching with the ending verse and benediction.

The chapter is called *dharma-prajnapti* — "the proclamation of dharma" — because in it the very content of Jain monastic dharma is announced. To know this chapter, the tradition holds, is to know the path; to live this chapter is to walk it.

The narration begins with Sudharma Swami addressing his disciple Jambu Swami, with the auspicious phrase **"Suyaṃ me āusaṃ! teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ"** — "I have heard, O long-lived one! Thus has it been declared by the Bhagavan." This formula, used at the opening of every Anga Agama, establishes the authority of unbroken oral transmission: what Sudharma teaches, he himself heard from Mahavira's own lips.

## SUTRA 4.1 — The Opening Declaration (Utthanika)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुयं मे आउसं ! तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं, इह खलु छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता, सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती ॥४.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.1 ||
suyaṃ me āusaṃ! teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ, iha khalu chajjīvaṇiyā nāmajjhayaṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ kāsaveṇaṃ paveiyā suakkhāyā supaṇṇattā, seyaṃ me ahijjiuṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ dhammapaṇṇattī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I have heard, O long-lived one! Thus was it declared by that Bhagavan: in this very Jain teaching, an Adhyayan named *Chh Jeevanikaya* (Six Classes of Life) has been proclaimed, well-spoken, and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, of the Kashyapa lineage. Beneficial it would be for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुयं मे** (suyaṃ me) = heard by me / I have heard
- **आउसं** (āusaṃ) = O long-lived one (a respectful address from Sudharma to his disciple Jambu)
- **तेणं भगवया** (teṇaṃ bhagavayā) = by that Bhagavan (Mahavira)
- **एवमक्खायं** (evamakkhāyaṃ) = thus declared
- **इह** (iha) = here, in this Jain shasan
- **खलु** (khalu) = indeed, certainly
- **छज्जीवणिया** (chajjīvaṇiyā) = the Six Jeevanikaya (six classes of living beings)
- **नामज्झयणं** (nāmajjhayaṇaṃ) = the Adhyayan named (so-called)
- **समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं** (samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ) = by Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira
- **कासवेणं** (kāsaveṇaṃ) = of the Kashyapa gotra (Mahavira's clan)
- **पवेइया** (paveiyā) = proclaimed
- **सुअक्खाया** (suakkhāyā) = well-spoken, fully narrated
- **सुपण्णत्ता** (supaṇṇattā) = well-expounded with example and reasoning
- **सेयं मे** (seyaṃ me) = beneficial / auspicious for me
- **अहिज्जिउं** (ahijjiuṃ) = to study, to learn, to recite
- **अज्झयणं** (ajjhayaṇaṃ) = the Adhyayan
- **धम्मपण्णत्ती** (dhammapaṇṇattī) = the proclamation of dharma (the alternate name of this chapter)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the opening sutra (*utthanika*) of the chapter — the formal declaration by Sudharma Swami to his disciple Jambu Swami that what follows has been heard directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. The phrasing **"suyaṃ me āusaṃ"** — "I have heard, O long-lived one" — is the standard opening of every Anga Agama. It establishes two things at once. First, it asserts the unbroken chain of oral transmission: Sudharma is not authoring; he is reporting. Second, it expresses Sudharma's own humility — he does not claim direct revelation, only faithful hearing.

The disciple is addressed as *āusaṃ* — "long-lived one" — a blessing as much as a name. Of all the qualities a disciple may have (good conduct, sharp intellect, faith, devotion), longevity is singled out because only the long-lived can carry the teaching forward to the next generation. By calling Jambu *āusaṃ*, Sudharma is implicitly entrusting him with the future of the shasan.

The chapter's two names appear in this very sutra. **Chh Jeevanikaya** ("Six Classes of Life") names the *content* — the six categories of beings that the chapter enumerates and protects. **Dhammapannatti** ("Proclamation of Dharma") names the *function* — the chapter is the formal announcement of what Jain monastic dharma actually is. To know this chapter is to know dharma; to follow it is to live dharma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Sudharma tells his disciple Jambu: "Listen — I heard this directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. He taught a chapter called the Six Classes of Life, also known as the Proclamation of Dharma. It will be good for you and for me to study it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Before any teaching can be received, the receiver must trust the lineage that carries it. Whose voice do you trust enough to let it shape how you live?

## SUTRA 4.2 — The Disciple's Question

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कयरा खलु सा छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती ? ॥४.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.2 ||
kayarā khalu sā chajjīvaṇiyā nāmajjhayaṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ kāsaveṇaṃ paveiyā suakkhāyā supaṇṇattā seyaṃ me ahijjiuṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ dhammapaṇṇattī ?

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

What, then, is that Adhyayan called *Chh Jeevanikaya* (Six Classes of Life), proclaimed by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage, well-spoken and well-expounded — the Adhyayan named *Dhammapannatti* (Proclamation of Dharma) which it would be beneficial for me to study?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कयरा** (kayarā) = which, what kind of, what is it
- **खलु** (khalu) = indeed
- **सा** (sā) = that
- (remaining words as in 4.1)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the disciple's response — the request to be told *what exactly* the Six Jeevanikaya Adhyayan contains. Jambu does not simply say "tell me"; he repeats the entire formula, including the lineage of transmission, the name of the speaker, and the name of the chapter. This is not redundancy but reverence: the disciple's question reproduces the teacher's framing word for word, signalling that he has received the introduction with full attention and is ready for the body of the teaching.

The Jain Agama style of question-and-answer mirrors a real classroom: the teacher introduces, the student requests, and the teacher then expounds. This sutra is the formal pivot between introduction and exposition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Jambu replies to Sudharma: "Tell me — what exactly is this Six Classes of Life chapter that Bhagavan Mahavira taught and that you say would be good for me to study?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A good question is itself a kind of preparation. When you ask, do you ask with the same attention with which you intend to listen?

This — what is now to be told — is that very Adhyayan called *Chh Jeevanikaya*, proclaimed and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage. It would be beneficial for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इमा** (imā) = this (about to be spoken now)
- (remaining words as in 4.1–4.2)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is Sudharma's transition sutra — his confirmation that what follows is the chapter Jambu has just asked about. The repeated full formula is again not redundancy but a teaching method: in oral transmission, the boundaries of a section must be clearly marked so that the listener knows when introduction ends and exposition begins. With this sutra, the introduction closes; the body of the chapter opens with sutra 4 (the enumeration of the six kayas).

Sudharma confirms: "Yes — what I am about to tell you now is exactly that chapter. It is good for both of us to study it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The doorway is itself part of the house. What does it mean to enter a teaching with the same care that the teacher has used to invite you in?

## SUTRA 4.4 — The Five Sthavar (One-Sensed Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं जहा– पुढविकाइया, आउकाइया, तेउकाइया, वाउकाइया, वणस्सइकाइया, तसकाइया ।

पुढवी चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । आऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । तेऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वाऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वणस्सई चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं ।

तंजहा– अग्गबीया, मूलबीया, पोरबीया, खंधबीया, बीयरुहा, संमुच्छिमा तणलया, वणस्सइकाइया, सबीया चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं ॥४.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.4 ||
taṃ jahā– puḍhavikāiyā, āukāiyā, teukāiyā, vāukāiyā, vaṇassaikāiyā, tasakāiyā |

puḍhavī cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | āū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | teū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | vāū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | vaṇassaī cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ |

taṃjahā– aggabīyā, mūlabīyā, porabīyā, khaṃdhabīyā, bīyaruhā, saṃmucchimā taṇalayā, vaṇassaikāiyā, sabīyā cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The six are these: (1) earth-bodied beings (*puḍhavikāiyā*), (2) water-bodied beings (*āukāiyā*), (3) fire-bodied beings (*teukāiyā*), (4) air-bodied beings (*vāukāiyā*), (5) plant-bodied beings (*vaṇassaikāiyā*), (6) mobile beings (*tasakāiyā*).

Earth has been declared as having consciousness (*cittamanta*). It is many-souled, each soul having its own separate existence — except where it has been transformed by *shastra* (a destroying force). Water has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Fire has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Air has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Plant-life has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra.

These are the kinds of plants: (1) those whose seed is at the tip (*aggabīyā* — like coriander), (2) those whose seed is in the root (*mūlabīyā* — like the lotus rhizome), (3) those whose seed is in the joint or node (*porabīyā* — like sugarcane, bamboo), (4) those whose seed is in the trunk (*khaṃdhabīyā* — like the banyan, fig), (5) those that grow from a seed (*bīyaruhā*), (6) those that arise spontaneously (*saṃmucchimā*) — the various grasses and creepers (*taṇalayā*), the plant-bodied beings, the seed-bearing — all are declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तं जहा** (taṃ jahā) = they are as follows
- **पुढविकाइया** (puḍhavikāiyā) = earth-bodied beings — beings whose body is earth (clay, stone, salt, ore, gold, silver, mica, diamond, gems)
- **आउकाइया** (āukāiyā) = water-bodied beings — beings whose body is water (rain, river, well, pond, fog, dew)
- **तेउकाइया** (teukāiyā) = fire-bodied beings — beings whose body is fire (hearth, furnace, ember, lightning, meteor, coal, flame)
- **वाउकाइया** (vāukāiyā) = air-bodied beings — beings whose body is air (high wind, whirlwind, dense wind, thin wind, gusty wind, pure wind)
- **वणस्सइकाइया** (vaṇassaikāiyā) = plant-bodied beings — beings whose body is vegetation
- **तसकाइया** (tasakāiyā) = mobile beings — those that can move of their own accord
- **चित्तमंतमक्खाया** (cittamaṃtamakkhāyā) = declared to be conscious / sentient (literally "endowed with chitta")
- **अणेगजीवा** (aṇegajīvā) = many-souled (each kaya holds countless individual souls)
- **पुढोसत्ता** (puḍhosattā) = each separately existing (each soul has its own independent existence)
- **अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं** (aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ) = except where transformed by shastra (a destroying agent — a force that makes the kaya *achit* / non-living)
- **अग्गबीया** (aggabīyā) = seed-at-tip plants (coriander)
- **मूलबीया** (mūlabīyā) = seed-at-root plants (lotus rhizome)
- **पोरबीया** (porabīyā) = seed-at-node plants (sugarcane, bamboo, reed)
- **खंधबीया** (khaṃdhabīyā) = seed-in-trunk plants (banyan, peepal, thor cactus)
- **बीयरुहा** (bīyaruhā) = seed-grown plants (those that grow from sown seed — wheat, barley)
- **संमुच्छिमा** (saṃmucchimā) = spontaneously-arising plants (mushrooms, certain grasses)
- **तणलया** (taṇalayā) = grasses and creepers (chameli, jasmine, jasmine vine)
- **सबीया** (sabīyā) = seed-bearing / together with seeds

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the doctrinal foundation of the entire chapter — and arguably of all Jain ethics. It announces, on the authority of the omniscient (*sarvajna*) Bhagavan Mahavira, that earth, water, fire, air, and plants are not inert matter but living, conscious beings. Each is *cittamanta* — endowed with consciousness. Each handful of soil, each drop of water, each spark, each gust, each blade of grass holds *aneka jiva* — countless individual souls — and each soul has its own *prithak satta*, its own separate existence. They are not a collective; each is an "I."

Modern science has only recently been able to confirm what Jain darshan asserted twenty-five centuries ago: a single drop of water holds thousands of microorganisms; a particle of soil teems with life. But Jain darshan goes further still — it holds that the very water itself, the very soil itself, the very fire itself is alive at the level of the substance. Each *one-sensed* being possesses only the sense of touch (*sparshendriya*); it cannot see, hear, smell, or taste, and it cannot move of its own accord. Its consciousness is dimmest. Yet it is consciousness, and therefore the soul within it is identical in essence to the soul in a human being — only more deeply veiled by karma.

The phrase **"aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ"** — "except where transformed by shastra" — is crucial. Shastra here means a "destroying agent" — anything that has acted on the kaya to render it non-living (*achit*). Cooked water is no longer a living water-body; baked clay is no longer a living earth-body; extinguished fire is no longer a living fire-body. The monk may use these *achit* substances; what he must absolutely avoid is harming or using *sachit* (still-living) substances. This single phrase is the entire foundation of Jain monastic alms-rules: monks accept only *prasuk* (cooked, processed, lifeless) food; they drink only boiled water; they walk on already-trodden paths; they sit only where the surface has been cleaned of life.

The plant section adds the eight categories of vegetation, organised by where the seed (the locus of new growth) resides. The classification is botanical-ethical: the monk who knows where life is concentrated knows where his harm could fall heaviest, and acts accordingly.

The phrase **"puḍhosattā"** — "each separately existing" — is also doctrinally precise. The vegetable kingdom contains both *pratyek* (each soul in its own body, like a banana plant) and *sadharan* (countless souls sharing a common body, like the potato or onion). For both kinds, the souls are individual; but in the *sadharan vanaspati*, an enormous number of souls share a single physical body. This is why traditional Jain ethics is especially strict about avoiding root vegetables — to harm one is to harm uncountable lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There are six categories of living beings in this universe. Five of them are immobile and one-sensed (they have only touch, no sight, hearing, smell, or taste, and they cannot move): earth, water, fire, air, and plants. Each handful of earth, each drop of water, each spark of fire, each puff of wind, and each piece of vegetation contains countless individual souls — each one a separate "I." They are alive until something destroys them and turns them lifeless. The sixth category — the mobile beings — will be described in the next sutras. (Note: this sutra mentions all six in its opening list but then only describes the five sthavar in detail; the sixth, *trasa*, gets its own full sutra.)

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a single drop of water on your fingertip contains countless living beings, what changes about how you live in the world?

## SUTRA 4.5 — The Sixth: Trasakaya (Mobile Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से जे पुणे इमे अणेगे बहवे तसा पाणा, तंजहा– अंडया पोयया जराउया रसया संसेइमा संमुच्छिमा उब्भिया उववाइया जेसिं केसिं च पाणाणं अभिक्कंतं, पडिक्कंतं, संकुचियं, पसारियं, रुयं, भंतं, तसियं, पलाइयं, आगइगइविण्णाया, जे य कीडपयंगा जा य कुंथुपिपीलिया सव्वे बेइंदिया सव्वे तेइंदिया सव्वे चउरिंदिया सव्वे पंचिंदिया सव्वे तिरिक्खजोणिया सव्वे णेरइया सव्वे मणुया सव्वे देवा, सव्वे पाणा परमाहम्मिया । एसो खलु छट्ठो जीवणिकाओ तसकाउ त्ति पवुच्चइ ॥४.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.5 ||
se je puṇe ime aṇege bahave tasā pāṇā, taṃjahā– aṃḍayā poyayā jarāuyā rasayā saṃseimā saṃmucchimā ubbhiyā uvavāiyā jesiṃ kesiṃ ca pāṇāṇaṃ abhikkaṃtaṃ, paḍikkaṃtaṃ, saṃkuciyaṃ, pasāriyaṃ, ruyaṃ, bhaṃtaṃ, tasiyaṃ, palāiyaṃ, āgaigaiviṇṇāyā, je ya kīḍapayaṃgā jā ya kuṃthupipīliyā savve beiṃdiyā savve teiṃdiyā savve cauriṃdiyā savve paṃciṃdiyā savve tirikkhajoṇiyā savve ṇeraiyā savve maṇuyā savve devā, savve pāṇā paramāhammiyā | eso khalu chaṭṭho jīvaṇikāo tasakāu tti pavuccai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now there are these many mobile beings (*trasa*), namely: (1) those born from eggs (*aṃḍaja* — birds, snakes), (2) those born without an outer covering (*potaja* — elephants, certain mammals born live), (3) those born wrapped in a placental membrane (*jarāyuja* — humans, cows, buffalo), (4) those born from spoiled liquid (*rasaja* — beings that arise in spoiled milk, ghee), (5) those born from sweat (*saṃsedaja* — lice, ticks), (6) those born from spontaneous coalescence (*saṃmurchima* — flies, mosquitoes), (7) those that emerge by breaking through the earth (*udbhija* — locusts, grasshoppers), (8) those born by sudden manifestation (*aupapātika* — gods and hell-beings, who manifest in their full form within a single muhurta).

These are beings that move forward, move backward, contract, stretch out, make sounds, become agitated, take fright, flee, and that have knowledge of coming and going. These include worms, butterflies, ants, lice; all two-sensed beings, all three-sensed beings, all four-sensed beings, all five-sensed beings; all animal-yoni beings, all hell-beings, all humans, all gods. All these beings are *paramā-dhārmika* (deeply suffering / devoutly desiring of happiness).

This, then, is the sixth class of life — declared to be *trasakaya* (the mobile-bodied beings).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तसा पाणा** (tasā pāṇā) = mobile beings — those that move of their own accord
- **अंडया** (aṃḍayā) = egg-born (peacocks, pigeons, snakes, fish)
- **पोयया** (poyayā) = without-covering-born (elephants, mammals born live without placental wrap)
- **जराउया** (jarāuyā) = placenta-born (humans, cows, buffalo)
- **रसया** (rasayā) = liquid-born (beings born in spoiled milk, ghee, fermented liquids)
- **संसेइमा** (saṃseimā) = sweat-born (lice, ticks, bedbugs)
- **संमुच्छिमा** (saṃmucchimā) = spontaneously coalescent (flies, mosquitoes, certain insects arising from environmental conjunction)
- **उब्भिया** (ubbhiyā) = earth-emerging (locusts, beetles, certain insects that break out of soil)
- **उववाइया** (uvavāiyā) = spontaneously manifest (gods born on celestial beds, hell-beings born in hell-pots — they appear in full youthful form within one muhurta)
- **अभिक्कंतं** (abhikkaṃtaṃ) = moves forward / approaches
- **पडिक्कंतं** (paḍikkaṃtaṃ) = moves backward / retreats
- **संकुचियं** (saṃkuciyaṃ) = contracts the body
- **पसारियं** (pasāriyaṃ) = stretches the body
- **रुयं** (ruyaṃ) = makes sounds, cries out
- **भंतं** (bhaṃtaṃ) = becomes agitated, confused
- **तसियं** (tasiyaṃ) = becomes frightened
- **पलाइयं** (palāiyaṃ) = flees
- **आगइगइविण्णाया** (āgaigaiviṇṇāyā) = has knowledge of coming and going (intelligent enough to find its way home)
- **बेइंदिया** (beiṃdiyā) = two-sensed (touch + taste — worms, snails)
- **तेइंदिया** (teiṃdiyā) = three-sensed (+ smell — ants, bedbugs, lice)
- **चउरिंदिया** (cauriṃdiyā) = four-sensed (+ sight — flies, butterflies, scorpions)
- **पंचिंदिया** (paṃciṃdiyā) = five-sensed (+ hearing — animals, humans, gods, hell-beings)
- **तिरिक्खजोणिया** (tirikkhajoṇiyā) = animal-yoni beings (sub-human five-sensed beings)
- **णेरइया** (ṇeraiyā) = hell-beings
- **मणुया** (maṇuyā) = humans
- **देवा** (devā) = gods
- **परमाहम्मिया** (paramāhammiyā) = deeply suffering / supremely desirous of happiness (commentators give two readings: *param + duḥkhī* = supremely afflicted, OR *param + dhārmika* = deeply righteous-natured / desiring of joy)
- **जीवणिकाओ** (jīvaṇikāo) = class / group of living beings
- **तसकाउ** (tasakāu) = mobile-bodied (trasakaya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the sixth and final category — the *trasakaya*, the mobile beings. While the five sthavar were grouped in a single previous sutra, the trasakaya gets its own dedicated sutra because of its extraordinary internal diversity: it includes everything from a worm to a god. The defining mark is the same throughout — the ability to move *of one's own accord*, to approach what one wants and avoid what one fears.

The sutra lists the eight modes of birth (*yoni*) for trasa beings — a remarkable taxonomy. Some of these (egg-born, placenta-born) are immediately recognisable; others (sweat-born, spontaneously-coalescent) reflect the older theory of spontaneous generation, which Jain darshan held even for beings that modern biology now traces to other sources. The two extraordinary categories are *udbhija* (those that break out of the earth — beetles, locusts) and especially *aupapātika* (gods and hell-beings, who do not pass through any womb but manifest in full form on a celestial bed or in a hell-pot within a single muhurta).

Then comes the sutra's emotional heart — the nine activities that distinguish the trasa being from the immobile sthavar: *moving, retreating, contracting, stretching, crying out, becoming agitated, taking fright, fleeing, knowing the way back home.* These are not abstract markers but visible signs of inner experience. A worm contracts when touched; a dog flees when struck; a child cries when frightened; a lost cow finds its way back to its master. Each activity is the outward sign of an inward soul. To witness these activities and still treat the being as object — to swat the fly, kick the dog, harm the cow — is the most direct possible violation of dharma.

The final phrase **"savve pāṇā paramāhammiyā"** is theologically rich and the commentaries give it two readings. The Churni reads it as *paramaduhkhita* — "all beings are supremely suffering." The Tika reads it as *paramadharmika* — "all beings are supremely desirous of dharma / of happiness." The two readings are not in conflict: every being suffers, and precisely because every being suffers, every being yearns for happiness. This single phrase is the foundation of Jain compassion. The monk does not protect beings out of duty; he protects them out of recognition that they, like him, are simply beings trying to escape pain.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The sixth class of life is the mobile beings — everything that can move on its own. They are born in eight different ways: from eggs, from wombs (with or without placenta), from spoiled liquids, from sweat, from spontaneous coalescence, by emerging from the earth, or by sudden manifestation (gods and hell-beings). They move toward what they want and away from what they fear; they cry, they freeze, they flee, they find their way home. They include every kind of insect, animal, human, hell-being, and god. And every single one of them — without exception — is suffering, and longing for happiness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you watch a fly try to escape your room, or a worm contract from a touch, what is the difference between you and them — really?

## SUTRA 4.6 — The Comprehensive Vow of Six-Kaya Restraint

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्चेसिं छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं णेव सयं दंडं समारंभिज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं दंडं समारंभाविज्जा, दंडं समारंभंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणिज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि, ण कारवेमि, करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणु- जाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥४.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.6 ||
iccesiṃ chaṇhaṃ jīvaṇikāyāṇaṃ ṇeva sayaṃ daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhijjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhāvijjā, daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇijjā, jāvajjīvāe tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ, maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi, ṇa kāravemi, karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Toward these six classes of living beings — I will not myself initiate harm; I will not have others initiate harm; nor will I approve of others who initiate harm. For as long as I live, by all three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and all three yogas (mind, speech, body) — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, I will not cause to be done, nor will I approve of another who does it. From that (past wrongdoing), O Lord, I turn back; I censure it; I confess it before my Guru; I let go of (that) sinful self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इच्चेसिं छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं** (iccesiṃ chaṇhaṃ jīvaṇikāyāṇaṃ) = toward these six classes of living beings
- **णेव सयं** (ṇeva sayaṃ) = neither myself
- **दंडं** (daṃḍaṃ) = harm-causing activity / "punishment" (here = act of violence against beings)
- **समारंभिज्जा** (samāraṃbhijjā) = will undertake / initiate
- **णेवण्णेहिं** (ṇevaṇṇehiṃ) = nor through others
- **समारंभाविज्जा** (samāraṃbhāvijjā) = will cause to be undertaken
- **दंडं समारंभंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणिज्जा** (daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇijjā) = nor will I give approval to another who is undertaking harm
- **जावज्जीवाए** (jāvajjīvāe) = for as long as I live (lifelong)
- **तिविहं तिविहेणं** (tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ) = three by three (three karanas × three yogas = nine modes)
- **मणेणं वायाए काएणं** (maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ) = by mind, by speech, by body
- **ण करेमि** (ṇa karemi) = I will not do
- **ण कारवेमि** (ṇa kāravemi) = I will not cause to be done
- **करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि** (karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi) = nor will I approve another who does
- **तस्स भंते** (tassa bhaṃte) = of that (past wrongdoing), O Lord
- **पडिक्कमामि** (paḍikkamāmi) = I turn back (from the wrongdoing)
- **णिंदामि** (ṇiṃdāmi) = I censure (privately, before my own conscience)
- **गरिहामि** (garihāmi) = I confess (publicly, before the Guru)
- **अप्पाणं वोसिरामि** (appāṇaṃ vosirāmi) = I let go of that (sinful) self

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the comprehensive vow — the centrepiece of the chapter and the foundational pratigya of all Jain monastic life. Here, having heard the description of all six classes of beings, the practitioner makes the formal pledge: he will not harm any of them, in any way, by any means, for the rest of his life. The structure is the *navakoti* — the nine-fold restraint — built from three karanas (doing, causing, approving) multiplied by three yogas (mind, speech, body). To plug all nine doors at once is to seal the soul against the entry of *himsa-janya karma*.

Note the parallel with the Karemi Bhante of the Avashyak Sutra: the vow-formula is structurally identical — same nine-koti formula, same four-fold pratikraman ending (*paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi*). The difference is the *content*: where the Avashyak vow renounces *savajja jog* (sinful activity in general), the Dashvaikalik vow renounces specifically the *daṃḍa* (harm-causing activity) directed at the *six classes of beings*. This is the moment when the abstract idea of ahimsa becomes a concrete, measurable, enforceable commitment.

The four-step pratikraman closing — *paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi* — is the same psychological progression we saw in the Avashyak chapter. *Paḍikkamāmi*: I turn back from the past wrongdoing. *Ṇiṃdāmi*: I see it as wrong before my own inner witness. *Garihāmi*: I confess it openly to my Guru, breaking the protective shell of pride. *Appāṇaṃ vosirāmi*: I release identification with the part of me that committed it. Without this fourth step, the commentators say, no purification is final — to regret a wrong while still considering it part of one's identity is to keep the karma bound. The vow is therefore not merely about future restraint but about a complete release of past harm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner formally vows before the Guru: "Toward all six categories of living beings — earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile beings — I will not commit harm myself, will not ask others to commit harm, and will not approve when others commit harm. For my whole life. By mind, by speech, by body. And whatever harm I have already done, I turn from it, I see it as wrong, I confess it openly, and I release the part of me that did it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A vow that closes nine doors at once — could you take such a vow toward even one being? Toward your nearest enemy? Toward yourself?
