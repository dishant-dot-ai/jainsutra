# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 4
# Chh Jeevanikaya (છ જીવનિકાય) — The Six Classes of Life

**Adhyayan 4 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 64–115 (PDF pages 124–187)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 19 prose sutras + 29 gathas = 48 sutras*

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fourth Adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik — *Chh Jeevanikaya*, "The Six Classes of Life" — is the longest, the most systematic, and the most foundational chapter of the entire sutra. It is the chapter in which Jain monastic life crystallises from intuition into formal vow. A novice (*nava-dikshit*) at his Vadi Diksha (greater initiation) is given exactly this chapter to internalise; from it he learns the six categories of beings he must protect, the five great vows by which he protects them, the renunciation of night-eating that completes the protection, and the careful restraint (*yatana*) by which the protection is maintained moment by moment.

The chapter is structured in three clear divisions, all bound together by the single unifying purpose of *ahimsa*:

1. **Sutras 1–6 (Jeevanikaya division):** Sudharma Swami opens the chapter by reciting to his disciple Jambu Swami what he himself heard from Bhagavan Mahavira — the full ennumeration of the six classes of living beings. Five are *sthavar* (immobile, one-sensed): earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, and plant-bodied beings. The sixth is *trasa* (mobile, two- to five-sensed): worms, insects, animals, humans, hell-beings, and gods. Sutra 6 is the comprehensive vow — the practitioner pledges, before the Guru, to abstain from harming any of the six categories by all three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and three yogas (mind, speech, body).

2. **Sutras 7–13 (Charitra-dharma division):** The chapter then formalises the Five Mahavrats (great vows) of Jain monasticism — non-violence (*pranatipata viraman*), truthfulness (*mrishavada viraman*), non-stealing (*adattadana viraman*), celibacy (*maithuna viraman*), and non-possession (*parigraha viraman*) — and adds the sixth practice of *ratri-bhojan viraman* (renunciation of night-eating). Sutra 13 is the concluding pratigya in which the monk takes upon himself the full discipline.

3. **Sutras 14–19 (Yatana division):** The chapter then turns to *yatana* — the careful, mindful restraint by which the monk avoids harming each of the six kayas. Six parallel sutras describe how the monk must walk, sit, sleep, eat, drink, accept alms, and use his belongings so that no being of any of the six classes is harmed.

4. **Gathas 1–29 (Verse division):** The chapter closes with twenty-nine verses in metre. The earlier gathas (1–12) repeat the *yatana-ayatana* teaching in poetic form and stress the central importance of *jnana* (knowledge) — for ignorance, however well-intentioned, cannot protect beings. The middle gathas (13–25) trace the spiritual development arc: from knowing the six categories, to renouncing the world, to taking the vows, to attaining samvara, then nirjara, then kevalajnana, and finally moksha. The closing gathas (26–29) seal the teaching with the ending verse and benediction.

The chapter is called *dharma-prajnapti* — "the proclamation of dharma" — because in it the very content of Jain monastic dharma is announced. To know this chapter, the tradition holds, is to know the path; to live this chapter is to walk it.

The narration begins with Sudharma Swami addressing his disciple Jambu Swami, with the auspicious phrase **"Suyaṃ me āusaṃ! teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ"** — "I have heard, O long-lived one! Thus has it been declared by the Bhagavan." This formula, used at the opening of every Anga Agama, establishes the authority of unbroken oral transmission: what Sudharma teaches, he himself heard from Mahavira's own lips.

## SUTRA 4.1 — The Opening Declaration (Utthanika)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुयं मे आउसं ! तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं, इह खलु छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता, सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती ॥४.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.1 ||
suyaṃ me āusaṃ! teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ, iha khalu chajjīvaṇiyā nāmajjhayaṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ kāsaveṇaṃ paveiyā suakkhāyā supaṇṇattā, seyaṃ me ahijjiuṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ dhammapaṇṇattī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I have heard, O long-lived one! Thus was it declared by that Bhagavan: in this very Jain teaching, an Adhyayan named *Chh Jeevanikaya* (Six Classes of Life) has been proclaimed, well-spoken, and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, of the Kashyapa lineage. Beneficial it would be for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुयं मे** (suyaṃ me) = heard by me / I have heard
- **आउसं** (āusaṃ) = O long-lived one (a respectful address from Sudharma to his disciple Jambu)
- **तेणं भगवया** (teṇaṃ bhagavayā) = by that Bhagavan (Mahavira)
- **एवमक्खायं** (evamakkhāyaṃ) = thus declared
- **इह** (iha) = here, in this Jain shasan
- **खलु** (khalu) = indeed, certainly
- **छज्जीवणिया** (chajjīvaṇiyā) = the Six Jeevanikaya (six classes of living beings)
- **नामज्झयणं** (nāmajjhayaṇaṃ) = the Adhyayan named (so-called)
- **समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं** (samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ) = by Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira
- **कासवेणं** (kāsaveṇaṃ) = of the Kashyapa gotra (Mahavira's clan)
- **पवेइया** (paveiyā) = proclaimed
- **सुअक्खाया** (suakkhāyā) = well-spoken, fully narrated
- **सुपण्णत्ता** (supaṇṇattā) = well-expounded with example and reasoning
- **सेयं मे** (seyaṃ me) = beneficial / auspicious for me
- **अहिज्जिउं** (ahijjiuṃ) = to study, to learn, to recite
- **अज्झयणं** (ajjhayaṇaṃ) = the Adhyayan
- **धम्मपण्णत्ती** (dhammapaṇṇattī) = the proclamation of dharma (the alternate name of this chapter)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the opening sutra (*utthanika*) of the chapter — the formal declaration by Sudharma Swami to his disciple Jambu Swami that what follows has been heard directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. The phrasing **"suyaṃ me āusaṃ"** — "I have heard, O long-lived one" — is the standard opening of every Anga Agama. It establishes two things at once. First, it asserts the unbroken chain of oral transmission: Sudharma is not authoring; he is reporting. Second, it expresses Sudharma's own humility — he does not claim direct revelation, only faithful hearing.

The disciple is addressed as *āusaṃ* — "long-lived one" — a blessing as much as a name. Of all the qualities a disciple may have (good conduct, sharp intellect, faith, devotion), longevity is singled out because only the long-lived can carry the teaching forward to the next generation. By calling Jambu *āusaṃ*, Sudharma is implicitly entrusting him with the future of the shasan.

The chapter's two names appear in this very sutra. **Chh Jeevanikaya** ("Six Classes of Life") names the *content* — the six categories of beings that the chapter enumerates and protects. **Dhammapannatti** ("Proclamation of Dharma") names the *function* — the chapter is the formal announcement of what Jain monastic dharma actually is. To know this chapter is to know dharma; to follow it is to live dharma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Sudharma tells his disciple Jambu: "Listen — I heard this directly from Bhagavan Mahavira. He taught a chapter called the Six Classes of Life, also known as the Proclamation of Dharma. It will be good for you and for me to study it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Before any teaching can be received, the receiver must trust the lineage that carries it. Whose voice do you trust enough to let it shape how you live?

## SUTRA 4.2 — The Disciple's Question

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कयरा खलु सा छज्जीवणिया नामज्झयणं समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं कासवेणं पवेइया सुअक्खाया सुपण्णत्ता सेयं मे अहिज्जिउं अज्झयणं धम्मपण्णत्ती ? ॥४.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.2 ||
kayarā khalu sā chajjīvaṇiyā nāmajjhayaṇaṃ samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ kāsaveṇaṃ paveiyā suakkhāyā supaṇṇattā seyaṃ me ahijjiuṃ ajjhayaṇaṃ dhammapaṇṇattī ?

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

What, then, is that Adhyayan called *Chh Jeevanikaya* (Six Classes of Life), proclaimed by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage, well-spoken and well-expounded — the Adhyayan named *Dhammapannatti* (Proclamation of Dharma) which it would be beneficial for me to study?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कयरा** (kayarā) = which, what kind of, what is it
- **खलु** (khalu) = indeed
- **सा** (sā) = that
- (remaining words as in 4.1)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the disciple's response — the request to be told *what exactly* the Six Jeevanikaya Adhyayan contains. Jambu does not simply say "tell me"; he repeats the entire formula, including the lineage of transmission, the name of the speaker, and the name of the chapter. This is not redundancy but reverence: the disciple's question reproduces the teacher's framing word for word, signalling that he has received the introduction with full attention and is ready for the body of the teaching.

The Jain Agama style of question-and-answer mirrors a real classroom: the teacher introduces, the student requests, and the teacher then expounds. This sutra is the formal pivot between introduction and exposition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Jambu replies to Sudharma: "Tell me — what exactly is this Six Classes of Life chapter that Bhagavan Mahavira taught and that you say would be good for me to study?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A good question is itself a kind of preparation. When you ask, do you ask with the same attention with which you intend to listen?

This — what is now to be told — is that very Adhyayan called *Chh Jeevanikaya*, proclaimed and well-expounded by the Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira of the Kashyapa lineage. It would be beneficial for me to study this Adhyayan, in which dharma itself is proclaimed.

- (remaining words as in 4.1–4.2)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is Sudharma's transition sutra — his confirmation that what follows is the chapter Jambu has just asked about. The repeated full formula is again not redundancy but a teaching method: in oral transmission, the boundaries of a section must be clearly marked so that the listener knows when introduction ends and exposition begins. With this sutra, the introduction closes; the body of the chapter opens with sutra 4 (the enumeration of the six kayas).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The doorway is itself part of the house. What does it mean to enter a teaching with the same care that the teacher has used to invite you in?

## SUTRA 4.4 — The Five Sthavar (One-Sensed Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं जहा– पुढविकाइया, आउकाइया, तेउकाइया, वाउकाइया, वणस्सइकाइया, तसकाइया ।

पुढवी चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । आऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । तेऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वाऊ चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं । वणस्सई चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा, पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं ।

तंजहा– अग्गबीया, मूलबीया, पोरबीया, खंधबीया, बीयरुहा, संमुच्छिमा तणलया, वणस्सइकाइया, सबीया चित्तमंतमक्खाया अणेगजीवा पुढोसत्ता अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं ॥४.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.4 ||
taṃ jahā– puḍhavikāiyā, āukāiyā, teukāiyā, vāukāiyā, vaṇassaikāiyā, tasakāiyā |

puḍhavī cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | āū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | teū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | vāū cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ | vaṇassaī cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā, puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ |

taṃjahā– aggabīyā, mūlabīyā, porabīyā, khaṃdhabīyā, bīyaruhā, saṃmucchimā taṇalayā, vaṇassaikāiyā, sabīyā cittamaṃtamakkhāyā aṇegajīvā puḍhosattā aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The six are these: (1) earth-bodied beings (*puḍhavikāiyā*), (2) water-bodied beings (*āukāiyā*), (3) fire-bodied beings (*teukāiyā*), (4) air-bodied beings (*vāukāiyā*), (5) plant-bodied beings (*vaṇassaikāiyā*), (6) mobile beings (*tasakāiyā*).

Earth has been declared as having consciousness (*cittamanta*). It is many-souled, each soul having its own separate existence — except where it has been transformed by *shastra* (a destroying force). Water has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Fire has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Air has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra. Plant-life has been declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra.

These are the kinds of plants: (1) those whose seed is at the tip (*aggabīyā* — like coriander), (2) those whose seed is in the root (*mūlabīyā* — like the lotus rhizome), (3) those whose seed is in the joint or node (*porabīyā* — like sugarcane, bamboo), (4) those whose seed is in the trunk (*khaṃdhabīyā* — like the banyan, fig), (5) those that grow from a seed (*bīyaruhā*), (6) those that arise spontaneously (*saṃmucchimā*) — the various grasses and creepers (*taṇalayā*), the plant-bodied beings, the seed-bearing — all are declared as having consciousness, many-souled, each separately existing — except where transformed by shastra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तं जहा** (taṃ jahā) = they are as follows
- **पुढविकाइया** (puḍhavikāiyā) = earth-bodied beings — beings whose body is earth (clay, stone, salt, ore, gold, silver, mica, diamond, gems)
- **आउकाइया** (āukāiyā) = water-bodied beings — beings whose body is water (rain, river, well, pond, fog, dew)
- **तेउकाइया** (teukāiyā) = fire-bodied beings — beings whose body is fire (hearth, furnace, ember, lightning, meteor, coal, flame)
- **वाउकाइया** (vāukāiyā) = air-bodied beings — beings whose body is air (high wind, whirlwind, dense wind, thin wind, gusty wind, pure wind)
- **वणस्सइकाइया** (vaṇassaikāiyā) = plant-bodied beings — beings whose body is vegetation
- **तसकाइया** (tasakāiyā) = mobile beings — those that can move of their own accord
- **चित्तमंतमक्खाया** (cittamaṃtamakkhāyā) = declared to be conscious / sentient (literally "endowed with chitta")
- **अणेगजीवा** (aṇegajīvā) = many-souled (each kaya holds countless individual souls)
- **पुढोसत्ता** (puḍhosattā) = each separately existing (each soul has its own independent existence)
- **अण्णत्थ सत्थपरिणएणं** (aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ) = except where transformed by shastra (a destroying agent — a force that makes the kaya *achit* / non-living)
- **अग्गबीया** (aggabīyā) = seed-at-tip plants (coriander)
- **मूलबीया** (mūlabīyā) = seed-at-root plants (lotus rhizome)
- **पोरबीया** (porabīyā) = seed-at-node plants (sugarcane, bamboo, reed)
- **खंधबीया** (khaṃdhabīyā) = seed-in-trunk plants (banyan, peepal, thor cactus)
- **बीयरुहा** (bīyaruhā) = seed-grown plants (those that grow from sown seed — wheat, barley)
- **संमुच्छिमा** (saṃmucchimā) = spontaneously-arising plants (mushrooms, certain grasses)
- **तणलया** (taṇalayā) = grasses and creepers (chameli, jasmine, jasmine vine)
- **सबीया** (sabīyā) = seed-bearing / together with seeds

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the doctrinal foundation of the entire chapter — and arguably of all Jain ethics. It announces, on the authority of the omniscient (*sarvajna*) Bhagavan Mahavira, that earth, water, fire, air, and plants are not inert matter but living, conscious beings. Each is *cittamanta* — endowed with consciousness. Each handful of soil, each drop of water, each spark, each gust, each blade of grass holds *aneka jiva* — countless individual souls — and each soul has its own *prithak satta*, its own separate existence. They are not a collective; each is an "I."

Modern science has only recently been able to confirm what Jain darshan asserted twenty-five centuries ago: a single drop of water holds thousands of microorganisms; a particle of soil teems with life. But Jain darshan goes further still — it holds that the very water itself, the very soil itself, the very fire itself is alive at the level of the substance. Each *one-sensed* being possesses only the sense of touch (*sparshendriya*); it cannot see, hear, smell, or taste, and it cannot move of its own accord. Its consciousness is dimmest. Yet it is consciousness, and therefore the soul within it is identical in essence to the soul in a human being — only more deeply veiled by karma.

The phrase **"aṇṇattha satthapariṇaeṇaṃ"** — "except where transformed by shastra" — is crucial. Shastra here means a "destroying agent" — anything that has acted on the kaya to render it non-living (*achit*). Cooked water is no longer a living water-body; baked clay is no longer a living earth-body; extinguished fire is no longer a living fire-body. The monk may use these *achit* substances; what he must absolutely avoid is harming or using *sachit* (still-living) substances. This single phrase is the entire foundation of Jain monastic alms-rules: monks accept only *prasuk* (cooked, processed, lifeless) food; they drink only boiled water; they walk on already-trodden paths; they sit only where the surface has been cleaned of life.

The plant section adds the eight categories of vegetation, organised by where the seed (the locus of new growth) resides. The classification is botanical-ethical: the monk who knows where life is concentrated knows where his harm could fall heaviest, and acts accordingly.

The phrase **"puḍhosattā"** — "each separately existing" — is also doctrinally precise. The vegetable kingdom contains both *pratyek* (each soul in its own body, like a banana plant) and *sadharan* (countless souls sharing a common body, like the potato or onion). For both kinds, the souls are individual; but in the *sadharan vanaspati*, an enormous number of souls share a single physical body. This is why traditional Jain ethics is especially strict about avoiding root vegetables — to harm one is to harm uncountable lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There are six categories of living beings in this universe. Five of them are immobile and one-sensed (they have only touch, no sight, hearing, smell, or taste, and they cannot move): earth, water, fire, air, and plants. Each handful of earth, each drop of water, each spark of fire, each puff of wind, and each piece of vegetation contains countless individual souls — each one a separate "I." They are alive until something destroys them and turns them lifeless. The sixth category — the mobile beings — will be described in the next sutras. (Note: this sutra mentions all six in its opening list but then only describes the five sthavar in detail; the sixth, *trasa*, gets its own full sutra.)

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a single drop of water on your fingertip contains countless living beings, what changes about how you live in the world?

## SUTRA 4.5 — The Sixth: Trasakaya (Mobile Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से जे पुणे इमे अणेगे बहवे तसा पाणा, तंजहा– अंडया पोयया जराउया रसया संसेइमा संमुच्छिमा उब्भिया उववाइया जेसिं केसिं च पाणाणं अभिक्कंतं, पडिक्कंतं, संकुचियं, पसारियं, रुयं, भंतं, तसियं, पलाइयं, आगइगइविण्णाया, जे य कीडपयंगा जा य कुंथुपिपीलिया सव्वे बेइंदिया सव्वे तेइंदिया सव्वे चउरिंदिया सव्वे पंचिंदिया सव्वे तिरिक्खजोणिया सव्वे णेरइया सव्वे मणुया सव्वे देवा, सव्वे पाणा परमाहम्मिया । एसो खलु छट्ठो जीवणिकाओ तसकाउ त्ति पवुच्चइ ॥४.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.5 ||
se je puṇe ime aṇege bahave tasā pāṇā, taṃjahā– aṃḍayā poyayā jarāuyā rasayā saṃseimā saṃmucchimā ubbhiyā uvavāiyā jesiṃ kesiṃ ca pāṇāṇaṃ abhikkaṃtaṃ, paḍikkaṃtaṃ, saṃkuciyaṃ, pasāriyaṃ, ruyaṃ, bhaṃtaṃ, tasiyaṃ, palāiyaṃ, āgaigaiviṇṇāyā, je ya kīḍapayaṃgā jā ya kuṃthupipīliyā savve beiṃdiyā savve teiṃdiyā savve cauriṃdiyā savve paṃciṃdiyā savve tirikkhajoṇiyā savve ṇeraiyā savve maṇuyā savve devā, savve pāṇā paramāhammiyā | eso khalu chaṭṭho jīvaṇikāo tasakāu tti pavuccai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now there are these many mobile beings (*trasa*), namely: (1) those born from eggs (*aṃḍaja* — birds, snakes), (2) those born without an outer covering (*potaja* — elephants, certain mammals born live), (3) those born wrapped in a placental membrane (*jarāyuja* — humans, cows, buffalo), (4) those born from spoiled liquid (*rasaja* — beings that arise in spoiled milk, ghee), (5) those born from sweat (*saṃsedaja* — lice, ticks), (6) those born from spontaneous coalescence (*saṃmurchima* — flies, mosquitoes), (7) those that emerge by breaking through the earth (*udbhija* — locusts, grasshoppers), (8) those born by sudden manifestation (*aupapātika* — gods and hell-beings, who manifest in their full form within a single muhurta).

These are beings that move forward, move backward, contract, stretch out, make sounds, become agitated, take fright, flee, and that have knowledge of coming and going. These include worms, butterflies, ants, lice; all two-sensed beings, all three-sensed beings, all four-sensed beings, all five-sensed beings; all animal-yoni beings, all hell-beings, all humans, all gods. All these beings are *paramā-dhārmika* (deeply suffering / devoutly desiring of happiness).

This, then, is the sixth class of life — declared to be *trasakaya* (the mobile-bodied beings).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तसा पाणा** (tasā pāṇā) = mobile beings — those that move of their own accord
- **अंडया** (aṃḍayā) = egg-born (peacocks, pigeons, snakes, fish)
- **पोयया** (poyayā) = without-covering-born (elephants, mammals born live without placental wrap)
- **जराउया** (jarāuyā) = placenta-born (humans, cows, buffalo)
- **रसया** (rasayā) = liquid-born (beings born in spoiled milk, ghee, fermented liquids)
- **संसेइमा** (saṃseimā) = sweat-born (lice, ticks, bedbugs)
- **संमुच्छिमा** (saṃmucchimā) = spontaneously coalescent (flies, mosquitoes, certain insects arising from environmental conjunction)
- **उब्भिया** (ubbhiyā) = earth-emerging (locusts, beetles, certain insects that break out of soil)
- **उववाइया** (uvavāiyā) = spontaneously manifest (gods born on celestial beds, hell-beings born in hell-pots — they appear in full youthful form within one muhurta)
- **अभिक्कंतं** (abhikkaṃtaṃ) = moves forward / approaches
- **पडिक्कंतं** (paḍikkaṃtaṃ) = moves backward / retreats
- **संकुचियं** (saṃkuciyaṃ) = contracts the body
- **पसारियं** (pasāriyaṃ) = stretches the body
- **रुयं** (ruyaṃ) = makes sounds, cries out
- **भंतं** (bhaṃtaṃ) = becomes agitated, confused
- **तसियं** (tasiyaṃ) = becomes frightened
- **पलाइयं** (palāiyaṃ) = flees
- **आगइगइविण्णाया** (āgaigaiviṇṇāyā) = has knowledge of coming and going (intelligent enough to find its way home)
- **बेइंदिया** (beiṃdiyā) = two-sensed (touch + taste — worms, snails)
- **तेइंदिया** (teiṃdiyā) = three-sensed (+ smell — ants, bedbugs, lice)
- **चउरिंदिया** (cauriṃdiyā) = four-sensed (+ sight — flies, butterflies, scorpions)
- **पंचिंदिया** (paṃciṃdiyā) = five-sensed (+ hearing — animals, humans, gods, hell-beings)
- **तिरिक्खजोणिया** (tirikkhajoṇiyā) = animal-yoni beings (sub-human five-sensed beings)
- **णेरइया** (ṇeraiyā) = hell-beings
- **मणुया** (maṇuyā) = humans
- **देवा** (devā) = gods
- **परमाहम्मिया** (paramāhammiyā) = deeply suffering / supremely desirous of happiness (commentators give two readings: *param + duḥkhī* = supremely afflicted, OR *param + dhārmika* = deeply righteous-natured / desiring of joy)
- **जीवणिकाओ** (jīvaṇikāo) = class / group of living beings
- **तसकाउ** (tasakāu) = mobile-bodied (trasakaya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the sixth and final category — the *trasakaya*, the mobile beings. While the five sthavar were grouped in a single previous sutra, the trasakaya gets its own dedicated sutra because of its extraordinary internal diversity: it includes everything from a worm to a god. The defining mark is the same throughout — the ability to move *of one's own accord*, to approach what one wants and avoid what one fears.

The sutra lists the eight modes of birth (*yoni*) for trasa beings — a remarkable taxonomy. Some of these (egg-born, placenta-born) are immediately recognisable; others (sweat-born, spontaneously-coalescent) reflect the older theory of spontaneous generation, which Jain darshan held even for beings that modern biology now traces to other sources. The two extraordinary categories are *udbhija* (those that break out of the earth — beetles, locusts) and especially *aupapātika* (gods and hell-beings, who do not pass through any womb but manifest in full form on a celestial bed or in a hell-pot within a single muhurta).

Then comes the sutra's emotional heart — the nine activities that distinguish the trasa being from the immobile sthavar: *moving, retreating, contracting, stretching, crying out, becoming agitated, taking fright, fleeing, knowing the way back home.* These are not abstract markers but visible signs of inner experience. A worm contracts when touched; a dog flees when struck; a child cries when frightened; a lost cow finds its way back to its master. Each activity is the outward sign of an inward soul. To witness these activities and still treat the being as object — to swat the fly, kick the dog, harm the cow — is the most direct possible violation of dharma.

The final phrase **"savve pāṇā paramāhammiyā"** is theologically rich and the commentaries give it two readings. The Churni reads it as *paramaduhkhita* — "all beings are supremely suffering." The Tika reads it as *paramadharmika* — "all beings are supremely desirous of dharma / of happiness." The two readings are not in conflict: every being suffers, and precisely because every being suffers, every being yearns for happiness. This single phrase is the foundation of Jain compassion. The monk does not protect beings out of duty; he protects them out of recognition that they, like him, are simply beings trying to escape pain.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The sixth class of life is the mobile beings — everything that can move on its own. They are born in eight different ways: from eggs, from wombs (with or without placenta), from spoiled liquids, from sweat, from spontaneous coalescence, by emerging from the earth, or by sudden manifestation (gods and hell-beings). They move toward what they want and away from what they fear; they cry, they freeze, they flee, they find their way home. They include every kind of insect, animal, human, hell-being, and god. And every single one of them — without exception — is suffering, and longing for happiness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you watch a fly try to escape your room, or a worm contract from a touch, what is the difference between you and them — really?

## SUTRA 4.6 — The Comprehensive Vow of Six-Kaya Restraint

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्चेसिं छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं णेव सयं दंडं समारंभिज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं दंडं समारंभाविज्जा, दंडं समारंभंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणिज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि, ण कारवेमि, करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणु- जाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥४.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.6 ||
iccesiṃ chaṇhaṃ jīvaṇikāyāṇaṃ ṇeva sayaṃ daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhijjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhāvijjā, daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇijjā, jāvajjīvāe tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ, maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi, ṇa kāravemi, karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Toward these six classes of living beings — I will not myself initiate harm; I will not have others initiate harm; nor will I approve of others who initiate harm. For as long as I live, by all three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and all three yogas (mind, speech, body) — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, I will not cause to be done, nor will I approve of another who does it. From that (past wrongdoing), O Lord, I turn back; I censure it; I confess it before my Guru; I let go of (that) sinful self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इच्चेसिं छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं** (iccesiṃ chaṇhaṃ jīvaṇikāyāṇaṃ) = toward these six classes of living beings
- **णेव सयं** (ṇeva sayaṃ) = neither myself
- **दंडं** (daṃḍaṃ) = harm-causing activity / "punishment" (here = act of violence against beings)
- **समारंभिज्जा** (samāraṃbhijjā) = will undertake / initiate
- **णेवण्णेहिं** (ṇevaṇṇehiṃ) = nor through others
- **समारंभाविज्जा** (samāraṃbhāvijjā) = will cause to be undertaken
- **दंडं समारंभंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणिज्जा** (daṃḍaṃ samāraṃbhaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇijjā) = nor will I give approval to another who is undertaking harm
- **जावज्जीवाए** (jāvajjīvāe) = for as long as I live (lifelong)
- **तिविहं तिविहेणं** (tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ) = three by three (three karanas × three yogas = nine modes)
- **मणेणं वायाए काएणं** (maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ) = by mind, by speech, by body
- **ण करेमि** (ṇa karemi) = I will not do
- **ण कारवेमि** (ṇa kāravemi) = I will not cause to be done
- **करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि** (karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi) = nor will I approve another who does
- **तस्स भंते** (tassa bhaṃte) = of that (past wrongdoing), O Lord
- **पडिक्कमामि** (paḍikkamāmi) = I turn back (from the wrongdoing)
- **णिंदामि** (ṇiṃdāmi) = I censure (privately, before my own conscience)
- **गरिहामि** (garihāmi) = I confess (publicly, before the Guru)
- **अप्पाणं वोसिरामि** (appāṇaṃ vosirāmi) = I let go of that (sinful) self

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the comprehensive vow — the centrepiece of the chapter and the foundational pratigya of all Jain monastic life. Here, having heard the description of all six classes of beings, the practitioner makes the formal pledge: he will not harm any of them, in any way, by any means, for the rest of his life. The structure is the *navakoti* — the nine-fold restraint — built from three karanas (doing, causing, approving) multiplied by three yogas (mind, speech, body). To plug all nine doors at once is to seal the soul against the entry of *himsa-janya karma*.

Note the parallel with the Karemi Bhante of the Avashyak Sutra: the vow-formula is structurally identical — same nine-koti formula, same four-fold pratikraman ending (*paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi*). The difference is the *content*: where the Avashyak vow renounces *savajja jog* (sinful activity in general), the Dashvaikalik vow renounces specifically the *daṃḍa* (harm-causing activity) directed at the *six classes of beings*. This is the moment when the abstract idea of ahimsa becomes a concrete, measurable, enforceable commitment.

The four-step pratikraman closing — *paḍikkamāmi, ṇiṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi* — is the same psychological progression we saw in the Avashyak chapter. *Paḍikkamāmi*: I turn back from the past wrongdoing. *Ṇiṃdāmi*: I see it as wrong before my own inner witness. *Garihāmi*: I confess it openly to my Guru, breaking the protective shell of pride. *Appāṇaṃ vosirāmi*: I release identification with the part of me that committed it. Without this fourth step, the commentators say, no purification is final — to regret a wrong while still considering it part of one's identity is to keep the karma bound. The vow is therefore not merely about future restraint but about a complete release of past harm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner formally vows before the Guru: "Toward all six categories of living beings — earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile beings — I will not commit harm myself, will not ask others to commit harm, and will not approve when others commit harm. For my whole life. By mind, by speech, by body. And whatever harm I have already done, I turn from it, I see it as wrong, I confess it openly, and I release the part of me that did it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A vow that closes nine doors at once — could you take such a vow toward even one being? Toward your nearest enemy? Toward yourself?

## SUTRA 4.7 — The First Mahavrat: Pranatipata Viraman (Renunciation of Killing)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पढमे भंते ! महव्वए पाणाइवायाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! पाणाइवायं पच्चक्खामि । से सुहुमं वा बायरं वा तसं वा थावरं वा, णेव सयं पाणे अइवाएज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं पाणे अइवायावेज्जा, पाणे अइवायंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि, तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । पढमे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ पाणाइवायाओ वेरमणं ॥४.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.7 ||
paḍhame bhaṃte ! mahavvae pāṇāivāyāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! pāṇāivāyaṃ paccakkhāmi | se suhumaṃ vā bāyaraṃ vā tasaṃ vā thāvaraṃ vā, ṇeva sayaṃ pāṇe aivāejjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ pāṇe aivāyāvejjā, pāṇe aivāyaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi ṇa kāravemi karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi, tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | paḍhame bhaṃte ! mahavvae uvaṭṭhiomi savvāo pāṇāivāyāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the first Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of *pranatipata* (killing of living beings); O Lord, I renounce all killing of beings. Whether subtle or gross, mobile or immobile — I will neither kill beings myself, nor cause others to kill beings, nor approve of others who kill beings. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, I will not cause to be done, nor will I approve of one who does. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure it; I confess it; I let go of (that) sinful self. In the first Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all killing of beings.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पढमे महव्वए** (paḍhame mahavvae) = in the first Mahavrat (great vow)
- **पाणाइवायाओ वेरमणं** (pāṇāivāyāo veramaṇaṃ) = renunciation of *pranatipata* — separating any of the ten *pranas* (life-energies) from a living being
- **सव्वं पाणाइवायं पच्चक्खामि** (savvaṃ pāṇāivāyaṃ paccakkhāmi) = I renounce all forms of killing
- **सुहुमं वा बायरं वा** (suhumaṃ vā bāyaraṃ vā) = subtle or gross
- **तसं वा थावरं वा** (tasaṃ vā thāvaraṃ vā) = mobile or immobile
- **णेव सयं पाणे अइवाएज्जा** (ṇeva sayaṃ pāṇe aivāejjā) = I will not myself kill any being
- **णेवण्णेहिं ... अइवायावेज्जा** (ṇevaṇṇehiṃ ... aivāyāvejjā) = nor cause others to kill
- **अइवायंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा** (aivāyaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā) = nor approve of others who kill
- **उवट्ठिओमि** (uvaṭṭhiomi) = I take my stand / I am present (in this vow)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the first and most important of the five Mahavrats. *Pranatipata* literally means "the destruction of *pranas*" — the ten life-forces by which a living being lives: the five sense-faculties, the three powers (mind, speech, body), the breath, and the lifespan itself. To kill is to separate one or more of these from a being. The vow is total: it covers the subtle and the gross (any size of being), the mobile and the immobile (any of the six kayas), and it closes all nine doors (three karanas × three yogas).

Why is this vow placed first? Because *himsa* — violence — is the root from which all other sins grow. Lying, stealing, sexual misconduct, and possessiveness are all, in a sense, forms of violence (against truth, against property, against another's body, against the soul's freedom). Renounce killing in its full breadth, and the rest follow.

The phrase **"pranatipata viraman"** rather than the more common "ahimsa" is itself instructive. Ahimsa is a state — non-harm. *Pranatipata viraman* is an act — the active turning-away from harm. The Mahavrats are formulated not as states to be admired but as acts to be performed, again and again, breath after breath. This is why the vow ends with **"uvaṭṭhiomi"** — "I take my stand" — present tense, active, ongoing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's first great vow is total non-killing: "Whether the being is tiny or large, moving or still, I will not kill it, will not have it killed, will not approve when it is killed — by my mind, my speech, or my body — for the rest of my life. Whatever harm I have caused before, I turn from it. I now stand in this vow."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Killing isn't only the ending of a life; it's the separation of any life-force from any being. Where, in your day, do you separate something living from what it needs to live?

## SUTRA 4.8 — The Second Mahavrat: Mrishavada Viraman (Renunciation of Falsehood)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहावरे दोच्चे भंते ! महव्वए मुसावायाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! मुसावायं पच्चक्खामि । से कोहा वा लोहा वा भया वा हासा वा णेव सयं मुसं वइज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं मुसं वायावेज्जा, मुसं वयंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । दोच्चे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ मुसावायाओ वेरमणं ॥४.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.8 ||
ahāvare docce bhaṃte ! mahavvae musāvāyāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! musāvāyaṃ paccakkhāmi | se kohā vā lohā vā bhayā vā hāsā vā ṇeva sayaṃ musaṃ vaijjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ musaṃ vāyāvejjā, musaṃ vayaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi ṇa kāravemi karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | docce bhaṃte ! mahavvae uvaṭṭhiomi savvāo musāvāyāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now next, in the second Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of *mrishavada* (falsehood); O Lord, I renounce all falsehood. From anger, from greed, from fear, from laughter — I will neither speak falsehood myself, nor cause others to speak falsehood, nor approve of others who speak falsehood. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause to be done, nor approve of one who does. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the second Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all falsehood.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दोच्चे महव्वए** (docce mahavvae) = in the second Mahavrat
- **मुसावायाओ** (musāvāyāo) = from *mrishavada* (false speech, untruth)
- **कोहा वा लोहा वा भया वा हासा वा** (kohā vā lohā vā bhayā vā hāsā vā) = from anger, from greed, from fear, from laughter — the four root causes of lying
- **मुसं वइज्जा** (musaṃ vaijjā) = will speak falsehood

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second Mahavrat is the renunciation of all falsehood. The sutra carefully identifies the four roots from which falsehood typically arises: anger (which makes us slander), greed (which makes us deceive for gain), fear (which makes us hide), and laughter (which makes us exaggerate or mock). All other sources can be reduced to these four — or to derivatives of them (pride and deceit are forms of greed; aversion is a form of anger). To root out lying, the practitioner must root out these four passions.

The commentators identify four kinds of falsehood: (1) *sadbhava-nishedha* — denying what exists (saying "the soul does not exist"); (2) *asadbhava-udbhavana* — asserting what does not exist (saying "the soul is all-pervading"); (3) *arthantara* — calling something by another name (calling a cow a horse); (4) *garha* — speech that produces hatred or hurts another's heart (calling a one-eyed person "one-eye," calling a eunuch "impotent"). The vow covers all four.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's second vow is total truthfulness: "From anger, from greed, from fear, or from a desire to amuse — I will not lie, will not have others lie, will not approve when they lie. For my whole life, in mind, speech, and body."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Most lies are not strategic — they slip out from one of four feelings. Which of the four (anger, greed, fear, laughter) most often makes you say what is not true?

## SUTRA 4.9 — The Third Mahavrat: Adattadana Viraman (Renunciation of Taking-What-Is-Not-Given)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहावरे तच्चे भंते ! महव्वए अदिण्णादाणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! अदिण्णादाणं पच्चक्खामि । से गामे वा णगरे वा रण्णे वा अप्पं वा बहुं वा अणुं वा थूलं वा चित्तमंतं वा अचित्तमंतं वा णेव सयं अदिण्णं गिण्हिज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं अदिण्णं गिण्हाविज्जा, अदिण्णं गिण्हन्ते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । तच्चे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ अदिण्णादाणाओ वेरमणं ॥४.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.9 ||
ahāvare tacce bhaṃte ! mahavvae adiṇṇādāṇāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! adiṇṇādāṇaṃ paccakkhāmi | se gāme vā ṇagare vā raṇṇe vā appaṃ vā bahuṃ vā aṇuṃ vā thūlaṃ vā cittamaṃtaṃ vā acittamaṃtaṃ vā ṇeva sayaṃ adiṇṇaṃ giṇhijjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ adiṇṇaṃ giṇhāvijjā, adiṇṇaṃ giṇhante vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā, jāvajjīvāe tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi ṇa kāravemi karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | tacce bhaṃte ! mahavvae uvaṭṭhiomi savvāo adiṇṇādāṇāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now next, in the third Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of *adattadana* (taking-what-is-not-given); O Lord, I renounce all taking-what-is-not-given. Whether in a village, in a city, in a forest — anything of small value or great value, of small quantity or large quantity, subtle or gross, conscious (a sentient being) or non-conscious (an inanimate object) — I will neither take what is not given myself, nor cause others to take what is not given, nor approve of others who take what is not given. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the third Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all taking-what-is-not-given.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तच्चे महव्वए** (tacce mahavvae) = in the third Mahavrat
- **अदिण्णादाणाओ** (adiṇṇādāṇāo) = from *adattadana* — *a* (not) + *datta* (given) + *adana* (taking) — taking what has not been given
- **गामे वा णगरे वा रण्णे वा** (gāme vā ṇagare vā raṇṇe vā) = in village, in city, or in forest
- **अप्पं वा बहुं वा** (appaṃ vā bahuṃ vā) = small or large (in value or quantity)
- **अणुं वा थूलं वा** (aṇuṃ vā thūlaṃ vā) = subtle/small or gross/large
- **चित्तमंतं वा अचित्तमंतं वा** (cittamaṃtaṃ vā acittamaṃtaṃ vā) = conscious (a sentient being) or non-conscious (inert object)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third Mahavrat extends the principle of non-violation from beings (Mahavrat 1) and speech (Mahavrat 2) to property — but with a Jain refinement. Where ordinary morality forbids "stealing," the Jain vow forbids the broader *adattadana* — taking *anything* that has not been given. The traditional commentators identify five kinds of "what has not been given": (1) *Deva-adatta* — what the Tirthankara has prohibited, (2) *Guru-adatta* — what the Guru has not permitted, (3) *Raja-adatta* — what the king/state has not authorised (entering a kingdom without royal permission, etc.), (4) *Grihapati-adatta* — what the householder has not offered (taking even a straw without explicit permission), (5) *Sadharmik-adatta* — what a fellow monk has not consented to (using another monk's bowl without asking).

The breadth is striking: in a village, in a city, in a forest; small thing, large thing; living being or inanimate object. Even a single blade of grass picked from a forest is *adattadana* if no one has given it. This is why a Jain monk asks permission before sitting in any space, before drawing water, before plucking even a leaf — and accepts only what is freely offered.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's third vow is total non-taking: "Whether in a village, a city, or a wilderness — anything of any value, any size, living or non-living — I will not take what has not been given to me, will not have others take it, will not approve when others take it. By mind, speech, and body, for my whole life."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in your day do you take without being given? Time? Attention? Space? A small thing — a paperclip from the office? Where does the line of "not given" actually fall?

## SUTRA 4.10 — The Fourth Mahavrat: Maithuna Viraman (Renunciation of Sexual Activity)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहावरे चउत्थे भंते ! महव्वए मेहुणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! मेहुणं पच्चक्खामि । से दिव्वं वा माणुसं वा तिरिक्खजोणियं वा णेव सयं मेहुणं सेवेज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं मेहुणं सेवावेज्जा, मेहुणं सेवंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । चउत्थे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ मेहुणाओ वेरमणं ॥४.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.10 ||
ahāvare cautthe bhaṃte ! mahavvae mehuṇāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! mehuṇaṃ paccakkhāmi | se divvaṃ vā māṇusaṃ vā tirikkhajoṇiyaṃ vā ṇeva sayaṃ mehuṇaṃ sevejjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ mehuṇaṃ sevāvejjā, mehuṇaṃ sevaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi ṇa kāravemi karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | cautthe bhaṃte ! mahavvae uvaṭṭhiomi savvāo mehuṇāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now next, in the fourth Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of *maithuna* (sexual activity); O Lord, I renounce all sexual activity. Whether with a divine being, a human being, or an animal-yoni being — I will neither engage in sexual activity myself, nor cause others to engage in sexual activity, nor approve of others who engage in sexual activity. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the fourth Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all sexual activity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउत्थे महव्वए** (cautthe mahavvae) = in the fourth Mahavrat
- **मेहुणाओ** (mehuṇāo) = from *maithuna* — sexual union, *abrahmacharya*
- **दिव्वं वा माणुसं वा तिरिक्खजोणियं वा** (divvaṃ vā māṇusaṃ vā tirikkhajoṇiyaṃ vā) = with a divine being, a human, or an animal-yoni being

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth Mahavrat is total *brahmacharya* — celibacy in mind, speech, and body. The sutra explicitly names three categories: divine, human, and animal — to close any imaginable loophole. (One ancient ascetic order claimed that sexual activity with a deity in dream was permissible; this sutra forecloses that argument.)

The Sthananga Sutra identifies four causes that produce sexual desire (*maithuna sangya*): (1) increase of flesh, blood, and semen in the body; (2) the rising of *veda mohaniya karma* (the karma that produces sexual identification — male-veda, female-veda, neuter-veda); (3) hearing or thinking of sexual matters; (4) reflecting on sexual experience. The Samavayanga Sutra prescribes nine *brahmacharya* fences (*nava vaad*) — restraints on dwelling place, conversation, posture, food, etc. — to keep these four causes from arising. The Uttaradhyayan adds ten *brahmacharya* samadhi-sthanas. To break any of these is to risk breaking the fourth Mahavrat itself.

The eight subtle limbs of *maithuna* — remembrance, narration, play, gazing, secret conversation, resolve, intention, and consummation — are all renounced. The vow is not merely about the act; it is about the entire field of sexual mentality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's fourth vow is total celibacy: "With no being — divine, human, or animal — will I engage in sexual activity, cause others to, or approve when others do. In mind, speech, and body. For my whole life. Whatever I have done before, I turn from it now."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A vow that closes off not only the act but also remembrance, narration, fantasy, and even the gaze — what does that ask of attention itself?

## SUTRA 4.11 — The Fifth Mahavrat: Parigraha Viraman (Renunciation of Possession)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहावरे पंचमे भंते ! महव्वए परिग्गहाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! परिग्गहं पच्चक्खामि । से अप्पं वा बहुं वा अणुं वा थूलं वा चित्तमंतं वा अचित्तमंतं वा, णेव सयं परिग्गहं परिगिण्हेज्जा णेवण्णेहिं परिग्गहं परिगिण्हाविज्जा परिग्गहं परिगिण्हंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि, ण कारवेमि, करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । पंचमे भंते ! महव्वए उवट्ठिओमि सव्वाओ परिग्गहाओ वेरमणं ॥४.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.11 ||
ahāvare paṃcame bhaṃte ! mahavvae pariggahāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! pariggahaṃ paccakkhāmi | se appaṃ vā bahuṃ vā aṇuṃ vā thūlaṃ vā cittamaṃtaṃ vā acittamaṃtaṃ vā, ṇeva sayaṃ pariggahaṃ parigiṇhejjā ṇevaṇṇehiṃ pariggahaṃ parigiṇhāvijjā pariggahaṃ parigiṇhaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi, ṇa kāravemi, karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | paṃcame bhaṃte ! mahavvae uvaṭṭhiomi savvāo pariggahāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now next, in the fifth Mahavrat, O Lord, there is renunciation of *parigraha* (possession); O Lord, I renounce all possession. Anything of small value or great value, of small quantity or large quantity, subtle or gross, conscious (a sentient being like a disciple or animal) or non-conscious (an inanimate object) — I will neither take possession myself, nor cause others to take possession, nor approve of others who take possession. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the fifth Mahavrat, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all possession.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पंचमे महव्वए** (paṃcame mahavvae) = in the fifth Mahavrat
- **परिग्गहाओ** (pariggahāo) = from *parigraha* — grasping, possession, ownership
- **परिग्गहं परिगिण्हेज्जा** (pariggahaṃ parigiṇhejjā) = will take possession

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fifth Mahavrat is the renunciation of *parigraha* — possession — and the sutra is careful to define possession with maximum breadth: small or large, valuable or trivial, sentient (a disciple, an animal) or inert (a coin, a cloth). The principle behind the breadth is the famous Dasvaikalik definition of *parigraha* (from Adhyayan 6): **"muccha pariggaho vutto"** — "attachment is what is called possession." It is not the *thing* but the *attachment* to the thing that constitutes parigraha. A monk who carries even his alms-bowl with attachment is bound; a monk who would carry a kingdom without attachment would (theoretically) be free. But because attachment so naturally attaches to objects, the safest renunciation is renunciation of the objects themselves.

Tradition divides parigraha into nine outer kinds (land, houses, silver, gold, wealth, grain, servants, animals, household goods) and fourteen inner kinds (the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed; the nine *nokashayas* — laughter, attachment, dislike, fear, sorrow, disgust, three sexual identifications; and *mithyatva* — wrong belief). The monk renounces *all* outer parigraha completely, and undertakes the lifelong work of renouncing the inner.

A separate three-fold classification is also given: *sharira* (body), *karma* (the karma matter clinging to the soul), and *upadhi* (the few permitted monastic implements). Even the body is, in this analysis, a form of parigraha that the soul will eventually shed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's fifth vow is total non-possession: "Anything small or large, valuable or trivial, living or non-living — I will not possess, will not have others possess, will not approve when others possess. By mind, speech, and body, for my whole life. The deepest possession is attachment itself, and from that too I now turn."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The text says possession is not the thing but the attachment. What is the smallest object you own that you would feel real pain to lose? Is the pain in the object — or in you?

## SUTRA 4.12 — The Sixth Vow: Ratri-bhojan Viraman (Renunciation of Night-Eating)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहावरे छट्ठे भंते ! वए राइभोयणाओ वेरमणं; सव्वं भंते ! राइभोयणं पच्चक्खामि । से असणं वा पाणं वा खाइमं वा साइमं वा णेव सयं राइं भुंजेज्जा, णेवण्णेहिं राइं भुंजाविज्जा, राइं भुंजंते वि अण्णे ण समणुजाणेज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविह तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतंपि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि । छट्ठे भंते ! वए उवट्ठिओमि, सव्वाओ राइभोयणाओ वेरमणं ॥४.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.12 ||
ahāvare chaṭṭhe bhaṃte ! vae rāibhoyaṇāo veramaṇaṃ; savvaṃ bhaṃte ! rāibhoyaṇaṃ paccakkhāmi | se asaṇaṃ vā pāṇaṃ vā khāimaṃ vā sāimaṃ vā ṇeva sayaṃ rāiṃ bhuṃjejjā, ṇevaṇṇehiṃ rāiṃ bhuṃjāvijjā, rāiṃ bhuṃjaṃte vi aṇṇe ṇa samaṇujāṇejjā, jāvajjīvāe tiviha tiviheṇaṃ maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ ṇa karemi ṇa kāravemi karaṃtaṃpi aṇṇaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi ṇiṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi | chaṭṭhe bhaṃte ! vae uvaṭṭhiomi, savvāo rāibhoyaṇāo veramaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Now next, in the sixth vow, O Lord, there is renunciation of *ratri-bhojan* (eating at night); O Lord, I renounce all night-eating. Of *ashana* (food), *pana* (drink), *khadima* (chewables — fruit, dry food), or *swadima* (savouries — mouth-fresheners) — I will neither eat at night myself, nor cause others to eat at night, nor approve of others who eat at night. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self. In the sixth vow, O Lord, I take my stand — total renunciation of all night-eating.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **छट्ठे वए** (chaṭṭhe vae) = in the sixth vow (note: *vae* not *mahavvae* — Jain tradition treats this as a vow but often distinct from the five great mahavrats)
- **राइभोयणाओ** (rāibhoyaṇāo) = from *ratri-bhojan* — night-eating (after sunset and before sunrise)
- **असणं वा पाणं वा खाइमं वा साइमं वा** (asaṇaṃ vā pāṇaṃ vā khāimaṃ vā sāimaṃ vā) = the four kinds of consumables — *ashana* (substantive food), *pana* (water/drink), *khadima* (chewable items like fruit, nuts), *swadima* (taste-items like cardamom, betel)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sixth vow — sometimes counted as a *mahavrat*, sometimes as an additional vow that completes the Mahavrats — is the total renunciation of all eating and drinking between sunset and sunrise. The four-fold list (*ashana, pana, khadima, swadima*) covers every form of intake, leaving no loophole.

Why is night-eating singled out for vow-status? Several reasons interlock. First, *ahimsa*: at night countless small life-forms (insects, microorganisms) are invisible and inevitably enter food and water — to eat at night is to consume lives one cannot see. Second, *digestion*: traditional medicine and Jain physiology hold that the digestive fire (*jathara-agni*) wanes after sunset; food taken at night is poorly digested and produces *ama* (toxic residue). Third, *sangrahaa* — eating at night encourages stockpiling, which leads to other parigraha. Fourth, *spiritual*: the night is the time for swadhyaya, dhyana, and sleep — eating disturbs all three.

For a lay practitioner, this is one of the few monastic vows commonly accepted in full — *chauviharu* (the four-fold pratyakhyana of all four kinds of food until sunrise) is widespread in observant Jain households.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk's sixth vow is total renunciation of night-eating: "Of food, drink, fruit, or savouries — I will not eat at night, will not have others eat at night, will not approve when others eat at night. By mind, speech, and body. For my whole life."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What we cannot see, we cannot consent to. What does it mean to put something into your body that the day's light has not first shown you?

- **इच्चेयाइं** (icceyāiṃ) = these (just-spoken)
- **पंच महव्वयाइं** (paṃca mahavvayāiṃ) = the five Mahavrats
- **राइभोयणवेरमणं छट्ठाइं** (rāibhoyaṇaveramaṇaṃ chaṭṭhāiṃ) = together with night-eating-renunciation as the sixth
- **अत्तहियट्ठयाए** (attahiyaṭṭhayāe) = for the welfare of one's own soul (*ātma-hita-arthāya*)
- **उवसंपज्जित्ताणं** (uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ) = having accepted (*upa* = near + *sampadya* = accepting) — having received from the Guru and accepted the vow
- **विहरामि** (viharāmi) = I live, I move forward

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the concluding pratigya — the formal acceptance verse that closes the vow-section of the chapter. Having heard each of the six vows expounded individually, the disciple now sums up his commitment in a single sentence: "All six together — I accept them, for my own welfare, and I live."

The phrase **"attahiyaṭṭhayāe"** — "for the welfare of one's own soul" — is doctrinally precise. The vows are not undertaken for show, for social approval, for fear of hell, or even for compassion alone. They are undertaken because they are *good for the one taking them*. Liberation is the deepest self-interest a being can pursue, and the vows are the path. To take them is not sacrifice; it is the soul's most rational act.

The phrase **"uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ"** is the technical term for receiving a vow from the Guru. The disciple does not invent the vows; he receives them, formally, from a teacher who has himself received them, in a chain reaching back to the Tirthankara. This is why the verse cannot be taken privately — it must be taken in the presence of a witness who carries the lineage.

With this sutra, the *charitra-dharma* section (sutras 7–13) closes. What follows is the *yatana* section (sutras 14–19) — the practical instructions by which the vows are kept moment by moment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple closes the vow-ceremony with a single sentence: "These five great vows, together with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth — I accept all of them, for the good of my own soul, and I live by them."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A vow is taken once, but lived a thousand times a day. What in your own life have you accepted formally that you must accept again every morning?

## SUTRA 4.14–4.19 — The Six Yatana Sutras (Mindful Restraint Toward Each Kaya)

These six parallel sutras describe the careful, vigilant restraint (*yatana*) the monk must practise toward each of the six classes of beings. The structure of every sutra is identical:

> "That monk or nun who is engaged in restraint, withdrawn from sin, and has formally renounced sinful karma — by day or by night, alone or in a gathering, asleep or awake — toward [this kaya] in any of these forms — [he/she] shall not himself touch, scrape, strike, break, agitate, scatter (or whatever specific verbs apply); shall not have another do so; shall not approve of another doing so. For as long as I live, by all three karanas and three yogas, by mind, speech, and body — I will not do, will not cause, will not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I let go of that sinful self."

What changes in each sutra is the *kaya* and the *list of specific harmful actions* that apply to it. Because the structure is repetitive, this commentary treats them as a single set; the unique elements of each are noted in turn.

### 4.14 — Prithvikaya Yatana (Earth-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से पुढविं वा भित्तिं वा सिलं वा लेलुं वा ससरक्खं वा कायं ससरक्खं वा वत्थं हत्थेण वा पाएण वा कट्ठेण वा किलिंचेण वा अंगुलियाए वा सिलागाए वा सिलागहत्थेण वा ण आलिहिज्जा ण विलिहिज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा...

**TRANSLATION:** Toward earth, a wall, a stone, a clod, a body covered with sentient dust, or clothes covered with sentient dust — with hand, foot, stick, bamboo splinter, finger, iron rod, or bundle of iron rods — the monk shall not draw, shall not draw repeatedly, shall not touch (rub against), shall not break it; shall not have others draw, draw repeatedly, touch, or break; shall not approve of others drawing, drawing repeatedly, touching, or breaking. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.) ⟨Six forms of *prithvikaya*: living soil, walls of canals/rivers/structures, large stones, fragments of stone (pebbles, brick), sentient dust on the body, sentient dust on clothing.⟩

### 4.15 — Apkaya Yatana (Water-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से उदगं वा ओसं वा हिमं वा महियं वा करगं वा हरतणुगं वा सुद्धोदगं वा उदउल्लं वा कायं, उदउल्लं वा वत्थं, ससिणिद्धं वा कायं, ससिणिद्धं वा वत्थं, ण आमुसिज्जा, ण संफुसिज्जा, ण आवीलिज्जा ण पवीलिज्जा, ण अक्खोडिज्जा ण पक्खोडिज्जा, ण आयाविज्जा ण पयाविज्जा...

**TRANSLATION:** Toward well-water, dew, snow, fog, hail, water-droplets on grass-tips, rain-water, a wet body, a wet cloth, a moistened body, or a moistened cloth — the monk shall not touch once or repeatedly, shall not press lightly or press hard, shall not shake once or shake repeatedly, shall not dry once or dry repeatedly (in the sun); shall not have others do these things; shall not approve of others doing them. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.) ⟨Eight forms of activity prohibited: touch, repeated touch, light press, hard press, shake, repeated shake, dry, repeated dry.⟩

### 4.16 — Tejaskaya Yatana (Fire-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से अगणिं वा इंगालं वा मुम्मुरं वा अच्चिं वा जालं वा अलायं वा सुद्धागणिं वा उक्कं वा; ण उंजिज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा ण उज्जालिज्जा ण पज्जालिज्जा ण णिव्वाविज्जा...

**TRANSLATION:** Toward fire, ember, smouldering cow-dung-fire, the tip of a flame (of lamp, etc.), connected flame, partially-burnt firewood, pure (smokeless) fire, or meteor/lightning — the monk shall not feed it (with fuel/oil), shall not strike or rub it, shall not break it, shall not kindle it (with fan or breath), shall not increase its blaze, shall not extinguish it; shall not have others do these things; shall not approve of others doing them. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.) ⟨Eight forms of *tejaskaya* are listed; six harmful actions prohibited.⟩

### 4.17 — Vayukaya Yatana (Air-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से सिएण वा विहुयणेण वा तालियंटेण वा पत्तेण वा पत्तभंगेण वा साहाए वा साहाभंगेण वा पिहुणेण वा पिहुणहत्थेण वा चेलेण वा चेलकण्णेण वा हत्थेण वा मुहेण वा अप्पणो वा कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं, ण फुमिज्जा, ण वीएज्जा...

**TRANSLATION:** With a white chowrie, a fan, a palm-leaf fan, a single leaf, a fragment of leaf, a branch, a fragment of branch, a peacock-feather brush, a bundle of peacock feathers, a cloth, a corner of cloth, the hand, or the mouth — toward his own body or any external object — the monk shall not blow, shall not fan; shall not have others blow or fan; shall not approve of others blowing or fanning. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.) ⟨Thirteen instruments of *vayukaya* harm enumerated; the harm consists in producing artificial wind that disturbs the wind-bodied beings.⟩

### 4.18 — Vanaspatikaya Yatana (Plant-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से बीएसु वा बीयपइट्ठेसु वा रूढेसु वा रूढपइट्ठेसु वा जाएसु वा जायपइट्ठेसु वा हरिएसु वा हरियपइट्ठेसु वा छिण्णेसु वा छिण्णपइट्ठेसु वा सचित्तेसु वा सचित्तकोलपडिणिस्सिएसु वा, ण गच्छेज्जा ण चिट्ठेज्जा ण णिसीएज्जा ण तुयट्टेज्जा...

**TRANSLATION:** Upon seeds, things resting on seeds, sprouts, things resting on sprouts, growths, things resting on growths, green plants, things resting on green plants, cut plants (still living), things resting on cut plants, sentient (plant) substances, or wood with insects/microorganisms attached — the monk shall not walk, shall not stand, shall not sit, shall not lie down; shall not have others walk, stand, sit, or lie there; shall not approve of others walking, standing, sitting, or lying there. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.) ⟨Twelve plant-related substrates listed; four prohibited actions: walk, stand, sit, lie.⟩

### 4.19 — Trasakaya Yatana (Mobile-Bodied Restraint)

**PRAKRIT (key clauses):** से कीडं वा पयंगं वा कुंथुं वा पिवीलियं वा हत्थंसि वा पायंसि वा बाहुंसि वा ऊरुंसि वा उदरंसि वा सीसंसि वा वत्थंसि वा पडिग्गहंसि वा कंबलंसि वा पायपुंछणंसि वा रयहरणंसि वा गोच्छंसि वा उंडगंसि वा दंडगंसि वा पीढगंसि वा फलगंसि वा सेज्जंसि वा संथारगंसि वा अण्णयरंसि वा तह‍प्पगारे उवगरणजाए; तओ संजयामेव पडिलेहिय पडिलेहिय, पमज्जिय पमज्जिय, एगंतमवणेज्जा, णो णं संघायमावज्जेज्जा ॥४.१९॥

**TRANSLATION:** If on the hand, foot, arm, thigh, belly, head, robe, alms-bowl, blanket, foot-wiper (*pādapro­ñchana*), monastic broom (*rajoharana*), tassel-cleaner, vessel for measuring food, staff, low seat, plank, bedding, straw-mat, or any similar piece of monastic equipment — there appears any worm, butterfly, *kunthu* (tiny ant), or ant — then, with vigilance, having carefully inspected and re-inspected, having gently swept and re-swept, the monk should remove the being to a solitary, safe place. He should not crush it, should not gather it together (with force), should not cause it injury. (Followed by the standard pratikraman closing.)

### COMBINED COMMENTARY (4.14–4.19)

These six sutras are the operational backbone of the entire chapter. Having renounced harm in principle (Sutra 6 and the Five Mahavrats), the monk must now know how to *actually live* without harming. These six sutras tell him: here is what your body and your possessions encounter every hour of the day; here is exactly what you must and must not do.

The structural genius of these sutras is in the universal opening clause — repeated in every one of the six — that brackets the monk's situation under six conditions: **"by day or by night, alone or in a gathering, asleep or awake."** No moment is exempt. The vow does not apply only when the monk is "on duty"; it applies in every breath of every state. This is the formal end of the household life's casual relation to action — every gesture is now under awareness.

Each sutra applies the universal nine-koti restraint (3 karanas × 3 yogas) to a *specific physical reality*. Earth: don't draw on it, don't strike it, don't break it. Water: don't touch wet surfaces, don't wring out, don't dry in sun. Fire: don't feed, don't fan, don't extinguish. Air: don't generate artificial wind. Plants: don't walk, sit, or lie on living vegetation. Mobile beings: when they appear, gently inspect, sweep, and place them safely aside.

The Trasakaya yatana (4.19) is especially tender. It is the only sutra of the six in which the monk is not merely told to *avoid* contact but is told *what to do* when contact happens. A mobile being — an ant, a worm, a butterfly — has landed on his hand, his robe, his bowl. He cannot simply ignore it; passive ignorance would be its own form of harm. So he must *pratilekhana* (carefully inspect, twice), *pramarjana* (gently sweep with the rajoharana, twice), and then *ekantam-apanayejja* — carry the being to a solitary, safe place where no further harm will reach it. The doubling of the verbs ("inspect-inspect, sweep-sweep") is itself the teaching: vigilance must be confirmed, not assumed.

The eighteen places where the being might appear (hand, foot, arm, thigh, belly, head, robe, bowl, blanket, foot-wiper, broom, tassel-cleaner, measuring-vessel, staff, low seat, plank, bedding, straw-mat) are an inventory of the monk's whole physical world. Six are body-parts; six are clothes-and-utensils; six are seating-and-sleeping equipment. Together they cover every surface a small being might land on.

Two essential phrases close the trasa-yatana: **"no naṃ saṅghāyamāvajjejjā"** — "he should not gather it together (with force)" or "should not crush it." *Saṅghāta* has two meanings: pressing the being's body so as to cause pain, and gathering many beings into one place where they cannot survive. Both are forbidden. The being is not to be relocated for the monk's convenience; it is to be released *for its own sake*.

The deeper teaching across all six sutras is the principle of *yatana* itself — careful, conscious, deliberate movement. Yatana is the opposite of *pramaada* (carelessness). The Jain monk is not merely someone who has taken vows; he is someone who has trained his nervous system to slow down, to look before stepping, to inspect before sitting, to listen before speaking, to consider before acting. Without this trained yatana, the vows become impossible to keep — which is why the next set of gathas opens with the *yatana-ayatana* contrast: with yatana, no karma binds; without it, even the well-vowed monk binds heavy karma.

### SIMPLY PUT

These six sutras give the monk practical instructions for protecting each of the six classes of beings, twenty-four hours a day, alone or in company, asleep or awake. Don't draw on living earth or strike stones. Don't touch wet surfaces or wring out wet cloth in the sun. Don't feed or fan or extinguish living fire. Don't generate artificial wind that disturbs the air-bodied beings. Don't walk, sit, or lie on living vegetation. And when a small mobile being lands on your body or possessions, carefully look at it, gently brush it off with the soft rajoharana, and place it safely in a solitary spot — never crush, never gather, never harm.

### CONTEMPLATE

The chapter does not say "be careful." It says: inspect twice, sweep twice, and place in a safe place. What in your own life would change if every action required this much attention?

# ⟨ THE GATHA SECTION (4.G1–4.G29) ⟩

What follows are twenty-nine numbered gathas — verses in poetic metre — that summarise, deepen, and seal the teachings of the prose sutras. The first twelve gathas develop the *yatana-ayatana* contrast (action with vs. without mindfulness) and stress the central importance of *jnana* (knowledge). The middle gathas (13–25) trace the spiritual development arc from knowing the six categories to attaining moksha. The closing gathas (26–29) contain the final benediction and the formal ending verse.

|| ગ.૩ || अजयं आसमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ ।

|| ગ.૬ || अजयं भासमाणो य, पाणभूयाइं हिंसइ ।
बंधई पावयं कम्मं, तं से होइ कडुयं फलं ॥६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.G1 || ajayaṃ caramāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||
|| 4.G2 || ajayaṃ ciṭṭhamāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||
|| 4.G3 || ajayaṃ āsamāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||
|| 4.G4 || ajayaṃ sayamāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||
|| 4.G5 || ajayaṃ bhuṃjamāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||
|| 4.G6 || ajayaṃ bhāsamāṇo ya, pāṇabhūyāiṃ hiṃsai | bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ, taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(1) One who walks without yatana harms the mobile and immobile beings. He binds sinful karma, and the fruit of that for him is bitter.
(2) One who stands without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.
(3) One who sits without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.
(4) One who lies down (sleeps) without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.
(5) One who eats without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.
(6) One who speaks without yatana harms beings. He binds sinful karma; the fruit is bitter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अजयं** (ajayaṃ) = without yatana — without mindful restraint, with carelessness, with *pramaada*
- **चरमाणो** (caramāṇo) = walking
- **चिट्ठमाणो** (ciṭṭhamāṇo) = standing
- **आसमाणो** (āsamāṇo) = sitting
- **सयमाणो** (sayamāṇo) = lying down / sleeping
- **भुंजमाणो** (bhuṃjamāṇo) = eating
- **भासमाणो** (bhāsamāṇo) = speaking
- **पाणभूयाइं** (pāṇabhūyāiṃ) = beings (literally: *prana* = mobile beings + *bhuta* = immobile/plants)
- **हिंसइ** (hiṃsai) = harms
- **बंधई पावयं कम्मं** (bandhaī pāvayaṃ kammaṃ) = he binds sinful karma
- **तं से होइ कडुयं फलं** (taṃ se hoi kaḍuyaṃ phalaṃ) = the fruit of that is bitter for him

### 5. COMMENTARY

These six gathas form a single rhythmic teaching by repetition. The structure is identical in each verse; only the activity changes — walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, eating, speaking. These are the six fundamental physical activities that fill every waking hour. The teaching is precise: *any* one of them, performed without *yatana*, becomes a vehicle for harm and a binder of karma.

The pivotal word is **ajayaṃ** — "without yatana." Yatana is not a feeling; it is *upayoga* — applied awareness, vigilant attention. Its opposite is *pramaada* — heedlessness, carelessness, the autopilot mode in which most of human life is lived. The Jain teaching is that even an apparently innocuous act (sitting on a chair, walking down a path, drinking water) becomes a karma-binding violence the moment awareness drops. The being one harms unknowingly is no less harmed for one's ignorance — and the karma binds whether one intended the harm or not.

The fruit (*phala*) is described as **kaḍuya** — bitter. The metaphor is botanical: bitter fruit is the natural ripening of poisonous seed. Sin sown without awareness still ripens; the seed does not need the gardener's permission to grow.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Six verses, one teaching: walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, eating, and speaking — if any of these are done without careful awareness, you will harm beings, bind karma, and reap bitter fruit. There is no exemption for inattention; the harm is real even when the harmer is asleep at the wheel.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When have you most recently acted on autopilot? What did you walk on, sit on, eat, or say without seeing it?

## SUTRA 4.G7–4.G8 — The Question and the Answer (Yatana Saves)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

|| ગ.૭ || कहं चरे कहं चिट्ठे, कहमासे कहं सए ।
कहं भुंजंतो भासंतो, पावकम्मं ण बंधइ ॥७॥

|| ગ.૮ || जयं चरे जयं चिट्ठे, जयमासे जयं सए ।
जयं भुंजंतो भासंतो, पावकम्मं ण बंधइ ॥८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.G7 || kahaṃ care kahaṃ ciṭṭhe, kahamāse kahaṃ sae | kahaṃ bhuṃjaṃto bhāsaṃto, pāvakammaṃ ṇa baṃdhai ||
|| 4.G8 || jayaṃ care jayaṃ ciṭṭhe, jayamāse jayaṃ sae | jayaṃ bhuṃjaṃto bhāsaṃto, pāvakammaṃ ṇa baṃdhai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(7) How should one walk, how should one stand, how should one sit, how should one lie down? How should one eat, how should one speak — so that one does not bind sinful karma?

(8) Walk with yatana, stand with yatana, sit with yatana, lie down with yatana. Eat with yatana, speak with yatana — and one does not bind sinful karma.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कहं** (kahaṃ) = how
- **चरे** (care) = should walk
- **चिट्ठे** (ciṭṭhe) = should stand
- **आसे** (āse) = should sit
- **सए** (sae) = should lie down
- **भुंजंतो** (bhuṃjaṃto) = while eating
- **भासंतो** (bhāsaṃto) = while speaking
- **पावकम्मं ण बंधइ** (pāvakammaṃ ṇa baṃdhai) = does not bind sinful karma
- **जयं** (jayaṃ) = with yatana, with mindful restraint, with vigilance

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two gathas form a celebrated question-and-answer pair. The disciple asks (gatha 7): if every form of activity without yatana binds karma, then how am I to act at all? Must I cease walking, standing, sitting, eating, speaking — that is, cease living? The teacher answers (gatha 8): no — keep doing all of them, but do them with *yatana*. The same six activities that bind karma when done carelessly bind no karma at all when done with awareness.

This is the Jain ethical pivot. The path is not renunciation of action but transformation of action. The same body that harms can also protect, if the awareness behind the body is awake. The single word **jayaṃ** (with yatana) is the entire difference between bondage and freedom.

The two gathas are often quoted together as the practical heart of Jain ethics. They are recited in monastic morning prayers and held up as the operational guide to every monk's day.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple asks: "How can I walk, stand, sit, sleep, eat, and speak without binding sin?" The answer comes back: "Do all of them — but do them with mindful awareness. With yatana, nothing you do will bind you."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The same act can either bind or free, depending on the awareness behind it. Which of your six daily activities — walking, standing, sitting, sleeping, eating, speaking — most needs your attention today?

## SUTRA 4.13 — Concluding Pratigya: The Purpose of the Six Vows

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्चेयाइं पंच महव्वयाइं राइभोयणवेरमणछट्ठाइं अत्तहियट्ठयाए उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरामि ॥४.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.13 ||
iccheyāiṃ paṃca mahavvayāiṃ rāibhoyaṇaveramaṇachaṭṭhāiṃ attahiyaṭṭhayāe uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

These five Mahavrats together with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth — having taken them upon myself for the welfare of my own soul, I now move forward (in monastic life).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इच्चेयाइं पंच महव्वयाइं** (iccheyāiṃ paṃca mahavvayāiṃ) = these five Mahavrats
- **राइभोयणवेरमणछट्ठाइं** (rāibhoyaṇaveramaṇachaṭṭhāiṃ) = with the renunciation of night-eating as the sixth
- **अत्तहियट्ठयाए** (attahiyaṭṭhayāe) = for the welfare of one's own soul (*atma-hita-artha*)
- **उवसंपज्जित्ताणं** (uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ) = having taken upon, having accepted (*upa+sampad*)
- **विहरामि** (viharāmi) = I move about, I live, I conduct my life

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing line of the Mahavrat-acceptance ritual. The disciple, having recited each of the five great vows plus the night-eating vow before the Guru, now declares the *purpose* and the *commitment* in a single sentence: "for the welfare of my own soul, I have accepted these and I now live in them." Note the radical interiority — *attahiyaṭṭhayāe*, "for the sake of my own soul's good." Not for fame, not for community approval, not for the gods. The vow is taken alone, even when taken in public.

The verb *viharāmi* — "I move about" — is the standard Jain monastic verb for "I live the wandering homeless life." The vows are not a state; they are a practice that walks.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"These five Mahavrats — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession — together with the sixth vow against night-eating — I have now accepted them, for my own soul's good. And so I begin my life as a monk."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is one practice you do "for your own soul's good," with no one watching, with no one to approve?

## SUTRA 4.14 — Yatana Toward Prithvi-kaya (Earth-Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा संजयविरयपडिहयपच्चक्खायपावकम्मे दिया वा राओ वा एगओ वा परिसागओ वा सुत्ते वा जागरमाणे वा; से पुढविं वा भित्तिं वा सिलं वा लेलुं वा ससरक्खं वा कायं ससरक्खं वा वत्थं; हत्थेणं वा पाएणं वा कट्ठेण वा किलिंचेण वा अंगुलियाए वा सिलागाए वा सिलागहत्थेण वा ण आलिहिज्जा ण विलिहिज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा; अण्णं ण आलिहावेज्जा ण विलिहावेज्जा ण घट्टावेज्जा ण भिंदावेज्जा; अण्णं आलिहंतं वा विलिहंतं वा घट्टंतं वा भिंदंतं वा ण समणुजाणिज्जा, जावज्जीवाए तिविहं तिविहेणं मणेणं वायाए काएणं; ण करेमि ण कारवेमि करंतं पि अण्णं ण समणुजाणामि, तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि णिंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥४.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.14 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā saṃjayaviraya-paḍihaya-paccakkhāya-pāvakamme diyā vā rāo vā egao vā parisāgao vā sutte vā jāgaramāṇe vā; se puḍhaviṃ vā bhittiṃ vā silaṃ vā leluṃ vā sasarakkhaṃ vā kāyaṃ sasarakkhaṃ vā vatthaṃ; hattheṇaṃ vā pāeṇaṃ vā kaṭṭheṇa vā kiliṃceṇa vā aṃguliyāe vā silāgāe vā silāgahattheṇa vā ṇa ālihijjā ṇa vilihijjā ṇa ghaṭṭijjā ṇa bhiṃdijjā; aṇṇaṃ ṇa ālihāvejjā ṇa vilihāvejjā ṇa ghaṭṭāvejjā ṇa bhiṃdāvejjā; aṇṇaṃ ālihaṃtaṃ vā vilihaṃtaṃ vā ghaṭṭaṃtaṃ vā bhiṃdaṃtaṃ vā ṇa samaṇujāṇijjā... [refrain as in 4.6] ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni — restrained, withdrawn from sin, having destroyed past sinful karma, having renounced future sin — by day or by night, alone or in assembly, asleep or awake — shall not, with hand, foot, stick, bamboo-splinter, finger, iron-spike, or bundle of spikes, scratch, dig, scrape, or break: earth, wall, rock, clod, dust-covered body, or dust-covered cloth. Nor cause another to do so. Nor approve another doing so. Lifelong, by all three karanas and three yogas, by mind-speech-body, I do not, do not cause, do not approve. From that, O Lord, I turn back; I censure; I confess; I release the sinful self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजय-विरय-पडिहय-पच्चक्खाय-पावकम्मे** (saṃjaya-viraya-paḍihaya-paccakkhāya-pāvakamme) = restrained (in the 17 forms of *samyama*), withdrawn (from the 18 sins), having destroyed past sinful karma (through *prayashchitta*), having renounced future sinful karma
- **पुढविं... भित्तिं... सिलं... लेलुं** (puḍhaviṃ... bhittiṃ... silaṃ... leluṃ) = the six forms of earth-body: living soil, embankment/wall, large rock, clods/pebbles, dust-covered body, dust-covered cloth
- **हत्थेणं... सिलागहत्थेण** (hattheṇaṃ... silāgahattheṇa) = the seven instruments — hand, foot, wood, bamboo-splinter, finger, iron-spike, bundle of spikes
- **आलिहिज्जा... भिंदिज्जा** (ālihijjā... bhiṃdijjā) = the four destructive actions — scratch (light line), dig deeper, scrape/touch with friction, break

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra opens the second half of Adhyayan-4: having vowed the six Mahavrats abstractly, the practitioner now applies them concretely — *kaya by kaya*. The sutra spells out, with characteristic Jain over-specificity, every kind of earth-body (six forms), every instrument of harm (seven), every grade of action (four), and the full nine-koti formula (3 karanas × 3 yogas).

The pattern repeats almost identically for the next five sutras (4.15-4.19), one per *kaya*. The verbal precision is a kind of meditation: by naming every possible mode of harm, the practitioner installs a vigilance that operates beneath conscious choice. The body learns: *do not scratch the earth, even idly*.

Note the five qualifying epithets of the monk himself: *bhikshu, samyata, virata, pratihata-papakarma, pratyakhyata-papakarma* — beggar, restrained, withdrawn, past-sin-destroyed, future-sin-renounced. This is a complete portrait of monastic identity in five compounds.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk vows: "I will not, with any tool — hand, foot, stick, splinter, finger, spike — scratch, dig, scrape, or break any form of living earth: soil, wall, rock, clod. Nor on a body or cloth caked with living dust. Nor will I have others do it. Nor approve when others do. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you idly scratch the dirt with a stick, what is the dirt? What is the stick? What is the scratching?

## SUTRA 4.15 — Yatana Toward Ap-kaya (Water-Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से उदगं वा ओसं वा हिमं वा महियं वा करगं वा हरतणुगं वा सुद्धोदगं वा उदउल्लं वा कायं, उदउल्लं वा वत्थं, ससिणिद्धं वा कायं, ससिणिद्धं वा वत्थं, ण आमुसिज्जा ण संफुसिज्जा, ण आवीलिज्जा ण पवीलिज्जा, ण अक्खोडिज्जा ण पक्खोडिज्जा, ण आयाविज्जा ण पयाविज्जा; अण्णं ण आमुसावेज्जा ण संफुसावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain as in 4.6] ॥४.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.15 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā... se udagaṃ vā osaṃ vā himaṃ vā mahiyaṃ vā karagaṃ vā haratanugaṃ vā suddhodagaṃ vā udaullaṃ vā kāyaṃ, udaullaṃ vā vatthaṃ, sasiṇiddhaṃ vā kāyaṃ, sasiṇiddhaṃ vā vatthaṃ, ṇa āmusijjā ṇa saṃphusijjā, ṇa āvīlijjā ṇa pavīlijjā, ṇa akkhoḍijjā ṇa pakkhoḍijjā, ṇa āyāvijjā ṇa payāvijjā... ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not, regarding water — well-water, dew, ice, fog, hail, dewdrops on green grass, rainwater — nor regarding a body wet with such water, nor a wet cloth, nor a body still moist, nor a moist cloth — touch once or repeatedly, squeeze lightly or hard, shake once or vigorously, dry by friction or by the sun. Nor cause another to do so. Nor approve another doing so. [9-koti refrain]

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उदगं... सुद्धोदगं** (udagaṃ... suddhodagaṃ) = the seven forms of water — well/borewell water, dew, ice/snow, fog, hail, dewdrops on green grass, rainwater
- **उदउल्लं वा कायं... ससिणिद्धं वा वत्थं** (udaullaṃ vā kāyaṃ... sasiṇiddhaṃ vā vatthaṃ) = body or cloth dripping with sentient water; body or cloth still moist
- **आमुसिज्जा... पयाविज्जा** (āmusijjā... payāvijjā) = the eight harmful actions in pairs — touch (once/repeated), squeeze (light/hard), shake (once/repeatedly), dry (by friction/by sun)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eight pairs — *touch / repeated touch, squeeze / hard squeeze, shake / vigorous shake, dry / sun-dry* — are notable for their granularity. Water-bodies are uniquely fragile in the Jain reckoning: a water-being can be killed by a single touch, because mere contact disturbs the water-body's integrity. Hence the doubling-up: *amusijjā* (touch once) is forbidden, *saṃphusijjā* (touch repeatedly) is also forbidden — neither is permitted, and naming both shuts off any rationalization.

The list of seven water-types covers natural water in all its visible forms — including dew on grass, which a monk might idly brush past. The vow extends to a body or cloth wet with such water: until it is fully dry, the *jiva-rasa* still clings.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk vows: "I will not touch, squeeze, shake, or dry — even once, even gently — any form of living water: well-water, dew, ice, fog, hail, dewdrops, rain. Nor a body or cloth wet with such water. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A water-drop is a being. To squeeze a wet cloth is to crush thousands. What does this do to your relationship with rain?

## SUTRA 4.16 — Yatana Toward Agni-kaya (Fire-Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से अगणिं वा इंगालं वा मुम्मुरं वा अच्चिं वा जालं वा अलायं वा सुद्धागणिं वा उक्कं वा; ण उज्जेज्जा ण घट्टिज्जा ण भिंदिज्जा ण उज्जालिज्जा ण पज्जालिज्जा ण णिव्वाविज्जा; अण्णं ण उज्जावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain] ॥४.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.16 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā... se agaṇiṃ vā iṃgālaṃ vā mummuraṃ vā acciṃ vā jālaṃ vā alāyaṃ vā suddhāgaṇiṃ vā ukkaṃ vā; ṇa ujjejjā ṇa ghaṭṭijjā ṇa bhiṃdijjā ṇa ujjālijjā ṇa pajjālijjā ṇa ṇivvāvijjā... ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not, regarding fire — fire, embers, smouldering coals or dung-fire, sparks, flame, half-burnt brand, pure fire (without fuel), or lightning/meteor-fire — feed (by adding fuel), stir, break (split a burning log), kindle (by fanning), inflame more, or extinguish. Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अगणिं... उक्कं** (agaṇiṃ... ukkaṃ) = the eight forms of fire — fire (heat), embers (live coals), smouldering (dung-fire, ash-coals), arc/spark (separated flame-tip), flame (attached blaze), half-burnt brand/torch, pure fire (no fuel — e.g. red-hot iron), lightning/meteor
- **उज्जेज्जा... णिव्वाविज्जा** (ujjejjā... ṇivvāvijjā) = the six harmful actions — feed with fuel, stir/strike against, split/break, kindle by fanning, inflame more, extinguish

### 5. COMMENTARY

Fire is a *kaya* — a body — in Jain ontology. A flame is a colony of fire-beings. Both feeding and extinguishing fire are *himsa*: feeding because it propagates the fire-bodies into new fuel (which itself may contain other lives); extinguishing because it kills the fire-bodies directly. The monk thus has no relationship with fire at all — he neither cooks, lights lamps, nor puts out fires.

The eight forms of fire cover an exhaustive phenomenology — from the steady heat of red-hot metal to the lightning that streaks across the sky. Even a meteor is a fire-body. Even a single spark.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk vows: "I will not feed, stir, split, kindle, inflame, or extinguish any fire — coals, sparks, flame, brand, lightning. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To put out a fire is also to kill. What ethical situations in modern life resemble this — where the obvious "good" act is itself a form of harm?

## SUTRA 4.17 — Yatana Toward Vayu-kaya (Air-Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से सिएणं वा विहुयणेण वा तालियंटेण वा पत्तेण वा, पत्तभंगेण वा साहाए वा साहाभंगेण वा पिहुणेण वा पिहुणहत्थेण वा चेलेण वा चेलकण्णेण वा, हत्थेण वा मुहेण वा अप्पणो वा कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं, ण फुमिज्जा, ण वीएज्जा; अण्णं ण फुमावेज्जा, ण वीयावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain] ॥४.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.17 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā... se sieṇaṃ vā vihuyaṇeṇa vā tāliyaṃṭeṇa vā patteṇa vā, pattabhaṃgeṇa vā sāhāe vā sāhābhaṃgeṇa vā pihuṇeṇa vā pihuṇahattheṇa vā celeṇa vā celakaṇṇeṇa vā, hattheṇa vā muheṇa vā appaṇo vā kāyaṃ, bāhiraṃ vāvi puggalaṃ, ṇa phumijjā, ṇa vīejjā... ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not — with white chowrie, fan, palmyra-fan, leaf or piece of leaf, branch or piece of branch, peacock-feather or bunch of feathers, cloth or cloth-edge, hand or mouth — blow or fan, neither one's own body nor an outer object (to cool it). Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिएणं... मुहेण** (sieṇaṃ... muheṇa) = thirteen instruments of wind-making — chowrie (white), small fan, palmyra-fan, leaf, leaf-piece, branch, branch-piece, peacock-feather, bunch of feathers, cloth, cloth-edge, hand, mouth
- **अप्पणो वा कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं** (appaṇo vā kāyaṃ, bāhiraṃ vāvi puggalaṃ) = one's own body (to cool), or an outer pudgala (e.g., hot food, hot water)
- **फुमिज्जा, वीएज्जा** (phumijjā, vīejjā) = blow (mouth), fan (instrument)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Air is a *kaya*. The wind generated by waving a fan disturbs and kills air-bodies — and the violence of intent is the same whether one fans for one's own comfort (cooling a sweaty body) or for an outer object (cooling tea). Both are renounced.

This sutra is striking because it forbids the most natural thermoregulation gesture humans have — fanning oneself in heat. The Jain monk simply endures heat. He does not even blow on his hot food.

The list of thirteen wind-instruments is suggestive: from royal chowrie to a piece of cloth to one's own breath, every conceivable apparatus is named.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk vows: "I will not blow with my mouth or fan with chowrie, fan, leaf, branch, peacock-feather, cloth, hand — neither to cool myself nor to cool food or water. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

You fan yourself when hot. The wind is alive. What is your body asking for that the air does not consent to give?

## SUTRA 4.18 — Yatana Toward Vanaspati-kaya (Plant-Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से बीएसु वा बीयपइट्ठेसु वा रूढेसु वा रूढपइट्ठेसु वा जाएसु वा जायपइट्ठेसु वा हरिएसु वा हरियपइट्ठेसु वा छिण्णेसु वा छिण्णपइट्ठेसु वा सचित्तेसु वा सचित्तकोलपडिणिस्सिएसु वा, ण गच्छेज्जा ण चिट्ठेज्जा ण णिसीएज्जा ण तुयट्टेज्जा; अण्णं ण गच्छावेज्जा... [9-koti refrain] ॥४.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.18 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā... se bīesu vā bīyapaiṭṭhesu vā rūḍhesu vā rūḍhapaiṭṭhesu vā jāesu vā jāyapaiṭṭhesu vā hariesu vā hariyapaiṭṭhesu vā chiṇṇesu vā chiṇṇapaiṭṭhesu vā sacittesu vā sacittakolapaḍiṇissiesu vā, ṇa gacchejjā ṇa ciṭṭhejjā ṇa ṇisīejjā ṇa tuyaṭṭejjā... ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... shall not — on seeds or things resting on seeds, on sprouts or things on sprouts, on grown plants or things on grown plants, on green grass or things on green grass, on cut (still-living) plants or things on cut plants, on other living vegetation or on wood that hosts wood-worms — walk, stand, sit, or lie down. Nor cause another. Nor approve another. [9-koti refrain]

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बीएसु... सचित्तकोलपडिणिस्सिएसु** (bīesu... sacittakolapaḍiṇissiesu) = the twelve states of vegetation: seeds, things-on-seeds; sprouts, things-on-sprouts; grown clusters, things-on-clusters; green grass, things-on-green-grass; cut-but-still-living vegetation, things-on-such; other living vegetation; wood-with-wood-worms (hosting other living beings)
- **गच्छेज्जा... तुयट्टेज्जा** (gacchejjā... tuyaṭṭejjā) = the four bodily harms — walk on, stand on, sit on, lie on

### 5. COMMENTARY

Where earth, water, fire, air had instrument-based harms (scratching, squeezing, fanning), vegetation has *bodily-presence* harms — simply walking on grass crushes the green beings beneath the foot. The sutra recognizes this and lists the four most basic body postures: walking, standing, sitting, lying. To do any of these on living vegetation is *himsa*.

The twelve states cover the full life cycle of a plant — seed → sprout → grown plant → green grass — and add the crucial Jain observation that even *cut* vegetation may still host life (a freshly cut branch with leaves still green is still *sachit*), and that wood may host *kola* (wood-worms), so even old timber must be checked.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk vows: "I will not walk on, stand on, sit on, or lie down on seeds, sprouts, grown plants, green grass, cut-but-living vegetation, or wood with worms. Nor on anything resting on these. Nor have others do it. Nor approve. Lifelong, in all nine modes."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The lawn you walk across is alive. Every blade is a being. What does it mean to consent to walk?

## SUTRA 4.19 — Yatana Toward Trasa-kaya (Mobile Beings): The Rescue Procedure

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से भिक्खू वा भिक्खुणी वा... से कीडं वा पयंगं वा कुंथुं वा पिवीलियं वा हत्थंसि वा पायंसि वा बाहुंसि वा ऊरुंसि वा उदरंसि वा सीसंसि वा वत्थंसि वा पडिग्गहंसि वा कंबलंसि वा पायपुच्छणसि वा रयहरणंसि वा गोच्छगंसि वा उंडगंसि वा दंडगंसि वा पीढगंसि वा फलगंसि वा सेज्जंसि वा संथारगंसि वा अण्णयरंसि वा तहप्पगारे उवगरणजाए; तओ संजयामेव पडिलेहिय पडिलेहिय, पमज्जिय पमज्जिय, एगंतमवणेज्जा, णो णं संघायमावज्जेज्जा ॥४.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.19 ||
se bhikkhū vā bhikkhuṇī vā... se kīḍaṃ vā payaṃgaṃ vā kuṃthuṃ vā pivīliyaṃ vā hatthaṃsi vā pāyaṃsi vā bāhuṃsi vā ūruṃsi vā udaraṃsi vā sīsaṃsi vā vatthaṃsi vā paḍiggahaṃsi vā kaṃbalaṃsi vā pāyapucchaṇasi vā rayaharaṇaṃsi vā gocchagaṃsi vā uṃḍagaṃsi vā daṃḍagaṃsi vā pīḍhagaṃsi vā phalagaṃsi vā sejjaṃsi vā saṃthāragaṃsi vā aṇṇayaraṃsi vā tahappagāre uvagaraṇajāe; tao saṃjayāmeva paḍilehiya paḍilehiya, pamajjiya pamajjiya, egaṃtamavaṇejjā, ṇo ṇaṃ saṃghāyamāvajjejjā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That bhikshu or bhikshuni... — when an insect, moth, *kunthu* (a tiny creature), or ant is on the hand, foot, arm, thigh, belly, head; on clothing, alms-bowl, blanket, foot-wiper, *rajoharana* (whisk-broom), *gocchaka* (cleaning-brush), urinal-pot, staff, stool, plank, bed, or straw-mat; or on any other such monastic implement — should, restrained, observe carefully again and again, sweep gently again and again, and place (the creature) in a safe solitary spot. Should not crowd them together. Should not crush them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कीडं वा पयंगं वा कुंथुं वा पिवीलियं** (kīḍaṃ vā payaṃgaṃ vā kuṃthuṃ vā pivīliyaṃ) = four representative trasa beings — insect, moth, *kunthu* (tiny crawler), ant
- **हत्थंसि... सीसंसि** (hatthaṃsi... sīsaṃsi) = six bodily locations — hand, foot, arm, thigh, belly, head
- **वत्थंसि... संथारगंसि** (vatthaṃsi... saṃthāragaṃsi) = twelve monastic implements — cloth, alms-bowl, blanket, foot-wiper, whisk-broom, cleaning-brush, urinal-pot, staff, stool, plank, bed, straw-mat
- **पडिलेहिय पडिलेहिय, पमज्जिय पमज्जिय** (paḍilehiya paḍilehiya, pamajjiya pamajjiya) = observe again and again, sweep gently again and again (the doubled gerund signals utmost care)
- **एगंतमवणेज्जा** (egaṃtamavaṇejjā) = place in a solitary safe spot
- **संघायमावज्जेज्जा** (saṃghāyamāvajjejjā) = press together / crowd into a heap (causing pain)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The structure of this sutra is unique among the six. For earth, water, fire, air, and plants, the formula was *don't touch, don't damage, don't approve damage*. But trasa beings — sentient mobile creatures — *will themselves climb onto the monk*. The negative formula does not work; an active rescue procedure is required.

The procedure has four steps: (1) **paḍilehiya paḍilehiya** — look carefully, again and again (the doubling is technical: it signals deliberate, slow, mistake-free observation); (2) **pamajjiya pamajjiya** — sweep gently with the rajoharana, again and again, never with bare hand; (3) **egantam avaṇejjā** — place in a solitary spot where the creature can survive (not on the path, not in a crowd); (4) **no ṇaṃ saṃghāyam āvajjejjā** — do not crowd them together (which would be its own form of *himsa*).

The eighteen named locations — six bodily, twelve in the monastic kit — are exhaustive. Every place a *kunthu* might wander, the monk must inspect.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When an insect, moth, tiny creature, or ant climbs onto the monk's body or onto any of his implements — clothing, bowl, blanket, broom, staff, bed — he must look carefully (twice), sweep gently (twice), and place the creature in a solitary safe spot. He must not crush it, and he must not crowd many together (which itself causes pain).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The smallest creature that crawls onto your hand — what would it require of you to remove it without harm? Twice-looking, twice-sweeping, finding the safe place — what would your day look like if every act were performed at that pace?
