# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 7
# Suvakyashuddhi (સુવાક્ય શુદ્ધિ) — Purity of Speech

**Adhyayan 7 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 288–325 (PDF pages 348–385)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The seventh adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is named **Suvakyashuddhi** — "the purification of well-spoken words." If the previous chapter (Mahachara Katha) laid down the eighteen sites of monastic conduct, this chapter takes up one of those sites — speech — and gives it an entire treatise of its own. The reason is simple: of the three yogas (mind, speech, body), speech is the most public, the most easily corrupted, and the most damaging when it goes wrong. A monk's body can be still, the mind can be silent, but the moment the lips move, the world hears — and the karmic consequences follow instantly.

The chapter rests on a fourfold classification of all human language: **(1) Satya** (truth), **(2) Asatya** (falsehood), **(3) Mishra** (mixed truth-and-falsehood), and **(4) Vyavahara** (conventional / transactional speech, which is neither strictly true nor strictly false but operates in the world). Of these four, only two — Satya and Vyavahara — are *kalpaniya* (permissible) for a monk. The other two — Asatya and Mishra — must be wholly renounced. But even the two permissible categories must be handled with discrimination: a sentence that is technically true ("this man is one-eyed") may still be impermissible if it injures, while a sentence that is conventionally accepted ("we will go tomorrow") may still be impermissible if it asserts certainty about a future the speaker cannot guarantee.

The chapter is therefore not a prohibition against lying alone. It is a fine-grained science of *what counts as a beneficial sentence*. The sutrakar (Shayyambhavasvami) walks the practitioner through case after case: speech about persons (men, women, callings), about animals, plants, and natural objects; speech about the past, present, and future; speech under the pressure of anger, fear, greed, or jest; speech that predicts, that commands, that praises, that flatters, that mocks. Each case is examined for its potential to harm a being, to bind karma, to break the speaker's vow of ahimsa, or to undermine the speaker's credibility before the world.

The over-arching principle is **bhasha-samiti** — the careful, restrained use of speech, in which the monk speaks only when speaking is necessary, and even then chooses words that are *anavadya* (sinless), *akarkasha* (gentle), *samupreksha* (well-considered), and *asandigdha* (free from doubt). The deeper aim is **vag-gupti** — the silence of the disciplined sage, broken only when the welfare of self or other genuinely requires sound. Suvakyashuddhi is, ultimately, the discipline by which the soul learns that words are deeds, and that every word leaves a trace.

The chapter contains roughly 55-58 numbered gathas. The closing verses summarize the fruit of speech-purity: the practitioner who masters this becomes loved in the world, honoured by the wise, and — by extinguishing the karmas bound through reckless speech — moves rapidly toward liberation.

## SUTRA 7.1 — The Four Languages and the Wise Monk's Choice

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउण्हं खलु भासाणं, परिसंखाय पण्णवं ।
दोण्हं तु विणयं सिक्खे, दो न भासिज्ज सव्वसो ॥७.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.1 ||
cauṇhaṃ khalu bhāsāṇaṃ, parisaṃkhāya paṇṇavaṃ |
doṇhaṃ tu viṇayaṃ sikkhe, do na bhāsijja savvaso ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The wise monk, having fully understood the four (kinds of) languages, should learn the discipline of two of them and should not speak the (other) two at all.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पण्णवं** (paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise / discerning monk
- **चउण्हं खलु भासाणं** (cauṇhaṃ khalu bhāsāṇaṃ) = of the four kinds of language indeed
- **परिसंखाय** (parisaṃkhāya) = having thoroughly examined / fully comprehended
- **दोण्हं तु** (doṇhaṃ tu) = of the (other) two
- **विणयं सिक्खे** (viṇayaṃ sikkhe) = should learn the discipline / proper use
- **दो** (do) = two (kinds)
- **न भासिज्ज सव्वसो** (na bhāsijja savvaso) = should not speak at all / utterly renounce

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens by giving the monk a map. Speech, the sutrakar says, comes in only four flavours: **Satya** (true), **Asatya** (false), **Mishra** (mixed — partly true, partly false), and **Vyavahara** (conventional / transactional, which is neither true nor false in essence but operates by social agreement, e.g., greetings, instructions, directions). The monk's task is not to "tell the truth" in the naive sense — it is to discriminate which of these four serves liberation and which obstructs it.

Of the four, two must be wholly renounced: **Asatya** (whatever is contrary to fact, whether spoken from passion or carelessness) and **Mishra** (whatever blends truth with untruth — because the untruthful element contaminates the whole). The remaining two, Satya and Vyavahara, are permissible — but only when used with **vinaya**, the discipline of considered, gentle, beneficial speech. Mere permissibility is not enough; even a true sentence must be examined for its effect before it leaves the mouth.

This opening verse therefore establishes the entire architecture of the chapter. Everything that follows — the rules about predicting the future, about addressing women, about describing animals, about giving commands — is an unfolding of this single principle: the monk must *know* speech before he *uses* speech.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Speech comes in four kinds — true, false, mixed, and merely conventional. A wise monk learns how to use only the two safe kinds (true and conventional) with care, and completely gives up the other two (false and mixed).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Before your next sentence leaves your mouth, can you ask: which of the four is this — and is it worth saying?

## SUTRA 7.2 — Even True Speech May Be Unspeakable

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जा य सच्चा अवत्तव्वा, सच्चामोसा य जा मुसा ।
जा य बुद्धेहिं णाइण्णा, न तं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.2 ||
jā ya saccā avattavvā, saccāmosā ya jā musā |
jā ya buddhehiṃ ṇāiṇṇā, na taṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That (speech) which, though true, is unspeakable; that which is mixed (truth-and-falsehood) and that which is false; and that which has not been sanctioned by the Enlightened Ones — the wise monk should not speak any of these.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जा य सच्चा अवत्तव्वा** (jā ya saccā avattavvā) = the speech which, though true, is not fit to be spoken (because harmful, painful, or productive of sin)
- **सच्चामोसा य** (saccāmosā ya) = and the mixed (truth-and-falsehood) speech
- **जा मुसा** (jā musā) = and the false speech
- **जा य बुद्धेहिं णाइण्णा** (jā ya buddhehiṃ ṇāiṇṇā) = and that which has not been practised / sanctioned by the Tirthankaras
- **न तं भासिज्ज पण्णवं** (na taṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise monk should not speak it

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first sutra established that two of the four languages are renounced. This second sutra delivers the more subtle teaching: even within the *permissible* category of Satya, there are sentences that must not be spoken. **Avaktavya satya** — "the unspeakable truth" — is truth that, when uttered, causes pain, increases hatred, fuels passion, dishonours the worthy, or simply binds karma without serving any liberatory purpose. The classic example: "this man is one-eyed" — factually correct, but cruel; or "let us cut this tree for timber" — practically true, but it incites destruction of plant-bodied beings.

The category **buddhehim na ainna** ("not practised by the awakened") is a catch-all: any sentence form that the Tirthankaras have not sanctioned in scripture is to be avoided, even if the speaker cannot articulate exactly why. This places the monk under the authority of tradition — speech is not to be invented case by case but learned from the lineage.

The teaching is profound: truth itself does not justify speech. Speech is justified only when it is true *and* harmless *and* beneficial *and* sanctioned. Three of those four conditions can be satisfied by a sentence that nevertheless fails the fourth, and such a sentence must not be spoken.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A wise monk does not speak: (1) a truth that hurts, (2) a half-truth, (3) a lie, or (4) any sentence that the awakened teachers have not approved.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you spoke a true sentence that you should not have spoken? What was the cost?

## SUTRA 7.3 — The Four Qualities of Permissible Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असच्चमोसं सच्चं च, अणवज्जमकक्कसं ।
समुप्पेहमसंदिद्धं, गिरं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.3 ||
asaccamosaṃ saccaṃ ca, aṇavajjamakakkasaṃ |
samuppehamasaṃdiddhaṃ, giraṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The wise monk should speak speech that is conventional (neither false nor mixed) or true, that is sinless, gentle, well-considered, and unambiguous.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **असच्चमोसं** (asaccamosaṃ) = vyavahara — conventional/transactional speech, which is not the "false-mixed" category
- **सच्चं च** (saccaṃ ca) = and true (speech)
- **अणवज्जं** (aṇavajjaṃ) = sinless, free of fault — does not provoke any sinful activity
- **अकक्कसं** (akakkasaṃ) = not harsh; gentle, soft, mild
- **समुप्पेहं** (samuppehaṃ) = well-considered, examined before being spoken
- **असंदिद्धं** (asaṃdiddhaṃ) = unambiguous, free from doubt or confusion
- **गिरं** (giraṃ) = speech, words
- **भासिज्ज पण्णवं** (bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise (monk) should speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the operational core of the chapter — the four qualities (chatur-guna) that every spoken sentence must possess. **Anavadya** (sinless): the words must not provoke a sinful action in anyone, must not wound any heart. **Akarkasha** (gentle): not sharp, not stinging — soft to the ear and warm to the heart. **Samupreksha** (well-considered): the monk thinks before speaking — "what gain or loss will follow? is this fit for me to say? will it hurt the listener?" Only after this internal review does the sentence emerge. **Asandigdha** (unambiguous): clear, definite, conveying knowledge without confusion or double meaning.

These four qualities are like a sieve. Even within the two permissible categories (true and conventional), a sentence must pass through all four. If it is true but harsh — it fails. If it is conventional but ambiguous — it fails. Only the sentence that survives all four filters is fit for monastic utterance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk does speak, the words must be: true or conventional, harmless, gentle, thought-out beforehand, and absolutely clear.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the four filters — sinless, gentle, considered, unambiguous — which one do your words most often fail?

## SUTRA 7.4 — The Unclear Mixed Speech is Forbidden

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयं च अट्ठमण्णं वा, जं तु णामेइ सासयं ।
स भासं सच्चमोसं च, तंपि धीरो विवज्जए ॥७.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.4 ||
eyaṃ ca aṭṭhamaṇṇaṃ vā, jaṃ tu ṇāmei sāsayaṃ |
sa bhāsaṃ saccamosaṃ ca, taṃpi dhīro vivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That (speech) which obscures this meaning or another, which conceals the eternal/conclusive sense — that mixed (truth-and-falsehood) speech also the steadfast (monk) should completely avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एयं च अट्ठं** (eyaṃ ca aṭṭhaṃ) = this meaning (the topic at hand)
- **अण्णं वा** (aṇṇaṃ vā) = or another meaning
- **जं तु णामेइ** (jaṃ tu ṇāmei) = which (speech) makes one bend / obscures / fails to make clear
- **सासयं** (sāsayaṃ) = the conclusive/decisive sense; or the eternal (truth pertaining to liberation)
- **स भासं सच्चमोसं च** (sa bhāsaṃ saccamosaṃ ca) = that (kind of) mixed truth-and-falsehood speech
- **तंपि धीरो विवज्जए** (taṃpi dhīro vivajjae) = even that the steadfast monk completely renounces

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra targets a more sophisticated kind of impermissible speech: language that, though not outright false, fails to make its meaning clear. Two readings are possible: (1) speech whose terms are so ambiguous that the listener cannot reach a definite conclusion about the topic, and (2) speech that, even if locally true, contradicts or obscures the path of liberation taught by the Jinas.

The classic example given by commentators: someone says "*ashvattha hanyatam*" — which can mean either "kill the ashvattha tree" or "Ashvattha (a man's name) should be killed." Such double-edged sentences create confusion in the listener and karmic risk for the speaker. The monk's speech must be straight-grained: one meaning, one direction, one outcome.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Speech whose meaning is unclear, or that confuses people about the path to liberation, must be completely avoided — even if some part of it is true.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever said something true in a way deliberately ambiguous, hoping to keep your options open? What were you protecting?

## SUTRA 7.5 — Even Mistaken Speech Carries Sin

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वितहं पि तहामुत्तिं, जं गिरं भासए नरो ।
तम्हा सो पुट्ठो पावेण, किं पुणं जो मुसं वए ॥७.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.5 ||
vitahaṃ pi tahāmuttiṃ, jaṃ giraṃ bhāsae naro |
tamhā so puṭṭho pāveṇa, kiṃ puṇaṃ jo musaṃ vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The man who, mistakenly (taking one form for another), speaks of something that is otherwise as if it were so — even he is touched by sin. How much more, then, the one who speaks an outright lie?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **नरो** (naro) = the person, the monk
- **वितहं पि** (vitahaṃ pi) = even something whose actual nature is otherwise (e.g., a person disguised in another form)
- **तहामुत्तिं** (tahāmuttiṃ) = (calling it) according to the (apparent) form
- **जं गिरं भासए** (jaṃ giraṃ bhāsae) = whatever speech he utters
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **सो पुट्ठो पावेण** (so puṭṭho pāveṇa) = he is touched / smeared by sin
- **किं पुणं जो मुसं वए** (kiṃ puṇaṃ jo musaṃ vae) = how much more (sinful) the one who speaks an outright lie

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra introduces a striking principle: even *unintentional* falsehood carries karmic stain. The example: a woman dressed as a man passes by, and the monk, seeing the form, calls her "a man" — though the statement matches the visual evidence, it is factually false. The monk has been deceived by appearances, but the karmic consequence still attaches because false words have left the lips.

If unintentional falsehood already binds karma, then **deliberate** falsehood (musavaada) binds it incomparably more. The verse uses the rhetorical **kim punaṃ** ("how much more, then?") to drive home the gravity. This teaching expands the monk's vigilance from "do not lie" to "do not speak with certainty about anything you have not verified" — the foundation of the next several sutras on doubt and the future.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even speaking falsely by mistake — such as calling a disguised woman a man because of how she looks — earns sin. Outright lying earns vastly more.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do you speak with confidence about things you have only seen from one angle? Where might appearance be deceiving you right now?

## SUTRA 7.6 — Forbidden Speech About the Future

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तम्हा गच्छामो वक्खामो, अमुगं वा णं भविस्सइ ।
अहं वा णं करिस्सामि, एसो वा णं करिस्सइ ॥७.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.6 ||
tamhā gacchāmo vakkhāmo, amugaṃ vā ṇaṃ bhavissai |
ahaṃ vā ṇaṃ karissāmi, eso vā ṇaṃ karissai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Therefore, the kind of speech such as:) "We will go," "We will speak/explain," "Such-and-such will happen," "I will do this," "He will do this" —

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore (because of the karmic risk explained in the previous verse)
- **गच्छामो** (gacchāmo) = "we will go" (definite future, first-person plural)
- **वक्खामो** (vakkhāmo) = "we will explain / discourse"
- **अमुगं वा णं भविस्सइ** (amugaṃ vā ṇaṃ bhavissai) = "such-and-such will happen"
- **अहं वा णं करिस्सामि** (ahaṃ vā ṇaṃ karissāmi) = "I will do this"
- **एसो वा णं करिस्सइ** (eso vā ṇaṃ karissai) = "this person will do this"

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra (paired with 7.7) presents a list of categorically definite future-tense sentences that the monk must not speak. The reason is not that these statements are *currently* false, but that the future is unknown — circumstances change, illness intervenes, karma matures, and the speaker may not be able to fulfil what was asserted with certainty. To speak the future as if it were guaranteed is to cross from speech-discipline into a kind of arrogance that pretends to omniscience.

Only the Kevali (omniscient one) can speak the future with certainty. Every other being must hedge — "I intend to," "I will try to," "circumstances permitting" — because every other being is subject to change.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Saying things like "we will go," "I will do this," "he will do that" — flatly, without qualification — is forbidden because no one but the omniscient knows what tomorrow holds.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do you commit your future self to actions you cannot guarantee? What would your speech sound like if you spoke only what you can verify?

## SUTRA 7.7 — Doubtful Speech About Past, Present, or Future

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवमाइ उ जा भासा, एसकालम्मि संकिया ।
संपयाइयमट्ठे वा, तंपि धीरो विवज्जए ॥७.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.7 ||
evamāi u jā bhāsā, esakālammi saṃkiyā |
saṃpayāiyamaṭṭhe vā, taṃpi dhīro vivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Such speech and the like, when uncertain regarding the future, or regarding present and past matters — the steadfast monk should also avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवमाइ उ जा भासा** (evamāi u jā bhāsā) = such language and similar
- **एसकालम्मि संकिया** (esakālammi saṃkiyā) = doubtful with reference to future time
- **संपयाइयमट्ठे वा** (saṃpayāiyamaṭṭhe vā) = or regarding present and past matters
- **तंपि धीरो विवज्जए** (taṃpi dhīro vivajjae) = even that the steadfast (monk) should renounce

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verse listed the future-tense forms; this verse extends the prohibition: any sentence asserting certainty about a matter of which the speaker is genuinely uncertain — whether that matter lies in the past, present, or future — must be avoided. The discipline is one of epistemic honesty: the monk speaks only what he *knows*. Where knowledge is partial, language must reflect the partiality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Any definite statement about something uncertain — past, present, or future — must be avoided.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you do not know, can you say "I do not know" — without filling the gap with confident invention?

## SUTRA 7.8 — Do Not Assert What You Do Not Know

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए ।
जमट्ठं तु न जाणेज्जा, एवमेयंति णो वए ॥७.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.8 ||
aīyammi ya kālammi, paccuppaṇṇamaṇāgae |
jamaṭṭhaṃ tu na jāṇejjā, evameyaṃti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Regarding past, present, or future time — whatever matter the (monk) does not know, he should not say "this is so" about it.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अईयम्मि य कालम्मि** (aīyammi ya kālammi) = past time
- **पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए** (paccuppaṇṇamaṇāgae) = present and future
- **जमट्ठं तु न जाणेज्जा** (jamaṭṭhaṃ tu na jāṇejjā) = whatever matter (he) does not know
- **एवमेयंति णो वए** (evameyaṃti ṇo vae) = should not say "it is thus"

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the positive formulation of the previous prohibition. Not knowing is a perfectly honourable state for any being below the Kevali. The vice is not in not-knowing but in *speaking as if one knows*. The monk's tongue must mirror his actual cognition: certain when certain, silent or qualified when uncertain.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

About anything in past, present, or future that you do not actually know — do not say "it is like this."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What do you currently believe and assert with confidence that you have never actually verified?

## SUTRA 7.9 — Where Doubt Exists, Do Not Assert

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए ।
जत्थ संका भवे तं तु, एवमेयंति णो वए ॥७.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.9 ||
aīyammi ya kālammi, paccuppaṇṇamaṇāgae |
jattha saṃkā bhave taṃ tu, evameyaṃti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Regarding past, present, or future time — wherever there is doubt, the (monk) should not say "it is thus."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जत्थ** (jattha) = wherever (in whatever matter)
- **संका भवे** (saṃkā bhave) = doubt exists
- **तं तु एवमेयंति णो वए** (taṃ tu evameyaṃti ṇo vae) = he should not say "this is so" about it

### 5. COMMENTARY

Where 7.8 addressed *ignorance*, 7.9 addresses *doubt* — a slightly different state, in which the monk has some information but not enough to commit. The rule is the same: do not assert. Even partial information, if conveyed as definite, becomes a form of falsehood when the underlying reality turns out otherwise.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Wherever the monk is in doubt — past, present, or future — he must not declare "it is so."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Notice the difference between "I think" and "it is." The first is yours; the second pretends to be the world's.

## SUTRA 7.10 — Where Certainty Exists, Speak Definitely

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए ।
निस्संकियं भवे जं तु, एवमेयं तु णिद्दिसे ॥७.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.10 ||
aīyammi ya kālammi, paccuppaṇṇamaṇāgae |
nissaṃkiyaṃ bhave jaṃ tu, evameyaṃ tu ṇiddise ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Regarding past, present, or future time — whatever is free from doubt, that he should declare "it is thus."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **निस्संकियं भवे जं तु** (nissaṃkiyaṃ bhave jaṃ tu) = whatever is free from doubt
- **एवमेयं तु णिद्दिसे** (evameyaṃ tu ṇiddise) = that he should declare as "it is thus"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The four-verse sequence (7.7–7.10) closes with the positive principle: speech-discipline is not silence in the face of all matters but precision matched to certainty. Where the monk truly knows — by direct experience, scriptural authority, or unambiguous perception — he should speak clearly and definitely. The faults of definite speech (listed in commentary): (1) violation of bhasha-samiti, (2) loss of the Jina's command, (3) loss of the layfolk's faith if a confident statement turns out wrong, (4) decline of devotion to dharma, (5) belittlement of the order. To avoid these, the monk distinguishes precisely between "I know" and "I am not sure," and lets his sentence-form follow.

The chapter at this point also gives the practitioner a translation table of impermissible-into-permissible forms: instead of "I will go," say "I have the intention to go" or "I will try to go"; instead of "He will do this," say "He has the capacity to do this" or "He intends to do this." Speech becomes a mirror of inner knowledge rather than a projection of inner desire.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Where there is no doubt — past, present, or future — the monk may speak with definite certainty. Where there is doubt, he must hedge.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you let your sentences have the same shape as your knowledge — definite where you know, soft where you do not?

## SUTRA 7.11 — Even True Speech, If Harsh, Is Forbidden

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव फरुसा भासा, गुरुभूओवघाइणी ।
सच्चा वि सा न वत्तव्वा, जओ पावस्स आगमो ॥७.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.11 ||
taheva pharusā bhāsā, gurubhūovaghāiṇī |
saccā vi sā na vattavvā, jao pāvassa āgamo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, harsh speech that gravely injures (other) beings — though true, it is not to be spoken, because from it sin enters (the soul).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise
- **फरुसा भासा** (pharusā bhāsā) = harsh speech
- **गुरुभूओवघाइणी** (gurubhūovaghāiṇī) = severely injurious to (other) beings
- **सच्चा वि सा** (saccā vi sā) = even if true
- **न वत्तव्वा** (na vattavvā) = is not to be spoken
- **जओ पावस्स आगमो** (jao pāvassa āgamo) = because from it the influx of sin (occurs)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra explicitly drives home what 7.2 stated in principle: a true sentence is not automatically a permissible sentence. If the words are harsh — sharp-edged, cutting, designed or destined to wound — the truth-content does not redeem them. The reason is karmic: harsh speech is itself a sinful activity (savadya yoga), and the influx (asrava) of sin attaches the moment the words leave the mouth, regardless of factual accuracy.

This is one of the most ethically demanding teachings in the chapter. The world frequently rewards "telling it like it is"; Jainism explicitly forbids it when "it" wounds.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A harsh sentence that wounds another being must not be spoken — even if it happens to be true. Sin attaches the moment such words are uttered.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When have you used the truth as a weapon? What sin entered with the satisfaction of being right?

## SUTRA 7.12 — Do Not Name People by Their Defects

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव काणं काणे त्ति, पंडगं पंडगे त्ति वा ।
वाहियं वावि रोगि त्ति, तेणं चोरे त्ति णो वए ॥७.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.12 ||
taheva kāṇaṃ kāṇe tti, paṇḍagaṃ paṇḍage tti vā |
vāhiyaṃ vāvi rogi tti, teṇaṃ core tti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, do not address the one-eyed as "one-eyed," the eunuch as "eunuch," the sick as "diseased," the thief as "thief."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **काणं काणे त्ति** (kāṇaṃ kāṇe tti) = (do not call) the one-eyed person "one-eyed"
- **पंडगं पंडगे त्ति वा** (paṇḍagaṃ paṇḍage tti vā) = or the eunuch "eunuch"
- **वाहियं वावि रोगि त्ति** (vāhiyaṃ vāvi rogi tti) = or the diseased person "sick / diseased"
- **तेणं चोरे त्ति णो वए** (teṇaṃ core tti ṇo vae) = or the thief "thief" — do not (so) speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

A direct application of 7.11: each of these words is factually accurate but socially and personally wounding. The monk does not deny that the one-eyed person has one eye — he simply refuses to make that defect the form of address. To call a person "thief" turns a single past act into a permanent identity, and to call a person "diseased" reduces their humanity to their illness. The monk's mouth refuses to participate in such reduction.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even if someone is one-eyed, infertile, sick, or has stolen — the monk must never address them by these labels.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do you reduce a person to a single fact about them — their job, their illness, their failure — when you speak to or about them?

## SUTRA 7.13 — The Wise Monk Knows the Faults of Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एएणण्णेण अट्ठेण, परो जेणुवहम्मइ ।
आयारभावदोसण्णू, न तं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.13 ||
eeṇaṇṇeṇa aṭṭheṇa, paro jeṇuvahammai |
āyārabhāvadosaṇṇū, na taṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

By any other meaning of this kind by which another (being) is hurt — the monk who knows the faults of conduct (in speech), who is wise, should not speak it.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एएण अण्णेण अट्ठेण** (eeṇa aṇṇeṇa aṭṭheṇa) = by this or any other (similar) meaning
- **जेण परो उवहम्मइ** (jeṇa paro uvahammai) = by which another being is hurt
- **आयारभाव-दोसण्णू** (āyārabhāva-dosaṇṇū) = the knower of the faults that pertain to right conduct (in speech)
- **पण्णवं** (paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise (monk)
- **न तं भासिज्ज** (na taṃ bhāsijja) = should not speak it

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra closes the section on harsh and wounding speech with a generalization: not just the four labels listed (one-eyed, eunuch, sick, thief), but *any sentence by any name* that wounds another, the wise monk avoids. The two qualifying epithets — *acharbhava-dosannu* (knower of faults of speech-conduct) and *pannava* (wise) — establish that this discrimination is itself a developed faculty, learned through scripture, the guru's instruction, and constant self-watching. An ordinary intellect cannot make these distinctions; the monk must cultivate them.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Any other sentence — beyond the four listed — that hurts a being must equally be avoided by the wise monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Beyond obvious insults, what subtler turns of phrase do you use that nonetheless wound the person you address?

## SUTRA 7.14 — Forbidden Insulting Forms of Address

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव होले गोले त्ति, साणे वा वसुले त्ति य ।
दमए दुहए वा वि, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.14 ||
taheva hole gole tti, sāṇe vā vasule tti ya |
damae duhae vā vi, ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise (do not address anyone as): "Fool!" "Bastard!" "Dog!" or "Outcaste!" "Wretch!" "Beggar!" — the wise monk should never speak so.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **होले** (hole) = "O fool!"
- **गोले त्ति** (gole tti) = "O bastard / one born of an illicit union!"
- **साणे वा** (sāṇe vā) = "O dog!"
- **वसुले त्ति य** (vasule tti ya) = "O outcaste / vrshala!"
- **दमए** (damae) = "O destitute / pauper!"
- **दुहए वा वि** (duhae vā vi) = "O wretch / unfortunate!"
- **णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं** (ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise monk should not speak so

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having forbidden truthful-but-wounding labels in 7.12, the sutrakar now forbids derogatory forms of address that may not even be factually true — "fool," "bastard," "dog," etc. These are *apabda* (cuss-words, abusive epithets), the verbal equivalent of striking another being. They originate from kashaya (passion) and create more kashaya in both speaker and listener. The monk who has taken the great vow against violence cannot allow such words on his tongue.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must never address anyone as fool, bastard, dog, outcaste, beggar, wretch, or any such insulting term.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What names do you call others — even silently in your mind — that you would refuse to call them aloud?

## SUTRA 7.15-16 — Forbidden Familial Forms of Address to Women

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अज्जिए पज्जिए वा वि, अम्मो माउस्सियत्ति य ।
पिउस्सिए भाइणेज्ज त्ति, धूए नत्तुणिए त्ति य ॥७.१५॥

हले हले त्ति अण्णे त्ति, भट्टे सामिणि गोमिणि ।
होले गोले वसुले त्ति, इत्थियं णेवमालवे ॥७.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.15-16 ||
ajjie pajjie vā vi, ammo māussiyatti ya |
piussie bhāiṇejja tti, dhūe nattuṇie tti ya ||7.15||

hale hale tti aṇṇe tti, bhaṭṭe sāmiṇi gomiṇi |
hole gole vasule tti, itthiyaṃ ṇevamālave ||7.16||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Do not address a woman as): "Grandmother!" "Great-grandmother!" "Mother!" "Maternal aunt!" "Paternal aunt!" "Niece!" "Daughter!" "Granddaughter!" — nor as "Hey!" "Hey you!" "O dear one (of low birth)!" "Madam!" "Mistress!" "Cattle-keeper's wife!" — nor as "Fool!" "Bastard!" "Outcaste!" — the monk should not call a woman by such terms.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अज्जिए, पज्जिए** = grandmother, great-grandmother
- **अम्मो** = mother
- **माउस्सिय** = maternal aunt
- **पिउस्सिए** = paternal aunt
- **भाइणेज्ज त्ति** = niece (sister's daughter)
- **धूए** = daughter
- **नत्तुणिए** = granddaughter
- **हले हले त्ति** = "Hey! Hey!" (familiar / casual)
- **अण्णे त्ति** = "O dear one" (often used for women of low birth)
- **भट्टे, सामिणि, गोमिणि** = "Madam," "Mistress," "Cattle-keeper's wife" — terms of social subordination/dominance
- **होले, गोले, वसुले** = abusive: fool, bastard, outcaste
- **इत्थियं णेवमालवे** = the monk should not address a woman so

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two paired verses cover a wide range of forbidden modes of addressing women. The first verse (7.15) lists *familial* terms — "mother," "aunt," "daughter," "granddaughter" — which a household member would naturally use, but which the monk must not, because such usage signals worldly attachment, raga, and the persistence of family-bonds in a heart that has formally renounced them. The second verse (7.16) lists *casual or commanding* terms ("hey!", "madam," "mistress") and *abusive* terms ("fool," "bastard"). All are forbidden for different reasons: the casual terms are intimate and familiar in a way unbecoming of a celibate; the commanding terms presume authority over the listener; the abusive terms wound.

The principle: the monk's address to any woman must be neither familiar (which suggests attachment), nor authoritative (which suggests hierarchy), nor abusive (which suggests passion). What remains is the neutral, name-or-gotra-based mode prescribed in the next verse.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not call a woman by family terms (mother, daughter, aunt), by casual or commanding terms (hey, madam, mistress), or by insults (fool, bastard, outcaste).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The forms of address you choose reveal your inner stance. Are your words to others coloured by attachment, by dominance, or by contempt?

## SUTRA 7.17 — The Permitted Way to Address a Woman

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

नामधिज्जेण णं बूया, इत्थीगोत्तेण वा पुणो ।
जहारिहमभिगिज्झ, आलविज्ज लविज्ज वा ॥७.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.17 ||
nāmadhijjeṇa ṇaṃ būyā, itthīgottena vā puṇo |
jahārihamabhigijjha, ālavijja lavijja vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monk) should address (a woman) by her (personal) name, or by her gotra (family-lineage name), or — having considered (her) suitability appropriately — should call her once or call her repeatedly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **नामधिज्जेण णं बूया** (nāmadhijjeṇa ṇaṃ būyā) = should address (her) by her (personal) name
- **इत्थीगोत्तेण वा पुणो** (itthīgottena vā puṇo) = or by her gotra/family-lineage name
- **जहारिहं अभिगिज्झ** (jahārihaṃ abhigijjha) = having grasped what is appropriate (to time, place, person)
- **आलविज्ज लविज्ज वा** (ālavijja lavijja vā) = should call her once, or call her repeatedly (as needed)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The positive prescription: when a monk must address a woman (e.g., during alms-collection or instruction), he should use her name or her family-lineage (gotra) name — neutral, respectful, free from familial attachment or social hierarchy. If neither is known, the *jaharihamabhigijjha* clause permits a context-appropriate form ("O lay-sister," "O Shravika," "O dharma-sheela") chosen with care. The address may be uttered once or multiple times depending on the practical need to draw attention.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The right way for a monk to address a woman is by her personal name or her family-lineage name, or — failing that — by some respectful, situation-appropriate term, used as often as needed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The neutrality of address is itself a form of respect. Do your words honour the person, or do they pull her into your private map?

## SUTRA 7.20 — The Permitted Way to Address a Man

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

नामधिज्जेण णं बूया, पुरिसगोत्तेण वा पुणो ।
जहारिहमभिगिज्झ, आलविज्ज लविज्ज वा ॥७.२०॥

|| 7.20 ||
nāmadhijjeṇa ṇaṃ būyā, purisagotteṇa vā puṇo |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monk) should address (a man) by his (personal) name, or by his gotra; having considered what is appropriate, should call him once, or repeatedly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **नामधिज्जेण** = by personal name
- **पुरिसगोत्तेण वा पुणो** = or by his (men's) gotra
- (Rest as in 7.17)

### 5. COMMENTARY

A monk addresses any man by his personal name, his lineage-name, or a respectful situation-appropriate form.

The simplest address — the person's own name — is also the most respectful. Why does the mind so often reach for something more familiar, more clever, or more diminishing?

## SUTRA 7.21 — Speech About Sex and Pancendriya Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पंचिंदियाण पाणाणं, एस इत्थी अयं पुमं ।
जाव णं न वियाणेज्जा, ताव जाइ त्ति आलवे ॥७.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.21 ||
paṃciṃdiyāṇa pāṇāṇaṃ, esa itthī ayaṃ pumaṃ |
jāva ṇaṃ na viyāṇejjā, tāva jāi tti ālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Regarding five-sensed beings: until one definitely knows "this is female, this is male," until then one should speak only by reference to the species/type.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पंचिंदियाण पाणाणं** (paṃciṃdiyāṇa pāṇāṇaṃ) = regarding five-sensed beings (humans and animals)
- **एस इत्थी अयं पुमं** (esa itthī ayaṃ pumaṃ) = "this is female, this is male"
- **जाव ण वियाणेज्जा** (jāva ṇa viyāṇejjā) = until (the monk) knows for certain
- **ताव जाइ त्ति आलवे** (tāva jāi tti ālave) = until then he should speak only by jati (species/type)

### 5. COMMENTARY

When the monk encounters an animal (or any five-sensed being other than a clearly identified human), he must not assign sex unless it is definitely known. To say "this dog is a female" or "this cow is male" without certainty is to assert what one does not know — the same fault as 7.8 and 7.9. The corrective: refer to the being by its species ("a dog," "a cow," "a bird") rather than guessing its sex. The principle applies equally to humans whose sex is not visually clear (e.g., a person in disguise, or seen at a distance).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

About any five-sensed being whose sex you do not definitely know, just call it by its species-name — not "male" or "female."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How quickly do you assign labels (gender, role, status) to beings you have only just glimpsed?

## SUTRA 7.22 — Do Not Describe Animals as Fit for Slaughter

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव मणुस्सं पसुं, पक्खिं वा वि सरीसवं ।
थूले पमेइले वज्झे, पाइमे त्ति य णो वए ॥७.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.22 ||
taheva maṇussaṃ pasuṃ, pakkhiṃ vā vi sarīsavaṃ |
thūle pameile vajjhe, pāime tti ya ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, seeing a human, a beast, a bird, or a reptile, (the monk) should not say "this is fat," "this is well-fleshed," "this is fit for killing," "this is fit for cooking."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** = likewise
- **मणुस्सं पसुं पक्खिं वा वि सरीसवं** = a human, beast, bird, or reptile/snake
- **थूले** = "this is fat / stout"
- **पमेइले** = "this has good flesh / is well-fleshed"
- **वज्झे** = "this is fit to be killed"
- **पाइमे त्ति** = "this is fit to be cooked"
- **णो वए** = should not speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most ethically advanced teachings in the chapter. The monk does not directly kill the animal; but if his casual remark — "what a fat cow!" — falls on the ear of a butcher, the monk has supplied the butcher with motive. Speech that *enables* violence is itself a form of violence. The monk's mouth must not become an instrument by which slaughter is suggested, even unintentionally.

The four words listed escalate in complicity: "fat" (an admiring observation), "well-fleshed" (an evaluation in food-terms), "fit to kill" (an explicit endorsement), "fit to cook" (anticipation of the killed product). All four are forbidden. The monk who sees a fat cow either says nothing or speaks of the animal in some other respect entirely.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk sees any animal, he must not say "it is fat," "it has good meat," "it is fit to kill," or "it is fit to cook" — such words can incite violence even when the speaker does not intend it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What casual remarks of yours about animals, food, or vulnerable beings might be enabling harm you would never directly cause?

## SUTRA 7.23 — Forbidden Speech About Mature Animals

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

परिवुड्ढे त्ति णं बूया, बूया उवचिए त्ति य ।
संजाए पीणिए वा वि, महाकाए त्ति आलवे ॥७.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.23 ||
parivuḍḍhe tti ṇaṃ būyā, būyā uvacie tti ya |
saṃjāe pīṇie vā vi, mahākāe tti ālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Of an animal) one should say "fully grown" / "well developed" / "mature" / "well nourished" / "of large body" (rather than the slaughter-suggesting terms forbidden in the previous verse).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **परिवुड्ढे त्ति णं बूया** (parivuḍḍhe tti ṇaṃ būyā) = should say "fully grown / well-grown"
- **बूया उवचिए त्ति य** (būyā uvacie tti ya) = should say "well-developed"
- **संजाए पीणिए वा वि** (saṃjāe pīṇie vā vi) = "mature" or "well-nourished"
- **महाकाए त्ति आलवे** (mahākāe tti ālave) = should call (it) "of large body"

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the positive complement to 7.22. The monk *may* describe the animal — these words ("fully grown," "well-nourished," "of large body") are factually similar to the forbidden ones, but they are framed in terms of the animal's own well-being and natural development rather than in terms of its usefulness to a killer or cook. The discrimination is fine but real: "fat" implies food; "well-grown" implies a being that has flourished. Same animal, two different worlds.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk does describe an animal, he should use words like "fully grown," "well-developed," "mature," "well-nourished," or "of large body" — words about the animal's own wellbeing, not about its meat-value.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The same observation can be framed in two opposite directions. Toward which direction does your default vocabulary lean?

## SUTRA 7.24 — Do Not Describe Cattle as Useful for Labour

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव गाओ दुज्झाओ, दम्मा गोरहग त्ति य ।
वाहिमा रहजोगि त्ति, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.24 ||
taheva gāo dujjhāo, dammā gorahaga tti ya |
vāhimā rahajogi tti, ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, "this cow is fit for milking," "these are bullocks fit to be tamed," "this is fit for carrying loads," "this is fit to be yoked to a chariot" — the wise monk should not so speak.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गाओ दुज्झाओ** (gāo dujjhāo) = "this cow is fit for milking"
- **दम्मा** (dammā) = "fit to be tamed/broken"
- **गोरहग त्ति य** (gorahaga tti ya) = "this is a bull / bullock / calf (fit for use)"
- **वाहिमा** (vāhimā) = "fit for carrying loads"
- **रहजोगि त्ति** (rahajogi tti) = "fit to be yoked to a chariot"
- **णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं** (ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ) = the wise monk should not speak so

### 5. COMMENTARY

The same principle as 7.22, applied to draught animals. To say "this cow is good for milking" or "this bull can pull a cart" sounds harmless, but it converts the animal into a tool, and supplies prospective users with the recommendation. The monk neither owns animals nor uses them — and his speech must reflect that distance from the economy of exploitation. Such evaluative language is *savadya bhasha* (sin-bearing speech), and the wise monk avoids it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not say "this cow gives good milk," "these bulls can be trained," "this animal can carry loads," "this one can pull a chariot" — such words make the animal into a tool.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often is your praise of another being actually praise of their usefulness to you?

## SUTRA 7.25 — Permitted Descriptions of Cattle

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जुवं गवे त्ति णं बूया, धेणुं रसदय त्ति य ।
रहस्से महल्लए वा वि, वए संवहणे त्ति य ॥७.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.25 ||
juvaṃ gave tti ṇaṃ būyā, dhenuṃ rasadaya tti ya |
rahasse mahallae vā vi, vae saṃvahaṇe tti ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Of cattle) one should say "this is a young bullock," "this is a milch-cow / juice-yielding cow," "this is small," "this is large," "this is mature," "this is sturdy."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जुवं गवे त्ति णं बूया** = should say "this bullock is young / a youth"
- **धेणुं रसदय त्ति य** = "this is a milch-cow / a cow that gives nourishing juice (milk)"
- **रहस्से** = "this is small / young"
- **महल्लए वा वि** = "or this is a large / great bull"
- **वए संवहणे त्ति य** = "this is mature / sturdy / capable of bearing"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The positive complement to 7.24. The monk may describe cattle by their *natural state* — young, mature, large, small, sturdy — but not by their *utility-to-humans*. The line is fine but consequential: "this cow gives good milk" speaks from the dairy-farmer's frame; "this is a milch-cow" is closer to the cow's own form. The Gujarati commentary stresses: the meaning of these neutral words must be understood from the explanations already given for the previous sutras.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk may say of cattle: "this is a young bull," "this is a milch-cow," "this is small / large / mature / sturdy" — words about their nature, not their usefulness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Speaking of beings as they are — rather than as we want to use them — is itself a form of non-violence. Where does your speech default to the economic frame?

## SUTRA 7.26-29 — Forbidden Speech About Trees

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव गंतुमुज्जाणं, पव्वयाणि वणाणि य ।
रुक्खा महल्ल पेहाए, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.२६॥

अलं पासायखंभाणं, तोरणाणं गिहाण य ।
फलिहग्गलणावाणं, अलं उदगदोणिणं ॥७.२७॥

पीढए चंगबेरे य, णंगले मइयं सिया ।
जंतलट्ठी व नाभी वा, गंडिया व अलं सिया ॥७.२८॥

आसणं सयणं जाणं, होज्जा वा किंचुवस्सए ।
भूओवघाइणिं भासं, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.26-29 ||
taheva gaṃtumujjāṇaṃ, pavvayāṇi vaṇāṇi ya |
rukkhā mahalla pehāe, ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||7.26||

alaṃ pāsāyakhaṃbhāṇaṃ, toraṇāṇaṃ gihāṇa ya |
phalihaggalaṇāvāṇaṃ, alaṃ udagadoṇiṇaṃ ||7.27||

pīḍhae caṃgabere ya, ṇaṃgale maiyaṃ siyā |
jaṃtalaṭṭhī va nābhī vā, gaṃḍiyā va alaṃ siyā ||7.28||

āsaṇaṃ sayaṇaṃ jāṇaṃ, hojjā vā kiṃcuvassae |
bhūovaghāiṇiṃ bhāsaṃ, ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||7.29||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, having gone to a garden, mountains, or forests and seen great trees, the wise monk should not speak (thus): "(These are) fit for palace pillars, fit for arches and houses, fit for door-bolts and boats, fit for water-troughs; fit for stools, basins, plough-handles or harrows; fit for press-poles, axles, anvils — fit for seats, beds, vehicles, or any (need) of the lodging." Such being-injurious speech the wise monk should not speak.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उज्जाणं** = garden / orchard
- **पव्वयाणि वणाणि** = mountains and forests
- **रुक्खा महल्ल पेहाए** = having seen large trees
- **पासायखंभाणं** = (fit) for palace-pillars
- **तोरणाणं गिहाण** = arches (of city gates) and houses
- **फलिहग्गल** = door-bolts
- **णावाणं** = boats
- **उदगदोणिणं** = wooden water-troughs / large water vessels
- **पीढए** = stools
- **चंगबेरे** = bowls / basins
- **णंगले** = ploughs
- **मइयं** = harrow (for levelling fields)
- **जंतलट्ठी** = oil-press / mill-pole
- **नाभी** = wheel-hubs / axles
- **गंडिया** = goldsmith's anvil
- **आसणं सयणं जाणं** = seats, beds, vehicles
- **उवस्सए** = at the lodging-place
- **भूओवघाइणिं भासं** = being-injurious speech

### 5. COMMENTARY

This four-verse sequence (7.26-29) covers a single theme: the monk's response to trees. Walking through forests and gardens, he will inevitably see grand trees. The temptation is to admire them in functional terms — "what fine timber that would make!" The sutrakar prohibits this entirely. To say "this tree would make a great pillar" is verbal enabling of arboricide — even if the monk himself never lifts an axe, his words supply the woodcutter with motive.

The list (palace pillars, arches, doors, boats, water-troughs, stools, basins, ploughs, harrows, mill-poles, axles, anvils, seats, beds, vehicles, monastic-quarter fittings) is exhaustive precisely to forestall every category of utilitarian appraisal. *Bhutopaghatini bhasha* — "speech that injures beings" — is the technical name for this category, because plants are *ekendriya jiva* (one-sensed beings) whose lives are extinguished by every felling. The monk's tongue must not become an instrument of even indirect violence to them.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk sees great trees in gardens, mountains, or forests, he must never describe them in terms of what they could be turned into — pillars, beams, doors, boats, troughs, stools, ploughs, axles, anvils, seats, beds, vehicles — because such talk encourages their destruction.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you look at a forest, what does your mind first see — the trees, or the timber?

## SUTRA 7.30-31 — Permitted Speech About Trees

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव गंतुमुज्जाणं, पव्वयाणि वणाणि य ।
रुक्खा महल्ल पेहाए, एवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.३०॥

जाइमंता इमे रुक्खा, दीहवट्टा महालया ।
पयायसाला विडिमा, वए दरिसणि त्ति य ॥७.३१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.30-31 ||
taheva gaṃtumujjāṇaṃ, pavvayāṇi vaṇāṇi ya |
rukkhā mahalla pehāe, evaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||7.30||

jāimaṃtā ime rukkhā, dīhavaṭṭā mahālayā |
payāyasālā viḍimā, vae darisaṇi tti ya ||7.31||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, having gone to a garden, mountains, or forests and seen great trees, the wise monk should speak (thus): "These trees are of noble species, tall and rounded, large; spreading-branched, full of small twigs, beautiful to behold."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जाइमंता इमे रुक्खा** = these trees are of noble species
- **दीह** = tall, long
- **वट्टा** = round-shaped (with broad trunk)
- **महालया** = grand / extensive
- **पयायसाला** = with widely spread branches
- **विडिमा** = with small twigs / sub-branches
- **दरिसणि त्ति** = "they are beautiful to look at"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The positive form: when the monk must speak of trees, he describes them in their *own glory* — noble species, tall, round, vast, spreading, branching, beautiful. This vocabulary praises the tree as a tree, not as potential lumber. The discrimination preserves the tree's dignity in language and removes any incitement to harm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk does describe trees, he may say: "noble species," "tall and round," "great," "spreading-branched," "thick with twigs," "beautiful to behold."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To name a thing by its own beauty rather than its usefulness — what would change if you tried this with everyone you met today?

## SUTRA 7.32 — Forbidden Speech About Fruits

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहा फलाइं पक्काइं, पायखज्जाइं णो वए ।
वेलोइयाइं टालाइं, वेहिमाइत्ति णो वए ॥७.३२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.32 ||
tahā phalāiṃ pakkāiṃ, pāyakhajjāiṃ ṇo vae |
veloiyāiṃ ṭālāiṃ, vehimāitti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, (the monk) should not say of fruits: "they are ripe," "they are fit to be cooked and eaten," "they are over-ripe (and so fit to be plucked)," "they are tender (without hard pits)," "they are fit to be cut in two / fit to be processed."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **फलाइं पक्काइं** = "the fruits are ripe"
- **पायखज्जाइं** = "fit to be cooked and eaten"
- **वेलोइयाइं** = "they are very ripe (and must be plucked before they spoil)"
- **टालाइं** = "they are tender, without hard inner stones"
- **वेहिमाइत्ति** = "they are fit to be split / processed"
- **णो वए** = should not so speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

The same principle as the trees, applied to their fruits. To pronounce "these mangoes are ripe" or "these are ready to be cooked" is to invite plucking and processing — and plucking a fruit is itself violence (even if the fruit is destined to fall, the act of plucking ends the fruit's natural completion). The monk avoids all evaluative language that would supply such motive.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not say of fruits: "they are ripe," "fit to cook and eat," "over-ripe so pluck them," "tender (no stone)," or "fit to cut and process."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The vocabulary of harvest assumes the harvester. What would silent observation of a fruit-laden tree be like?

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असंथडा इमे अंबा, बहुणिव्वडिमा फला ।
वएज्ज बहुसंभूया, भूयरूव त्ति वा पुणो ॥७.३३॥

|| 7.33 ||
asaṃthaḍā ime aṃbā, bahuṇivvaḍimā phalā |
vaejja bahusaṃbhūyā, bhūyarūva tti vā puṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(One should say:) "These mango-trees are unable (to bear their load), the fruits are clustered in many bunches" — should say "they are abundantly fruited," "they are beautiful in many forms."

Positive form: the monk may admire the fruit-tree as a flourishing being — "these mangoes are so heavy with fruit the branches cannot bear it"; "the fruit grows in abundance"; "they look beautiful." The tree is praised for its own being, not for what can be taken from it.

## SUTRA 7.34 — Forbidden Speech About Crops

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेवोसहिओ पक्काओ, णीलियाओ छवीइय ।
लाइमा भज्जिमाओ त्ति, पिहुखज्ज त्ति णो वए ॥७.३४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.34 ||
tahevosahio pakkāo, ṇīliyāo chavīiya |
lāimā bhajjimāo tti, pihukhajja tti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, (of grains and crops) the monk should not say: "they are ripe," "they are green / soft-skinned," "they are fit to be reaped," "they are fit to be roasted in a pan," "they are fit to be parched (and eaten as poha)."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ओसहिओ पक्काओ** = "the grains/crops have ripened"
- **णीलियाओ** = "they are still green, fresh-coloured"
- **छवीइय** = "having pods / sheaths"
- **लाइमा** = "fit to be reaped / harvested"
- **भज्जिमाओ त्ति** = "fit to be parched in a pan"
- **पिहुखज्ज त्ति** = "fit to be made into puffed rice / eaten roasted"
- **णो वए** = should not say

### 5. COMMENTARY

The same teaching applied to grains and crops. Each phrase forbidden is one that would prompt the householder to begin a violent activity — reaping, threshing, parching, processing — that destroys the lives in the grain (which are *ekendriya jiva*) and the many small lives caught up in agricultural work. The monk's speech must remain neutral.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not say of crops: "they are ripe," "still green," "in pod," "fit to harvest," "fit to roast," "fit to parch into poha."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of what you call "neutral observation" is actually quiet endorsement?

## SUTRA 7.35 — Permitted Speech About Crops

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रूढा बहुसंभूया, थिरा ऊसढा वि य ।
गब्भियाओ पसूयाओ, ससाराओ त्ति आलवे ॥७.३५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.35 ||
rūḍhā bahusaṃbhūyā, thirā ūsaḍhā vi ya |
gabbhiyāo pasūyāo, sasārāo tti ālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monk) may say: "they have sprouted," "they are abundantly grown," "they have become firm," "they have risen up," "they are bearing within (in seed)," "they have brought forth," "they have come to fruition (full-grained)."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

The seven stages of plant growth (per the commentary):
- **रूढा** (rūḍhā) = sprouted (after seed is sown, the first shoot emerges)
- **बहुसंभूया** (bahusaṃbhūyā) = abundantly grown — the first tender leaves appear
- **थिरा** (thirā) = firm, with stable roots
- **ऊसढा** (ūsaḍhā) = grown upward, the stalk has risen
- **गब्भियाओ** (gabbhiyāo) = bearing the grain within, but not yet emerged
- **पसूयाओ** (pasūyāo) = brought forth / the grain has emerged from the sheath
- **ससाराओ** (sasārāo) = come to full essence — the full-grained, mature crop

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most botanically precise verses in the chapter. The sutrakar gives the monk a seven-stage vocabulary for the natural development of a crop, all of which is permissible because none of it suggests harvest or use. Each word names a phase of *the plant's own life-process*, not a phase of human exploitation. The monk who must speak of crops uses these terms.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk may describe crops in seven natural stages: just sprouted, well-grown, firm, risen up, bearing grain within, having brought forth grain, full-grained — all words about the plant's own development.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A vocabulary that names what *is* — not what can be *taken* — is itself a discipline of seeing.

## SUTRA 7.36 — Do Not Praise Feasts, Slaughter, or River-Crossings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव संखडिं णच्चा, किच्चं कज्जं ति णो वए ।
तेणगं वावि वज्झे त्ति, सुतित्थ त्ति य आवगा ॥७.३६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.36 ||
taheva saṃkhaḍiṃ ṇaccā, kiccaṃ kajjaṃ ti ṇo vae |
teṇagaṃ vāvi vajjhe tti, sutittha tti ya āvagā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, knowing of a feast (sankhadi), the monk should not say "this is a worthy / proper / meritorious act"; nor of a (caught) thief should he say "he is fit to be killed"; nor of a river should he say "this is a good ford."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संखडिं णच्चा** (saṃkhaḍiṃ ṇaccā) = knowing (of) a sankhadi — a large feast or congregation where food is prepared on a great scale (wedding, shraddha, funeral feast, etc.)
- **किच्चं कज्जं ति णो वए** = should not say "this is a worthy/meritorious act"
- **तेणगं वावि वज्झे त्ति** = (seeing) a thief, should not say "he is fit to be killed"
- **सुतित्थ त्ति य आवगा** = nor of a river should say "this is a good ford / easy crossing"

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three diverse cases joined by a single principle: the monk's praise creates karmic complicity. (1) A *sankhadi* (mass feast) involves great violence — large-scale cooking, milling of grain, killing of countless small lives in fire and water-use. To praise it is to endorse the violence. (2) Of a captured thief, to say "he deserves to die" is to endorse capital violence. (3) Of a river, to say "this is a beautiful ford to cross" is to encourage water-borne lives' disturbance and to endorse activity (crossing) that may not be necessary.

The monk's silence around these matters is not callousness — it is refusal to lend the moral weight of his words to acts of violence, however socially normalized.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not call a feast "a worthy event," a thief "fit to be killed," or a river "a fine crossing point" — each such phrase fuels violence.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do you offer verbal blessing to events whose underlying violence you would not personally inflict?

## SUTRA 7.37 — Permitted Neutral Speech in Such Cases

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संखडिं संखडिं बूया, पणियट्ठ त्ति तेणगं ।
बहुसमाणि तित्थाणि, आवगाणं वियागरे ॥७.३७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.37 ||
saṃkhaḍiṃ saṃkhaḍiṃ būyā, paṇiyaṭṭha tti teṇagaṃ |
bahusamāṇi titthāni, āvagāṇaṃ viyāgare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(If he must speak,) the monk should call a feast "a feast," a thief "one who has staked his life (for goods)"; should describe river-banks as "very level."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संखडिं संखडिं बूया** = should call a feast simply "a feast" (descriptive, neutral)
- **पणियट्ठ त्ति तेणगं** = should call a thief "one who has gambled his life for wealth" (paniyatth — one who has put his life on the line)
- **बहुसमाणि तित्थाणि** = "the fords are very level / even"
- **आवगाणं वियागरे** = describes (rivers' fords) reflectively

### 5. COMMENTARY

The neutral substitutes given here preserve communicability without endorsement. *Sankhadi* called *sankhadi* — a flat description, no praise. The thief is named not by the moral verdict ("villain," "deserves death") but by the existential fact — *paniyattha*, "one who has wagered his life for goods" — which is more compassionate, more accurate, and morally non-prescriptive. The river-bank is called "very level" — descriptive, without inviting or recommending the crossing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk must mention these things, he should call a feast simply "a feast," a thief "one who staked his life for goods," and a river-bank "very level" — neutral words without judgment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To name without endorsing or condemning is itself a high art. Where do you most often add the unnecessary verdict?

## SUTRA 7.38 — Do Not Speak Casually of Rivers

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहा णईओ पुण्णाओ, कायतिज्ज त्ति णो वए ।
णावाहिं तारिमाओ त्ति, पाणिपेज्ज त्ति णो वए ॥७.३८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.38 ||
tahā ṇaīo puṇṇāo, kāyatijja tti ṇo vae |
ṇāvāhiṃ tārimāo tti, pāṇipejja tti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, of rivers (the monk) should not say: "they are full," "they can be crossed by the body / swimming," "they can be crossed by boat," "their water can be drunk from the bank."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णईओ पुण्णाओ** = "the rivers are full of water"
- **कायतिज्ज त्ति** = "they can be swum across with the body"
- **णावाहिं तारिमाओ त्ति** = "they can be crossed by boat"
- **पाणिपेज्ज त्ति** = "their water is fit for drinking by hand from the bank"
- **णो वए** = should not say

### 5. COMMENTARY

Each of these four phrases is in itself accurate, but each contains an implicit endorsement of an activity that injures water-bodied beings (*apkaya jiva*). To say "this river is full" prompts crossing; to say "swimmable" or "boat-crossable" actively recommends the means; to say "drinkable from the bank" recommends consuming raw, sentient water. The monk's tongue must not give any of these recommendations.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not say of rivers: "they are full," "swimmable," "boat-crossable," or "the water can be drunk from the bank."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The endorsement of harm hides inside the most ordinary descriptive sentences. What "neutral" sentences of yours actually carry recommendations?

|| 7.39 ||

## SUTRA 7.40 — Do Not Endorse Sinful Activities

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव सावज्जं जोगं, परस्सट्ठाए णिट्ठियं ।
कीरमाणं ति वा णच्चा, सावज्जं ण लवे मुणी ॥७.४०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.40 ||
taheva sāvajjaṃ jogaṃ, parassaṭṭhāe ṇiṭṭhiyaṃ |
kīramāṇaṃ ti vā ṇaccā, sāvajjaṃ ṇa lave muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, knowing that a sinful activity has been completed for another's sake, or is being performed (now), the monk should not utter sin-bearing speech (about it).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सावज्जं जोगं** = sin-bearing activity (savadya yoga)
- **परस्सट्ठाए णिट्ठियं** = completed for the sake of another
- **कीरमाणं ति वा णच्चा** = or knowing it is being performed (currently)
- **सावज्जं ण लवे मुणी** = the monk should not utter sin-bearing speech (about it)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The principle generalized: any sinful act — completed for someone, currently being done, or planned — must not draw approving speech from the monk. Approval (*anumodana*) is one of the three modes by which karma binds (the other two being doing and causing-to-be-done). The monk who himself does not act and does not commission still binds karma if his words endorse what others have done.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

About any sinful activity that has been completed, is being done, or will be done by householders — the monk must not utter approving words.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you compliment a friend on a result whose making you would not have endorsed, where does the karma sit?

## SUTRA 7.41 — Do Not Praise Cooked Food

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुकडे त्ति सुपक्के त्ति, सुच्छिण्णे सुहडे मडे ।
सुणिट्ठिए सुलट्ठे त्ति, सावज्जं वज्जए मुणी ॥७.४१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.41 ||
sukaḍe tti supakke tti, succhiṇṇe suhaḍe maḍe |
suṇiṭṭhie sulaṭṭhe tti, sāvajjaṃ vajjae muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Well-prepared!" "Well-cooked!" "Well-trimmed!" "Well-de-bittered!" "Well-seasoned with ghee!" "Well-finished!" "Wonderfully delicious!" — such sin-bearing speech the monk shall avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुकडे त्ति** = "this has been well-prepared / well-arranged"
- **सुपक्के त्ति** = "well-cooked / well-baked"
- **सुच्छिण्णे** = "well-cut / well-trimmed"
- **सुहडे** = "the bitterness has been well removed"
- **मडे** = "well-anointed (with ghee, etc.)"
- **सुणिट्ठिए** = "completed by proper method"
- **सुलट्ठे त्ति** = "exceedingly delicious / lovely"
- **सावज्जं वज्जए मुणी** = the monk avoids such sin-bearing words

### 5. COMMENTARY

A direct application of 7.40 to food. The household has just prepared a meal involving fire-violence, water-violence, vegetable-violence, and (sometimes) cooking-of-living-jivas. To praise the result — "what wonderful cooking!" — is to endorse the violence. The monk who comes for alms must not become the food-critic of the kitchen. He receives, eats with detachment, and is silent on the merits of the preparation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

About food, the monk must not say: "well-prepared," "well-cooked," "well-cut," "the bitterness is well removed," "well-seasoned with ghee," "made by perfect method," "absolutely delicious." Such praise endorses violence.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Praise of a result silently endorses the means. When did you last praise something whose making you would not have wanted to witness?

## SUTRA 7.42 — Speak of Acts as Effortful, Not as Praiseworthy

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पयत्तपक्क त्ति व पक्कमालवे, पयत्तछिण्ण त्ति व छिण्णमालवे ।
पयत्तलट्ठि त्ति व कम्महेउयं, पहारगाढ त्ति व गाढमालवे ॥७.४२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.42 ||
payattapakka tti va pakkamālave, payattachiṇṇa tti va chiṇṇamālave |
payattalaṭṭhi tti va kammaheuyaṃ, pahāragāḍha tti va gāḍhamālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Of a cooked thing, (one may) say "it has been cooked with effort"; of a cut thing, say "it has been cut with effort"; of a karma-binding action (or skilfully made object), say it has been "done with effort"; of a heavy blow (struck on someone), say "a forceful blow was inflicted."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पयत्तपक्क त्ति** = "cooked with great effort"
- **पक्कमालवे** = should say of the cooked thing
- **पयत्तछिण्ण त्ति** = "cut with great effort"
- **छिण्णमालवे** = should say of the cut thing
- **पयत्तलट्ठि त्ति** = "made by great labour"
- **कम्महेउयं** = of a karma-causing object/action
- **पहारगाढ त्ति** = "a forceful blow was struck"
- **गाढमालवे** = should say of the (struck/wounded) person without taking sides

### 5. COMMENTARY

The positive complement to 7.41. When the monk *must* mention an act that involved violence (cooking, cutting, striking), he frames it as *effortful* rather than *excellent*. The phrase "cooked with effort" acknowledges the labour without praising the result; the phrase "a forceful blow was struck" describes the violence factually, without taking the side of either striker or struck. The monk neither approves nor condemns; he merely describes — without raga (attachment) or dvesha (aversion).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk must speak of cooked, cut, or made things, he should say "made with effort" — not "made well." Of a violent blow, he should say "a forceful blow was struck" — without picking sides.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To describe without praising or condemning is the speech of one who has stopped picking sides. Where in your life are you still adding the verdict?

## SUTRA 7.43 — Do Not Use Superlatives or Inexpressibles

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वुक्कसं परग्घं वा, अउलं णत्थि एरिसं ।
अचक्कियमवत्तव्वं, अचिंतं चेव णो वए ॥७.४३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.43 ||
savvukkasaṃ paragghaṃ vā, aulaṃ ṇatthi erisaṃ |
acakkiyamavattavvaṃ, aciṃtaṃ ceva ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"This is the very best of all," "this is most precious," "incomparable, nothing like it exists," "indescribable," "unutterable," "unthinkable" — (such words) the monk should not say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सव्वुक्कसं** = "this is the very best of everything"
- **परग्घं वा** = "this is most valuable / priceless"
- **अउलं** = "incomparable, beyond comparison"
- **णत्थि एरिसं** = "there is nothing like this"
- **अचक्कियं** = "no one is capable (of producing this)"
- **अवत्तव्वं** = "indescribable"
- **अचिंतं** = "unthinkable / inconceivable"
- **णो वए** = should not say

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra forbids hyperbole and superlatives. The reasons: (1) such language is rarely literally true — most "best in the world" claims are exaggerations and therefore *musavaada*; (2) such language inflates the value of worldly things and stokes attachment in the listener; (3) such absolute language is reserved (in Jain teaching) for the Tirthankaras, the Siddhas, and liberated states — to apply it to mundane objects is a kind of sacrilege. The monk's speech remains measured, precise, and free of inflation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not say: "this is the very best," "the most precious," "unmatched," "indescribable," "unutterable," "unthinkable" — such inflated speech is forbidden.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you reach for the superlative, what are you actually trying to do — describe, or impress?

## SUTRA 7.44 — Do Not Promise to Convey Every Word

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वमेयं वइस्सामि, सव्वमेयं ति णो वए ।
अणुवीइ सव्वं सव्वत्थ, एवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.४४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.44 ||
savvameyaṃ vaissāmi, savvameyaṃ ti ṇo vae |
aṇuvīi savvaṃ savvattha, evaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I will tell (your) entire (message)," "this is exactly all" — (the monk) should not say so. The wise monk should speak everything, everywhere, only after due reflection (anuvichi).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सव्वमेयं वइस्सामि** = "I will tell your entire message (to the receiver)"
- **सव्वमेयं ति णो वए** = "this is exactly all" — should not say
- **अणुवीइ** = after due reflection / weighing of consequences
- **सव्वं सव्वत्थ** = everything, in every place
- **एवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं** = thus the wise monk should speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

If asked to carry a message, the monk must not promise "I'll tell them everything you said" or "this is the complete message" — because he cannot guarantee that he will remember every word, that the receiver will be available, that no detail will be omitted. The promise itself becomes a falsehood the moment any portion is forgotten or modified. Instead, the monk uses the word "all" (when he must) only after careful prior reflection on what is truly conveyable.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not promise "I'll tell them everything" or "this is exactly all" — speech that uses "all" must be preceded by careful consideration.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The word "all" is one of the most common lies. When did you last say "all" and mean less?

## SUTRA 7.45 — Do Not Speak the Language of Commerce

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुक्कीयं वा सुविक्कीयं, अकिज्जं किज्जमेव वा ।
इमं गिण्ह इमं मुंच, पणियं णो वियागरे ॥७.४५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.45 ||
sukkīyaṃ vā suvikkīyaṃ, akijjaṃ kijjameva vā |
imaṃ giṇha imaṃ muṃca, paṇiyaṃ ṇo viyāgare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"You bought this well," "you sold this well," "this is not fit to do," "this is fit to do," "take this," "give this up" — (the monk) should not so speak in matters of commerce.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुक्कीयं** = "you bought it well"
- **सुविक्कीयं** = "you sold it well"
- **अकिज्जं** = "this transaction is not worth doing"
- **किज्जमेव वा** = "this is fit to do"
- **इमं गिण्ह** = "take/buy this"
- **इमं मुंच** = "give up / sell this"
- **पणियं णो वियागरे** = should not so speak in commercial matters

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not say in trade matters: "you bought well," "you sold well," "this deal is not good," "this is a good deal," "buy this," "sell this." All commercial speech is renounced.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you give business advice you have no stake in, what part of you is borrowing the role of merchant?

## SUTRA 7.46 — When Speech About Trade is Necessary, Speak Without Endorsement

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्पग्घे वा महग्घे वा, कए वा विक्कए वि वा ।
पणियट्ठे समुप्पण्णे, अणवज्जं वियागरे ॥७.४६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.46 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **महग्घे वा** = or of high value
- **कए वा विक्कए वि वा** = in buying or in selling

The same fact can be reported or recommended. The monk reports.

## SUTRA 7.47 — Do Not Issue Commands to Householders

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेवासंजय धीरो, आस एहि करेहि वा ।
सयं चिट्ठ वयाहि त्ति, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥७.४७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.47 ||
tahevāsaṃjaya dhīro, āsa ehi karehi vā |
sayaṃ ciṭṭha vayāhi tti, ṇevaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, the steadfast wise (monk) should not say to an unrestrained (householder): "sit here," "come here," "do this," "lie down," "stand," "go (there)."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **असंजय** (asaṃjaya) = the unrestrained one — i.e., a householder (who has not taken monastic vows)
- **धीरो** = the steadfast (monk)
- **आस** = "sit (here)"
- **एहि** = "come (here)"
- **करेहि वा** = "do this"
- **सयं** = "lie down"
- **चिट्ठ** = "stand"
- **वयाहि त्ति** = "go (to such-and-such place)"
- **णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं** = the wise monk should not so speak

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra extends the monk's discipline to the *imperative mood* itself. To command the householder — "sit," "come," "do," "go" — is to enter into the householder's domain of activity. The householder's actions are governed by *asanyam* (lack of restraint) and inevitably involve subtle violence; for the monk to direct those actions is to become karmically responsible for them, even if the householder's act was already going to happen.

The Gujarati commentary suggests permissible alternatives: rather than "sit here," say "you may sit nearby if you wish, no need to stand"; rather than "come back later," say "we are at our alms-time now, please come at a more convenient hour for both of us." The principle: the monk does not command — he describes circumstances and lets the householder choose.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not order householders: "sit here," "come here," "do this," "lie down," "stand," "go there." Such commanding speech makes the monk responsible for the household activity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The imperative mood is a form of authority. Where do you exercise it without realising it has consequences?

## SUTRA 7.48 — Do Not Call the Unworthy "Sadhu"

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बहवे इमे असाहू, लोए वुच्चंति साहुणो ।
ण लवे असाहुं साहुत्ति, साहुं साहुत्ति आलवे ॥७.४८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.48 ||
bahave ime asāhū, loe vuccaṃti sāhuṇo |
ṇa lave asāhuṃ sāhutti, sāhuṃ sāhutti ālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Many in this world who are not (true) sadhus are called sadhus. (The monk) should not call a non-sadhu "sadhu"; he should call a (true) sadhu "sadhu."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बहवे इमे असाहू** = many in this world are not (true) sadhus
- **लोए वुच्चंति साहुणो** = (yet they) are called "sadhus" by the world
- **ण लवे असाहुं साहुत्ति** = (the monk) should not call the non-sadhu "sadhu"
- **साहुं साहुत्ति आलवे** = should call the (true) sadhu "sadhu"

A precise teaching about religious titles. The world is loose with the word "sadhu" — applying it to anyone in robes, anyone who claims spiritual authority, anyone with a following. The next sutra (7.49) gives the four marks by which a true sadhu is identified.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The world calls many people "sadhu" who are not really sadhus. The monk must not extend that label to the unworthy — he calls a true sadhu "sadhu" only.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How readily do you bestow titles on people who have not earned them? What does that habit cost the community?

## SUTRA 7.49 — The Four Marks of a True Sadhu

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णाणदंसणसंपण्णं, संजमे य तवे रयं ।
एवं गुणसमाउत्तं, संजयं साहुमालवे ॥७.४९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.49 ||
ṇāṇadaṃsaṇasaṃpaṇṇaṃ, saṃjame ya tave rayaṃ |
evaṃ guṇasamāuttaṃ, saṃjayaṃ sāhumālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(One who is) endowed with right knowledge and right perception, and devoted to self-restraint and austerity — being thus equipped with (these four) qualities, the (true) self-restrained one — call him "sadhu."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णाणदंसणसंपण्णं** = endowed with samyag-jnana (right knowledge) and samyag-darshana (right perception)
- **संजमे रयं** = devoted to samyam (self-restraint, the great vows)
- **तवे रयं** = devoted to tapas (austerity)
- **एवं गुणसमाउत्तं** = thus possessed of (these four) qualities
- **संजयं** = the truly self-restrained one
- **साहुमालवे** = (him alone) call "sadhu"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The four-fold criterion is precise: *samyag-jnana* + *samyag-darshana* + *samyam* + *tapas*. This is the classical definition of the path to liberation (the same four named in the *Tattvartha Sutra*'s *samyag-darshana-jnana-charitra-tapasi marga*). Where these four are present, "sadhu" is a true name; where they are absent, "sadhu" is a lie.

The commentary notes a subtlety: the monk's discipline is more strict toward Jain monks (who must be evaluated by these four qualities) than toward non-Jain ascetics (where conventional forms of address may apply, since the monk is not bound to recognize Jain vows in non-Jain contexts). But within the Jain order, no slack is permitted. A Jain monk who neglects loch (hair-pulling), pratikraman (daily confession), restricted travel, or proper monastic dress, has crossed the line and should not be called sadhu — silence is the right response in such cases.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true sadhu has four marks: right knowledge, right perception, devotion to self-restraint, and devotion to austerity. Call only such a one "sadhu."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you call someone by an honorific, are you naming what they are — or what you wish they were?

## SUTRA 7.50 — Do Not Predict Outcomes of Wars

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

देवाणं मणुयाणं च, तिरियाणं च वुग्गहे ।
अमुगाणं जओ होउ, मा वा होउ त्ति णो वए ॥७.५०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.50 ||
devāṇaṃ maṇuyāṇaṃ ca, tiriyāṇaṃ ca vuggahe |
amugāṇaṃ jao hou, mā vā hou tti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In conflicts among gods, humans, or animals — "may such-and-such party win," "may such-and-such party not win" — (the monk) should not say so.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **देवाणं मणुयाणं च तिरियाणं च** = of gods, humans, and animals
- **वुग्गहे** = in conflict / battle / war
- **अमुगाणं जओ होउ** = "may victory be to such-and-such (party)"
- **मा वा होउ त्ति** = "or may it not be"
- **णो वए** = should not say

### 5. COMMENTARY

Wars and conflicts are sites of enormous violence. To wish either side victory is to endorse the violence on that side; to wish either side defeat is to endorse the violence done to them. The monk's tongue stays out of partisan conflict entirely. Beyond that, predicting outcomes is forbidden by the same logic as 7.6-7.10: only the omniscient knows who will win. To predict and be wrong is to lose credibility for the dharma; to predict and be right is to nourish the speaker's pride and the laity's superstition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In any conflict — among gods, humans, or animals — the monk must not say "may this side win" or "may this side lose." No partisan or predictive speech.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your speech-energy is invested in cheering on one side of a fight that has nothing to do with your liberation?

## SUTRA 7.51 — Do Not Predict Weather, Health, or Prosperity

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वाओ वुटुं च सीउण्हं, खेमं धायं सिवं ति वा ।
कयाणु हुज्ज एयाणि, मा वा होउ त्ति णो वए ॥७.५१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.51 ||
vāo vuṭuṃ ca sīuṇhaṃ, khemaṃ dhāyaṃ sivaṃ ti vā |
kayāṇu hujja eyāṇi, mā vā hou tti ṇo vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"When will (favourable) wind, rain, cool, heat, peace, abundant harvest, prosperity arise?" or "may they not arise" — (the monk) should not say so.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वाओ** = wind
- **वुटुं** = rain
- **सीउण्हं** = cold or heat
- **खेमं** = peace / safety / freedom from troubles
- **धायं** = abundance of food / good harvest
- **सिवं** = absence of disease / auspicious well-being
- **कयाणु हुज्ज एयाणि** = "when will these come about?"
- **मा वा होउ त्ति** = "or may they not come about"
- **णो वए** = should not say

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk does not predict weather, public health, harvest, or general welfare. The reason is twofold: (1) such predictions are speculative — only the omniscient knows; if the prediction proves false, the layperson loses faith in the monk and in the dharma; (2) such talk is *useless prattle* (*nirarthak pravartan*) — speaking of natural phenomena does not change them, and the speaker accumulates either complacency (if right) or shame (if wrong) without spiritual gain. The monk's silence on such matters preserves both his dignity and his focus.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not predict or wish about weather, public health, harvests, peace, or prosperity — neither "may these come" nor "may these not come."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your conversation is about things you cannot influence and cannot predict?

## SUTRA 7.52 — Do Not Deify Clouds, Sky, or People

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव मेहं व नहं व माणवं, ण देव देव त्ति गिरं वएज्जा ।
संमुच्छिए उण्णए वा पओए, वएज्ज वा वुट्टे बलाहए त्ति ॥७.५२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.52 ||
taheva mehaṃ va nahaṃ va māṇavaṃ, ṇa deva deva tti giraṃ vaejjā |
saṃmucchie uṇṇae vā paoe, vaejja vā vuṭṭe balāhae tti ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, of a cloud, the sky, or a (great) human, the monk should not utter speech (calling them) "god, god." (Of a cloud one) may say "the cloud has gathered," "it has risen high," or "this cloud-mass has rained."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मेहं** = a cloud
- **नहं** = the sky
- **माणवं** = a (notable) human
- **ण देव देव त्ति** = should not call "god, god"
- **गिरं वएज्जा** = should not utter such speech
- **संमुच्छिए** = "(the cloud) has gathered / become dense"
- **उण्णए वा** = "or has risen high"
- **पओए** = the cloud-mass
- **वुट्टे बलाहए त्ति** = "this cloud has rained"

### 5. COMMENTARY

A precise theological teaching. Ordinary religion deifies natural phenomena — clouds become rain-gods, sky becomes sky-god, charismatic people become incarnations. Jain doctrine refuses all such deification: clouds are pudgala (matter), the sky is ajiva-dravya (a non-living substance), and even the most miraculous human is still a samsari jiva bound to embodiment. To call any of them "god" is *mithyatva* (false belief) and *musavaada* (falsehood) at once.

The monk speaks of clouds in their actual physics: gathered, risen, raining. The wonder remains; the false reverence is removed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must not call a cloud, the sky, or any extraordinary human "god." Of a cloud, he may simply say "it has gathered," "it has risen high," "this cloud has rained."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The reverence we offer to weather and to charismatic people — what is it really seeking?

## SUTRA 7.53 — Permitted Speech About Sky and Powerful People

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अंतलिक्खे त्ति णं बूया, गुज्झाणुचरियत्ति य ।
रिद्धिमंतं णरं दिस्स, रिद्धिमंतं ति आलवे ॥७.५३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.53 ||
aṃtalikkhe tti ṇaṃ būyā, gujjhāṇucariyatti ya |
riddhimaṃtaṃ ṇaraṃ dissa, riddhimaṃtaṃ ti ālave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Of the sky) one should say "antariksha" or "the path traversed by the celestials"; seeing a person of (worldly) prosperity, one should say "(this is) a prosperous person."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अंतलिक्खे त्ति णं बूया** = should call (the sky) "antariksha" (atmosphere / mid-region)
- **गुज्झाणुचरिय त्ति** = "the path traversed by the unseen (devas)"
- **रिद्धिमंतं णरं दिस्स** = seeing a wealthy / endowed person
- **रिद्धिमंतं ति आलवे** = should call him "wealthy / endowed"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The neutral, non-deifying vocabulary. The sky is *antariksha* — the in-between space — or "the path by which the unseen devas travel" (a poetic but factually neutral description). The wealthy or powerful person is *riddhimanta* — "endowed with worldly prosperity" — a description of *what they have*, not a deification of *what they are*. The monk preserves accuracy and refuses inflation simultaneously.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk may call the sky "antariksha" or "the path the devas travel"; a wealthy person, simply "wealthy / endowed."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To name accurately is to refuse both worship and contempt. Where do your words lean toward one or the other?

## SUTRA 7.54 — Closing Instruction: Avoid All Sin-Endorsing or Wounding Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव सावज्जणुमोयणी गिरा, ओहारिणी जा य परोवघाइणी ।
से कोह लोह भयसा व माणवो, ण हासमाणो वि गिरं वइज्जा ॥७.५४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.54 ||
taheva sāvajjaṇumoyaṇī girā, ohāriṇī jā ya parovaghāiṇī |
se koha loha bhayasā va māṇavo, ṇa hāsamāṇo vi giraṃ vaijjā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, the speech that endorses sin, that asserts certainty (where there is none), that injures another being — the (wise) man should not utter it under (the influence of) anger, greed, fear, or pride; not even while jesting.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सावज्जणुमोयणी गिरा** = speech that endorses sinful activity
- **ओहारिणी** = speech that asserts certainty (where it should not)
- **जा य परोवघाइणी** = and that which injures other beings
- **से माणवो** = he, the wise man (the monk)
- **कोह लोह भयसा व माणवो** = under anger, greed, fear, or pride
- **हासमाणो वि** = even while jesting / for fun
- **गिरं ण वइज्जा** = should not utter (such) speech

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the great summary verse. Three categories of speech are banned in absolute terms: (1) *savadya-anumodani* — that which endorses sin; (2) *avadharini / oharini* — that which asserts unwarranted certainty; (3) *parovaghatini* — that which wounds others. And four inner states are named as the roots from which such speech most often springs: krodha (anger), lobha (greed), bhaya (fear), mana (pride). Plus a fifth — *hasamana* (jest, joking) — which the monk often imagines is harmless but which is included here because even in jest, sin-endorsing or wounding speech accumulates karma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk must never utter speech that endorses sin, asserts false certainty, or wounds others — not under anger, greed, fear, pride, or even in jest.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

"I was only joking" is one of the most common cover-stories for harmful speech. What hides under your jests?

## SUTRA 7.55 — The Reward of Pure Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुवक्कसुद्धिं समुपेहिया मुणी, गिरं च दुट्ठं परिवज्जए सया ।
मियं अदुट्ठं अणुवीइ भासए, सयाण मज्झे लहइ पसंसणं ॥७.५५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.55 ||
suvakkasuddhiṃ samupehiyā muṇī, giraṃ ca duṭṭhaṃ parivajjae sayā |
miyaṃ aduṭṭhaṃ aṇuvīi bhāsae, sayāṇa majjhe lahai pasaṃsaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who has well examined the purification of well-spoken words, who always avoids corrupted speech, who speaks measured (limited), uncorrupted speech with due reflection — finds praise among the company of the noble.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुवक्कसुद्धिं** = the purification of well-spoken words (the entire teaching of this chapter)
- **समुपेहिया** = having well examined / contemplated
- **मुणी** = the monk
- **गिरं च दुट्ठं परिवज्जए सया** = should always avoid corrupted (faulty) speech
- **मियं** = measured, limited, restrained
- **अदुट्ठं** = uncorrupted
- **अणुवीइ** = with due reflection
- **भासए** = speaks
- **सयाण मज्झे** = in the midst of the noble (sat-purushas)
- **लहइ पसंसणं** = obtains praise

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first of three closing summary verses. The monk who has actually internalized the chapter's teaching — who has examined the purity of his own speech, who consistently rejects faulty utterance, and who speaks only measured, uncorrupted, well-considered words — receives the natural reward: praise among the noble. This is not mere social standing; it is the recognition by the spiritually mature that this monk has achieved one of the rarest of disciplines. To be praised by the wise is itself a confirmation that the path is bearing fruit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk who studies the purity of speech, avoids faulty speech, and speaks only measured, clean, well-considered words — earns praise among the truly noble.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The opinion of the wise about your speech is a more accurate mirror than the opinion of the crowd. Whose praise are you actually seeking?

## SUTRA 7.56 — The Discipline of the Six Restraints

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

भासाइ दोसे य गुणे य जाणिया, तीसे य दुट्ठे परिवज्जए सया ।
छसु संजए सामणिए सया जए, वएज्ज बुद्धे हियमणुलोमियं ॥७.५६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.56 ||
bhāsāi dose ya guṇe ya jāṇiyā, tīse ya duṭṭhe parivajjae sayā |
chasu saṃjae sāmaṇie sayā jae, vaejja buddhe hiyamaṇulomiyaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowing well the faults and virtues of speech, always rejecting its corruptions, restrained in the (protection of) the six (kinds of life-bodies), always vigilant in the monastic state — the awakened (monk) should speak that which is beneficial and pleasing (to all beings).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भासाइ दोसे य गुणे य जाणिया** = having known well the faults and virtues of speech
- **तीसे य दुट्ठे परिवज्जए सया** = always rejecting its corrupted forms
- **छसु संजए** = restrained / careful in (the protection of) the six (kinds of life-bodies — earth, water, fire, air, plant, mobile)
- **सामणिए सया जए** = always vigilant in the monastic state (sramanya)
- **बुद्धे** = the awakened
- **हियमणुलोमियं वएज्ज** = should speak that which is beneficial and pleasing / agreeable

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second summary verse expands the principle to the full monastic life. Speech-purity is not an isolated discipline; it sits inside *chasu samjae* (the six-fold restraint, protecting the six kinds of jivas) and *samaniye saya jae* (constant vigilance in the monastic state). The monk who has all three layers — knowing faults and virtues of speech, rejecting all faulty speech, and being constantly restrained in the larger monastic discipline — is qualified to speak. And what he speaks is *hita* (beneficial) and *anulomik* (agreeable, in harmony with the listener) — words that genuinely help and that fall pleasingly on the ear.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The awakened monk — knowing speech's faults and virtues, always avoiding corrupted speech, restrained toward all six kinds of beings, vigilant in monastic life — should speak only what is beneficial and agreeable to all.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Beneficial *and* agreeable. Most speech achieves at most one. What discipline is required to achieve both?

## SUTRA 7.57 — Closing Verse: The Fruit of the Discipline (||ti bemi||)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

परिक्खभासी सुसमाहिइंदिए, चउक्कसायावगए अणिस्सए ।
स णिद्धुणे धुण्णमलं पुरेकडं, आराहए लोगमिणं तहा परं ॥
॥त्ति बेमि॥ ॥७.५७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 7.57 ||
parikkhabhāsī susamāhiiṃdie, cauakkasāyāvagae aṇissae |
sa ṇiddhuṇe dhuṇṇamalaṃ purekaḍaṃ, ārāhae logamiṇaṃ tahā paraṃ ||
|| ti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The (monk) who speaks only after examination, whose senses are well-controlled, who has overcome the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed), who is unattached — he shakes off the karmic dust accumulated from previous (deeds), and accomplishes (the welfare of) this world and the next. — Thus do I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **परिक्खभासी** = one who speaks only after examination (parikshapurvak)
- **सुसमाहिइंदिए** = with senses well-controlled (susamahit-indriya)
- **चउक्कसायावगए** = having gone beyond the four kashayas (anger, pride, deceit, greed)
- **अणिस्सए** = unattached, without (worldly) support
- **स** = he, that monk
- **णिद्धुणे** = shakes off
- **धुण्णमलं पुरेकडं** = the karmic-dust accumulated from previous deeds (purvarjit / shakeable karma-mala)
- **आराहए** = accomplishes / wins / fulfils
- **लोगमिणं तहा परं** = (the welfare of) this world and the next
- **त्ति बेमि** = "Thus I declare" — the standard concluding formula spoken by Sudharma Swami to his disciple Jambu Swami, by which he certifies that what he has just transmitted is the very teaching of Bhagavan Mahavira

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing verse names four qualities that, taken together, complete the discipline of speech: (1) *parikshabhashi* — speaks only after careful examination; (2) *susamahit-indriya* — has all five senses under control; (3) *chatur-kashaya-apagata* — has overcome anger, pride, deceit, and greed; (4) *anissaya* — is unattached, free of worldly support and dependence. When these four are present, the monk's speech itself becomes a force of purification: it shakes off the accumulated karma-dust of past deeds, and accomplishes welfare in both this world and the next.

The closing **ti bemi** ("thus I declare") is the canonical signature of Sudharma Swami, the fifth Gandhara of Bhagavan Mahavira. Through him the teaching reached his disciple Jambu Swami, and from there to all subsequent ages. The phrase certifies that this entire chapter is not the composition of the editor but the direct teaching of the Tirthankara.

The chapter's deepest message: pure speech is not a mere social courtesy or even a moral discipline — it is a karma-shaking practice. Every sentence the monk would have spoken in fault is a karmic bond that does not form; every sentence spoken in care is a small act of liberation. The fruit, named here as *loga imanam taha param* ("this world and the next"), is the sequence of progressively purer rebirths that culminates in the final liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk who speaks only after careful thought, whose senses are calm, who has conquered the four inner passions, and who is free from worldly attachment — shakes off the karmic dust of his past deeds, and wins the welfare of this life and the next. Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Speech is karma in motion. Every sentence either binds or releases. Which way does yours move today?

## END OF ADHYAYAN 7

॥ અધ્યયન-૭ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Adhyayan-7 sampurna — "Adhyayan 7 is complete.")*

The chapter closes with Sudharma's signature **ti bemi**. What began as a fourfold classification of language has unfolded into a complete science of monastic speech: when to speak and when to be silent, how to address persons of both sexes, how to describe trees and crops and rivers and animals without endorsing the violence done to them, how to refuse the language of commerce and command, how to deflate the false deification of natural phenomena and powerful humans, and finally how to recognise the speech-purity that wins praise among the noble and shakes off the karma of past lives.

The next chapter — Adhyayan 8, *Achara-pranidhi* — turns from speech-purity to the larger field of monastic conduct: protection of the six life-bodies, discipline over senses and sleep, conquest of the kashayas, and the various aids the monk needs to convert the body itself into a vehicle of liberation.
