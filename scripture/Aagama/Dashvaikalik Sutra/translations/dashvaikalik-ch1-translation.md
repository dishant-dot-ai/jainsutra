# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 1
# Drumapushpika (દ્રુમપુષ્પિકા) — The Tree-Flower

**Adhyayan 1 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 1–15 (PDF pages 62–75)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 5*

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The Dashvaikalik Sutra is one of the four Mūla Sūtras of the Jain canon — the foundational texts that every newly initiated monk traditionally memorises before all others. It was composed by Acharya Shyambhava (Shayyambhavāchārya) in the 4th century BCE for his young son Manak, who, having renounced the world to follow his father into monkhood, had only a short remaining lifespan. To compress the essence of the entire monastic teaching into something Manak could absorb in his short remaining time, Shyambhava extracted the sap of the wider canon and arranged it into ten short adhyayans (chapters) that could be studied at any time of day or night, even outside the four traditional sandhi (transition) hours when other study was prohibited. From this fact — *dasha* (ten) plus *vaikalika* (study at any time / non-traditional time) — the sutra takes its name.

The first adhyayan is called **Drumapushpika** — "tree-flower" — because at its heart lies one of the most beautiful images in all Jain literature: the bee that draws nectar from many flowers without harming any one of them, takes only what it needs, and is bound to no single bloom. This bee is the perfect emblem of the Jain monk's begging round (madhukari vritti, "the bee-like way of life"). Just as the bee's livelihood does not damage the very flowers that sustain it, the monk's livelihood — going from house to house, taking small portions from food the householder has already prepared for their own family — must not damage the householders who sustain the monastic order.

The chapter opens with a single foundational definition: religion (dharma) is the supreme good, and it consists of three things — non-violence (ahimsa), restraint (samyama), and austerity (tapa). Even the celestial beings, the text says, bow to one whose mind rests always in this dharma. From there the chapter unfolds the bee-and-flower simile in three stages: first the simile itself, then the monk's pledge to live by it, and finally the four marks of a true monk who has perfected this way — wakefulness, non-attachment, contentment with various householders' offerings, and mastery of the senses.

This is the chapter that teaches the very first lesson of monkhood: how to eat without harming the world that feeds you.

## SUTRA 1.1 — The Nature and Glory of Dharma

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धम्मो मंगलमुक्किट्ठं, अहिंसा संजमो तवो ।
देवा वि तं णमंसंति, जस्स धम्मे सया मणो ॥१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.1 ||
dhammo maṅgalamukkiṭṭhaṃ, ahiṃsā saṃjamo tavo |
devā vi taṃ ṇamaṃsanti, jassa dhamme sayā maṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Dharma is the supreme blessing — and that dharma is non-violence, restraint, and austerity. Even the celestial beings bow in salutation to the one whose mind is always settled in this dharma.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **धम्मो** (dhammo) = dharma, religion, the right way
- **मंगलं** (maṅgalaṃ) = blessing, auspicious, that which destroys sin and brings well-being
- **उक्किट्ठं** (ukkiṭṭhaṃ) = supreme, foremost, highest of all
- **अहिंसा** (ahiṃsā) = non-violence, non-harming of living beings
- **संजमो** (saṃjamo) = restraint, self-control, withdrawal from sinful activity
- **तवो** (tavo) = austerity, asceticism, the burning away of karmas through inner and outer discipline
- **देवा वि** (devā vi) = even the celestial beings (and by extension, even those of the highest worldly station — emperors and the like)
- **तं** (taṃ) = to that one
- **णमंसंति** (ṇamaṃsanti) = bow, salute, pay reverence
- **जस्स** (jassa) = whose
- **धम्मे** (dhamme) = in the dharma
- **सया** (sayā) = always, constantly
- **मणो** (maṇo) = mind, heart

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening verse is the auspicious invocation (mangalacharan) of the entire scripture, and within its short twenty-eight syllables it contains the whole programme of the monastic life. Acharya Shyambhava, knowing that the young Manak had little time, wastes none of his own. He defines what the path is, what its three components are, what its reward is, and who is qualified to enjoy that reward — all in a single verse.

The word *dharma* in Jainism does not mean "religion" in the sectarian sense the modern world tends to imagine. The text behind this verse explains it through a Sanskrit definition: *dhāraṇāt dharmaḥ* — "that which holds, that which sustains, is dharma." Two senses are drawn out. The first is worldly dharma — the duties that hold a family, a village, a kingdom together, the codes of social and political life. The second is the deeper one: that which holds a soul back from sliding into degraded states of being and which lifts it toward higher, freer states. This second is the true dharma, and it is what the verse names. The right view, the right knowledge, the right conduct, the various practices of self-purification — these are dharma in the higher sense, because they "sustain" the soul in its own true nature.

The three components named here — non-violence, restraint, and austerity — are not three separate practices but three faces of one practice. Non-violence is the goal: to harm no living being, including oneself, in thought, word, or deed. Restraint is the means: by withdrawing the senses and the mind from the harmful activities they would otherwise drift into, the practitioner keeps non-violence intact. Austerity is the purification: through fasting, simplification, study, meditation, and the willing endurance of discomfort, the karmas already bound to the soul are burnt away. The text observes that without restraint, non-violence cannot be kept; without non-violence, restraint has no purpose; without austerity, neither can be deepened. They depend on one another and complete one another.

The verse calls this dharma *ukkittha* — supreme, the highest. The word literally means "that which has nothing higher above it." Why is this dharma supreme? Because, the commentators explain, it produces only good — never harm to oneself, never harm to another, never any seed of future suffering. Many things in the world are called auspicious — golden vases, conch shells, swastikas, sacred letters — and these may bring momentary worldly prosperity, but they cannot remove the root suffering of birth and death. The dharma of non-violence, restraint, and austerity does, and so it alone deserves to be called supreme.

The second half of the verse turns to the reward. Even the celestial beings — those who already possess the highest worldly powers, beauty, and pleasures, those whom ordinary humans serve and worship — themselves bow to the practitioner whose mind rests always in this dharma. This is a striking inversion. The deva is normally the served, not the server. Yet, the text says, the deva himself recognises that the worldly glory he enjoys is temporary, while the spiritual glory of the true practitioner is permanent. The deva therefore voluntarily takes the lower seat. The practical implication for the seeker is profound: nothing in the world stands above one whose mind is genuinely settled in dharma — neither rank, nor wealth, nor force. The practitioner becomes worthy of the salutation of the very beings whose blessings the world ordinarily seeks.

The crucial qualifier is *sayā maṇo* — "whose mind is always there." It is not enough to perform the practices outwardly while the mind wanders. The salutation is offered to the one whose inner attention has truly come to rest in non-violence, restraint, and austerity, not as duties performed but as the natural texture of consciousness itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The very best thing a person can build their life around is doing no harm, holding the senses in check, and willingly giving up comfort for inner growth — and anyone who lives this way, even quietly, becomes worthy of the deepest respect, because they are already living what everyone else, deep down, knows is true.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When my mind drifts during the day, where does it most often go — and is that direction toward harm or away from it?

## SUTRA 1.2 — The Bee and the Flower

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहा दुमस्स पुप्फेसु, भमरो आविअइ रसं ।
ण य पुप्फं किलामेइ, सो य पीणेइ अप्पयं ॥१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.2 ||
jahā dumassa pupphesu, bhamaro āviai rasaṃ |
ṇa ya pupphaṃ kilāmei, so ya pīṇei appayaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as the bee draws nectar in measured amounts from the flowers of a tree — without causing pain to the flower — and yet by that very draught nourishes itself.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जहा** (jahā) = just as, in the manner of
- **दुमस्स** (dumassa) = of a tree (druma)
- **पुप्फेसु** (pupphesu) = on / from the flowers
- **भमरो** (bhamaro) = the bee (also called madhukara, the honey-maker)
- **आविअइ** (āviai) = sips, drinks in a measured way
- **रसं** (rasaṃ) = nectar, juice
- **ण य** (ṇa ya) = and not
- **पुप्फं** (pupphaṃ) = the flower
- **किलामेइ** (kilāmei) = pains, troubles, fatigues
- **सो य** (so ya) = and that one (the bee)
- **पीणेइ** (pīṇei) = nourishes, satisfies, fills
- **अप्पयं** (appayaṃ) = itself

### 5. COMMENTARY

After the opening verse defined dharma in its highest abstract form, the second verse drops the abstraction and offers an image — and the image is so well-suited to the monk's life that the entire chapter takes its name from it. The bee, drawing nectar from flowers, becomes the perfect picture of how a renunciant lives in the world.

Look closely at the bee. The tree puts out flowers as part of its own natural unfolding — not for the bee's sake, but as part of its own life-cycle. The bee comes uninvited. It alights on one flower, takes a measured sip — never enough to drain the flower, never so rough as to bruise it — and moves to the next. It does not harm the colour, the scent, the texture, or the structure of the flower. It does not pluck the flower from the tree. It does not return to the same flower again and again. And yet, gathering small amounts from many flowers, the bee is nourished and lives.

The lesson for the monk is exact. The monk does not produce his own food — to do so would require killing soil-life, water-life, fire-life, vegetation, and small creatures, and would entangle him in a web of harm. Instead, the monk takes from what householders have already prepared for themselves, never asking that anything be cooked specially, never asking for a portion that would deprive the household, never returning to the same house repeatedly so as to become a burden. He visits many houses, takes a small portion from each, and is nourished. The household barely notices the loss; the monk is fed; nothing has been disturbed.

Notice how carefully the verse chooses its verbs. The bee *āviai* — sips with restraint — it does not gulp. The bee does not *kilāmei* — does not weary the flower. And it *pīṇei appayaṃ* — nourishes itself. Three precise actions: take little, harm nothing, sustain oneself. These are the three rules of monastic alms compressed into one image. Acharya Shyambhava could have stated them as commandments. He instead lets the bee say them, because an image lodges in the heart in a way that a rule does not.

There is also a deeper teaching here about the relationship between the renunciant and the householder. The Jain tradition does not treat the monk and the householder as opposites who have nothing to do with each other. They are interdependent — like the bee and the flower. The householder runs the household, raises the family, supports society, and prepares food. The monk receives a small share, and in return offers something the householder cannot easily produce alone: the living presence of someone walking the path of liberation, who can teach, inspire, and remind. The bee pollinates the flower; the monk's example pollinates the spiritual life of the household. Both flourish together. The whole architecture of the Jain four-fold sangha (monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen) rests on this image.

The text also draws a contrast that is left implicit but is worth naming. A wasp or a locust does not behave like a bee. A wasp may sting; a locust may strip the plant entirely. There are renunciants in the wider world who behave more like locusts — demanding food, demanding it specially prepared, draining the household, becoming a burden on those who feed them. The Jain tradition is unusually firm that this is not the way. The monk is to be a bee, never a locust. The verse is therefore not just a description but a discipline.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A bee takes only a little nectar from each flower, never hurts the flower, and still gets fed — and that is exactly how a monk should live: take a small share from many homes, harm no one, and let the world that feeds him keep flourishing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I take what I need from the world around me — food, time, attention — am I a bee or am I something heavier?

## SUTRA 1.3 — Liberated Monks Live This Way

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एमेए समणा मुत्ता, जे लोए संति साहुणो ।
विहंगमा व पुप्फेसु, दाणभत्तेसणे रया ॥१.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.3 ||
emee samaṇā muttā, je loe santi sāhuṇo |
vihaṅgamā va pupphesu, dāṇabhattesaṇe rayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In just the same way live the liberated monks — those who in this world are the true sadhus — engaged in the careful seeking of food given by the householder, like the bees that move from flower to flower.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एमेए** (emee) = in just this way, in the manner described
- **समणा** (samaṇā) = śramaṇas, monks who labour for liberation; the word also carries the senses of one who pacifies the passions, one who treats all beings as equal, and one whose mind is auspicious toward all
- **मुत्ता** (muttā) = liberated, free — free both from outer possessions and from inner attachments such as desire, hatred, and delusion
- **जे** (je) = those who
- **लोए** (loe) = in the world
- **संति** (santi) = exist, are present
- **साहुणो** (sāhuṇo) = sadhus, holy ones, those who pursue the means of liberation
- **विहंगमा व** (vihaṅgamā va) = like the air-going one (a poetic name for the bee, "that which moves through the air") — also rendered as "like flying birds among the flowers"
- **पुप्फेसु** (pupphesu) = among the flowers
- **दाणभत्तेसणे** (dāṇabhattesaṇe) = in the careful seeking (eshana) of given (dāna) food (bhakta) — meaning food freely offered by the householder, not demanded
- **रया** (rayā) = engaged in, devoted to, absorbed in

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verse gave the image. This verse applies it. The bee was a comparison; now Acharya Shyambhava names directly what the comparison is comparing. The true monks of this world — the *sahanos*, the liberated ones — live exactly as the bee does, moving from house to house in search of food that has been freely given.

The verse uses three different words for the monk, each carrying a different shade of meaning. *Samaṇa* (śramaṇa) literally means "one who labours" — labours, that is, on the path of liberation, exhausting the karmas through inner effort. The same word also means "one who pacifies" the passions of anger, pride, deceit, and greed; "one who is equal" toward all beings, treating friend and stranger alike; and "one whose mind is gently auspicious" (sumana) toward all. *Muttā* means "liberated" or "freed" — freed from external possessions like wealth and family, and freed from internal possessions like attachment, hatred, and delusion. *Sāhuṇo* (sadhus) means "those who accomplish" — those who, by the steady practice of right view, right knowledge, and right conduct, accomplish the goal of liberation. To call someone by all three names at once is to say: they are working hard, they are inwardly free, and they are actually getting somewhere.

The crucial phrase is *dāṇa-bhatta-esaṇe rayā* — "engaged in the seeking of food that is given." Each word matters. *Dāṇa* (given) emphasises that the food must be a free gift — never demanded, never coerced, never produced for the monk's sake. *Bhatta* (food) here means cooked food prepared in the natural course of the household's own life. *Eshana* (careful seeking) is a technical term in Jain monastic practice: the monk inspects the food for forty-seven possible faults — was a living being killed for it? was it cooked specially for him? does it contain any prohibited substance? was it stolen or coerced from the giver? — and only food clean of all these faults is accepted. *Rayā* (engaged in) suggests that this seeking is not done casually but with devoted attention. Together the phrase paints a monk who, even at the moment of taking food, is keeping a careful inner discipline.

The verse uses a slightly different word for "bee" than the previous one — *vihangama*, literally "the sky-mover," the one who moves through the air. The shift is meaningful. The earlier word *bhamara* emphasised the bee's behaviour toward the flower (taking little, causing no harm). This word emphasises the bee's mobility — it does not settle. It moves freely among many flowers, attached to none. The monk too does not settle. He visits many houses but is not bound to any one of them. Whether the household is rich or poor, high-born or low, of his own community or another, makes no difference to him. He is the bee that flies through the sky and lights wherever a flower opens.

There is a quiet but important social teaching encoded here. Jain mendicancy makes no caste distinctions. The monk takes food from a king's palace and from a labourer's hut with the same equanimity, applying the same forty-seven inspections to both. The "bee-like way of life" is therefore also a discipline of social impartiality. The monk who would visit only the wealthy houses, or only the houses of his former community, has stopped being a bee and become something else — a parasite of a particular flower-bed, no longer free.

The verse's deepest point, however, is contained in the word *muttā* — liberated. The monk who lives this way is called liberated not because he has finally reached liberation but because, in this very moment of begging, he is already free. He carries no possessions. He demands nothing. He depends on no single household. He is bound by no preference. The very act of going for alms in the bee-like way is itself a small experience of liberation — a daily rehearsal of what total freedom feels like.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The real monks of this world live exactly like the bees — they move from home to home, take only what is freely offered, harm no one in the process, and stay free of any single attachment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in my life have I made myself dependent on — and what would it feel like to live, even for a day, without that one dependence?

## SUTRA 1.4 — The Monk's Pledge of Faultless Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वयं च वित्तिं लब्भामो, ण य कोइ उवहम्मइ ।
अहागडेसु रीयंते, पुप्फेसु भमरा जहा ॥१.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.4 ||
vayaṃ ca vittiṃ labbhāmo, ṇa ya koi uvahammai |
ahāgaḍesu rīyante, pupphesu bhamarā jahā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

We will obtain our livelihood in such a way that not even one living being is harmed — moving among householders' already-prepared food, just as the bees move among flowers.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वयं** (vayaṃ) = we (the monks)
- **च** (ca) = and (used here as a verse-completer)
- **वित्तिं** (vittiṃ) = livelihood, sustenance, the means of bodily maintenance (vritti)
- **लब्भामो** (labbhāmo) = will obtain, will receive
- **ण य** (ṇa ya) = and not
- **कोइ** (koi) = anyone, any living being
- **उवहम्मइ** (uvahammai) = is harmed, is injured, is killed (upahanyate)
- **अहागडेसु** (ahāgaḍesu) = among that which is *yathākṛta* — already prepared, made by the householder for their own use
- **रीयंते** (rīyante) = will move, will go forth
- **पुप्फेसु** (pupphesu) = among the flowers
- **भमरा** (bhamarā) = the bees
- **जहा** (jahā) = just as

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the monk's vow. The first three verses described — first the principle (dharma is the supreme blessing), then the image (the bee), then the application (this is how true monks live). Now in the fourth verse the monks themselves speak. They pledge what they will do.

The vow has a precise structure. First the monk declares the means: *vayaṃ vittiṃ labbhāmo* — "we shall obtain our livelihood." The body must be maintained; even the renunciant cannot escape the body's need for food. Second, the monk declares the limit: *ṇa ya koi uvahammai* — "and not one being shall be harmed." The means must not violate the highest principle. Third, the monk declares the method: *ahāgaḍesu rīyante* — "we shall move among the already-prepared." This last word, *yathākṛta*, is the technical term for food that the householder has cooked for the family's own consumption, with no thought of the monk. Such food is permitted; food cooked specially for the monk is not, because that would entangle the monk in the killing of vegetable life, fire-life, and water-life that the special cooking required.

The vow's precision makes a sharp distinction that the wider world often misses. There are two ways to receive food. The first is to ask for it, to expect it, perhaps even to demand it as a religious right; the household then prepares food specially, and the renunciant is now indirectly the cause of all the harm involved in that preparation. The second is to come quietly, to take only from what was already going to be cooked anyway, to leave if nothing is available, and to expect nothing. The Jain tradition is firm that only the second is true alms-seeking. The first is a kind of theft dressed in religious clothing — the renunciant is, in effect, conscripting the household into the chain of harm that the renunciation was supposed to escape.

The ending of the verse — *pupphesu bhamarā jahā*, "just as the bees among flowers" — is structurally beautiful. The chapter began with this image, returned to it in verse three, and now seals the monk's vow with it again. The repetition is not literary decoration. It is teaching by repetition: the bee, the bee, the bee. Acharya Shyambhava wants the image to lodge so deeply in the young monk's mind that the moment he steps out for alms, the picture of the bee comes up unbidden and shapes his every motion. This is how a good teacher trains a young disciple — not by abstract rules but by giving him a single living image that contains the rules.

The text behind this verse explains that the great commentator Haribhadra Suri described the monk's alms-seeking as *sarva-sampatkari* — "the producer of all true wealth." Through this practice the monk acquires every form of inner wealth: knowledge, perception, character, peace. Six varieties of begging are mentioned: *madhukari* (bee-like, taking small portions from many), *gochari* (cow-like, grazing wherever a patch of grass appears without choosing the best), *gandapana* (medicine-like, taking only the limited amount needed for the body's "illness" of hunger), *akshamprana* (wheel-greasing, taking only what is needed to keep the body moving toward liberation), *garta-purani* (pit-filling, filling the body's emptiness with whatever is available), and *daha-upashamani* (fire-quenching, taking enough to extinguish the burning of hunger). All six images carry the same teaching: take little, take what is freely available, take only enough to keep the body running on its journey toward the goal.

There is one more thing worth noticing. The verse uses the plural — *vayaṃ*, "we." This is not one isolated monk's pledge but the collective vow of the entire monastic community. From Mahavira's time to the present day, every Jain monk and nun has, in effect, taken this same vow — to live so lightly on the world that not one being is harmed by their living. This is among the most ambitious commitments any community has ever made, and it has been kept, in its essential form, for over twenty-five hundred years.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monks make this promise: we will get just enough food to stay alive, and we will get it in a way that hurts absolutely nobody — taking only from what was already being cooked, just like bees that take nectar from flowers without spoiling them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would I have to change in my own life so that the way I sustain myself harms no one — not even indirectly?

## SUTRA 1.5 — The Four Marks of a True Monk

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

महुगारसमा बुद्धा, जे भवंति अणिस्सिया ।
णाणापिंडरया दंता, तेण वुच्चंति साहुणो ॥त्ति बेमि ॥१.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.5 ||
mahugārasamā buddhā, je bhavanti aṇissiyā |
ṇāṇāpiṃḍarayā dantā, teṇa vuccanti sāhuṇo || ti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those who are awakened, who are like the honey-makers, who are unattached to any single household, who are content with food of varied kinds gathered from many doors, and who are masters of their senses — for these reasons they are rightly called sadhus. So I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **महुगारसमा** (mahugārasamā) = like the honey-maker (madhukara), the bee — who takes a little from many flowers without binding to one
- **बुद्धा** (buddhā) = awakened, enlightened, knowers of the truth, those who have understood what is to be done and what is not
- **जे** (je) = who
- **भवंति** (bhavanti) = are
- **अणिस्सिया** (aṇissiyā) = unattached, not dependent, not bound to any one household, family, place, or person
- **णाणापिंडरया** (ṇāṇāpiṃḍarayā) = devoted to taking varied kinds of food (pinda) — content with whatever varieties of pure alms are received from many different homes
- **दंता** (dantā) = restrained, those who have subdued the senses and the mind
- **तेण** (teṇa) = for that reason, on account of these qualities
- **वुच्चंति** (vuccanti) = are called, are spoken of as
- **साहुणो** (sāhuṇo) = sadhus, holy ones
- **त्ति बेमि** (ti bemi) = "thus I declare" — the traditional closing formula by which Sudharma Swami transmits to Jambu Swami what he heard directly from Mahavira

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing verse of the chapter is a definition. After four verses of image and vow, Acharya Shyambhava now lists the four qualities by which a true monk is to be recognised. To be called a sadhu — a holy one — a person must possess all four. These are not optional ornaments; they are the working definition.

The first quality is *buddhā* — awakened. The Jain use of this word is older than and independent of its later Buddhist usage. To be *buddha* here means to be *jagrut* — wakeful, alert, knowing what is right action and what is wrong, knowing what is one's true nature and what is not. The sadhu is not merely a person who has formally renounced; the sadhu is a person whose understanding is awake. Without inner wakefulness, the renunciation is only a costume change.

The second quality is *mahugāra-samā aṇissiyā* — "like the honey-maker, unattached." This carries forward the chapter's central image one final time. The sadhu does not rest his weight on any single support. He does not lean on a particular family for food, on a particular village for residence, on a particular community for identity, on a particular relationship for emotional comfort. The text behind the verse spells out four kinds of unattachment learnt from the bee: (1) the bee is not based at any one flower; (2) the bee does not pre-decide which flower to visit next, accepting whichever opens; (3) the bee does not become attached to any one flower, however lovely; (4) the bee is not bound by family or kin. The sadhu, in the same four ways, is not based at, does not pre-decide, does not become attached to, and is not bound by — any household, time, family, caste, or class.

The third quality is *ṇāṇāpiṃḍa-rayā* — "content with varied alms." Three layers are unpacked. First, the sadhu willingly receives food of various kinds from various households — fresh and stale, tasty and tasteless, plain and flavoured — and is equally content with all. Second, the sadhu may take various *abhigrahas* (specific resolves, such as "today I will accept only if the giver is standing on the threshold," or "today I will accept only if a particular condition is met"), and is content with whatever the resolve permits. Third, the sadhu sometimes practises *vritti-sankshep* — restricting the search to a smaller area or a smaller time — and is content with what such restriction yields. In all three layers the inner principle is the same: contentment with whatever pure alms come, no demand for more, no preference for the better.

The fourth quality is *dantā* — restrained. Five senses of this word are drawn out: (1) one who suppresses the disturbances of the senses and the mind; (2) one who keeps the senses regulated; (3) one who burns up the soul with restraint and austerity; (4) one who is constantly engaged in suppressing anger, pride, deceit, and greed; (5) one who suppresses oneself by oneself — that is, one who has taken charge of one's own inner life and no longer waits for circumstances to impose discipline from outside.

These four qualities work together. Wakefulness without unattachment becomes spiritual snobbery. Unattachment without contentment becomes restless wandering. Contentment without restraint becomes dull complacency. Restraint without wakefulness becomes mere mechanical discipline. All four held together produce what the verse calls the sadhu — a person whose life is the steady ringing of these four notes.

The phrase *ti bemi* — "thus I declare" — is the formal closing of the chapter. It signals that what has been said is not the speaker's personal opinion but the transmission of what was heard from a higher authority. In the layered transmission of the Jain canon, Mahavira spoke; the chief disciple Sudharma Swami remembered; Sudharma transmitted to his disciple Jambu Swami; the line of monks preserved it. *Ti bemi* is the verbal seal: this is not invention; this is the voice of the awakened ones, faithfully passed down. The young Manak, hearing this seal, was being told that what he had just learnt was not his father's clever teaching but the very words of the path itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A true monk is a person with four qualities: he is wide awake to what really matters, he is not stuck on any one person or place, he is content with whatever simple food he gets from many different homes, and he keeps a firm hand on his own senses and feelings — and this is what makes him deserve the name "sadhu."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of these four — wakefulness, unattachment, contentment, restraint — which one is most absent in me right now, and what would change if I cultivated it?

The first chapter closes here. With the bee-and-flower image, the foundation of monastic life has been laid: a way of living in the world that takes only what is freely given, harms no one, and produces in the practitioner the four qualities by which a true monk is known. The next chapter — Shramanyapurvak — turns from the outer discipline of food to the inner discipline of the heart, where the deeper enemy is found: the pull of desire itself.
