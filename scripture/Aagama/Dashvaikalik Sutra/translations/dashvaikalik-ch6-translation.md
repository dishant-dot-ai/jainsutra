# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 6
# Mahachara Katha (મહાચાર કથા) — The Great Account of Conduct

**Adhyayan 6 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 240–286 (PDF pages 302–348)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: ~69*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The sixth adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is named **Mahachara Katha** — "The Great Account of Conduct." Where the third adhyayan (Kshullakachar Katha) gave a brief account of the fifty-two anacharas (forbidden acts) that a monk must avoid, this much longer adhyayan returns to the same territory and unfolds it in full. Here the eighteen *sthanas* (sites, or stations) of right conduct are explained one by one, from every angle — rule and exception, prohibition and permission, the harm that follows from breaking each rule, and the gain that follows from keeping it.

The eighteen sthanas are: (1) non-violence, (2) truthfulness, (3) non-stealing, (4) celibacy, (5) non-possession, (6) renunciation of night-eating; the six classes of life-protection — (7) earth-bodies, (8) water-bodies, (9) fire-bodies, (10) air-bodies, (11) plant-bodies, (12) mobile beings; and the six rules of monastic discipline — (13) avoidance of forbidden food (akalpya-varjana), (14) avoidance of householder's prepared food (gruhastha-bhojana), (15) not sitting on beds and seats (palang), (16) not sitting in the householder's home (gruh), (17) the vow against bathing (asnan), and (18) renunciation of bodily ornamentation (vibhusha).

Together these eighteen form the "great conduct" — *mahachara* — that defines the monk's life. The chapter opens with kings, ministers, brahmins, and warriors approaching an Acharya in a garden outside the city, asking him to explain the conduct of Jain monks. The Acharya's answer becomes this entire adhyayan. The text is sometimes also called *Dharmarthakama* — "for those who desire the goal of dharma" — taking its name from a phrase in the fourth gatha. The conduct it describes is called *bhima* (terrible, awe-inspiring) and *duradhishtita* (extremely difficult to undertake) — not because Jain dharma is harsh, but because true non-violence, true truthfulness, and true non-possession require a level of inner watchfulness that ordinary worldly life never demands.

The chapter closes by declaring that this conduct alone leads to liberation; that it is unmatched in any other tradition, past, present, or future; and that whoever — young or old, healthy or sick — undertakes it without break and without crack walks the path of the Tirthankaras themselves.

## SUTRA 6.1–6.2 — The Question of the Kings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णाणदंसणसंपण्णं, संजमे य तवे रयं ।
गणिमागमसंपण्णं, उज्जाणम्मि समोसढं ॥६.१॥

रायाणो रायमच्चा य, माहणा अदुव खत्तिया ।
पुच्छंति निहुयप्पाणो, कहं भे आयारगोयरो ॥६.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.1 ||
ṇāṇadaṃsaṇasaṃpaṇṇaṃ, saṃjame ya tave rayaṃ |
gaṇimāgamasaṃpaṇṇaṃ, ujjāṇammi samosaḍhaṃ ||

|| 6.2 ||
rāyāṇo rāyamaccā ya, māhaṇā aduva khattiyā |
pucchaṃti nihuyappāṇo, kahaṃ bhe āyāragoyaro ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

To the Acharya — endowed with knowledge and right perception, absorbed in restraint and austerity, accomplished in the scriptures, the leader of the spiritual order, seated in a garden outside the city — kings, ministers, brahmins, and warriors, with composed and steady minds, approach and ask: "What, O Reverend One, is the conduct and the begging-discipline of your monks?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णाणदंसणसंपण्णं** (ṇāṇadaṃsaṇasaṃpaṇṇaṃ) = endowed with knowledge (jñāna) and right perception (darśana)
- **संजमे य तवे रयं** (saṃjame ya tave rayaṃ) = absorbed in restraint (saṃyama) and austerity (tapa)
- **गणिमागमसंपण्णं** (gaṇim āgamasaṃpaṇṇaṃ) = the gaṇi (head of the gaṇa, the spiritual community), accomplished in the āgama (scriptures — eleven Aṅgas, fourteen Pūrvas)
- **उज्जाणम्मि समोसढं** (ujjāṇammi samosaḍhaṃ) = seated in the udyāna (garden / grove outside the city)
- **रायाणो** (rāyāṇo) = kings
- **रायमच्चा** (rāyamaccā) = royal ministers
- **माहणा** (māhaṇā) = brahmins
- **अदुव** (aduva) = or
- **खत्तिया** (khattiyā) = kshatriyas (warriors)
- **पुच्छंति** (pucchaṃti) = ask
- **निहुयप्पाणो** (nihuyappāṇo) = with composed, steady, undisturbed minds (niścala-citta)
- **कहं** (kahaṃ) = of what kind, how
- **भे** (bhe) = your (your monks')
- **आयारगोयरो** (āyāragoyaro) = ācāra (right conduct) and gocara (begging-discipline)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with a scene rather than a doctrine. Outside the city, in a garden, sits an Acharya — a teacher complete in five qualities: endowed with knowledge, endowed with perception, absorbed in restraint, absorbed in austerity, and a master of the scriptures. To him come people of every station of worldly life — kings who govern, ministers who advise, brahmins who carry the religious tradition of the land, and warriors who defend it. They do not come as adversaries or sceptics; they come *with composed minds*. The text uses the word *nihuyappāṇo* — "with steadied selves" — and the commentary stresses this is no idle curiosity. Only a settled, focused mind can receive a true answer; a restless questioner cannot hear what is given.

Their question is simple but vast: "What is the conduct of your monks?" The word *āyāra-goyaro* contains both *ācāra* (the inner code — the great vows, the rules of restraint, the rules of austerity) and *gocara* (the outer practice, especially the begging-round and the daily activities of monastic life). The questioners want to understand not only the philosophy but the living practice — what does a monk actually do, and why?

The whole of the rest of the adhyayan is the Acharya's answer. The frame is significant: the reply that follows is not addressed to monks alone, but to the world. It is conduct held up for inspection by kings and householders — meaning it must be defensible, intelligible, and worthy of universal respect. The Jain path does not hide; it sets its rules out in the open garden where any informed person may come and ask.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Important people from the city — kings, ministers, scholars, soldiers — come quietly and respectfully to a great spiritual teacher sitting outside the city, and ask him: "Please tell us — how do your monks actually live?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you want to truly understand something sacred, can you arrive at it the way these kings did — with a steadied mind, leaving the noise of your station behind?

## SUTRA 6.3 — The Acharya, Calm and Skilled, Replies

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेसिं सो णिहुओ दंतो, सव्वभूयसुहावहो ।
सिक्खाए सुसमाउत्तो, आयक्खइ वियक्खणो ॥६.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.3 ||
tesiṃ so ṇihuo daṃto, savvabhūyasuhāvaho |
sikkhāe susamāutto, āyakkhai viyakkhaṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

To them, that one — calm, master of the senses, the bringer of well-being to all living beings, fully equipped with right learning, the discerning Acharya — gives his answer.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेसिं** (tesiṃ) = to them (the questioners)
- **सो** (so) = he (the Acharya)
- **णिहुओ** (ṇihuo) = composed, free of fear, of steady mind
- **दंतो** (daṃto) = the tamer of the senses (dānta)
- **सव्वभूयसुहावहो** (savvabhūyasuhāvaho) = the bringer of well-being to all living beings
- **सिक्खाए सुसमाउत्तो** (sikkhāe susamāutto) = perfectly equipped with right learning (śikṣā — both the learning of scripture and the practice of conduct)
- **आयक्खइ** (āyakkhai) = explains, narrates, replies
- **वियक्खणो** (viyakkhaṇo) = the discerning one, the wise one (vicakṣaṇa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Before the answer comes, the answerer is described — and described in five qualities, exactly matching the Acharya-image of the previous gathas. The text is teaching by the very form of its sentences: a true teacher must be these five things before his words can carry weight.

He is *composed* — free from anxiety, free from the eagerness to impress. He is *master of his senses* — not pulled by what he sees and hears, so that his answer comes from settled awareness rather than from reaction. He is *the bringer of well-being to all beings* — meaning his teaching is not for the benefit of one party, one class, or even one species; whoever hears it should be lifted up. He is *equipped with right learning* — both the textual learning (scriptures studied) and the practical learning (conduct lived). And he is *discerning* — he knows which question deserves which answer, when to speak briefly and when to expand.

The deeper point is that the Jain teaching is not delivered abstractly. It is delivered through a person who has become it. The conduct described in this adhyayan is the very conduct the Acharya himself embodies — and it is because he embodies it that the kings and brahmins are willing to come and listen. Words follow life. Lifeless words cannot transmit dharma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The teacher who answers is not just a knowledgeable man — he is calm, free of grasping, kind to every creature, fully trained, and wise enough to know what to say. Only such a person can really teach the path.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone asks you about something you know — do you answer from a settled inner place, or from the urge to be seen as knowing?

## SUTRA 6.4 — Listen, You Who Desire the Goal of Dharma

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

हंदि धम्मत्थकामाणं, निग्गंथाणं सुणेह मे ।
आयारगोयरं भीमं, सयलं दुरहिट्ठियं ॥६.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.4 ||
haṃdi dhammatthakāmāṇaṃ, niggaṃthāṇaṃ suṇeha me |
āyāragoyaraṃ bhīmaṃ, sayalaṃ durahiṭṭhiyaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Listen now — hear from me the entire conduct and discipline of the unattached ones (the Nirgranthas) who desire only the goal of dharma; a conduct awe-inspiring, complete, and exceedingly difficult for ordinary people to undertake."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **हंदि** (haṃdi) = come now! / listen! (an attention-calling particle)
- **धम्मत्थकामाणं** (dhammatthakāmāṇaṃ) = of those whose desire (kāma) is the goal (artha) of dharma — i.e., of those who seek liberation alone
- **निग्गंथाणं** (niggaṃthāṇaṃ) = of the Nirgranthas — the unattached ones, free of all knots (granthi) of inner and outer possession
- **सुणेह मे** (suṇeha me) = hear from me
- **आयारगोयरं** (āyāragoyaraṃ) = the conduct (ācāra) and the begging-discipline (gocara)
- **भीमं** (bhīmaṃ) = awe-inspiring, terrible (in the sense: severe, demanding)
- **सयलं** (sayalaṃ) = entire, complete
- **दुरहिट्ठियं** (durahiṭṭhiyaṃ) = duradhiṣṭhita — exceedingly difficult to firmly undertake

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Acharya now opens the answer with three loaded words: *bhima* (awe-inspiring), *sayala* (complete), and *durahitthiya* (very hard to undertake). He is not selling the path. He is naming it accurately. Liberation requires a way of life that, heard plainly, makes ordinary listeners shiver — pulling out one's hair by hand, walking on bare feet for life, never using a vehicle, enduring the discomforts of weather, complete celibacy, complete renunciation of night-eating, the most subtle non-violence, owning nothing, accepting no money. The text uses *bhima* — "terrible" — for exactly this reason. Listening to the rules can frighten an ordinary person.

But notice to whom he addresses the answer: *dhammatthakāmāṇaṃ niggaṃthāṇaṃ* — "to the Nirgranthas who desire only the goal of dharma." This is the qualifying frame. For those whose only desire is liberation, the conduct is not terrible at all; it is the path home. For those who still want pleasures, status, and ease, the conduct will sound impossible. The same set of rules is freedom for one and prison for another, depending on what the heart actually wants.

The word *Nirgrantha* — "without knots" — is the older Jain self-designation, predating the more familiar Shvetambara and Digambara labels. It points to the essence of monkhood: not robes, not orders, not lineages, but the dissolution of every knot — the outer knots of property and the inner knots of attachment, hatred, and ego. The conduct that follows is the technique by which those knots come undone.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The teacher says: "Listen carefully. I am about to describe the way of life of those who want nothing except liberation. It is severe, complete, and very hard for ordinary people to even begin — but it is the true path."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If liberation were truly the only thing you wanted, would the rules that now sound severe still sound severe?

## SUTRA 6.5 — Found in No Other Tradition, Past or Future

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णण्णत्थ एरिसं वुत्तं, जं लोए परम दुच्चरं ।
विउलट्ठाणभाइस्स, ण भूयं ण भविस्सइ ॥६.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.5 ||
ṇaṇṇattha erisaṃ vuttaṃ, jaṃ loe parama duccaraṃ |
viulaṭṭhāṇabhāissa, ṇa bhūyaṃ ṇa bhavissai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Such a conduct as this — the most difficult to walk in the whole world, belonging to those who hold the great spiritual stations — has not been taught anywhere else; it has not existed in the past in any other tradition, and it will not arise in the future in any other tradition.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णण्णत्थ** (ṇaṇṇattha) = nowhere else, in no other tradition / school
- **एरिसं** (erisaṃ) = of this kind, like this
- **वुत्तं** (vuttaṃ) = has been spoken / taught
- **जं** (jaṃ) = which
- **लोए** (loe) = in the world
- **परम दुच्चरं** (parama duccaraṃ) = the supremely hard to walk
- **विउलट्ठाणभाइस्स** (viulaṭṭhāṇabhāissa) = of those who hold the vipula-sthāna — the vast / great stations (the great spiritual practices and stages leading to liberation)
- **ण भूयं** (ṇa bhūyaṃ) = was not (in past time)
- **ण भविस्सइ** (ṇa bhavissai) = will not be (in future time)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha makes the boldest claim of the chapter. The Acharya declares that the conduct he is about to describe is found nowhere else — not in any other tradition of the past, not in any other tradition that will arise in the future. The commentary is careful to note that this is not boastful self-promotion; it is a precise factual statement about the nature of the path. The full set of rules — the most subtle non-violence even toward earth, water, fire, air, and plant lives; perfectly truthful and harm-free speech; ownerless living; absolute celibacy; the renunciation of night-eating; the rejection of bathing and ornamentation as inner-purity practices — this complete combination is unique to the Jin-shāsana, the teaching of the Tirthankaras.

The point is not sectarian pride. The point is that liberation requires an exact technology, and the Tirthankaras are the ones who have mapped it after attaining complete self-knowledge. Any path that omits even one element of the technology cannot reach the same destination. A good map for getting to a nearby village will not get you to the summit of Everest; only the right map for the right destination will work. This adhyayan is offering the right map.

The phrase *viula-tthana-bhai* — "those who hold the vast stations" — refers to the eighteen sthanas about to be enumerated. To "hold" a sthana is to make it one's permanent inner ground, not merely to perform it from time to time. That kind of holding, says the Acharya, is what no other tradition teaches in full.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The teacher says: "This exact way of life — every detail of it together — is not taught in any other religion or philosophy. It never has been, and never will be, found anywhere except in the teaching of the Tirthankaras."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are you willing to accept that the path to the highest goal might require something far more specific than the general spirituality you have so far accepted?

## SUTRA 6.6 — For Children, the Old, the Sick, and the Healthy Alike

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सखुड्डगवियत्ताणं, वाहियाणं च जे गुणा ।
अखंडफुडिया कायव्वा, तं सुणेह जहा तहा ॥६.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.6 ||
sakhuḍḍagaviyattāṇaṃ, vāhiyāṇaṃ ca je guṇā |
akhaṃḍaphuḍiyā kāyavvā, taṃ suṇeha jahā tahā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The qualities — the rules of conduct — that are to be observed by both the young and the mature, by the sick and the healthy alike, unbroken and uncracked, exactly as they are: hear them now, just as they truly are.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सखुड्डग** (sakhuḍḍaga) = together with the small / the young (kṣullaka — children, the immature)
- **वियत्ताणं** (viyattāṇaṃ) = of the mature, the grown (vyakta — fully developed in body and mind)
- **वाहियाणं** (vāhiyāṇaṃ) = of the diseased, the sick
- **च** (ca) = and (also implies: and the healthy)
- **जे गुणा** (je guṇā) = the qualities — meaning here, the rules of conduct
- **अखंड** (akhaṃḍa) = unbroken
- **फुडिया** (phuḍiyā) = uncracked, not split
- **कायव्वा** (kāyavvā) = to be done, to be observed
- **तं** (taṃ) = those (rules)
- **सुणेह** (suṇeha) = hear
- **जहा तहा** (jahā tahā) = exactly as they are

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous gatha could have been heard as elitist — a path so exalted it must belong to special people. This gatha corrects that immediately. The conduct about to be described is meant for *every* mumukshu (seeker of liberation), regardless of age and regardless of physical condition. Young or old, sick or healthy — the eighteen sthanas apply. Adjustments for circumstance exist (and the chapter will detail them), but the core rules are universal.

Two key words deserve attention. *Akhanda* means "unbroken" — observed continuously, not picked up and put down. *Phudiya* (Sanskrit *sphuṭita*) means "cracked" — and the negation *akhanda-aphudiya* means "not even cracked." A pot can be technically unbroken yet have hairline cracks that leak; conduct can be technically maintained yet riddled with small compromises that drain its power. The Acharya is asking for a conduct that is whole at every level — not just unbroken but uncracked.

This standard sounds severe, but the commentary explains it gently: it is the destination, not the starting point. The young practitioner begins where they are, the old practitioner adjusts what they must, the sick practitioner is given allowances by the Guru — but the inner aspiration is always the same: the unbroken, uncracked vow. Conduct that is asked of all, in proportion to capacity, but never abandoned by anyone.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The rules of conduct apply to everyone — young and old, sick and well — and they should be kept whole, with no breaks and no hidden cracks. Now hear them exactly as they are.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your own practice is there a hairline crack — something technically kept, but quietly leaking?

## SUTRA 6.7 — The Eighteen Stations: To Break Even One Is to Fall

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दस अट्ठ य ठाणाइं, जाइं बालोवरज्झइ ।
तत्थ अण्णयरे ठाणे, निग्गंथत्ताओ भस्सइ ॥६.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.7 ||
dasa aṭṭha ya ṭhāṇāiṃ, jāiṃ bālovarajjhai |
tattha aṇṇayare ṭhāṇe, niggaṃthattāo bhassai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There are eighteen stations of conduct; whichever immature seeker transgresses any one among them, falls from the state of being a Nirgrantha (an unattached monk).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दस अट्ठ य** (dasa aṭṭha ya) = ten and eight = eighteen
- **ठाणाइं** (ṭhāṇāiṃ) = stations (sthāna) — the eighteen sites of right conduct
- **जाइं** (jāiṃ) = which (the eighteen)
- **बालो** (bālo) = the immature seeker, the spiritually undeveloped (bāla)
- **अवरज्झइ** (avarajjhai) = transgresses, violates, acts contrary to
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there, among them
- **अण्णयरे ठाणे** (aṇṇayare ṭhāṇe) = in any one station
- **निग्गंथत्ताओ** (niggaṃthattāo) = from the state of being a Nirgrantha (the unattached monkhood)
- **भस्सइ** (bhassai) = falls, slips

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Acharya now names the structure of the entire chapter: there are *eighteen sthanas* — eighteen stations or sites of right conduct. The text uses the elegant compound *dasa-attha* ("ten and eight") rather than *atthaarasa* ("eighteen") to fit the metre, but the meaning is the same. These eighteen stations will be enumerated in the next gatha and explained one by one through the rest of the adhyayan.

The crucial teaching of this gatha is the standard of integrity: to break even *one* of the eighteen is to fall from monkhood. The Jain monastic ideal is not "keep most of the rules and you are mostly a monk." It is wholeness. The eighteen sthanas form a single fabric; pull one thread and the whole garment unravels. A monk who is perfect in seventeen but careless in the eighteenth is not seventeen-eighteenths a monk — he has lost the state of Nirgrantha-hood entirely until that lapse is repaired.

The word *bala* (translated as "immature seeker") is significant. It does not mean "child"; it means a practitioner who is spiritually undeveloped — one who lacks the discrimination to see why each rule matters. Such a person, taking even one rule lightly, slips. The mature practitioner (*panḍita*), by contrast, holds all eighteen with equal care because they understand the unity behind them. The standard is severe, but the standard is also what keeps monkhood from quietly hollowing out into appearance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There are eighteen rules of conduct. A foolish or careless monk who breaks even one of them stops being a true monk in that moment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you tell yourself "this one small thing doesn't really count," what is the deeper unity you are quietly tearing?

## SUTRA 6.8 — The Names of the Eighteen Stations

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वयछक्कं कायछक्कं, अकप्पो गिहिभायणं ।
पलियंकं णिसेज्जा य, सिणाणं सोहवज्जणं ॥६.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.8 ||
vayachakkaṃ kāyachakkaṃ, akappo gihibhāyaṇaṃ |
paliyaṃkaṃ ṇisejjā ya, siṇāṇaṃ sohavajjaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The six vows; the six classes of life-protection; (the avoidance of) forbidden food; (the avoidance of) eating from the householder's vessels; (the avoidance of) sitting on couches; (the avoidance of) sitting in the householder's home; the renunciation of bathing; and the renunciation of bodily ornamentation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वयछक्कं** (vayachakkaṃ) = the six vows (vrata-ṣaṭka) — the five great vows + the renunciation of night-eating
- **कायछक्कं** (kāyachakkaṃ) = the six classes of bodied beings (kāya-ṣaṭka) — earth, water, fire, air, plants, and mobile beings — to be protected
- **अकप्पो** (akappo) = (avoidance of) the akalpya — that which is forbidden, especially food and articles unfit for monks
- **गिहिभायणं** (gihibhāyaṇaṃ) = (avoidance of eating from) the householder's vessel
- **पलियंकं** (paliyaṃkaṃ) = (avoidance of) the palaṃka — the couch / bed / cushioned seat
- **णिसेज्जा** (ṇisejjā) = (avoidance of) sitting in the householder's home
- **सिणाणं** (siṇāṇaṃ) = (renunciation of) bathing (snāna)
- **सोहवज्जणं** (sohavajjaṇaṃ) = renunciation of bodily ornamentation / beautification (śobhā-varjana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This single gatha lists the entire structure of the rest of the adhyayan. Counted out, the eighteen are: (1–5) the five great vows of non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession; (6) renunciation of night-eating; (7–12) the six classes of life-protection — earth-bodies, water-bodies, fire-bodies, air-bodies, plant-bodies, and mobile beings; (13) avoidance of forbidden food and articles; (14) avoidance of eating from the householder's plates and vessels; (15) avoidance of sitting on cushioned couches and beds; (16) avoidance of sitting in the householder's home; (17) renunciation of bathing; (18) renunciation of ornamentation, jewellery, and bodily beautification.

The architecture is deliberate. The first six are the *internal* vows — what the monk swears not to do at the level of intent. The next six are the *external* applications of the first vow (non-violence) at the level of life-forms — non-violence is not merely toward humans and animals but toward every embodied being, even invisible ones. The final six are the *practical disciplines* of daily monastic life — what the monk does not eat, does not sit on, does not stay in, does not pour over himself, does not put on his body. Together they cover intent, ethical scope, and lived form.

The chapter from this point onward will take each of the eighteen and unfold it: stating the prohibition, giving the reason, naming the harm of breaking it, naming the benefit of keeping it. This gatha is the table of contents.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The eighteen rules are: the six vows (no killing, no lying, no stealing, no sex, no possessions, no night-eating); the protection of six classes of life (earth, water, fire, air, plants, and moving creatures); not taking forbidden food; not eating from the householder's plates; not sitting on beds; not sitting in the householder's house; not bathing; and not decorating the body.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a single life of yours had to be governed by eighteen exact rules, would the rules feel like chains — or like the supports of a building?

## SUTRA 6.9 — First Station: Non-Violence (Restraint Toward All Beings)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थिमं पढमं ठाणं, महावीरेण देसियं ।
अहिंसा णिउणा दिट्ठा, सव्वभूएसु संजमो ॥६.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.9 ||
tatthimaṃ paḍhamaṃ ṭhāṇaṃ, mahāvīreṇa desiyaṃ |
ahiṃsā ṇiuṇā diṭṭhā, savvabhūesu saṃjamo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Among them, this is the first station, taught by Lord Mahavira: non-violence — known and seen with subtle precision — which is restraint toward all living beings.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तत्थ इमं** (tattha imaṃ) = among them, this
- **पढमं ठाणं** (paḍhamaṃ ṭhāṇaṃ) = the first station
- **महावीरेण देसियं** (mahāvīreṇa desiyaṃ) = taught (deśita) by Mahavira
- **अहिंसा** (ahiṃsā) = non-violence
- **णिउणा दिट्ठा** (ṇiuṇā diṭṭhā) = seen with subtle precision (nipuṇa) — perceived completely and exactly by the omniscient one
- **सव्वभूएसु** (savvabhūesu) = toward all living beings
- **संजमो** (saṃjamo) = restraint (saṃyama)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Of the eighteen stations, the first — and the foundation of all the rest — is *ahiṃsā*: non-violence. The text adds an essential qualifier: *niuṇā diṭṭhā* — "seen with subtle precision." This is not vague kindness or general harmlessness. The non-violence taught by Mahavira is the non-violence as *seen by an omniscient eye* — meaning: perceived in its complete exactness, accounting for every form of life, every level of intent, every angle of harm. Other traditions accept non-violence as a principle; only the Jain tradition develops it to the level of subtle technical precision that includes the protection of single-sense beings (earth, water, fire, air, plant) along with multi-sense beings.

The gatha then equates non-violence with another word: *saṃyama* — restraint. The two are inseparable. Non-violence is the *result*; restraint of the senses, restraint of the mind, restraint of action toward all beings is the *means*. You cannot be non-violent without being restrained, and you cannot be truly restrained without becoming non-violent. This is why the traditional formula *ahimsa parmo dharma* ("non-violence is the supreme dharma") is in Jain understanding identical with *samyamo parmo dharma* ("restraint is the supreme dharma") — they are two faces of one practice.

The phrase *savva-bhuesu* — "toward all living beings" — sets the scope wider than perhaps any other ethical teaching in human history. Every being, from the microbe to the human, from the earth-body to the deva, is the subject of the vow. To live with this vow is to begin reading the world differently — every step, every breath, every drop of water, every movement of speech becomes a place where the vow is either kept or broken.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The first and most important rule, taught by Mahavira with complete precision, is non-violence — which means holding back from harm toward every living thing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you next reach for something — a switch, a glass of water, a quick word — can you pause and ask: who is being touched by what I am about to do?

## SUTRA 6.10 — All Beings, Knowing or Unknowing, Are Not to Be Harmed

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जावंति लोए पाणा, तसा अदुव थावरा ।
ते जाणमजाणं वा, ण हणे णो वि घायए ॥६.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.10 ||
jāvaṃti loe pāṇā, tasā aduva thāvarā |
te jāṇamajāṇaṃ vā, ṇa haṇe ṇo vi ghāyae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whatever living beings exist in the world, mobile or immobile, them — knowingly or unknowingly — one should not kill oneself, nor cause others to kill.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जावंति** (jāvaṃti) = however many
- **लोए** (loe) = in the world
- **पाणा** (pāṇā) = living beings (prāṇa)
- **तसा** (tasā) = mobile (trasa) — beings with two or more senses that can move
- **अदुव** (aduva) = or
- **थावरा** (thāvarā) = immobile (sthāvara) — beings with only one sense (earth, water, fire, air, plants)
- **ते** (te) = them
- **जाणं** (jāṇaṃ) = knowingly
- **अजाणं** (ajāṇaṃ) = unknowingly
- **वा** (vā) = or
- **ण हणे** (ṇa haṇe) = (one) should not kill oneself
- **णो वि घायए** (ṇo vi ghāyae) = nor cause others to kill (and by implication, nor approve of others killing)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha extends the first vow into its full scope. Two divisions are named: *trasa* (mobile beings — anything with two or more senses, capable of voluntary movement) and *sthāvara* (immobile beings — earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, and plant-bodied, each having only the single sense of touch). The vow covers both. The Jain analysis is unique in its insistence that the single-sense beings — the speck of soil, the drop of water, the flame, the puff of air, the blade of grass — are also living beings deserving protection.

The gatha specifies *jāṇaṃ ajāṇaṃ vā* — "knowingly or unknowingly." This is the true test of the vow. To not kill *knowingly* is the easy half; to not kill *unknowingly* requires the cultivation of constant awareness — the discipline of looking before stepping, sweeping before sitting, seeing before drinking. The unknowing harms are the ones that sustained spiritual practice slowly eliminates, not by adding rules but by deepening attentiveness.

The classical formula completes the vow with three modes: *na haṇe* (do not kill yourself), *no vi ghāyae* (do not cause others to kill), and — implicit in the larger structure of the vow — do not approve when others kill. This is the navakoti (nine-fold) frame familiar from the Karemi Bhante: three karanas (do, cause, approve) by three yogas (mind, speech, body). The full vow seals all nine doors against violence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Every living thing in the world — whether it can move, like an animal, or whether it is still, like a plant or a drop of water — should not be killed by you, whether you knew it was alive or not, and you should not get others to kill it either.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of the harm you do every day is unknown to you — and what would change if you committed to seeing it?

## SUTRA 6.11 — Every Being Wants to Live; None Wants to Die

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वे जीवा वि इच्छंति, जीविउं ण मरिज्जिउं ।
तम्हा पाणिवहं घोरं, निग्गंथा वज्जयंति णं ॥६.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.11 ||
savve jīvā vi icchaṃti, jīviuṃ ṇa marijjiuṃ |
tamhā pāṇivahaṃ ghoraṃ, niggaṃthā vajjayaṃti ṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

All living beings wish to live; none wishes to die. Therefore the Nirgranthas renounce the terrible act of killing living beings.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सव्वे जीवा वि** (savve jīvā vi) = all living beings (souls)
- **इच्छंति** (icchaṃti) = wish, desire
- **जीविउं** (jīviuṃ) = to live
- **ण मरिज्जिउं** (ṇa marijjiuṃ) = not to die
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **पाणिवहं** (pāṇivahaṃ) = killing of living beings (prāṇi-vadha)
- **घोरं** (ghoraṃ) = terrible, dreadful
- **निग्गंथा** (niggaṃthā) = the Nirgranthas (unattached monks)
- **वज्जयंति** (vajjayaṃti) = renounce, abandon, abstain from
- **णं** (ṇaṃ) = (an emphatic particle for completeness)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha gives the *reason* for the first vow, and in giving it Mahavira sets out one of the most radical ethical principles in human thought: the fact that every living being — without exception — wants to live and does not want to die is itself the entire foundation of non-violence. There is no need for theology, no need for command, no need for divine sanction. The will-to-live in the other being *is* the moral demand on you.

The argument is symmetrical. *I* want to live and do not want to die. *Every other being* wants the same. To kill another is to inflict on them what I would refuse to have inflicted on me. This is the universal Jain answer to the question "why be non-violent?" — not because a god orders it, not because of consequences in another life, but because the will-to-live is the same in every soul, and recognising that sameness is the awakening of the moral self.

The text uses the strong word *ghora* — "terrible" — for the act of killing. Not "regrettable," not "unhelpful" — *terrible*. The killing of a living being, however small, is a violent rupture of the universe of wills-to-live, and the practitioner who has truly seen this cannot do it. The Nirgrantha therefore *renounces* it — *vajjayaṃti* — completely, by all three karanas and all three yogas.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Every single living thing in the world wants to live and does not want to die. That is why the monks completely refuse to do the terrible act of killing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you allow yourself to feel — really feel — that the smallest creature wants to live exactly as you want to live, what changes inside you?

## SUTRA 6.12 — Second Station: Truthfulness (No Harmful or Untrue Speech)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्पणट्ठा परट्ठा वा, कोहा वा जइ वा भया ।
हिंसगं ण मुसं बूया, णो वि अण्णं वयावए ॥६.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.12 ||
appaṇaṭṭhā paraṭṭhā vā, kohā vā jai vā bhayā |
hiṃsagaṃ ṇa musaṃ būyā, ṇo vi aṇṇaṃ vayāvae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whether for one's own sake or for another's, whether out of anger or out of fear, one should not speak harmful or untrue words oneself, nor cause another to speak them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अप्पणट्ठा** (appaṇaṭṭhā) = for one's own sake (ātma-arthe)
- **परट्ठा वा** (paraṭṭhā vā) = or for another's sake
- **कोहा वा** (kohā vā) = or out of anger (krodha) — and by implication, also out of pride, deceit, and greed (the four kashayas)
- **जइ वा भया** (jai vā bhayā) = or out of fear — and by implication, also for amusement (hāsya)
- **हिंसगं** (hiṃsagaṃ) = harmful (to others), violence-causing
- **ण मुसं बूया** (ṇa musaṃ būyā) = should not speak (the) untrue / false (mṛṣā)
- **णो वि अण्णं वयावए** (ṇo vi aṇṇaṃ vayāvae) = nor cause another to speak (it)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second sthana is truthfulness. This gatha names the typical motivations from which untruthful or harmful speech arises — and then forbids them all. *For one's own sake* (to gain something, to escape blame); *for another's sake* (to help a friend, to flatter a patron); *out of anger* (and by implication, also out of pride, deceit, and greed — the four passions); *out of fear* (and by implication, also for amusement, to entertain). All five classical causes of false speech are covered.

Notice that the gatha does not simply say "do not speak untruth." It says "do not speak *harmful* untruth" — *hiṃsagaṃ musaṃ*. The Jain second vow is not the same as Western "honesty"; it is more refined. Speech is to be both *true* and *non-harmful*. A truth that wounds without cause is itself a violation of the second vow. The vow demands speech that is true, beneficial, kind, and timely — speech that does not become a weapon, even when factually correct.

And as with all the vows, the prohibition extends not only to speaking falsely oneself but to causing another to speak falsely — and by the larger structure, to approving of another's false speech. The vow covers all three karanas. The monk who would never tell a lie himself but happily quotes another's lie has not fully kept the vow.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Whether to help yourself or someone else, whether out of anger or out of fear — never say anything false or hurtful, and never get someone else to say it for you.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the five reasons people lie — gain, helping a friend, anger, fear, fun — which one is your weakest spot, and what truth would you have to face to close it?

## SUTRA 6.13 — False Speech Is Censured by All; the Liar Is Trusted by None

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मुसावाओ उ लोगम्मि, सव्वसाहूहिं गरहिओ ।
अविस्सासो य भूयाणं, तम्हा मोसं विवज्जए ॥६.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.13 ||
musāvāo u logammi, savvasāhūhiṃ garahio |
avissāso ya bhūyāṇaṃ, tamhā mosaṃ vivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

False speech is censured in the world by all good persons; (the liar becomes) untrustworthy to all living beings; therefore one should completely renounce falsehood.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मुसावाओ** (musāvāo) = false speech (mṛṣāvāda)
- **उ** (u) = (a particle)
- **लोगम्मि** (logammi) = in the world
- **सव्वसाहूहिं** (savvasāhūhiṃ) = by all good / saintly persons
- **गरहिओ** (garahio) = censured, condemned
- **अविस्सासो** (avissāso) = untrustworthy, untrustworthiness
- **य** (ya) = and
- **भूयाणं** (bhūyāṇaṃ) = to (all) living beings
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **मोसं** (mosaṃ) = falsehood (mṛṣā)
- **विवज्जए** (vivajjae) = (one) should completely abandon (vivarjayet)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The gatha gives two reasons to abandon false speech, one social and one spiritual. The social reason: *false speech is condemned by all good persons everywhere*. Across every culture and tradition, the liar is despised — there is no school of thought in human history that praises lying as a virtue. The Buddhist five precepts include the abstention from false speech; the Brahmanical, Christian, Islamic, and secular ethical systems all agree. The Jain teaching simply observes that on this point, all wisdom traditions converge, and the practitioner should accept that universal verdict.

The spiritual reason: *the liar becomes untrustworthy to all living beings*. Trust, once lost, is extraordinarily hard to rebuild. A person who has been caught lying once is doubted twice; caught twice, doubted always. And this loss of trust is not just an external inconvenience — it cuts the practitioner off from the community of fellow seekers, from the Guru, from the householders who support the order. Without trust, the spiritual ecosystem cannot function. The liar isolates themselves.

The commentary lists the typical failures of the second vow that arise from each of the four passions: lying out of anger (calling someone a slave when they are not); lying out of pride (claiming to be a scholar when one is not); lying out of deceit (complaining of foot pain to avoid the begging-round); lying out of greed (calling tasteless food unworthy when it is simply not what one wanted); lying out of fear (denying a fault to escape penance); lying for amusement (jokes that distort the truth). All of these the practitioner is to abandon.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lying is condemned everywhere by every wise person, and a liar ends up being trusted by no one. So give up all forms of false speech, completely.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you lied "for a small reason" — and what did it cost the deeper trust between you and the world?

## SUTRA 6.14–6.15 — Third Station: Non-Stealing (Take Nothing Without Permission)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चित्तमंतमचित्तं वा, अप्पं वा जइ वा बहुं ।
दंतसोहणमित्तं पि, उग्गहंसि अजाइया ॥६.१४॥

तं अप्पणा ण गिण्हंति, णो वि गिण्हावए परं ।
अण्णं वा गिण्हमाणंपि, णाणुजाणंति संजया ॥६.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.14 ||
cittamaṃtamacittaṃ vā, appaṃ vā jai vā bahuṃ |
daṃtasohaṇamittaṃ pi, uggahaṃsi ajāiyā ||

|| 6.15 ||
taṃ appaṇā ṇa giṇhaṃti, ṇo vi giṇhāvae paraṃ |
aṇṇaṃ vā giṇhamāṇaṃpi, ṇāṇujāṇaṃti saṃjayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Anything — sentient or non-sentient, small or large, even so much as a toothpick — that lies within (another's) holding and has not been formally requested: that, the restrained ones do not take themselves, nor cause others to take, nor approve when others take.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चित्तमंतं** (cittamaṃtaṃ) = sentient (with consciousness — e.g., a disciple who would join the order)
- **अचित्तं वा** (acittaṃ vā) = or non-sentient (cloth, vessel, food, medicine)
- **अप्पं वा** (appaṃ vā) = small (in size, quantity, or value)
- **जइ वा बहुं** (jai vā bahuṃ) = or large (in size, quantity, or value)
- **दंतसोहणमित्तं पि** (daṃtasohaṇamittaṃ pi) = even as much as a toothpick (a wisp of grass for cleaning the teeth)
- **उग्गहंसि** (uggahaṃsi) = within (another's) holding / domain (avagraha — that which belongs to another's authority)
- **अजाइया** (ajāiyā) = without having formally asked (ayācitvā)
- **तं** (taṃ) = that (object)
- **अप्पणा ण गिण्हंति** (appaṇā ṇa giṇhaṃti) = (they) do not take themselves
- **णो वि गिण्हावए परं** (ṇo vi giṇhāvae paraṃ) = nor cause another to take
- **अण्णं वा गिण्हमाणंपि** (aṇṇaṃ vā giṇhamāṇaṃpi) = or another who is taking
- **णाणुजाणंति** (ṇāṇujāṇaṃti) = (they) do not approve / consent
- **संजया** (saṃjayā) = the restrained ones (saṃyata — the monks)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third sthana is *adattadana-virati* — refraining from taking what has not been given. This pair of gathas defines stealing in the most refined form found in any ethical tradition. Stealing is the taking of *anything* — sentient or non-sentient, large or small, *even a toothpick* — that is within another's authority, without that authority's permission. The phrase *daṃtasohaṇamittaṃ pi* — "even as much as a toothpick" — is the famous Jain marker: there is no such thing as a "trivial" violation of this vow. To take a blade of grass to clean one's teeth without asking the owner is theft.

The first gatha names two important categories. *Sentient* objects include disciples — to "ordain" someone as a monk without the permission of their parents would itself be stealing (taking a person from another's authority without consent). *Non-sentient* objects include the standard articles a monk uses: clothes, vessels, food, medicine. All of these come from householders; all must be received by formal asking. The second pair, *small or large*, removes the loophole by which the mind reasons "it was so little, it doesn't count."

The second gatha then completes the vow with the navakoti (nine-fold) seal: not taken oneself, not caused to be taken by another, not approved when another takes. The restrained one — *saṃyata* — closes all three doors. The principle is luminous: ownership is real, and respecting another's boundary even down to a wisp of grass is the practice that dissolves the deeper grasping at the root of all human conflict. Where stealing exists, trust collapses; where this vow is kept, even the smallest objects become safe in the open.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monks do not take *anything* — living or non-living, big or small, even a tiny toothpick — that belongs to someone else without first asking permission. They do not take it themselves, do not get others to take it, and do not approve when others take it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How many things in your day have you "just taken" — time, attention, a small item, an idea — without ever formally asking for permission?

## SUTRA 6.16 — Fourth Station: Celibacy (The Awe-Inspiring Vow)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अबंभचरियं घोरं, पमायं दुरहिट्ठियं ।
णायरंति मुणी लोए, भेयायतणवज्जिणो ॥६.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.16 ||
abaṃbhacariyaṃ ghoraṃ, pamāyaṃ durahiṭṭhiyaṃ |
ṇāyaraṃti muṇī loe, bheyāyataṇavajjiṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monks in the world, who avoid every site of breakage of the vow, do not practise un-celibacy — which is terrible, full of negligence, and exceedingly hard to undertake (in the sense of being a path full of suffering).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अबंभचरियं** (abaṃbhacariyaṃ) = un-celibacy (abrahmacarya) — sexual conduct
- **घोरं** (ghoraṃ) = terrible (in result)
- **पमायं** (pamāyaṃ) = full of negligence (pramāda — heedlessness, the principal cause of karmic bondage)
- **दुरहिट्ठियं** (durahiṭṭhiyaṃ) = exceedingly hard to undertake; (here in the sense of) hard-to-bear in its consequences, undesirable as a refuge
- **णायरंति** (ṇāyaraṃti) = (they) do not practise / do not engage in
- **मुणी** (muṇī) = the monks (muni)
- **लोए** (loe) = in the world
- **भेयायतणवज्जिणो** (bheyāyataṇavajjiṇo) = those who avoid every site (āyatana) of the breakage (bheda) of the vow

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth sthana is *brahmacarya* — celibacy, literally "wandering in Brahman, wandering in the Self." The word means much more than physical chastity; it means dwelling in one's true nature, undistracted by sensual pursuit. *Un-celibacy* is here described with three sharp adjectives: *ghora* (terrible — leading to terrible suffering), *pamāya* (the very embodiment of negligence — the cardinal vice that opens the door to all karmic bondage), and *durahiṭṭhiya* (a place exceedingly difficult to occupy — meaning, full of pain, no true refuge). Sexual indulgence is described not in terms of moral disapproval but in terms of its actual phenomenology in a spiritual life: it is a place of suffering masquerading as a place of pleasure.

The phrase *bheyāyatana-vajjino* — "those who avoid every site of the breakage" — is the practical key to keeping this vow. The Jain analysis identifies many "sites" where the vow is at risk: living in places where the opposite sex resides, listening to talk about the opposite sex, gazing at the limbs of the opposite sex, sitting alone with the opposite sex, recalling past sexual experiences, eating rich and stimulating food, sleeping in soft beds, using ornamentation. The practitioner who would keep the vow does not merely resist temptation when it arises — they avoid the *sites* where temptation arises in the first place. The defence is structural, not heroic.

This is one of the most psychologically realistic teachings in the chapter. The text does not say "be strong against desire"; it says "do not enter the rooms where desire is born." A monk who avoids the nine fences (nava-vāḍa) around celibacy keeps the vow without strain; a monk who steps inside them and trusts willpower to hold will sooner or later fall.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monks do not engage in any sexual conduct, because it leads to terrible suffering, is the heart of carelessness, and is a place of pain disguised as pleasure. And they avoid every situation that could even bring such conduct close.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "rooms" do you enter — physical, mental, digital — where the very atmosphere makes a slip almost certain? What would change if you simply did not enter them?

## SUTRA 6.17 — Un-Celibacy: The Root of Un-Dharma, the Storehouse of Great Faults

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मूलमेयमहम्मस्स, महादोससमुस्सयं ।
तम्हा मेहुणसंसग्गं, निग्गंथा वज्जयंति णं ॥६.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.17 ||
mūlameyamahammassa, mahādosasamussayaṃ |
tamhā mehuṇasaṃsaggaṃ, niggaṃthā vajjayaṃti ṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

This (un-celibacy) is the root of un-dharma, the towering accumulation of great faults; therefore the Nirgranthas renounce sexual contact entirely.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मूलमेय** (mūlam eyaṃ) = this (is the) root (mūla)
- **अहम्मस्स** (ahammassa) = of un-dharma (adharma) — of all that is contrary to the path
- **महादोससमुस्सयं** (mahādosasamussayaṃ) = a great accumulation (samucchraya) of great faults (mahā-doṣa)
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **मेहुणसंसग्गं** (mehuṇasaṃsaggaṃ) = sexual contact / association (maithuna-saṃsarga)
- **निग्गंथा** (niggaṃthā) = the Nirgranthas
- **वज्जयंति णं** (vajjayaṃti ṇaṃ) = renounce, abandon (it) entirely

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha gives the *reason* for the celibacy vow — and it is unsparing. Un-celibacy is named the *root* of all un-dharma — the source from which all other failures of the spiritual life flow — and a *great accumulation of great faults*. The commentary lists what follows from sexual indulgence: dishonesty (one lies to obtain or conceal it), deceit, theft (one steals to provide for the dependent family that follows), even violence (in jealous rage). One sin gives birth to many; sexual involvement opens the floodgate.

There is also the practical reasoning of *family*. The moment sexual conduct produces a partner, then children, then dependents — the entire household structure follows, and the monk's renunciation is finished. The Jain order protects the renunciant's ability to remain renunciant by closing this door at the root. As long as celibacy holds, none of the entanglements of householder life can grow; once celibacy breaks, all of them follow.

The text uses the strong term *saṃsarga* — *contact, association* — rather than only the act itself. The Nirgranthas renounce not only sexual conduct but the *association* that produces it — the company, the conversations, the gazing, the imaginative dwelling. The mature spiritual life closes the entire ecology of the vice, not just the final act. This is not prudery; it is the realistic acknowledgement that an embodied being cannot rely on willpower at the last moment. The vow is kept by structuring one's whole life so that the last moment never arises.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Sexual conduct is the root of everything that goes wrong in spiritual life and the source of countless other faults. So the monks completely give up not just the act, but every form of association and contact that leads to it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If one habit could be called the "root" of your own falling-away from your highest values — what would it be, and would you be willing to close it at the root?

## SUTRA 6.18 — Fifth Station: Non-Possession (The Storing of Anything Is Forbidden)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बिडमुब्भेइमं लोणं, तिल्लं सप्पिं च फाणियं ।
ण ते सण्णिहिमिच्छंति, णायपुत्तवओरया ॥६.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.18 ||
biḍamubbheimaṃ loṇaṃ, tillaṃ sappiṃ ca phāṇiyaṃ |
ṇa te saṇṇihimicchaṃti, ṇāyaputtavaorayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Salt that is fire-prepared, salt that is mineral, oil, ghee, and liquid jaggery — those who are devoted to the words of the Son of the Jnata clan (Lord Mahavira) do not desire to store such things even (overnight).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बिड** (biḍa) = fire-prepared salt (roasted / baked salt — bidlavaṇa, sekela mīṭhu, rendered acit)
- **उब्भेइमं** (ubbheimaṃ) = mineral salt extracted (from earth) by means of weapons / tools (udbhid — extracted, made acit by being broken with a tool)
- **लोणं** (loṇaṃ) = salt (lavaṇa)
- **तिल्लं** (tillaṃ) = oil (taila)
- **सप्पिं** (sappiṃ) = ghee (sarpis — clarified butter)
- **च फाणियं** (ca phāṇiyaṃ) = and liquid jaggery (phāṇita — molten / liquid gur)
- **ण** (ṇa) = not
- **ते** (te) = they (the monks)
- **सण्णिहिं** (saṇṇihiṃ) = storage / hoarding (sannidhi — keeping food overnight or for later use)
- **इच्छंति** (icchaṃti) = wish, desire
- **णायपुत्त** (ṇāyaputta) = the Son of the Jnata clan — Lord Mahavira
- **वओरया** (vaorayā) = devoted to (his) words / teaching (vaca-rata)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fifth sthana is *aparigraha* — non-possession. It begins not with grand renunciations but with a simple list of food items: prepared salt, mineral salt, oil, ghee, and liquid jaggery. Why this list? Because these are the items that, unlike fresh-cooked food, can be *stored* — they last overnight, they keep for days. The Jain monastic principle is that whatever can be stored is the doorway to possessiveness, and the doorway must be closed.

The keyword is *sannidhi* — "storage." A monk receives food on each day's begging-round and consumes it on that day; nothing is kept for tomorrow. This is the practical face of non-possession: trust that tomorrow's food will come, today, fresh, by the begging-round. The moment one keeps a jar of ghee for "in case I cannot get any tomorrow," the inner stance has shifted from non-possession to possession — and with it comes the entire psychology of the householder, who plans, hoards, and protects.

The phrase *ṇāyaputta-vaorayā* — "devoted to the words of the Son of the Jnata clan" — is a beautiful self-designation. *Ñātaputra* (the Son of Jnata) is one of the names of Mahavira. To be *vaca-rata* — "delighted in his words" — is to be a true follower. The gatha is saying: those whose hearts are tuned to Mahavira's teaching simply *do not want* to store. The vow is not enforced from outside; it is a natural expression of devotion to the teaching.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even simple long-lasting food items — like salt, oil, ghee, or jaggery — the monks do not want to store. Those who love Mahavira's teaching simply do not desire to keep anything for later.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What do you store "just in case" — and is the storing protecting you, or quietly building the very anxiety it claims to defeat?

## SUTRA 6.19 — Even the Wish to Store Is the Touch of Greed

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लोहस्सेस अणुफासो, मण्णे अण्णयरामवि ।
जे सिया सण्णिहीकामे, गिही पव्वइए ण से ॥६.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.19 ||
lohasses aṇuphāso, maṇṇe aṇṇayarāmavi |
je siyā saṇṇihīkāme, gihī pavvaie ṇa se ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The Tirthankaras) declare: any storage at all of any thing whatsoever is itself a touch of greed; whoever wishes to store is a householder, not an ordained one.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लोहस्स** (lohassa) = of greed (lobha)
- **एस अणुफासो** (esa aṇuphāso) = this (is) a touch / contact (anusparśa) — the trace, the influence, the staining
- **मण्णे** (maṇṇe) = (the Tirthankara) considers / says — (used here to indicate the authoritative declaration)
- **अण्णयरामवि** (aṇṇayarāmavi) = (of) any one whatsoever (of the storables)
- **जे सिया** (je siyā) = whoever may be
- **सण्णिहीकामे** (saṇṇihīkāme) = desirous of storage (sannidhi-kāma)
- **गिही** (gihī) = a householder
- **पव्वइए ण से** (pavvaie ṇa se) = he is not ordained / not a monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha pushes the vow inward. It is not enough to say the monk does not actually store; the gatha says even the *wish to store* — the inner inclination — is already the touch of greed. The word *anusparśa* is exquisite: it means a "light touching," a contact, a brush. The Tirthankaras say: any storing at all, of anything whatsoever, leaves on the soul this *brush* of greed. The vow is therefore not about the external act alone but about the inner movement that precedes it.

Then the verse delivers the verdict: *whoever wishes to store is a householder, not an ordained one*. This is one of the sharpest sentences in the chapter. It cuts through the ambiguity that ordination might be confirmed by robes and rituals. Mahavira says: ordination is confirmed by the inner state. A robed person who in his heart desires to hoard *is* a householder, no matter what title he carries; a person without robes whose heart is free of the wish to store is closer to the monastic ideal than the storer in robes.

The implication for the practitioner is profound. The vow of non-possession is to be inspected not at the level of the cupboard but at the level of the desire. *Do I want to keep something for tomorrow?* — the question to be asked daily, hourly. The monk's task is to cultivate the inner state in which the wish itself does not arise — and the householder's task is to recognise that, even within householder life, the slow reduction of the wish-to-hoard is the slow ascent of the spiritual path.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Mahavira teaches: even just *wanting* to store something is already a touch of greed. Anyone who wants to keep things for later is really a householder at heart — not a true monk, no matter what they wear.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you check the size of your savings, your supplies, your collections — what is the inner feeling? Security, or the quiet tightness of greed?

## SUTRA 6.20 — But Robes, Bowls, and Tools Are Kept for Restraint, Not Possession

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जंपि वत्थं व पायं वा, कंबलं पायपुंछणं ।
तंपि संजमलज्जट्ठा, धारंति परिहरंति य ॥६.२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.20 ||
jaṃpi vatthaṃ va pāyaṃ vā, kaṃbalaṃ pāyapuṃchaṇaṃ |
taṃpi saṃjamalajjaṭṭhā, dhāraṃti pariharaṃti ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whatever cloth, bowl, blanket, or foot-wiper (the broom — rajoharaṇa) the monks do keep, they keep and use it solely for the sake of restraint and modesty.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जंपि** (jaṃpi) = whatever (the monk does keep)
- **वत्थं व** (vatthaṃ va) = clothing
- **पायं वा** (pāyaṃ vā) = bowl / vessel
- **कंबलं** (kaṃbalaṃ) = blanket
- **पायपुंछणं** (pāyapuṃchaṇaṃ) = the foot-wiper / broom (rajoharaṇa — a soft brush of woollen tufts used for sweeping the path before sitting, etc.)
- **तंपि** (taṃpi) = even that
- **संजमलज्जट्ठा** (saṃjamalajjaṭṭhā) = for the sake of restraint (saṃyama) and modesty (lajjā)
- **धारंति** (dhāraṃti) = (they) keep / hold
- **परिहरंति य** (pariharaṃti ya) = and (they) use / wear

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha addresses an obvious question. If the monk renounces possession, why do the Sthavirakalpi monks (those of the older, traditional order) carry clothes, bowls, blankets, and the rajoharaṇa? The answer: these articles are kept *not for possession but for two specific purposes* — *saṃyama* (restraint, the maintenance of the monastic discipline itself) and *lajjā* (modesty, the avoidance of being naked in public).

The first purpose, *restraint*, requires explanation. The bowl is needed because the monk must accept food from the householder; without a bowl, food cannot be received. The blanket is needed for protection from cold (so that the monk does not become physically incapacitated and unable to perform the practices). The rajoharaṇa is needed for sweeping insects and dust from the place of sitting — a tool of non-violence itself. These articles serve the vows; they do not serve the self.

The second purpose, *modesty*, addresses why clothes are worn at all. The Sthavirakalpi monk wears garments not for fashion or comfort but to avoid shocking ordinary people who would be disturbed by nakedness. (The Jinakalpi monk, of the more ancient and rigorous order, has transcended even this and wanders without robes.) The point: even the things the monk carries are not "possessions" but *instruments of practice*. They belong to the vow, not to the monk; they serve restraint, not self.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The cloth, bowl, blanket, and broom that the monks do carry are not possessions — they are kept and used only to keep the discipline going and to maintain basic modesty in society.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the things you "need," how many serve your higher purpose, and how many quietly serve your sense of self?

## SUTRA 6.21 — Possession Is Not the Object; Possession Is Attachment to It

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण सो परिग्गहो वुत्तो, णायपुत्तेण ताइणा ।
मुच्छा परिग्गहो वुत्तो, इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा ॥६.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.21 ||
ṇa so pariggaho vutto, ṇāyaputteṇa tāiṇā |
mucchā pariggaho vutto, ii vuttaṃ mahesiṇā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It (the cloth, bowl, etc.) is not called "possession" by the Son of the Jnata clan, the Protector of beings. *Attachment* is what is called possession — so the great seer has declared.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण** (ṇa) = not
- **सो** (so) = that (object — cloth, bowl, etc.)
- **परिग्गहो** (pariggaho) = possession (parigraha)
- **वुत्तो** (vutto) = is called / is said to be
- **णायपुत्तेण** (ṇāyaputteṇa) = by the Son of the Jnata clan (Mahavira)
- **ताइणा** (tāiṇā) = the Protector (trāyin — protector of all souls)
- **मुच्छा** (mucchā) = attachment / infatuation (mūrcchā — literally, fainting / clinging)
- **परिग्गहो वुत्तो** (pariggaho vutto) = is called possession
- **इइ** (ii) = thus
- **वुत्तं** (vuttaṃ) = has been said
- **महेसिणा** (mahesiṇā) = by the great seer (maharṣi — the Tirthankara, the Ganadharas)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most important psychological-philosophical statements in all of Jain ethics. Mahavira — *the Protector of beings* — declares that *the object itself is not the possession*. What is the possession? *Mūrcchā* — attachment, infatuation, the inner clinging of the mind to the object.

This single insight reframes the entire discipline of non-possession. A monk holding a bowl with no attachment is not possessing it; a householder gazing at his bank statement with longing is possessing it utterly. The locus of possession is not the warehouse but the heart. This is why the gatha can declare that the monk's robes, bowl, and broom — though physically held — are not *parigraha*. They are held but not *clung to*. The vow is kept at the level where it actually matters: in the inner relation, not the outer arrangement.

The implication for householders is liberating and demanding at once. Liberating: one does not have to give up everything to begin the path of non-possession; one has to begin by loosening the inner clinging. Demanding: even the householder who claims to "own little" but is consumed by attachment to that little has not begun. The work is interior. The practitioner who can hold a fortune with detachment is closer to the vow than the one who guards a single rupee with longing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Mahavira said: the *thing* you hold is not what makes it a possession. *Attachment* is what makes it a possession. A monk holding a bowl with no clinging is not a "possessor" — but a person clinging to a single coin is.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of everything you own, what would actually hurt to lose? *That* is what you possess.

## SUTRA 6.22 — The Awakened Hold Even the Body Without Possessiveness

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वत्थुवहिणा बुद्धा, संरक्खणपरिग्गहे ।
अवि अप्पणो वि देहम्मि, णायरंति ममाइयं ॥६.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.22 ||
savvatthuvahiṇā buddhā, saṃrakkhaṇapariggahe |
avi appaṇo vi dehammi, ṇāyaraṃti mamāiyaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The awakened ones, even when they keep articles for the protection (of life and the discipline), do not practise the sense of "mine-ness" — not even toward their own body.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सव्वत्थु** (savvatthu) = (with) all (necessary) things
- **उवहिणा** (uvahiṇā) = with the equipment / requisites (upadhi)
- **बुद्धा** (buddhā) = the awakened ones (the wise, the realised — here, the monks)
- **संरक्खणपरिग्गहे** (saṃrakkhaṇapariggahe) = in the keeping (parigraha — here in its neutral sense of "having") for the sake of protection (of the bodies of six classes of beings, of the body itself, of the discipline)
- **अवि** (avi) = even
- **अप्पणो वि** (appaṇo vi) = even of one's own
- **देहम्मि** (dehammi) = with regard to the body
- **णायरंति** (ṇāyaraṃti) = (they) do not practise / do not entertain
- **ममाइयं** (mamāiyaṃ) = the sense of "this is mine" (mamatva — possessiveness)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha reaches the summit of the non-possession teaching. The awakened ones, while necessarily keeping certain articles for the protection of life and the maintenance of discipline (clothes, bowl, broom, blanket — *saṃrakṣaṇa-parigraha*), do not allow the sense of "mine" (*mamatva*) to attach even to those articles — and, the gatha goes further, *not even to their own body*.

The body is the most natural object of "mine." Yet the awakened ones do not say "my body" in the inner clinging sense. They use the body as an instrument for the practice of dharma; they protect it as one protects a borrowed vehicle for the duration of a journey; they do not identify with it. This is the highest reach of non-possession: even the most intimate "possession" — this physical form — is held in non-attachment. When the body is held in this way, the small worldly possessions become trivially easy to hold without grasping. The body is the master test; pass it, and the rest follows.

The teaching also resolves a paradox. How can the Nirgrantha keep anything at all and still be "without knots"? Because *parigraha* is defined by attachment, not by holding. The awakened one holds without clinging — like an open hand on which something rests but does not grip. The instruments of practice are kept, used, and one day set down, with no inner mark of possession at any point. This is the genuine *aparigraha* — the inner state in which even one's own body is held without "mine-ness," and the world becomes, for the first time, light.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The truly awakened, even when they keep necessary tools to protect life and discipline, never say "this is mine" — not even about their own body. They use the body, but they do not grasp it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you released the inner whisper of "this is mine" from your own body — for one minute — what would still feel like *you*?

## SUTRA 6.23 — Sixth Station: Renunciation of Night-Eating (A Daily Austerity)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहो णिच्चं तवोकम्मं, सव्वबुद्धेहिं वण्णियं ।
जा य लज्जासमा वित्ती, एगभत्तं च भोयणं ॥६.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.23 ||
aho ṇiccaṃ tavokammaṃ, savvabuddhehiṃ vaṇṇiyaṃ |
jā ya lajjāsamā vittī, egabhattaṃ ca bhoyaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

How wonderful — this perpetual austerity, praised by all the awakened ones (the Tirthankaras): a way of living harmonious with restraint, and eating only once (in the day, never at night).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अहो** (aho) = ah! / how wonderful! (an exclamation of admiration)
- **णिच्चं** (ṇiccaṃ) = perpetual, daily
- **तवोकम्मं** (tavokammaṃ) = the act of austerity (tapas-karman)
- **सव्वबुद्धेहिं** (savvabuddhehiṃ) = by all the awakened ones (the Tirthankaras, the omniscient ones)
- **वण्णियं** (vaṇṇiyaṃ) = praised, commended
- **जा य** (jā ya) = and which
- **लज्जासमा वित्ती** (lajjāsamā vittī) = a way of living (vṛtti) harmonious with restraint (lajjā here = saṃyama; "vṛtti suited to the modesty of restraint")
- **एगभत्तं च भोयणं** (egabhattaṃ ca bhoyaṇaṃ) = and eating only once (in the day) — eka-bhakta — i.e., not eating at night

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sixth sthana — the renunciation of night-eating — is here described as a *perpetual austerity*. The text uses the word *aho* — an exclamation of wonder — to express how remarkable it is that the Tirthankaras have praised this single discipline as a daily, lifelong tapas. Other austerities are practised on certain days; this one is practised every day. By eating only during daylight hours and never at night, the monk performs an ongoing minor fast that becomes part of the very rhythm of life.

The phrase *lajjāsamā vittī* — a way of living "harmonious with restraint" — is the deeper teaching. *Lajjā* here is being used as a synonym for *saṃyama* (restraint), as the text explained earlier (sutra 6.20). A *vṛtti* (way of life, mode of subsistence) that suits restraint is one in which food serves the discipline rather than the discipline serving food. *Eka-bhakta* — eating once in the day — is precisely such a vṛtti. The body is sustained without becoming the centre of attention; sense-restraint is exercised at every meal; the long break between sunset and sunrise becomes an extended practice.

The reasoning is twofold. First, ethical: night-eating greatly increases the chance of consuming small living beings (insects drawn to lamps, organisms invisible in the dark). Second, meditative: the discipline of fasting from sunset to sunrise quiets the body, sharpens awareness, and builds mastery over the tongue and stomach — the senses most often given to gluttony. Combined, the practice is non-violence and inner austerity at once.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

How wonderful! All the awakened ones praise this daily small austerity: a way of life that suits restraint, and eating only in the daytime — never at night.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What single small daily discipline could become the quiet backbone of your whole life — practised so steadily it stops feeling like a discipline at all?

## SUTRA 6.24–6.25 — Why Night-Eating Is Forbidden

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दिया ताइं विवज्जिज्जा, राओ तत्थ कहं चरे ॥६.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.24 ||
saṃtime suhumā pāṇā, tasā aduva thāvarā |
jāiṃ rāo apāsaṃto, kahamesaṇiyaṃ care ||

|| 6.25 ||
udaullaṃ bīyasaṃsattaṃ, pāṇā ṇivaḍiyā mahiṃ |
diyā tāiṃ vivajjijjā, rāo tattha kahaṃ care ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There exist these subtle living beings, both mobile and immobile, which one cannot see at night; how then would one (perform) the spotless investigation (of food)? Hands wet with water, food mixed with seeds, insects fallen on the earth — these one can avoid in daytime; how then would one walk among them at night?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संतिमे** (saṃtime) = there exist these
- **सुहुमा पाणा** (suhumā pāṇā) = subtle living beings
- **तसा अदुव थावरा** (tasā aduva thāvarā) = mobile or immobile
- **जाइं** (jāiṃ) = which
- **राओ अपासंतो** (rāo apāsaṃto) = at night, not seeing
- **कहं** (kahaṃ) = how
- **एसणियं चरे** (esaṇiyaṃ care) = (would one) carry out the (spotless) investigation (eṣaṇā) of food
- **उदउल्लं** (udaullaṃ) = wet with water (a hand or vessel still wet — would harm water-bodied beings)
- **बीयसंसत्तं** (bīyasaṃsattaṃ) = mixed with seeds (food still containing live seeds)
- **पाणा णिवडिया महिं** (pāṇā ṇivaḍiyā mahiṃ) = living beings (insects, ants) fallen on the earth
- **दिया** (diyā) = in daytime
- **ताइं** (tāiṃ) = those (harms)
- **विवज्जिज्जा** (vivajjijjā) = (one) can avoid
- **राओ तत्थ कहं चरे** (rāo tattha kahaṃ care) = at night, how would one walk (safely) among them?

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two gathas give the practical reasoning for the night-eating prohibition, which is grounded entirely in the first vow of non-violence. At night, in lamp-light or darkness, the practitioner cannot perform proper *eṣaṇā* — the careful inspection of food, hands, vessels, and the path. Subtle living beings of all kinds — insects, ants, eggs, microscopic organisms — cannot be seen. To eat under such conditions is to almost certainly consume living beings, to drop crumbs that attract insects to their death, and to walk a path where small lives are crushed.

The second gatha gives concrete examples. A wet hand or vessel will harm water-bodied beings if used carelessly. Food that contains live seeds (*bījaṃ saṃsattam*) will, if taken, be a violation of vegetable-life protection. Insects — drawn to lamps or simply fallen on the ground — will be inadvertently crushed or eaten. *In daytime these can be seen and avoided; at night they cannot.* The argument is empirical, not dogmatic. The vow against night-eating is not a ritual taboo but a direct extension of the non-violence vow into the time of day when sight fails.

This is also why even householders observing the basic six-fold vows of Jain practice are taught to avoid eating after sunset. The principle is universal: where vision cannot guarantee non-harm, the practice withdraws. The Jain dietary discipline is, at its core, the discipline of seeing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There are tiny living beings, moving and still, that you simply cannot see at night. How could you possibly check your food for them? Wet hands, food with seeds in it, insects fallen on the floor — in daylight you can avoid harming them; at night you cannot.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much harm in your life happens not because you chose it, but because you acted without enough light to see what you were about to do?

## SUTRA 6.26 — Therefore the Nirgranthas Do Not Eat at Night

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.26 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Seeing this fault and (it being thus) declared by the Son of the Jnata clan: the Nirgranthas eat no food of any kind at night.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एयं च दोसं** (eyaṃ ca dosaṃ) = and this fault
- **दट्ठूणं** (daṭṭhūṇaṃ) = having seen
- **णायपुत्तेण भासियं** (ṇāyaputteṇa bhāsiyaṃ) = (it has been) declared by the Son of the Jnata clan (Mahavira)
- **सव्वाहारं** (savvāhāraṃ) = food of every kind (the four kinds: aśana, pāna, khādya, svādya — staple food, drink, chewable, savoury)
- **ण भुंजंति** (ṇa bhuṃjaṃti) = (they) do not eat
- **णिग्गंथा** (ṇiggaṃthā) = the Nirgranthas
- **राइभोयणं** (rāibhoyaṇaṃ) = night-eating (rātri-bhojana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha closes the section on night-eating with the categorical statement: *the Nirgranthas eat no food of any kind at night*. The phrase *savvāhāraṃ* — "all food" — covers the four classical categories of consumables: staple food (aśana), drink (pāna), chewables (khādya), and savouries (svādya). Not even water is taken at night. The vow is total.

The cited authority is again Mahavira — the Son of the Jnata clan — who, having seen the harms enumerated in the previous gathas, declared the prohibition. The wording is significant: *seeing this fault, it has been declared*. The teaching is grounded in observation, not arbitrary command. Anyone who observes carefully sees the same harm; the Tirthankara's authority is the authority of one who has seen completely.

For monks, the rule is absolute and lifelong. For householders, it is one of the simpler practices that can be adopted as a foundational discipline — and is, in traditional Jain families, the most widely kept of all the lay vows. Eating only between sunrise and sunset (or, in stricter practice, completing the evening meal well before sunset) becomes the daily marker of a life that takes non-violence seriously even in its smallest acts.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Seeing all these problems clearly, Mahavira declared: the monks eat no food, of any kind, at night.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would change in your awareness if you marked the boundary of sunset as the daily boundary of your eating?

## SUTRA 6.27–6.29 — Seventh Station: Restraint Toward Earth-Bodied Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुढविकायं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा ।
तिविहेणं करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया ॥६.२७॥

पुढविकायं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए ।
तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे ॥६.२८॥

तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं ।
पुढविकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए ॥६.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.27 ||
puḍhavikāyaṃ ṇa hiṃsaṃti, maṇasā vayasā kāyasā |
tivihaeṇaṃ karaṇajoeṇa, saṃjayā susamāhiyā ||

|| 6.28 ||
puḍhavikāyaṃ vihiṃsaṃto, hiṃsai u tayassie |
tase ya vivihe pāṇe, cakkhuse ya acakkhuse ||

|| 6.29 ||
tamhā eyaṃ viyāṇittā, dosaṃ duggaivaḍḍhaṇaṃ |
puḍhavikāyasamāraṃbhaṃ, jāvajjīvāe vajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm earth-bodied beings — by mind, by speech, or by body — through any of the three modes (doing, causing, approving). One who harms earth-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures who dwell on or within the earth — both the visible and the invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming earth-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुढविकायं** (puḍhavikāyaṃ) = earth-bodied beings (pṛthvīkāya — clay, soil, stone, mineral)
- **ण हिंसंति** (ṇa hiṃsaṃti) = (they) do not harm
- **मणसा वयसा कायसा** (maṇasā vayasā kāyasā) = by mind, by speech, by body
- **तिविहेणं करणजोएण** (tivihaeṇaṃ karaṇajoeṇa) = by the three modes — karaṇa (doing, causing, approving) and yoga (the three instruments of mind, speech, body)
- **संजया सुसमाहिया** (saṃjayā susamāhiyā) = the well-composed (susamāhita) restrained ones (saṃyata)
- **विहिंसंतो** (vihiṃsaṃto) = (one who is) harming
- **तयस्सिए** (tayassie) = those who depend on it / dwell on it (tad-āśrita)
- **तसे ... पाणे** (tase ... pāṇe) = mobile beings
- **चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे** (cakkhuse ya acakkhuse) = visible (cākṣuṣa) and invisible (acākṣuṣa)
- **दुग्गइवड्ढणं** (duggaivaḍḍhaṇaṃ) = multiplying evil destinies (durgati-vardhana)
- **समारंभं** (samāraṃbhaṃ) = activity, the violent undertaking
- **जावज्जीवाए वज्जए** (jāvajjīvāe vajjae) = (one) should renounce for as long as one lives

### 5. COMMENTARY

The seventh sthana opens the second movement of the chapter — the *six classes of life-protection* (kāya-ṣaṭka). These six gathas, repeated in the same triplet form for each of the six classes (earth, water, fire, air, plants, mobile beings), unfold the full scope of the first vow of non-violence. The structure is identical for all six: (1) the well-composed monk does not harm them by any of the nine doors; (2) one who harms them also harms the dependent beings living among them; (3) therefore renounce that activity for life.

The first kāya is *pṛthvīkāya* — earth-bodied beings. According to the Jain analysis of life, even the mineral world has consciousness at its most rudimentary level; clay, soil, salt crystals, stones in their natural state are not inert matter but very small living beings with the single sense of touch. To dig, plough, build with raw clay, or mine is to inflict harm on these single-sense souls.

The second gatha (6.28) extends the harm: when one digs the earth, one not only harms the earth-bodied beings themselves but also countless other beings — the worms, insects, microbes, and small creatures (visible and invisible) that depend on the soil for their existence. One act of harm always cascades. This is the deeper logic of Jain non-violence: harm is never localised; every action ripples outward through interdependent webs of life. The monk who keeps this vow does not therefore engage in any of the agricultural, mining, construction, or excavation activities that constitute ordinary worldly life. He sits, walks, and lives lightly — taking water and food prepared by others (already rendered acit by the householder for their own purposes), and never himself initiating harm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The well-disciplined monks never harm earth-bodied beings — not by thinking it, saying it, or doing it; not personally, by another, or by approval. Whoever harms the earth also harms all the small creatures living on and in it, seen and unseen. So, knowing this leads to bad rebirths, the monks renounce digging and disturbing the earth for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What invisible communities of life are disturbed by the actions you take without thinking — and what would it mean to begin seeing them?

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm water-bodied beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms water-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures dwelling within the water — both the visible and the invisible. ### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आउकायं** (āukāyaṃ) = water-bodied beings (apkāya — every drop of water in its natural state is a living body)
- (Other terms identical to 6.27–6.29)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eighth sthana applies the same triplet to *apkāya* — water-bodied beings. According to Jain biology, every drop of natural water is itself a single-sense living body. To draw water from a well, river, or rainfall is to inflict harm on countless of these tiny souls. To bathe in a river, to splash, to step into a pond — each act crushes their lives. And, as the second gatha emphasises, water also hosts countless other creatures — the microbes, plants, fish, and insects that dwell within it.

The monk's response is total: never to draw water himself, never to wade in rivers, never to walk in the rain, never to use water as a means of cooling or cleaning that has not been rendered acit (lifeless) by the natural processes of household use (boiling, mixing with ash, etc.). When the monk needs water for drinking, he begs from a householder for water that has already been used for the householder's own purpose and is now no longer alive — the same principle by which the monk takes food. The monk does not initiate the killing; he accepts what has already been done by others for their own legitimate reasons.

This vow creates the famous Jain monastic posture toward water: the avoidance of bathing (which becomes its own sthana later in the chapter), the careful sieving of drinking water, the refusal to walk in rain, the reverence with which water is handled. For Jain practice, water is not a resource but a community of small lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciplined monks never harm water-bodied beings — not in thought, word, or deed; not by themselves or through others. Whoever harms water harms all the tiny creatures inside it too. So, knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they renounce all such harming for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you held a glass of water and felt that it was *alive*? What changes when you do?

## SUTRA 6.33–6.36 — Ninth Station: Restraint Toward Fire-Bodied Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जायतेयं ण इच्छंति, पावगं जलइत्तए ।
तिक्खमण्णयरं सत्थं, सव्वओ वि दुरासयं ॥६.३३॥

पाईणं पडिणं वावि, उड्ढं अणुदिसामवि ।
अहे दाहिणओ वावि, दहे उत्तरओ वि य ॥६.३४॥

भूयाणमेसमाघाओ, हव्ववाहो ण संसओ ।
तं पईवपयावट्ठा, संजया किंचि णारभे ॥६.३५॥

तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं ।
तेउकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए ॥६.३६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.33 ||
jāyateyaṃ ṇa icchaṃti, pāvagaṃ jalaittae |
tikkhamaṇṇayaraṃ satthaṃ, savvao vi durāsayaṃ ||

|| 6.34 ||
pāīṇaṃ paḍiṇaṃ vāvi, uḍḍhaṃ aṇudisāmavi |
ahe dāhiṇao vāvi, dahe uttarao vi ya ||

|| 6.35 ||
bhūyāṇamesamāghāo, havvavāho ṇa saṃsao |
taṃ paīvapayāvaṭṭhā, saṃjayā kiṃci ṇārabhe ||

|| 6.36 ||
tamhā eyaṃ viyāṇittā, dosaṃ duggaivaḍḍhaṇaṃ |
teukāyasamāraṃbhaṃ, jāvajjīvāe vajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monks) do not wish to kindle fire — that sin-causing thing, sharp like any other weapon, an unbearable refuge from every side. (For fire) burns whatever is in the east, west, above, in the cross-directions, below, in the south, and in the north. This very fire-carrier is, without doubt, a destroyer of living beings; therefore the restrained ones undertake nothing whatsoever for the sake of (kindling fire for) lamplight or warming. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming fire-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जायतेयं** (jāyateyaṃ) = (literally: "born of brilliance") fire (a poetic name for agni)
- **ण इच्छंति** (ṇa icchaṃti) = (they) do not desire
- **पावगं** (pāvagaṃ) = the sin-causer (pāvaka — ironic: the popular meaning is "purifier" but Jain usage marks fire as a producer of sin)
- **जलइत्तए** (jalaittae) = to kindle, to light
- **तिक्खं** (tikkhaṃ) = sharp
- **अण्णयरं सत्थं** (aṇṇayaraṃ satthaṃ) = (sharper than) any other weapon
- **सव्वओ** (savvao) = on all sides (a weapon edged on every side)
- **दुरासयं** (durāsayaṃ) = an unbearable / unsafe refuge (no one falling into it can survive)
- **पाईणं पडिणं ... उत्तरओ वि य** (pāīṇaṃ paḍiṇaṃ ... uttarao vi ya) = east, west, above, cross-directions, below, south, north — the seven directions
- **दहे** (dahe) = burns
- **भूयाणमेस आघाओ** (bhūyāṇam esa āghāo) = this is a destroyer / striker of beings
- **हव्ववाहो** (havvavāho) = the carrier of the oblation — fire (havya-vāha)
- **ण संसओ** (ṇa saṃsao) = there is no doubt
- **पईव** (paīva) = lamp (pradīpa)
- **पयाव** (payāva) = warming oneself (pratāpa — sitting near a fire for warmth)
- **अट्ठा** (aṭṭhā) = for the sake of
- **किंचि णारभे** (kiṃci ṇārabhe) = (the monks) do not undertake anything

### 5. COMMENTARY

The ninth sthana introduces fire — *teukāya / agnikāya* — and the commentary's framing is striking. This four-gatha section (the only six-class entry that is more than three gathas) gives unusually full reasoning, because of all the six kāyas, fire is the most uniquely destructive. The text uses the word *pāvaka* — "the sin-causer" — for fire, deliberately inverting the popular religious term, which traditionally means "the purifier." For Jain analysis, fire is a uniquely powerful weapon: sharper than any blade, edged on every side, and a refuge from which no being trapped within can escape. Other weapons cut from one direction; fire burns from all directions at once.

The second gatha (6.34) drives the point home with the listing of the seven directions: east, west, above, cross-directions, below, south, north — fire spreads to all of them. This is what makes it different from a weapon. A blade affects only what it touches; a fire affects everything in its zone, in every direction, and continues to spread until it consumes all available fuel. To light even a small flame is to release a destroyer that the monk cannot fully control. The third gatha then forbids even the practical uses of fire that ordinary people consider innocent: kindling it for lamplight (paīva), kindling it for warming oneself (payāva). Even these — the monks "undertake nothing whatsoever."

The implication is severe but consistent. The monk does not light a lamp to read his scriptures at night (and so the night-eating prohibition becomes self-enforcing), does not light a fire to warm himself in winter (and so endures cold as an austerity), does not cook (and so depends entirely on the householder for food). The fire-vow shapes the entire texture of monastic life. Fire is the one element that the monk most resolutely refuses to touch — because once touched, it cannot be controlled, and the lives within and around it cannot be protected.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monks do not even want to light a fire — because fire is sharper than any weapon, has no safe side, and burns living things in every direction. Even for lighting a lamp or warming themselves, they refuse to start a fire. So, knowing fire harms beings and leads to bad rebirths, they give up all such activity for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "small useful fires" do you light in your own life — that, once lit, burn far more than you intended?

- **अणिलस्स** (aṇilassa) = of air (anila)
- **समारंभं** (samāraṃbhaṃ) = the violent undertaking, disturbance
- **बुद्धा** (buddhā) = the awakened ones (the Tirthankaras)
- **तारिसं** (tārisaṃ) = like that (i.e., comparable to fire-disturbance)
- **सावज्जबहुलं** (sāvajjabahulaṃ) = full of sin (sāvadya)
- **तायीहिं** (tāīhiṃ) = by the protectors (tāyī — those who protect the six classes of life)
- **सेवियं** (seviyaṃ) = practised
- **तालियंटेण** (tāliyaṃṭeṇa) = with a palm-leaf fan
- **पत्तेण** (patteṇa) = with a leaf
- **साहा विहुयणेण वा** (sāhā vihuyaṇeṇa vā) = or with the shaking of a branch (i.e., a hand-fan)
- **वीइउमिच्छंति** (vīiumicchaṃti) = wish to fan
- **वीयावेऊण वा परं** (vīyāveūṇa vā paraṃ) = or cause another to fan
- **वायमुईरंति** (vāyamuīraṃti) = stir up wind (vāyu-udīraṇa)
- **जयं परिहरंति** (jayaṃ pariharaṃti) = use with care / yatanā

### 5. COMMENTARY

The tenth sthana addresses *vāyukāya / anilakāya* — air-bodied beings. The Jain teaching that even air is a community of single-sense beings is one of the most far-reaching extensions of non-violence in human ethics. Every breeze, every gust, every disturbance of the still air involves the harm of these very subtle living bodies. The Tirthankaras consider the disturbance of air to be as serious as the disturbance of fire — not because the harm is as visibly destructive, but because both are equally violations of the protection-of-life principle.

The practical implications, set out in 6.38–6.39, are detailed. The monk does not use any fan: no palm-leaf, no leaf plucked from a tree, no branch waved as a fan, no hand-fan. He does not have another fan him. And even when handling his own permitted equipment — robe, bowl, blanket, broom — he handles them with such care (*yatana*) that he does not stir up wind in the air around him. The garments are worn slowly, the broom moved gently, the bowl carried evenly. The whole bodily life of the monk is one of slow, deliberate, wind-minimising motion.

This vow shapes the very *aesthetic* of Jain monasticism. The famous slow, smooth, almost ceremonial movement of Jain monks and nuns is not affectation; it is the air-vow lived. To walk quickly is to disturb air. To speak loudly is to disturb air. To gesture sharply is to disturb air. The vow thereby produces a body that has become quiet at the most subtle level — an outer expression of the inner stillness the practice cultivates.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The Tirthankaras say disturbing the air is just as bad as disturbing fire. So the monks never fan themselves — not with a leaf, not with a branch, not with anyone's help. Even when handling their own clothes and tools, they move slowly so as not to stir up wind. They renounce all such air-disturbing activity for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How fast and forcefully do you move through the air around you — and what would change if every gesture became deliberate?

## SUTRA 6.41–6.43 — Eleventh Station: Restraint Toward Plant-Bodied Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वणस्सइं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा ।
तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया ॥६.४१॥

वणस्सइं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए ।
तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे ॥६.४२॥

तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं ।
वणस्सइसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए ॥६.४३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.41 ||
vaṇassaiṃ ṇa hiṃsaṃti, maṇasā vayasā kāyasā |
tivihaeṇa karaṇajoeṇa, saṃjayā susamāhiyā ||

|| 6.42 ||
vaṇassaiṃ vihiṃsaṃto, hiṃsai u tayassie |
tase ya vivihe pāṇe, cakkhuse ya acakkhuse ||

|| 6.43 ||
tamhā eyaṃ viyāṇittā, dosaṃ duggaivaḍḍhaṇaṃ |
vaṇassaisamāraṃbhaṃ, jāvajjīvāe vajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm plant-bodied beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms plant-bodied beings also harms the various mobile and immobile creatures who depend on them — both visible and invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming plant-bodied beings for as long as one lives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वणस्सइं** (vaṇassaiṃ) = plant-bodied beings (vanaspatikāya — roots, trees, leaves, flowers, fruits, vegetables in their living state)
- (Other terms identical to 6.27–6.29)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eleventh sthana applies the standard triplet to *vanaspatikāya* — plant-bodied beings. Plants too are single-sense beings (with the sense of touch), and to pluck, cut, uproot, or break them is to inflict harm on their living bodies. The second gatha (6.42) again emphasises the dependent ecology: plants harbour countless other lives — insects, microbes, small creatures — and the destruction of a plant always means the destruction of these dependents as well.

The monk does not pluck flowers, does not pick fruits, does not gather vegetables, does not even touch growing plants if it can be avoided. The plant matter that supports his body comes entirely through the householder's vessel — vegetables that have been cooked, fruits that have been cut, all rendered *acit* (no longer living) by the householder's own kitchen process before reaching the monk. The monk's principle: do not initiate the harming, accept only what has already been transformed for the householder's own legitimate purpose.

This is, again, the strict application of the non-violence vow. The plant-protection vow underlies the entire Jain dietary culture — including the avoidance of root vegetables (which contain whole live plants in dormant form, capable of regenerating), of fresh-picked produce by monks, and the careful timing of food preparation. The principle reaches into ordinary practice as the daily reverence for plant life that marks Jain households.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The well-disciplined monks never harm plants — in thought, word, or deed; alone or through others. Whoever harms plants harms all the small creatures living in them too. Knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they renounce harming plants for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What grows around you — and how aware are you that it lives, breathes, and shelters lives smaller than itself?

## SUTRA 6.44–6.46 — Twelfth Station: Restraint Toward Mobile Beings

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तसकायं ण हिंसंति, मणसा वयसा कायसा ।
तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजया सुसमाहिया ॥६.४४॥

तसकायं विहिंसंतो, हिंसइ उ तयस्सिए ।
तसे य विविहे पाणे, चक्खुसे य अचक्खुसे ॥६.४५॥

तम्हा एयं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं ।
तसकायसमारंभं, जावज्जीवाए वज्जए ॥६.४६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.44 ||
tasakāyaṃ ṇa hiṃsaṃti, maṇasā vayasā kāyasā |
tivihaeṇa karaṇajoeṇa, saṃjayā susamāhiyā ||

|| 6.45 ||
tasakāyaṃ vihiṃsaṃto, hiṃsai u tayassie |
tase ya vivihe pāṇe, cakkhuse ya acakkhuse ||

|| 6.46 ||
tamhā eyaṃ viyāṇittā, dosaṃ duggaivaḍḍhaṇaṃ |
tasakāyasamāraṃbhaṃ, jāvajjīvāe vajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The well-composed restrained ones do not harm mobile beings — by mind, speech, or body — through any of the three modes. One who harms mobile beings also harms the various other mobile and immobile beings who dwell in association with them — both visible and invisible. Therefore, having known this fault as the multiplier of evil destinies, one should renounce the activity of harming mobile beings for as long as one lives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तसकायं** (tasakāyaṃ) = mobile-bodied beings (trasakāya — beings with two or more senses: worms, insects, animals, birds, humans)
- (Other terms identical to 6.27–6.29)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The twelfth sthana — the last of the six kāya-protections — covers *trasakāya*, mobile beings. These are the beings with two or more senses: from the simple worm (two senses — touch and taste), through ants and insects (three senses), bees and butterflies (four senses), to fish, birds, mammals, and humans (five senses). All of them — visible and invisible, small and large — are protected by the monk's vow.

The monk's task with respect to mobile beings is one of constant attentiveness. Insects appear suddenly — in food, in water, in the path. The monk inspects everything: the place where he sits is swept gently with the broom; food is examined before eating; water is filtered before drinking; the path is watched at every step. *Yatana* — careful attentiveness — is the inner discipline that operationalises this vow.

The second gatha makes the now-familiar point: harming a mobile being also harms the dependents — the other small creatures in its vicinity, the eggs in its body, the microbes on its skin. Every act of harm is in fact many acts of harm. The monk who has internalised this teaching does not need to be told to be careful; the carefulness arises naturally from the seeing.

With this twelfth sthana, the great six-fold vow of life-protection is complete. From the imperceptible single-sense earth-body to the most developed five-sense human, every form of life is brought under the canopy of non-violence. The vow has covered the entire spectrum of consciousness in the universe — and shown that the monk's life is the one in which no part of that spectrum is allowed to suffer at his hands.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciplined monks never harm any moving creature — from the tiniest worm to the largest animal — in thought, word, or deed; alone or through others. Whoever harms a moving creature harms others connected to it as well. Knowing this leads to bad rebirths, they give up such harming for life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you stopped your foot in mid-step because you saw a small life on the ground? What kind of person becomes someone who does that naturally?

## SUTRA 6.47 — Thirteenth Station: Renunciation of Forbidden Articles (Akalpya)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जाइं चत्तारि अभोज्जाइं, इसिणाहारमाइणि ।
ताइं तु विवज्जंतो, संजमं अणुपालए ॥६.४७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 6.47 ||
jāiṃ cattāri abhojjāiṃ, isiṇāhāramāiṇi |
tāiṃ tu vivajjaṃto, saṃjamaṃ aṇupālae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The four (categories) which are inedible for sages — beginning with the food (and including drink, chewables, savouries) — those, indeed, completely renouncing, one should preserve restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जाइं** (jāiṃ) = which
- **चत्तारि** (cattāri) = four
- **अभोज्जाइं** (abhojjāiṃ) = inedible / forbidden (abhojya)
- **इसिणाहारमाइणि** (isiṇāhāramāiṇi) = (the four categories) of which the food (āhāra) of sages (ṛṣi) is the first — i.e., the four categories: aśana (staple food), pāna (drink), khādya (chewable), svādya (savoury)
- **ताइं तु विवज्जंतो** (tāiṃ tu vivajjaṃto) = renouncing those (in their forbidden forms — i.e., food obtained by methods forbidden for monks)
- **संजमं अणुपालए** (saṃjamaṃ aṇupālae) = (one) should preserve / sustain restraint

### 5. COMMENTARY

The thirteenth sthana opens the third movement of the chapter — the six rules of practical monastic discipline — beginning with the renunciation of *akalpya* (the forbidden, the unfit-for-monks). The four categories of consumables — *aśana* (staple food), *pāna* (drink), *khādya* (chewables), and *svādya* (savouries) — are not inherently forbidden in themselves; what is forbidden is their *acquisition by methods unfit for a sage*. Food obtained through any of the dosha (faults) of pinda-eshana — food cooked specifically for the monk, food obtained from forbidden households, food bought, food forced upon a host, food still containing live beings, food past its proper time — all of this becomes *akalpya* and must be refused.

This sthana refers back to the entire fifth adhyayan (Pinda-eshana), which lists in great detail the forty-six doshas of food acquisition that a monk must avoid. The thirteenth sthana of conduct is, in a sense, the application of all that previous teaching to the daily practice. The monk preserves *saṃyama* — restraint — precisely by refusing the forbidden food, even when hunger urges, even when the food appears appetising, even when refusing causes embarrassment. The discipline of refusal is itself the discipline of monkhood.

The verse uses the verb *anupālae* — "should preserve / should continue protecting." Restraint is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing protection. Every meal-time is a fresh exercise of the vow. The monk who keeps his discipline at the moment of eating is the monk who keeps his monkhood at all.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The four categories of food — staple food, drink, chewables, and savouries — should be refused whenever they come in forms unfit for monks (such as being cooked specifically for them). By refusing such food, the monk keeps his vow of restraint alive.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "small allowance" do you make at the moment of choosing — and what discipline collapses the moment you make it?
