# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 1
# Pindeshana (પિંડૈષણા) — The Search for Faultless Alms-Food

**Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 1 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 129–165 (PDF pages 189–225)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fifth Adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is called **Pindeshana** — literally "the search for the food-lump." Of all chapters in the Jain canon, none treats the rules of monastic alms-conduct with greater precision or at greater length. The chapter is divided into two Uddeshas (sub-chapters); the present Uddesha 1 lays out, in over a hundred verses, the entire arc of going for alms — from the moment the monk steps out of the upashraya (lodging) until the moment the food touches the lips.

The word **Pindeshana** is formed from two Prakrit terms: **pinda** means a "lump" or "body" of any object, and is here used for the four kinds of food a monk may accept (**ashana** — solid food; **pana** — drink; **khadima** — chewables such as dried fruit; **svadima** — flavour-fresheners such as cardamom). **Eshana** means "search," "examination," or "investigation." The combined word therefore means: the careful examination of food before, during, and after acceptance, so that the monk's vow of total non-violence remains unbroken.

The tradition speaks of three kinds of eshana: (1) **Gaveshana** — the search for faultless food, which means avoiding the 16 *udgama* faults (donor-side faults arising from how the food was prepared) and the 16 *utpadana* faults (faults arising from the monk's own conduct in obtaining it); (2) **Grahaneshana** — the careful acceptance, avoiding the 10 *eshana* faults that occur at the moment of receiving; (3) **Paribhogaisheshana** — the dispassionate consumption, avoiding the 5 *manda* faults of indulgent eating. In total, these famous **42 doshas (faults)** form the framework of the entire chapter. Uddesha 1 covers all three layers — gaveshana, grahaneshana, and paribhogaisheshana — while Uddesha 2 returns to amplify the remaining faults of conduct.

The deeper teaching is that for a Jain monk or nun, *to obtain food at all is itself a perilous spiritual endeavour*. The body must be nourished if the practice is to continue, but the food must be obtained without injury to a single living being, without burdening the householder, and without arousing greed in the seeker. The Uddesha lays down rule after rule — where to walk, where to look, where not to enter, what not to accept, what not to think — so that this daily walk may itself become an act of meditation. As the Jinas teach, the monk who has mastered Pindeshana has mastered the most ordinary and the most difficult of all spiritual disciplines: turning hunger itself into a form of liberation.

Acharya Haribhadrasuri, in his **Ashtaka Prakarana**, names three kinds of begging: **dinavritti bhiksha** (the begging of the helpless and disabled), **paurushaghni bhiksha** (begging by the able-bodied who could otherwise work — and which destroys their inner manhood), and **sarvasampatkari bhiksha** (the all-prosperity-bringing begging of the true monk who, through his restrained reception of food, benefits himself, the giver, and the world). Only this third is the begging of the Jain shramana.

Uddesha 1 begins with the qualities of the monk who is fit to go for alms, then describes how to walk, where to look, which houses to enter and which to avoid, how to behave at the door, how to receive the food, how to bring it back, how to confess to the Guru, how to share it with fellow monks, and finally how to consume it without attachment. Every verse rests on a single principle: that even the meal is a spiritual act, and that even hunger must bow to non-violence.

## SUTRA 5.1.1 — When and How to Set Out for Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संपत्ते भिक्खकालंमि, असंभंतो अमुच्छिओ ।
इमेण कमजोगेण, भत्तपाणं गवेसए ॥५.१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.1 ||
saṃpatte bhikkhakālaṃmi, asaṃbhaṃto amucchio |
imeṇa kamajogeṇa, bhattapāṇaṃ gavesae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When the time for alms-seeking has arrived, free from agitation and free from grasping attachment, the monk should search for food and drink by the following step-by-step method.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खकालंमि** (bhikkhakālaṃmi) = at the time of alms-seeking
- **संपत्ते** (saṃpatte) = when it has been reached / arrived
- **असंभंतो** (asaṃbhaṃto) = free from mental agitation, free from haste, calm-minded
- **अमुच्छिओ** (amucchio) = free from grasping attachment to the senses; free from craving for tastes
- **इमेण** (imeṇa) = by this (about to be described)
- **कमजोगेण** (kamajogeṇa) = step-by-step method, by the systematic procedure of bhikshacharya
- **भत्तपाणं** (bhattapāṇaṃ) = food and drink
- **गवेसए** (gavesae) = should search, should investigate

### 5. COMMENTARY

The opening verse fixes the moment from which everything else flows: *when the right time has come*. Jain monastic tradition teaches that the day of a monk is divided into four parts; the first is for self-study, the second for meditation, the third for going out to seek alms, and the fourth for further study. This third quarter — when householders' meals are naturally finished and food is no longer being cooked — is the proper time. To go before this is to disturb the household; to go after it is to find no food, or to imply impatience. The text quietly names this time without specifying a clock, because in different regions the householders' meal-time will fall at different hours; the monk learns to read the rhythm of the place he is in.

But arrival of the time is not enough. The monk must arrive at the time *inwardly* as well: free from agitation (asambhanto) and free from grasping (amucchio). Agitation here means anxious haste — the nervous monk who fears that other monks will have collected the best food first, or who rushes from house to house without inner composure. Grasping here is more subtle: it is the inward leaning of the senses toward tasty food. The monk who walks with a mental note of which house gives sweets has already broken the vow of non-attachment, even before any food is touched. Both states must fall away. Only the unhurried, unattached walker is fit to investigate the purity of food.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the proper time for collecting food has come, the monk should set out calmly, without any rush in his mind or any greed for tasty food, and follow the step-by-step rules that are about to be explained.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Before you reach for what you need today, can you first set down the hurry and the wanting?

## SUTRA 5.1.2 — Walking Calmly Toward the Village

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से गामे वा णगरे वा, गोयरग्ग गओ मुणी ।
चरे मंदमणुव्विग्गो, अव्वक्खित्तेण चेयसा ॥५.१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.2 ||
se gāme vā ṇagare vā, goyaragga gao muṇī |
care maṃdamaṇuvviggo, avvakkhitteṇa ceyasā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That monk, having gone out for alms in a village or city, should walk slowly, free from inner disturbance, with an undistracted, single-pointed mind.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **से** (se) = he, that
- **गामे वा णगरे वा** (gāme vā ṇagare vā) = in a village or in a city
- **गोयरग्ग गओ** (goyaragga gao) = gone out for alms-seeking (literally: gone forth as a "cow-tip" wanderer — see commentary)
- **मुणी** (muṇī) = the monk
- **मंदम्** (maṃdam) = slowly, with measured pace
- **अणुव्विग्गो** (aṇuvviggo) = free from inner disturbance, untroubled, calm
- **अव्वक्खित्तेण चेयसा** (avvakkhitteṇa ceyasā) = with an undistracted, one-pointed mind
- **चरे** (care) = should walk

### 5. COMMENTARY

The very word for the monk's alms-walk — **gocharya** (Prakrit *goyara*, "cow-tip") — is itself a teaching. The cow grazing in a meadow does not stand in one place stripping a single bush bare; she takes a tuft here, a tuft there, never enough from any one place to deplete it, never showing preference for one patch of grass over another, moving steadily without favour. Just so, the monk passes from house to house, taking only a small amount from each, never enough to inconvenience any one family, treating rich and poor houses alike, harbouring no preference for tasty food over plain. The image was chosen, the commentators note, with great care: it dignifies the act, removes any taint of beggary, and makes the alms-walk into an act of spiritual symmetry.

Two qualities are demanded of the walking monk. First, **mandam** — slowness. The hurried walker cannot watch his step, and the careful watching of every footfall (irya-samiti, the discipline of walking) is itself one of the five cardinal disciplines of Jain monastic life. To walk slowly is not laziness; it is the visible form of inner mindfulness. Second, **avvakkhitteṇa ceyasā** — with an undistracted mind. The mind, like a calf, easily runs after sights and sounds. The text gives the lovely example of the merchant's calf: the merchant's daughter-in-law was so absorbed in feeding the calf one day that, even though she was dressed in her finest clothes and jewels, the calf paid no attention to her ornaments — its mind was wholly on the food. The monk's mind too should be wholly on the search for faultless food, with no glance to the side at the world's distractions.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk goes out to collect food in a town or city, he should walk slowly, with a peaceful mind, and pay attention only to the food-search — not to anything else around him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you do the most ordinary thing today — walking, eating, working — with a mind that is not also chasing five other things?

## SUTRA 5.1.3 — Watching the Path

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुरओ जुगमायाए, पेहमाणो महिं चरे ।
वज्जंतो बीयहरियाइं, पाणे य दगमट्टियं ॥५.१.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.3 ||
purao jugamāyāe, pehamāṇo mahiṃ care |
vajjaṃto bīyahariyāiṃ, pāṇe ya dagamaṭṭiyaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He should walk while looking ahead at the ground a yoke's-length in front, avoiding seeds and green plants, living creatures, and water and damp earth.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुरओ** (purao) = ahead, in front
- **जुगमायाए** (jugamāyāe) = a yoke's-length (yuga-matra) — about four hand-spans, the length of an ox-cart's yoke; roughly the height of a man, or four hastas (~6 feet)
- **पेहमाणो** (pehamāṇo) = looking, watching carefully
- **महिं** (mahiṃ) = the earth, the ground
- **चरे** (care) = should walk
- **वज्जंतो** (vajjaṃto) = avoiding
- **बीयहरियाइं** (bīyahariyāiṃ) = seeds and green vegetation (sprouting plants)
- **पाणे** (pāṇe) = living creatures (small insects and beings)
- **दगमट्टियं** (dagamaṭṭiyaṃ) = water and (damp) earth (which contain water-bodied beings)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the practical application of *irya-samiti* — the discipline of watchful walking. The text fixes the visual range with precision: a *yuga* — the length of an ox-cart's yoke, roughly four hastas (the distance from elbow-tip to fingertip times four), or about the height of a man. To look much further ahead is to lose sight of the ground at one's feet; to look only at one's feet is to be unable to anticipate. The yoke-length compromise — looking neither too near nor too far — allows the monk to see two-sense and three-sense small beings, to notice green sprouts, damp patches and insects in time to step around them.

The four categories named here are no random list. *Beeja* (seeds) and *hariya* (sprouting plants) are one-sense plant-bodied beings. *Pana* (creatures) refers to two-sense beings and above — worms, ants, insects. *Daga* (water) is water-bodied beings; *mattiya* (wet earth) contains earth-bodied beings. In a single sentence the verse covers four of the six classes of living beings (the chhajjivanikaya of the previous chapter). The monk who walks watching the ground is therefore actively practising non-violence toward four whole categories of life with every step.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should walk looking carefully at the ground about six feet in front, so he can step around seeds, sprouting plants, small creatures, water, and damp soil — because every one of these contains tiny living beings.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What small lives might be saved today simply because you slowed down and looked where you placed your feet?

## SUTRA 5.1.4 — Avoiding Treacherous Paths

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ओवायं विसमं खाणुं, विज्जलं परिवज्जए ।
संकमेण ण गच्छेज्जा, विज्जमाणे परक्कमे ॥५.१.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.4 ||
ovāyaṃ visamaṃ khāṇuṃ, vijjalaṃ parivajjae |
saṃkameṇa ṇa gacchejjā, vijjamāṇe parakkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He should avoid pits, uneven ground, tree-stumps and muddy paths. He should not walk over makeshift bridges (over water or pits) when an alternative way exists.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ओवायं** (ovāyaṃ) = pits, hollows, depressions in the ground
- **विसमं** (visamaṃ) = uneven, rough ground
- **खाणुं** (khāṇuṃ) = tree-stumps, sharp stubble
- **विज्जलं** (vijjalaṃ) = mud, marshy or slippery places
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should leave aside, should avoid
- **संकमेण** (saṃkameṇa) = by makeshift bridges — planks, stones, or wooden boards laid across water or pits
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not walk
- **विज्जमाणे** (vijjamāṇe) = when present (when an alternative exists)
- **परक्कमे** (parakkame) = should walk by (the better path)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is principally about the monk's own bodily safety — but its deeper purpose is to protect the discipline of non-violence. If the monk falls or slips on a pit, slippery mud or tree-stump, several harms follow at once: the body may be injured (self-violence), and falling crushes the small creatures and plants beneath (other-violence). A makeshift bridge laid by villagers across a stream or ditch is also unsafe — it may collapse, and crossing it may disturb water-bodied beings. So the rule is conditional: only when no proper path exists may such treacherous routes be used, and then only with extra care. The principle is wider than walking: when two paths exist, the monk always chooses the one that protects life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should avoid pits, uneven ground, tree-stumps, and muddy spots; and he should not cross temporary planks over water or holes if a normal path is available.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When two ways are open before you, do you choose the one that is easier or the one that protects more life?

## SUTRA 5.1.5 — The Cost of a Slip

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पवडंते व से तत्थ, पक्खलंते व संजए ।
हिंसेज्ज पाणभूयाइं, तसे अदुव थावरे ॥५.१.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.5 ||
pavaḍaṃte va se tattha, pakkhalaṃte va saṃjae |
hiṃsejja pāṇabhūyāiṃ, tase aduva thāvare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If that restrained monk falls or slips on such (uneven ground), he will harm living beings — whether mobile or immobile.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **से संजए** (se saṃjae) = that restrained one, the monk
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there, on such ground
- **पवडंते** (pavaḍaṃte) = falling
- **पक्खलंते** (pakkhalaṃte) = slipping, stumbling
- **हिंसेज्ज** (hiṃsejja) = would commit violence against
- **पाणभूयाइं** (pāṇabhūyāiṃ) = living beings, creatures
- **तसे** (tase) = mobile (two-sense and above)
- **अदुव थावरे** (aduva thāvare) = or the immobile (one-sense — earth, water, fire, air, plant)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse states the consequence that justifies the previous one. A single slip — a stumble in a pit, a fall on slippery ground — kills not only one or two creatures but whole categories of them: the visible mobile beings (insects, worms) crushed by the falling body, and the invisible immobile beings (earth-bodied, water-bodied, plant-bodied) crushed beneath it. The monk's vow of non-violence is not a matter of intent only; even an accidental slip created by careless walking destroys life and binds karma. So the rule of avoiding bad ground is not bodily caution but spiritual integrity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk slips or falls on rough ground, he will harm both the visible little creatures and the invisible tiny lives in earth, water and plants — so even a stumble is a serious matter.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How many living things does one moment of carelessness affect — even when no harm was meant?

## SUTRA 5.1.6 — The Better Path Wins

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तम्हा तेण ण गच्छेज्जा, संजए सुसमाहिए ।
सइ अण्णेण मग्गेण, जयमेव परक्कमे ॥५.१.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.6 ||
tamhā teṇa ṇa gacchejjā, saṃjae susamāhie |
sai aṇṇeṇa maggeṇa, jayameva parakkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Therefore, the well-composed restrained monk should not go by such a path; if another path exists, he should walk by it carefully.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **तेण** (teṇa) = by that (treacherous) path
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not go
- **संजए सुसमाहिए** (saṃjae susamāhie) = the well-composed restrained monk
- **सइ अण्णेण मग्गेण** (sai aṇṇeṇa maggeṇa) = if there is another path
- **जयमेव** (jayameva) = with care, with vigilance
- **परक्कमे** (parakkame) = should proceed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the practical conclusion of the previous two verses. Where a safer alternative exists, the monk must take it. Only where no alternative exists may the dangerous path be walked, and even then with full mindfulness. The phrase **jayameva** — "with care alone" — is the sutra's signature word: nothing the monk does is to be done without yatana, the slow, deliberate, watchful awareness that protects every form of life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

So if a better path is available, the careful monk should always take it; he should only use a difficult path when no other choice exists, and even then very mindfully.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your day are you choosing a harmful shortcut when a slower, safer path is right beside you?

## SUTRA 5.1.7 — Avoiding Heaps That Soil the Foot

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इंगालं छारियं रासिं, तुसरासिं च गोमयं ।
ससरक्खेहिं पाएहिं, संजओ तं णाइक्कमे ॥५.१.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.7 ||
iṃgālaṃ chāriyaṃ rāsiṃ, tusarāsiṃ ca gomayaṃ |
sasarakkhehiṃ pāehiṃ, saṃjao taṃ ṇāikkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A heap of charcoal, of ash, of chaff, or of cow-dung — the restrained monk should not step on any of these with feet covered in soil-particles.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इंगालं** (iṃgālaṃ) = (a heap of) charcoal
- **छारियं रासिं** (chāriyaṃ rāsiṃ) = a heap of ash
- **तुसरासिं** (tusarāsiṃ) = a heap of chaff (husks)
- **गोमयं** (gomayaṃ) = a heap of cow-dung
- **ससरक्खेहिं पाएहिं** (sasarakkhehiṃ pāehiṃ) = with feet covered in soil-particles (sachitta-raj — living earth-particles)
- **संजओ** (saṃjao) = the restrained monk
- **तं** (taṃ) = those (heaps)
- **णाइक्कमे** (ṇāikkame) = should not step over, should not tread upon

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is a finely observed rule. While walking, the monk's feet may pick up small particles of living earth (sachitta-raj). If he then steps on a heap of ash, charcoal, chaff, or dung, the moist earth-particles on his foot get pressed onto these heaps — and earth-bodied beings die. The verse therefore tells the monk: do not step on such heaps if your feet are dirty. If the path requires it, he should first carefully wipe his feet with the rajoharana (the soft woollen broom carried by Jain monks). The principle once again: even tiny earth-bodied lives are worth this much care.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk has dust on his feet (which contains tiny earth-creatures), he should not step on heaps of charcoal, ash, chaff, or dung — because pressing the earth-creatures into those heaps would kill them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even what we leave on the dust of our shoes carries life — what does this say about how lightly we should tread?

## SUTRA 5.1.8 — Conditions Under Which Not to Go for Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण चरेज्ज वासे वासंते, महियाए व पडंतीए ।
महावाए व वायंते, तिरिच्छसंपाइमेसु वा ॥५.१.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.8 ||
ṇa carejja vāse vāsaṃte, mahiyāe va paḍaṃtīe |
mahāvāe va vāyaṃte, tiricchasaṃpāimesu vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should not walk for alms when rain is falling, when fog is descending, when a great wind is blowing, or when flying creatures are crossing the path in numbers.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वासे वासंते** (vāse vāsaṃte) = when rain is falling
- **महियाए व पडंतीए** (mahiyāe va paḍaṃtīe) = or when fog/mist is descending
- **महावाए व वायंते** (mahāvāe va vāyaṃte) = or when a great wind is blowing
- **तिरिच्छसंपाइमेसु वा** (tiricchasaṃpāimesu vā) = or when sideways-flying creatures (flies, mosquitoes, butterflies, moths) are abundant in the air
- **ण चरेज्ज** (ṇa carejja) = should not walk

### 5. COMMENTARY

Each of the four conditions named is a circumstance in which an extra category of life would be harmed. Rain-water itself contains countless water-bodied beings; to walk through rain crushes them with every step, and water from the body or robes drips onto fresh creatures and earth. Fog is the same in finer form: its tiny droplets each contain a water-soul. A great wind whips up dust full of earth-bodied beings and forces them into the eyes, mouth, and ears of any walker; it also stirs up air-bodied beings. And when flying insects are dense in the air, walking forces them into the body and crushes them. In all four conditions, the rule is the same: the monk waits. Even hunger does not override the principle.

This verse also teaches a deeper rhythm: the monk's life is shaped by the weather of the world, not against it. He moves with the conditions that allow life to flourish; he stays still when movement would cost lives. This responsiveness — this willingness to be inconvenienced for the sake of the smallest beings — is the inner heart of monastic non-violence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not go for food when it is raining, foggy, very windy, or when many small flying insects are in the air — because walking through any of these would kill many invisible little beings.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you wait, even when you are hungry, if your moving forward would hurt others?

## SUTRA 5.1.9 — Avoiding the Quarter of the Courtesans

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण चरेज्ज वेससामंते, बंभचेरवसाणुए ।
बंभयारिस्स दंतस्स, होज्जा तत्थ विसोत्तिया ॥५.१.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.9 ||
ṇa carejja vesasāmaṃte, baṃbhaceravasāṇue |
baṃbhayārissa daṃtassa, hojjā tattha visottiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk dedicated to the practice of celibacy should not walk near the dwellings of courtesans; for even a sense-controlled celibate monk may there encounter disturbance to his vow.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वेससामंते** (vesasāmaṃte) = near the dwelling of courtesans (vesa = courtesan; samanta = nearby)
- **बंभचेरवसाणुए** (baṃbhaceravasāṇue) = the monk dwelling in (or following) the practice of celibacy
- **बंभयारिस्स** (baṃbhayārissa) = of the celibate monk
- **दंतस्स** (daṃtassa) = of the sense-controlled
- **होज्जा** (hojjā) = there may arise
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there
- **विसोत्तिया** (visottiyā) = disturbance, mental disorder, an outward turning of the inward stream of restraint
- **ण चरेज्ज** (ṇa carejja) = should not walk

### 5. COMMENTARY

The discipline of the previous verses was about non-violence; the discipline now turns to the second great vow — celibacy (brahmacharya). The text speaks plainly: even the monk who has mastered his senses should not walk in the neighbourhood of courtesans, for the very air of such a place can disturb the inward current of restraint. The Prakrit word **visottiya** is striking — it means the *outward turning* of a stream that should be flowing inward. The monk's restraint is like a river flowing inward toward the soul; near such places, the current may be deflected outward. Even the strongest celibate cannot count on his strength here; the rule is to remove oneself from temptation rather than test it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not even walk near the area where courtesans live, because even a self-controlled monk may find his vow of celibacy disturbed there.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What places do you walk through, telling yourself you are strong enough — when wisdom would simply walk around?

## SUTRA 5.1.10 — The Doubts That Arise From Such Visits

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणायतणे चरंतस्स, संसग्गिए अभिक्खणं ।
होज्ज वयाणं पीला, सामण्णम्मि य संसओ ॥५.१.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.10 ||
aṇāyatane caraṃtassa, saṃsaggie abhikkhaṇaṃ |
hojja vayāṇaṃ pīlā, sāmaṇṇammi ya saṃsao ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the monk who repeatedly walks in unsuitable places and keeps such company, there arises pain to his vows and doubt about his very monkhood.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अणायतणे** (aṇāyatane) = in an unsuitable place, an "anayatan" (lit. "non-residence" — a place not fit for spiritual life)
- **चरंतस्स** (caraṃtassa) = of one who walks (there)
- **संसग्गिए** (saṃsaggie) = through (such) contact / association
- **अभिक्खणं** (abhikkhaṇaṃ) = repeatedly, again and again
- **होज्ज वयाणं पीला** (hojja vayāṇaṃ pīlā) = there arises pain to the vows (the inward weakening of the vows)
- **सामण्णम्मि** (sāmaṇṇammi) = in monkhood (shramanyam)
- **य संसओ** (ya saṃsao) = and doubt

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verse warned about a single walk; this one warns about the cumulative effect. Repeated exposure to wrong company gradually wears down the vows — like a soft drip of water that hollows even rock. Worse still, repeated exposure breeds doubt: the monk begins to question whether his life of restraint is worth it. The sutra-author names the danger precisely: not just *vrata-pida* (pain to the vows), but *shramanya-samshaya* (doubt about being a monk at all). Many a monk has fallen not by one great failure but by a hundred small associations whose effect was invisible until it was decisive.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk who keeps walking near and mixing with the wrong people will gradually find his vows weakening, and may even start to doubt his entire monastic life.

What "small" associations are slowly eroding what you most want to protect?

## SUTRA 5.1.12 — Avoiding Dangerous Places on the Path

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

साणं सूइयं गाविं, दित्तं गोणं हयं गयं ।
संडिब्भं कलहं जुद्धं, दूरओ परिवज्जए ॥५.१.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.12 ||
sāṇaṃ sūiyaṃ gāviṃ, dittaṃ goṇaṃ hayaṃ gayaṃ |
saṃḍibbhaṃ kalahaṃ juddhaṃ, dūrao parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should keep at a distance from a (rabid) dog, a newly-calved cow, an enraged ox, a horse, an elephant, a place where children are at play, a quarrel, or a fight.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **साणं** (sāṇaṃ) = a dog (especially a rabid or aggressive one)
- **सूइयं गाविं** (sūiyaṃ gāviṃ) = a newly-calved cow (likely to charge to protect her calf)
- **दित्तं गोणं** (dittaṃ goṇaṃ) = an enraged or maddened bull/ox
- **हयं** (hayaṃ) = a horse (uncontrolled)
- **गयं** (gayaṃ) = an elephant (uncontrolled)
- **संडिब्भं** (saṃḍibbhaṃ) = a place where children are absorbed in play (and may not notice the monk)
- **कलहं** (kalahaṃ) = a quarrel (verbal dispute)
- **जुद्धं** (juddhaṃ) = a fight (physical)
- **दूरओ** (dūrao) = from a distance
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid (go around)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The text now lists eight specific dangers to avoid on the alms-route. The first five are animals capable of sudden violence: a stray or rabid dog may attack and bite; a newly-calved cow becomes ferociously protective and will charge anyone near her calf; an enraged bull, an excited horse or elephant — animals stronger than a human and unpredictable when agitated — can maim or kill. The sixth is more subtle: children absorbed in cricket or other games may collide with the monk, knock him over, or accidentally injure him. The seventh and eighth are human dangers: a verbal quarrel that the monk's presence may inflame, and a fight in which the monk could be drawn in or hurt as a bystander.

The monk avoids all of these not because his life is more precious than another's, but because if he is hurt, killed, or drawn into combat, the discipline itself is lost. Self-protection here is not selfishness; it is preservation of the conditions under which the vow can continue.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should walk well around dogs, newly-calved cows, angry bulls, horses, elephants, places where children play, quarrels, and fights — to protect himself and the discipline.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What situations on your daily route do you walk straight into, when going around would be the wiser choice?

## SUTRA 5.1.13 — Inner Composure While Walking

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणुण्णए णावणए, अप्पहिट्ठे अणाउले ।
इंदियाणि जहाभागं, दमइत्ता मुणी चरे ॥५.१.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.13 ||
aṇuṇṇae ṇāvaṇae, appahiṭṭhe aṇāule |
iṃdiyāṇi jahābhāgaṃ, damaittā muṇī care ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Neither held high nor bowed low, neither over-elated nor agitated, the monk should walk having subdued each of his senses according to its proper sphere.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अणुण्णए** (aṇuṇṇae) = not raised high (neither in body nor in pride)
- **णावणए** (ṇāvaṇae) = not bowed too low (neither in body nor in despondency)
- **अप्पहिट्ठे** (appahiṭṭhe) = not over-elated (in favourable situations)
- **अणाउले** (aṇāule) = not agitated (in unfavourable situations)
- **इंदियाणि** (iṃdiyāṇi) = the senses
- **जहाभागं** (jahābhāgaṃ) = each according to its sphere
- **दमइत्ता** (damaittā) = having subdued
- **मुणी चरे** (muṇī care) = the monk should walk

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the inner posture that should accompany the outer alms-walk. The body should be neither held proudly upright nor bent in beggarly humility — the monk is neither superior nor inferior; he simply walks straight. The mind should not swing into elation when food is offered easily, nor into agitation when it is refused. Each of the five senses must be held in check: the eyes not roving toward attractive sights, the ears not chasing music or gossip, the nose not pulled by the smell of cooking, the tongue not anticipating taste, the body not seeking comfortable touch. Each sense, the verse says, should be subdued *jahabhagam* — within its own proper sphere of necessary activity, no more.

The commentators note that the two pairs (high/low, elated/agitated) cover both physical bearing and emotional state. *Anunnae* applies both to the body held proudly aloft and to the inner pride of caste, learning, or austerity. *Navanae* applies both to the cringing posture and to the inner despondency that comes when one feels unworthy. Both are to be set aside; what remains is the steady, equable walking of one whose interior is balanced.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should walk neither proudly nor too humbly, neither thrilled when things go his way nor upset when they don't, keeping each sense quiet and under control.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of your senses pulls you most easily off centre — and what would it look like to walk with it gently held?

## SUTRA 5.1.14 — Manner of Walking and Bearing

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दवदवस्स ण गच्छेज्जा, भासमाणो य गोयरे ।
हसंतो णाभिगच्छेज्जा, कुलं उच्चावयं सया ॥५.१.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.14 ||
davadavassa ṇa gacchejjā, bhāsamāṇo ya goyare |
hasaṃto ṇābhigacchejjā, kulaṃ uccāvayaṃ sayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should not walk hurriedly, nor talking, nor laughing, while going for alms — and (he should go) always to households both high and low.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दवदवस्स** (davadavassa) = hurriedly, with rapid steps
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not walk
- **भासमाणो** (bhāsamāṇo) = while talking
- **गोयरे** (goyare) = on the alms-walk
- **हसंतो** (hasaṃto) = while laughing
- **णाभिगच्छेज्जा** (ṇābhigacchejjā) = should not approach (households)
- **कुलं उच्चावयं** (kulaṃ uccāvayaṃ) = households high and low (rich and poor)
- **सया** (sayā) = always

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three negative rules and one positive rule are combined in a single verse. The monk should not hurry — hurried walking damages the discipline of watching the path and creates the impression of impatience. He should not chat with other monks or with householders along the way — silence preserves the inwardness of the walk and keeps irya-samiti pure. He should not laugh — laughter, especially loud laughter, draws attention, breaks decorum, and undermines the seriousness of the alms-walk. And the positive rule: the monk should go to *high and low* households alike. He must not show favouritism — not avoid the poor because their food is plain, not seek out the rich because their food is varied. The cow does not graze only the lush patches; the monk too takes from all.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

While going for food, the monk should not walk in a hurry, should not chat, should not laugh — and he must visit both rich and poor homes equally without preference.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where do you show favouritism in your daily comings-and-goings — and what would it cost you to give it up?

## SUTRA 5.1.15 — Where Not to Look on the Way

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आलोयं थिग्गलं दारं, संधिं दगभवणाणि य ।
चरंतो ण विणिज्झाए, संकट्ठाणं विवज्जए ॥५.१.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.15 ||
āloyaṃ thiggalaṃ dāraṃ, saṃdhiṃ dagabhavaṇāṇi ya |
caraṃto ṇa viṇijjhāe, saṃkaṭṭhāṇaṃ vivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

While walking, the monk should not stare at windows, holes (in walls), doorways, gaps, or water-houses (bathing rooms); he should avoid all places that may arouse suspicion.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आलोयं** (āloyaṃ) = a window, an opening for air-and-light (alok = view)
- **थिग्गलं** (thiggalaṃ) = a hole patched in a wall, a wall-opening
- **दारं** (dāraṃ) = a doorway
- **संधिं** (saṃdhiṃ) = a gap, crevice (such as those made by burglars in walls)
- **दगभवणाणि** (dagabhavaṇāṇi) = water-houses — bathing rooms, washing stations
- **चरंतो** (caraṃto) = while walking
- **ण विणिज्झाए** (ṇa viṇijjhāe) = should not stare, should not gaze closely
- **संकट्ठाणं** (saṃkaṭṭhāṇaṃ) = places of suspicion
- **विवज्जए** (vivajjae) = should avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eyes of the monk reveal his inner state. If he is seen pausing to look at windows, doorways, gaps, or bathing rooms, householders will at once suspect him — they may take him for a thief casing the house, or a man of unclean motives gazing at women bathing or dressing. Either suspicion destroys his character in the eyes of others and harms the reputation of the entire monastic order. The principle is broader than mere appearance: the monk should *not* be looking at such places at all, because doing so shows that the eye and mind are not yet inward. The verse closes with the wider rule: avoid all places that may arouse suspicion. If even an innocent action could be misunderstood, the wise monk simply removes himself from the situation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

While walking, the monk must not stare at windows, holes in walls, doorways, gaps, or bathrooms — and he should stay away from any place where his behaviour could be misunderstood.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do your eyes go to places that, even innocently, would cause others to wonder about you?

## SUTRA 5.1.16 — Avoid Royal Treasuries and Hidden Stores

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रण्णो गिहवईणं च, रहस्सारक्खियाणि य ।
संकिलेसकरं ठाणं, दूरओ परिवज्जए ॥५.१.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.16 ||
raṇṇo gihavaīṇaṃ ca, rahassārakkhiyāṇi ya |
saṃkilesakaraṃ ṭhāṇaṃ, dūrao parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should keep at a great distance from the secret guarded places of the king and of wealthy householders — those places that produce mental disturbance.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **रण्णो** (raṇṇo) = of the king
- **गिहवईणं** (gihavaīṇaṃ) = of (wealthy) householders, the city's leading merchants
- **रहस्सारक्खियाणि** (rahassārakkhiyāṇi) = secretly guarded places — treasuries, armouries, council-chambers

## SUTRA 5.1.17 — Forbidden and Permitted Households

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिकुट्ठं कुलं ण पविसे, मामगं परिवज्जए ।
अचियत्तं कुलं ण पविसे, चियत्तं पविसे कुलं ॥५.१.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.17 ||
paḍikuṭṭhaṃ kulaṃ ṇa pavise, māmagaṃ parivajjae |
aciyattaṃ kulaṃ ṇa pavise, ciyattaṃ pavise kulaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should not enter a forbidden household, should avoid one that has refused him; should not enter a household held in contempt — but should enter the household that is well-regarded.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पडिकुट्ठं कुलं** (paḍikuṭṭhaṃ kulaṃ) = a forbidden household — one excluded by scriptural rule (the unwholesome, anarya, or jugupsita houses)
- **ण पविसे** (ṇa pavise) = should not enter
- **मामगं** (māmagaṃ) = a household that has refused — where the master has said "do not come to my house" (or, in another reading: "my own / familiar house" — see commentary)
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid
- **अचियत्तं कुलं** (aciyattaṃ kulaṃ) = a disliked / unrespected household — those of bad reputation, meat-eaters, drinkers, courtesans, or others looked down upon
- **चियत्तं कुलं** (ciyattaṃ kulaṃ) = a well-regarded household — those of good conduct and reputation
- **पविसे** (pavise) = should enter

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the first explicit rule about *which* households the monk may enter. Three categories are forbidden:

(1) **Forbidden** (paḍikuṭṭha) — households formally excluded by scripture. The Acharanga Sutra (2/1/2) lists twelve such kulas — those engaged in occupations involving violence, those of butcher-castes, brewers, fishermen, and so on. Among the four traditional varnas, the monks generally took alms from Brahmin, Kshatriya, and Vaishya households, avoiding these excluded ones.

(2) **Refusal-marked** (māmaga) — a household where the master has said: "do not come to my house." The monk respects this and never returns there. (Alternatively, *māmaga* may mean "my own / familiar" house — a relative's house — which the monk also avoids to prevent emotional attachment.)

(3) **Disliked / disreputable** (aciyatta) — homes with very bad reputation: those known for dishonest dealings, immoral conduct, courtesan-houses, or drunkards. To enter such houses harms the monk's own reputation and the order's standing in the community.

But the fourth category — *ciyatta*, the well-regarded — is the one the monk *should* enter. These are households of good reputation, clean conduct, and proper food (vegetarian, conscientious). Among such, the monk goes from house to house without preference.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not enter scripturally forbidden homes, homes that have asked him not to come, or disreputable homes — but he should enter the homes of people known for good conduct.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What kind of company shapes you most — and is the choice of where to go a small thing or a large one?

## SUTRA 5.1.18 — Opening Doors Without Permission

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

साणी पावारपिहियं, अप्पणा णावपंगुरे ।
कवाडं णो पणुल्लिज्जा, उग्गहंसि अजाइया ॥५.१.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.18 ||
sāṇī pāvārapihiyaṃ, appaṇā ṇāvapaṃgure |
kavāḍaṃ ṇo paṇullijjā, uggahaṃsi ajāiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A door covered with a sackcloth or curtain — the monk should not lift it himself; he should not push open a closed door without first asking permission.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **साणी** (sāṇī) = a hemp/sackcloth screen, a thatched hanging
- **पावार** (pāvāra) = a thick cotton or woven curtain
- **पिहियं** (pihiyaṃ) = covered (with), screened by
- **अप्पणा** (appaṇā) = by oneself
- **णावपंगुरे** (ṇāvapaṃgure) = should not lift / open
- **कवाडं** (kavāḍaṃ) = the door panel
- **णो पणुल्लिज्जा** (ṇo paṇullijjā) = should not push open
- **उग्गहंसि** (uggahaṃsi) = with permission (avagraha — the granted right)
- **अजाइया** (ajāiyā) = without having asked

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse teaches the social-spiritual courtesy of approach. The household belongs to the householder, and even the smallest action — pushing open a door, lifting a curtain — is an intrusion if done without permission. If the householder is bathing or sleeping, a sudden uncovering by the monk causes embarrassment and may even cause the householder to speak harshly. From that single discourtesy, mistrust of the entire monastic order can grow. So the rule: the monk waits at the door, gives an audible cough or signal, and enters only when invited. The principle is once again the protection of the conditions under which the alms-relationship can continue.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should not push open a closed door or lift a curtain at the entrance of a house himself — he must first ask the householder's permission.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In your daily life, where do you push past invisible doors that were not yet opened to you?

## SUTRA 5.1.19 — Bodily Needs Before Going

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य, वच्चमुत्तं ण धारए ।
ओगासं फासुयं णच्चा, अणुण्णविय वोसिरे ॥५.१.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.19 ||
goyaraggapaviṭṭho ya, vaccamuttaṃ ṇa dhārae |
ogāsaṃ phāsuyaṃ ṇaccā, aṇuṇṇaviya vosire ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who has entered upon the alms-walk should not suppress the urge for stool or urine; finding a faultless place and obtaining permission, he should release them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गोयरग्गपविट्ठो** (goyaraggapaviṭṭho) = one who has entered upon the alms-walk
- **वच्चमुत्तं** (vaccamuttaṃ) = stool and urine
- **ण धारए** (ṇa dhārae) = should not hold back, should not suppress
- **ओगासं** (ogāsaṃ) = a place
- **फासुयं** (phāsuyaṃ) = lifeless / faultless (non-living, free of small beings)
- **णच्चा** (ṇaccā) = having known, having recognised
- **अणुण्णविय** (aṇuṇṇaviya) = having taken permission (from the owner of the place)
- **वोसिरे** (vosire) = should release / let go

### 5. COMMENTARY

A practical and humane rule. If during the alms-walk a natural urge arises, the monk must not suppress it — suppression damages the body and weakens the eyes and life-force (the verse quoted: "by suppressing urine the eyes weaken, by suppressing stool the very life is shortened"). But the release must be in a faultless place — earth that does not contain visible sprouts or insects — and the monk must ask the owner's permission to use it. Even bodily relief is to be done with non-violence and with social respect. Indeed, the verse implies that bodily needs should be attended to *before* setting out for alms; only if a need arises mid-walk should the monk pause to address it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk feels the need to relieve himself during the alms-walk, he must not hold it in — but he should find a clean spot, ask the owner's permission, and only then relieve himself.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do you push your body past its own quiet signals — and what does it cost you?

## SUTRA 5.1.20 — Avoiding Dark and Low Doorways

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णीयदुवारं तमसं, कुट्टगं परिवज्जए ।
अचक्खुविसओ, जत्थ पाणा दुप्पडिलेहगा ॥५.१.२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.20 ||
ṇīyaduvāraṃ tamasaṃ, kuṭṭagaṃ parivajjae |
acakkhuvisao, jattha pāṇā duppaḍilehagā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should avoid storerooms with low doors and full of darkness, where the eye cannot see and where small creatures cannot be properly inspected.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णीयदुवारं** (ṇīyaduvāraṃ) = with low doorways
- **तमसं** (tamasaṃ) = dark, full of darkness
- **कुट्टगं** (kuṭṭagaṃ) = a storeroom, an underground chamber, a granary-like enclosed space
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid
- **अचक्खुविसओ** (acakkhuvisao) = beyond the range of the eye, where vision cannot reach
- **जत्थ** (jattha) = where
- **पाणा** (pāṇā) = small creatures (two-sense and above)
- **दुप्पडिलेहगा** (duppaḍilehagā) = difficult to inspect, hard to see

A dark, low-doored storeroom is dangerous on three counts: the monk cannot see the small creatures that may be on the floor and so cannot keep irya-samiti; he cannot examine the food properly for faults; and a stooping entry through a low door leaves him vulnerable to falls or to suspicions of pilferage. Where vision fails, every layer of the alms-discipline fails. So the monk simply does not enter such places.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should avoid storerooms with low doors and dark interiors, because he cannot see the small creatures or properly check the food.

## SUTRA 5.1.21 — Avoiding Spaces with Scattered Seeds or Fresh Plaster

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जत्थ पुप्फाइं बीयाइं, विप्पइण्णाइं कोट्ठए ।
अहुणोवलित्तं उल्लं, दट्ठूण परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.21 ||
jattha pupphāiṃ bīyāiṃ, vippaiṇṇāiṃ koṭṭhae |
ahuṇovalittaṃ ullaṃ, daṭṭhūṇa parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A storeroom where flowers and seeds are scattered, or which is freshly plastered and wet — seeing this, the monk should turn away.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जत्थ** (jattha) = where
- **कोट्ठए** (koṭṭhae) = in the storeroom, by its doorway
- **पुप्फाइं** (pupphāiṃ) = flowers
- **बीयाइं** (bīyāiṃ) = seeds (grains)
- **विप्पइण्णाइं** (vippaiṇṇāiṃ) = scattered, strewn about
- **अहुणोवलित्तं** (ahuṇovalittaṃ) = freshly plastered (with cow-dung paste, the traditional floor-finish)
- **उल्लं** (ullaṃ) = wet (still damp)
- **दट्ठूण** (daṭṭhūṇa) = having seen
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

Two kinds of unsafe entry are named. First, a place where flowers (one-sense plant beings still alive) or grains (potential one-sense plant beings) are scattered on the floor — to walk over them crushes them. Second, a place freshly plastered with damp cow-dung — the monk's feet would sink into the soft surface, kill the moisture-loving organisms, and dirty the householder's freshly cleaned space (which would then need re-plastering, killing more plant and earth beings). Where either condition is visible, the monk steps back.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk sees flowers, scattered grains, or a freshly plastered wet floor at a household entrance, he should not enter — he would harm small lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "freshly plastered floors" do you walk across in others' lives, leaving marks behind?

## SUTRA 5.1.22 — Animals or Children Blocking the Doorway

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एलगं दारगं साणं, वच्छगं वावि कोट्ठए ।
उल्लंघिया ण पविसे, विउहित्ताण व संजए ॥५.१.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.22 ||
elagaṃ dāragaṃ sāṇaṃ, vacchagaṃ vāvi koṭṭhae |
ullaṃghiyā ṇa pavise, viuhittāṇa va saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If a goat (or ram), a child, a dog, or a calf is in the doorway of the storeroom, the restrained monk should not enter by stepping over them or by pushing them aside.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एलगं** (elagaṃ) = a goat (commentators differ — the Churni reads "ram"; the Tika reads "goat-kid")
- **दारगं** (dāragaṃ) = a child
- **साणं** (sāṇaṃ) = a dog
- **वच्छगं** (vacchagaṃ) = a calf
- **वावि कोट्ठए** (vāvi koṭṭhae) = also at the doorway / in the entrance-room
- **उल्लंघिया** (ullaṃghiyā) = by stepping over
- **ण पविसे** (ṇa pavise) = should not enter
- **विउहित्ताण** (viuhittāṇa) = by pushing aside / forcibly moving
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

The doorway may be blocked by a small living being. The monk's options are limited and all the obvious ones are forbidden: he should not step *over* the creature (which may startle it into harming itself or him — the goat may butt the monk with its horns and break his alms-bowl; the dog may bite; the calf may kick; the child may fall and be injured, leading to his parents' anger toward the monk). Nor should he forcibly push the creature aside (which is itself a small violence, and may injure the child or animal). The implicit instruction: if the doorway is so blocked, simply wait — or move on to the next house. ⚠️ The Prakrit *elaga* is rendered as "ram/billy-goat" by some commentators and "lamb/kid" by others; either reading preserves the rule.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If a goat, child, dog, or calf is sitting in the doorway, the monk must not step over them or push them aside — he should wait or move on to another house.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something small blocks your way, do you push past it — or do you stop and notice that even the obstacle deserves consideration?

## SUTRA 5.1.23 — How to Look Inside the House

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असंसत्तं पलोएज्जा, णाइदूरावलोयए ।
उप्फुल्लं ण विणिज्झाए, णियट्टिज्ज अयंपिरो ॥५.१.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.23 ||
asaṃsattaṃ paloejjā, ṇāidūrāvaloyae |
upphullaṃ ṇa viṇijjhāe, ṇiyaṭṭijja ayaṃpiro ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should look (for the offering) without attachment, neither gazing too far nor staring with wide eyes; if no food is offered, he should turn back without speaking harshly or pleadingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **असंसत्तं** (asaṃsattaṃ) = without attachment, dispassionately
- **पलोएज्जा** (paloejjā) = should look around
- **णाइदूरावलोयए** (ṇāidūrāvaloyae) = should not look too far (into distant rooms or distant objects)
- **उप्फुल्लं** (upphullaṃ) = with widely opened (eager / surprised) eyes
- **ण विणिज्झाए** (ṇa viṇijjhāe) = should not stare
- **णियट्टिज्ज** (ṇiyaṭṭijja) = should turn back, should leave
- **अयंपिरो** (ayaṃpiro) = without speaking — neither in harsh anger nor in pleading complaint

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse refines the discipline of the eyes at the householder's door. The monk's gaze should be even and impartial — neither lingering on the women of the household, nor on the ornaments and furnishings, nor on the food vessels with greedy interest. He should not stare deep into the house (which suggests prying) nor open his eyes wide in surprise at any rich sight (which suggests envy or amazement). And if the householder offers nothing — or offers only a little, or only stale food, or speaks harshly — the monk simply turns and leaves *without a word*: no angry retort, no pleading complaint, no plea for more. Equanimity in the face of refusal is itself a major spiritual victory.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should glance into the house calmly, not stare into the distance or with wide eyes, and if no food is given he should leave without saying anything bitter or whining.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you ask and receive nothing, what comes out of your mouth — and what does that reveal about your inside?

## SUTRA 5.1.24 — Honouring the Boundaries of the Household

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अइभूमिं ण गच्छेज्जा, गोयरग्गगओ मुणी ।
कुलस्स भूमिं जाणित्ता, मियं भूमिं परक्कमे ॥५.१.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.24 ||
aibhūmiṃ ṇa gacchejjā, goyaraggagao muṇī |
kulassa bhūmiṃ jāṇittā, miyaṃ bhūmiṃ parakkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk on the alms-walk should not go beyond the permitted area; knowing the boundary of the household, he should walk only within the proper measured space.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अइभूमिं** (aibhūmiṃ) = beyond the boundary, into the forbidden inner area
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not go
- **गोयरग्गगओ** (goyaraggagao) = one who has gone for alms
- **मुणी** (muṇī) = the monk
- **कुलस्स भूमिं** (kulassa bhūmiṃ) = the (proper) boundary of the household
- **जाणित्ता** (jāṇittā) = knowing
- **मियं भूमिं** (miyaṃ bhūmiṃ) = the measured / limited area
- **परक्कमे** (parakkame) = should walk

### 5. COMMENTARY

Each household, by local custom, has an unspoken boundary up to which guests may proceed. Some homes welcome a visitor only at the doorway; some allow him into the front room; some — generally those of close family of the monastic community — into the kitchen itself. The monk learns these conventions and respects them. To go *atibhumi* — beyond the boundary — is a breach of trust: it makes the monk look like a trespasser, embarrasses the women of the household, and may be reported as misconduct. The discipline here is again about preserving the trust on which the entire alms-system rests.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should learn how far inside each home he is allowed to go, and never go beyond that boundary uninvited.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your relationships do you walk just one step further than you were welcomed?

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थेव पडिलेहेज्जा, भूमिभागं वियक्खणो ।
सिणाणस्स य वच्चस्स, संलोगं परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.25 ||
tattheva paḍilehejjā, bhūmibhāgaṃ viyakkhaṇo |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There itself, the wise monk should examine the patch of ground (where he will stand); and he should keep out of sight of bathing-rooms and toilets.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तत्थेव** (tattheva) = there itself (in the proper area)
- **पडिलेहेज्जा** (paḍilehejjā) = should inspect (the ground for living beings)
- **भूमिभागं** (bhūmibhāgaṃ) = the patch of ground
- **वियक्खणो** (viyakkhaṇo) = the wise / discerning (monk)
- **सिणाणस्स** (siṇāṇassa) = of the bathing room
- **वच्चस्स** (vaccassa) = of the toilet
- **संलोगं** (saṃlogaṃ) = direct sight, line of vision
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid

दगमट्टियआयाणे, बीयाणि हरियाणि य ।
परिवज्जंतो चिट्ठेज्जा, सव्विंदिय समाहिए ॥५.१.२६॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Avoiding the paths used for fetching water and earth, and (places strewn with) seeds and green plants, the monk should stand still, with all senses well composed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दगमट्टियआयाणे** (dagamaṭṭiyaāyāṇe) = the path where water and earth (mud, clay) are fetched and carried
- **बीयाणि** (bīyāṇi) = seeds, grains

## SUTRA 5.1.27 — Accept Only What Is Permissible

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थ से चिट्ठमाणस्स, आहरे पाणभोयणं ।
अकप्पियं ण इच्छेज्जा, पडिगाहेज्ज कप्पियं ॥५.१.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.27 ||
tattha se ciṭṭhamāṇassa, āhare pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ |
akappiyaṃ ṇa icchejjā, paḍigāhejja kappiyaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When the monk is standing there (in the proper place) and food and drink are brought, he should not desire what is impermissible (akalpaniya) and should accept only what is permissible (kalpaniya).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there
- **से चिट्ठमाणस्स** (se ciṭṭhamāṇassa) = of him standing (the monk)
- **आहरे** (āhare) = (the giver) brings
- **पाणभोयणं** (pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ) = drink and food
- **अकप्पियं** (akappiyaṃ) = impermissible / faulty (akalpaniya)
- **ण इच्छेज्जा** (ṇa icchejjā) = should not desire / accept
- **पडिगाहेज्ज** (paḍigāhejja) = should accept
- **कप्पियं** (kappiyaṃ) = permissible / faultless (kalpaniya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse opens the section on **grahaneshana** — the rules of acceptance. The monk has now arrived, has stood properly, and the householder is bringing food and drink. The single rule: **distinguish.** What is *kalpaniya* — fit for a Jain monk to accept — is taken; what is *akalpaniya* — unfit — is refused. The Prakrit *kalpa* means rule, conduct, ordinance, propriety. Food obtained without violating any of the 42 doshas is *kalpaniya*; food touched even by one of these faults is *akalpaniya*. This is the entire test, applied to every offering.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When food and drink are brought to him, the monk should accept only what is faultless according to the rules, and refuse what is faulty.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the test by which you decide what to take and what to leave — and is it as clear as the monk's?

## SUTRA 5.1.28 — Refuse Food Brought Spilling

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आहरंती सिया तत्थ, परिसाडेज्ज भोयणं ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.२८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.28 ||
āharaṃtī siyā tattha, parisāḍejja bhoyaṇaṃ |
diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe, ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the woman bringing the food (along the way) spills the food (or drink), the monk should refuse her offer, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आहरंती** (āharaṃtī) = the woman bringing (the food)
- **सिया** (siyā) = perhaps, if it should happen that
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there (along the way)
- **परिसाडेज्ज** (parisāḍejja) = spills, scatters, drops
- **भोयणं** (bhoyaṇaṃ) = food (and drink)
- **दिंतियं** (diṃtiyaṃ) = the giver (woman who is giving)
- **पडियाइक्खे** (paḍiyāikkhe) = should refuse, should decline
- **ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं** (ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ) = "such (food) is not permissible for me"

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the famous **chhardita-dosha** — the "spilling fault." When the giver brings the food in such a way that drops fall along the path, three harms follow: (1) the spilled food may attract ants and insects, who will be killed when stepped on; (2) the spilled liquid harms the air-bodied beings as it falls; (3) the carelessness shows that the giver herself is not attentive. So the monk refuses politely: "such food does not suit my discipline." The refusal is gentle but firm. There is no scolding, no anger; just the simple statement of the rule.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the person bringing food is spilling some of it on the way, the monk should politely refuse: "I cannot accept food given in this way."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What gifts have you received recently that arrived spilled, hurried, or careless — and what was the cost of accepting them anyway?

## SUTRA 5.1.29 — Refuse If Beings Are Crushed Underfoot

सम्मद्दमाणी पाणाणि, बीयाणि हरियाणि य ।
असंजमकरिं णच्चा, तारिसं परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२९॥

|| 5.1.29 ||
sammaddamāṇī pāṇāṇi, bīyāṇi hariyāṇi ya |
asaṃjamakariṃ ṇaccā, tārisaṃ parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the giver, while bringing the food, crushes underfoot living creatures, seeds or green plants, the monk — knowing this to be an unrestrained act — should refuse such food.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सम्मद्दमाणी** (sammaddamāṇī) = crushing, treading upon
- **पाणाणि** (pāṇāṇi) = creatures (mobile two-sense and above)
- **बीयाणि** (bīyāṇi) = seeds (plant-bodied beings)
- **हरियाणि** (hariyāṇi) = green plants

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the person bringing food walks over insects, seeds, or fresh plants, the monk should refuse the food — because accepting it would make him a partner in those small killings.

## SUTRA 5.1.30-31 — Sahriya, Nikkhitta, and Udaka-Doshas

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

साहट्टु णिक्खिवित्ताणं, सचित्तं घट्टियाणि य ।
तहेव समणट्ठाए, उदगं संपणुल्लिया ॥५.१.३०॥

ओगाहइत्ता चलइत्ता, आहरे पाणभोयणं ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.३१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.30 ||
sāhaṭṭu ṇikkhivittāṇaṃ, sacittaṃ ghaṭṭiyāṇi ya |
taheva samaṇaṭṭhāe, udagaṃ saṃpaṇulliyā ||

|| 5.1.31 ||
ogāhaittā calaittā, āhare pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ |
diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe, ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If, for the sake of the monk, the giver — by combining (mixed-with-living-beings food in one place), by setting it aside (placing other things over living beings), by touching living beings, or by stirring or wading through (water containing living beings) — brings the food and drink, the monk should refuse her offer, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **साहट्टु** (sāhaṭṭu) = combining, gathering together (sachitta and achitta items mixed; or pouring living-water-soaked liquids from one vessel into another)
- **णिक्खिवित्ताणं** (ṇikkhivittāṇaṃ) = setting aside, placing the lifeless food on top of living beings (or moving living things to fetch the food)
- **सचित्तं** (sacittaṃ) = a living being
- **घट्टियाणि** (ghaṭṭiyāṇi) = touching, brushing against
- **समणट्ठाए** (samaṇaṭṭhāe) = for the sake of the monk
- **उदगं** (udagaṃ) = water
- **संपणुल्लिया** (saṃpaṇulliyā) = pushing aside, stirring (water containing life)
- **ओगाहइत्ता** (ogāhaittā) = wading into, entering (rain-puddles or pools of fresh water)
- **चलइत्ता** (calaittā) = moving / disturbing (the water)
- **आहरे पाणभोयणं** (āhare pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ) = brings food and drink
- **दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे** (diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe) = should refuse the giver
- **ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं** (ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ) = "such (food) is not permissible for me"

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two verses together describe four interconnected donor-side faults that all destroy the purity of the food:

(1) **Sahriya (sahṛta) dosha** — combining: pouring milk soaked with living water-beings from one vessel to another, mixing sachitta (living) and achitta (lifeless) substances together, gathering items from many vessels into one to look generous, etc. Wherever the combining itself harms life, the food is unfit.

(2) **Nikkhitta (nikṣipta) dosha** — setting-aside: placing the lifeless food temporarily on top of living things (such as fresh grain or vegetation) before giving; or, having brought the food, dropping any lifeless object onto a living being while serving.

(3) **Sachittaṃ ghaṭṭitam** — touching living beings: while serving, brushing the hand against fresh seeds, vegetables, salt-pieces, or even a fire. The touch alone is enough to taint the food.

(4) **Udaka-doshas** — water faults (verse 31 amplifies): pouring or pushing fresh sachitta water aside; wading into a puddle of fresh rainwater to bring the food; stirring up a pool. All these involve the killing of water-bodied beings *for the monk's sake*.

In each case, the food may be physically pure but spiritually impure — the monk cannot accept what required violence to deliver. The refusal-formula is the same: "such food is not permissible for me."

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the giver, in serving the monk, mixes living things together with the food, sets the food on top of living things, touches living beings, or wades through fresh water — the monk must refuse, because all of this kills tiny lives for his sake.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What hidden costs might be in what comes to your table — costs that nobody, including you, thought to count?

## SUTRA 5.1.32 — Refuse If the Hand or Vessel Is Pre-Wet (Purakarma Dosha)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुरेकम्मेण हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.३२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.32 ||
purekammeṇa hattheṇa, davvīe bhāyaṇeṇa vā |
diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe, ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the giver brings the food with a hand, ladle, or vessel that has just been (washed in fresh water — i.e., affected by the fault of "preceding action"), the monk should refuse, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुरेकम्मेण** (purekammeṇa) = with the "preceding-action fault" (purah-karma) — affected by an act done just before the giving (typically: hand or vessel washed in fresh sachitta water for the purpose of serving the monk)
- **हत्थेण** (hattheṇa) = with the hand
- **दव्वीए** (davvīe) = with a ladle
- **भायणेण वा** (bhāyaṇeṇa vā) = or with a vessel
- **दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे** (diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe) = should refuse the giver
- **ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं** (ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ) = "such (food) is not permissible for me"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The **purakarma dosha** is the "preceding-action fault." If the householder, on seeing the monk arrive, washes her hands or the serving ladle in fresh (sachitta) water *before* serving — that washing kills water-beings, and the killing is *for the monk's sake*. So the food carried in such a freshly-wet hand or vessel is unfit. The monk politely refuses. The principle: even pre-action done in preparation for the monk counts as violence done for him.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel has just been freshly washed in water (which would have killed water-beings for the monk's sake), the monk must refuse the food.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "little preparations" do others make on your behalf that you never see — and what would change if you did?

## SUTRA 5.1.33-34 — The Many Forms of "Lipta-Dosha" (Smeared Hand or Vessel)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं उदुल्ले ससिणिद्धे, ससरक्खे मट्टिआऊसे ।
हरियाले हिंगुलए, मणोसिला अंजणे लोणे ॥५.१.३३॥

गेरुय वण्णिय सेढिय, सोरट्ठिय पिट्ठु कुक्कुसकए य ।
उक्किट्ठमसंसट्ठे, संसट्ठे चेव बोधव्वे ॥५.१.३४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.33 ||
evaṃ udulle sasiṇiddhe, sasarakkhe maṭṭiā-ūse |
hariyāle hiṃgulae, maṇosilā aṃjaṇe loṇe ||

|| 5.1.34 ||
geruya vaṇṇiya seḍhiya, soraṭṭhiya piṭṭhu kukkusakae ya |
ukkiṭṭhamasaṃsaṭṭhe, saṃsaṭṭhe ceva bodhavve ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the same way (the monk should refuse food brought by a hand or vessel) that is wet, smeared with moist substance, covered with living dust, smeared with mud or salt-earth, with yellow orpiment, with vermilion, with red arsenic, with collyrium, with salt, with red ochre, with chalk-clay, with white clay, with alum (gopichandan), with flour, with husk-bran — whether thinly smeared or thickly smeared, all these are to be understood as faulty.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवं** (evaṃ) = in the same way (as the previous verse)
- **उदुल्ले** (udulle) = wet (with water)
- **ससिणिद्धे** (sasiṇiddhe) = greasy / damp with water-traces
- **ससरक्खे** (sasarakkhe) = covered with living dust (sachitta-raj)
- **मट्टिआऊसे** (maṭṭiā-ūse) = with mud or salt-earth (alkaline soil)
- **हरियाले** (hariyāle) = with yellow orpiment (haritala)
- **हिंगुलए** (hiṃgulae) = with vermilion (red mineral pigment)
- **मणोसिला** (maṇosilā) = with red arsenic / realgar
- **अंजणे** (aṃjane) = with collyrium (eye-paint)
- **लोणे** (loṇe) = with salt
- **गेरुय** (geruya) = with red ochre
- **वण्णिय** (vaṇṇiya) = with yellow paint-clay
- **सेढिय** (seḍhiya) = with chalk / white clay (used for whitening)
- **सोरट्ठिय** (soraṭṭhiya) = with alum-paste / gopichandan
- **पिट्ठु** (piṭṭhu) = with flour (of any grain)
- **कुक्कुसकए** (kukkusakae) = with husk-bran / chaff-powder
- **उक्किट्ठमसंसट्ठे** (ukkiṭṭhamasaṃsaṭṭhe) = thinly applied / faintly smeared (perhaps "with the powder freshly ground and not yet wiped off")
- **संसट्ठे** (saṃsaṭṭhe) = thickly smeared / well-covered
- **चेव बोधव्वे** (ceva bodhavve) = should be understood (as faulty)

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two verses enumerate the many specific substances which, if present on the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel, render the food unfit for acceptance. The list covers: water-soaked or water-greasy hands; hands covered in living dust (earth-bodied beings); mud, salt-earth (which contains earth-beings); various mineral pigments and dyes (orpiment, vermilion, realgar, collyrium, ochre, paint-clays, alum/gopichandan — many of these are used for body decoration, religious marks, or wall painting); salt (which is sachitta in its raw form); freshly ground flour and husk-bran (fresh food-particles still containing plant-life). Whether the substance is *thinly* present or *thickly* coating the hand, the food is faulty in either case. This is one of the most detailed lists in the entire chapter, and shows just how meticulously the monk examines what touches the food before he accepts it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel has any living moisture, dust, mud, salt, mineral paints, dyes, or fresh flour-powder on it — the monk must understand that the food is unfit and should refuse it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of what comes through the hands of others carries traces of what they were just doing — and how much of you sticks to what you offer?

## SUTRA 5.1.35 — Pashchat-Karma Dosha (Subsequent-Action Fault)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असंसट्ठेण हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा ।
दिज्जमाणं ण इच्छेज्जा, पच्छाकम्मं जहिं भवे ॥५.१.३५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.35 ||
asaṃsaṭṭheṇa hattheṇa, davvīe bhāyaṇeṇa vā |
dijjamāṇaṃ ṇa icchejjā, pacchākammaṃ jahiṃ bhave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Even if the food is given with a hand, ladle, or vessel that is *not* smeared (and so seemingly clean), the monk should not accept it if it would lead to a "subsequent-action" fault (the giver washing the hand or vessel afterwards, killing water-beings).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **असंसट्ठेण** (asaṃsaṭṭheṇa) = un-smeared, clean (free of the aforementioned faults)
- **हत्थेण** (hattheṇa) = with the hand
- **दव्वीए भायणेण वा** (davvīe bhāyaṇeṇa vā) = with a ladle or vessel
- **दिज्जमाणं** (dijjamāṇaṃ) = (food) being given
- **ण इच्छेज्जा** (ṇa icchejjā) = should not desire / should not accept
- **पच्छाकम्मं** (pacchākammaṃ) = the "subsequent-action fault" (pashchat-karma) — washing the hand or vessel in fresh water after serving the monk
- **जहिं भवे** (jahiṃ bhave) = where it would occur

### 5. COMMENTARY

Here is the mirror of the **purakarma** fault (verse 32). Even if the hand and vessel are clean *now*, the monk must refuse if he knows that the giver will, *after* serving him, wash that hand or vessel in fresh sachitta water — killing water-beings on his behalf. The monk's responsibility extends both *before* the giving (purakarma) and *after* (pashchatkarma). The Antagada Sutra cites the example of Queen Devaki who, after offering modaks, deliberately left the plate as it was (un-washed) precisely to spare the monks this fault. The teaching for the householder is also quiet but profound: serve the monk in such a way that the vessel can simply be set aside or used for the family's own meal, not specially cleaned for the monk's sake.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even if the giver's hand or ladle looks clean, the monk should refuse the food if he knows the giver will wash that hand or ladle in fresh water afterwards — because that washing would kill water-beings for the monk's sake.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What care extends not just to the moment you act, but to the moments that follow — and who is harmed by what you do *after* the kindness?

## SUTRA 5.1.36 — When the Hand Is Already Smeared With Acceptable Food

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संसट्ठेण य हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा ।
दिज्जमाणं पडिच्छिज्जा, जं तत्थेसणियं भवे ॥५.१.३६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.36 ||
saṃsaṭṭheṇa ya hattheṇa, davvīe bhāyaṇeṇa vā |
dijjamāṇaṃ paḍicchijjā, jaṃ tattheṣaṇiyaṃ bhave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If, on the other hand, food is given by a hand, ladle, or vessel that is smeared with already-permissible (lifeless) food-substance — the monk *should* accept it, provided the food itself is faultless.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संसट्ठेण** (saṃsaṭṭheṇa) = smeared (with already-cooked, lifeless, permissible food substance — not with sachitta substances)
- **हत्थेण** (hattheṇa) = with the hand
- **दव्वीए भायणेण वा** (davvīe bhāyaṇeṇa vā) = with a ladle or vessel
- **दिज्जमाणं** (dijjamāṇaṃ) = (food) being given
- **पडिच्छिज्जा** (paḍicchijjā) = should accept
- **जं तत्थेसणियं भवे** (jaṃ tattheṣaṇiyaṃ bhave) = if the food there is faultless / permissible (eshaniya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the constructive complement of the previous verses. If the giver's hand or vessel is already coated with the same kind of (lifeless, permissible) food she is now serving — for example, her hand has rice on it because she just served her own family — the monk *should* accept. Why? Because here there is no purakarma fault (no fresh washing was done before) and no pashchatkarma fault (the householder will not need to wash her hand in fresh water afterwards — the smear is the same food, and she will simply continue eating or the smear will dry naturally). The whole logic of the previous verses is preserved.

This principle expresses a beautiful balance in the alms-tradition: the monk accepts food that is naturally given as part of the household's normal life, and refuses food that requires the household to do anything special — anything violent — *for him*.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the giver's hand or vessel is already smeared with the same kind of permissible food (because she was just serving her family), the monk should accept — there's no extra harm done for his sake.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When can you receive simply, without anyone needing to do anything extra for you?

## SUTRA 5.1.37 — When Two Are Eating, One Invites

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुण्हं तु भुंजमाणाणं, एगो तत्थ णिमंतए ।
दिज्जमाणं ण इच्छेज्जा, छंदं से पडिलेहए ॥५.१.३७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.37 ||
duṇhaṃ tu bhuṃjamāṇāṇaṃ, ego tattha ṇimaṃtae |
dijjamāṇaṃ ṇa icchejjā, chaṃdaṃ se paḍilehae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When two persons are eating together (or co-own the food), and one of them invites the monk to take some — the monk should not (immediately) accept what is being offered, but should first try to read the intent (and consent) of the other.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दुण्हं तु** (duṇhaṃ tu) = of two (persons)
- **भुंजमाणाणं** (bhuṃjamāṇāṇaṃ) = while eating (or jointly owning the food)
- **एगो** (ego) = one (person)
- **तत्थ णिमंतए** (tattha ṇimaṃtae) = invites (the monk) there
- **दिज्जमाणं** (dijjamāṇaṃ) = (the food) being given
- **ण इच्छेज्जा** (ṇa icchejjā) = should not (immediately) desire / accept
- **छंदं** (chaṃdaṃ) = the (other person's) wish / intent
- **से** (se) = his (the second person's)
- **पडिलेहए** (paḍilehae) = should examine, should observe

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the famous **anisrishta dosha** — the "without-co-owner's-consent" fault. When food is jointly owned (two brothers running a shop together; spouses sharing a household; partners in a business), the consent of *all* owners is required for the gift to be valid. If one offers but the other is silent or visibly reluctant, accepting the food violates the second owner's third great vow (non-stealing) by proxy — the monk would be receiving what was not freely given by all rightful owners. So the monk *first* observes the second person's facial expression, body language, eye-contact — to read whether there is genuine consent or hidden displeasure. Only if both clearly consent should the monk accept.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If two people share the food and only one invites the monk, the monk should pause and read the other person's expression — only if both seem willing should he accept.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something is offered to you, do you also notice the silent face of the second giver — or do you only hear the loud yes?

## SUTRA 5.1.38 — When Both Invite

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुण्हं तु भुंजमाणाणं, दोवि तत्थ णिमंतए ।
दिज्जमाणं पडिच्छिज्जा, जं तत्थेसणियं भवे ॥५.१.३८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.1.38 ||
duṇhaṃ tu bhuṃjamāṇāṇaṃ, dovi tattha ṇimaṃtae |
dijjamāṇaṃ paḍicchijjā, jaṃ tattheṣaṇiyaṃ bhave ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When two persons are eating (or jointly own the food), and both invite the monk — the monk should accept what is being offered, provided the food itself is faultless.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दुण्हं तु भुंजमाणाणं** (duṇhaṃ tu bhuṃjamāṇāṇaṃ) = of two who are eating (or jointly owning the food)
- **दोवि** (dovi) = both
- **तत्थ णिमंतए** (tattha ṇimaṃtae) = invite (the monk) there
- **दिज्जमाणं पडिच्छिज्जा** (dijjamāṇaṃ paḍicchijjā) = should accept what is being offered
- **जं तत्थेसणियं भवे** (jaṃ tattheṣaṇiyaṃ bhave) = if the food there is faultless

### 5. COMMENTARY

The constructive complement of the previous rule. When both joint owners freely consent and explicitly offer — the consent is complete, the third great vow is preserved, and the monk may accept (as long as the food itself meets all the other tests of faultlessness). This pair of verses (37-38) together teaches a deep principle about gifts: a gift requires the willing consent of *all* who own what is being given.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When two people own the food and both willingly invite the monk, he should accept — provided the food itself is faultless.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What gifts in your life have come with full consent — and what came with only half?
