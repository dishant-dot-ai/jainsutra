## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The ninth adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is named **Vinay Samadhi** — "the equipoise that arises from humility." It is divided into four uddeshas (sub-sections), each illuminating a different facet of the disciple's path: the first describes the disciple's bearing toward the Guru and the catastrophic consequences of disrespecting him; the second sets out the marks of the trained and untrained disciple, with the famous parable of the broken thorn; the third paints the portrait of the disciple worthy of universal worship; and the fourth completes the chapter with the four samadhis — vinaya, shruta, tapa, and achara — into which the entire monastic life is to be poured.

In the Jain tradition, **vinaya** is not the mere "humility" of bowed shoulders. The word means *vishishta naya* — distinguished conduct, the specific bearing fitting for one who has accepted a teacher's discipline. The Jnata Sutra (in the Thavachchaputra dialogue) defines the very root of the Jain dharma as vinaya: where other systems may be rooted in ritual, in faith, or in austerity, the Jain dharma is rooted in the disciple's right relationship with the Guru. Without vinaya there is no shruta (the scriptural learning will not stick); without shruta there is no charitra (right conduct); without charitra there is no moksha. Vinaya is therefore the very foundation of the building.

The Aupapatika Sutra distinguishes seven kinds of inner-vinaya — knowledge-vinaya, perception-vinaya, conduct-vinaya, mind-vinaya, speech-vinaya, body-vinaya, and *upachara-vinaya* (the vinaya of personal attendance on the teacher). It is this last one — upachara-vinaya — that is the central subject of this adhyayan. The other six are present, but folded inside the seventh: when a disciple bows before the Guru, fetches his alms-bowl, asks permission before sitting down, accepts correction without protest, and arranges his speech and silence around the Guru's wishes, *all* the kinds of vinaya are simultaneously practised in that one act.

A second note: the chapter is called *Vinay-Samadhi*, not merely *Vinaya*. The word **samadhi** here means inner peace, inner balance, the soul's natural ease. The chapter's central claim is that humility is not a heavy duty laid upon the disciple but the very *cause* of his deepest peace. The disciple who throws away vinaya throws away his own samadhi; the disciple who embraces vinaya enters samadhi without seeking it. The four sub-sections together form a single argument: *all* paths to inner peace — the path of humility, the path of scripture, the path of austerity, the path of conduct — converge on the disciple who has first learned to bow.

## UDDESHA 1 — Vinay Samadhi (The Disciple's Bearing Toward the Guru)

The first uddesha opens the chapter with a sober warning: the disciple who fails to learn vinaya — whether out of pride, anger, intoxication, or sloth — is like a bamboo that bears its own fruit only to die. Through eight progressive metaphors (bamboo, fire, snake, jumping into fire, breaking a mountain with one's head, waking a sleeping lion, striking a spear-tip, and the foolish despising of a small snake), the Sutrakara hammers home one teaching: disrespect of the Guru destroys the disciple, not the Guru. The uddesha closes with the disciple's positive duty — to revere the Guru as the Brahmin reveres his sacred fire, to bow with body, speech, and mind, and to recognise the Acharya as a great mine of qualities, comparable to the rising sun, the full moon, and Indra in the assembly of gods.

## SUTRA 9.1.1 — The Four Causes of Indiscipline

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

थंभा व कोहा व मयप्पमाया, गुरुस्सगासे विणयं ण सिक्खे ।
सो चेव उ तस्स अभूइभावो, फलं व कीयस्स वहाय होइ ॥९.१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.1 ||
thaṃbhā va kohā va mayappamāyā, gurussagāse viṇayaṃ ṇa sikkhe |
so ceva u tassa abhūibhāvo, phalaṃ va kīyassa vahāya hoi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He who, out of stiffness (pride), out of anger, out of intoxication (with status, learning, etc.), or out of sloth — does not learn vinaya in the presence of the Guru — that very (indiscipline) becomes the cause of his ruin (loss of qualities), just as the bamboo's fruit becomes the cause of its own destruction.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **थंभा** (thaṃbhā) = from stiffness — pride, mental hardness, the rigidity of one who cannot bend
- **कोहा** (kohā) = from anger
- **मयप्पमाया** (mayappamāyā) = from intoxication (mada — eight kinds: caste, family, strength, beauty, austerity, learning, fortune, power) and from heedlessness (pramada — sloth, carelessness)
- **गुरुस्सगासे** (gurussagāse) = in the presence of the Guru
- **विणयं** (viṇayaṃ) = vinaya, humility-discipline
- **ण सिक्खे** (ṇa sikkhe) = does not learn / does not practise
- **सो चेव** (so ceva) = that very (indiscipline)
- **तस्स** (tassa) = of him
- **अभूइभावो** (abhūibhāvo) = the destruction of his prosperity (abhuti = absence of vibhuti/wealth-of-qualities); ruin of his attainments
- **फलं व** (phalaṃ va) = like the fruit
- **कीयस्स** (kīyassa) = of the bamboo
- **वहाय होइ** (vahāya hoi) = becomes the cause of (its own) slaughter

### 5. COMMENTARY

The uddesha begins by naming the four enemies of vinaya: pride, anger, intoxication, and heedlessness. These are not abstractions but real psychological states that disciples bring to the Guru's feet. The proud disciple cannot bend; the angry disciple cannot listen; the intoxicated disciple — drunk on caste, family, scholarship, or austerities — does not feel he needs to learn; the heedless disciple simply forgets to be alert. Even one of these four is enough to keep vinaya from taking root.

The image given is shattering in its precision. The bamboo bears no fruit through most of its life, but when it finally fruits, that fruiting is the signal of its death — the bamboo dies after producing seed. So too the disciple who masters scripture and ritual without first mastering vinaya: his "fruit" — his learning, his austerity, his apparent attainment — becomes the very thing that kills his inner growth. The fruit is real; the fruit is impressive; but the fruit is fatal.

The Sutrakara is making a deep psychological point: it is not the *absence* of qualities that ruins a disciple but the *presence* of qualities held without humility. A disciple with little learning and great vinaya can still grow; a disciple with great learning and no vinaya is already finished, though he does not know it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If you bring stiffness, anger, ego-intoxication, or sloth to your teacher's feet, you will not learn humility — and without humility, every accomplishment you do gather will be like the bamboo's fruit: the very sign of your death.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the four — stiffness, anger, intoxication, sloth — most often prevents you from truly learning from someone wiser than you?

## SUTRA 9.1.2 — Disrespecting the Guru as "Dull" or "Young"

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे यावि मंदित्ति गुरुं विइत्ता, डहरे इमे अप्पसुए त्ति णच्चा ।
हीलंति मिच्छं पडिवज्जमाणा, करेंति आसायण ते गुरूणं ॥९.१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.2 ||
je yāvi maṃditti guruṃ viittā, ḍahare ime appasue tti ṇaccā |
hīlaṃti micchaṃ paḍivajjamāṇā, kareṃti āsāyaṇa te gurūṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those who, having decided "this Guru is dull (slow-witted)," or having concluded "this one is young, this one has little scriptural learning" — and so adopting a wrong (perverse) view, despise (the Guru) — they commit *ashatana* (transgression / dishonour) of the Gurus.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे यावि** (je yāvi) = whichever (disciples)
- **गुरुं** (guruṃ) = the Guru
- **मंदि त्ति विइत्ता** (maṃdi tti viittā) = having known (him) as "dull" / lacking intelligence
- **डहरे** (ḍahare) = young in age
- **इमे** (ime) = this one
- **अप्पसुए त्ति णच्चा** (appasue tti ṇaccā) = having decided "(he is) one with little scriptural knowledge"
- **मिच्छं पडिवज्जमाणा** (micchaṃ paḍivajjamāṇā) = adopting a wrong view, behaving contrary (to what is fitting)
- **हीलंति** (hīlaṃti) = they belittle, despise, mock
- **करेंति आसायण** (kareṃti āsāyaṇa) = they commit *ashatana* (offense, dishonour, contempt)
- **ते** (te) = they
- **गुरूणं** (gurūṇaṃ) = of the Gurus

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra catalogues the three favourite excuses by which a disciple talks himself out of vinaya. The first: "this Guru is dull — he doesn't really understand much." The second: "this Guru is young — younger than I am, in fact, so why should I bow?" The third: "this Guru has little scriptural learning — I have read more sutras than he has." Each of these is psychologically realistic; each is a way the disciple's inner pride finds a justification for disrespect.

The Sutrakara's response is uncompromising: regardless of whether such judgments are *true*, the disciple's act of disrespecting the Guru is *ashatana* — a culpable transgression. Ashatana literally means "dragging down" — the disciple tries to drag the Guru down in his own estimation and in others' estimation. But, as the next sutra will explain, the actual effect is to drag the disciple down, not the Guru.

The text also notes that an Acharya's seat is normally given to one of high learning and discipline — but in agama-permitted circumstances, when a more qualified candidate is unavailable, an Acharya may be installed for other reasons (auspicious birth, fortune, etc.). The disciple is not entitled to second-guess the lineage's appointment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who looks at his Guru and thinks "he is dull," "he is too young," or "he doesn't know enough scripture" — and on that basis disrespects him — is committing a serious offence, regardless of whether his judgment is correct.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever used a teacher's apparent shortcomings as your private permission to stop respecting them?

## SUTRA 9.1.3 — Even Sound Acharyas Are Burned by the Disciple's Disrespect

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पगईइ मंदा वि भवंति एगे, डहरा वि य जे सुयबुद्धोववेया ।
आयारमंता गुणसुट्ठिअप्पा, जे हीलिया सिहिरिव भास कुज्जा ॥९.१.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.3 ||
pagaīi maṃdā vi bhavaṃti ege, ḍaharā vi ya je suyabuddhovaveyā |
āyāramaṃtā guṇasuṭṭhiappā, je hīliyā sihiriva bhāsa kujjā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Some are by nature slow-witted (in worldly matters), and some, though young in age, are endowed with scripture and intelligence; (yet all such Acharyas are) endowed with right conduct and well-established in qualities. Such (Acharyas), if they are despised, will (cause the disciple's qualities to be reduced to ashes) like fire (reduces things to ash).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पगईइ** (pagaīi) = by nature, by inherent disposition
- **मंदा वि** (maṃdā vi) = even slow-witted (in some respects)
- **भवंति एगे** (bhavaṃti ege) = some happen to be
- **डहरा वि य** (ḍaharā vi ya) = and even though young
- **सुयबुद्धोववेया** (suyabuddhovaveyā) = endowed with shruta (scripture) and buddhi (intelligence)
- **आयारमंता** (āyāramaṃtā) = endowed with right conduct
- **गुणसुट्ठिअप्पा** (guṇasuṭṭhiappā) = whose self is well-established in qualities
- **जे हीलिया** (je hīliyā) = those, when despised
- **सिहि-रिव** (sihi-riva) = like fire
- **भास कुज्जा** (bhāsa kujjā) = make (the disciple) into ash

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the third of the opening trio. Sutra 1 named the four causes; Sutra 2 named the three excuses; this Sutra 3 reveals the consequence — through the unforgettable image of fire. Even an Acharya who is genuinely slow on certain worldly matters, or genuinely young in years, may nevertheless be deeply established in conduct and inner qualities. Such a one is a living fire. He is not malicious — fire harbours no malice — but to touch him with disrespect is to be burned.

The image is exact. Fire does not "intend" to burn; it burns by its very nature when touched wrongly. The Acharya does not "intend" the disciple's destruction; the disciple is destroyed by the very nature of the offense he has chosen to commit. The disciple's qualities — knowledge, perception, character, austerity — are the fuel; the disrespect is the spark; the loss is total.

The traditional gloss adds that what is consumed is not the Guru's standing but the disciple's *inner accumulation*. Ashatana of the Guru burns away the karma-merit accrued through years of practice. Many disciples, the commentators warn, complete their entire monastic career and find at the end that nothing remains, because at some point they had silently scorned the Guru in their hearts and never repaired the breach.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Some Acharyas may genuinely be unschooled in some worldly matter, and some may be very young — but if their conduct is sound and their inner life is steady, they are like fire. Disrespect them, and your own attainments turn to ash — not because they wished it, but because that is simply what fire does.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If respect were a fire that warmed you, and disrespect were a fire that burned you — would you change anything about how you carry yourself before your teachers?

## SUTRA 9.1.4 — The Snake-Disturber

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे यावि णागं डहरंति णच्चा, आसायए से अहियाय होइ ।
एवायरियं पि हु हीलयंतो, णियच्छइ जाइपहं खु मंदो ॥९.१.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.4 ||
je yāvi ṇāgaṃ ḍaharaṃti ṇaccā, āsāyae se ahiyāya hoi |
evāyariyaṃ pi hu hīlayaṃto, ṇiyacchai jāipahaṃ khu maṃdo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whoever, having decided "this snake is small (a mere baby)," torments it — that becomes for his own harm. Just so, the foolish (disciple) who despises an Acharya enters the path of (repeated) births (in lower realms — one-sensed beings, etc.).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे यावि** (je yāvi) = whoever (an ignorant person)
- **णागं** (ṇāgaṃ) = a snake
- **डहरंति णच्चा** (ḍaharaṃti ṇaccā) = having decided "(it is) small / young"
- **आसायए** (āsāyae) = torments / mistreats / pokes
- **से अहियाय होइ** (se ahiyāya hoi) = becomes (the cause) of harm to him (the tormentor)
- **एवायरियं पि हु** (evāyariyaṃ pi hu) = thus, an Acharya as well
- **हीलयंतो** (hīlayaṃto) = (the disciple) despising (him)
- **मंदो** (maṃdo) = the foolish, undisciplined one
- **णियच्छइ** (ṇiyacchai) = enters / arrives at
- **जाइपहं खु** (jāipahaṃ khu) = the path of (repeated) births — the cycle of taking lower births (one-sensed beings, etc.)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The image now intensifies. A foolish person sees a small snake and, judging by its size, decides it is harmless and pokes it. The result is no different from poking a large snake — the venom is in the small snake just as much as in the large. The harm comes back on the tormentor, not on the snake.

The Sutrakara applies this directly: a disciple who looks at an Acharya and judges by external smallness — small in age, small in worldly learning, small in physical stature, small in fame — and on that basis disrespects him, has done exactly what the snake-poker has done. The Acharya's spiritual venom is real even if his worldly stature is small. The disciple who provokes that venom is the one who suffers.

The consequence is named: *jaipaha* — the path of (lower) births. The disciple who disrespects the Acharya falls, life after life, into one-sensed bodies (plants, earth-bodied beings, water-bodied beings). The cumulative damage of a single ashatana, repeated in mind through a lifetime, can postpone liberation by countless cycles.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A fool sees a small snake and thinks it cannot bite — but the venom does not depend on the snake's size, and the fool ends up harmed. So too, a disciple who looks at an Acharya and judges him "small" by some outer measure, and on that basis disrespects him, falls into endless lower births.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you measure someone by their outward smallness, what are you blind to that you would never knowingly provoke?

## SUTRA 9.1.5 — Even an Angered Cobra Cannot Match an Angered Acharya's Loss

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आसीविसो यावि परं सुरुट्ठो, किं जीवणासाओ परं णु कुज्जा ।
आयरियपाया पुण अप्पसण्णा, अबोहि आसायण णत्थि मोक्खो ॥९.१.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.5 ||
āsīviso yāvi paraṃ suruṭṭho, kiṃ jīvaṇāsāo paraṃ ṇu kujjā |
āyariyapāyā puṇa appasaṇṇā, abohi āsāyaṇa ṇatthi mokkho ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Even a poison-fanged cobra, when extremely enraged — what more can it do beyond the destruction of (one) life? But the venerable feet of the Acharyas, when displeased — (the result is) loss of bodhi (right faith), the offence of *ashatana*, and there is no liberation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आसीविसो यावि** (āsīviso yāvi) = even a venomous cobra (āśīviṣa = one with poison in its fang)
- **परं सुरुट्ठो** (paraṃ suruṭṭho) = extremely enraged
- **किं जीवणासाओ परं णु कुज्जा** (kiṃ jīvaṇāsāo paraṃ ṇu kujjā) = what more can it do beyond destroying (one) life?
- **आयरियपाया** (āyariyapāyā) = the venerable feet of the Acharyas (a respectful idiom for "the Acharyas themselves")
- **पुण** (puṇa) = however, on the other hand
- **अप्पसण्णा** (appasaṇṇā) = displeased, made unhappy
- **अबोहि** (abohi) = absence of bodhi (right faith); attainment of mithyatva (wrong view)
- **आसायण** (āsāyaṇa) = ashatana (the karmic record of the offense)
- **णत्थि मोक्खो** (ṇatthi mokkho) = there is no liberation

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now sharpens the comparison with mathematical precision. A venomous cobra, fully enraged, can only do *one* thing: destroy this one body. After that, the soul moves on. The damage is bounded — terrible, but bounded.

But an Acharya, made displeased by the disciple's ashatana, costs the disciple something far worse than the body: the disciple loses *bodhi* — the very capacity to receive right faith — and falls into mithyatva (the false world-view). And once mithyatva is taken on, liberation becomes impossible until that view is shed, which may take innumerable lives. So a snakebite costs one life; the Guru's displeasure costs the path itself.

The phrase **abohi-asayan natthi mokkho** has been read in several ways by the commentators: (1) "by abodhi-ashatana there is no liberation" — i.e., the karma generated by ashatana causes the disciple to lose right faith and so cuts off liberation; (2) "as long as the abodhi (caused by ashatana) is not exhausted, there is no moksha" — i.e., the karma must be exhausted before liberation can again become possible. Either reading preserves the Sutrakara's point.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A cobra at its angriest can only kill your body once. But if you make an Acharya unhappy, you lose your right-faith itself, you carry the ashatana karma, and the door of liberation closes — which is far worse than dying.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If the worst a snake could do was take one life, but the worst your own pride could do was take liberation itself — which would you guard against more carefully?

## SUTRA 9.1.6 — Three Foolish Acts Compared to Disrespecting the Guru

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो पावगं जलियमवक्कमिज्जा, आसीवीसं वावि हु कोवइज्जा ।
जो वा विसं खायइ जीवियट्ठी, एसोवमासायणया गुरुणं ॥९.१.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.6 ||
jo pāvagaṃ jaliyamavakkamijjā, āsīvīsaṃ vāvi hu kovaijjā |
jo vā visaṃ khāyai jīviyaṭṭhī, esovamāsāyaṇayā guruṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He who would step into a blazing fire, or who would enrage a poison-fanged cobra, or who, wanting to live, would yet eat poison — *this* is the comparison for the *ashatana* of the Gurus.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जो पावगं जलियं अवक्कमिज्जा** (jo pāvagaṃ jaliyaṃ avakkamijjā) = he who would step (forward) into a blazing fire
- **आसीवीसं वावि हु कोवइज्जा** (āsīvīsaṃ vāvi hu kovaijjā) = or who would (deliberately) enrage a poison-fanged cobra
- **जो वा विसं खायइ जीवियट्ठी** (jo vā visaṃ khāyai jīviyaṭṭhī) = or who, desirous of life, would yet eat (deadly) poison
- **एसोवमा** (esovamā) = this is the comparison / this is the simile
- **आसायणया गुरुणं** (āsāyaṇayā guruṇaṃ) = for the ashatana of the Gurus

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now stacks three overlapping images together. To disrespect the Guru is, simultaneously, like (1) stepping into a blazing fire, (2) provoking a venomous cobra, and (3) consuming poison while wanting to live. Each is an act of self-destruction performed by a person who imagines himself in control. Each guarantees the actor's harm. None can be undone.

The triple image is meant to overwhelm any rationalisation the disciple might be constructing in his mind. "Maybe just this once" — no, fire does not negotiate. "Maybe a small disrespect won't matter" — no, the cobra's fang does not measure your intent. "Maybe I can manage the consequences" — no, the poison reaches the heart. The disciple who disrespects the Guru is, in every direction at once, working against his own life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

To disrespect your Guru is exactly like walking into a fire, poking an angry cobra, or eating poison while still wanting to live — three impossibilities of self-care, all collapsed into one foolish act.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in your life are you treating like fire — knowing it will burn you, but stepping closer anyway?

## SUTRA 9.1.7 — Even If the Three Failed, Disrespecting the Guru Still Has No Escape

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिया हु से पावय णो डहेज्जा, आसीविसो वा कुविओ ण भक्खे ।
सिया विसं हालहलं ण मारे, ण यावि मोक्खो गुरुहीलणाए ॥९.१.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.7 ||
siyā hu se pāvaya ṇo ḍahejjā, āsīviso vā kuvio ṇa bhakkhe |
siyā visaṃ hālahalaṃ ṇa māre, ṇa yāvi mokkho guruhīlaṇāe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It might happen that the (raging) fire would not burn (him); the enraged poison-fanged cobra might not eat (kill) him; the deadly halahala poison might not kill (him) — but there is no release (no escape) from (the karma generated by) the disrespect of the Guru.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया हु** (siyā hu) = it is possible that, perhaps
- **से पावय णो डहेज्जा** (se pāvaya ṇo ḍahejjā) = the (terrible) fire might not burn him
- **आसीविसो वा कुविओ ण भक्खे** (āsīviso vā kuvio ṇa bhakkhe) = or the enraged poison-fanged cobra might not eat (him)
- **सिया विसं हालहलं ण मारे** (siyā visaṃ hālahalaṃ ṇa māre) = the deadly *halahala* poison might (somehow) not kill (him)
- **ण यावि मोक्खो** (ṇa yāvi mokkho) = but there is never release / liberation
- **गुरुहीलणाए** (guruhīlaṇāe) = from the disrespect of the Guru

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now anticipates a clever objection. "What if I survive the fire — protected by mantra? What if the cobra refuses to bite — paralysed by some charm? What if the poison fails to kill — neutralised by some antidote? Then perhaps disrespecting the Guru could also pass without consequence."

The reply is decisive: yes, it is *possible* that fire, snake, or poison may fail to do its full work — by some unusual circumstance, mantra, or accident. But there is *no* circumstance in which ashatana of the Guru fails to bind its karma. The karmic mechanism of ashatana is more reliable than the physical danger of fire or poison. Where the world's worst poisons may, by chance, spare a victim, ashatana never spares.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

It is at least possible that fire might not burn you, that an angry cobra might not bite you, that even halahala poison might fail to kill you — but it is *never* possible to escape the karmic cost of disrespecting your Guru. There is no antidote, no chance of survival.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The world's worst dangers sometimes spare the foolish. Why does this danger never spare anyone?

## SUTRA 9.1.8 — Three More Foolish Comparisons

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो पव्वयं सिरसा भित्तुमिच्छे, सुत्तं व सीहं पडिबोहएज्जा ।
जो वा दए सत्तिअग्गे पहारं, एसोवमासायणया गुरुणं ॥९.१.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.8 ||
jo pavvayaṃ sirasā bhittumicche, suttaṃ va sīhaṃ paḍibohaejjā |
jo vā dae sattiagge pahāraṃ, esovamāsāyaṇayā guruṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He who would wish to break a mountain with his head, or who would (try to) wake a sleeping lion, or who would strike (his hand or foot) on the point of a spear — *this* is the comparison for the *ashatana* of the Gurus.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जो पव्वयं सिरसा भित्तुं इच्छे** (jo pavvayaṃ sirasā bhittuṃ icche) = he who would wish to break a mountain with (his) head
- **सुत्तं व सीहं पडिबोहएज्जा** (suttaṃ va sīhaṃ paḍibohaejjā) = or would awaken a sleeping lion
- **जो वा दए सत्ति-अग्गे पहारं** (jo vā dae satti-agge pahāraṃ) = or who would strike (his hand/foot) on the tip of a spear
- **एसोवमा आसायणया गुरुणं** (esovamā āsāyaṇayā guruṇaṃ) = this is the comparison for the ashatana of the Gurus

### 5. COMMENTARY

A second triple-image, parallel to Sutra 6 but escalating. There the three were fire-cobra-poison; here the three are mountain-lion-spear. The earlier triple was about *external* dangers (heat, venom, poison) entering the body. This new triple is about the *foolishness* of the act itself: a person trying to break a mountain with his head succeeds only in breaking his own head; a person waking a sleeping lion only invites his own death; a person striking the spear-point only impales his own hand.

The pattern is significant. Disrespecting the Guru is not "an attack on the Guru that backfires" — it is a sheer act of self-mutilation in which the Guru is not even diminished. The mountain stands; the lion was sleeping peacefully; the spear was simply lying there. The Guru, likewise, is undisturbed by the disciple's disrespect. It is the disciple alone who is broken, eaten, or pierced.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

To disrespect your Guru is like trying to break a mountain with your forehead, like waking a sleeping lion, like jabbing your hand on the tip of a spear — three perfect ways to destroy yourself while leaving the other party untouched.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you "strike out" against a teacher in your mind, who is actually being broken in that moment?

## SUTRA 9.1.9 — Even If the Three Failed, the Guru's Disrespect Still Cannot Be Survived

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिया हु सीसेण गिरिं पि भिंदे, सिया हु सीहो कुविओ ण भक्खे ।
सिया ण भिंदेज्ज व सत्तिअग्गं, ण यावि मोक्खो गुरुहीलणाए ॥९.१.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.9 ||
siyā hu sīseṇa giriṃ pi bhiṃde, siyā hu sīho kuvio ṇa bhakkhe |
siyā ṇa bhiṃdejja va sattiaggaṃ, ṇa yāvi mokkho guruhīlaṇāe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It might happen that one could break a mountain with his head; it might happen that an enraged lion would not eat (him); it might happen that the spear-tip would not pierce (him) — but there is no release from the disrespect of the Guru.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया हु** (siyā hu) = it is possible that, perhaps
- **सीसेण गिरिं पि भिंदे** (sīseṇa giriṃ pi bhiṃde) = (one) might (somehow) break a mountain with the head
- **सिया हु सीहो कुविओ ण भक्खे** (siyā hu sīho kuvio ṇa bhakkhe) = the enraged lion might not eat (him)
- **सिया ण भिंदेज्ज व सत्तिअग्गं** (siyā ṇa bhiṃdejja va sattiaggaṃ) = the spear-tip might not pierce (him)
- **ण यावि मोक्खो गुरुहीलणाए** (ṇa yāvi mokkho guruhīlaṇāe) = but there is never release from the disrespect of the Guru

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the structural twin of Sutra 7 — the second "even if" stanza. By divine fortune, the impossible may sometimes happen: the mountain may yield, the lion may stay calm, the spear-tip may turn aside. But by no fortune at all can the karma of disrespecting the Guru be evaded.

The Sutrakara is making one last attempt to seal off every route by which the disciple might rationalise his disrespect. Whatever escape the world offers from physical danger, no equivalent escape is offered from this karmic danger. The lesson is to be *more* careful around the Guru than one would be around fire, snake, poison, mountains, lions, or spears — because all of those have at least a small statistical chance of sparing you, and ashatana of the Guru has none.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even if you somehow broke the mountain with your head, even if the lion stayed asleep, even if the spear refused to pierce — there is still no escape from the karma of disrespecting your Guru. That single danger has no exception.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it tell you about the weight of vinaya, that the Sutrakara ranks Guru-disrespect as more inescapable than fire, lion, snake, mountain, and spear combined?

## SUTRA 9.1.10 — The Disciple's Conclusion: Stay Pleasing to the Guru

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयरियपाया पुण अप्पसण्णा, अबोहि आसायण णत्थि मोक्खो ।
तम्हा अणाबाहसुहाभिकंखी, गुरुप्पसायाभिमुहो रमेज्जा ॥९.१.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.10 ||
āyariyapāyā puṇa appasaṇṇā, abohi āsāyaṇa ṇatthi mokkho |
tamhā aṇābāhasuhābhikaṃkhī, guruppasāyābhimuho ramejjā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Indeed, when the venerable feet of the Acharyas are displeased, there is loss of bodhi, the offence of ashatana, and no liberation. Therefore, the (disciple) who longs for unobstructed bliss should sport (engage himself) facing the Guru's grace (with vinaya, samyama, tapa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयरियपाया पुण अप्पसण्णा** (āyariyapāyā puṇa appasaṇṇā) = when, on the other hand, the Acharyas are displeased
- **अबोहि आसायण णत्थि मोक्खो** (abohi āsāyaṇa ṇatthi mokkho) = (the result is) abodhi, ashatana, no liberation
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **अणाबाह-सुह-अभिकंखी** (aṇābāha-suha-abhikaṃkhī) = the seeker of unobstructed bliss (anabadha-sukha = the bliss that knows no interruption, i.e., moksha)
- **गुरुप्पसाय-अभिमुहो** (guruppasāya-abhimuho) = facing toward the Guru's grace (prasada)
- **रमेज्जा** (ramejjā) = should engage / should rest / should remain absorbed (in vinaya, samyama, tapa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eight images now resolve into a single positive instruction. The disciple, having been shown what disrespect destroys, is finally told what to *do*: orient yourself toward the Guru's grace and remain there. The word **abhimukho** is precise — it means "facing toward." The disciple is to live with his face turned toward the Guru's pleasure, the way a flower turns toward the sun.

The motivation given is **anabadha-sukha** — the unobstructed bliss of liberation. This is the only bliss worth seeking, because all worldly bliss is sooner or later obstructed by something (illness, age, separation, death). Liberation alone is the bliss that nothing can interrupt. And the only road to it, the Sutrakara is saying, runs through the Guru's grace.

The verb **ramejjā** is also significant. It does not say "merely obey the Guru" — it says "play in / rest in / take delight in" the Guru's grace. Vinaya is not a duty held against one's will but a place of inner play, where the disciple finds his joy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the Guru is displeased, the disciple loses everything — right faith, karmic merit, and liberation itself. So the disciple who wants the unbroken bliss of liberation should keep his face turned toward the Guru's grace and rest there happily.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If "facing toward the Guru's grace" was not a duty but a place of play, how would your way of being in front of a teacher change?

## SUTRA 9.1.11 — Like the Brahmin to His Sacred Fire

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहाहिअग्गी जलणं णमंसे, णाणाहुईमंतपयाभिसित्तं ।
एवायरियं उवचिट्ठएज्जा, अणंतणाणोवगओ वि संतो ॥९.१.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.11 ||
jahāhiaggī jalaṇaṃ ṇamaṃse, ṇāṇāhuīmaṃtapayābhisittaṃ |
evāyariyaṃ uvaciṭṭhaejjā, aṇaṃtaṇāṇovagao vi saṃto ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as a Brahmin who maintains the sacred fire (āhitāgni) bows to the fire that is consecrated by various oblations and mantra-syllables — even so should (the disciple) attend upon the Acharya, even though (he himself may have already) attained infinite knowledge.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जहा** (jahā) = just as
- **आहिअग्गी** (āhiaggī) = the Brahmin who has installed the sacred fire (Agnihotri)
- **जलणं णमंसे** (jalaṇaṃ ṇamaṃse) = bows to the fire
- **णाणाहुई-मंत-पयाभिसित्तं** (ṇāṇāhuī-maṃta-payābhisittaṃ) = (the fire) consecrated by various oblations and mantra-padas
- **एवं** (evaṃ) = thus
- **आयरियं उवचिट्ठएज्जा** (āyariyaṃ uvaciṭṭhaejjā) = (the disciple) should serve / attend upon the Acharya
- **अणंतणाणोवगओ वि संतो** (aṇaṃtaṇāṇovagao vi saṃto) = even though he may have come to (be possessed of) infinite knowledge

### 5. COMMENTARY

After ten sutras of warnings, the Sutrakara turns to a positive image. The Agnihotri Brahmin is one who has formally installed a sacred fire in his home and who, lifelong, makes oblations into it, recites mantras over it, and bows down before it as the visible god. He does this not because the fire has done him a personal favour, but because he has accepted the fire as his deity.

In just this way, the Sutrakara says, a disciple should attend upon the Acharya. The remarkable detail is in the phrase "**even though he may have come to infinite knowledge**." This is a startling concession. Even if the disciple were somehow to attain kevalajnana (omniscience) before the Guru — even then, he should continue to bow, to serve, to attend. The vinaya is not a contractual exchange ("I bow because I am still ignorant") but an unconditional posture ("I bow because the Guru's relationship to me established my entire path").

The point is profound: vinaya is not lost when knowledge is gained. The Guru-disciple relationship is not a temporary scaffolding to be discarded once the building is up. It is the permanent ground of the disciple's gratitude.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The Brahmin bows to the sacred fire he has consecrated, again and again, all his life — and even if a disciple were to gain infinite knowledge, he should still attend upon his Acharya in just the same way. Vinaya is not paid off by enlightenment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If gaining everything you ever sought from a teacher would not lessen your debt to them, what does that say about the nature of true vinaya?

## SUTRA 9.1.12 — How to Honour the Teacher: Body, Speech, Mind

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जस्संतिए धम्मपयाइं सिक्खे, तस्संतिए वेणइयं पउंजे ।
सक्कारए सिरसा पंजलीओ, कायगिरा भो मणसा य णिच्चं ॥९.१.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.12 ||
jassaṃtie dhammapayāiṃ sikkhe, tassaṃtie veṇaiyaṃ pauṃje |
sakkārae sirasā paṃjalīo, kāyagirā bho maṇasā ya ṇiccaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the presence of the (teacher) from whom one has learned the words of dharma, one should always practise vinaya. (One should) honour (him) with the head (bowed) and folded palms — by body, by speech, O (disciple)!, and by mind — always.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जस्संतिए** (jassaṃtie) = in whose presence / from whom
- **धम्मपयाइं** (dhammapayāiṃ) = the words / verses / sentences of dharma
- **सिक्खे** (sikkhe) = (one) learns / has learned
- **तस्संतिए** (tassaṃtie) = in his presence
- **वेणइयं पउंजे** (veṇaiyaṃ pauṃje) = should employ vinaya (the action proper to vinaya)
- **सक्कारए** (sakkārae) = should honour
- **सिरसा पंजलीओ** (sirasā paṃjalīo) = with the head (bowed) and folded palms
- **कायगिरा** (kāyagirā) = by body and by speech
- **भो** (bho) = O (vocative — addressed to the disciple)
- **मणसा य णिच्चं** (maṇasā ya ṇiccaṃ) = and by mind, always

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra makes vinaya practical. Vinaya is not merely an inner attitude; it must be expressed through three channels — body, speech, and mind. The body bows, joins the palms, and presses the forehead to the ground in five-fold prostration (panchanga-vandana). The speech utters words of acceptance (**tahatti** — "so be it"), of greeting (**matthayena vandami** — "I bow to you with my head"), of praise. The mind holds steady, unwavering inner faith, and recognises the Guru as the highest object of veneration.

The Sutrakara emphasises **niccham** — "always." The disciple's vinaya is not for special occasions (the formal lecture, the ceremonial visit) but for every encounter, every word, every glance. Vinaya as an episode of behaviour is shallow; vinaya as the disciple's permanent posture is what the Sutrakara is asking for.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Whoever taught you the words of dharma — always practise vinaya in their presence. Bow your head, fold your palms, honour them with your body, your speech, and your mind — every day, every moment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In the presence of the person from whom you have learned the most about life, what does your body say? Your speech? Your mind?

## SUTRA 9.1.13 — The Disciple's Vow: I Will Always Worship the Teachers Who Always Teach Me

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लज्जा दया संजम बंभचेरं, कल्लाणभागिस्स विसोहिठाणं ।
जे मे गुरू सययमणुसासयंति, ते हं गुरू सययं पूययामि ॥९.१.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.13 ||
lajjā dayā saṃjama baṃbhaceraṃ, kallāṇabhāgissa visohiṭhāṇaṃ |
je me gurū sayayamaṇusāsayaṃti, te haṃ gurū sayayaṃ pūyayāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For one who shares in welfare (one bound for liberation), the places of (inner) purification are: (the sense of) shame (at sin), compassion, self-restraint, and brahmacharya. (Therefore I declare:) those Gurus who continually instruct me — those Gurus I continually worship.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लज्जा** (lajjā) = (the inner) shame at committing sin / fear of transgression
- **दया** (dayā) = compassion (toward all beings)
- **संजम** (saṃjama) = self-restraint (samyama — protection of the six classes of jivas)
- **बंभचेरं** (baṃbhaceraṃ) = brahmacharya (celibacy, the discipline of all 18,000 sheelangas)
- **कल्लाणभागिस्स** (kallāṇabhāgissa) = of the one who is destined for / shares in *kalyana* (welfare, liberation)
- **विसोहिठाणं** (visohiṭhāṇaṃ) = the place / station of inner purification
- **जे मे गुरू** (je me gurū) = whichever Gurus
- **सययं अणुसासयंति** (sayayaṃ aṇusāsayaṃti) = continually instruct (me)
- **ते हं गुरू** (te haṃ gurū) = those Gurus, I
- **सययं पूययामि** (sayayaṃ pūyayāmi) = continually worship

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the disciple's own first-person declaration — and it is unique in the chapter for being spoken in the voice of the disciple himself, not the Sutrakara. The disciple looks at his life and sees that the four highest spiritual qualities — *lajja* (the shame that keeps one away from sin), *daya* (compassion that prevents harm), *samyama* (self-restraint that protects all beings), and *brahmacharya* (the celibacy that establishes the soul in its own nature) — are all the *fruit* of the Guru's continual teaching.

Without the Guru's continual reminder, lajja withers; without the Guru's example, daya hardens; without the Guru's discipline, samyama loosens; without the Guru's vigilance, brahmacharya falters. The disciple sees this clearly and concludes: the Gurus are the *cause* of every good thing in me. Therefore — I will worship them continually, just as continually as they teach me.

The Sutrakara has put this declaration into the disciple's own mouth deliberately. The earlier sutras showed that disrespect of the Guru is impossible; this sutra shows that respect of the Guru is also *natural* — it arises spontaneously the moment the disciple sees his own development clearly. Vinaya, in the end, is not a command; it is a recognition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For the disciple destined for liberation, the four pillars of inner purification are shame at sin, compassion, self-restraint, and brahmacharya — and all four come from the Guru's continual instruction. So the disciple says: "Those who continually teach me — them I continually worship."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Make a list of the qualities you most value in yourself today. How many of them came from someone who continually instructed you?

## SUTRA 9.1.14 — The Acharya as the Rising Sun

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहा णिसंते तवणच्चिमाली, पभासइ केवलं भारहं तु ।
एवायरिओ सुयसीलबुद्धिए, विरायइ सुरमज्झे व इंदो ॥९.१.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.14 ||
jahā ṇisaṃte tavaṇaccimālī, pabhāsai kevalaṃ bhārahaṃ tu |
evāyario suyasīlabuddhie, virāyai suramajjhe va iṃdo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as, at the end of night, the sun garlanded with hot rays illuminates the entire Bharata-kshetra — even so the Acharya, by (his) shruta, sheela, and buddhi, shines (in the assembly of monks) like Indra in the midst of the gods.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जहा** (jahā) = just as
- **णिसंते** (ṇisaṃte) = at the end of the night
- **तवण-अच्चि-माली** (tavaṇa-acci-mālī) = (the sun) garlanded with hot/burning rays
- **पभासइ** (pabhāsai) = illuminates
- **केवलं भारहं तु** (kevalaṃ bhārahaṃ tu) = the entire Bharata-kshetra
- **एवं आयरिओ** (evaṃ āyario) = even so the Acharya
- **सुय-सील-बुद्धिए** (suya-sīla-buddhie) = by (his) scriptural knowledge, conduct, and intelligence
- **विरायइ** (virāyai) = shines (resplendently)
- **सुरमज्झे व इंदो** (suramajjhe va iṃdo) = like Indra in the midst of the gods

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter now turns from the disciple's response to the Acharya's grandeur. Three similes follow in three sutras (14, 15, 16), each illustrating the Acharya's stature.

The first simile is the *sun*. Just as the sun, rising at the end of night, illuminates the whole Bharata-kshetra, awakening sleepers and stirring them to their work, so the Acharya — endowed with shruta (scriptural learning), sheela (conduct), and buddhi (intelligence) — illuminates the realm of jivas and ajivas, awakens the disciples from the sleep of ignorance, and stirs them to the work of self-purification. The sun does not merely give light; it gives the energy of the day. The Acharya does not merely give knowledge; he gives the energy by which the disciple's whole life is set in motion.

The second image embedded in the same gatha — Indra among the gods — emphasises the Acharya's *eminence*. As Indra is supreme in the assembly of devas, so the Acharya is supreme in the assembly of monks, surrounded by his disciples as Indra by the gods.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

As the rising sun lights up the entire land at dawn, so the Acharya, with his scripture, conduct, and intelligence, shines among the monks like Indra in the assembly of the gods.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a true teacher enters a room, what kind of light enters with him?

## SUTRA 9.1.15 — The Acharya as the Full Moon

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहा ससी कोमुइजोगजुत्तो, णक्खत्ततारागणपरिवुड्डप्पा ।
खे सोहइ विमले अब्भमुक्के, एवं गणी सोहइ भिक्खुमज्झे ॥९.१.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.15 ||
jahā sasī komuijogajutto, ṇakkhattatārāgaṇaparivuḍḍappā |
khe sohai vimale abbhamukke, evaṃ gaṇī sohai bhikkhumajjhe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as the moon, joined with the (light of the autumn) Kaumudi (full-moon night), surrounded by hosts of constellations and stars, shines in the pure cloud-free sky — even so the Gani (head of the gana, the Acharya) shines in the midst of the bhikshus (monks).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जहा** (jahā) = just as
- **ससी** (sasī) = the moon
- **कोमुइ-जोगजुत्तो** (komui-jogajutto) = joined with the brilliance of the autumn full-moon night (Kaumudi = Kartik purnima)
- **णक्खत्त-तारा-गण-परिवुड्डप्पा** (ṇakkhatta-tārā-gaṇa-parivuḍḍappā) = surrounded by hosts of constellations and stars
- **खे** (khe) = in the sky
- **विमले अब्भमुक्के** (vimale abbhamukke) = pure (and) free of clouds
- **सोहइ** (sohai) = shines
- **एवं गणी** (evaṃ gaṇī) = thus the Gani (the leader of the monastic gana)
- **सोहइ भिक्खुमज्झे** (sohai bhikkhumajjhe) = shines in the midst of the monks

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second simile balances the first. Where the sun was *fierce* — burning rays, scorching heat — the moon is *cool*. Where the sun's energy is *bhima* (terrifying), the moon's energy is *kanta* (gentle). The Sutrakara's commentators explain that the Acharya combines both qualities: bhima, by which he disciplines the unruly with stern instruction; kanta, by which he comforts the suffering disciple drawn from the burning forest of samsara.

The image is precise. The autumn full moon shines in a cloudless sky, encircled by constellations and stars. So the Acharya, encircled by his disciples (the constellations) and the lay community (the stars), shines in a "cloudless sky" — meaning a community free from the obscuring clouds of dispute, indiscipline, and impure conduct. The cool light of the moon awakens the night-blooming lotuses and gladdens the heart; the Acharya's cool wisdom awakens the dormant qualities of his disciples and gladdens the hearts of all who behold him.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as the autumn full moon, surrounded by stars and constellations, shines in a cloudless sky, the Acharya shines in the midst of his community of monks — cool, gentle, encircled by those who reflect his light.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A true teacher is both a sun (fierce when needed) and a moon (gentle when needed). Which do you find harder to receive?

## SUTRA 9.1.16 — The Acharya as a Mine of Qualities

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

महागरा आयरिया महेसी, समाहिजोगे सुयसीलबुद्धिए ।
संपाविउं कामे अणुत्तराइं, आराहए तोसइ धम्मकामी ॥९.१.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.16 ||
mahāgarā āyariyā mahesī, samāhijoge suyasīlabuddhie |
saṃpāviuṃ kāme aṇuttarāiṃ, ārāhae tosai dhammakāmī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The Acharyas are the great Maharshis, great mines (akara) of samadhi-yoga, of shruta, of sheela, and of buddhi. The seeker of dharma, desirous of obtaining the unsurpassed (jewels of knowledge etc., the bliss of liberation), should worship (the Acharyas) and (by his vinaya-conduct) bring them satisfaction.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **महागरा** (mahāgarā) = great mines (akara — a mine, an inexhaustible source)
- **आयरिया महेसी** (āyariyā mahesī) = the Acharyas, the great Rishis (Maharshi)
- **समाहिजोगे सुयसीलबुद्धिए** (samāhijoge suyasīlabuddhie) = (mines) of samadhi-yoga, shruta, sheela, and buddhi
- **संपाविउं कामे अणुत्तराइं** (saṃpāviuṃ kāme aṇuttarāiṃ) = (the disciple) desirous of obtaining the unsurpassed (knowledge-jewels, anuttara-sukha = the bliss of moksha)
- **आराहए** (ārāhae) = should worship / serve
- **तोसइ** (tosai) = should bring (the Acharya) to satisfaction
- **धम्मकामी** (dhammakāmī) = the seeker of dharma

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third simile is no longer celestial but geological. The Acharya is a *mine* — an inexhaustible source — of four treasures: samadhi-yoga (a mind, speech, and body settled in samadhi), shruta (scriptural mastery), sheela (conduct of the eighteen thousand sheelangas), and buddhi (the four kinds of intelligence). A mine is not used up by one or two visitors; it gives, and gives, and gives.

The sutra's instruction is two-fold. The disciple must (1) **arahae** — worship, fully execute the Acharya's instructions; and (2) **tosai** — bring the Acharya to inner satisfaction by his vinaya-conduct. It is not enough to outwardly perform the rituals of respect; the Acharya must actually be made happy by the disciple's growth and bearing. A disciple whose conduct merely satisfies the letter of the rule but does not bring his Guru *joy* has not completed the work of vinaya.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The Acharyas are great Maharshis — inexhaustible mines of samadhi, scripture, conduct, and intelligence. The seeker who wants the unsurpassed jewels of liberation should worship them and live in such a way that they are actually pleased.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

To "satisfy" your teacher — not just to obey, but to actually bring them inner happiness by what you have become — what would you have to change?

## SUTRA 9.1.17 — The Closing Vow: Hear, Ponder, Serve, Attain

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुच्चाण मेहावि सुभासियाइं, सुस्सूसए आयरियप्पमत्तो ।
आराहइत्ताण गुणे अणेगे, से पावइ सिद्धिमणुत्तरं ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥९.१.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.1.17 ||
succāṇa mehāvi subhāsiyāiṃ, sussūsae āyariyappamatto |
ārāhaittāṇa guṇe aṇege, se pāvai siddhimaṇuttaraṃ || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having heard the well-spoken (instructions), the wise one, vigilant and undistracted, should serve the Acharya. Having worshipped (cultivated) many qualities, he attains the unsurpassed *siddhi* (perfection — liberation). — Thus do I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुच्चाण** (succāṇa) = having heard
- **मेहावि** (mehāvi) = the wise / intelligent (disciple)
- **सुभासियाइं** (subhāsiyāiṃ) = the well-spoken (instructions, subhashita)
- **सुस्सूसए** (sussūsae) = should serve / wait upon (the verb of *shushrusha*)
- **आयरिय** (āyariya) = the Acharya
- **अप्पमत्तो** (appamatto) = vigilant, undistracted, free from heedlessness (apramatta)
- **आराहइत्ताण गुणे अणेगे** (ārāhaittāṇa guṇe aṇege) = having worshipped / cultivated the many qualities (samyag-jnana, samyag-darshana, samyag-charitra, tapa, vinaya, etc.)
- **से पावइ** (se pāvai) = he attains
- **सिद्धिं अणुत्तरं** (siddhiṃ aṇuttaraṃ) = unsurpassed perfection (siddhi = moksha)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = "thus I declare" (the standard Sudharma-to-Jambu closing formula)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first uddesha closes with a single compressed gatha that traces the entire arc of the disciple's life: hear, ponder, serve, attain.

(1) **succana subhasiyaiṃ** — "having heard the well-spoken." The disciple's life begins by listening — not casually, but with full intelligence (mehavi). The wise disciple distinguishes the well-spoken from the merely-spoken.

(2) **sussusae appamatto** — "should serve, vigilant." Hearing alone is not enough; the disciple must serve (shushrusha = the longing-to-hear that becomes the longing-to-serve). And he must serve **apramatta** — without a single moment of heedlessness, lest he undo in one careless instant what he has built up over years.

(3) **arahaittana guṇe aṇege** — "having cultivated many qualities." Liberation is not the fruit of any single virtue. The Uttaradhyayana Sutra (Adhy. 18) speaks of the *thousands* of qualities the bhikshu must cultivate. A disciple who has only one or two great virtues will not reach the goal; the cumulative practice of many is required.

(4) **siddhim aṇuttaraṃ** — "unsurpassed perfection." The end is moksha — the final liberation. And the Sutrakara seals the teaching with **tti bemi** — the formal "thus I declare," the voice of Sudharmaswami passing the teaching to Jambuswami.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Having heard the well-spoken instructions, the wise disciple should serve the Acharya without a moment's heedlessness. By cultivating many qualities through that service, he attains the unsurpassed perfection of liberation. — Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Hear → Serve → Cultivate → Attain. Where in this four-step chain does your own practice most often break?

## UDDESHA 2 — The Tree of Dharma and the Two Disciples

The second uddesha opens with the great parable of the *tree of dharma*: the root is vinaya, the trunk is right knowledge, the branches are compassion, the leaves are the five great vows, the flowers are the burning-up of cravings, the fruit is moksha, and the sweet juice is the unbroken bliss of liberation. Just as a tree without watered roots withers and bears no fruit, so the disciple without vinaya withers, no matter how many other practices he undertakes.

The Sutrakara then paints two contrasting portraits — the **avinita** (untrained) disciple, who is angry, ignorant, stiff, harsh, deceitful, and shrewdly dishonest, drowning in samsara like a log adrift in a flooded river; and the **suvinita** (well-trained) disciple, who reflects the gentle qualities of his Guru and rises in this life and the next. Examples are drawn from horses and elephants (who, when untrained, are beaten and live as slaves but, when well-trained, are honoured and adorned), from human beings (where the untrained are mutilated and starve, while the trained gain wealth and fame), and even from the gods (where the untrained devas serve as slaves while the trained shine in glory).

The uddesha closes with the worldly comparison: even princes endure beatings and harshness from their craft-masters in order to learn worldly skills — how much more, then, must the seeker of liberation accept the discipline of the spiritual Guru?

## SUTRA 9.2.1 — The Tree of Dharma

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मूलाउ खंधप्पभवो दुमस्स, खंधाउ पच्छा समुवेंति साहा ।
साहप्पसाहा विरुहंति पत्ता, तओ सि पुप्फं च फलं रसो य ॥९.२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.1 ||
mūlāu khaṃdhappabhavo dumassa, khaṃdhāu pacchā samuveṃti sāhā |
sāhappasāhā viruhaṃti pattā, tao si pupphaṃ ca phalaṃ raso ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

From the root arises the trunk of the tree; from the trunk, afterwards, come forth the branches; from the branches, the sub-branches arise, then the leaves; from these come its flowers, its fruit, and its (sweet) juice.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मूलाउ** (mūlāu) = from the root
- **खंधप्पभवो** (khaṃdhappabhavo) = the trunk arises (skandha-prabhava)
- **दुमस्स** (dumassa) = of the tree (druma)
- **खंधाउ पच्छा** (khaṃdhāu pacchā) = from the trunk, afterwards
- **समुवेंति साहा** (samuveṃti sāhā) = come forth the branches (shakha)
- **साहप्पसाहा** (sāhappasāhā) = the sub-branches (prashakha) from the branches
- **विरुहंति पत्ता** (viruhaṃti pattā) = the leaves grow forth
- **तओ सि** (tao si) = then, of that (tree)
- **पुप्फं च** (pupphaṃ ca) = the flower (pushpa)
- **फलं** (phalaṃ) = the fruit
- **रसो य** (raso ya) = and the (sweet) juice / sap

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara opens the second uddesha with one of the most famous extended similes in the Jain canon — the tree of dharma. The simile sets up a sequence: root → trunk → branches → sub-branches → leaves → flowers → fruit → juice. Each part depends on the part before it. Without the root there is no trunk; without the trunk, no branches; and so on. The whole edifice rests on the unseen root.

The image is preparatory. This first gatha makes the *natural* point that all life in a tree flows from the root — without committing yet to any spiritual interpretation. The next gatha will deliver the punch line: the root of the tree of dharma is *vinaya*, and its final fruit is *moksha*. The patient ascent through stem, branch, leaf, flower, and fruit is the patient ascent of the disciple's life through scripture, conduct, austerity, and finally liberation.

The traditional commentators expand the simile to ten parts of the tree mapped onto ten qualities of dharma: the root is vinaya; the bulb-region is fortitude; the trunk is knowledge; the bark is auspicious disposition; the branches are compassion; the sub-branches are truth, non-stealing, brahmacharya, etc.; the leaves are the great vows and the disposition of dharma-meditation; the flowers are the destruction of cravings; the fruit is moksha; and the sweet juice is the unobstructed bliss of liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A tree grows from its root upward — first the trunk, then the branches, then the sub-branches, then the leaves, then the flowers, then the fruit, and finally the sweet juice. Every layer depends on the one below.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you traced the "tree" of your spiritual practice down to its root, what would you find at the bottom?

## SUTRA 9.2.2 — Vinaya is the Root, Moksha is the Fruit

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं धम्मस्स विणओ, मूलं परमो से मोक्खो ।
जेण कित्तिं सुयं सिग्घं, णीसेसं चाभिगच्छइ ॥९.२.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.2 ||
evaṃ dhammassa viṇao, mūlaṃ paramo se mokkho |
jeṇa kittiṃ suyaṃ siggaṃ, ṇīsesaṃ cābhigacchai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In just this way, vinaya is the root of dharma, and the supreme (final fruit) of it is moksha. By this (vinaya), one quickly attains fame, scriptural knowledge, praise, and (ultimately) the complete (moksha — liberation from all karma).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवं** (evaṃ) = in just this way (continuing the previous simile)
- **धम्मस्स** (dhammassa) = of dharma
- **विणओ** (viṇao) = vinaya
- **मूलं** (mūlaṃ) = (is) the root
- **परमो से मोक्खो** (paramo se mokkho) = the supreme (fruit) of it is moksha
- **जेण** (jeṇa) = by which (vinaya)
- **कित्तिं** (kittiṃ) = fame (kirti — the spreading of one's good qualities in all directions)
- **सुयं** (suyaṃ) = scriptural knowledge (shruta)
- **सिग्घं** (siggaṃ) = praise (shlagha — open commendation)
- **णीसेसं** (ṇīsesaṃ) = the complete / the unsurpassed (nihshreyas = moksha; or: nihsheshatā = totality)
- **चाभिगच्छइ** (cābhigacchai) = and one attains

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the punch line of the parable. As the natural tree's root is its only true source, so vinaya is the only true source of dharma. Knowledge, conduct, austerity, faith — none of these can grow in a soul that has not first grown a root of vinaya. And the supreme final fruit is moksha — total liberation.

The Sutrakara also names the *intermediate* fruits, those that ripen along the way: kirti (the natural spreading of one's good name in all directions), shruta (the disciple's growing mastery of scripture), and shlagha (the open praise of the wise). These are not sought directly — the disciple does not pursue fame, scholarship, or praise. They simply ripen on the tree because the root is healthy. The Sanskrit niti-shloka is well-known: *vinayad yati patratam* — "by vinaya one attains worthiness."

The deeper point: the disciple never has to ask "what spiritual quality should I cultivate first?" The answer is always vinaya. Plant that root, water it daily, and the entire tree grows on its own.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In just the same way as a tree grows from its root, vinaya is the root of dharma and its supreme fruit is liberation. From this single root come fame, scriptural knowledge, praise, and ultimately the complete release from karma.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If vinaya is truly the only root from which all spiritual fruit can grow, why do so many seekers spend so much time on the leaves and flowers and so little on the root?

## SUTRA 9.2.3 — The Untrained Disciple: Adrift Like a Log

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे य चंडे मिए थद्धे, दुव्वाई णियडी सढे ।
वुज्झइ से अविणीयप्पा, कट्ठं सोयगयं जहा ॥९.२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.3 ||
je ya caṃḍe mie thaddhe, duvvāī ṇiyaḍī saḍhe |
vujjhai se aviṇīyappā, kaṭṭhaṃ soyagayaṃ jahā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He who is fierce-tempered (chanda), ignorant like a beast (mriga), stiff with pride (thaddha), harsh-spoken (durvadi), deceitful (nikriti), and crookedly cunning (shatha) — that untrained-self drowns (in the ocean of samsara), just as a log fallen into a (flooded) current.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चंडे** (caṃḍe) = fierce-tempered, easily and intensely angered (especially against the Guru)
- **मिए** (mie) = (like a) beast — dull, lacking discernment, mriga (lit. "wild animal" — used here for one without viveka)
- **थद्धे** (thaddhe) = stiff (with pride from caste, wealth, learning, etc.); cannot bow
- **दुव्वाई** (duvvāī) = harsh-spoken; one who speaks rudely before the Guru or others
- **णियडी** (ṇiyaḍī) = deceitful, given to māyā, expert in concealment
- **सढे** (saḍhe) = shrewdly cunning; one who feigns devotion in austerity but is internally indifferent or insincere
- **वुज्झइ** (vujjhai) = drowns / is swept away
- **से अविणीयप्पा** (se aviṇīyappā) = that untrained-self (avinita-atma)
- **कट्ठं** (kaṭṭhaṃ) = a log of wood
- **सोयगयं** (soyagayaṃ) = fallen into a current / a flood
- **जहा** (jahā) = just as

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now sketches the avinita disciple in six strokes — six dosha that mark the untrained. The first is **chanda** — the easily flaring rage that turns even on the Guru. The second is **mriga** — the bestial dullness of one who cannot tell right from wrong. The third is **thaddha** — the stiffness of pride that prevents bowing. The fourth is **durvadi** — the harshness of speech that wounds even when nothing wrong has been done. The fifth is **nikriti** — the love of secret deceit, the comfort of hidden pretence. The sixth is **shatha** — the cunning that feigns devotion while harbouring opposition.

These six are not random; they are the natural enemies of vinaya. Where vinaya softens, anger hardens; where vinaya sees clearly, mriga-dullness blurs; where vinaya bends, thaddha-stiffness stands rigid; where vinaya speaks gently, durvadi snarls; where vinaya is transparent, nikriti hides; where vinaya is genuine, shatha pretends.

The image of the disciple's fate is unforgettable: a log of wood thrown into a flooded river. The log has no will, no destination, no anchor. It is carried wherever the current pleases. So too the disciple who carries these six dosha — he is no longer a self-directed soul; he is samsara's plaything, drifting from birth to birth without ever finding the still point.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who is angry, dull-witted, stiff with pride, harsh in speech, deceitful, and cunningly insincere — that untrained-self drowns in the ocean of samsara like a log of wood swept along by a flood.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the six — anger, dullness, stiffness, harshness, deceit, cunning — which one most often makes you "the log in the flood" rather than the swimmer with a destination?

## SUTRA 9.2.4 — Indiscipline Drives Away Goddess Lakshmi

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विणयं पि जो उवाएणं, चोइओ कुप्पइ णरो ।
दिव्वं सो सिरिमेज्जंतिं, दंडेण पडिसेहए ॥९.२.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.4 ||
viṇayaṃ pi jo uvāeṇaṃ, coio kuppai ṇaro |
divvaṃ so sirimejjaṃtiṃ, daṃḍeṇa paḍisehae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The man who, even when (kindly) urged by (skilful) means toward vinaya, becomes angry — he is (acting like one who) drives away with a stick the divine Lakshmi (goddess of fortune) coming to his door.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णरो** (ṇaro) = a person, a man
- **उवाएणं** (uvāeṇaṃ) = by some skilful means (upaya — gentle persuasion, gradual instruction)
- **विणयं पि** (viṇayaṃ pi) = even toward vinaya, even toward (the path of) discipline
- **चोइओ** (coio) = (when) urged, prompted, instructed
- **कुप्पइ** (kuppai) = becomes angry
- **दिव्वं** (divvaṃ) = divine, unsurpassed
- **सिरिं** (siriṃ) = Lakshmi (Shri — the goddess of fortune)
- **एज्जंतिं** (ejjaṃtiṃ) = (who is) coming (to his door)
- **दंडेण** (daṃḍeṇa) = with a stick
- **पडिसेहए** (paḍisehae) = drives away, repels

### 5. COMMENTARY

The image is brutally apt. People in the world will labour all their lives in pursuit of *divya Lakshmi* — divine prosperity, the fullness of life. But here the Sutrakara says: when a Guru gently urges the disciple toward vinaya — and the disciple responds with anger — that is exactly like Lakshmi herself walking toward your door, bringing every blessing she has, and you driving her off with a stick.

Apply this to spiritual life: the Guru's instruction *is* the Lakshmi of the soul. To accept the instruction is to receive immeasurable wealth. To resist it with anger is to perform an act of stupendous foolishness — to chase away the very treasure one is supposed to be cultivating. The text calls such a person *mahamurkha* (a great fool), and means it literally: there is no greater foolishness in the spiritual order than rejecting the Guru's gentle correction.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If your Guru kindly urges you toward humility-discipline and you respond with anger, you are like a man who beats off goddess Lakshmi with a stick when she walks up to his door bringing every blessing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a teacher gently corrects you and you flare with irritation — what wealth are you driving away?

## SUTRA 9.2.5 — The Untrained Horse and Elephant: Slaves and Sufferers

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव अविणीयप्पा, उववज्झा हया गया ।
दीसंति दुहमेहंता, आभिओगमुवट्ठिया ॥९.२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.5 ||
taheva aviṇīyappā, uvavajjhā hayā gayā |
dīsaṃti duhamehaṃtā, ābhiogamuvaṭṭhiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, the untrained-self horses and elephants — though fit to be ridden — are seen suffering, placed in slave-service.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = in just the same way
- **उववज्झा** (uvavajjhā) = fit / suitable for being mounted (as conveyances)
- **अविणीयप्पा** (aviṇīyappā) = the untrained-self ones (horses, elephants)
- **हया गया** (hayā gayā) = horses (haya) and elephants (gaja)
- **दीसंति** (dīsaṃti) = are seen
- **दुहमेहंता** (duhamehaṃtā) = experiencing suffering / pain
- **आभिओगमुवट्ठिया** (ābhiogamuvaṭṭhiyā) = placed in *abhiyogika* condition — the state of forced service / slavery (the abhiyogika devas are the lowest class who must serve the higher devas)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now turns to the animal world for the next image. A horse or elephant is, by birth, a creature destined for honour — to be groomed, decorated, and ridden by kings. But the *untrained* horse and the *untrained* elephant lose all that natural dignity. They are beaten, harnessed roughly, set to bear burdens, and live a life of suffering as slaves.

The application is direct. The disciple, by his very humanity, is destined for honour — for the dignity of liberation. But the *untrained* disciple loses that natural dignity exactly as the untrained beast loses his. He becomes a slave to his own dosha (anger, pride, deceit), a slave to others' contempt, and ultimately a slave to samsara itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as untrained horses and elephants — though born for honour — end up beaten and used as slaves, so too the untrained disciple loses his natural dignity and lives in suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean that "training" is the very thing that lets a creature become honoured rather than enslaved?

## SUTRA 9.2.6 — The Trained Horse and Elephant: Glory and Wealth

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव सुविणीयप्पा, उववज्झा हया गया ।
दीसंति सुहमेहंता, इड्ढिं पत्ता महायसा ॥९.२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.6 ||
taheva suviṇīyappā, uvavajjhā hayā gayā |
dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā, iḍḍhiṃ pattā mahāyasā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, the well-trained-self horses and elephants — fit to be ridden — are seen experiencing happiness, having attained great wealth (riddhi) and great fame (mahayasha).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = in just the same way
- **सुविणीयप्पा** (suviṇīyappā) = the well-trained-self ones (suvinita-atma)
- **उववज्झा** (uvavajjhā) = fit for being ridden
- **हया गया** (hayā gayā) = horses and elephants
- **दीसंति सुहमेहंता** (dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā) = are seen experiencing happiness
- **इड्ढिं पत्ता** (iḍḍhiṃ pattā) = having attained wealth / prosperity (riddhi)
- **महायसा** (mahāyasā) = of great fame (mahayasha)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The mirror image of the previous sutra. The same horses and elephants, born to the same nature, but well-trained — and look at them now. They live in the king's stables; they are fed the finest grain; they are bathed and decorated; they bear royal saddles in processions; they are surrounded by attendants. Riddhi and yasha — wealth and fame — flow toward them naturally, not because they sought it but because their training made them worthy of it.

The disciple should hear this carefully. He is not asked to *seek* wealth or fame; he is asked to be well-trained, and the worthiness will draw what he needs. The well-trained disciple in the spiritual order receives the trust of his Acharya, the affection of his fellow-monks, the reverence of the laity, and the blessings of the deities — all without ever asking for them. They come because *suvinita* is naturally attractive.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as well-trained horses and elephants live in happiness, surrounded by wealth and great fame, so too the well-trained disciple naturally attracts honour and inner riches.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the difference between "seeking honour" and "becoming worthy of honour"?

## SUTRA 9.2.7 — The Untrained Human: Mutilated and Despised

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव अविणीयप्पा, लोगंसि णरणारिओ ।
दीसंति दुहमेहंता, छाया ते विगलिंदिया ॥९.२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.7 ||
taheva aviṇīyappā, logaṃsi ṇaraṇārio |
dīsaṃti duhamehaṃtā, chāyā te vigaliṃdiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, untrained-self men and women in the world are seen experiencing suffering, beaten (with whips and weapons, leaving wound-shadows / scars), with their senses mutilated (nose, ears, etc., cut off).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = in just the same way
- **अविणीयप्पा** (aviṇīyappā) = the untrained-self ones
- **लोगंसि** (logaṃsi) = in the world
- **णरणारिओ** (ṇaraṇārio) = men and women
- **दीसंति दुहमेहंता** (dīsaṃti duhamehaṃtā) = are seen experiencing suffering
- **छाया ते** (chāyā te) = (their) bodies bearing the wound-marks of whips (chaya — shadow / scar / loss of beauty)
- **विगलिंदिया** (vigaliṃdiyā) = with their senses (indriyas) mutilated — nose, ears cut off, etc.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara extends the principle from animals to human beings. The untrained human — male or female — undergoes in this very life what the untrained animal undergoes: physical punishment (in the ancient world, untrained criminals were whipped, beaten, and sometimes had their nose or ears amputated as a public mark of disgrace) and the suffering of a mutilated body. The traditional commentaries note three readings of *chaya vigalindiya*: (1) "robbed of dignity / their senses unable to engage their objects" (the blind, deaf, mute), (2) "afflicted by hunger and weakness," (3) "wound-marked by whip-blows and missing nose/ears."

The application is unsparing. A spiritually untrained human being suffers the same inward mutilation in this very life — not always physically, but psychologically: cut off from the ability to perceive truth (the inner deafness), cut off from the ability to speak with care (the inner muteness), cut off from the inner organs of conscience.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In this very world, untrained men and women are seen suffering — beaten, scarred, with their senses cut off. So too the spiritually untrained suffer an inward mutilation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If indiscipline disfigures the body in the world's sight, what does it disfigure in the soul's sight?

## SUTRA 9.2.8 — The Untrained Human: Beaten, Insulted, Hungry, Thirsty

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दंडसत्थपरिजुण्णा, असब्भवयणेहिं य ।
कलुणा विवण्णछंदा, खुप्पिवासाए परिगया ॥९.२.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.8 ||
daṃḍasatthaparijuṇṇā, asabbhavayaṇehiṃ ya |
kaluṇā vivaṇṇachaṃdā, khuppivāsāe parigayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Worn down by sticks and weapons, (insulted) by uncivil words, pitiable, (forced into) dependent humble disposition, beset by hunger and thirst.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दंडसत्थपरिजुण्णा** (daṃḍasatthaparijuṇṇā) = worn down (parijirṇa) by sticks (danda) and weapons (shastra)
- **असब्भवयणेहिं य** (asabbhavayaṇehiṃ ya) = and (struck) by uncivil words (asabhya-vachana) of contempt
- **कलुणा** (kaluṇā) = pitiable, in a wretched state
- **विवण्ण-छंदा** (vivaṇṇa-chaṃdā) = of altered (broken) volition — forced into dependent humility, no longer free in their will (paradhina)
- **खुप्पिवासाए** (khuppivāsāe) = (afflicted) by hunger (kshudh) and thirst (pipasa)
- **परिगया** (parigayā) = beset on all sides

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra continues the previous one, sketching the social consequences of indiscipline in unsparing detail. The untrained person in society is (1) physically punished — beaten with sticks, wounded with weapons; (2) verbally degraded — abused with uncivil speech; (3) reduced to a pitiable state where everyone looks at them with sorrow rather than respect; (4) made dependent — their own will broken, forced to live by another's permission; and (5) suffering basic deprivation — hunger and thirst that they have no power to remedy.

The cumulative picture is of someone whose entire life has been hollowed out by their own indiscipline. Note that the Sutrakara is not blaming society for this; he is showing that *indiscipline itself* delivers a person to this fate. A trained person, even if poor, retains dignity; an untrained person, even if outwardly comfortable, sooner or later collapses into this picture.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The untrained suffer beatings and insults, become pitiable and dependent, and live with hunger and thirst — all consequences of indiscipline in their own conduct.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a person is "beset on all sides" by suffering, how often is the *root* of it their own untrained self?

## SUTRA 9.2.9 — The Trained Human: Glorious and Prosperous

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव सुविणीयप्पा, लोगंसि णरणारिओ ।
दीसंति सुहमेहंता, इड्ढिं पत्ता महायसा ॥९.२.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.9 ||
taheva suviṇīyappā, logaṃsi ṇaraṇārio |
dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā, iḍḍhiṃ pattā mahāyasā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, well-trained-self men and women in the world are seen experiencing happiness, having attained great wealth and great fame.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव सुविणीयप्पा** (taheva suviṇīyappā) = just so, the well-trained ones
- **लोगंसि णरणारिओ** (logaṃsi ṇaraṇārio) = in the world, men and women
- **दीसंति सुहमेहंता** (dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā) = are seen experiencing happiness
- **इड्ढिं पत्ता** (iḍḍhiṃ pattā) = having attained wealth (riddhi)
- **महायसा** (mahāyasā) = (and) great fame (mahayasha)

The mirror-sutra. The well-trained men and women of the world live in happiness, attain wealth, and earn great fame. Note that the *outer* circumstances — birth, body, opportunity — may be similar between the trained and the untrained. What separates them is the inner training. The trained life flowers; the untrained life withers. The Sutrakara is making the same point a second time so that the disciple cannot escape the contrast.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In just the same world, well-trained men and women are seen happy, wealthy, and famous — the very opposite of the fate of the untrained.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Two people in identical circumstances — one trained, one not. What in your own life would training shift, even without circumstances changing?

## SUTRA 9.2.10 — Even Untrained Devas Live as Slaves

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव अविणीयप्पा, देवा जक्खा य गुज्झगा ।
दीसंति दुहमेहंता, आभिओगमुवट्ठिया ॥९.२.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.10 ||
taheva aviṇīyappā, devā jakkhā ya gujjhagā |
dīsaṃti duhamehaṃtā, ābhiogamuvaṭṭhiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, the untrained-self devas, yakshas, and guhyakas (subterranean Bhavanapati gods) are seen experiencing suffering, placed in slave-service.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **देवा** (devā) = devas (the celestial beings — Vaimanika and Jyotishi)
- **जक्खा** (jakkhā) = yakshas (a class of Vyantara devas)
- **गुज्झगा** (gujjhagā) = guhyakas (Bhavanapati devas, who reside in subterranean realms)
- **दुहमेहंता** (duhamehaṃtā) = experiencing suffering
- **आभिओगमुवट्ठिया** (ābhiogamuvaṭṭhiyā) = (placed) in abhiyogika condition (the lowest class of devas, who must serve the higher devas as servants)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now extends the principle even into the heavenly realms. Even there — among devas, yakshas, and bhavanapati gods — the untrained ones do not enjoy their celestial state freely. They become *abhiyogika* devas, the lowest tier of the deva-realm, who must serve the higher devas as Indra's stable-keepers, palanquin-bearers, and door-guards. They taste the heavens but as slaves, not as masters.

This is a powerful teaching: even celestial birth does not exempt one from the consequences of indiscipline. The dosha of the soul travel with the soul into whatever realm it next takes birth in. The disciple who imagines that "if I just get a good rebirth my problems will be solved" is mistaken — without training, even the deva-realm becomes a place of servitude.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even untrained devas, yakshas, and bhavanapati gods are seen suffering in the heavens — placed as slaves, made to serve the higher devas. Indiscipline degrades even celestial birth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If even the heavens cannot save an untrained soul from servitude, what hope is there in any external "good circumstance" without inner training?

## SUTRA 9.2.11 — Trained Devas Shine in Glory

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव सुविणीयप्पा, देवा जक्खा य गुज्झगा ।
दीसंति सुहमेहंता, इड्ढिं पत्ता महायसा ॥९.२.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.11 ||
taheva suviṇīyappā, devā jakkhā ya gujjhagā |
dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā, iḍḍhiṃ pattā mahāyasā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just so, the well-trained-self devas, yakshas, and guhyakas are seen experiencing happiness, having attained great wealth and great fame.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव सुविणीयप्पा** (taheva suviṇīyappā) = just so, the well-trained ones
- **देवा जक्खा य गुज्झगा** (devā jakkhā ya gujjhagā) = devas, yakshas, and guhyakas (bhavanapati gods)
- **दीसंति सुहमेहंता** (dīsaṃti suhamehaṃtā) = are seen experiencing happiness

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing of the threefold parallel — animals, humans, devas. In each case, the untrained suffer and the trained flourish. The Sutrakara has now built an irrefutable case across all three realms of birth: training is what determines well-being, not the realm of birth itself.

The well-trained devas — those who carried the seeds of vinaya from their previous human birth — enjoy the deva-realm in full glory: maha-riddhi (great celestial wealth, divine palaces, divine attendants) and maha-yasha (great fame, recognition by Indra and the assembly of devas).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The well-trained devas, yakshas, and bhavanapati gods are seen in full happiness, with celestial wealth and great fame. Training delivers glory in every realm of birth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If training determines well-being everywhere — earth, world, heavens — what does that say about the *one* thing worth investing in across lives?

## SUTRA 9.2.12 — The Disciple Who Serves the Acharya: His Learning Grows

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे आयरिय उवज्झायाणं, सुस्सूसावयणंकरा ।
तेसिं सिक्खा पवड्ढंति, जलसित्ता इव पायवा ॥९.२.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.12 ||
je āyariya uvajjhāyāṇaṃ, sussūsāvayaṇaṃkarā |
tesiṃ sikkhā pavaḍḍhaṃti, jalasittā iva pāyavā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those who serve the Acharyas and Upadhyayas, and who carry out (their) words — their learning grows, just as trees (grow) when watered.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे** (je) = those who
- **आयरिय उवज्झायाणं** (āyariya uvajjhāyāṇaṃ) = of (toward) the Acharyas and the Upadhyayas
- **सुस्सूसा-वयणं-करा** (sussūsā-vayaṇaṃ-karā) = render service (shushrusha) and carry out (their) commands
- **तेसिं सिक्खा** (tesiṃ sikkhā) = their learning / training (shiksha)
- **पवड्ढंति** (pavaḍḍhaṃti) = grow / increase
- **जलसित्ता इव** (jalasittā iva) = like water-watered (trees)
- **पायवा** (pāyavā) = trees (padapa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Now the sutra returns to the tree-image of the chapter's opening. The disciple who serves his Acharyas and Upadhyayas — and crucially, who *acts on their instructions* — finds that his learning grows the way a watered tree grows. Service and obedience are the water; learning is the visible growth.

The point is that knowledge does not "stick" to a disciple by mere intellectual effort. A disciple may attend every lecture and read every sutra and still not grow, if there is no humble service of the teacher behind it. Conversely, a disciple of modest intellectual gifts who serves his Acharya with devotion finds his understanding *increasing on its own*, because the channel through which knowledge flows has been opened.

The two kinds of *shiksha* are noted by the commentators: **grahana-shiksha** (the learning of what is right and wrong) and **asevana-shiksha** (the practice that makes that learning real). Both grow when the disciple serves; both wither when he does not.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Those who serve the Acharyas and Upadhyayas and carry out their instructions — their learning grows like a tree that is watered every day.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If knowledge flows through the channel of service, how watered is the tree of your own learning?

## SUTRA 9.2.13 — Even the Worldly Crafts Require a Teacher

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्पणट्ठा परट्ठा वा, सिप्पा णेउणियाणि य ।
गिहिणो उवभोगट्ठा, इहलोगस्स कारणा ॥९.२.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.13 ||
appaṇaṭṭhā paraṭṭhā vā, sippā ṇeuṇiyāṇi ya |
gihiṇo uvabhogaṭṭhā, ihalogassa kāraṇā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For their own sake or for the sake of others, householders learn the (worldly) crafts (shilpa) and skills (naipunya), in order to enjoy / sustain (themselves) — purely for the sake of this world.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अप्पणट्ठा** (appaṇaṭṭhā) = for one's own sake (atma-artha)
- **परट्ठा वा** (paraṭṭhā vā) = or for the sake of others (para-artha — family, dependents)
- **सिप्पा** (sippā) = crafts (shilpa — pottery, smithing, carpentry, painting, etc.)
- **णेउणियाणि य** (ṇeuṇiyāṇi ya) = and skills (naipunya — mastery of arts)
- **गिहिणो** (gihiṇo) = the householders (grihi)
- **उवभोगट्ठा** (uvabhogaṭṭhā) = for the sake of enjoyment / sustenance
- **इहलोगस्स कारणा** (ihalogassa kāraṇā) = for the sake of (success in) this world (only)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now makes a worldly comparison. In the ancient world, men were said to learn 72 crafts and women 64 — for personal sustenance, for family support, for community service, or for sheer enjoyment. None of these crafts could be learned alone; each required a teacher, and the apprentice had to submit to the teacher's discipline to acquire the skill. This is true even though the goal of these crafts is purely worldly — limited to "this life" (ihaloka).

The Sutrakara is preparing an *a fortiori* argument that will be sealed in Sutra 16: if even worldly skills, sought only for this life's comfort, require humble apprenticeship to a teacher — how much more must the spiritual aspirant submit to his Guru, since the goal is not just this life but the eternal one?

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For their own benefit or for their families', householders learn worldly crafts and skills — purely to make a living in this life. And even for that, they go to a teacher.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

For purely worldly skills, people willingly submit to a teacher. Why does that submission feel so much harder when the goal is spiritual?

## SUTRA 9.2.14 — Even Princes Endure Beatings to Learn

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जेण बंधं वहं घोरं, परियावं च दारुणं ।
सिक्खमाणा णियच्छंति, जुत्ता ते ललिइंदिया ॥९.२.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.14 ||
jeṇa baṃdhaṃ vahaṃ ghoraṃ, pariyāvaṃ ca dāruṇaṃ |
sikkhamāṇā ṇiyacchaṃti, juttā te laliiṃdiyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those (princes and rich men's sons) of delicate sensibilities, while learning (a craft), submit themselves to (the teacher's) terrible binding (restraint), severe blows (vadha), and harsh punishment (dāruṇa paritāpa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ललिइंदिया** (laliiṃdiyā) = those of delicate / pampered senses (lalita-indriya — princes and well-born youths whose bodies are unaccustomed to hardship)
- **ते** (te) = they (princes etc.)
- **सिक्खमाणा** (sikkhamāṇā) = while learning (the craft from the teacher)
- **णियच्छंति** (ṇiyacchaṃti) = submit to / undergo / receive
- **जेण** (jeṇa) = (that) by which (the teacher)
- **जुत्ता** (juttā) = bound, fastened (in restraint or by the teacher's discipline)
- **बंधं** (baṃdhaṃ) = (terrible) binding / restraint
- **वहं घोरं** (vahaṃ ghoraṃ) = severe blows / beating (ghora vadha)
- **परियावं च दारुणं** (pariyāvaṃ ca dāruṇaṃ) = and harsh / cruel torment (dāruṇa paritāpa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara intensifies the worldly comparison. Even princes — pampered youths whose bodies have never known hardship — when they go to a craft-master to learn (say, archery, swordsmanship, or a fine art), willingly accept being bound, beaten, and disciplined harshly. They do not say "I am the king's son; how can you treat me this way?" They submit, because they understand that the skill cannot be acquired without the discipline.

This was a real social fact in ancient India: princes lived in the gurukula on equal terms with other students, ate plain food, slept on simple mats, and accepted physical correction from the teacher. The Sutrakara holds this up as a mirror: if even princes endured this for worldly skills, what excuse does the spiritual disciple have for resisting the (far gentler) discipline of his Guru?

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even princes — used to soft living — when they go to learn a craft, accept being bound, beaten, and harshly disciplined by their teachers. They submit because they want the skill.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you would willingly endure hardship to acquire a worldly skill, what would you willingly endure to acquire liberation?

## SUTRA 9.2.15 — Yet Even Such Princes Worship Their Teachers

- **णिद्देसवत्तिणो** (ṇiddesavattiṇo) = remaining within (his) directive — staying within his command

### 5. COMMENTARY

The point sharpens further. Not only do the princes endure the harsh discipline — they actually *worship* the teacher who imposes it. They bring him gifts, they bow to him, they remain joyfully within his command, and they consider themselves blessed by the relationship. Why? Because they know that the teacher, harsh as he seems, is the doorway to the skill they want.

The Sutrakara is now hammering the comparison. If even an exterior-only worldly relationship — between a prince and his archery-master — produces this kind of joyful submission, what should the spiritual disciple's bearing be toward the Guru who is opening for him the doorway to liberation? Anything less than full vinaya and full satisfaction would be inconsistent.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Those very princes — beaten and disciplined — *worship* their craft-teachers. They honour, bow, and remain happily within the teacher's command, because they know the skill is worth it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a prince worships the master who beats him for an archery-skill, how should you bear yourself toward the master who is teaching you the path to liberation?

## SUTRA 9.2.16 — How Much More for the Spiritual Seeker

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

किं पुण जे सुयग्गाही, अणंतहियकामए ।
आयरिया जं वए भिक्खू, तम्हा तं णाइवत्तए ॥९.२.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.16 ||
kiṃ puṇa je suyaggāhī, aṇaṃtahiyakāmae |
āyariyā jaṃ vae bhikkhū, tamhā taṃ ṇāivattae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

What more, then, of the one who is a receiver of scripture, who longs for the infinite welfare (of liberation)? Whatever the Acharyas say, the bhikshu should therefore not transgress (it).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **किं पुण** (kiṃ puṇa) = what more (need be said about) / how much more (for one who)...
- **जे** (je) = he who (is)
- **सुयग्गाही** (suyaggāhī) = a receiver of scripture (shruta-grahi)
- **अणंत-हिय-कामए** (aṇaṃta-hiya-kāmae) = longs for the infinite welfare (ananta-hita = moksha)
- **आयरिया जं वए** (āyariyā jaṃ vae) = whatever the Acharyas say
- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the bhikshu / monk
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **तं णाइवत्तए** (taṃ ṇāivattae) = should not transgress (na ativartate) it

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the conclusion of the worldly-comparison argument. *Kim puna* — "how much more!" If even a worldly student endures and worships his teacher for a worldly skill, how much more should the spiritual seeker — who longs for *ananta-hita*, the infinite good of moksha — accept the Acharya's command without the slightest transgression?

The verb **na ativartate** is precise: "should not step beyond." The Acharya's word is a line; the disciple does not cross it. Not by argument, not by interpretation, not by exception, not by delay. Whatever the Acharya says, the bhikshu does. This is the strict standard the Sutrakara establishes for the monk.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If even princes accept harsh discipline for a worldly craft, how much more should the seeker of liberation accept his Acharya's command? Whatever the Acharya says, the bhikshu must not transgress.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The seeker of moksha demands far less of his teacher than the prince demanded of his craft-master. Why is the spiritual disciple often less obedient than the worldly apprentice?

## SUTRA 9.2.17 — Vinaya in Practice: Lower Bed, Lower Walk, Lower Seat

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णीयं सेज्जं गई ठाणं, णीयं च आसणाणि य ।
णीयं च पाए वंदिज्जा, णीयं कुज्जा य अंजलि ॥९.२.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.17 ||
ṇīyaṃ sejjaṃ gaī ṭhāṇaṃ, ṇīyaṃ ca āsaṇāṇi ya |
ṇīyaṃ ca pāe vaṃdijjā, ṇīyaṃ kujjā ya aṃjali ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The disciple should accept) a low bed, low gait, low standing-place, and low seats; (he should) bow to (the Guru's) feet (in lowness), and (he should) form the salutation (anjali) (in a posture of) lowness.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णीयं** (ṇīyaṃ) = lower (than the Guru's)
- **सेज्जं** (sejjaṃ) = bed / mat (shayya)
- **गई** (gaī) = walking gait / movement (gati — humbly, behind the Guru)
- **ठाणं** (ṭhāṇaṃ) = standing-place (sthana)
- **आसणाणि य** (āsaṇāṇi ya) = and seats (asana)
- **णीयं च पाए वंदिज्जा** (ṇīyaṃ ca pāe vaṃdijjā) = (he should) bow to (the Guru's) feet (in low posture)
- **णीयं कुज्जा य अंजलि** (ṇīyaṃ kujjā ya aṃjali) = (he should) make the anjali (folded palms) in lowness

### 5. COMMENTARY

After the principle, the practice. The Sutrakara now lists six concrete bodily disciplines by which the disciple's vinaya is expressed. (1) The bed: never higher or more comfortable than the Guru's. (2) The gait: never walking ahead of the Guru, never too close (which causes friction), never too far (which shows distance). (3) The standing-place: never standing higher than the Guru, never crowding ahead, behind, or beside. (4) The seat: always lower than the Guru's; even if forced by circumstance to sit on a stool or platform, the disciple takes care that his seat is lower. (5) The bowing: bowing at the Guru's feet with the head touching them in respect (panchanga-vandana). (6) The anjali: the folded-palm salutation made with the head bowed.

The principle is that vinaya is not abstract; it must be visible in the body's geometry. The disciple who genuinely respects the Guru will naturally arrange his body so as never to claim a higher position. A disciple who claims equal or higher physical positioning is, even without speaking, declaring his pride.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple's bed should be lower than the Guru's. He should walk humbly behind, stand humbly to the side, sit on a lower seat, bow at the Guru's feet, and fold his palms with his head bowed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you are with someone you genuinely revere, what do you notice about how your body arranges itself?

## SUTRA 9.2.18 — When You Accidentally Touch the Guru

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संघट्टइत्ता काएणं, तहा उवहिणामवि ।
खमेह अवराहं मे, वएज्ज ण पुणो त्ति य ॥९.२.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.18 ||
saṃghaṭṭaittā kāeṇaṃ, tahā uvahiṇāmavi |
khameha avarāhaṃ me, vaejja ṇa puṇo tti ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having (accidentally) touched (the Acharya) by the body, or even by an article (bowl, robe, etc.), (the disciple) should say: "Forgive my offence; I will not (do so) again."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संघट्टइत्ता** (saṃghaṭṭaittā) = having (accidentally) touched / collided with
- **काएणं** (kāeṇaṃ) = by the body
- **तहा उवहिणामवि** (tahā uvahiṇāmavi) = or even by an upadhi (an article — robe, bowl, staff)
- **खमेह अवराहं मे** (khameha avarāhaṃ me) = "forgive (kshamayata) my offence (aparadha)"
- **वएज्ज** (vaejja) = (the disciple) should say
- **ण पुणो त्ति य** (ṇa puṇo tti ya) = "(I will) not again" — and (so on)

### 5. COMMENTARY

A wonderfully practical sutra. In the close quarters of monastic life, accidental physical contact with the Guru — bumping into him, brushing him with a robe, accidentally striking him with an alms-bowl — is bound to happen. Vinaya does not allow the disciple to dismiss such an event ("it was an accident — no big deal"). Rather, the disciple immediately stops, addresses the Guru, asks for forgiveness for the offence (even an accidental one), and pledges that he will be more careful in future.

The point is not legalistic. The point is that vinaya cultivates *constant attention* to the Guru's person — and the way that attention is cultivated is precisely by treating even accidental contact as an occasion for asking forgiveness. A disciple who routinely says "khameha avaraham me" each time he accidentally touches the Guru becomes a disciple whose entire bodily presence is alert to the Guru's presence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If you accidentally touch the Acharya — by your body or even by a bowl or robe — say at once: "Forgive my offence; I will not do it again."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it cultivate in you to ask forgiveness even for accidents?

## SUTRA 9.2.19 — The Untrained Disciple Acts Only When Ordered Repeatedly

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुग्गओ वा पओएणं, चोइओ वहइ रहं ।
एवं दुबुद्धि किच्चाणं, वुत्तो वुत्तो पकुव्वइ ॥९.२.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.19 ||
duggao vā paoeṇaṃ, coio vahai rahaṃ |
evaṃ dubuddhi kiccāṇaṃ, vutto vutto pakuvvai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as a poor (untrained) bullock, prodded again and again by the goad, bears the chariot — even so the dull-witted (disciple) performs the (Acharya's) tasks (only when) told and re-told.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दुग्गओ** (duggao) = the dull / wretched (untrained) bullock (a poor or untrained ox)
- **पओएणं** (paoeṇaṃ) = with the goad / prod
- **चोइओ** (coio) = (when) prodded
- **वहइ रहं** (vahai rahaṃ) = (only then) bears the chariot
- **एवं** (evaṃ) = even so
- **दुबुद्धि** (dubuddhi) = the dull-witted (disciple)
- **किच्चाणं** (kiccāṇaṃ) = of the (Acharya's) tasks
- **वुत्तो वुत्तो** (vutto vutto) = (only when) told and told (repeatedly)
- **पकुव्वइ** (pakuvvai) = performs (them)

### 5. COMMENTARY

A vivid simile of the *avinita* in action. The poorly-trained bullock will not pull the chariot until prodded; even then, after a step or two, it stops and must be prodded again. So is the dull-witted disciple — he understands the Acharya's instruction, but he does not move until prodded; even after he starts, he stops, and must be prodded again. The Acharya is reduced to the role of perpetual goader.

The contrast is with the **vinita** disciple, who anticipates the Acharya's wish — who, by reading the Acharya's signal (alochita) and the Acharya's gesture (ingita), already knows what is needed and is doing it before any word is spoken. The dull-witted disciple costs the Acharya energy that should be going into teaching; the wise disciple gives the Acharya energy back.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Like a poorly-trained bullock that pulls the chariot only when prodded again and again, the dull-witted disciple does the Acharya's work only when told and re-told.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you serve someone you respect, do you wait to be told, or do you anticipate?

## SUTRA 9.2.20 — Don't Reply From Your Seat — Get Up and Listen

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आलवंते लवंते वा, ण णिसेज्जाए पडिस्सुणे ।
मोत्तूण आसणं धीरो, सुस्सूसाए पडिस्सुणे ॥९.२.२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.20 ||
ālavaṃte lavaṃte vā, ṇa ṇisejjāe paḍissuṇe |
mottūṇa āsaṇaṃ dhīro, sussūsāe paḍissuṇe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the Guru) calls (once) or calls (again and again), (the disciple) should not answer (while seated) on his seat. The wise (disciple), having abandoned the seat, should answer (the Guru) with shushrusha (respectful attention).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आलवंते** (ālavaṃte) = (when the Guru) calls (once)
- **लवंते वा** (lavaṃte vā) = or calls (repeatedly)
- **ण णिसेज्जाए** (ṇa ṇisejjāe) = not from the seat
- **पडिस्सुणे** (paḍissuṇe) = should answer / acknowledge
- **मोत्तूण आसणं** (mottūṇa āsaṇaṃ) = having left the seat
- **धीरो** (dhīro) = the wise (disciple)
- **सुस्सूसाए** (sussūsāe) = with shushrusha (respectful attention to hear)
- **पडिस्सुणे** (paḍissuṇe) = (then) should reply

### 5. COMMENTARY

Another concrete vinaya-rule. When the Guru calls — whether once or several times — the disciple does not reply while sitting where he is. He gets up, approaches the Guru, draws near with attention, and only then responds. This is so even if the disciple is in the middle of some other activity.

The principle is double. First, the act of getting up and approaching declares the disciple's *willing readiness*; a reply tossed over the shoulder from a distance declares the opposite. Second, the act of approaching positions the disciple to actually hear what the Guru wants — full attention is needed for the instruction to be received correctly.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the Guru calls — whether once or many times — don't answer from where you are sitting. Get up, approach with attention, and respond.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it cost you to get up when called? What does it give you?

## SUTRA 9.2.21 — Knowing the Time, the Mood, and the Means

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कालं छंदोवयारं च, पडिलेहित्ताण हेउहिं ।
तेण तेण उवाएणं, तं तं संपडिवायए ॥९.२.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.21 ||
kālaṃ chaṃdovayāraṃ ca, paḍilehittāṇa heuhiṃ |
teṇa teṇa uvāeṇaṃ, taṃ taṃ saṃpaḍivāyae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having ascertained, with (right) reasons, the time, the (Guru's) intent, and the (proper mode of) attendance — (the disciple), by such-and-such a means, should accomplish such-and-such (a task).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कालं** (kālaṃ) = the time / season (cold/hot, day/night, opportune/inopportune)
- **छंद** (chaṃda) = the (Guru's) intention / desire
- **उवयारं** (uvayāraṃ) = the (proper) mode of attendance / service / honour
- **पडिलेहित्ताण** (paḍilehittāṇa) = having ascertained (pratilekhya — having examined carefully)
- **हेउहिं** (heuhiṃ) = with (right) reasons / inferences
- **तेण तेण उवाएणं** (teṇa teṇa uvāeṇaṃ) = by that-and-that (appropriate) means
- **तं तं संपडिवायए** (taṃ taṃ saṃpaḍivāyae) = should accomplish that-and-that (task)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The disciple does not just receive the Acharya's command and rush to execute it mechanically. The trained disciple first reads three things: (1) the **kala** — the time of day, the season, the urgency — what is appropriate to bring at this hour, in this weather; (2) the **chanda** — the Guru's actual desire, even unspoken (does the Guru want hot water because of cold weather, cool water because of summer, etc.); and (3) the **upachara** — the right *manner* of service (the right gesture, the right word, the right pace). Only after reading these does the disciple choose the means.

The traditional examples are charming: in summer, the Guru likes shade; in cold regions, hot milk; in hot regions, cool water. The trained disciple notices these details without being told. A disciple who serves "by the rule book" without reading time, mood, and proper manner is technically obedient but spiritually deaf. A disciple who reads the Guru's true desire — even before the Guru speaks it — is the truly trained one.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Notice the time, the Guru's mood, and the right way of serving. Then choose the right means and accomplish the task.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to "read" what someone needs before they ask?

## SUTRA 9.2.22 — The Trained Gain, the Untrained Lose

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विवत्ती अविणीयस्स, संपत्ती विणियस्स य ।
जस्सेयं दुहओ णायं, सिक्खं से अभिगच्छइ ॥९.२.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.22 ||
vivattī aviṇīyassa, saṃpattī viṇiyassa ya |
jasseyaṃ duhao ṇāyaṃ, sikkhaṃ se abhigacchai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Calamity (vipatti) for the untrained, and prosperity (sampatti) for the trained — he who knows both these (consequences) attains (true) learning.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **विवत्ती** (vivattī) = calamity / disaster (vipatti — loss of qualities, suffering, fall)
- **अविणीयस्स** (aviṇīyassa) = of the untrained (avinita)
- **संपत्ती** (saṃpattī) = prosperity (sampatti — wealth of good qualities)
- **विणियस्स य** (viṇiyassa ya) = and of the trained (vinita)
- **जस्सेयं दुहओ णायं** (jasseyaṃ duhao ṇāyaṃ) = he by whom both these (consequences) are known
- **सिक्खं** (sikkhaṃ) = (true) learning / discipline (shiksha)
- **से अभिगच्छइ** (se abhigacchai) = he attains

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra summarises the entire uddesha so far in two words: *vipatti* and *sampatti*. The untrained reap calamity in this life and the next; the trained reap prosperity. The disciple who has truly understood *both* consequences — not just abstractly but viscerally, so that the avinita-life feels frightening and the vinita-life feels desirable — that disciple has attained the genuine inner *shiksha*.

The Sutrakara is making a subtle psychological point. Mere knowledge of the rule ("vinaya is required") is not enough. The disciple must internally see, with certainty, what indiscipline costs and what discipline gains. Only when those two consequences are inwardly felt does the disciple begin to act on the basis of vinaya rather than on the basis of impulse. This is the moment when "training" becomes a chosen orientation rather than an external imposition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Calamity follows the untrained; prosperity follows the trained. The disciple who clearly sees both consequences begins to acquire real inner training.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Until you have *seen* what indiscipline actually costs, can vinaya ever feel like more than a rule?

## SUTRA 9.2.23 — The Disciple Who Will Never Reach Liberation

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे यावि चंडे मइ इड्ढिगारवे, पिसुणे णरे साहसहीणपेसणे ।
अदिट्ठधम्मे विणए अकोविए, असंविभागी ण हु तस्स मुक्खो ॥९.२.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.23 ||
je yāvi caṃḍe mai iḍḍhigārave, pisuṇe ṇare sāhasahīṇapesaṇe |
adiṭṭhadhamme viṇae akovie, asaṃvibhāgī ṇa hu tassa mukkho ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whoever is fierce-tempered (chanda), proud of his intellect and wealth (mati-riddhi-gaurava), a back-biter (pishuna), a man of reckless action (sahasika), heedless of the (Guru's) command (hina-preshana), one who has not seen the dharma (adrishta-dharma), unskilled in vinaya (vinaye akovida), and one who does not share with others (asanvibhagi) — for him there is certainly no liberation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे यावि** (je yāvi) = whoever (any monk)
- **णरे** (ṇare) = a person
- **चंडे** (caṃḍe) = fierce-tempered, easily angered
- **मइ-इड्ढि-गारवे** (mai-iḍḍhi-gārave) = proud of his intellect (mati) and his attainments / wealth (riddhi-gaurava)
- **पिसुणे** (pisuṇe) = a back-biter, slanderer (pishuna)
- **साहस** (sāhasa) = reckless action (saahasa — taking on improper or violent acts)
- **हीणपेसणे** (hīṇapesaṇe) = one who is deficient in carrying out the (Guru's) command (hina-preshana)
- **अदिट्ठधम्मे** (adiṭṭhadhamme) = one who has not seen / experienced the dharma (the conduct and scripture)
- **विणए अकोविए** (viṇae akovie) = unskilled / unlearned in vinaya
- **असंविभागी** (asaṃvibhāgī) = one who does not share (food, robes, etc.) with fellow monks
- **ण हु तस्स मुक्खो** (ṇa hu tassa mukkho) = there is certainly no liberation for him

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara catalogues eight dosha that together close the door of liberation. The list is not random; it is a complete picture of the **avinita** in the monastic order: (1) **chanda** — fierce-tempered; (2) **mati-riddhi-gaurava** — vain about his learning and his attainments; (3) **pishuna** — given to slander and gossip behind others' backs; (4) **sahasika** — rashly committing improper acts (the Prashnavyakarana lists this as a synonym for himsa); (5) **hina-preshana** — careless in carrying out the Guru's commands; (6) **adrishta-dharma** — has not understood scripture or conduct; (7) **vinaye akovida** — completely unskilled in the practice of vinaya; (8) **asanvibhagi** — selfish, never shares his alms or possessions with fellow monks.

Each one of these would be enough to cause trouble. Together, they make liberation impossible. The Sutrakara's verdict is decisive: **na hu tassa mukkho** — "there is certainly no liberation for him." The word *hu* is emphatic; this is not a probability but a certainty. Such a monk may wear the robe and follow the outward forms, but the eight dosha that he carries within will block the door.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk who is fierce-tempered, proud of his learning and gains, a slanderer, recklessly improper, careless of the Guru's commands, ignorant of dharma, untrained in vinaya, and unwilling to share — for such a one there is certainly no liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If even one of these eight is enough to slow the path, what does carrying many do?

## SUTRA 9.2.24 — The Disciple Who Crosses the Ocean of Samsara

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिद्देसवित्ती पुण जे गुरूणं, सुयत्थधम्मा विणयम्मि कोविया ।
तरितु ते ओघमिणं दुरुत्तरं, खवित्तु कम्मं गइमुत्तमं गया ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥९.२.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.2.24 ||
ṇiddesavittī puṇa je gurūṇaṃ, suyatthadhammā viṇayammi koviyā |
taritu te oghamiṇaṃ duruttaraṃ, khavittu kammaṃ gaimuttamaṃ gayā || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

But those who remain within the (Gurus') command, who are masters of the meaning of scripture and dharma, and who are skilled in vinaya — they cross this hard-to-cross flood (samsara), and having destroyed (their) karma, they have attained (and continue to attain) the supreme destination. — Thus do I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णिद्देसवित्ती** (ṇiddesavittī) = remaining within (the Gurus') directive — staying in nirdesha (the Guru's command)
- **पुण** (puṇa) = but, on the other hand
- **जे गुरूणं** (je gurūṇaṃ) = those of the Gurus
- **सुयत्थधम्मा** (suyatthadhammā) = masters of the meaning of scripture and dharma (shrutartha-dharma)
- **विणयम्मि कोविया** (viṇayammi koviyā) = skilled (kovida) in vinaya
- **तरितु ते** (taritu te) = they, having crossed
- **ओघमिणं** (oghamiṇaṃ) = this flood (ogha = samsara as ocean)
- **दुरुत्तरं** (duruttaraṃ) = hard-to-cross (durutara)
- **खवित्तु कम्मं** (khavittu kammaṃ) = having destroyed (kshapayitva) karma
- **गइमुत्तमं गया** (gaimuttamaṃ gayā) = have gone to the supreme destination (uttama gati = siddhi)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = thus I declare

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing gatha of the second uddesha mirrors the closing of the first. Where Sutra 9.2.23 named the eight dosha that close the door, this sutra names the three qualities that open it: (1) **niddesa-vittī** — remaining within the Guru's command; (2) **shrutartha-dharma** — mastering the meaning of scripture and dharma (not just the words but their substance); (3) **vinaye kovida** — skilled in the actual practice of vinaya.

A disciple of these three qualities crosses the **ogha** — the flood of samsara — even though it is "hard to cross." Note the past-tense form **gayā** ("have gone"): the Sutrakara is describing the historical attainment of countless Acharyas of the past, and continuing for those of the present and the future. The path is real, the door is open, and many have walked through it. The closing **tti bemi** is the formal seal.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

But those who stay within the Guru's command, who have grasped the meaning of scripture and dharma, and who are skilled in vinaya — they cross the hard-to-cross ocean of samsara, destroy their karma, and reach the supreme destination. Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Within the Guru's command — meaning, mastery, vinaya. If these three are the boat that crosses samsara, which oar are you weakest in?

## UDDESHA 3 — The Disciple Worthy of Worship

The third uddesha shifts perspective. Where the first painted the disciple's bearing toward the Guru and the second contrasted the trained and untrained disciple, the third now paints the *fully realised* disciple — the one whose character is so transparently good that he himself becomes worthy of the world's worship. The chapter draws on the philosopher's-stone image: as the touch of the *parasmani* (philosopher's stone) turns iron to gold, so the touch of a true Guru's vinaya can turn an ordinary disciple into one who is reverenced everywhere.

The fifteen gathas of this uddesha enumerate, in successive portraits, the qualities of the disciple worthy of worship: vigilant in service, careful to read the Acharya's intent, polite to elder monks even if they themselves are senior in initiation, content with whatever alms they receive, equanimous toward both praise and abuse, careful of speech, free of ostentation, free of curiosity, indifferent to honour and dishonour, modest, respected by all. The closing gatha promises the supreme fruit: such a disciple, by constant attendance on the Guru, casts off all karmic stain and reaches the radiant Siddha-state — like the moon emerging from clouds.

## SUTRA 9.3.1 — Worship of the Acharya as the Brahmin Worships His Fire

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयरियं अग्गिमिवाहियग्गी, सुस्सूसमाणो पडिजागरिज्जा ।
आलोइयं इंगियमेव णच्चा, जो छंदमाराहयइ स पुज्जो ॥९.३.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.1 ||
āyariyaṃ aggimivāhiyaggī, sussūsamāṇo paḍijāgarijjā |
āloiyaṃ iṃgiyameva ṇaccā, jo chaṃdamārāhayai sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Just as the Brahmin) who has installed the sacred fire (worships) the fire — (so should the disciple) attentively serve the Acharya, vigilant in (every) act. Having understood (the Acharya's) sight (alochita) and bodily gestures (ingita) — he who fulfils (the Acharya's) wish, is worshipworthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आहियग्गी** (āhiyaggī) = the (Brahmin) who has installed the sacred fire (Agnihotri)
- **अग्गिं** (aggiṃ) = (worships) the fire
- **इव** (iva) = just as
- **आयरियं** (āyariyaṃ) = the Acharya
- **सुस्सूसमाणो** (sussūsamāṇo) = (the disciple) serving (with shushrusha)
- **पडिजागरिज्जा** (paḍijāgarijjā) = should be vigilant (in every task)
- **आलोइयं** (āloiyaṃ) = (the Acharya's) gaze / glance
- **इंगियमेव णच्चा** (iṃgiyameva ṇaccā) = and the bodily gesture (ingita) — having known
- **जो छंदं आराहयइ** (jo chaṃdaṃ ārāhayai) = he who fulfils (the Acharya's) desire (chanda)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is the worship-worthy one

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third uddesha opens by recalling the powerful image from the first (Sutra 9.1.11): the Brahmin's lifelong devotion to his sacred fire. Just as that Brahmin is forever vigilant — never letting the fire go out, never being inattentive to its needs — so the disciple should attend to the Acharya. Vigilance is the first quality of the worship-worthy disciple.

Beyond mere attendance, the trained disciple develops a more delicate skill: reading the Acharya's *alochita* (gaze) and *ingita* (gesture). The Acharya may glance toward the bowl, indicating thirst; he may shift his shoulder, indicating cold; he may look toward the door, indicating he wishes to go out. The vigilant disciple reads these signals and acts before any word is spoken. He fulfils the Acharya's *chanda* — desire — without the Acharya having to express it.

The hita-upadesha verse cited in the commentary captures the principle:

*akarair ingitair gatya, cheshtaya bhashanena cha |
netra-vaktra-vikaraishcha, lakshyate'ntargataṃ manaḥ ||*

"By posture, gesture, gait, action, speech, and the changes of eye and face — the inner mind is read."

The disciple who masters this art of reading becomes the worship-worthy one (**sa pujjo**). The verb has no tense; the commentators read it as both "is" and "becomes" — such a disciple is now worship-worthy, and such a disciple eventually becomes one of the great Acharyas himself, joining the lineage of those who are worshipped.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as the fire-Brahmin attends his sacred fire vigilantly, the disciple should attentively serve the Acharya — and reading even his glance and gestures, fulfil his unspoken wishes. Such a disciple becomes worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you can anticipate what someone you respect needs, before they speak — what does that say about how present you are with them?

## SUTRA 9.3.2 — Vinaya for the Sake of Conduct, Acceptance of the Word

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयारमट्ठा विणयं पउंजे, सुस्सूसमाणो परिगिज्झ वक्कं ।
जहोवइट्ठं अभिकंखमाणो, गुरुं तु णासाययइ स पुज्जो ॥९.३.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.2 ||
āyāramaṭṭhā viṇayaṃ pauṃje, sussūsamāṇo parigijjha vakkaṃ |
jahovaiṭṭhaṃ abhikaṃkhamāṇo, guruṃ tu ṇāsāyayai sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The disciple who) employs vinaya for the sake of (mastering) conduct, attentively listening, and grasping (the Guru's) word; longing to act exactly as instructed; and never offends the Guru — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयारमट्ठा** (āyāramaṭṭhā) = for the sake of (learning) conduct (achara-artha — to acquire achara, or for the Guru's purpose)
- **विणयं पउंजे** (viṇayaṃ pauṃje) = practises vinaya
- **सुस्सूसमाणो** (sussūsamāṇo) = listening with longing (shushrusha — the wish-to-hear)
- **परिगिज्झ वक्कं** (parigijjha vakkaṃ) = grasping (the Guru's) word (vakya)
- **जहोवइट्ठं** (jahovaiṭṭhaṃ) = exactly as instructed (yathopadishta)
- **अभिकंखमाणो** (abhikaṃkhamāṇo) = longing to perform (the act)
- **गुरुं तु णासाययइ** (guruṃ tu ṇāsāyayai) = does not commit ashatana of the Guru
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra names four further qualities of the worship-worthy. (1) **Vinaya for the sake of achara** — the disciple practises vinaya not for show, not for inheritance, not for honour, but for the actual purpose of acquiring right conduct. The motive is internal, not external. (2) **Shushrusha** — listening with the longing-to-hear. The disciple attends every word the Guru speaks with a silent inward thirst. (3) **Yathopadishta-abhikanksha** — longing to act *exactly* as instructed. Not "more or less as instructed," not "in the spirit of the instruction," but precisely as instructed, with desire for the act. (4) **Na asatayati** — never offends the Guru, never commits the smallest ashatana.

A disciple of these four qualities, the Sutrakara declares, is the worship-worthy one. The deeper teaching is that all four are fundamentally a single quality — the disciple's complete *availability* to the Guru's purpose. When the disciple is utterly available, vinaya becomes effortless; the listening becomes thirsty; the action becomes exact; and offence becomes impossible.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who practises vinaya for the actual sake of mastering conduct, listens with longing, grasps the Guru's word, longs to act exactly as instructed, and never offends — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the four — right motive, longing-to-hear, exact action, no offence — which is the hardest for you to sustain?

## SUTRA 9.3.3 — Vinaya Even Toward Senior Monks (Even If Younger in Initiation)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रायणिएसु विणयं पउंजे, डहरा वि य जे परियायजेट्ठा ।
णीयत्तणे वट्टइ सच्चवाई, उवायवं वक्ककरे स पुज्जो ॥९.३.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.3 ||
rāyaṇiesu viṇayaṃ pauṃje, ḍaharā vi ya je pariyāyajeṭṭhā |
ṇīyattaṇe vaṭṭai saccavāī, uvāyavaṃ vakkakare sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The disciple who) practises vinaya toward those richer-in-qualities (ratnadhika); (and toward) those even young (in age) but senior in initiation (paryaya-jyeshtha); (he who) lives in lowness (humility), is truthful, attentive, and a doer of (the Guru's) word — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **रायणिएसु** (rāyaṇiesu) = toward the ratnadhikas (those rich in spiritual jewels — in the spiritual community, those higher in qualities or initiation)
- **विणयं पउंजे** (viṇayaṃ pauṃje) = practises vinaya
- **डहरा वि य** (ḍaharā vi ya) = even (toward) those young (in age)
- **जे परियायजेट्ठा** (je pariyāyajeṭṭhā) = (who are) senior in initiation (paryaya-jyeshtha — older in monastic seniority)
- **णीयत्तणे वट्टइ** (ṇīyattaṇe vaṭṭai) = lives in lowness (humility, the state of being humble before the senior)
- **सच्चवाई** (saccavāī) = truthful
- **उवायवं** (uvāyavaṃ) = attentive (with proper means / careful service)
- **वक्ककरे** (vakkakare) = doer of (the Guru's) word
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

A subtle and important sutra. In the monastic order, seniority is reckoned not by age but by *paryaya* — the number of years since one's diksha (initiation). A monk initiated at twenty may be the senior of a monk initiated at sixty, if the first was initiated earlier. The sutra warns: do not be misled by externals. A young-looking monk who took diksha early is your spiritual senior; you must show him vinaya regardless of his age.

The four qualities of the worship-worthy in this gatha are: (1) **vinaya** even toward those younger in age but senior in diksha, (2) living in **nichattana** — humility — at all times, (3) being **satyavadi** — truthful, and (4) being **vakkakare** — a doer of the Guru's word, attentive to the proper means. Such a monk does not say "but he is younger than me!" — he sees only the spiritual seniority and bows.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who practises vinaya even toward monks younger in age but senior in initiation — who lives in humility, speaks truthfully, attends carefully, and does the Guru's word — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If spiritual seniority is measured by something other than age — what would change about whom you bow to?

## SUTRA 9.3.4 — Contentment with Pure Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अण्णायउंछं चरइ विसुद्धं, जवणट्ठया समुयाणं च णिच्चं ।
अलद्धुयं णो परिदेविज्जा, लद्धुं ण विकत्थयइ स पुज्जो ॥९.३.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.4 ||
aṇṇāyauṃchaṃ carai visuddhaṃ, javaṇaṭṭhayā samuyāṇaṃ ca ṇiccaṃ |
aladdhuyaṃ ṇo paridevijjā, laddhuṃ ṇa vikatthayai sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The disciple who) lives by pure (faultless) alms (collected) from unfamiliar (households) (alms gathered without prior arrangement), always doing samudanika (begging from many houses), for the sake of sustaining (samyama-yatra) — when (he) does not get (alms), does not lament; when (he) does get them, does not boast — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अण्णाय-उंछं** (aṇṇāya-uṃchaṃ) = (a little from each of) unfamiliar (households) — *ajnata bhiksha* (where the household has not been pre-informed) and *uncha* (taking a little from many houses)
- **विसुद्धं** (visuddhaṃ) = pure / faultless (free from the 42 doshas of bhiksha)
- **चरइ** (carai) = goes (for alms) / lives by
- **जवणट्ठया** (javaṇaṭṭhayā) = for the sake of sustenance (yapana — to keep the body going for samyama)
- **समुयाणं** (samuyāṇaṃ) = samudanika begging (going to many houses, not depending on one)
- **णिच्चं** (ṇiccaṃ) = always
- **अलद्धुयं णो परिदेविज्जा** (aladdhuyaṃ ṇo paridevijjā) = when not received, does not lament / wail / become sorrowful
- **लद्धुं ण विकत्थयइ** (laddhuṃ ṇa vikatthayai) = when received, does not boast / praise (himself or the giver)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra paints the worship-worthy disciple's relationship to food. Three qualities are named:

(1) **Ajnata-uncha** — the disciple goes to households that have not been pre-informed of his coming (so no special preparations are made on his behalf — which would defile the alms with the dosha of *adhakarmika*, food prepared *for* the monk). And he takes only a little from each of many houses, never burdening any one householder.

(2) **Vishuddha** — the alms are pure, free from the 42 doshas of bhiksha (the various ways alms can be defiled by the source, the preparation, or the manner of receiving).

(3) **Yapanartha** — the purpose is sustenance only — to keep the body alive for the practice of samyama. There is no thought of taste, no thought of fullness, no thought of pleasure.

And the heart of the sutra: when alms are *not* received, the disciple does not wail "what a wretched day; I am unfortunate; this village has no good people." And when alms *are* received, he does not boast "see how blessed I am; my merit brought me such fine food." Both reactions reveal attachment to outcome; the true monk is equanimous in either case. This equanimity, the Sutrakara says, is what makes him worship-worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who collects pure alms from many unfamiliar households, only for sustenance — who neither laments when he gets nothing nor boasts when he gets plenty — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something you needed does not come, do you wail? When it does, do you boast? What would equanimity look like in either case?

## SUTRA 9.3.5 — Contentment with Lodging, Bedding, Food, Water

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संथारसिज्जासणभत्तपाणे अप्पिच्छया अइलाभे वि संते ।
जो एवमप्पाणभितोसएज्जा, संतोसपाहण्णरए स पुज्जो ॥९.३.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.5 ||
saṃthārasijjāsaṇabhattapāṇe appicchayā ailābhe vi saṃte |
jo evamappāṇabhitosaejjā, saṃtosapāhaṇṇarae sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In (the matter of) the bedding (santhara), the lodging (shayya), the seat (asana), the food (bhakta), and the water (pana) — even when there is great gain — (the disciple who) keeps (his) wishes few; he who thus keeps himself satisfied, principally devoted to contentment — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संथार-सिज्जा-आसण-भत्त-पाणे** (saṃthāra-sijjā-āsaṇa-bhatta-pāṇe) = in (the matter of) bedding, lodging, seat, food, and water
- **अइलाभे वि संते** (ailābhe vi saṃte) = even when there is great gain (atilabha)
- **अप्पिच्छया** (appicchayā) = (with) few wishes (alpa-iccha)
- **जो एवं अप्पाणं अभितोसएज्जा** (jo evaṃ appāṇaṃ abhitosaejjā) = he who thus keeps himself satisfied
- **संतोसपाहण्णरए** (saṃtosapāhaṇṇarae) = principally devoted to contentment (santosha-pradhana — making santosha the dominant quality)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra extends the previous one from food to all five basic monastic supports: bedding, lodging, seat, food, and water. Even when these come abundantly, the disciple keeps his wishes few. He does not say "let me take a little extra since it is offered" or "this is a good time to stock up." His desires remain cut down to the bare requirement of samyama.

The phrase **santosha-pradhana** is precise — *santosha* (contentment) is the **pradhana** (chief, principal) quality of his nature. He is not a person with many qualities including occasional contentment; he is *fundamentally a contented being*, and other qualities flow from that core.

The text observes: santosha is the first sign of true vairagya. Without renunciation, the joy of contentment cannot be experienced; without contentment, the renunciate cannot maintain his renunciation. The vairagi soul progressively reduces his needs; the attached soul progressively expands them. The worship-worthy disciple has chosen the first path.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who, even when bedding, lodging, seat, food, and water are abundantly available, keeps his wishes few — and makes contentment the dominant quality of his life — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When abundance is available, you can either expand your wants to match it or keep them small. Which do you usually do?

## SUTRA 9.3.6 — Bearing the Arrow-Sharp Words

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सक्का सहेउं आसाइ कंटया, अओमया उच्छहया णरेणं ।
अणासए जो उ सहिज्ज कंटए, वईमए कण्णसरे स पुज्जो ॥९.३.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.6 ||
sakkā sahetuṃ āsāi kaṃṭayā, aomayā ucchahayā ṇareṇaṃ |
aṇāsae jo u sahijja kaṃṭae, vaīmae kaṇṇasare sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It is possible (sakka) for a man, when prompted by (worldly) hope (asha), to bear iron-thorns (arrows). But he who bears, without any hope, the arrow-sharp word-thorns piercing the ears — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सक्का सहेउं** (sakkā sahetuṃ) = (it is) possible to bear
- **आसाइ** (āsāi) = (when prompted) by hope (asha — for wealth, victory, fame)
- **कंटया अओमया** (kaṃṭayā aomayā) = iron thorns / arrows (ayomaya kantaka)
- **उच्छहया णरेणं** (ucchahayā ṇareṇaṃ) = (by) a man (filled with) energetic resolve
- **अणासए** (aṇāsae) = without any (worldly) hope (anasha)
- **जो उ सहिज्ज** (jo u sahijja) = but he who bears
- **कंटए** (kaṃṭae) = the thorns
- **वईमए** (vaīmae) = (made of) speech (vag-maya)
- **कण्णसरे** (kaṇṇasare) = piercing the ears (karna-shara, lit. "ear-arrow") or "ear-sharp" — words sharp like arrows aimed at the ear
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

A penetrating comparison. A worldly man, hoping for wealth or victory, can endure being pierced by literal iron arrows in battle — the hope of the prize gives him the strength to bear the wound. But the spiritual seeker who bears, *without any hope of reward whatsoever*, the arrow-sharp words that others fire at him — that man is greater than the warrior.

The reason: an arrow pierces the body and the wound heals; a sharp word pierces the heart, and the wound becomes the seed of long-lasting enmity (the next sutra will spell this out). To bear physical pain is one thing; to bear words that cut into the heart, while keeping the heart steady, is another. The first requires bodily courage; the second requires inner courage of a much rarer order.

The Sutrakara is preparing the disciple for the inevitable reality of community life: people will say cruel things. The worship-worthy monk does not retaliate, does not retreat, and does not even *hope* for any consolation. He simply bears.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A worldly man can endure iron arrows in battle when hope of the prize drives him. But the disciple who bears arrow-sharp words piercing his ears, without any hope at all — he is worship-worthy.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

It takes more inner strength to bear an unkind word with no hope of reward than to bear a wound for a prize. Why?

## SUTRA 9.3.7 — The Permanent Wound of Harsh Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मुहुत्तदुक्खा उ हवंति कंटया, अओमया ते वि तओ सुउद्धरा ।
वाया दुरुत्ताणि दुरुद्धराणि, वेराणुबंधीणि महब्भयाणि ॥९.३.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.7 ||
muhuttadukkhā u havaṃti kaṃṭayā, aomayā te vi tao suuddharā |
vāyā duruttāṇi duruddharāṇi, verāṇubaṃdhīṇi mahabbhayāṇi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Iron thorns (arrows) cause only momentary pain, and they too can be easily extracted. But harsh words are hard to extract, generate continuing enmity, and are causes of great fear (great future suffering).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अओमया कंटया** (aomayā kaṃṭayā) = iron thorns / arrows
- **मुहुत्त-दुक्खा** (muhutta-dukkhā) = (cause) only momentary pain (muhurta-duhkha)
- **तओ सुउद्धरा** (tao suuddharā) = and they too are easily extracted (suudhara — easy to pull out)
- **वाया दुरुत्ताणि** (vāyā duruttāṇi) = (but) harsh-spoken words
- **दुरुद्धराणि** (duruddharāṇi) = are hard to extract (durudhara)
- **वेराणुबंधीणि** (verāṇubaṃdhīṇi) = generating continuing enmity (vairanubandhini — binding feud across lives)
- **महब्भयाणि** (mahabbhayāṇi) = (and are) of great fear / cause of great future suffering (mahabhaya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra explains why bearing harsh words is so much harder than bearing physical wounds. The metallic arrow gives momentary pain (muhurta-duhkha — pain for an hour or so), and once extracted, the body heals. But the verbal arrow lodges in the heart, and unlike a metal arrow, it cannot be physically pulled out. It festers; it generates the pattern of *vairanubandha* — continuing enmity that follows the soul from life to life; and it becomes the seed of mahabhaya — great future suffering.

The Sutrakara is teaching the disciple two things at once. First: when others speak harsh words to you, understand that you are receiving something worse than an arrow, and bear it with corresponding inner strength. Second: never speak harsh words to others, because you would be inflicting on them a wound that would persist across lifetimes and bind both of you in karmic enmity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Iron arrows cause only brief pain and can be pulled out easily. But harsh words cannot be extracted, they create lasting enmity, and they are causes of great future suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a harsh word is worse than an arrow — for both the speaker and the hearer — what does that demand of how you speak?

## SUTRA 9.3.8 — Bearing Verbal Wounds with the Heroism of Dharma

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समावयंता वयणाभिघाया, कण्णंगया दुम्मणियं जणंति ।
धम्मो त्ति किच्चा परमग्गसूरे, जिइंदिए जो सहइ स पुज्जो ॥९.३.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.8 ||
samāvayaṃtā vayaṇābhighāyā, kaṇṇaṃgayā dummaṇiyaṃ jaṇaṃti |
dhammo tti kiccā paramaggasūre, jiiṃdie jo sahai sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The blows of (harsh) words, falling (upon him), entering the ears, generate ill-feeling (in the mind). But the supreme hero (paramagra-shura), who has conquered the senses — taking it as "this is my dharma" — he who bears (such words), is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **समावयंता** (samāvayaṃtā) = falling upon (him); coming (from all directions)
- **वयणाभिघाया** (vayaṇābhighāyā) = the blows of words (vachana-abhighata)
- **कण्णंगया** (kaṇṇaṃgayā) = entering the ears
- **दुम्मणियं जणंति** (dummaṇiyaṃ jaṇaṃti) = generate ill-feeling (daurmanasya — sorrow / disturbance of mind)
- **धम्मो त्ति किच्चा** (dhammo tti kiccā) = having taken (it as) "this is dharma" (dharma is what i have to do)
- **परमग्ग-सूरे** (paramagga-sūre) = the supreme hero (parama-agra-shura — "supreme front-rank warrior")
- **जिइंदिए** (jiiṃdie) = the conqueror of the senses (jitendriya)
- **जो सहइ** (jo sahai) = he who bears
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra now turns from the *fact* of verbal wounding to the disciple's *response*. Yes, harsh words enter the ears and naturally cause inner distress. The point is not to be made of stone — the disciple feels the pain. But the worship-worthy disciple does two things in that moment.

First, he reframes: **dhammo tti kiccha** — "this is my dharma." That is, he treats the act of bearing the verbal wound *not* as a passive endurance, but as the active practice of his dharma. Bearing patiently *is* the dharma, in this moment. Once it is named as dharma, the bearing becomes meaningful, even sweet.

Second, he summons heroism: **paramagra-shura**. The Sthanaanga Sutra distinguishes four kinds of heroes — the war-hero, the austerity-hero, the charity-hero, and the dharma-hero. Of these the dharma-hero is supreme, because his battlefield is the harder one (his own inner self), his enemy is invisible, and his prize is moksha. The disciple who bears verbal wounds with this kind of heroism — neither flinching, nor retaliating, nor hating — has earned the title of *paramagra-shura*. And such a disciple is worship-worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Harsh words falling on the ears naturally cause inner distress. But the supreme hero — the conqueror of the senses — who bears them by saying "this is my dharma" — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What changes when you reframe an act of endurance as "this is my dharma" rather than "this is my misfortune"?

## SUTRA 9.3.9 — The Fourfold Vinaya of Speech

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अवण्णायं च परम्मुहस्स, पच्चक्खओ पडिणीयं च भासं ।
ओहारिणिं अप्पियकारिणिं च, भासं ण भासिज्ज सया स पुज्जो ॥९.३.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.9 ||
avaṇṇāyaṃ ca parammuhassa, paccakkhao paḍiṇīyaṃ ca bhāsaṃ |
ohāriṇiṃ appiyakāriṇiṃ ca, bhāsaṃ ṇa bhāsijja sayā sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(He who) never speaks the slander (avarna-vada) of one (who is) absent (parangmukha); nor the contradicting (pratyaniika) speech to one face-to-face; nor the categorically-asserting (avadharini) speech (about uncertain matters); nor the unpleasant-causing (apriyakarini) speech — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **परम्मुहस्स** (parammuhassa) = of one (who is) absent (paranga-mukha — turned away, behind one's back)
- **अवण्णायं** (avaṇṇāyaṃ) = the slander / blame (avarna-vada)
- **पच्चक्खओ** (paccakkhao) = face-to-face (pratyaksha)
- **पडिणीयं भासं** (paḍiṇīyaṃ bhāsaṃ) = contradicting / hostile speech (pratyaniika)
- **ओहारिणिं** (ohāriṇiṃ) = (categorically) asserting (avadharini — making a final pronouncement on a matter that is uncertain or unknown)
- **अप्पियकारिणिं** (appiyakāriṇiṃ) = causing displeasure (apriyakarini)
- **भासं ण भासिज्ज सया** (bhāsaṃ ṇa bhāsijja sayā) = should never speak such speech
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sutrakara now lists four kinds of speech the worship-worthy disciple never uses: (1) **avarnavada** — speaking ill of someone behind their back; (2) **pratyanika-bhasha** — speaking words of contradiction, opposition, or hostility to someone in their presence; (3) **avadharini** — making categorical pronouncements about matters that are uncertain or about the future, where one cannot truly know; and (4) **apriyakarini** — speech that causes displeasure, harshness, harm, or pain.

The four together amount to a comprehensive vinaya of speech. The disciple does not slander; does not pick fights; does not pronounce dogmatically; does not wound. The Jain teaching of *bhasha-samiti* (carefulness in speech) is given concrete form here. The worship-worthy disciple's speech is, by these four exclusions, made naturally pleasant, accurate, peaceful, and harmless.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who never speaks slander behind anyone's back, never contradicts hostilely face-to-face, never makes dogmatic pronouncements about uncertain matters, and never says anything that causes displeasure — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the four — slander, contradiction, dogmatism, hurt — which slips out of your speech most often unnoticed?

## SUTRA 9.3.10 — The Equanimous Disciple Free of Six Faults

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अलोलुए अक्कुहए अमाई, अपिसुणे यावि अदीणवित्ती ।
णो भावए णो विय भावियप्पा, अकोउहल्ले य सया स पुज्जो ॥९.३.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.10 ||
alolue akkuhae amāī, apisuṇe yāvi adīṇavittī |
ṇo bhāvae ṇo viya bhāviyappā, akouhalle ya sayā sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(He who is) free of greedy craving (for taste etc.), free of (mantra/tantra) tricks, free of deceit, free of slander, of un-pitiable (un-broken) bearing, neither praising himself before others nor allowing others to praise him, and always free of (unspiritual) curiosity — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अलोलुए** (alolue) = not greedy / not given to lolupata (especially in food, taste, comfort)
- **अक्कुहए** (akkuhae) = free of *kuhaka* — free of mantra-tantra trickery, sleight-of-hand showmanship to impress people
- **अमाई** (amāī) = free of *maya* (deceit)
- **अपिसुणे** (apisuṇe) = not a slanderer (a-pishuna)
- **अदीणवित्ती** (adīṇavittī) = of un-pitiable bearing (a-dina-vritti — not adopting a pitiable / cringing demeanour to beg)
- **णो भावए** (ṇo bhāvae) = does not have others (his praise) cultivated (does not get others to praise him)
- **णो विय भावियप्पा** (ṇo viya bhāviyappā) = does not himself cultivate self-praise (self-promotion); also: not influenced by others' adulation
- **अकोउहल्ले** (akouhalle) = free of (unspiritual) curiosity (a-kautuhala — about jokes, spectacles, novelties)
- **सया** (sayā) = always
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

A long catalog of negative qualities — what the worship-worthy is *not*. Six are named:

(1) **Alolue** — not greedy, especially not greedy for tasty food, soft clothing, comfortable lodging. The lolupa monk is constantly attached to small pleasures; the worship-worthy is free.

(2) **Akkuhae** — does not perform mantra-tantra demonstrations, fake miracles, sleight-of-hand to impress the laity. Such tricks are common temptations for monks who want followers; the worship-worthy refuses them.

(3) **Amaii** — no deceit, no pretending to be what one is not.

(4) **Apisuṇe** — no slander, no whispering against fellow-monks behind their backs.

(5) **Adinavitti** — no pitiable demeanour. The monk does not adopt a beggar's cringe to manipulate sympathy. The two senses are: (a) does not become dejected when faced with hardship, (b) does not adopt a pitiable manner to extract alms.

(6) **No bhavae no viya bhaviyappa** — does not get others to praise him, does not engage in self-praise. (The phrase has been read in three ways by the commentators: (a) does not influence others with bad bhava and is not influenced by others' bhava; (b) does not get others to praise him and does not praise himself; (c) does not frighten others and is not frightened by others.)

(7) **Akouhalle** — free of unspiritual curiosity, about novelties, spectacles, jokes, gossip. Curiosity is a pull on the mind toward objects irrelevant to liberation.

A monk free of all these is naturally worship-worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who is not greedy, does not perform fake miracles, has no deceit, no slander, no cringing demeanour, who neither praises himself nor solicits praise, and who is free of unspiritual curiosity — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Look at these seven and ask honestly: which one is your blind spot?

## SUTRA 9.3.11 — The Equanimous Disciple Who Knows Himself

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गुणेहिं साहू अगुणेहिं असाहू, गिण्हाहि साहूगुण मुंचऽसाहू ।
वियाणिया अप्पगमप्पएणं, जो रागदोसेहिं समो स पुज्जो ॥९.३.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.11 ||
guṇehiṃ sāhū aguṇehiṃ asāhū, giṇhāhi sāhūguṇa muṃcasāhū |
viyāṇiyā appagamappaeṇaṃ, jo rāgadosehiṃ samo sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

By (his) qualities (guna) one is a sadhu; by (his) absence-of-qualities (a-guna) one is an a-sadhu. (Therefore, O disciple,) take up the qualities of the sadhu, and abandon those of the a-sadhu. Having known the self by the self — he who is equanimous in (matters of) attraction and aversion, is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गुणेहिं साहू** (guṇehiṃ sāhū) = (one is) a sadhu by qualities
- **अगुणेहिं असाहू** (aguṇehiṃ asāhū) = an a-sadhu by absence-of-qualities (faults)
- **गिण्हाहि साहूगुण** (giṇhāhi sāhūguṇa) = take up the qualities of the sadhu
- **मुंच असाहू** (muṃca asāhū) = abandon (the qualities) of the a-sadhu (the faults)
- **वियाणिया** (viyāṇiyā) = having known
- **अप्पगं अप्पएणं** (appagaṃ appaeṇaṃ) = the self by the self (atma by atma — through self-introspection)
- **जो रागदोसेहिं समो** (jo rāgadosehiṃ samo) = who is equanimous in (matters of) raga (attraction) and dvesha (aversion)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is a charged sutra. First, it makes a definitional point: a sadhu is not someone who wears the robe; a sadhu is one who has *qualities* (vinaya etc.). And an *asadhu* is one who has the opposite (anger, pride, deceit etc.) — *even if* he wears the robe. Sadhutva is an internal matter, not an external label.

The Sutrakara then issues a direct command in second person: **ginhahi sahu-guna munca asahu** — "Take up the qualities of the sadhu! Abandon those of the asadhu!" The disciple is to actively cultivate the sadhu-qualities and actively abandon the asadhu-qualities. This is conscious self-construction.

But how is this done? **Viyaniya appagam appaenam** — "by knowing the self through the self." The disciple turns inward. He observes which qualities he has and which faults he has. He is honest about both. This is *atma-nirikshana* (self-introspection) — the practice that the Sutrakara puts at the centre of the path.

The fruit of this self-knowledge is **rag-dvesha-sama** — equanimity in matters of attraction and aversion. The disciple no longer reacts with raga to pleasant things and dvesha to unpleasant; he sees both as karmic flows passing through, and remains balanced. Such a disciple, at last, is worship-worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A sadhu is one with qualities; the absence of qualities makes one an asadhu. So take up the sadhu-qualities and drop the rest. The disciple who has known himself through himself, and remains equanimous in attraction and aversion — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you "know yourself by yourself" — what qualities do you find that you would honestly want to take up, and what faults to drop?

## SUTRA 9.3.12 — Despises No One, Free of Pride and Anger

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव डहरं च महल्लगं वा, इत्थिं पुमं पव्वइयं गिहिं वा ।
णो हीलए णो विय खिंसइज्जा, थंभं च कोहं च चए स पुज्जो ॥९.३.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.12 ||
taheva ḍaharaṃ ca mahallagaṃ vā, itthiṃ pumaṃ pavvaiyaṃ gihiṃ vā |
ṇo hīlae ṇo viya khiṃsaijjā, thaṃbhaṃ ca kohaṃ ca cae sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The disciple who) does not despise nor verbally insult — whether (the other be) a child or an elder, a woman or a man, an initiated (monk/nun) or a householder — and who has abandoned stiffness (pride) and anger — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = in that very way / similarly
- **डहरं च महल्लगं वा** (ḍaharaṃ ca mahallagaṃ vā) = (whether) a child (dahara) or an elder (mahallaga)
- **इत्थिं पुमं** (itthiṃ pumaṃ) = a woman or a man (stri or purusha)
- **पव्वइयं गिहिं वा** (pavvaiyaṃ gihiṃ vā) = an initiated one (pravrajita) or a householder (grihastha)
- **णो हीलए** (ṇo hīlae) = does not despise / mock once
- **णो विय खिंसइज्जा** (ṇo viya khiṃsaijjā) = nor (despises) repeatedly / does not shame, embarrass, ridicule
- **थंभं च कोहं च चए** (thaṃbhaṃ ca kohaṃ ca cae) = abandons stiffness (pride) and anger
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

The disciple's universal regard. He does not despise *anyone* — not based on age (child or elder), not based on gender (woman or man), not based on status (monk or householder). The Sutrakara names six categories specifically to leave no escape: every conceivable other person falls under one of these, and none is to be despised.

The two verbs are precise. **Hilae** is to despise once, to look down on. **Khinsaijja** is to shame, to ridicule repeatedly, to embarrass publicly. The worship-worthy disciple abstains from both.

And the root causes are named: **thambha** (the stiffness of pride that thinks oneself above others) and **koha** (the anger that wants to wound others). Both of these the disciple abandons. The Sutrakara is making it clear: contempt is always a symptom of pride or anger; cure those, and contempt vanishes. The disciple who has done this is fit to be worshipped.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple who never despises anyone — child or elder, woman or man, monk or householder — and who has abandoned both pride and anger — he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Whom do you secretly despise? And what would change if you saw that contempt as just pride or anger wearing a mask?

## SUTRA 9.3.13 — Worshipping the Worship-Worthy Acharya

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे माणिया सययं माणयंति, जत्तेण कण्णं व णिवेसयंति ।
ते माणए माणरिहे तवस्सी, जिइंदिए सच्चरए स पुज्जो ॥९.३.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.13 ||
je māṇiyā sayayaṃ māṇayaṃti, jatteṇa kaṇṇaṃ va ṇivesayaṃti |
te māṇae māṇarihe tavassī, jiiṃdie saccarae sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The Acharyas, who themselves are honoured and) honour (their disciples) in return continually — placing (the disciple) in (his) proper place with effort, just as (a father) settles (his) daughter (in a worthy household). Those (Acharyas) — worthy of honour — the austerity-practising, sense-conquering, truth-devoted disciple should honour. (Such a disciple) is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे** (je) = those (Acharyas) who
- **माणिया** (māṇiyā) = honoured (by the disciples through service / vinaya)
- **सययं माणयंति** (sayayaṃ māṇayaṃti) = continually honour (the disciples in return — by teaching them, training them, advancing them through the curriculum)
- **जत्तेण** (jatteṇa) = with effort / care (yatnena)
- **कण्णं व** (kaṇṇaṃ va) = like a daughter (kanya)
- **णिवेसयंति** (ṇivesayaṃti) = settle (the disciple in a fit station — as a father settles his daughter in a fit household)
- **ते** (te) = those (Acharyas)
- **माणए** (māṇae) = should honour (the disciple should honour them)
- **माणरिहे** (māṇarihe) = (the Acharyas) worthy of honour
- **तवस्सी** (tavassī) = the austerity-practising (disciple)
- **जिइंदिए** (jiiṃdie) = the conqueror of senses
- **सच्चरए** (saccarae) = devoted to truth (satyarata)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he (the disciple who honours such Acharyas) is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

The image is touching. A father raises his daughter with love, watches over her, and at the appropriate time finds her a fit husband and a fit household — and settles her there with care. He is not finished with her at the wedding; his concern continues. So too the Acharya: he raises the disciple, trains him, and at the appropriate time settles him in his proper spiritual station — a station worthy of his abilities.

The principle: the relationship between Guru and disciple is reciprocal. The disciple offers vinaya; the Acharya offers training, advancement, and care. Neither is one-directional. The disciple who honours the Acharya, sees the Acharya's ongoing care for him, and responds with continued honour and ardent practice — that disciple is worship-worthy.

The disciple's three qualities are named: **tavassi** (practising austerity), **jitendriya** (conqueror of the senses), **saccarae** (devoted to truth). With these three qualities, his honouring of the Acharya is genuine, and his worth to be honoured himself is established.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The Acharyas, who are honoured and who honour their disciples in return — settling them in their right station with effort, like a father settling his daughter in a fit household — those Acharyas the austere, sense-conquering, truth-devoted disciple should honour. Such a disciple is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a true teacher invests in you the way a father invests in his daughter's future, what does that ask of your honouring of him?

## SUTRA 9.3.14 — Hearing the Subhashita and Practising the Five Mahavratas

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेसिं गुरूणं गुणसायराणं, सोच्चाण मेहावि सुभासियाइं ।
चरे मुणी पंचरए तिगुत्तो, चउक्कसायावगए स पुज्जो ॥९.३.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.14 ||
tesiṃ gurūṇaṃ guṇasāyarāṇaṃ, soccāṇa mehāvi subhāsiyāiṃ |
care muṇī paṃcarae tigutto, cauk­kasāyāvagae sa pujjo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having heard the well-spoken (instructions) of those Gurus who are oceans of qualities, the wise monk should conduct himself (delighting in) the five (mahavratas), guarded by the three (guptis), and free of the four kashayas — he is worship-worthy.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेसिं गुरूणं** (tesiṃ gurūṇaṃ) = of those Gurus
- **गुणसायराणं** (guṇasāyarāṇaṃ) = (who are) oceans of qualities (guna-sagara)
- **सोच्चाण** (soccāṇa) = having heard
- **मेहावि** (mehāvi) = the wise (monk)
- **सुभासियाइं** (subhāsiyāiṃ) = the well-spoken (subhashita instructions)
- **चरे मुणी** (care muṇī) = the monk should conduct himself
- **पंचरए** (paṃcarae) = (delighting) in the five (mahavratas — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, brahmacharya, non-possession)
- **तिगुत्तो** (tigutto) = guarded by the three (guptis — of mind, speech, body)
- **चउक्कसाय-अवगए** (cauk­kasāya-avagae) = free of the four kashayas (anger, pride, deceit, greed)
- **स पुज्जो** (sa pujjo) = he is worship-worthy

### 5. COMMENTARY

This near-final gatha gathers the entire monastic ideal into a single line. The monk is to: (1) hear the well-spoken words of the Gurus who are oceans of qualities, (2) practice the five great vows (mahavratas) — ahimsa, satya, asteya, brahmacharya, aparigraha; (3) guard the three avenues (guptis) of mind, speech, and body; and (4) be free of the four kashayas — krodha (anger), mana (pride), maya (deceit), lobha (greed).

These are the standard formulations of the Jain monastic life. The Sutrakara is showing that the worship-worthy disciple is not someone with a unique or special practice; he is someone who *fully* practises the standard practice. The standard, when actually lived, is itself extraordinary. The disciple who hears the subhashita with reverence finds the strength to practise the five vows, the three guptis, and the conquest of the four kashayas. And this disciple is worship-worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Having heard the well-spoken instructions of the Gurus who are oceans of qualities, the wise monk should live by the five great vows, the three guptis, and freedom from the four kashayas — and he is worthy of worship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The standard practice — fully lived — is the extraordinary life. Why do we so often look elsewhere for "the special practice"?

## SUTRA 9.3.15 — The Final Fruit: The Moon Emerging from the Clouds

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गुरुमिह सययं पडियरिय मुणी, जिणमयणिउणे अभिगमकुसले ।
धुणिय रयमलं पुरेकडं, भासुरमउलं गइं गओ ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥९.३.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.3.15 ||
gurumiha sayayaṃ paḍiyariya muṇī, jiṇamayaṇiuṇe abhigamakusale |
dhuṇiya rayamalaṃ purekaḍaṃ, bhāsuramaulaṃ gaiṃ gao || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who continually attends to the Guru in this (world), who is skilled in the doctrines of the Jina (jinamati-nipuna), who is expert in (right) approach (abhigama-kushala) — having shaken off the karmic dust accumulated from before, has gone (and continues to go) to the radiant unequalled destination. — Thus do I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गुरुमिह सययं पडियरिय** (gurumiha sayayaṃ paḍiyariya) = continually attending the Guru in this (world) (paricharya — service through proper means)
- **मुणी** (muṇī) = the monk
- **जिण-मय-णिउणे** (jiṇa-maya-ṇiuṇe) = skilled in the doctrines of the Jina (jinamata-nipuna)
- **अभिगम-कुसले** (abhigama-kusale) = expert in (right) approach / treatment / behaviour (abhigama-kushala — knowing how to receive Acharyas, treat fellow monks, conduct oneself in lokottara matters)
- **धुणिय** (dhuṇiya) = having shaken off (dhutva)
- **रयमलं** (rayamalaṃ) = the karmic dust and stain (raja-mala)
- **पुरेकडं** (purekaḍaṃ) = previously accumulated (purakrita — done in past lives)
- **भासुरम्** (bhāsuram) = radiant, brilliant
- **अउलं** (aulaṃ) = unequalled (atula)
- **गइं गओ** (gaiṃ gao) = has attained (and continues to attain) the destination (siddhagati)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = thus I declare

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing gatha of the third uddesha. The disciple who has, throughout this chapter, been described as worship-worthy — what becomes of him? The Sutrakara now seals the answer: he reaches the radiant unequalled destination — moksha.

Three qualities of the disciple are emphasised: (1) **paridyariya** — continual service / attendance on the Guru, with full understanding of the proper means of service; (2) **jinamata-nipuna** — skilled in the doctrines of the Jina, having grasped the Jain teaching deeply; (3) **abhigama-kushala** — expert in the right approach, the right behaviour, the right etiquette toward Acharyas, fellow monks, and laity (lokottara conduct).

By this combination of service, doctrinal mastery, and behavioural skill, the disciple **dhuniya rayamalam purekadam** — shakes off the karmic dust accumulated through countless past lives — like a man shaking dust from his clothing. And he attains **bhasuram aulam gaim** — the radiant, unequalled destination, which is the Siddha-loka.

The verb tense is past (**gao**), but the commentators read it as inclusive of past, present, and future: "such a disciple has gone (in past times), goes (now), and will go (in future) to the supreme destination." The path is real; many have walked it; many are walking it; many will walk it. The closing **tti bemi** is the formal seal.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk who continually serves the Guru, masters the Jina's doctrines, and is skilled in proper conduct — shakes off the karmic dust of past lives and reaches the radiant, unequalled destination of liberation. Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Service to a Guru, mastery of the teaching, skill in conduct — three legs of one stool. Which is your strongest leg, which your weakest?

## UDDESHA 4 — The Four Samadhis

The fourth and final uddesha completes the chapter (and indeed the principal teaching of the Dashvaikalik Sutra) by enumerating the four samadhis — the four kinds of inner equipoise into which the entire monastic life pours itself. The opening gatha lists them: **Vinaya-samadhi**, **Shruta-samadhi**, **Tapa-samadhi**, and **Achara-samadhi**. The second gatha sub-divides each samadhi into four parts (4 × 4 = 16 sub-points). And the closing gatha promises the final fruit — liberation, or in the case of remaining karma, birth as a great Mahardhika deva.

A unique feature of this uddesha is that the prose-formulae of the four samadhis (sutras 1, 2, 3, 4, 5) are followed by verses (gathas) that summarise each samadhi's content — making this uddesha both prose and verse, with seven numbered sutras in total.

The structure of the four samadhis is parallel: each consists of *four* sub-points, and each is sealed by an injunction that the practice should not be undertaken for *this-world reward, other-world reward, fame, or any motive other than the destruction of karma* (with the partial exception of vinaya-samadhi, whose four points concern attentive listening, scriptural understanding, freedom from pride). The architecture is rigorous and beautiful.

## SUTRA 9.4.1 — The Four Samadhis Named

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुयं मे आउसं ! तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं — इह खलु थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं चत्तारि विणयसमाहिट्ठाणा पण्णत्ता । कयरे खलु ते थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं चत्तारि विणयसमाहि-ट्ठाणा पण्णत्ता ? इमे खलु ते थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं चत्तारि विणयसमाहिट्ठाणा पण्णत्ता । तंजहा — विणयसमाहि, सुयसमाहि, तवसमाहि, आयारसमाहि ।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus have I heard, O Long-lived (Jambu)! By that Bhagavan (Mahavira) it has been declared thus — In this (Jain order), indeed, by the venerable Sthavira-bhagavants, four stations of vinaya-samadhi have been propounded. What, indeed, are those four stations of vinaya-samadhi propounded by the Sthavira-bhagavants? These, indeed, are the four stations of vinaya-samadhi propounded by the Sthavira-bhagavants — namely: vinaya-samadhi, shruta-samadhi, tapas-samadhi, achara-samadhi.

In vinaya, in shruta, in tapas, and in achara — the wise (panditas), the conquerors of the senses, ever take inner delight (in their atman).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुयं मे आउसं** (suyaṃ me āusaṃ) = "Thus have I heard, O long-lived (Jambu)!" — the standard opening of Sudharmaswami transmitting from Mahavira to Jambuswami
- **तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं** (teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ) = it has been thus declared by that Bhagavan (Mahavira)
- **इह खलु** (iha khalu) = in this (Jain teaching), indeed
- **थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं** (therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ) = by the venerable Sthaviras (the elder, mature, experienced monks)
- **चत्तारि विणयसमाहि-ट्ठाणा पण्णत्ता** (cattāri viṇayasamāhi-ṭṭhāṇā paṇṇattā) = four stations of vinaya-samadhi have been propounded
- **तंजहा** (taṃjahā) = namely
- **विणयसमाहि, सुयसमाहि, तवसमाहि, आयारसमाहि** (viṇayasamāhi, suyasamāhi, tavasamāhi, āyārasamāhi) = vinaya-samadhi, shruta-samadhi, tapas-samadhi, achara-samadhi
- **विणए सुए य तवे आयारे** (viṇae sue ya tave āyāre) = in vinaya, in shruta, in tapas, in achara
- **णिच्चं पंडिया** (ṇiccaṃ paṃḍiyā) = always the wise (panditas)
- **अभिरामयंति अप्पाणं** (abhirāmayaṃti appāṇaṃ) = take delight in / make their atman delight in
- **जे भवंति जिइंदिया** (je bhavaṃti jiiṃdiyā) = those who are conquerors of the senses

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth uddesha opens with the formal Sudharmaswami-to-Jambuswami transmission formula (*suyaṃ me āusaṃ*) — heard from the Bhagavan Mahavira. This declaration has come down from the Sthavira-bhagavants — the venerable elder monks who preserved the teaching. There are *four* stations of *vinaya-samadhi* (or, more broadly, four samadhis collectively named under "vinaya-samadhi" since the chapter as a whole is named for vinaya).

The four are named: (1) **Vinaya-samadhi** — the equipoise of humility-discipline; (2) **Shruta-samadhi** — the equipoise of scriptural mastery; (3) **Tapa-samadhi** — the equipoise of austerity; (4) **Achara-samadhi** — the equipoise of right conduct.

The word *samadhi* here has multiple senses — it means (a) the welfare of the soul (atma-hita), (b) the gathering-up and stabilisation of qualities, (c) the one-pointed concentration of mind, and (d) the soul's inner steadiness in its own nature. All four senses are operative here. Each samadhi is a domain into which the entire monastic life is to be poured, and each yields the soul's deep inner balance.

The closing gatha emphasises two qualifications of the practitioner: he must be **pandita** (wise, having clear understanding) and **jitendriya** (conqueror of the senses). Without wisdom, the practice is mechanical; without sense-conquest, the practice is constantly disturbed. With both, the disciple delights in his own atma — and the four samadhis become natural.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Sudharmaswami told Jambuswami: "I heard from Mahavira — the elder monks have propounded four samadhis: of vinaya, of scripture, of austerity, of conduct. The wise sense-conquerors always delight inwardly in these four."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Four domains — vinaya, shruta, tapas, achara — into which the whole life is poured. Are you pouring evenly into all four, or unevenly?

## SUTRA 9.4.2 — Vinaya-Samadhi (The Four Sub-Points)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउव्विहा खलु विणयसमाहि भवइ; तं जहा — अणुसासिज्जंतो सुस्सूसइ, सम्मं संपडिवज्जइ, वेयमाराहइ, ण य भवइ अत्तसंपग्गहिए, चउत्थं पयं भवइ । भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो ।

पेहेइ हियाणुसासणं, सुस्सूसइ तं च पुणो अहिट्ठए ।
ण य माणमएण मज्जइ, विणयसमाहि आयय​ट्ठिए ॥९.४.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.2 ||
cauvvihā khalu viṇayasamāhi bhavai; taṃ jahā — aṇusāsijjaṃto sussūsai, sammaṃ saṃpaḍivajjai, veyamārāhai, ṇa ya bhavai attasaṃpaggahie, cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai | bhavai ya ittha silogo |

pehei hiyāṇusāsaṇaṃ, sussūsai taṃ ca puṇo ahiṭṭhae |
ṇa ya māṇamaeṇa majjai, viṇayasamāhi āyayaṭṭhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Indeed, vinaya-samadhi is fourfold; namely — (1) being instructed (by the Guru), one (eagerly) desires to hear; (2) one rightly accepts (the instruction); (3) one practises (the way) Veda (= shruta = scripture) (says); (4) one does not become self-grasping (full of self-praise) — this is the fourth point. And here there is a verse:

(The seeker fit for moksha) considers the beneficial instruction (with care and reverence); attentively desires to hear it, and then practises it; and does not become intoxicated with the wine of pride — this is vinaya-samadhi for the long-aiming (moksha-seeking) one.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउव्विहा खलु विणयसमाहि भवइ** (cauvvihā khalu viṇayasamāhi bhavai) = vinaya-samadhi is indeed of four kinds
- **अणुसासिज्जंतो सुस्सूसइ** (aṇusāsijjaṃto sussūsai) = (when) being instructed (by the Guru), (the disciple) eagerly desires to hear
- **सम्मं संपडिवज्जइ** (sammaṃ saṃpaḍivajjai) = rightly accepts (the instruction; commits to acting on it)
- **वेयं आराहइ** (veyaṃ ārāhai) = practises (the way) the Veda (= shruta = scripture / agama) prescribes
- **ण य भवइ अत्तसंपग्गहिए** (ṇa ya bhavai attasaṃpaggahie) = and does not become self-grasping (atta-sampaggahita — full of self-praise, or "self-gathered" in pride)
- **चउत्थं पयं भवइ** (cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai) = (this) is the fourth point
- **पेहेइ हियाणुसासणं** (pehei hiyāṇusāsaṇaṃ) = considers (looks upon, studies) the beneficial instruction
- **सुस्सूसइ तं च पुणो अहिट्ठए** (sussūsai taṃ ca puṇo ahiṭṭhae) = desires to hear it, and then practises it (literally, "is established in" it)
- **ण य माणमएण मज्जइ** (ṇa ya māṇamaeṇa majjai) = does not become intoxicated with the wine of pride (mana-mada)
- **विणयसमाहि आयय​ट्ठिए** (viṇayasamāhi āyayaṭṭhie) = (this is) vinaya-samadhi for the long-aiming (moksha-seeker, ayata-arthi)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first samadhi — vinaya — has four sub-points:

(1) **Anushasyamano shushrushati** — when the Guru gives instruction, the disciple desires to hear with reverence and attention. He does not interrupt; he does not turn away; he listens with longing.

(2) **Samyak sampratipadyate** — he rightly accepts what he hears. He does not just listen; he commits to practising. Mere intellectual reception is not vinaya-samadhi; the commitment to act is essential.

(3) **Veda-aradhayati** — he practises in conformity with the Veda (here meaning *shruta* — the agama, the scriptural tradition). His action follows the scriptural guidelines, not his own innovations.

(4) **Na bhavati atta-sampaggahita** — he does not become "self-grasping" — that is, he does not begin to congratulate himself on his vinaya, does not say "see how humble I am," does not let pride creep into the very practice of humility. This subtle fourth point is critical: many disciples destroy their own vinaya-samadhi by becoming proud of being humble. The fourth point closes that loophole.

The accompanying gatha summarises: the seeker considers the instruction, eagerly hears it, practises it, and does not get intoxicated with pride. This is vinaya-samadhi for the long-aiming (the ayata-arthin, the seeker of liberation).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Vinaya-samadhi has four parts: (1) eagerly hearing the Guru's instruction, (2) rightly accepting it, (3) practising as scripture says, (4) not becoming proud of one's own vinaya. The seeker who does all four has the equipoise of vinaya.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The fourth point — not getting proud of being humble — is the trickiest. Have you ever caught yourself doing it?

## SUTRA 9.4.3 — Shruta-Samadhi (The Four Sub-Points)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउव्विहा खलु सुयसमाहि भवइ; तं जहा — सुयं मे भविस्सइ त्ति अज्झाइयव्वं भवइ, एगग्गचित्तो भविस्सामि त्ति अज्झाइयव्वं भवइ, अप्पाणं ठावइस्सामि त्ति अज्झाइयव्वं भवइ, ठिओ परं ठावइस्सामि त्ति अज्झाइयव्वं भवइ, चउत्थं पयं भवइ । भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो ।

णाणमेगग्गचित्तो य, ठिओ य ठावयई परं ।
सुयाणि य अहिज्जित्ता, रओ सुयसमाहिए ॥९.४.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.3 ||
cauvvihā khalu suyasamāhi bhavai; taṃ jahā — suyaṃ me bhavissai tti ajjhāiyavvaṃ bhavai, eggaggacitto bhavissāmi tti ajjhāiyavvaṃ bhavai, appāṇaṃ ṭhāvaissāmi tti ajjhāiyavvaṃ bhavai, ṭhio paraṃ ṭhāvaissāmi tti ajjhāiyavvaṃ bhavai, cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai | bhavai ya ittha silogo |

ṇāṇamegaggacitto ya, ṭhio ya ṭhāvayaī paraṃ |
suyāṇi ya ahijjittā, rao suyasamāhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Indeed, shruta-samadhi is fourfold; namely — (1) "scripture will be acquired by me" — for this (purpose) one should study; (2) "I shall become one-pointed in mind" — for this one should study; (3) "I shall establish my own self (in dharma)" — for this one should study; (4) "having become established (myself), I shall establish others" — for this one should study; this is the fourth point. And here there is a verse:

(The monk who, by study, gains) knowledge, becomes one-pointed in mind, becomes established (in dharma), and (then) establishes others — having studied the scriptures — is delighted in the equipoise of shruta.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुयं मे भविस्सइ त्ति** (suyaṃ me bhavissai tti) = thinking "scripture will be acquired by me" / "I will gain scriptural knowledge"
- **अज्झाइयव्वं भवइ** (ajjhāiyavvaṃ bhavai) = one should study (it is fitting to study with this motive)
- **एगग्गचित्तो भविस्सामि त्ति** (eggaggacitto bhavissāmi tti) = thinking "I shall become one-pointed in mind"
- **अप्पाणं ठावइस्सामि त्ति** (appāṇaṃ ṭhāvaissāmi tti) = thinking "I shall establish my own self (in dharma)"
- **ठिओ परं ठावइस्सामि त्ति** (ṭhio paraṃ ṭhāvaissāmi tti) = thinking "having become established, I shall establish others (in dharma)"
- **चउत्थं पयं भवइ** (cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai) = (this) is the fourth point
- **णाणं एगग्गचित्तो य** (ṇāṇaṃ egaggacitto ya) = (gains) knowledge and (becomes) one-pointed
- **ठिओ य ठावयई परं** (ṭhio ya ṭhāvayaī paraṃ) = is established (himself) and establishes others
- **सुयाणि य अहिज्जित्ता** (suyāṇi ya ahijjittā) = having studied the scriptures
- **रओ सुयसमाहिए** (rao suyasamāhie) = (he is) delighted in shruta-samadhi

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second samadhi — shruta — also has four sub-points, framed as four motivations for study:

(1) **"Scripture will be acquired by me"** — the disciple studies because he genuinely wants to acquire scriptural knowledge. Without scriptural knowledge, the disciple does not know the inner principles of the Jain dharma; he may drift into other sectarian views or extreme positions. So the first motive is straightforward: I want to know the agama.

(2) **"I shall become one-pointed in mind"** — the disciple studies because the very act of disciplined study collects the mind. Where the wandering mind cannot be focused by force, the absorbing study of scripture naturally focuses it. The Uttaradhyayana 23 records Keshi-Gautam dialogue: "How do you control the mind-horse?" Gautam replies: "I control it with the rein of scripture."

(3) **"I shall establish my own self in dharma"** — study deepens the disciple's inner footing in dharma. When uncertainty arises in life, scripture-knowledge gives him stable ground to stand on.

(4) **"Having established myself, I shall establish others"** — once stable, the disciple uses his learning to help other seekers stabilise. Knowledge becomes the gift one passes on.

The closing gatha gathers all four: the disciple gains knowledge, becomes one-pointed, is himself established, establishes others — and so is delighted in shruta-samadhi. Notice the absence of any motive of fame, wealth, or worldly success; pure self-and-other welfare is the only motive.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Shruta-samadhi has four parts: studying so that you (1) acquire scripture, (2) become one-pointed in mind, (3) establish yourself in dharma, (4) and then establish others. Study with these motives is the equipoise of scripture.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you study, what is your motive? Knowledge, focus, self-stability, helping others — or something else?

## SUTRA 9.4.4 — Tapa-Samadhi (The Four Sub-Points)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउव्विहा खलु तवसमाहि भवइ; तं जहा — णो इहलोगट्ठयाए तवमहिट्ठिज्जा, णो परलोगट्ठयाए तवमहिट्ठिज्जा, णो कित्तिवण्णसद्दसिलोगट्ठयाए तवमहिट्ठिज्जा, णण्णत्थ णिज्जरट्ठयाए तवमहिट्ठिज्जा, चउत्थं पयं भवइ । भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो ।

विविहगुणतवोरए य णिच्चं, भवइ णिरासए णिज्जरट्ठिए ।
तवसा धुणइ पुराणपावगं, जुत्तो सया तवसमाहिए ॥९.४.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.4 ||
cauvvihā khalu tavasamāhi bhavai; taṃ jahā — ṇo ihalogaṭṭhayāe tavamahiṭṭhijjā, ṇo paralogaṭṭhayāe tavamahiṭṭhijjā, ṇo kittivaṇṇasaddasilogaṭṭhayāe tavamahiṭṭhijjā, ṇaṇṇattha ṇijjaraṭṭhayāe tavamahiṭṭhijjā, cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai | bhavai ya ittha silogo |

vivihaguṇatavorae ya ṇiccaṃ, bhavai ṇirāsae ṇijjaraṭṭhie |
tavasā dhuṇai purāṇapāvagaṃ, jutto sayā tavasamāhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Indeed, tapa-samadhi is fourfold; namely — (1) one should not undertake austerity for the sake of (gain in) this world; (2) one should not undertake austerity for the sake of (gain in) the other world (heavens); (3) one should not undertake austerity for the sake of fame, recognition, sound, or praise; (4) one should not undertake austerity except for the sake of nirjara (destruction of karma); this is the fourth point. And here there is a verse:

Engaged always in (the) various-quality-austerities, free from (worldly) hope, aiming at nirjara — by tapas (he) shakes off the ancient karma; (he is) ever joined to the equipoise of tapas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णो इहलोगट्ठयाए तवमहिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇo ihalogaṭṭhayāe tavamahiṭṭhijjā) = should not undertake austerity for the sake of this-world (gains — wealth, health, special powers, lokika-siddhi)
- **णो परलोगट्ठयाए** (ṇo paralogaṭṭhayāe) = nor for the sake of other-world (gains — heavenly birth, deva-luxuries)
- **णो कित्तिवण्णसद्दसिलोगट्ठयाए** (ṇo kittivaṇṇasaddasilogaṭṭhayāe) = nor for the sake of *kirti* (universal fame), *varna* (regional fame), *shabda* (local fame in five-twenty-five villages), or *shloka* (praise / shlagha)
- **णण्णत्थ णिज्जरट्ठयाए** (ṇaṇṇattha ṇijjaraṭṭhayāe) = (one should undertake austerity) for nothing other than for the sake of nirjara (destruction of karma)
- **विविहगुणतवोरए** (vivihaguṇatavorae) = engaged in various-quality austerities (vividha-guna-tapas — the twelve kinds: six external, six internal)
- **णिच्चं भवइ णिरासए** (ṇiccaṃ bhavai ṇirāsae) = is always free of (worldly) hope (nirashaka)
- **णिज्जरट्ठिए** (ṇijjaraṭṭhie) = aiming at nirjara
- **तवसा धुणइ पुराणपावगं** (tavasā dhuṇai purāṇapāvagaṃ) = by tapas (he) shakes off the ancient sin / karma
- **जुत्तो सया तवसमाहिए** (jutto sayā tavasamāhie) = (he is) always joined to tapa-samadhi

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third samadhi — tapas (austerity) — has its four sub-points framed *negatively* — three "do not" statements followed by one positive injunction:

(1) **Not for this-world gain** — austerity must not be undertaken for *aihika* benefit (worldly wealth, special teja-leshya powers, health benefits, control over the senses for worldly pleasure). Many monks have undertaken austerity to acquire siddhis; that motive defiles the austerity.

(2) **Not for other-world gain** — also not for *paralaukika* gain (heavenly birth, deva-luxuries, future human prosperity). The austerity must not be a "merit-investment" for future enjoyment.

(3) **Not for fame** — not for *kirti* (universal renown), *varna* (regional renown), *shabda* (local fame), or *shloka* (praise). The Sutrakara enumerates fame in four widening circles to leave no escape; *every* form of fame-motivation defiles the austerity.

(4) **Only for nirjara** — austerity should be undertaken *exclusively* for the destruction of karma. Nothing else. The single legitimate motive is karmic purification.

The closing gatha shows the disciple who follows these four — engaged in the twelve kinds of tapas (six external: anashana, unodari, vritti-sankshepa, rasa-tyaga, kayaklesha, sanlinata; six internal: prayaschitta, vinaya, vaiyavritya, svadhyaya, dhyana, vyutsarga) — free of all hope, aiming only at nirjara — and through tapas, "shaking off the ancient sin" (karmic accumulation from past lives) the way a man shakes dust from his clothes.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Tapa-samadhi has four parts: undertaking austerity (1) not for this world, (2) not for the next world, (3) not for fame, (4) only for the destruction of karma. The disciple who lives by these four rests in the equipoise of austerity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you undertake any kind of discipline, look honestly at your motive. Is it free of all four traps — this-world, next-world, fame, anything-other-than-purification?

## SUTRA 9.4.5 — Achara-Samadhi (The Four Sub-Points)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउव्विहा खलु आयारसमाहि भवइ; तं जहा — णो इहलोगट्ठयाए आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा, णो परलोगट्ठयाए आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा, णो कित्तिवण्णसद्दसिलोगट्ठयाए आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा, णण्णत्थ आरहंतेहिं हेऊहिं आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा, चउत्थं पयं भवइ । भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो ।

जिणवयणरए अत्तित्तिणे, पडिपुण्णायतमायय​ट्ठिए ।
आयारसमाहिसंवुडे, भवइ य दंते भावसंधए ॥९.४.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.5 ||
cauvvihā khalu āyārasamāhi bhavai; taṃ jahā — ṇo ihalogaṭṭhayāe āyāramahiṭṭhijjā, ṇo paralogaṭṭhayāe āyāramahiṭṭhijjā, ṇo kittivaṇṇasaddasilogaṭṭhayāe āyāramahiṭṭhijjā, ṇaṇṇattha ārahaṃtehiṃ heūhiṃ āyāramahiṭṭhijjā, cauttha­ṃ payaṃ bhavai | bhavai ya ittha silogo |

jiṇavayaṇarae attittiṇe, paḍipuṇṇāyatamāyayaṭṭhie |
āyārasamāhisaṃvuḍe, bhavai ya daṃte bhāvasaṃdhae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Indeed, achara-samadhi is fourfold; namely — (1) one should not undertake (right) conduct for the sake of (gain in) this world; (2) nor for the sake of (gain in) the other world; (3) nor for the sake of fame, recognition, sound, or praise; (4) one should not undertake (right) conduct except for the (sake of the) reasons (purposes) declared by the Arihants; this is the fourth point. And here there is a verse:

(The monk who) delights in the words of the Jina, (is) free of agitation, complete (in scriptural knowledge), aiming at the (long, supreme) goal — (he is) hedged-in by the equipoise of achara, (he is) self-controlled, (he is) the maintainer of pure inner-states.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णो इहलोगट्ठयाए आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇo ihalogaṭṭhayāe āyāramahiṭṭhijjā) = should not undertake conduct for the sake of this-world (gain)
- **णो परलोगट्ठयाए** (ṇo paralogaṭṭhayāe) = nor for the sake of the other-world
- **णो कित्तिवण्णसद्दसिलोगट्ठयाए** (ṇo kittivaṇṇasaddasilogaṭṭhayāe) = nor for the sake of fame (in its four widening circles)
- **णण्णत्थ आरहंतेहिं हेऊहिं आयारमहिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇaṇṇattha ārahaṃtehiṃ heūhiṃ āyāramahiṭṭhijjā) = should undertake conduct for no reason other than the (purposes) declared by the Arihants
- **जिणवयणरए** (jiṇavayaṇarae) = delighting in the words of the Jina
- **अत्तित्तिणे** (attittiṇe) = free of agitation (a-tantanata — without irritability or fluster)
- **पडिपुण्ण** (paḍipuṇṇa) = complete (especially in scriptural knowledge)
- **आयय​ट्ठिए** (āyayaṭṭhie) = aiming at the long / supreme (goal of moksha)
- **आयारसमाहिसंवुडे** (āyārasamāhisaṃvuḍe) = hedged-in (sanvrita) by achara-samadhi
- **दंते** (daṃte) = self-controlled (danta — having subdued mind and senses)
- **भावसंधए** (bhāvasaṃdhae) = the maintainer of pure inner-states (bhava-sandhaka — preserving the inner purity / "joining toward bhavas of moksha")

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth and final samadhi — achara — has its four sub-points framed identically to tapa-samadhi: three "do not" statements and one positive. Right conduct must not be undertaken for: (1) this-world gain, (2) other-world gain, (3) fame in its four widening circles. And it must be undertaken (4) only for *the reasons declared by the Arihants*.

The phrasing "**arahantehim heuhim**" is significant. The Sutrakara does not say "for the sake of nirjara only" (as he did with tapas), because the purposes for which conduct-rules exist are *multiple*: some serve samvara (stopping karma-inflow), some serve nirjara (destroying karma), some serve sharira-nirvaha (sustaining the body for samyama), some serve sangha-vyavastha (community order), some serve viveka-vyavahara (proper conduct between monks). Each rule has its own purpose, declared by the omniscient Arihants. The disciple should follow each rule with its proper purpose in mind, not import worldly motives into it.

The accompanying gatha names six qualities of the monk who attains achara-samadhi: (1) *jinavayana-rae* — delighting in Jina-words; (2) *attittiṇe* — free of irritability; (3) *padipunna* — complete (in scriptural knowledge for the purpose of conduct); (4) *ayayaṭṭhie* — aiming at the supreme (moksha-arthi); (5) *daṃte* — self-controlled (mind and senses subdued); (6) *bhavasaṃdhae* — maintainer of pure inner-states.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Achara-samadhi has four parts: undertaking right conduct (1) not for this world, (2) not for the next world, (3) not for fame, (4) only for the reasons the Arihants have declared. The disciple who lives by these four — delighting in Jina-words, free of agitation, complete, self-controlled, and maintaining pure inner-states — is hedged-in by the equipoise of conduct.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you follow a rule, do you understand the *purpose* the rule serves — or do you follow it mechanically?

## SUTRA 9.4.6 — The Fruit of the Four Samadhis

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अभिगम चउरो समाहिओ, सुविसुद्धो सुसमाहियप्पओ ।
विउलहियं सुहावहं पुणो, कुव्वइ सो पयखेममप्पणो ॥९.४.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.6 ||
abhigama cauro samāhio, suvisuddho susamāhiyappao |
viulahiyaṃ suhāvahaṃ puṇo, kuvvai so payakhemamappaṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having understood the four samadhis (and practised them), well-purified, well-established in samadhi (within samyama) — he attains the great-good, the bliss-bringing, the well-being-bringing supreme station for himself.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभिगम चउरो समाहिओ** (abhigama cauro samāhio) = having known / understood the four samadhis
- **सुविसुद्धो** (suvisuddho) = (becoming) extremely pure
- **सुसमाहियप्पओ** (susamāhiyappao) = well-established (in samyama / samadhi within himself); susamahita-atmaka
- **विउलहियं** (viulahiyaṃ) = the great / vast benefit (vipula-hita)
- **सुहावहं** (suhāvahaṃ) = bringing sukha (bliss)
- **पुणो कुव्वइ** (puṇo kuvvai) = (he) attains / makes (for himself, again) — i.e. moksha
- **सो** (so) = he
- **पयखेमं** (payakhemaṃ) = the well-being-bringing supreme station (pada-kshema = nirvana-pada — the kshema-pada, the place of safety, moksha)
- **अप्पणो** (appaṇo) = for himself (atmanaḥ)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The penultimate gatha summarises the fruit. The disciple who has *known* the four samadhis (abhigama), *practised* them, become extremely *pure* (suvishuddha), and become *well-established* in samadhi within his own samyama — that disciple attains the *vipula-hita* (vast benefit), the *sukhavaha* (bliss-bringing), the *kshema-pada* (the place of supreme safety) — moksha itself.

The structure is: knowledge → practice → purification → stabilisation → attainment. The disciple does not skip steps. Knowing is not enough; practising is necessary. Practising is not enough; the practice must produce inner purification. Purification is not enough; the disciple must become *stably* established in samadhi. Only then does the supreme attainment follow.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Having known the four samadhis, practised them, become extremely pure, and become well-established — the monk attains for himself the supreme well-being, the bliss-bringing place of safety: liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Know → practise → purify → stabilise → attain. At which step are you presently working?

## SUTRA 9.4.7 — The Final Promise: Liberation, or Mahardhika Deva

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जाइमरणाओ मुच्चइ, इत्थंथं च चयइ सव्वसो ।
सिद्धे वा हवइ सासए, देवे वा अप्परए महिड्ढिए ॥ त्ति बेमि ॥९.४.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 9.4.7 ||
jāimaraṇāo muccai, itthaṃthaṃ ca cayai savvaso |
siddhe vā havai sāsae, deve vā apparae mahiḍḍhie || tti bemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He is liberated from (the cycle of) birth and death; he completely abandons (all conditions of) being-here (in samsara). He becomes either the eternal Siddha, or — (if a little karma remains) — a deva of great splendour (mahardhika) with little karmic-dust (left). — Thus do I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जाइमरणाओ मुच्चइ** (jāimaraṇāo muccai) = is liberated from birth and death
- **इत्थंथं** (itthaṃthaṃ) = (the conditions of) being-here in samsara (atrastha — Sanskrit; itthatha or ittham-thaṃ in Prakrit, both meaning "(samsara) here-state")
- **चयइ सव्वसो** (cayai savvaso) = completely abandons / casts off
- **सिद्धे वा हवइ सासए** (siddhe vā havai sāsae) = becomes either an eternal Siddha
- **देवे वा अप्परए** (deve vā apparae) = or (becomes) a deva (with) little karmic-residue (alpa-raja — small remaining karma-dust)
- **महिड्ढिए** (mahiḍḍhie) = of great splendour / great wealth (mahardhika)
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = thus I declare

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing gatha of the entire chapter — and effectively of the principal Dashvaikalik Sutra. The fruit of the four samadhis is comprehensive: *jaimarano muchchai* — the disciple is liberated from the cycle of birth and death; *ittham cha chayai savvaso* — he completely casts off all the conditions of being-here in samsara (every kind of birth, every kind of body, every kind of suffering).

The result is one of two: (1) **siddhe va havai sasae** — he becomes the eternal Siddha (if all karma is destroyed in this very life); or (2) **deve va apparae mahiddhie** — if a little karma remains, he is born as a Mahardhika deva — a deva of great wealth, great splendour, surrounded by celestial enjoyments — but with very little karmic dust remaining. Such a deva will, in his very next life or shortly thereafter, attain final liberation.

The Sutrakara is generous in his closing: even the disciple who does not quite reach the final goal in this life, but has practised the four samadhis sincerely, is *guaranteed* a celestial birth of the highest kind, with a near-immediate path to moksha. There is no failure in this practice; only complete success or near-complete success.

The closing **tti bemi** seals the chapter — and with it, the principal teaching of the Dashvaikalik Sutra.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

(The disciple who has practised the four samadhis) is liberated from birth and death and casts off all of samsara. He becomes either an eternal Siddha, or — if a little karma remains — a deva of great splendour with near-final purification. Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The path of vinaya, shruta, tapas, and achara — pursued sincerely — has no failure. Either liberation, or a celestial birth one step away. What does it mean to take up a path that cannot fail?

## END OF ADHYAYAN 9

॥ અધ્યયન-૯ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Adhyayan-9 sampurna — "The ninth chapter is complete.")*

The ninth adhyayan closes here. From the disciple's first bow before the Guru — through the parable of the dharma-tree and the contrast of the trained and the untrained — through the portrait of the disciple worthy of universal worship — to the final architecture of the four samadhis and their fruit of liberation: the Sutrakara has built a single integrated teaching. *Vinaya-Samadhi* is not just a chapter; it is the gateway through which every other Jain practice must pass. The Guru-disciple relationship is the engine; humility is the fuel; scriptural mastery, austerity, and right conduct are the wheels; and the destination is moksha itself.
