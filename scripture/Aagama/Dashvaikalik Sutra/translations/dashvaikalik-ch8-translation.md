# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 8
# Achar Pranidhi (આચાર પ્રણિધિ) — The Resolution of Conduct

**Adhyayan 8 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 325–390 (PDF pages 386–451 approx.)*
*Total Sutras in this Adhyayan: 64*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The eighth chapter of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is named **Achar Pranidhi** — literally, *the resolution of conduct*. The word *achar* means the path of right conduct (discipline, observance, the lived practice that produces liberation), and *pranidhi* means a treasury, a deep deposit, a focused resolve. To "obtain the achar pranidhi" is to receive the deposit of monastic discipline and to resolve oneself fully into it. The text describes this chapter, in its own opening reflection, as a "storehouse" — not a single straight-line teaching but a basket into which the sutra-author has packed a wide variety of practical instructions on the daily conduct of a monk.

Unlike the chapters before it which have a single thematic spine — Shadjivnikaya on the six classes of life, Pinda-eshana on the rules of alms, Suvakya-shuddhi on speech — this chapter ranges across the whole of monastic life. It opens with renewed instructions on protecting the six classes of life (earth-, water-, fire-, air-, plant-, and mobile-bodied beings); it adds a unique discussion of *eight kinds of subtle beings* (snehasukshma, pushpasukshma, prani-sukshma, uttinga-sukshma, panaka-sukshma, beeja-sukshma, hari-sukshma, anda-sukshma) which are so minute that ordinary observation cannot detect them. It then moves through the careful inspection (pratilekhana) of equipment, the conduct of a monk inside a layperson's house, restraint of all five senses, restraint of the tongue and stomach, the renunciation of night-eating, the abandonment of self-praise and other-disparagement, the duty to confess transgressions without concealment, the discipline of the pupil before the teacher, the conquest of the four kashayas (anger, pride, deceit, greed), control of sleep and laughter, careful study, meditation, and at last the soul's culmination in liberation.

The chapter takes its name from the conviction that mere ordination is not enough. Entering the monastic order is, the text says, only "stepping onto the target"; the real practice is in *living* the conduct, day by day, hour by hour, sense by sense. The Achar Pranidhi is therefore a manual of daily resolve — a chapter that returns the monk's attention again and again to the small, easily-overlooked moments where discipline is either kept or lost. It ends with the seven flowers of accomplishment: love of the people, fame, an end to the great disease of mithyatva (false-belief), kashaya, and ignorance, and finally the liberation that is the fruit of every preceding chapter.

What follows are the sixty-four sutras of this chapter, each accompanied by translation, anvaya, and commentary.

## SUTRA 8.1 — The Vow to Teach the Conduct

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयारप्पणिहिं लद्धुं, जहा कायव्व भिक्खुणा ।
तं भे उदाहरिस्सामि, आणुपुव्विं सुणेह मे ॥८.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.1 ||
āyārappaṇihiṃ laddhuṃ, jahā kāyavva bhikkhuṇā |
taṃ bhe udāharissāmi, āṇupuvviṃ suṇeha me ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having received the resolution of conduct (achar pranidhi), the manner in which a monk must conduct himself — that I shall narrate to you in due order; listen to me.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयारप्पणिहिं** (āyārappaṇihiṃ) = the resolution-treasury of conduct; the inward deposit of monastic discipline
- **लद्धुं** (laddhuṃ) = having obtained / having received
- **जहा कायव्व** (jahā kāyavva) = as it is to be performed / the manner of doing
- **भिक्खुणा** (bhikkhuṇā) = by a monk (bhikshu)
- **तं भे उदाहरिस्सामि** (taṃ bhe udāharissāmi) = that to you I shall narrate
- **आणुपुव्विं** (āṇupuvviṃ) = in due succession, in order
- **सुणेह मे** (suṇeha me) = listen to me

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with a teacher's pledge to a pupil. The teacher does not begin with rules but with a frame: "you have received the discipline; now learn how to live it." The Gujarati commentary draws an important distinction here. Receiving the achar (taking ordination, accepting the great vows) is not itself liberation — it is only "stepping onto the target." Once the arrow has reached the target, the real work of penetration, of working through layer after layer, begins. This is why the very first word of the chapter is *laddhuṃ* — "having received" — and the next word is *jahā* — "as it is to be performed."

The traditional commentaries explain *achar* as the various means that lead to liberation, and *pranidhi* as the storehouse, the gathering, the focused inward holding of those means. Achar-pranidhi together is therefore the *concentrated resolve* of a monk to live the conduct fully. It is both the discipline received from outside (from the Guru) and the discipline assumed inside (the monk's own resolve). The chapter to follow is the unfolding of what that resolve looks like in actual hour-by-hour practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"You have stepped into monastic life. Now I will tell you, step by step, exactly how to live it. Listen."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have committed to a path, do you mistake the commitment itself for the practice — or do you understand that the commitment is only the first step?

## SUTRA 8.2 — The Six Classes of Life

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुढवि दग अगणि मारुय, तणरुक्ख सबीयगा ।
तसा य पाणा जीवित्ति, इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा ॥८.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.2 ||
puḍhavi daga agaṇi māruya, taṇarukkha sabīyagā |
tasā ya pāṇā jīvitti, ii vuttaṃ mahesiṇā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, all vegetation including grass, trees and seed-bearing plants, and the mobile (trasa) beings — all these are living beings; thus has it been declared by the great seers (the omniscient ones).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुढवि** (puḍhavi) = prithvi-kaya (earth-bodied beings)
- **दग** (daga) = ap-kaya (water-bodied)
- **अगणि** (agaṇi) = agni-kaya (fire-bodied)
- **मारुय** (māruya) = vayu-kaya (air-bodied)
- **तणरुक्ख सबीयगा** (taṇarukkha sabīyagā) = grass, trees, and seed-bearing plants (all vegetation, vanaspati-kaya)
- **तसा य पाणा** (tasā ya pāṇā) = the mobile (trasa) living beings (two-, three-, four-, and five-sensed beings)
- **जीवित्ति** (jīvitti) = (these) are living beings
- **इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा** (ii vuttaṃ mahesiṇā) = thus has been declared by the great seers (the omniscient ones)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the foundational classification of all life as the Jain tradition sees it. Earth, water, fire, and air — which the modern world treats as inert substances — are here treated as *bodies of one-sensed living beings*, each capable of suffering, each entitled to protection. To these four immobile (sthavara) categories the sutra adds a fifth — vegetation, which the Jain tradition further subdivides into pratyeka (single-souled plants) and sadharana (multi-souled plants like roots and tubers). The sixth category, *trasa*, holds all beings that can move on their own — insects, animals, humans, devas.

The line "इइ वुत्तं महेसिणा" — "thus has been declared by the great seers" — anchors the classification not in observation but in the omniscient knowledge of the Tirthankaras. The monk's compassion is therefore not a sentiment but a *knowledge-ethic*: because the seers have seen these beings, the monk must protect them. This sutra is the philosophical backbone of every rule that follows in this chapter.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The omniscient ones have declared that there are six classes of living beings: earth, water, fire, air, plants, and the mobile creatures. All six are alive.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If even the soil and the water and the flame are alive, how would your daily movements change?

## SUTRA 8.3 — Non-Violence by Mind, Speech, and Body

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेसिं अच्छणजोएण, निच्चं होयव्वयं सिया ।
मणसा कायवक्केण, एवं हवइ संजए ॥८.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.3 ||
tesiṃ acchaṇajoeṇa, niccaṃ hoyavvayaṃ siyā |
maṇasā kāyavakkeṇa, evaṃ havai saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Toward those (six classes of beings), one must always remain in the mode of non-violence — by mind, by body, and by speech. Only thus does one become a true samyata (a self-restrained monk).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेसिं** (tesiṃ) = toward them (the six classes)
- **अच्छणजोएण** (acchaṇajoeṇa) = by the activity of non-violence (akshana-yoga: a-kshana = non-injury, yoga = engagement)
- **निच्चं होयव्वयं सिया** (niccaṃ hoyavvayaṃ siyā) = one should always remain (so)
- **मणसा कायवक्केण** (maṇasā kāyavakkeṇa) = by mind, by body, by speech
- **एवं हवइ संजए** (evaṃ havai saṃjae) = thus one becomes samyata (truly self-restrained)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra defines what it means to be a *samyata*, a true monk. It is not a costume or an ordination certificate; it is a sustained mode of non-violence held continuously across all three doors of activity — thought, word, and body. The rare term *acchaṇa-joeṇa* literally means "engagement in non-injury": every act of body, every word, every thought must be screened through the question, "does this injure another being?" If even one of the three is left unscreened, the monk's status as samyata is incomplete. This is why the threefold formula recurs throughout this chapter.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

To be a real monk is to refuse, always, by thought-word-body, to harm any of those six classes of beings.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the three doors — thought, word, body — which one in you is the leakiest?

## SUTRA 8.4 — Care of the Earth-Bodied

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुढविं भित्तिं सिलं लेलुं, नेव भिंदे न संलिहे ।
तिविहेण करणजोएण, संजए सुसमाहिए ॥८.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.4 ||
puḍhaviṃ bhittiṃ silaṃ leluṃ, neva bhinde na saṃlihe |
tivihena karaṇajoeṇa, saṃjae susamāhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Pure earth, walls, stone, lumps of clay — the well-composed restrained monk shall neither break nor scratch (any of these), by all three karanas and three yogas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुढविं भित्तिं सिलं लेलुं** (puḍhaviṃ bhittiṃ silaṃ leluṃ) = pristine earth, the inside of a wall (or river-bank), stone, lumps of clay
- **नेव भिंदे न संलिहे** (neva bhinde na saṃlihe) = neither break nor scrape/scratch
- **तिविहेण करणजोएण** (tivihena karaṇajoeṇa) = by the threefold karana (doing, causing, approving) and the threefold yoga (mind, speech, body)
- **संजए सुसमाहिए** (saṃjae susamāhie) = the well-composed monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having affirmed that earth is itself a body of life, the chapter moves to what that means in practice. The monk is forbidden to dig, scratch, scrape, or even chip away at any of the four substrates listed: untouched ground, the inner face of a wall, stone, or a clod of clay. The prohibition uses two precise Prakrit verbs — *bhinde* (to break, to splinter) and *samlihe* (to scrape, to abrade) — to cover both the dramatic act of digging and the smaller, more invisible act of idly scratching at a wall with a fingernail. The monk's relationship to the very ground he walks upon is one of restraint, not use.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The well-composed monk does not break, scratch, or scrape any uncultivated ground, wall, stone, or clay-lump — by any of the nine modes (three karanas times three yogas).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where do you, in idle moments, casually damage what you take for "lifeless"?

## SUTRA 8.5 — On Sitting and Seats

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुद्धपुढवीए न निसीए, ससरक्खम्मि य आसणे ।
पमज्जित्तु निसीएज्जा, जाइत्ता जस्स उग्गहं ॥८.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.5 ||
suddhapuḍhavīe na nisīe, sasarakkhammi ya āsaṇe |
pamajjittu nisīejjā, jāittā jassa uggahaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monk) shall not sit upon untouched (sentient) earth, nor upon a seat covered with sentient dust. Having asked permission of the owner, and having gently brushed (the surface), only then shall he sit.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुद्धपुढवीए** (suddhapuḍhavīe) = upon pristine earth (i.e. earth still sentient, not made achitta by any treatment)
- **न निसीए** (na nisīe) = let him not sit
- **ससरक्खम्मि य आसणे** (sasarakkhammi ya āsaṇe) = nor on a seat that has sentient dust upon it
- **पमज्जित्तु** (pamajjittu) = having gently brushed (with the rajoharana)
- **निसीएज्जा** (nisīejjā) = then he may sit
- **जाइत्ता जस्स उग्गहं** (jāittā jassa uggahaṃ) = having asked the owner of (that place's) permission/avagraha

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra introduces two key disciplines that govern every act of sitting in the monk's life. First, the monk does not sit on still-sentient ground or on any surface dusted with sentient particles, because to do so would crush living earth-beings. Second, the monk does not occupy any place without first formally asking the owner's permission (*avagraha*) — a rule that protects against the subtler fault of taking what has not been freely given. Only after both conditions are satisfied — sentient surfaces ruled out *and* permission obtained — does the monk perform *pramarjana*, the gentle brushing with the woollen broom (rajoharana) to displace any tiny mobile beings that may have settled. Then, and only then, may he sit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't sit on living earth or on a dusty seat. First ask the owner of the place. Then sweep gently with the broom. Then sit.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What habit could you transform if you paused to ask permission before each small act of taking?

## SUTRA 8.6 — Care of Water-Bodied Life

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सीओदगं न सेविज्जा, सिलावुट्ठं हिमाणि य ।
उसिणोदगं तत्तफासुयं, पडिगाहेज्ज संजए ॥८.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.6 ||
sīodagaṃ na sevijjā, silāvuṭṭhaṃ himāṇi ya |
usiṇodagaṃ tattaphāsuyaṃ, paḍigāhejja saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk shall not consume cold (sentient) water, nor hailstones, nor snow or ice. He shall accept only hot water (which has been thoroughly boiled and rendered lifeless) — the monk shall accept (only) such achitta, prasuk water.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सीओदगं** (sīodagaṃ) = cold water (still sentient)
- **न सेविज्जा** (na sevijjā) = let him not use / consume
- **सिलावुट्ठं** (silāvuṭṭhaṃ) = hailstones (literally "stone-rain")
- **हिमाणि य** (himāṇi ya) = and snow / ice / frost
- **उसिणोदगं तत्तफासुयं** (usiṇodagaṃ tattaphāsuyaṃ) = hot water that has been fully heated through (and so rendered prasuk = lifeless)
- **पडिगाहेज्ज संजए** (paḍigāhejja saṃjae) = the restrained monk shall accept

### 5. COMMENTARY

Water in its natural state is the body of countless ap-kaya beings. To drink it is to crush them. The Jain monastic discipline therefore restricts the monk's water entirely to *prasuk* water — water in which life-force has been destroyed by sufficient heating. The text uses two technical words: *tatta* (fully heated through) and *phasuya* (prasuk, lifeless). The Gujarati commentary notes carefully that water which is merely *warm* (e.g. naturally warm spring water) is not prasuk — only water that has been actively boiled. Even the act of avoiding cold water is, here, an act of compassion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk does not drink cold water, hailstones, or snow. He accepts only water that has been boiled enough to be lifeless.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What ordinary substance, taken without thought, contains lives you have never paused to count?

## SUTRA 8.7 — On a Wet Body

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उदउल्लं अप्पणो कायं, नेव पुंछे न संलिहे ।
समुप्पेह तहाभूयं, नो णं संघट्टए मुणी ॥८.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.7 ||
udaullaṃ appaṇo kāyaṃ, neva puṃche na saṃlihe |
samuppeha tahābhūyaṃ, no ṇaṃ saṃghaṭṭae muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If his own body is wet (with sentient water), the monk shall neither wipe it nor rub it. Seeing it to be in that state, he shall not even touch it.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उदउल्लं अप्पणो कायं** (udaullaṃ appaṇo kāyaṃ) = his own body wet (with sentient water)
- **नेव पुंछे न संलिहे** (neva puṃche na saṃlihe) = let him neither wipe (with cloth) nor rub (with hand)
- **समुप्पेह तहाभूयं** (samuppeha tahābhūyaṃ) = observing (it to be) in that state
- **नो णं संघट्टए मुणी** (no ṇaṃ saṃghaṭṭae muṇī) = the monk shall not even touch / rub

### 5. COMMENTARY

A monk on alms-rounds may be caught in unexpected rain or may have to ford a stream; his body becomes wet with sentient water. To dry the body — by wiping with a cloth or rubbing with the hand — would crush the still-living water-bodies clinging to the skin. The instruction is therefore counterintuitive: do not dry yourself. Sit, observe (samuppeha = "having looked carefully"), wait until the water has dried by itself or evaporated to harmlessness, and only then move. The discipline of compassion runs deeper than personal comfort.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If your body is wet with living water, don't wipe or rub it. Look carefully — wait — don't even touch it (until the water-beings are gone).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In how many small "harmless" comforts does another life pay the price?

## SUTRA 8.8 — Care of Fire-Bodied Life

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इंगालं अगणि अच्चिं, अलायं वा सजोइयं ।
न उंजेज्जा न घट्टेज्जा, णो णं णिव्वावए मुणी ॥८.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.8 ||
iṃgālaṃ agaṇi acciṃ, alāyaṃ vā sajoiyaṃ |
na uṃjejjā na ghaṭṭejjā, ṇo ṇaṃ ṇivvāvae muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk shall not kindle, shall not touch, and shall not extinguish — coals, fire (within iron), flame, a torch with broken flame, or any fire that bears light.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इंगालं** (iṃgālaṃ) = burning coal (without flame)
- **अगणि** (agaṇi) = fire (e.g. heated iron)
- **अच्चिं** (acciṃ) = a flame
- **अलायं** (alāyaṃ) = a torch / firebrand
- **सजोइयं** (sajoiyaṃ) = with light / with flame
- **न उंजेज्जा** (na uṃjejjā) = let him not kindle / fan
- **न घट्टेज्जा** (na ghaṭṭejjā) = let him not touch
- **णो णं णिव्वावए** (ṇo ṇaṃ ṇivvāvae) = nor extinguish

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fire-restriction is uniquely strict in Jain monasticism. Fire-beings are tejaskaya — living beings whose body is the flame itself. To kindle a fire is to bring such beings into existence in conditions that will quickly kill them; to feed a fire is to perpetuate that violence; to extinguish a fire is to crush the fire-beings outright. Even merely *touching* a flame is forbidden, because the touch agitates and kills them. This is the reason a Jain monk does not cook, does not warm himself by a fire on a cold night, does not blow out a lamp. The threefold negation — "do not kindle, do not touch, do not extinguish" — covers the full life-cycle of the fire-being.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk doesn't light a fire, doesn't touch one, and doesn't put one out — fire is a body of life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it cost to belong to a tradition where you do not even put out a flame to spare an insect's pain?

## SUTRA 8.9 — Care of Air-Bodied Life

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तालियंटेण पत्तेण, साहाविहुयणेण वा ।
न वीएज्ज अप्पणो कायं, बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं ॥८.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.9 ||
tāliyaṃṭeṇa patteṇa, sāhāvihuyaṇeṇa vā |
na vīejja appaṇo kāyaṃ, bāhiraṃ vāvi puggalaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The wise monk shall not fan — by a palmyra fan, by a leaf, by a tree-branch, or by a peacock-feather — neither his own body nor any external substance (such as hot food).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तालियंटेण** (tāliyaṃṭeṇa) = with a palmyra-leaf fan
- **पत्तेण** (patteṇa) = with a (broad) leaf
- **साहाविहुयणेण** (sāhāvihuyaṇeṇa) = with a swayed branch
- **वा** (vā) = or (with a peacock-feather morchhal)
- **न वीएज्ज** (na vīejja) = let him not fan
- **अप्पणो कायं** (appaṇo kāyaṃ) = his own body
- **बाहिरं वावि पुग्गलं** (bāhiraṃ vāvi puggalaṃ) = nor any external matter (hot food, hot milk, etc.)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Air, like water and fire, is a body of life — vayu-kaya. To stir the air deliberately is to throw vayu-beings into violent motion and kill them. The monk therefore does not fan himself in heat, does not blow on hot food to cool it, and does not wave a feather over an offering. The list of fanning instruments is exhaustive precisely so the rule cannot be evaded by a clever substitution: not a palmyra fan, not a leaf, not a branch, not even the soft peacock-feather morchhal will do. The discipline is total.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk doesn't fan himself or his food — by leaf, branch, fan, or feather. Even moving air kills.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your day is spent moving the world to suit your comfort?

## SUTRA 8.10 — Care of Vegetation

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तणरुक्खं न छिंदेज्जा, फलं मूलं च कस्सइ ।
आमगं विविहं बीयं, मणसा वि न पत्थए ॥८.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.10 ||
taṇarukkhaṃ na chiṃdejjā, phalaṃ mūlaṃ ca kassai |
āmagaṃ vivihaṃ bīyaṃ, maṇasā vi na patthae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk shall not cut grass or trees, nor anyone's fruit or root, and shall not — even by mind — desire any kind of unprocessed (still sentient) seed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तणरुक्खं न छिंदेज्जा** (taṇarukkhaṃ na chiṃdejjā) = let him not cut grass or tree
- **फलं मूलं च कस्सइ** (phalaṃ mūlaṃ ca kassai) = nor anyone's fruit or root
- **आमगं विविहं बीयं** (āmagaṃ vivihaṃ bīyaṃ) = various kinds of unripe / still-sentient seed
- **मणसा वि न पत्थए** (maṇasā vi na patthae) = let him not even desire (with the mind)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The discipline now reaches the fifth class — vanaspati-kaya, plant life. The monk does not pluck, does not cut, does not break a stem; he does not consume any unripe seed (which is still sentient and would germinate); and crucially, he does not even *want* to. The phrase *manasa vi na patthae* — "let him not even desire by mind" — is the moral hinge: the monk's restraint is not merely behavioural but goes all the way down into the level of intention. To want something is already to have begun the karma of taking it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't cut grass, don't break a fruit or root, don't even mentally crave a sentient seed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What desires, never acted on, still bind you because you welcomed them in?

## SUTRA 8.11 — Where Not to Stand

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गहणेसु ण चिट्ठिज्जा, बीएसु हरिएसु वा ।
उदगम्मि तहा निच्चं, उत्तिंगपणगेसु वा ॥८.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.11 ||
gahaṇesu ṇa ciṭṭhijjā, bīesu hariesu vā |
udagammi tahā niccaṃ, uttiṃgapaṇagesu vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He shall never stand within thickets of trees, nor upon seeds, nor upon green vegetation, nor upon the *udaka* plant (a water-borne vanaspati), nor upon ant-holes or moss-covered ground.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गहणेसु** (gahaṇesu) = in dense thickets / clumps of trees
- **ण चिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇa ciṭṭhijjā) = let him not stand
- **बीएसु** (bīesu) = upon seeds (rice etc.)
- **हरिएसु वा** (hariesu vā) = or upon green plants
- **उदगम्मि** (udagammi) = upon udaka (a kind of water-plant; ananta-kayika)
- **उत्तिंगपणगेसु** (uttiṃgapaṇagesu) = upon ant-hills (uttinga = insect-burrow / mushroom) and panaka (the five-coloured moss / mould)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk's choice of *where to stand* is itself a discipline of compassion. Standing in a tree-thicket would shake the branches and disturb plant-beings; standing on seeds, green grass, or the water-plant udaka would crush them; standing on an ant-hill or moss would destroy the colonies of subtle beings beneath. The five forbidden zones cover every class of plant and every nest of mobile life. The principle is consistent: the body's mere weight is itself a potential weapon.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't stand in dense plant-clumps, on seeds, on grass, on water-plants, on ant-hills, or on moss.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where does your body simply being there inflict damage you have never named?

## SUTRA 8.12 — The Whole Conduct of Non-Violence

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तसे पाणे न हिंसेज्जा, वाया अदुव कम्मुणा ।
उवरओ सव्वभूएसु, पासेज्ज विविहं जगं ॥८.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.12 ||
tase pāṇe na hiṃsejjā, vāyā aduva kammuṇā |
uvarao savvabhūesu, pāsejja vivihaṃ jagaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He shall not injure mobile (trasa) beings — neither by speech nor by deed (nor by mind). Withdrawn from harming all beings, he shall see the world (with all its variety) (with the eye of equanimity).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तसे पाणे** (tase pāṇe) = the mobile (trasa) living beings
- **न हिंसेज्जा** (na hiṃsejjā) = let him not injure
- **वाया अदुव कम्मुणा** (vāyā aduva kammuṇā) = by speech or by deed (and by extension, by mind)
- **उवरओ सव्वभूएसु** (uvarao savvabhūesu) = ceased / withdrawn (from harm) toward all beings
- **पासेज्ज विविहं जगं** (pāsejja vivihaṃ jagaṃ) = let him observe the variegated world (with knowledge-sight)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sixth class — trasa beings — completes the circle. After the five immobile classes, the monk now extends his non-violence to all moving beings. The triad *mind-speech-body* is restated, and the verse closes with a quietly profound instruction: *pasejja vivihaṃ jagaṃ* — "see the variegated world." Once the monk has truly stopped harming, his vision changes: the world that was once a field of objects to be used becomes a community of beings to be witnessed. Non-violence and right perception ripen together.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Do not harm any moving creature — by word, deed, or mind. Stand back from all violence, and look at the world's variety with the eye of compassion.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you stop trying to use the world, what do you start to see in it?

## SUTRA 8.13 — The Eight Subtle Beings (1): The Resolve

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अट्ट सुहुमाइं पेहाए, जाइं जाणित्तु संजए ।
दयाहिगारी भूएसु, आस चिट्ठ सएहि वा ॥८.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.13 ||
aṭṭa suhumāiṃ pehāe, jāiṃ jāṇittu saṃjae |
dayāhigārī bhūesu, āsa ciṭṭha saehi vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(There are) eight (classes of) subtle beings; observing them carefully and knowing them, the monk becomes fully entitled to compassion toward all beings — and only then does he sit, stand, or sleep.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अट्ट सुहुमाइं** (aṭṭa suhumāiṃ) = the eight subtle (classes of) beings
- **पेहाए** (pehāe) = having seen / observed
- **जाइं जाणित्तु** (jāiṃ jāṇittu) = having known which (they are)
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **दयाहिगारी भूएसु** (dayāhigārī bhūesu) = becomes the rightful holder of compassion toward (all) beings
- **आस चिट्ठ सएहि वा** (āsa ciṭṭha saehi vā) = (only then) sits, stands, or lies down

### 5. COMMENTARY

The text now opens a unique discussion: beyond the six general classes of life there are eight categories of *subtle* beings (sukshma) so minute that ordinary observation will not detect them. The monk's compassion is incomplete until he has *learned to see* these beings — until he has internalised what they are, where they live, when they appear. Only after that knowledge is his every posture (sitting, standing, lying down) a true act of dayā. The sutra-author uses the precise term *dayadhikari* — "one entitled to compassion" — to make the point that compassion toward what one cannot see is not a sentiment, it is a discipline trained by knowledge.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There are eight kinds of subtle beings. Until the monk learns to recognise them, his compassion is incomplete; only after seeing them is he fit to sit, stand, or sleep.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What lives are you harming right now simply because you have never learned to see them?

## SUTRA 8.14 — The Eight Subtle Beings (2): The Pupil's Question

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कयराइं अट्ट सुहुमाइं, जाइं पुच्छिज्ज संजए ।
इमाइं ताइं मेहावी, आइक्खिज्ज वियक्खणो ॥८.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.14 ||
kayarāiṃ aṭṭa suhumāiṃ, jāiṃ pucchijja saṃjae |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Which are those eight subtle beings?" — when the monk-pupil so asks, the wise and intelligent (teacher) shall answer: "These are they."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कयराइं अट्ट सुहुमाइं** (kayarāiṃ aṭṭa suhumāiṃ) = which (are) the eight subtle (beings)?
- **जाइं पुच्छिज्ज संजए** (jāiṃ pucchijja saṃjae) = when the monk-pupil thus questions

## SUTRA 8.15 — The Eight Subtle Beings (3): The List

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिणेहं पुप्फसुहुमं च, पाणुत्तिंगं तहेव य ।
पणगं बीयहरियं च, अंडसुहुमं च अट्ठमं ॥८.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.15 ||
siṇehaṃ pupphasuhumaṃ ca, pāṇuttiṃgaṃ taheva ya |
paṇagaṃ bīyahariyaṃ ca, aṃḍasuhumaṃ ca aṭṭhamaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(1) Sneha-sukshma (subtle moisture), (2) pushpa-sukshma (subtle flower), (3) prani-sukshma (subtle creature), (4) uttinga-sukshma (subtle insect-burrow), (5) panaka-sukshma (subtle moss/mould), (6) bīja-sukshma (subtle seed), (7) hari-sukshma (subtle sprout / vegetation), (8) and the eighth, anda-sukshma (subtle egg).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिणेहं** (siṇehaṃ) = subtle moisture: dew, mist, fog, fine drizzle, droplets exuding from plants
- **पुप्फसुहुमं** (pupphasuhumaṃ) = subtle flower: tiny flowers (like banyan or udumbara) too small to detect easily
- **पाणुत्तिंगं** (pāṇuttiṃgaṃ) = subtle creature: extremely tiny mobile beings (kunthuva etc.) of the colour of their habitat
- **पणगं** (paṇagaṃ) = subtle moss / mould (panak): the five-coloured fungi growing in damp places
- **बीयहरियं** (bīyahariyaṃ) = subtle seeds (bija) and subtle sprouts (haritak)
- **अंडसुहुमं** (aṃḍasuhumaṃ) = subtle eggs (of bees, spiders, ants, lizards, geckos)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra gives the canonical list of eight sukshma jiva categories that every monk must learn to recognise. The Gujarati commentary expands each in detail: *sneha* covers all five forms of subtle water (dew, frost, fog, fine rain, exuded plant moisture); *pushpa* covers the tiny flowers of trees like banyan that scatter unnoticed across the path; *prani* covers the smallest mobile beings (kunthus) which are camouflaged to the colour of their surroundings and visible only when they move; *uttinga* covers various burrow-makers and small mushrooms; *panaka* is the five-coloured mould or moss that develops on damp surfaces and ripening fruits; *bīja* covers the tiniest of seeds (poppy, mustard); *harita* covers the just-emerging sprouts of the rainy season; and *anda* covers the eggs of bees, spiders, ants, lizards, and geckos.

The classification is precise because the monk must conduct *every* movement — sitting, walking, sleeping, brushing, sweeping — in awareness of these. It is a knowledge-test of compassion: dayā that is informed by exact knowledge is no longer sentiment, it is yoga.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The eight subtle beings are: tiny moisture, tiny flowers, tiny creatures, tiny burrow-dwellers, tiny moulds, tiny seeds, tiny sprouts, and tiny eggs.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you actually believed eight invisible classes of life were always present, how slowly and gently would you live?

## SUTRA 8.16 — The Vigilant Monk

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवमेयाणि जाणित्ता, सव्वभावेण संजए ।
अप्पमत्तो जए निच्चं, सव्विंदियसमाहिए ॥८.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.16 ||
evameyāṇi jāṇittā, savvabhāveṇa saṃjae |
appamatto jae niccaṃ, savviṃdiyasamāhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowing these (eight subtle beings) thus, with all his being, the monk — vigilant, with all his senses well-composed — shall always strive (to protect them).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवमेयाणि जाणित्ता** (evameyāṇi jāṇittā) = having known these (subtle beings) in this way
- **सव्वभावेण संजए** (savvabhāveṇa saṃjae) = the monk, with all his being / by every aspect
- **अप्पमत्तो** (appamatto) = unfailingly vigilant (apramatta)
- **जए निच्चं** (jae niccaṃ) = always strives (yatana)
- **सव्विंदियसमाहिए** (savviṃdiyasamāhie) = with all his senses well-composed (in samadhi)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra closes the eight-subtle-beings discussion with the disciplinary instruction: *appamatto jae niccam* — "unfailingly vigilant, always striving." The Gujarati commentary explains *savvabhavena* in three ways: (1) knowing the beings in all their forms, (2) striving with one's whole capacity, (3) understanding what one knows in fullness. The phrase *savvindiya-samahie* — "all senses gathered into composure" — names the inner condition without which true vigilance is impossible. The teaching is that knowledge of the eight is useless without the steady inward composure that lets that knowledge govern action moment by moment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Once you know the eight subtle beings, hold all your senses in composure and stay vigilant — always — to protect them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it cost to be unfailingly vigilant? What does it cost not to be?

## SUTRA 8.17 — Pratilekhana of Equipment

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धुवं च पडिलेहिज्जा, जोगसा पायकंबलं ।
सेज्जमुच्चारभूमिं च, संथारं अदुवासणं ॥८.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.17 ||
dhuvaṃ ca paḍilehijjā, jogasā pāyakaṃbalaṃ |
sejjamuccārabhūmiṃ ca, saṃthāraṃ aduvāsaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Always, with concentrated attention (yogic care), the monk shall inspect (do pratilekhana of) — bowls and woollen blanket, the place of sleeping, the place of toilet, the bedding-mat, and the seat.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **धुवं च पडिलेहिज्जा** (dhuvaṃ ca paḍilehijjā) = always, certainly, at the proper time, do pratilekhana
- **जोगसा** (jogasā) = with yoga / focused mental concentration
- **पायकंबलं** (pāyakaṃbalaṃ) = bowl (patra) and woollen blanket (kambala)
- **सेज्जं** (sejjaṃ) = sleeping place (shayya)
- **उच्चारभूमिं** (uccārabhūmiṃ) = toilet ground
- **संथारं** (saṃthāraṃ) = the bedding-mat (samstaraka)
- **अदुवासणं** (aduvāsaṇaṃ) = and the seat (asana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

*Pratilekhana* — "looking-over" — is the ritualised inspection of every item of monastic equipment to ensure no living being has settled on or in it. The sutra mandates that this inspection be done *dhuvaṃ* (always, at the prescribed times — usually morning and evening) and *jogasā* (with focused attention, not as mechanical ritual). The list is exhaustive: the alms-bowl, the wool blanket, the place where one sleeps, the place where one relieves oneself, the mat one sits on, the seat one occupies. Every surface that the monk's body will contact is checked, because each contact is a potential act of violence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Always inspect — with focused attention — your bowl, blanket, sleeping spot, toilet area, mat, and seat (before using them).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in your daily life would change if you inspected every surface for life before you used it?

## SUTRA 8.18 — Disposal of Bodily Wastes

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उच्चारं पासवणं, खेलं सिंघाण जल्लियं ।
फासुयं पडिलेहित्ता, परिट्ठावेज्ज संजए ॥८.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.18 ||
uccāraṃ pāsavaṇaṃ, khelaṃ siṃghāṇa jalliyaṃ |
phāsuyaṃ paḍilehittā, pariṭṭhāvejja saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk, having inspected a prasuk (lifeless) ground, shall there dispose of — faeces, urine, phlegm, nasal mucus, and bodily perspiration / dirt.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उच्चारं** (uccāraṃ) = faeces (mal)
- **पासवणं** (pāsavaṇaṃ) = urine (mutra)
- **खेलं** (khelaṃ) = phlegm (kapha)
- **सिंघाण** (siṃghāṇa) = nasal mucus
- **जल्लियं** (jalliyaṃ) = perspiration / bodily dirt (jall)

- **परिट्ठावेज्ज संजए** (pariṭṭhāvejja saṃjae) = the restrained monk shall discharge / dispose

The Gujarati commentary notes that the Uttaradhyayan Sutra gives ten conditions for the qualification of the disposal-ground; the monk must select a place that fulfils them. Even the body's most ordinary functions are, here, an occasion for compassion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk inspects an absolutely lifeless area and only there discharges body-wastes — faeces, urine, phlegm, mucus, sweat.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean that even how one disposes of waste can be an act of compassion?

## SUTRA 8.19 — Conduct in a Layperson's House

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पविसित्तु परागारं, पाणट्ठा भोयणस्स वा ।
जयं चिट्ठे मियं भासे, न य रूवेसु मणं करे ॥८.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.19 ||
pavisittu parāgāraṃ, pāṇaṭṭhā bhoyaṇassa vā |
jayaṃ ciṭṭhe miyaṃ bhāse, na ya rūvesu maṇaṃ kare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having entered a layperson's house for water or food, the monk shall stand carefully (yatana-purvak), shall speak measuredly, and shall not turn his mind toward (any) form (i.e. shall not be attracted by the women etc. inside).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पविसित्तु परागारं** (pavisittu parāgāraṃ) = having entered another's (a householder's) house
- **पाणट्ठा भोयणस्स वा** (pāṇaṭṭhā bhoyaṇassa vā) = for water or food (alms)
- **जयं चिट्ठे** (jayaṃ ciṭṭhe) = stand with care / vigilance
- **मियं भासे** (miyaṃ bhāse) = speak measuredly (in limited words)
- **न य रूवेसु मणं करे** (na ya rūvesu maṇaṃ kare) = and do not turn the mind toward forms (beautiful sights)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk on alms-rounds enters spaces he does not own. The sutra triangulates his discipline: be careful in posture, restrained in speech, and detached in perception. The third instruction — *na ya rūvesu maṇaṃ kare* — "do not turn the mind to forms" — names the most subtle danger: the eye sees, the mind reaches, attachment is born, and the monastic vow is internally injured even if no external misconduct has occurred. The discipline is to enter, receive, and leave without the mind ever having anchored anywhere.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Enter the layperson's house carefully for alms; stand alert, speak little, and don't let your mind get caught on any beautiful form.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often does your mind wander into territory your body has carefully kept out of?

## SUTRA 8.20 — Restraint in Reporting What One Sees and Hears

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बहुं सुणेहि कण्णेहिं, बहुं अच्छीहिं पिच्छइ ।
न य दिट्ठं सुयं सव्वं, भिक्खू अक्खाउमरिहइ ॥८.२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.20 ||
bahuṃ suṇehi kaṇṇehiṃ, bahuṃ acchīhiṃ picchai |
na ya diṭṭhaṃ suyaṃ savvaṃ, bhikkhū akkhāumarihai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk hears much with his ears, sees much with his eyes — but he is not entitled to repeat (to others) all that he has seen and heard.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बहुं सुणेहि कण्णेहिं** (bahuṃ suṇehi kaṇṇehiṃ) = hears much with the ears
- **बहुं अच्छीहिं पिच्छइ** (bahuṃ acchīhiṃ picchai) = sees much with the eyes
- **न य दिट्ठं सुयं सव्वं** (na ya diṭṭhaṃ suyaṃ savvaṃ) = but not all that has been seen and heard
- **भिक्खू अक्खाउमरिहइ** (bhikkhū akkhāumarihai) = the monk is fit (qualified) to repeat

### 5. COMMENTARY

A monk on alms-rounds inevitably overhears family quarrels, sees household intimacies, witnesses transactions and emotions that do not belong to him. The sutra establishes that *reception is not licence to broadcast*. To pass on what one has overheard would be to harm the householder, would foment quarrels, would damage the monastic order's reputation, and would chain the monk himself in pointless gossip-karma. The monk must be a sieve through which information enters but does not exit unfiltered.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk hears and sees a lot inside other people's homes — but he isn't allowed to repeat all of it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your life do you confuse "I saw it" with "I'm entitled to share it"?

## SUTRA 8.21 — Total Withdrawal from Householder Affairs

सुयं वा जइ वा दिट्ठं, न लवेज्जोवघाइयं ।
न य केणइ उवाएण, गिहिजोगं समायरे ॥८.२१॥

|| 8.21 ||

Whatever the monk has heard or seen, if it would harm another, he shall not speak of it. And by no means whatsoever shall he engage in any householder-activity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुयं वा जइ वा दिट्ठं** (suyaṃ vā jai vā diṭṭhaṃ) = what is heard or what is seen
- **न लवेज्जोवघाइयं** (na lavejjovaghāiyaṃ) = if injurious (to another), let him not say it
- **न य केणइ उवाएण** (na ya keṇai uvāeṇa) = and by no means / by no device
- **गिहिजोगं समायरे** (gihijogaṃ samāyare) = engage in householder-activity (errand-running, message-carrying, etc.)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra turns the previous principle into an absolute. Speech that harms a layperson is forbidden — even when factually true. And the monk must never, by any pretext, become *useful* to the householder by carrying out errands or relaying messages or running household business. This is a deep protection: once the monk becomes practically useful to lay society, his independence dissolves and his discipline begins to bend around the lay agenda. Total withdrawal from *gihi-yoga* (householder-engagement) is the only safeguard.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If something you saw or heard would harm someone, don't say it. And never, by any trick, get drawn into running householder affairs.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What helpfulness in your life slowly compromises the deeper commitment you have made?

## SUTRA 8.22 — Restraint of the Tongue: Praise of Food

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिट्ठाणं रसणिज्जूढं, भद्दगं पावगं ति वा ।
पुड्ढो वावि अपुड्ढो वा, लाभालाभं न णिद्दिसे ॥८.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.22 ||
ṇiṭṭhāṇaṃ rasaṇijjūḍhaṃ, bhaddagaṃ pāvagaṃ ti vā |
puḍḍho vāvi apuḍḍho vā, lābhālābhaṃ na ṇiddise ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Whether asked or unasked (by a layperson), the monk shall not declare regarding food — "this is well-spiced and savoury," "this is bland and tasteless," "this is good," "this is bad," "today I received good alms," or "today I received nothing."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णिट्ठाणं** (ṇiṭṭhāṇaṃ) = food endowed with all good qualities (well-cooked, tasty, well-spiced)
- **रसणिज्जूढं** (rasaṇijjūḍhaṃ) = food devoid of taste (bland, dry)
- **भद्दगं पावगं ति वा** (bhaddagaṃ pāvagaṃ ti vā) = "good" or "bad"
- **पुड्ढो वावि अपुड्ढो वा** (puḍḍho vāvi apuḍḍho vā) = whether asked or not asked
- **लाभालाभं न णिद्दिसे** (lābhālābhaṃ na ṇiddise) = he shall not declare "I got" or "I did not get" (to a layperson)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The tongue's discipline is twofold — restraint of food and restraint of speech *about* food. Even when a householder asks the monk how the alms-round went, the monk does not answer with evaluations of taste or with reports of plenty or scarcity. To do so would betray inner attachment to food (raga and dvesha around taste), would induce the next householder to over-prepare or feel inadequate, and would tie the monk's spiritual life to the day's culinary lottery. The discipline is to receive what comes, in silence about its quality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Asked or unasked, the monk does not say to a householder: "the food was great," "it was bland," "today's alms were rich," or "today I got nothing."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often does your speech about what you received reveal more about your attachment than about the gift?

## SUTRA 8.23 — Begging Without Greed

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

न य भोयणम्मि गिद्धो, चरे उंछं अयंपिरो ।
अफासुयं न भुंजेज्जा, कीयमुद्देसियाहडं ॥८.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.23 ||
na ya bhoyaṇammi giddho, care uṃchaṃ ayaṃpiro |
aphāsuyaṃ na bhuṃjejjā, kīyamuddesiyāhaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Without being attached to delicious food, the monk shall (silently, without idle talk) gather alms (a little from each of many houses). He shall not eat what is aprasuk (still life-bearing), nor what was bought (with money), nor what was prepared specifically for him, nor what was brought to him (from elsewhere).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **न य भोयणम्मि गिद्धो** (na ya bhoyaṇammi giddho) = without being greedy for delicious food
- **चरे उंछं** (care uṃchaṃ) = let him gather alms (a little from many homes — uncha-vritti)
- **अयंपिरो** (ayaṃpiro) = without idle conversation
- **अफासुयं** (aphāsuyaṃ) = aprasuk (still containing life)
- **न भुंजेज्जा** (na bhuṃjejjā) = let him not eat
- **कीयं** (kīyaṃ) = bought (for the monk's sake)
- **उद्देसियं** (uddesiyaṃ) = prepared with the intention of (offering to) the monk
- **आहडं** (āhaḍaṃ) = brought (carried to the monk from another place)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three classical *doshas* (faults) of food-acceptance are formalised here: *krīta* (purchased for the monk), *audeshika* (prepared specifically for the monk), and *abhyahrita* (brought to the monk from elsewhere). Each of these makes the monk's food a *cause* of household activity — purchase, cooking, transport — and so makes the monk indirectly responsible for the himsa of those activities. The monk must instead practise *uncha-vritti* (gleaning a little from many houses) so that no single household is burdened. And he must do so silently, without flattery or chatter that would extract more.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk doesn't crave tasty food. He silently collects a little from many houses. He doesn't eat what's still alive, what was bought for him, what was cooked for him, or what was brought to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in your life is "given to you" but only because you arranged for it to come?

## SUTRA 8.24 — No Hoarding, No Patron-Bondage

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सण्णिहिं च न कुव्वेज्जा, अणुमायं पि संजए ।
मुहाजीवी असंबद्धे, हवेज्ज जगणिस्सिए ॥८.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.24 ||
saṇṇihiṃ ca na kuvvejjā, aṇumāyaṃ pi saṃjae |
muhājīvī asaṃbaddhe, havejja jagaṇissie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk shall not hoard / store (food) — not even an atom's worth. Free-living (without remuneration), unattached (to any patron-household), he shall live as one supported by the wide world.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सण्णिहिं** (saṇṇihiṃ) = storing / hoarding (sannidhi) — keeping food overnight
- **च न कुव्वेज्जा** (ca na kuvvejjā) = let him not do
- **अणुमायं पि संजए** (aṇumāyaṃ pi saṃjae) = the monk, not even an atom
- **मुहाजीवी** (muhājīvī) = mudha-jivi: living free of charge — without taking payment, without using money
- **असंबद्धे** (asaṃbaddhe) = unattached / unbound (to any single household or village)
- **हवेज्ज जगणिस्सिए** (havejja jagaṇissie) = let him live "supported by (the whole) world" — wandering across vast regions

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three independence-principles are stated together. *Asannidhi* — no hoarding, even of a grain, even overnight — keeps the monk dependent on each new day's mercy of alms and prevents the spiritual atrophy that comes with food-security. *Muhajivi* — taking nothing for spiritual service — protects the monk from becoming a paid functionary. *Asambaddha* — unbound to any one house or village — keeps the monk wandering across the wider world, "jaga-nissie," supported by no one in particular and so by everyone in general. Together they preserve the monk's spiritual mobility.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk doesn't hoard even an atom of food, doesn't take payment for spiritual help, and isn't tied to any one household — he wanders the wide world as his support.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What independence have you traded away for the comfort of being fed by a single, predictable source?

## SUTRA 8.25 — Contentment with the Dry and the Plain

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लूहवित्ती सुसंतुट्ठे, अप्पिच्छे सुहरे सिया ।
आसुरत्तं न गच्छेज्जा, सोच्चा णं जिणसासणं ॥८.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.25 ||
lūhavittī susaṃtuṭṭhe, appicche suhare siyā |
āsurattaṃ na gacchejjā, soccā ṇaṃ jiṇasāsaṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Living on dry and tasteless food, well-content, of few wants, easily filled — having heard the Jina-teaching, he shall not yield to anger.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लूहवित्ती** (lūhavittī) = living on dry / coarse food
- **सुसंतुट्ठे** (susaṃtuṭṭhe) = well-contented
- **अप्पिच्छे** (appicche) = of few desires (alpa-iccha)
- **सुहरे** (suhare) = easily filled / easily satisfied (su-bhara)
- **सिया** (siyā) = let him be
- **आसुरत्तं न गच्छेज्जा** (āsurattaṃ na gacchejjā) = let him not fall into "asura-ness" (anger; the demonic asuras are pre-eminently angry)
- **सोच्चा णं जिणसासणं** (soccā ṇaṃ jiṇasāsaṇaṃ) = having heard / kept in mind the Jina's teaching

### 5. COMMENTARY

Four chained virtues complete the food-discipline arc: *lūha-vritti* (living on dry food) → *santosha* (contentment) → *appichha* (few wants) → *subharata* (easy satisfaction). Each ripens into the next. The verse closes with the warning against *asura-tva* — the disposition of anger — using the powerful image that an angry monk is no longer a saint but an asura. The remedy is not effort but *remembrance*: hearing the Jina's teaching and keeping it in mind. Anger dissolves not by suppression but by recall.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk lives on plain dry food, content, wanting little, easily filled. Hearing the Jina's word, he refuses to slip into anger.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you remember a deep teaching at the right moment, what passion in you simply lets go?

## SUTRA 8.26 — Restraint of All Five Senses

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कण्णसोक्खेहिं सद्देहिं, पेमं णाभिनिवेसए ।
दारुणं कक्कसं फासं, कायेण अहियासए ॥८.२६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.26 ||
kaṇṇasokkhehiṃ saddehiṃ, pemaṃ ṇābhinivesae |
dāruṇaṃ kakkasaṃ phāsaṃ, kāyeṇa ahiyāsae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Toward sounds that are pleasing to the ear, the monk shall not let attachment establish itself. Toward dreadful and harsh touches, he shall — through the body — endure them with equanimity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कण्णसोक्खेहिं** (kaṇṇasokkhehiṃ) = pleasing-to-the-ear (sweet sounds)
- **सद्देहिं** (saddehiṃ) = on / toward sounds
- **पेमं णाभिनिवेसए** (pemaṃ ṇābhinivesae) = let him not establish attachment (raga)
- **दारुणं कक्कसं फासं** (dāruṇaṃ kakkasaṃ phāsaṃ) = dreadful, harsh touch
- **कायेण अहियासए** (kāyeṇa ahiyāsae) = let him bear (it) with the body

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra is a compressed instruction in two-sided sense-restraint. With the first sense mentioned (hearing) and the last (touch), the verse encloses all five and demands the same discipline of each: do not attach to the favourable, do not recoil from the unfavourable. The Gujarati commentary opens this out into a full doctrine — the monk must hold equal-mindedness toward the *anukula* (favourable) objects of all five senses (which would tempt to raga) and the *pratikula* (unfavourable) objects (which would tempt to dvesha), and through this equanimity he abandons both attachment and aversion. *Dāruṇa* and *kakkasa* are technical terms — the first naming what causes harm (cold, heat, sharp objects), the second naming what is rough (the wear of long walking, hard ground).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When sweet sounds reach you, don't let attachment take hold; when harsh painful touches come, bear them through the body with equanimity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which sense in you is most quickly captured — and which most quickly repelled?

## SUTRA 8.27 — Endurance as the Great Fruit

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खुहं पिवासं दुस्सेज्जं, सीउण्हं अरइं भयं ।
अहियासे अव्वहिओ, देहदुक्खं महाफलं ॥८.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.27 ||
khuhaṃ pivāsaṃ dussejjaṃ, sīuṇhaṃ araiṃ bhayaṃ |
ahiyāse avvahio, dehadukkhaṃ mahāphalaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Hunger, thirst, an uncomfortable bed, cold, heat, mental restlessness, fear — let the unagitated monk bear all these. The pain of the body (so endured) is of great fruit.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **खुहं** (khuhaṃ) = hunger
- **पिवासं** (pivāsaṃ) = thirst
- **दुस्सेज्जं** (dussejjaṃ) = uncomfortable / harsh sleeping place
- **सीउण्हं** (sīuṇhaṃ) = cold and heat
- **अरइं** (araiṃ) = displeasure / restlessness
- **भयं** (bhayaṃ) = fear
- **अहियासे** (ahiyāse) = let him endure (with composure)
- **अव्वहिओ** (avvahio) = unagitated, undistressed
- **देहदुक्खं महाफलं** (dehadukkhaṃ mahāphalaṃ) = the body's suffering is of great fruit (yields liberation)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the chapter's most quoted verses — *deha-dukkhaṃ mahāphalaṃ*: "the body's suffering is of great fruit." It states the foundational logic of monastic austerity. The seven parishaha (afflictions) listed cover the body's most basic distresses; instead of fleeing them, the monk *bears* them with an unagitated mind. The Gujarati commentary deepens this with two readings: (1) the natural reading — body-pain endured equanimously is the great fruit of liberation; (2) the deeper reading — *as long as you have a body, suffering is in the body, not in you*; remembering the body's transience and frangibility (*kshana-bhangurata*), you bear the pain by realising it does not touch the soul. Either way, the equanimous endurance of physical pain is the engine of mokshic transformation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Hunger, thirst, hard beds, cold, heat, restlessness, fear — bear them all without flinching. Body-pain endured this way bears the great fruit of liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What you are unwilling to endure in the body — what does it cost the soul?

## SUTRA 8.28 — Renunciation of Night-Eating

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अत्थंगयम्मि आइच्चे, पुरत्था य अणुग्गए ।
आहारमाइयं सव्वं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ॥८.२८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.28 ||
atthaṃgayammi āicce, puratthā ya aṇuggae |
āhāramāiyaṃ savvaṃ, maṇasā vi ṇa patthae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

After the sun has set, and before it has risen in the east — the monk shall not even mentally desire any kind of food, drink, or any other ingestible.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अत्थंगयम्मि आइच्चे** (atthaṃgayammi āicce) = when the sun has set
- **पुरत्था य अणुग्गए** (puratthā ya aṇuggae) = and before it has risen in the east
- **आहारमाइयं सव्वं** (āhāramāiyaṃ savvaṃ) = all kinds of food and the like (āhāra-ādi: the four kinds of food)
- **मणसा वि ण पत्थए** (maṇasā vi ṇa patthae) = he shall not even mentally desire

### 5. COMMENTARY

The vow of *ratri-bhojan-tyaga* (renunciation of night-eating) is a sixth fundamental vow for Jain monks (in addition to the five great vows) and is a core lay vow as well. The biological reason — that countless small life-forms move into food in the dark — supports the spiritual reason: that night-eating tends toward indulgence and dulls the discipline of the dawn. The remarkable feature of this sutra is the depth of its prohibition: *manasa vi na patthae* — "not even by mind." Where the lay vow merely forbids the act of eating, the monk's vow forbids even the *desire* for it. To fantasise about food at night is itself a violation of the vow. The Gujarati commentary explains: "even the wish (sankalpa, abhilasha) for night food makes the vow defective."

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

From sundown to sunrise, the monk doesn't eat — and doesn't even let his mind want to eat.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What vow do you keep externally but mentally violate every night?

## SUTRA 8.29 — Six Qualities for Inner Peace

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अतितिणे अचवले, अप्पभासी मियासणे ।
हवेज्ज उयरे दंते, थोवं लद्धुं न खिंसए ॥८.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.29 ||
atitiṇe acavale, appabhāsī miyāsaṇe |
havejja uyare daṃte, thovaṃ laddhuṃ na khiṃsae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The monk shall be) free of restless complaining, free of fickleness, of measured speech, of moderate eating — a tamer of the stomach; receiving little (in alms), he shall not blame (the giver).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अतितिणे** (atitiṇe) = free of *tantanāt* (restless agitation, complaint) — tan-tanāt-rahit
- **अचवले** (acavale) = free of fickleness / restless behaviour
- **अप्पभासी** (appabhāsī) = of little / measured speech
- **मियासणे** (miyāsaṇe) = of moderate eating (mita-ashana)
- **हवेज्ज उयरे दंते** (havejja uyare daṃte) = let him be the *tamer of the belly*
- **थोवं लद्धुं न खिंसए** (thovaṃ laddhuṃ na khiṃsae) = receiving (only) a little, let him not censure (the donor)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Six chained qualities for the monk's inner peace: (1) no restless complaining (atitine), (2) no behavioural fickleness (acavale), (3) measured speech (appabhasi), (4) measured eating (miyasane), (5) tamed belly (uyare dante), (6) no blame for small alms (thovam laddhum na khimsae). Each one disciplines a specific channel of disturbance. The fifth and sixth, *udar-danta* and *na khimsae*, are particularly important: to "tame the belly" is to refuse to be enslaved by hunger, and to "not censure" the giver is to refuse to project one's own deprivation outward as blame. Together they produce an inwardly serene monk who walks lightly through the world.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't fret, don't fidget, speak little, eat little, master your stomach, and if you get only a little alms — don't blame the giver.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you receive less than you wanted, where does your mind first reach to place the blame?

## SUTRA 8.30 — No Self-Praise, No Disparagement of Others

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण बाहिरं परिभवे, अत्ताणं ण समुक्कसे ।
सुय लाभे ण मज्जेज्जा, जच्चा तवस्सि बुद्धिए ॥८.३०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.30 ||
ṇa bāhiraṃ paribhave, attāṇaṃ ṇa samukkase |
suya lābhe ṇa majjejjā, jaccā tavassi buddhie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Let him not disparage another, nor exalt himself. Having gained scriptural knowledge, let him not become intoxicated by it; nor by his birth, nor by his austerities, nor by his intellect.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण बाहिरं परिभवे** (ṇa bāhiraṃ paribhave) = let him not despise / belittle others (bāhiraṃ = the external other)
- **अत्ताणं ण समुक्कसे** (attāṇaṃ ṇa samukkase) = let him not exalt himself (samutkarṣa = praising oneself)
- **सुय लाभे ण मज्जेज्जा** (suya lābhe ṇa majjejjā) = upon gaining scriptural knowledge, let him not be intoxicated
- **जच्चा तवस्सि बुद्धिए** (jaccā tavassi buddhie) = nor by birth (jati), by austerity (tapas), or by intellect (buddhi)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra targets *māna-kashāya* (the passion of pride) on the two faces by which it appears: *ātma-praśaṃsā* (self-praise) and *para-paribhava* (disparagement of others). Self-praise inflates the ego upward; disparagement crushes others downward. Both arise from the same root and both block spiritual progress. The text names four classical grounds on which a monk might be tempted to pride — birth (jati), austerities (tapas), intellect (buddhi), and scriptural attainment (shruta-labha) — and forbids intoxication on any of them. The Gujarati commentary cites two tight scriptural lines: *je paribhavayi paraṃ jaṇaṃ saṃsāre parivattati mahaṃ* — "he who disparages others wanders long in samsara" — and *na paraṃ vajjijasi ayaṃ kusīle* — "do not call another low or wicked." A monk who has gained much may, through self-importance, lose all.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't put others down; don't pump yourself up. Don't be drunk on your scripture, your birth, your austerity, or your intellect.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In which of the four — birth, austerity, intellect, learning — do you most easily become inflated?

## SUTRA 8.31 — Quick Withdrawal from Wrong Conduct

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से जाणमजाणं वा, कट्टु आहम्मियं पयं ।
संवरे खिप्पमप्पाणं, बीयं तं ण समायरे ॥८.३१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.31 ||
se jāṇamajāṇaṃ vā, kaṭṭu āhammiyaṃ payaṃ |
saṃvare khippamappāṇaṃ, bīyaṃ taṃ ṇa samāyare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the monk — knowingly or unknowingly — has performed an unrighteous act, let him quickly draw himself back (in samvara), and let him never repeat that act a second time.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **से** (se) = that monk
- **जाणमजाणं वा** (jāṇamajāṇaṃ vā) = knowingly or unknowingly
- **कट्टु आहम्मियं पयं** (kaṭṭu āhammiyaṃ payaṃ) = having done an unrighteous / discipline-violating deed
- **संवरे खिप्पमप्पाणं** (saṃvare khippamappāṇaṃ) = let him quickly restrain himself / withdraw
- **बीयं तं ण समायरे** (bīyaṃ taṃ ṇa samāyare) = and not perform it a second time

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse acknowledges what so many disciplinary texts will not: the monk *will* err — sometimes through ignorance of the rule, sometimes through momentary capture by passion. The sutra does not despair of this; it gives a precise twofold remedy. *First*, immediate samvara — quick interior withdrawal from the wrong, instant alochana before the Guru, and prayashchitta. *Second*, total resolve never to repeat: because each repetition deepens the *samskara* (habit-impression) and a deepened samskara becomes an addiction. The text's deep insight is that the recovery is not in the regret but in the non-repetition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If a monk does something wrong — knowing or not — he stops himself fast and never does it again.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The wound of an old habit is not the first slip; it is the second. Where in your life is the second?

## SUTRA 8.32 — Confession Without Concealment

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणायारं परक्कम्म, णेव गूहे ण णिण्हवे ।
सुई सया वियडभावे, असंसत्ते जिइंदिए ॥८.३२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.32 ||
aṇāyāraṃ parakkamma, ṇeva gūhe ṇa ṇiṇhave |
suī sayā viyaḍabhāve, asaṃsatte jiindie ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having performed an unmonastic act, the monk shall neither hide it (slightly) nor deny it (entirely). Let him be ever pure-hearted, manifest in disposition, unattached, and master of his senses.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अणायारं** (aṇāyāraṃ) = an act outside monastic conduct
- **परक्कम्म** (parakkamma) = having committed
- **णेव गूहे** (ṇeva gūhe) = let him not slightly conceal
- **ण णिण्हवे** (ṇa ṇiṇhave) = let him not entirely deny / hide
- **सुई सया** (suī sayā) = always pure-hearted
- **वियडभावे** (viyaḍabhāve) = of manifest, transparent disposition
- **असंसत्ते** (asaṃsatte) = unattached
- **जिइंदिए** (jiindie) = master of the senses (jitendriya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the second half of the failure-recovery teaching. Sutra 31 said: do not repeat. Sutra 32 says: do not hide. Two technical verbs are used — *gūhe* (to hide partially, to soften the truth) and *ṇiṇhave* (to deny entirely, to claim it never happened). The monk is forbidden both. The Gujarati commentary explains why: confession (alochana) before the Guru is itself a kind of penance, and accepting one's fault as a fault is the very first step that restores purity. A monk who hides is one who stays bound; a monk who confesses transparently and accepts whatever prayashchitta the Guru imposes becomes — over time — *asamsakta* (unattached) and *jitendriya* (sense-conqueror). The path to mastery passes through honesty, not concealment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk has done something wrong, he doesn't hide a part of it or deny all of it. He stays pure, transparent, unattached, and self-controlled.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What about your transgressions do you partially soften — and what do you completely deny?

## SUTRA 8.33 — The Discipline of the Pupil

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अमोहं वयणं कुज्जा, आयरियस्स महप्पणो ।
तं परिगिज्झ वायाए, कम्मुणा उववायए ॥८.३३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.33 ||
amohaṃ vayaṇaṃ kujjā, āyariyassa mahappaṇo |
taṃ parigijjha vāyāe, kammuṇā uvavāyae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The (pupil-monk) shall make fruitful — never let go to waste — the words of the great-souled Acharya. Having accepted them with speech (e.g. "tahatti"), he shall fulfil them by deed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अमोहं वयणं कुज्जा** (amohaṃ vayaṇaṃ kujjā) = let him make (the Acharya's) word amogha — fruitful, not wasted
- **आयरियस्स महप्पणो** (āyariyassa mahappaṇo) = of the Acharya, the great-souled one
- **तं परिगिज्झ वायाए** (taṃ parigijjha vāyāe) = having accepted (the instruction) by speech (saying *tahatti*, "so be it")
- **कम्मुणा उववायए** (kammuṇā uvavāyae) = let him fulfil it by action

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra defines the pupil's discipline as a three-stage cycle: hear, accept, fulfil. The pupil hears the Acharya's instruction, accepts it verbally with the formula *tahatti* ("yes, so it is"), and then enacts it with the body. Only when all three stages are complete is the Acharya's word *amogha* — "not in vain," fruitful. Verbal-only acceptance is mere lip-service; physical-only obedience without inner consent is also incomplete. The full cycle of mind-speech-body is required for true discipleship. This is the foundational logic of Jain *vinaya* (humility-discipline) — the very lifeblood of monastic learning.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The pupil makes the Acharya's word fruitful — accepts it aloud (with "yes, so be it") and then carries it out in deed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you receive guidance from a teacher, where does the cycle break — at hearing, at accepting, or at doing?

## SUTRA 8.34 — The Brevity of Life

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अधुवं जीवियं णच्चा, सिद्धिमग्गं वियाणिया ।
विणियट्टिज्ज भोगेसु, आउं परिमियमप्पणो ॥८.३४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.34 ||
adhuvaṃ jīviyaṃ ṇaccā, siddhimaggaṃ viyāṇiyā |
viṇiyaṭṭijja bhogesu, āuṃ parimiyamappaṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowing life to be impermanent, knowing the path to liberation, knowing one's own life-span to be brief and bounded — let him withdraw from sensual indulgences.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अधुवं जीवियं णच्चा** (adhuvaṃ jīviyaṃ ṇaccā) = knowing life to be impermanent
- **सिद्धिमग्गं वियाणिया** (siddhimaggaṃ viyāṇiyā) = knowing the path of liberation
- **विणियट्टिज्ज भोगेसु** (viṇiyaṭṭijja bhogesu) = let him retire from sensual pleasures
- **आउं परिमियमप्पणो** (āuṃ parimiyamappaṇo) = his own life-span being brief and limited

### 5. COMMENTARY

The teaching pivot for the whole next stretch of the chapter. The argument has three premises and one conclusion. *Premise 1*: life is impermanent. *Premise 2*: the path of liberation is now known to you. *Premise 3*: your own life is short. *Conclusion*: therefore turn away from sensual indulgence. The logic is unsparing. The human birth in the karmabhumi with access to the Jina-teaching is statistically rare; squandering it on bhoga is to throw away the rarest opportunity of countless births.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Life is short and uncertain, the way out is known, your time is limited — so step back from sensory pleasures.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you knew your remaining time was measured in days, what would suddenly become unworth doing?

## SUTRA 8.35 — Practise While Capable

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बलं थामं च पेहाए, सद्धामारुग्गमप्पणो ।
खेत्तं कालं च विण्णाय, तहप्पाणं णिजुंजए ॥८.३५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.35 ||
balaṃ thāmaṃ ca pehāe, saddhāmāruggamappaṇo |
khettaṃ kālaṃ ca viṇṇāya, tahappāṇaṃ ṇijuṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Examining one's own physical strength, mental firmness, faith, and health — and discerning the right place and the right time — let one engage oneself in spiritual practice accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बलं** (balaṃ) = (physical) strength
- **थामं** (thāmaṃ) = mental firmness / steadiness (sthāman)
- **पेहाए** (pehāe) = having examined
- **सद्धा** (saddhā) = faith / shraddha
- **आरुग्गं** (āruggaṃ) = health (arogya)
- **अप्पणो** (appaṇo) = of oneself
- **खेत्तं कालं च विण्णाय** (khettaṃ kālaṃ ca viṇṇāya) = knowing the field (place) and the time
- **तहप्पाणं णिजुंजए** (tahappāṇaṃ ṇijuṃjae) = let him engage himself accordingly

### 5. COMMENTARY

A monk's practice cannot be a one-size-fits-all imposition; it must be calibrated. The sutra names six factors for self-assessment — body-strength, mental firmness, faith, health, the right place, the right time. The monk must not undertake more than capacity allows (because broken austerities damage faith and reputation), nor less than capacity permits (because wasting capacity is its own sin). The Gujarati commentary stresses that what *kshetra* and *kala* (place and time) demand differs from region to region and age to age; the wise monk reads both internal and external conditions before committing to a tap or vow.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Look at your strength, your firmness, your faith, your health — read the place and the time — then take up the practice that fits.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What practice have you undertaken without measuring yourself, and what practice have you avoided because you measured yourself too small?

## SUTRA 8.36 — Practise Before Decline

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जरा जाव ण पीडेइ, वाही जाव ण वड्ढइ ।
जाविंदिया ण हायंति, ताव धम्मं समायरे ॥८.३६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 8.36 ||
jarā jāva ṇa pīḍei, vāhī jāva ṇa vaḍḍhai |
jāviṃdiyā ṇa hāyaṃti, tāva dhammaṃ samāyare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

So long as old age does not yet press, so long as disease does not yet grow, so long as the senses do not yet decline — practise the dharma now.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जरा जाव ण पीडेइ** (jarā jāva ṇa pīḍei) = so long as old age does not afflict
- **वाही जाव ण वड्ढइ** (vāhī jāva ṇa vaḍḍhai) = so long as disease does not increase
- **जाविंदिया ण हायंति** (jāviṃdiyā ṇa hāyaṃti) = so long as the senses do not weaken
- **ताव धम्मं समायरे** (tāva dhammaṃ samāyare) = until then, practise the dharma

### 5. COMMENTARY

A famous urgency-verse, paired with 8.35. Practice has prerequisites — a body still functional, senses still responsive, mind still firm. All three decay with time. The sutra commands the practitioner to act *before* decline arrives, not after. The Gujarati commentary observes that postponement is itself a subtle form of pramada (negligence); when the body fails, even the basic mahavratas become hard to keep, let alone the higher tap. *Therefore: while you can, you must.*

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before old age pains you, before disease grows in you, before your senses fail — practise the dharma now.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What are you postponing for "later"? On what basis do you assume that "later" will come?
