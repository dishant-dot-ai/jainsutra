# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 2
# Pindeshana (પિંડેષણા) — The Investigation of Alms (Part 2)

**Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 2 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 204 onward (PDF pages 264 onward)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fifth Adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is called **Pindeshana** — "the careful investigation of the food-lump." It is divided into two Uddeshas (sub-chapters). The first Uddesha (one hundred verses) covered the alms-tour itself: how a monk walks to the householder's door, what to accept, what to reject, the forty-two faults of food-collection (eshana doshas), the proper etiquette inside the home, and the formal procedures of confession (alochana) and renunciation (kayotsarga) on returning to the lodging. With that earlier Uddesha the food has been brought back; now the question is what to do with it.

This second Uddesha (Bije Uddeshak) takes up the conduct *after* the alms have been received. Its themes are: how the monk eats (without leaving residue, without choosing flavours, with cleanliness in the bowl); the discipline of taste (eating sweet or bitter food with the same equanimity); the rare second alms-tour and its proper time and conditions; how to pass other beggars and other living creatures on the road without disturbing them; how to behave at the door of a house where another mendicant is already begging; how to share the alms with one's co-monks under the Acharya, the Upadhyaya, and the elders; and finally the careful disposal of any food that is unfit, leftover, or accidentally fallen — disposing of it on lifeless ground, away from living beings, with full mindfulness.

Where the first Uddesha guarded the *acquiring* of food from injury and impurity, this second Uddesha guards the *consuming* of it — so that the body which is a vehicle for liberation is sustained without the mind being entangled in like and dislike. The chapter closes with the great verse "ધૂમ-દોષ" — the fault of "smoke" — that the monk who eats with even the faintest preference for one taste over another defiles an otherwise pure meal. The arc of the entire fifth Adhyayan is therefore complete: from the doorstep, to the bowl, to the mouth, to the heart that must remain unattached.

## SUTRA 5.2.1 — Eating Without Leaving a Residue

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिग्गहं संलिहित्ताणं, लेवमायाए संजए ।
दुगंधं वा सुगंधं वा, सव्वं भुंजे ण छड्डुए ॥५.२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.1 ||
paḍiggahaṃ saṃlihittāṇaṃ, levamāyāe saṃjae |
dugaṃdhaṃ vā sugaṃdhaṃ vā, savvaṃ bhuṃje ṇa chaḍḍue ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk, having wiped the alms-bowl clean with his finger down to the last smear of food-residue, should eat all of it — whether ill-smelling or sweet-smelling — and not throw away even the smallest portion.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **पडिग्गहं** (paḍiggahaṃ) = the alms-bowl (patra)
- **लेवमायाए** (levamāyāe) = down to the very residue / smear of food clinging to the bowl
- **संलिहित्ताणं** (saṃlihittāṇaṃ) = having wiped clean (with the finger)
- **दुगंधं** (dugaṃdhaṃ) = ill-smelling / unpleasant
- **वा** (vā) = or
- **सुगंधं** (sugaṃdhaṃ) = pleasant-smelling / agreeable
- **सव्वं** (savvaṃ) = all of it (the entire alms)
- **भुंजे** (bhuṃje) = should eat / consume
- **ण छड्डुए** (ṇa chaḍḍue) = should not throw away / leave behind even a little

### 5. COMMENTARY

This first verse of the second Uddesha sets the keynote of the whole chapter: when the monk eats, he must finish what he has accepted — no leftover, no food thrown away, no preference shown. The image is precise. The monk wipes the inner curve of the bowl with his finger so that not even a smear of food clings to it; whatever was placed in the bowl by the householder is consumed in full.

Two reasons stand behind this discipline. The first is the protection of life: any food carelessly discarded becomes a breeding ground for ants, flies, and microscopic beings, and so the monk who throws food away creates injury where there was none. The second is the discipline of taste. The terms "ill-smelling" and "pleasant-smelling" here are not literal — the commentators clarify that they stand for the whole spectrum of agreeable and disagreeable food that a monk receives. By eating both with the same care, the monk refuses to let his tongue dictate his choices. To leave the disagreeable food and finish only the agreeable would be to surrender to the senses; the alms-tour was not undertaken to please the body but to sustain it.

There is a third, subtler implication. The monk who throws away food disrespects the householder's gift, displays distaste in front of others, and brings disrepute to the order. Cleanliness of bowl and equanimity of palate together form a single discipline — the outer act and the inner attitude reinforce each other.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After taking food from a household, the monk should eat every last bit of it — even wiping the bowl clean with his finger — whether the food tastes good or bad, and never throw any of it away.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you eat something you do not like, can you finish it with the same calm gratitude as something you love?

## SUTRA 5.2.2-3 — When a Second Alms-Tour Becomes Necessary

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सेज्जा निसीहियाए, समावण्णो य गोयरे ।
अयावयट्ठा भोच्चाणं, जइ तेणं ण संथरे ॥५.२.२॥

तओ कारणसमुप्पण्णे, भत्तपाणं गवेसए ।
विहिणा पुव्वुत्तेणं, इमेण उत्तरेण य ॥५.२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.2 ||
sejjā nisīhiyāe, samāvaṇṇo ya goyare |
ayāvayaṭṭhā bhoccāṇaṃ, jai teṇaṃ ṇa saṃthare ||

|| 5.2.3 ||
tao kāraṇasamuppaṇṇe, bhattapāṇaṃ gavesae |
vihiṇā puvvuttaeṇaṃ, imeṇa uttareṇa ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(2) If the monk, having returned with alms to the resting place or the study-place and eaten what he has obtained, finds that the food was insufficient and his sustenance is not maintained by it —
(3) Then, when such a need has arisen, he should go in search of food and drink again, by the procedure already taught (in the first Uddesha) and by the further procedure to be taught here.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सेज्जा** (sejjā) = at the lodging / resting place
- **निसीहियाए** (nisīhiyāe) = at the study-place (svadhyaya bhumi) or short rest-place
- **समावण्णो** (samāvaṇṇo) = having arrived (with the alms)
- **गोयरे** (goyare) = from the alms-tour
- **अयावयट्ठा** (ayāvayaṭṭhā) = insufficient — less than what is needed for sustenance
- **भोच्चाणं** (bhoccāṇaṃ) = having eaten
- **जइ तेणं ण संथरे** (jai teṇaṃ ṇa saṃthare) = if he is not sustained by it
- **तओ** (tao) = then
- **कारणसमुप्पण्णे** (kāraṇasamuppaṇṇe) = when such a (legitimate) cause has arisen
- **भत्तपाणं गवेसए** (bhattapāṇaṃ gavesae) = let him search for food and drink (again)
- **विहिणा पुव्वुत्तेणं** (vihiṇā puvvuttaeṇaṃ) = by the procedure already stated (in the first Uddesha)
- **इमेण उत्तरेण य** (imeṇa uttareṇa ya) = and by the further procedure stated here

### 5. COMMENTARY

The general rule is one alms-tour per day. But the verse acknowledges that life does not always cooperate with the rule. The monk may, on a particular day, have brought back too little food — perhaps fewer households gave, or what was given did not last, or the monk underestimated what his fellows would need. In that case, having eaten what he has, hunger is not pacified and bodily strength is not restored.

The verse then permits a second alms-tour, but only on condition: a *legitimate cause* must have arisen. This is not a loophole for greed; it is a concession for genuine need. The commentary lists possible reasons — illness in the community, an unexpected guest, severe hunger, weather that interrupted the first tour, fasting practitioners who need a small amount of food to break their austerity, travel ahead that requires energy, and so on. In all such cases the monk does not simply walk out again; he applies the same discipline taught in the first Uddesha — the forty-two faults to be avoided, the proper time, the careful inspection of food and place — and additionally observes the further rules that this second Uddesha is about to teach.

The structure of these two verses is therefore a hinge between the two Uddeshas: the first taught the basic procedure, the second teaches the extension. The monk's freedom is real but disciplined — he may go again, but only under cause, and only by procedure.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If after one alms-round and eating the food the monk still does not have enough to sustain him, only then — and following all the same careful rules — may he go out for a second round.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you allow yourself an exception to a rule you live by, can you tell honestly whether the cause is real need or quiet desire?

## SUTRA 5.2.4 — The Right Time for Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कालेण णिक्खमे भिक्खू, कालेण य पडिक्कमे ।
अकालं च विवज्जित्ता, काले कालं समायरे ॥५.२.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.4 ||
kāleṇa ṇikkhame bhikkhū, kāleṇa ya paḍikkame |
akālaṃ ca vivajjittā, kāle kālaṃ samāyare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should go out (for alms) at the right time and return at the right time; avoiding the wrong time, he should perform each task at its appropriate time.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **कालेण** (kāleṇa) = at the (proper) time
- **णिक्खमे** (ṇikkhame) = should go out (for alms)
- **य** (ya) = and
- **कालेण** (kāleṇa) = at the (proper) time
- **पडिक्कमे** (paḍikkame) = should return (to the lodging)
- **अकालं** (akālaṃ) = the wrong time / unfit time
- **विवज्जित्ता** (vivajjittā) = having abandoned / avoided
- **काले** (kāle) = at the (right) time
- **कालं समायरे** (kālaṃ samāyare) = he should perform the (corresponding) timely activity

### 5. COMMENTARY

Time-consciousness is a deep theme in monastic life. The monk's day is divided into segments — study, meditation, alms-tour, eating, rest — and each has its appointed slot. To collect alms, the monk should go when householders are normally cooking and eating, because food is then naturally being prepared and the gift causes no extra trouble. Going too early disturbs sleeping families; going too late catches them with leftovers or with no food at all, putting the householder in awkwardness.

The verse also has a second, broader meaning. "Right time" includes not only the clock-time of the village but also the right circumstance: weather, the monk's own physical condition, the rhythm of the lodging's other duties. A monk who tries to do everything regardless of timing — alms in monsoon-rain, study at dinner-hour, rest when others are wakeful — burdens both himself and the community. The verse's repeated use of the word "kāla" (time) is deliberate; the monk is being asked to live in harmony with the unfolding of the day rather than impose his will upon it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should go out for food at the proper time, come back at the proper time, and avoid wrong times — doing each thing when it is naturally meant to be done.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much friction in your day comes from doing the right thing at the wrong time?

## SUTRA 5.2.5 — The Cost of Wrong Timing

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अकाले चरसि भिक्खू, कालं ण पडिलेहसि ।
अप्पाणं च किलामेसि, सण्णिवेसं च गरिहसि ॥५.२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.5 ||
akāle carasi bhikkhū, kālaṃ ṇa paḍilehasi |
appāṇaṃ ca kilāmesi, saṇṇivesaṃ ca garihasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O monk! If you wander (for alms) at the wrong time, you do not consider the proper time; you exhaust your own self, and you (end up) blaming the very village (that gave nothing).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = O monk!
- **अकाले** (akāle) = at the wrong time
- **चरसि** (carasi) = (if) you go (for alms)
- **कालं** (kālaṃ) = the proper time
- **ण पडिलेहसि** (ṇa paḍilehasi) = you do not look out for / inspect
- **अप्पाणं** (appāṇaṃ) = your own self
- **किलामेसि** (kilāmesi) = you make weary, you cause distress to
- **सण्णिवेसं** (saṇṇivesaṃ) = the village / settlement
- **गरिहसि** (garihasi) = you blame, you slander

### 5. COMMENTARY

Where the previous verse stated the rule positively, this verse states it as a warning, addressed almost in the second-person voice of a teacher correcting a young disciple. Three consequences follow from going at the wrong time. First, **bodily exhaustion** — the monk walks the village and returns with little or nothing, having spent his strength for no result. Second, **failure of mindfulness** — by ignoring the time, he has already broken the fundamental discipline of being present in the right activity at the right moment. Third, and subtlest, **slander of the village** — finding no alms, the monk's mind drifts into "this place gave nothing, these people are without faith," when in fact the fault was his own timing.

This is a profound psychological observation. When we fail because of our own miscalculation, we are deeply tempted to blame the circumstance. The verse holds the monk accountable: if you went at the wrong time, the empty bowl is not the village's fault, it is yours; do not slander the very people whose hospitality keeps the order alive. To preserve right speech and right view, one must first take responsibility for one's own timing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If a monk goes out for food at the wrong time, he tires himself for nothing and then unfairly blames the town that had nothing to give him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something you tried did not work, how often is it tempting to blame the world rather than examine your own timing?

## SUTRA 5.2.6 — Equanimity When Alms Are Not Received

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सइ काले चरे भिक्खू, कुज्जा पुरिसकारियं ।
अलाभो त्ति ण सोएज्जा, तवो त्ति अहियासए ॥५.२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.6 ||
sai kāle care bhikkhū, kujjā purisakāriyaṃ |
alābho tti ṇa soejjā, tavo tti ahiyāsae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should go out at the proper time and make full effort; (yet) if there is no gain, he should not grieve over it — bearing it (instead) as an austerity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **सइ** (sai) = at the proper time / when the time is right
- **काले** (kāle) = at the (alms-)time
- **चरे** (care) = should go (for alms)
- **पुरिसकारियं** (purisakāriyaṃ) = manly effort / personal endeavour
- **कुज्जा** (kujjā) = should make
- **अलाभो त्ति** (alābho tti) = (with the thought) "non-gain" / "I got nothing"
- **ण सोएज्जा** (ṇa soejjā) = should not grieve, should not be saddened
- **तवो त्ति** (tavo tti) = (with the thought) "this is austerity"
- **अहियासए** (ahiyāsae) = should bear / endure (the hunger and the thirst)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is among the most quoted in Jain monastic teaching, because it captures the heart of the monk's relationship to food. The procedure is two-step: do everything in your power, then accept whatever comes. Step one is *purisakāra* — go at the right time, walk through the village, knock at doors, make the full effort. Step two is *anavishada* — if despite all effort the bowl remains empty, do not be downcast.

The reframing is the masterstroke. What looks like a failure (no alms received) is recast as a success of a different kind: an unplanned austerity (tapas) has spontaneously arrived. The monk who in another context might have undertaken a one-day fast as a chosen discipline now receives that very fast as a gift of circumstance. Seen this way, there is nothing to grieve — the body's hunger today is the soul's purification.

This is not denial of pain. The verse explicitly uses the word **ahiyāsae** — "endure," "bear with steadiness." Hunger is real, the body does feel it. But the monk's attitude transforms the experience: the same hunger that breeds frustration in one mind breeds equanimity in another. The Jain insistence on *bhavana* (mental disposition) as the deciding factor in the karmic effect of any event finds clear expression here.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should go out at the right time and try his best, but if he gets no food he should not be sad — he should accept the hunger as a kind of fast quietly given to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When your best effort produces nothing, can you receive the empty result as a gift instead of a grievance?

## SUTRA 5.2.7 — Avoiding Animals Gathered for Their Own Food

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेवुच्चावया पाणा, भत्तट्ठाए समागया ।
तं उज्जुयं ण गच्छेज्जा, जयमेव परक्कमे ॥५.२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.7 ||
tahevuccāvayā pāṇā, bhattaṭṭhāe samāgayā |
taṃ ujjuyaṃ ṇa gacchejjā, jayameva parakkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, when small and large creatures have gathered (in some place) for the sake of food, the monk should not go straight (through that place); only with mindful care should he proceed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise / similarly
- **उच्चावया पाणा** (uccāvayā pāṇā) = small and large living beings (animals, birds, insects)
- **भत्तट्ठाए** (bhattaṭṭhāe) = for the sake of food (and drink)
- **समागया** (samāgayā) = gathered, assembled
- **तं** (taṃ) = (toward) them / (toward) that place
- **उज्जुयं** (ujjuyaṃ) = straight / directly
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not go
- **जयमेव** (jayameva) = with mindful care alone
- **परक्कमे** (parakkame) = should proceed / move

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk on his alms-tour walks through a living world. The verse imagines a scene that would be ordinary in any village: birds and small animals have gathered at a spot to eat their own food — perhaps grain scattered for them, perhaps water in a trough, perhaps fruit fallen from a tree. The monk's path crosses this gathering. What should he do?

The instruction is precise. He should not walk *straight through* the gathering, because his approach would frighten the birds into flight, the animals into scattering, and would interrupt their meal. To break their meal is itself a form of *antaraya* — placing an obstacle between a being and its food, which Jain ethics regards as a serious harm. Either some other path should be chosen, or, if none exists, the monk should walk with extreme mindfulness — slowly, quietly, taking a route around them, so that the creatures' meal is not disturbed.

This rule is not about kindness in the sentimental sense. It is the ahimsa of detail: a great vow of non-harm sustained by the smallest acts of attention. The monk who would never strike an animal must also never frighten one; the monk who would never withhold his own food must also never deny another creature theirs.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk sees small or large animals gathered somewhere to eat, he should not walk straight through them — he should take another path or move around them very carefully so as not to disturb their meal.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In your daily walking and moving, how often do you notice the smaller lives whose moment you are about to cross?

## SUTRA 5.2.8 — Not Sitting and Not Preaching at the Householder's Door

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य, ण णिसीएज्ज कत्थई ।
कहं च ण पबंधेज्जा, चिट्ठित्ताण व संजए ॥५.२.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.8 ||
goyaraggapaviṭṭho ya, ṇa ṇisīejja katthaī |
kahaṃ ca ṇa pabaṃdhejjā, ciṭṭhittāṇa va saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having entered (the house) for alms, the restrained monk should not sit down anywhere there; nor, while standing, should he begin a long discourse.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य** (goyaraggapaviṭṭho ya) = and having entered (a house) on the alms-tour
- **कत्थई** (katthaī) = anywhere (in the house)
- **ण णिसीएज्ज** (ṇa ṇisīejja) = should not sit down
- **चिट्ठित्ताण व** (ciṭṭhittāṇa va) = or (even) while standing
- **कहं** (kahaṃ) = a discourse / sermon / extended talk
- **ण पबंधेज्जा** (ṇa pabaṃdhejjā) = should not weave / extend / undertake at length

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk's purpose at the householder's door is narrow and clear: receive the alms, then leave. The verse forbids two natural temptations. The first is **sitting down** — settling into a chair or a corner of the home invites familiarity, prolongs the visit, increases the householder's burden, and may expose the monk to sights, sounds, and conversations that erode the discipline of detachment. The second is **giving a long sermon** — even while standing in the doorway, the monk should not turn the alms-encounter into an extended teaching session. Religious discourse has its proper place (the upashraya, the gathering of disciples) and proper time; the doorstep during the householder's mealtime is neither.

This is a delicate teaching. The monk *is* a teacher, and the householder may even invite a discourse out of devotion. But the verse reminds the monk that the alms-tour is a discipline of austerity, not an opportunity for influence. To linger and teach at every door would convert the begging-bowl into a stage and the discipline into a performance. The monk takes only food; he does not take attention.

The verse also implicitly protects the householder. A long visit interrupts cooking, eating, and family life. The monk's restraint — receiving the alms, blessing the household, and moving on — is itself a form of respect.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk has entered a house for alms, he should not sit down anywhere or, even while standing, give a long sermon — he is there only for food.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you visit someone for one specific purpose, can you keep the visit to that purpose without overstaying or overspeaking?

## SUTRA 5.2.9 — Not Leaning on Anything in the Householder's Home

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अग्गलं फलिहं दारं, कवाडं वावि संजए ।
अवलंबिया ण चिट्ठिज्जा, गोयरग्गगओ मुणी ॥५.२.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.9 ||
aggalaṃ phalihaṃ dāraṃ, kavāḍaṃ vāvi saṃjae |
avalaṃbiyā ṇa ciṭṭhijjā, goyaraggagao muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk who has gone for alms should not stand leaning on a door-bolt, a cross-bar, a doorway, or even a door-panel.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गोयरग्गगओ** (goyaraggagao) = (the monk) who has gone for alms
- **संजए मुणी** (saṃjae muṇī) = the restrained monk
- **अग्गलं** (aggalaṃ) = a small bolt (used to bolt a door)
- **फलिहं** (phalihaṃ) = a cross-bar / large bolt-stick (used to brace a door)
- **दारं** (dāraṃ) = a doorway / door-frame
- **कवाडं** (kavāḍaṃ) = a door-panel
- **वावि** (vāvi) = or
- **अवलंबिया** (avalaṃbiyā) = leaning upon, taking support of
- **ण चिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇa ciṭṭhijjā) = should not stand

### 5. COMMENTARY

The detail of this verse is striking. Four kinds of household fixtures are named — the small bolt, the cross-bar, the door-frame itself, the door-panel — and the monk is told to lean on none of them. The point is universal: while waiting at a householder's home, the monk should stand free, supported only by his own legs.

Why such precision? Three reasons emerge. First, **modesty of bearing**: a monk leaning casually against a doorway looks restless, distracted, even attached to comfort. The household will form an impression that does not befit the order. Second, **possible damage**: the bolt or door-panel may be loose, and if the monk's weight breaks it, the householder suffers a loss for which the monk is responsible — and if the broken piece falls and injures a small creature, there is *jiva-virodhana* as well. Third, **inner attitude**: leaning on something is a small sign of having relaxed one's vigilance; the monk's body should remain alert because the discipline is alert. Even a few minutes at a door is part of the practice.

This is a verse that demonstrates how thoroughly the Jain code of conduct extends into the smallest gestures. Standing without support is not arbitrary fastidiousness; it is the bodily expression of an inner stance — self-supported, attentive, present.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

While waiting for alms at someone's house, the monk should not lean against the door, the doorframe, the bolt, or any part of the entrance — he should stand straight on his own.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you are waiting somewhere briefly, what do you reach for to lean against — and what is it about you that wants the support?

## SUTRA 5.2.10-11 — Yielding to Other Mendicants at the Door

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समणं माहणं वावि, किविणं वा वणीमगं ।
उवसंकमंतं भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१०॥

तमइक्कमित्तु ण पविसे, ण चिट्ठे चक्खुगोयरे ।
एगंतमवक्कमित्ता, तत्थ चिट्ठिज्ज संजए ॥५.२.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.10 ||
samaṇaṃ māhaṇaṃ vāvi, kiviṇaṃ vā vaṇīmagaṃ |
uvasaṃkamaṃtaṃ bhattaṭṭhā, pāṇaṭṭhāe va saṃjae ||

|| 5.2.11 ||
tamaikkamittu ṇa pavise, ṇa ciṭṭhe cakkhugoyare |
egaṃtamavakkamittā, tattha ciṭṭhijja saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(10) (When) a (Buddhist) monk, a brahmin, a destitute person, or a beggar is approaching (a house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water, the restrained monk —
(11) — should not bypass that one and enter (the house first); nor should he stand within their line of sight; he should withdraw to a place apart and stand there.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **समणं** (samaṇaṃ) = a (non-Jain) monk / wandering ascetic
- **माहणं** (māhaṇaṃ) = a brahmin
- **वावि** (vāvi) = or
- **किविणं** (kiviṇaṃ) = a destitute / poor person
- **वा वणीमगं** (vā vaṇīmagaṃ) = or a beggar (one who lives by begging)
- **भत्तट्ठा** (bhattaṭṭhā) = for food
- **पाणट्ठाए व** (pāṇaṭṭhāe va) = or for water
- **उवसंकमंतं** (uvasaṃkamaṃtaṃ) = approaching / coming toward (the house)
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **तं** (taṃ) = that one
- **अइक्कमित्तु** (aikkamittu) = bypassing / overtaking
- **ण पविसे** (ṇa pavise) = should not enter (the house)
- **चक्खुगोयरे** (cakkhugoyare) = within (their) line of sight
- **ण चिट्ठे** (ṇa ciṭṭhe) = should not stand
- **एगंतं** (egaṃtaṃ) = a place apart / secluded spot
- **अवक्कमित्ता** (avakkamittā) = having withdrawn
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there
- **चिट्ठिज्जा** (ciṭṭhijjā) = should stand

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two verses paint a scene that must have been common in ancient India. A householder's door, and at the same moment several seekers of food converging on it — a wandering Buddhist or other ascetic, a brahmin priest expecting his daily portion, a poor man, a beggar, and the Jain monk himself. What is the right thing to do?

The instruction is to yield. The Jain monk should not push ahead of the others, should not overtake them, should not even stand where they can see him competing for the same food. He should withdraw to a place apart and stand there, waiting his turn until the other supplicants have left.

The reasons reach into both ethics and prudence. From the side of ethics, every other being also has a need for food, and the monk who races them to the door violates the spirit of *aparigraha* (non-grasping). From the side of prudence, if a Jain monk is seen competing aggressively for food, the laypeople will lose respect for the order and the householder may feel pressured to favour one over the others, breeding ill-will. From the side of compassion, those other seekers may need the food more than the monk — they may have nowhere else to turn, while the Jain monk has many other doors. To stand aside, out of sight, is to acknowledge their priority quietly, without asking for credit.

The phrase **na chiṭṭhe cakkhugoyare** ("should not stand within their line of sight") is especially fine. Even the *appearance* of competing must be avoided. The monk's discipline is so subtle that it watches not only what he does but what others see him doing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If another monk, brahmin, poor person, or beggar is approaching a house for food or water, the Jain monk should not race ahead of them or even stand where they can see him — he should quietly step aside and wait until they are finished.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When another person is reaching for the same thing you want, can you step aside without resentment and without making a show of stepping aside?

## SUTRA 5.2.12 — Why Yielding Matters

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वणीमगस्स वा तस्स, दायगस्सुभयस्स वा ।
अप्पत्तियं सिया हुज्जा, लहुत्तं पवयणस्स वा ॥५.२.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.12 ||
vaṇīmagassa vā tassa, dāyagassubhayassa vā |
appattiyaṃ siyā hujjā, lahuttaṃ pavayaṇassa vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Otherwise) of that beggar, or of the giver, or of both, displeasure may arise; or there may be a lessening (of esteem) of the (Jain) teaching itself.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया** (siyā) = it may be that (perhaps) — (if the monk does not step aside)
- **तस्स वणीमगस्स** (tassa vaṇīmagassa) = of that beggar (who was approaching)
- **वा दायगस्स** (vā dāyagassa) = or of the giver (the householder)
- **उभयस्स वा** (ubhayassa vā) = or of both
- **अप्पत्तियं** (appattiyaṃ) = displeasure / ill-feeling
- **हुज्जा** (hujjā) = would arise
- **पवयणस्स वा** (pavayaṇassa vā) = or of the doctrine / of the Jain teaching
- **लहुत्तं** (lahuttaṃ) = lightness / lessening of esteem / disrepute

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse explains the reasoning behind the previous one. Three possible harms follow if the Jain monk pushes ahead of the other beggar. First, **the beggar feels displeasure** — humiliated at being overtaken, perhaps angry at losing food he was about to receive. Second, **the householder feels displeasure** — caught in an uncomfortable competition between two needy seekers, and forced to choose. Third, **the order itself is brought low** — observers will say "look how these Jain monks behave, no different from any common scrambler for food," and the *pravachana* (the teaching, the order, the public face of Jain dharma) suffers a loss of dignity.

The verse displays a particularly mature ethical sensibility: an action is wrong not only when it directly harms someone, but also when it generates displeasure, breaks social trust, or diminishes the credibility of the spiritual community. The monk does not live in isolation; everything he does either polishes or tarnishes the reputation of the entire order. That awareness of the wider impact — of one's tiny act on the *pavayana* — is the mark of a mature practitioner.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk pushes ahead of another beggar, the beggar will feel hurt, the householder will feel awkward, and people will think badly of the entire Jain teaching — so the small act of yielding protects everyone.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do your smallest actions carry weight you never see — affecting people, relationships, or causes far beyond what you imagined?

## SUTRA 5.2.13 — When the Other Beggar Has Left

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिसेहिए व दिण्णे वा, तओ तम्मि णियत्तिए ।
उवसंकमेज्ज भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.13 ||
paḍisehie va diṇṇe vā, tao tammi ṇiyattie |
uvasaṃkamejja bhattaṭṭhā, pāṇaṭṭhāe va saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When that (other) beggar has either been refused or has been given (alms), and has thereupon turned away from that place, then the restrained monk may approach (the house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पडिसेहिए** (paḍisehie) = (when the beggar has been) refused
- **व दिण्णे** (va diṇṇe) = or (has been) given (alms)
- **तओ** (tao) = then
- **तम्मि** (tammi) = from that (door / place)
- **णियत्तिए** (ṇiyattie) = (when) he has turned away / withdrawn
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **भत्तट्ठा** (bhattaṭṭhā) = for food
- **पाणट्ठाए व** (pāṇaṭṭhāe va) = or for water
- **उवसंकमेज्ज** (uvasaṃkamejja) = may approach (the house)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the etiquette begun two verses earlier. The Jain monk has stepped aside out of sight; the other beggar has now finished his interaction with the householder — either he was refused (perhaps the household had nothing to give) or he received something. In either case, he has left the doorway. Only now does the Jain monk approach.

The instruction is so simple that it might seem almost over-cautious, but it carries real wisdom. By waiting until the previous transaction is complete and the other person has departed, the monk avoids any appearance of having capitalized on the rejection or competed for the gift. The two visits remain entirely separate. The householder is not pressured by the simultaneous presence of two requests; the previous beggar's experience is not coloured by feeling watched; the monk's request stands on its own.

This three-verse sequence (10-11-12-13) is a small masterpiece of monastic etiquette: the situation is observed, the right action is named, the reasons are given, and the timing of resumption is specified. Few ethical codes combine such practical precision with such delicate sensitivity to the feelings of others.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Once the other beggar has left the door — whether they were given food or refused — only then may the Jain monk go up to the house for his own alms.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something has just gone wrong for someone else, can you wait long enough that your own arrival is not entangled with their disappointment?
