# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 2
# Pindeshana (પિંડેષણા) — The Investigation of Alms (Part 2)

**Adhyayan 5, Uddesha 2 of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 204 onward (PDF pages 264 onward)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fifth Adhyayan of the Dashvaikalik Sutra is called **Pindeshana** — "the careful investigation of the food-lump." It is divided into two Uddeshas (sub-chapters). The first Uddesha (one hundred verses) covered the alms-tour itself: how a monk walks to the householder's door, what to accept, what to reject, the forty-two faults of food-collection (eshana doshas), the proper etiquette inside the home, and the formal procedures of confession (alochana) and renunciation (kayotsarga) on returning to the lodging. With that earlier Uddesha the food has been brought back; now the question is what to do with it.

This second Uddesha (Bije Uddeshak) takes up the conduct *after* the alms have been received. Its themes are: how the monk eats (without leaving residue, without choosing flavours, with cleanliness in the bowl); the discipline of taste (eating sweet or bitter food with the same equanimity); the rare second alms-tour and its proper time and conditions; how to pass other beggars and other living creatures on the road without disturbing them; how to behave at the door of a house where another mendicant is already begging; how to share the alms with one's co-monks under the Acharya, the Upadhyaya, and the elders; and finally the careful disposal of any food that is unfit, leftover, or accidentally fallen — disposing of it on lifeless ground, away from living beings, with full mindfulness.

Where the first Uddesha guarded the *acquiring* of food from injury and impurity, this second Uddesha guards the *consuming* of it — so that the body which is a vehicle for liberation is sustained without the mind being entangled in like and dislike. The chapter closes with the great verse "ધૂમ-દોષ" — the fault of "smoke" — that the monk who eats with even the faintest preference for one taste over another defiles an otherwise pure meal. The arc of the entire fifth Adhyayan is therefore complete: from the doorstep, to the bowl, to the mouth, to the heart that must remain unattached.

## SUTRA 5.2.1 — Eating Without Leaving a Residue

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिग्गहं संलिहित्ताणं, लेवमायाए संजए ।
दुगंधं वा सुगंधं वा, सव्वं भुंजे ण छड्डुए ॥५.२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.1 ||
paḍiggahaṃ saṃlihittāṇaṃ, levamāyāe saṃjae |
dugaṃdhaṃ vā sugaṃdhaṃ vā, savvaṃ bhuṃje ṇa chaḍḍue ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk, having wiped the alms-bowl clean with his finger down to the last smear of food-residue, should eat all of it — whether ill-smelling or sweet-smelling — and not throw away even the smallest portion.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **पडिग्गहं** (paḍiggahaṃ) = the alms-bowl (patra)
- **लेवमायाए** (levamāyāe) = down to the very residue / smear of food clinging to the bowl
- **संलिहित्ताणं** (saṃlihittāṇaṃ) = having wiped clean (with the finger)
- **दुगंधं** (dugaṃdhaṃ) = ill-smelling / unpleasant
- **वा** (vā) = or
- **सुगंधं** (sugaṃdhaṃ) = pleasant-smelling / agreeable
- **सव्वं** (savvaṃ) = all of it (the entire alms)
- **भुंजे** (bhuṃje) = should eat / consume
- **ण छड्डुए** (ṇa chaḍḍue) = should not throw away / leave behind even a little

### 5. COMMENTARY

This first verse of the second Uddesha sets the keynote of the whole chapter: when the monk eats, he must finish what he has accepted — no leftover, no food thrown away, no preference shown. The image is precise. The monk wipes the inner curve of the bowl with his finger so that not even a smear of food clings to it; whatever was placed in the bowl by the householder is consumed in full.

Two reasons stand behind this discipline. The first is the protection of life: any food carelessly discarded becomes a breeding ground for ants, flies, and microscopic beings, and so the monk who throws food away creates injury where there was none. The second is the discipline of taste. The terms "ill-smelling" and "pleasant-smelling" here are not literal — the commentators clarify that they stand for the whole spectrum of agreeable and disagreeable food that a monk receives. By eating both with the same care, the monk refuses to let his tongue dictate his choices. To leave the disagreeable food and finish only the agreeable would be to surrender to the senses; the alms-tour was not undertaken to please the body but to sustain it.

There is a third, subtler implication. The monk who throws away food disrespects the householder's gift, displays distaste in front of others, and brings disrepute to the order. Cleanliness of bowl and equanimity of palate together form a single discipline — the outer act and the inner attitude reinforce each other.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After taking food from a household, the monk should eat every last bit of it — even wiping the bowl clean with his finger — whether the food tastes good or bad, and never throw any of it away.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you eat something you do not like, can you finish it with the same calm gratitude as something you love?

## SUTRA 5.2.2-3 — When a Second Alms-Tour Becomes Necessary

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सेज्जा निसीहियाए, समावण्णो य गोयरे ।
अयावयट्ठा भोच्चाणं, जइ तेणं ण संथरे ॥५.२.२॥

तओ कारणसमुप्पण्णे, भत्तपाणं गवेसए ।
विहिणा पुव्वुत्तेणं, इमेण उत्तरेण य ॥५.२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.2 ||
sejjā nisīhiyāe, samāvaṇṇo ya goyare |
ayāvayaṭṭhā bhoccāṇaṃ, jai teṇaṃ ṇa saṃthare ||

|| 5.2.3 ||
tao kāraṇasamuppaṇṇe, bhattapāṇaṃ gavesae |
vihiṇā puvvuttaeṇaṃ, imeṇa uttareṇa ya ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(2) If the monk, having returned with alms to the resting place or the study-place and eaten what he has obtained, finds that the food was insufficient and his sustenance is not maintained by it —
(3) Then, when such a need has arisen, he should go in search of food and drink again, by the procedure already taught (in the first Uddesha) and by the further procedure to be taught here.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सेज्जा** (sejjā) = at the lodging / resting place
- **निसीहियाए** (nisīhiyāe) = at the study-place (svadhyaya bhumi) or short rest-place
- **समावण्णो** (samāvaṇṇo) = having arrived (with the alms)
- **गोयरे** (goyare) = from the alms-tour
- **अयावयट्ठा** (ayāvayaṭṭhā) = insufficient — less than what is needed for sustenance
- **भोच्चाणं** (bhoccāṇaṃ) = having eaten
- **जइ तेणं ण संथरे** (jai teṇaṃ ṇa saṃthare) = if he is not sustained by it
- **तओ** (tao) = then
- **कारणसमुप्पण्णे** (kāraṇasamuppaṇṇe) = when such a (legitimate) cause has arisen
- **भत्तपाणं गवेसए** (bhattapāṇaṃ gavesae) = let him search for food and drink (again)
- **विहिणा पुव्वुत्तेणं** (vihiṇā puvvuttaeṇaṃ) = by the procedure already stated (in the first Uddesha)
- **इमेण उत्तरेण य** (imeṇa uttareṇa ya) = and by the further procedure stated here

### 5. COMMENTARY

The general rule is one alms-tour per day. But the verse acknowledges that life does not always cooperate with the rule. The monk may, on a particular day, have brought back too little food — perhaps fewer households gave, or what was given did not last, or the monk underestimated what his fellows would need. In that case, having eaten what he has, hunger is not pacified and bodily strength is not restored.

The verse then permits a second alms-tour, but only on condition: a *legitimate cause* must have arisen. This is not a loophole for greed; it is a concession for genuine need. The commentary lists possible reasons — illness in the community, an unexpected guest, severe hunger, weather that interrupted the first tour, fasting practitioners who need a small amount of food to break their austerity, travel ahead that requires energy, and so on. In all such cases the monk does not simply walk out again; he applies the same discipline taught in the first Uddesha — the forty-two faults to be avoided, the proper time, the careful inspection of food and place — and additionally observes the further rules that this second Uddesha is about to teach.

The structure of these two verses is therefore a hinge between the two Uddeshas: the first taught the basic procedure, the second teaches the extension. The monk's freedom is real but disciplined — he may go again, but only under cause, and only by procedure.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If after one alms-round and eating the food the monk still does not have enough to sustain him, only then — and following all the same careful rules — may he go out for a second round.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you allow yourself an exception to a rule you live by, can you tell honestly whether the cause is real need or quiet desire?

## SUTRA 5.2.4 — The Right Time for Alms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कालेण णिक्खमे भिक्खू, कालेण य पडिक्कमे ।
अकालं च विवज्जित्ता, काले कालं समायरे ॥५.२.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.4 ||
kāleṇa ṇikkhame bhikkhū, kāleṇa ya paḍikkame |
akālaṃ ca vivajjittā, kāle kālaṃ samāyare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should go out (for alms) at the right time and return at the right time; avoiding the wrong time, he should perform each task at its appropriate time.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **कालेण** (kāleṇa) = at the (proper) time
- **णिक्खमे** (ṇikkhame) = should go out (for alms)
- **य** (ya) = and
- **कालेण** (kāleṇa) = at the (proper) time
- **पडिक्कमे** (paḍikkame) = should return (to the lodging)
- **अकालं** (akālaṃ) = the wrong time / unfit time
- **विवज्जित्ता** (vivajjittā) = having abandoned / avoided
- **काले** (kāle) = at the (right) time
- **कालं समायरे** (kālaṃ samāyare) = he should perform the (corresponding) timely activity

### 5. COMMENTARY

Time-consciousness is a deep theme in monastic life. The monk's day is divided into segments — study, meditation, alms-tour, eating, rest — and each has its appointed slot. To collect alms, the monk should go when householders are normally cooking and eating, because food is then naturally being prepared and the gift causes no extra trouble. Going too early disturbs sleeping families; going too late catches them with leftovers or with no food at all, putting the householder in awkwardness.

The verse also has a second, broader meaning. "Right time" includes not only the clock-time of the village but also the right circumstance: weather, the monk's own physical condition, the rhythm of the lodging's other duties. A monk who tries to do everything regardless of timing — alms in monsoon-rain, study at dinner-hour, rest when others are wakeful — burdens both himself and the community. The verse's repeated use of the word "kāla" (time) is deliberate; the monk is being asked to live in harmony with the unfolding of the day rather than impose his will upon it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should go out for food at the proper time, come back at the proper time, and avoid wrong times — doing each thing when it is naturally meant to be done.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much friction in your day comes from doing the right thing at the wrong time?

## SUTRA 5.2.5 — The Cost of Wrong Timing

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अकाले चरसि भिक्खू, कालं ण पडिलेहसि ।
अप्पाणं च किलामेसि, सण्णिवेसं च गरिहसि ॥५.२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.5 ||
akāle carasi bhikkhū, kālaṃ ṇa paḍilehasi |
appāṇaṃ ca kilāmesi, saṇṇivesaṃ ca garihasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O monk! If you wander (for alms) at the wrong time, you do not consider the proper time; you exhaust your own self, and you (end up) blaming the very village (that gave nothing).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = O monk!
- **अकाले** (akāle) = at the wrong time
- **चरसि** (carasi) = (if) you go (for alms)
- **कालं** (kālaṃ) = the proper time
- **ण पडिलेहसि** (ṇa paḍilehasi) = you do not look out for / inspect
- **अप्पाणं** (appāṇaṃ) = your own self
- **किलामेसि** (kilāmesi) = you make weary, you cause distress to
- **सण्णिवेसं** (saṇṇivesaṃ) = the village / settlement
- **गरिहसि** (garihasi) = you blame, you slander

### 5. COMMENTARY

Where the previous verse stated the rule positively, this verse states it as a warning, addressed almost in the second-person voice of a teacher correcting a young disciple. Three consequences follow from going at the wrong time. First, **bodily exhaustion** — the monk walks the village and returns with little or nothing, having spent his strength for no result. Second, **failure of mindfulness** — by ignoring the time, he has already broken the fundamental discipline of being present in the right activity at the right moment. Third, and subtlest, **slander of the village** — finding no alms, the monk's mind drifts into "this place gave nothing, these people are without faith," when in fact the fault was his own timing.

This is a profound psychological observation. When we fail because of our own miscalculation, we are deeply tempted to blame the circumstance. The verse holds the monk accountable: if you went at the wrong time, the empty bowl is not the village's fault, it is yours; do not slander the very people whose hospitality keeps the order alive. To preserve right speech and right view, one must first take responsibility for one's own timing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If a monk goes out for food at the wrong time, he tires himself for nothing and then unfairly blames the town that had nothing to give him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something you tried did not work, how often is it tempting to blame the world rather than examine your own timing?

## SUTRA 5.2.6 — Equanimity When Alms Are Not Received

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सइ काले चरे भिक्खू, कुज्जा पुरिसकारियं ।
अलाभो त्ति ण सोएज्जा, तवो त्ति अहियासए ॥५.२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.6 ||
sai kāle care bhikkhū, kujjā purisakāriyaṃ |
alābho tti ṇa soejjā, tavo tti ahiyāsae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should go out at the proper time and make full effort; (yet) if there is no gain, he should not grieve over it — bearing it (instead) as an austerity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **सइ** (sai) = at the proper time / when the time is right
- **काले** (kāle) = at the (alms-)time
- **चरे** (care) = should go (for alms)
- **पुरिसकारियं** (purisakāriyaṃ) = manly effort / personal endeavour
- **कुज्जा** (kujjā) = should make
- **अलाभो त्ति** (alābho tti) = (with the thought) "non-gain" / "I got nothing"
- **ण सोएज्जा** (ṇa soejjā) = should not grieve, should not be saddened
- **तवो त्ति** (tavo tti) = (with the thought) "this is austerity"
- **अहियासए** (ahiyāsae) = should bear / endure (the hunger and the thirst)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is among the most quoted in Jain monastic teaching, because it captures the heart of the monk's relationship to food. The procedure is two-step: do everything in your power, then accept whatever comes. Step one is *purisakāra* — go at the right time, walk through the village, knock at doors, make the full effort. Step two is *anavishada* — if despite all effort the bowl remains empty, do not be downcast.

The reframing is the masterstroke. What looks like a failure (no alms received) is recast as a success of a different kind: an unplanned austerity (tapas) has spontaneously arrived. The monk who in another context might have undertaken a one-day fast as a chosen discipline now receives that very fast as a gift of circumstance. Seen this way, there is nothing to grieve — the body's hunger today is the soul's purification.

This is not denial of pain. The verse explicitly uses the word **ahiyāsae** — "endure," "bear with steadiness." Hunger is real, the body does feel it. But the monk's attitude transforms the experience: the same hunger that breeds frustration in one mind breeds equanimity in another. The Jain insistence on *bhavana* (mental disposition) as the deciding factor in the karmic effect of any event finds clear expression here.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should go out at the right time and try his best, but if he gets no food he should not be sad — he should accept the hunger as a kind of fast quietly given to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When your best effort produces nothing, can you receive the empty result as a gift instead of a grievance?

## SUTRA 5.2.7 — Avoiding Animals Gathered for Their Own Food

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेवुच्चावया पाणा, भत्तट्ठाए समागया ।
तं उज्जुयं ण गच्छेज्जा, जयमेव परक्कमे ॥५.२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.7 ||
tahevuccāvayā pāṇā, bhattaṭṭhāe samāgayā |
taṃ ujjuyaṃ ṇa gacchejjā, jayameva parakkame ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, when small and large creatures have gathered (in some place) for the sake of food, the monk should not go straight (through that place); only with mindful care should he proceed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise / similarly
- **उच्चावया पाणा** (uccāvayā pāṇā) = small and large living beings (animals, birds, insects)
- **भत्तट्ठाए** (bhattaṭṭhāe) = for the sake of food (and drink)
- **समागया** (samāgayā) = gathered, assembled
- **तं** (taṃ) = (toward) them / (toward) that place
- **उज्जुयं** (ujjuyaṃ) = straight / directly
- **ण गच्छेज्जा** (ṇa gacchejjā) = should not go
- **जयमेव** (jayameva) = with mindful care alone
- **परक्कमे** (parakkame) = should proceed / move

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk on his alms-tour walks through a living world. The verse imagines a scene that would be ordinary in any village: birds and small animals have gathered at a spot to eat their own food — perhaps grain scattered for them, perhaps water in a trough, perhaps fruit fallen from a tree. The monk's path crosses this gathering. What should he do?

The instruction is precise. He should not walk *straight through* the gathering, because his approach would frighten the birds into flight, the animals into scattering, and would interrupt their meal. To break their meal is itself a form of *antaraya* — placing an obstacle between a being and its food, which Jain ethics regards as a serious harm. Either some other path should be chosen, or, if none exists, the monk should walk with extreme mindfulness — slowly, quietly, taking a route around them, so that the creatures' meal is not disturbed.

This rule is not about kindness in the sentimental sense. It is the ahimsa of detail: a great vow of non-harm sustained by the smallest acts of attention. The monk who would never strike an animal must also never frighten one; the monk who would never withhold his own food must also never deny another creature theirs.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk sees small or large animals gathered somewhere to eat, he should not walk straight through them — he should take another path or move around them very carefully so as not to disturb their meal.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In your daily walking and moving, how often do you notice the smaller lives whose moment you are about to cross?

## SUTRA 5.2.8 — Not Sitting and Not Preaching at the Householder's Door

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य, ण णिसीएज्ज कत्थई ।
कहं च ण पबंधेज्जा, चिट्ठित्ताण व संजए ॥५.२.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.8 ||
goyaraggapaviṭṭho ya, ṇa ṇisīejja katthaī |
kahaṃ ca ṇa pabaṃdhejjā, ciṭṭhittāṇa va saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having entered (the house) for alms, the restrained monk should not sit down anywhere there; nor, while standing, should he begin a long discourse.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य** (goyaraggapaviṭṭho ya) = and having entered (a house) on the alms-tour
- **कत्थई** (katthaī) = anywhere (in the house)
- **ण णिसीएज्ज** (ṇa ṇisīejja) = should not sit down
- **चिट्ठित्ताण व** (ciṭṭhittāṇa va) = or (even) while standing
- **कहं** (kahaṃ) = a discourse / sermon / extended talk
- **ण पबंधेज्जा** (ṇa pabaṃdhejjā) = should not weave / extend / undertake at length

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk's purpose at the householder's door is narrow and clear: receive the alms, then leave. The verse forbids two natural temptations. The first is **sitting down** — settling into a chair or a corner of the home invites familiarity, prolongs the visit, increases the householder's burden, and may expose the monk to sights, sounds, and conversations that erode the discipline of detachment. The second is **giving a long sermon** — even while standing in the doorway, the monk should not turn the alms-encounter into an extended teaching session. Religious discourse has its proper place (the upashraya, the gathering of disciples) and proper time; the doorstep during the householder's mealtime is neither.

This is a delicate teaching. The monk *is* a teacher, and the householder may even invite a discourse out of devotion. But the verse reminds the monk that the alms-tour is a discipline of austerity, not an opportunity for influence. To linger and teach at every door would convert the begging-bowl into a stage and the discipline into a performance. The monk takes only food; he does not take attention.

The verse also implicitly protects the householder. A long visit interrupts cooking, eating, and family life. The monk's restraint — receiving the alms, blessing the household, and moving on — is itself a form of respect.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the monk has entered a house for alms, he should not sit down anywhere or, even while standing, give a long sermon — he is there only for food.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you visit someone for one specific purpose, can you keep the visit to that purpose without overstaying or overspeaking?

## SUTRA 5.2.9 — Not Leaning on Anything in the Householder's Home

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अग्गलं फलिहं दारं, कवाडं वावि संजए ।
अवलंबिया ण चिट्ठिज्जा, गोयरग्गगओ मुणी ॥५.२.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.9 ||
aggalaṃ phalihaṃ dāraṃ, kavāḍaṃ vāvi saṃjae |
avalaṃbiyā ṇa ciṭṭhijjā, goyaraggagao muṇī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk who has gone for alms should not stand leaning on a door-bolt, a cross-bar, a doorway, or even a door-panel.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गोयरग्गगओ** (goyaraggagao) = (the monk) who has gone for alms
- **संजए मुणी** (saṃjae muṇī) = the restrained monk
- **अग्गलं** (aggalaṃ) = a small bolt (used to bolt a door)
- **फलिहं** (phalihaṃ) = a cross-bar / large bolt-stick (used to brace a door)
- **दारं** (dāraṃ) = a doorway / door-frame
- **कवाडं** (kavāḍaṃ) = a door-panel
- **वावि** (vāvi) = or
- **अवलंबिया** (avalaṃbiyā) = leaning upon, taking support of
- **ण चिट्ठिज्जा** (ṇa ciṭṭhijjā) = should not stand

### 5. COMMENTARY

The detail of this verse is striking. Four kinds of household fixtures are named — the small bolt, the cross-bar, the door-frame itself, the door-panel — and the monk is told to lean on none of them. The point is universal: while waiting at a householder's home, the monk should stand free, supported only by his own legs.

Why such precision? Three reasons emerge. First, **modesty of bearing**: a monk leaning casually against a doorway looks restless, distracted, even attached to comfort. The household will form an impression that does not befit the order. Second, **possible damage**: the bolt or door-panel may be loose, and if the monk's weight breaks it, the householder suffers a loss for which the monk is responsible — and if the broken piece falls and injures a small creature, there is *jiva-virodhana* as well. Third, **inner attitude**: leaning on something is a small sign of having relaxed one's vigilance; the monk's body should remain alert because the discipline is alert. Even a few minutes at a door is part of the practice.

This is a verse that demonstrates how thoroughly the Jain code of conduct extends into the smallest gestures. Standing without support is not arbitrary fastidiousness; it is the bodily expression of an inner stance — self-supported, attentive, present.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

While waiting for alms at someone's house, the monk should not lean against the door, the doorframe, the bolt, or any part of the entrance — he should stand straight on his own.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you are waiting somewhere briefly, what do you reach for to lean against — and what is it about you that wants the support?

## SUTRA 5.2.10-11 — Yielding to Other Mendicants at the Door

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समणं माहणं वावि, किविणं वा वणीमगं ।
उवसंकमंतं भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१०॥

तमइक्कमित्तु ण पविसे, ण चिट्ठे चक्खुगोयरे ।
एगंतमवक्कमित्ता, तत्थ चिट्ठिज्ज संजए ॥५.२.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.10 ||
samaṇaṃ māhaṇaṃ vāvi, kiviṇaṃ vā vaṇīmagaṃ |
uvasaṃkamaṃtaṃ bhattaṭṭhā, pāṇaṭṭhāe va saṃjae ||

|| 5.2.11 ||
tamaikkamittu ṇa pavise, ṇa ciṭṭhe cakkhugoyare |
egaṃtamavakkamittā, tattha ciṭṭhijja saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(10) (When) a (Buddhist) monk, a brahmin, a destitute person, or a beggar is approaching (a house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water, the restrained monk —
(11) — should not bypass that one and enter (the house first); nor should he stand within their line of sight; he should withdraw to a place apart and stand there.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **समणं** (samaṇaṃ) = a (non-Jain) monk / wandering ascetic
- **माहणं** (māhaṇaṃ) = a brahmin
- **वावि** (vāvi) = or
- **किविणं** (kiviṇaṃ) = a destitute / poor person
- **वा वणीमगं** (vā vaṇīmagaṃ) = or a beggar (one who lives by begging)
- **भत्तट्ठा** (bhattaṭṭhā) = for food
- **पाणट्ठाए व** (pāṇaṭṭhāe va) = or for water
- **उवसंकमंतं** (uvasaṃkamaṃtaṃ) = approaching / coming toward (the house)
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **तं** (taṃ) = that one
- **अइक्कमित्तु** (aikkamittu) = bypassing / overtaking
- **ण पविसे** (ṇa pavise) = should not enter (the house)
- **चक्खुगोयरे** (cakkhugoyare) = within (their) line of sight
- **ण चिट्ठे** (ṇa ciṭṭhe) = should not stand
- **एगंतं** (egaṃtaṃ) = a place apart / secluded spot
- **अवक्कमित्ता** (avakkamittā) = having withdrawn
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there
- **चिट्ठिज्जा** (ciṭṭhijjā) = should stand

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two verses paint a scene that must have been common in ancient India. A householder's door, and at the same moment several seekers of food converging on it — a wandering Buddhist or other ascetic, a brahmin priest expecting his daily portion, a poor man, a beggar, and the Jain monk himself. What is the right thing to do?

The instruction is to yield. The Jain monk should not push ahead of the others, should not overtake them, should not even stand where they can see him competing for the same food. He should withdraw to a place apart and stand there, waiting his turn until the other supplicants have left.

The reasons reach into both ethics and prudence. From the side of ethics, every other being also has a need for food, and the monk who races them to the door violates the spirit of *aparigraha* (non-grasping). From the side of prudence, if a Jain monk is seen competing aggressively for food, the laypeople will lose respect for the order and the householder may feel pressured to favour one over the others, breeding ill-will. From the side of compassion, those other seekers may need the food more than the monk — they may have nowhere else to turn, while the Jain monk has many other doors. To stand aside, out of sight, is to acknowledge their priority quietly, without asking for credit.

The phrase **na chiṭṭhe cakkhugoyare** ("should not stand within their line of sight") is especially fine. Even the *appearance* of competing must be avoided. The monk's discipline is so subtle that it watches not only what he does but what others see him doing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If another monk, brahmin, poor person, or beggar is approaching a house for food or water, the Jain monk should not race ahead of them or even stand where they can see him — he should quietly step aside and wait until they are finished.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When another person is reaching for the same thing you want, can you step aside without resentment and without making a show of stepping aside?

## SUTRA 5.2.12 — Why Yielding Matters

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वणीमगस्स वा तस्स, दायगस्सुभयस्स वा ।
अप्पत्तियं सिया हुज्जा, लहुत्तं पवयणस्स वा ॥५.२.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.12 ||
vaṇīmagassa vā tassa, dāyagassubhayassa vā |
appattiyaṃ siyā hujjā, lahuttaṃ pavayaṇassa vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Otherwise) of that beggar, or of the giver, or of both, displeasure may arise; or there may be a lessening (of esteem) of the (Jain) teaching itself.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया** (siyā) = it may be that (perhaps) — (if the monk does not step aside)
- **तस्स वणीमगस्स** (tassa vaṇīmagassa) = of that beggar (who was approaching)
- **वा दायगस्स** (vā dāyagassa) = or of the giver (the householder)
- **उभयस्स वा** (ubhayassa vā) = or of both
- **अप्पत्तियं** (appattiyaṃ) = displeasure / ill-feeling
- **हुज्जा** (hujjā) = would arise
- **पवयणस्स वा** (pavayaṇassa vā) = or of the doctrine / of the Jain teaching
- **लहुत्तं** (lahuttaṃ) = lightness / lessening of esteem / disrepute

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse explains the reasoning behind the previous one. Three possible harms follow if the Jain monk pushes ahead of the other beggar. First, **the beggar feels displeasure** — humiliated at being overtaken, perhaps angry at losing food he was about to receive. Second, **the householder feels displeasure** — caught in an uncomfortable competition between two needy seekers, and forced to choose. Third, **the order itself is brought low** — observers will say "look how these Jain monks behave, no different from any common scrambler for food," and the *pravachana* (the teaching, the order, the public face of Jain dharma) suffers a loss of dignity.

The verse displays a particularly mature ethical sensibility: an action is wrong not only when it directly harms someone, but also when it generates displeasure, breaks social trust, or diminishes the credibility of the spiritual community. The monk does not live in isolation; everything he does either polishes or tarnishes the reputation of the entire order. That awareness of the wider impact — of one's tiny act on the *pavayana* — is the mark of a mature practitioner.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If the monk pushes ahead of another beggar, the beggar will feel hurt, the householder will feel awkward, and people will think badly of the entire Jain teaching — so the small act of yielding protects everyone.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do your smallest actions carry weight you never see — affecting people, relationships, or causes far beyond what you imagined?

## SUTRA 5.2.13 — When the Other Beggar Has Left

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिसेहिए व दिण्णे वा, तओ तम्मि णियत्तिए ।
उवसंकमेज्ज भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.13 ||
paḍisehie va diṇṇe vā, tao tammi ṇiyattie |
uvasaṃkamejja bhattaṭṭhā, pāṇaṭṭhāe va saṃjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When that (other) beggar has either been refused or has been given (alms), and has thereupon turned away from that place, then the restrained monk may approach (the house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पडिसेहिए** (paḍisehie) = (when the beggar has been) refused
- **व दिण्णे** (va diṇṇe) = or (has been) given (alms)
- **तओ** (tao) = then
- **तम्मि** (tammi) = from that (door / place)
- **णियत्तिए** (ṇiyattie) = (when) he has turned away / withdrawn
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **भत्तट्ठा** (bhattaṭṭhā) = for food
- **पाणट्ठाए व** (pāṇaṭṭhāe va) = or for water
- **उवसंकमेज्ज** (uvasaṃkamejja) = may approach (the house)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the etiquette begun two verses earlier. The Jain monk has stepped aside out of sight; the other beggar has now finished his interaction with the householder — either he was refused (perhaps the household had nothing to give) or he received something. In either case, he has left the doorway. Only now does the Jain monk approach.

The instruction is so simple that it might seem almost over-cautious, but it carries real wisdom. By waiting until the previous transaction is complete and the other person has departed, the monk avoids any appearance of having capitalized on the rejection or competed for the gift. The two visits remain entirely separate. The householder is not pressured by the simultaneous presence of two requests; the previous beggar's experience is not coloured by feeling watched; the monk's request stands on its own.

This three-verse sequence (10-11-12-13) is a small masterpiece of monastic etiquette: the situation is observed, the right action is named, the reasons are given, and the timing of resumption is specified. Few ethical codes combine such practical precision with such delicate sensitivity to the feelings of others.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Once the other beggar has left the door — whether they were given food or refused — only then may the Jain monk go up to the house for his own alms.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something has just gone wrong for someone else, can you wait long enough that your own arrival is not entangled with their disappointment?

## SUTRA 5.2.14-15 — Food Offered with Sentient Flowers (Plucked)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उप्पलं पउमं वावि, कुमुयं वा मगदंतियं ।
अण्णं वा पुप्फसच्चित्तं, तं च संलुंचिया दए ॥५.२.१४॥

तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.14 ||
uppalaṃ paumaṃ vāvi, kumuyaṃ vā magadaṃtiyaṃ |
aṇṇaṃ vā pupphasaccittaṃ, taṃ ca saluṃciyā dae ||

|| 5.2.15 ||
taṃ bhave bhattapāṇaṃ tu, saṃjayāṇa akappiyaṃ |
diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe, ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If a (giver) plucks a blue lotus, a (red) lotus, or a white moon-lotus, or a magadanti (jasmine-like) flower, or any other sentient flower, and (with hands so soiled by the plucking) offers food —

that food and drink would be unfit (akalpya) for restrained monks. (The monk) should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उप्पलं** (uppalaṃ) = blue lotus
- **पउमं** (paumaṃ) = red/day lotus
- **कुमुयं** (kumuyaṃ) = white night-lotus
- **मगदंतियं** (magadaṃtiyaṃ) = magadanti (jasmine type)
- **पुप्फसच्चित्तं** (pupphasaccittaṃ) = sentient flower
- **संलुंचिया** (saluṃciyā) = having plucked / having torn off
- **दए** (dae) = (would) give
- **अकप्पियं** (akappiyaṃ) = unallowable / unfit
- **पडियाइक्खे** (paḍiyāikkhe) = (the monk) should refuse / decline
- **ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं** (ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ) = "such (food) does not befit me"

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two verses form a single instruction. The image is precise: a householder, wishing to offer food to the visiting monk, plucks a fresh lotus or flower — perhaps to garnish the offering, perhaps as a gesture of welcome — and with that same act, sentient life has been destroyed. The food itself may be perfectly cooked and otherwise pure; but the giver's hands, having just torn living petals, now carry the trace of that violence into the dish. For a Jain monk, the food becomes inadmissible.

The monk's response is gentle but firm. He is not silent (which would leave the giver puzzled) and not accusatory (which would shame them). He simply explains: "such (food) does not befit me." The phrasing places the limit on himself, not on the giver — a small linguistic kindness that preserves the householder's dignity while protecting the rule.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If someone plucks a fresh flower and then offers you food with the same hands, the food is no longer pure for a Jain monk. He politely declines: "this isn't allowable for me."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do small acts of harm cling unnoticed to the gifts we give? And can a refusal be both clear and kind at the same time?

## SUTRA 5.2.16-17 — Food Offered After Crushing Sentient Flowers

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उप्पलं पउमं वावि, कुमुयं वा मगदंतियं ।
अण्णं वा पुप्फसच्चित्तं, तं च सम्मद्दिया दए ॥५.२.१६॥

तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.16 ||
uppalaṃ paumaṃ vāvi, kumuyaṃ vā magadaṃtiyaṃ |
aṇṇaṃ vā pupphasaccittaṃ, taṃ ca sammaddiyā dae ||

|| 5.2.17 ||
taṃ bhave bhattapāṇaṃ tu, saṃjayāṇa akappiyaṃ |
diṃtiyaṃ paḍiyāikkhe, ṇa me kappai tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If a (giver) crushes / treads upon a blue lotus, a red lotus, a white moon-lotus, a magadanti, or any other sentient flower, and (so) offers food —

that food and drink would be unfit for restrained monks. He should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सम्मद्दिया** (sammaddiyā) = having crushed / mashed / trodden
- (rest as in 5.2.14-15)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This pair extends the previous rule with a different mode of harm. There the violence was *plucking* (saṃluñcana); here it is *crushing* (saṃmardana). The flower may have already fallen, or be lying scattered on the floor where the householder walks; the giver, hurrying to serve, treads on it or wipes it aside with a foot or hand. Even this passive, almost incidental harm is enough to render the food unfit.

The repetition is deliberate. The text could have said "plucked or crushed" in a single line, but instead the two cases are given full and parallel verses. This is the sutra's way of saying: every distinct mode of injury matters; do not assume that what is forbidden by one verb is allowed by another. Awareness must be specific.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

If someone crushes or tramples a sentient flower while serving you, the food cannot be accepted either. The monk again declines politely.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What harm do my feet do that my hands would never permit? Does carelessness deserve a different name simply because it was unintended?

## SUTRA 5.2.18-19 — Sachitta Roots, Stems, Shoots and Greens

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सालुयं वा विरालियं, कुमुयं उप्पलणालियं ।
मुणालियं सासवणालियं, उच्छुखंडं अणिव्वुडं ॥५.२.१८॥

तरुणगं वा पवालं, रुक्खस्स तणगस्स वा ।
अण्णस्स वावि हरियस्स, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.18 ||
sāluyaṃ vā virāliyaṃ, kumuyaṃ uppalaṇāliyaṃ |
muṇāliyaṃ sāsavaṇāliyaṃ, ucchukhaṃḍaṃ aṇivvuḍaṃ ||

|| 5.2.19 ||
taruṇagaṃ vā pavālaṃ, rukkhassa taṇagassa vā |
aṇṇassa vāvi hariyassa, āmagaṃ parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A lotus-bulb (sāluya), a palāsha-root (virāliya), the stalk of a white night-lotus or of a blue lotus, a lotus-fibre (muṇāliya), a stalk of mustard greens, a sugarcane segment — if not yet rendered acittic (aṇivvuḍa, not yet "cooled" / processed by fire or other means) —

likewise tender shoots or sprouts of a tree or of grass, or of any other green vegetation — being raw / sentient (āmaga) — (the monk) should completely avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सालुयं** (sāluyaṃ) = lotus-bulb / kamal-kanda
- **विरालियं** (virāliyaṃ) = palāsha-root (or velā-parva, joints of certain creepers)
- **उप्पलणालियं** (uppalaṇāliyaṃ) = stalk of blue lotus
- **मुणालियं** (muṇāliyaṃ) = lotus-fibre / lotus-tendril
- **सासवणालियं** (sāsavaṇāliyaṃ) = stalk of mustard plant (sarsav)
- **उच्छुखंडं** (ucchukhaṃḍaṃ) = piece of sugarcane
- **अणिव्वुडं** (aṇivvuḍaṃ) = not yet rendered acittic / not "cooled" by processing
- **तरुणगं पवालं** (taruṇagaṃ pavālaṃ) = tender shoot / new sprout
- **रुक्खस्स तणगस्स** (rukkhassa taṇagassa) = of tree, of grass
- **हरियस्स** (hariyassa) = of green / fresh vegetation
- **आमगं** (āmagaṃ) = raw / unripe / still sentient
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should completely give up / avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

The teaching now lists, with a botanist's care, the kinds of plant matter that remain sentient until processed. Roots, bulbs, stalks, fibres, fresh segments of cane, tender shoots — anything green and uncooked is still living. The Jain category here is *aṇivvuḍa / āmaga*: vegetation that has not yet been rendered acittic by fire, drying, salt, prolonged time, or some other transformation. As long as the life-force is still present, the matter cannot enter the monk's bowl.

The verses do not moralize; they enumerate. This is characteristic of the alms-discipline literature: precise lists protect against the rationalizations that vague principles invite. A monk who hears "avoid sentient food" might reason his way around an exception; a monk who has memorized "lotus-bulb, palāsha-root, mustard-stalk, sugarcane piece" simply recognizes them and steps back.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lotus bulbs, fresh stalks, lotus fibres, raw sugarcane segments, tender shoots and any uncooked green vegetation are still alive. The monk avoids all of these completely.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When does life leave a plant? Not when it is plucked, but only when it has been thoroughly transformed. How many things we treat as "ingredients" are still, in some sense, alive?

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तरुणियं वा छिवाडिं, आमियं भज्जियं सइं ।
दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.२०॥

|| 5.2.20 ||
taruṇiyaṃ vā chivāḍiṃ, āmiyaṃ bhajjiyaṃ saiṃ |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(If the giver offers) tender pods (chivāḍi — pods of mung, gram, peanut, etc.) which are still raw, or which have been roasted only once — (the monk) should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

- **तरुणियं** (taruṇiyaṃ) = tender (with seed not yet hardened)
- **छिवाडिं** (chivāḍiṃ) = pod (of mung, gram, peanut, etc.)
- **आमियं** (āmiyaṃ) = raw / unripe
- **भज्जियं सइं** (bhajjiyaṃ saiṃ) = roasted (only) once
- **पडियाइक्खे** (paḍiyāikkhe) = should refuse

The verse names a food that looks ready but is not yet acittic. The phrase "roasted only once" (bhajjiyaṃ saiṃ) is a small technical detail with a large implication: half-processed food is not the same as processed food. The monk does not approximate; he waits for the full transformation, or he declines.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Tender or only-once-roasted pods — whose seeds inside may still be alive — are not yet pure for the monk. He politely refuses such food.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a process is half-finished, do I treat it as if it were done? Where in life does "almost ready" become a dangerous shortcut?

## SUTRA 5.2.21 — Berries, Bamboo Shoots, and Wood-Apple Pulp

तहा कोलमणुस्सिण्णं, वेलुयं कासवणालियं ।
तिलपप्पडगं णीमं, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.21 ||
tahā kolamaṇussiṇṇaṃ, veluyaṃ kāsavaṇāliyaṃ |
tilapappaḍagaṃ ṇīmaṃ, āmagaṃ parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise berries (kola — jujube/ber) not (sufficiently) heated, bamboo shoots, kāsava-stalks (a water-grass kanda or shrīparṇī fruit), tilapapaḍa (sesame-flatbread / fresh peanut/coconut chunks), nīma (kadamba-fruit) — being raw — (the monk) should avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहा** (tahā) = likewise
- **कोलं** (kolaṃ) = ber / jujube fruit
- **अणुस्सिण्णं** (aṇussiṇṇaṃ) = not heated through (by fire, etc.)
- **वेलुयं** (veluyaṃ) = bamboo shoot (kareli)
- **कासवणालियं** (kāsavaṇāliyaṃ) = kāsava-stalks / shrīparṇī fruit
- **तिलपप्पडगं** (tilapappaḍagaṃ) = sesame-flatbread / fresh tilapapaḍī
- **णीमं** (ṇīmaṃ) = kadamba fruit (or nīpa)
- **आमगं** (āmagaṃ) = (being) raw
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should completely avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

The list continues with specific items the monk is likely to encounter on the alms route. Each is a foodstuff that the householder may genuinely think of as "prepared," but each retains a residual sentience until fully transformed. Jujube berries that have not been thoroughly heated; bamboo shoots; certain water-plant stalks; sesame patties made from raw paste; kadamba fruit — all are common, all are inviting, and all are off-limits while still raw.

Reading the list, one feels the monk's careful eye traveling over the dish before it enters the bowl. This is not paranoia; it is *upayoga* — applied awareness — turned upon the very surface of daily food.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Berries not thoroughly heated, bamboo shoots, raw tilapapaḍī, kadamba fruit, and similar partly-prepared foods are still sentient. The monk avoids them all.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How many "everyday" things would change if I looked at them with the same precision I bring to important decisions?

## SUTRA 5.2.22 — Raw Flours, Bran, and Lukewarm Water

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव चाउलं पिट्ठं, वियडं वा तत्ताणिव्वुडं ।
तिलपिट्ठं पूइपिण्णागं, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.22 ||
taheva cāulaṃ piṭṭhaṃ, viyaḍaṃ vā tattāṇivvuḍaṃ |
tilapiṭṭhaṃ pūipiṇṇāgaṃ, āmagaṃ parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise raw rice-flour (cāulaṃ piṭṭhaṃ); or hot/lukewarm water that has not been fully boiled (viyaḍaṃ tattāṇivvuḍaṃ); raw sesame-flour (tilapiṭṭhaṃ); rotten / fermented oil-cake bran (pūipiṇṇāgaṃ) — being raw / sentient — (the monk) should avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise
- **चाउलं पिट्ठं** (cāulaṃ piṭṭhaṃ) = rice-flour (raw / not properly cooked)
- **वियडं** (viyaḍaṃ) = water (esp. cleaned / drained)
- **तत्ताणिव्वुडं** (tattāṇivvuḍaṃ) = heated but not fully "cooled" / not boiled enough to become acittic
- **तिलपिट्ठं** (tilapiṭṭhaṃ) = sesame flour
- **पूइपिण्णागं** (pūipiṇṇāgaṃ) = rotten bran / spoiled oil-cake
- **आमगं** (āmagaṃ) = raw
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = (he) should avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

The technical term *tattāṇivvuḍa* deserves attention. Water that has been heated but not boiled long enough — or that has been boiled but has stood beyond its prescribed window of time — is, by Jain analysis, no longer fully acittic. It has either not yet died as water-life, or it has begun to come back to life. Either way, it is sentient again, and it must be avoided. This sensitivity to the *life-window* of processed substances is one of the most distinctive features of Jain monastic practice.

The same logic applies to flours and to spoiled bran: each is examined not for whether it is "edible" by ordinary standards, but for whether life still inhabits it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Raw rice flour, water that has been only partly boiled, raw sesame flour, and rotten oil-cake bran are all still sentient (or have become so again) and must be refused.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Could the same substance be alive at one moment, dead at another, and alive again later? What does that tell us about the world's quiet ceaseless motion?

## SUTRA 5.2.23 — Raw Wood-Apple, Citron, and Roots

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कविट्ठं माउलिंगं च, मूलगं मूलगत्तियं ।
आमं असत्थपरिणयं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ॥५.२.२३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.23 ||
kaviṭṭhaṃ māuliṃgaṃ ca, mūlagaṃ mūlagattiyaṃ |
āmaṃ asatthapariṇayaṃ, maṇasā vi ṇa patthae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Wood-apple (kapittha), citron (matuluṅga / bijoru), radish (mūlaka), or radish-segments — being raw and not (yet) transformed by any (sterilizing) instrument (asatthapariṇayaṃ) — he should not desire even mentally.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कविट्ठं** (kaviṭṭhaṃ) = wood-apple
- **माउलिंगं** (māuliṃgaṃ) = citron / bijoru
- **मूलगं** (mūlagaṃ) = radish
- **मूलगत्तियं** (mūlagattiyaṃ) = pieces / segments of radish
- **आमं** (āmaṃ) = raw
- **असत्थपरिणयं** (asatthapariṇayaṃ) = not transformed by an instrument (i.e., not made acittic by any process)
- **मणसा वि** (maṇasā vi) = even mentally / even in thought
- **ण पत्थए** (ṇa patthae) = should not desire / should not crave

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse goes one important step further than the previous ones. There the monk was told to avoid raw foods physically; here he is told not to desire them *even mentally*. The discipline reaches inward. It is not enough to keep the bowl clean if the mind keeps asking, "if only that wood-apple were dry, if only that radish were cooked." Such longing is itself a fault, because it conditions the monk to seek opportunities where none should exist.

This is a recurring move in Jain ethics: bodily restraint is the first wall, mental restraint is the second, and the second is harder. *Maṇasā vi ṇa patthae* — "do not desire even in mind" — is one of the discipline's most demanding instructions.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Raw wood-apple, citron, radish, and similar foods are not even to be wished for, let alone accepted. The discipline closes the door on the mind, not just on the bowl.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What do I refuse with my hands while still longing for it in my heart? Where does that quiet longing eventually lead?

## SUTRA 5.2.24 — Raw Fruit-Flours, Seed-Powders, and Wild Fruits

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव फलमंथूणि, बीयमंथूणि जाणिया ।
विहेलगं पियालं च, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.24 ||
taheva phalamaṃthūṇi, bīyamaṃthūṇi jāṇiyā |
vihelagaṃ piyālaṃ ca, āmagaṃ parivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise fruit-flours (powders of jujube etc.), seed-flours (powders of wheat, gram, barley etc.) — knowing (them to be raw); also vibhītaka (one of the trifala fruits) and piyāla (chironji-fruit / rāyaṇ-fruit / grape) — being raw — he should avoid.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव** (taheva) = likewise
- **फलमंथूणि** (phalamaṃthūṇi) = powders of fruits
- **बीयमंथूणि** (bīyamaṃthūṇi) = powders of seeds (wheat, gram, barley, etc.)
- **जाणिया** (jāṇiyā) = having known / recognized
- **विहेलगं** (vihelagaṃ) = vibhītaka — one of the three trifala fruits
- **पियालं** (piyālaṃ) = piyāla / chironji-fruit (or rāyaṇ-fruit, or grape)
- **आमगं** (āmagaṃ) = raw
- **परिवज्जए** (parivajjae) = should avoid

### 5. COMMENTARY

The list of "raw vegetal foods to refuse" closes here. The verses 18-24 together form a small reference catalogue covering bulbs, stalks, fibres, shoots, pods, berries, flours, brans, fruits, and roots — each named with enough specificity that no monk will mistake the rule for vagueness. The closing instruction *jāṇiyā* — "having known / having recognized" — places the burden on the monk's discrimination: the rule does not protect those who do not learn the categories.

The seven verses (18-24) together teach a single unspoken lesson: knowing what counts as alive is itself a spiritual skill, and a monk who shrinks from such knowledge will fail the discipline despite his good intentions.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Powders of raw fruits or seeds, vibhītaka fruit, chironji or grape — when raw — must be avoided. The monk learns to recognize each one by name.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of right action depends not on willpower but on the quiet labor of learning the categories properly?

## SUTRA 5.2.25 — Samudānika: Begging Equally from All Households

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समुयाणं चरे भिक्खू, कुलं उच्चावयं सया ।
णीयं कुलमइक्कम्म, ऊसढं णाभिधारए ॥५.२.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.25 ||
samuyāṇaṃ care bhikkhū, kulaṃ uccāvayaṃ sayā |
ṇīyaṃ kulamaikkamma, ūsaḍhaṃ ṇābhidhārae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A monk should always beg in the *samudānika* manner — going to households both high (rich) and low (poor) — without bypassing a poor house in order to head straight for a wealthy one.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **समुयाणं** (samuyāṇaṃ) = the samudānika manner (going to a sequence of houses without selection)
- **चरे भिक्खू** (care bhikkhū) = the monk should walk / go for alms
- **कुलं उच्चावयं** (kulaṃ uccāvayaṃ) = households high and low (rich and poor)
- **सया** (sayā) = always
- **णीयं कुलं** (ṇīyaṃ kulaṃ) = a poor / lowly house
- **अइक्कम्म** (aikkamma) = having bypassed / having stepped over
- **ऊसढं** (ūsaḍhaṃ) = a wealthy house
- **णाभिधारए** (ṇābhidhārae) = he should not head for / should not aim at

### 5. COMMENTARY

*Samudānika bhikṣā-vṛtti* — "begging from all alike" — is one of the most beautiful disciplines of the Jain monk. The rule is simple: walk a continuous route, take whatever each household freely offers, and never skip a poor house in order to reach a rich one. The discipline serves two purposes at once. It guards the monk's *anāsakti* (non-attachment), since the bowl ceases to be a tool of preference. And it protects the dignity of the poor, who are not made to feel that the holy man considered their offering beneath him.

Two temptations are being named here: the temptation of luxury (always heading to the rich) and the temptation of relationship (cultivating wealthy patrons whose support becomes habitual). Both, when followed, slowly hollow out the monkhood from within.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk should beg from rich and poor houses alike, in their natural order. He must never skip a humble home in order to reach a wealthy one.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If I had to take whatever was offered, in whatever order it came, what stories about myself would I be unable to keep telling?

## SUTRA 5.2.26 — Without Despondency, Without Attachment

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अदीणो वित्तिमेसिज्जा, ण विसीएज्ज पंडिए ।
अमुच्छिओ भोयणम्मि, मायण्णे एसणा रए ॥५.२.२६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.26 ||
adīṇo vittimesijjā, ṇa visīejja paṃḍie |
amucchio bhoyaṇammi, māyaṇṇe esaṇā rae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Without despondency (adīno), the wise (paṇḍita) monk should seek his livelihood (vṛtti / alms); he should not become depressed (when little is found). Unattached to food, knowing the right measure, he should be devoted to the rules of *eṣaṇā* (the discipline of seeking).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अदीणो** (adīṇo) = without dejection / not crestfallen
- **वित्तिमेसिज्जा** (vittimesijjā) = he should seek his livelihood (i.e., alms)
- **ण विसीएज्ज** (ṇa visīejja) = should not become despondent / sink
- **पंडिए** (paṃḍie) = the wise monk
- **अमुच्छिओ** (amucchio) = unattached / not infatuated
- **भोयणम्मि** (bhoyaṇammi) = with respect to food
- **मायण्णे** (māyaṇṇe) = knowing the right measure
- **एसणा रए** (esaṇā rae) = devoted to (the rules of) eṣaṇā

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse names the inner posture that the alms-discipline aspires to. Two extremes are forbidden. On one side, *dīnatā* — feeling diminished or pitiful when alms are scarce, or putting on a show of poverty before householders to extract sympathy. On the other side, *mūrcchā* — getting engrossed in food when it comes plentiful and tasty. Either extreme corrupts the alms-walk; the true posture is steady between them.

Two further qualities are added: *māyaṇṇa* (knowing the right measure — taking only as much as is appropriate, not more, not less) and *esaṇā rae* (loving the rules of seeking — not resenting the discipline as a burden but embracing it as the very form of one's freedom). Together these four — adīna, amūrcchita, māyaṇṇa, esaṇā-rae — describe the spiritual signature of the Jain alms-walker.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The wise monk seeks alms without self-pity when little comes and without greed when much comes. He takes the right measure, and he loves the very discipline of seeking.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When the world gives me little, do I shrink? When it gives me much, do I forget myself? What would it take to remain steady through both?

## SUTRA 5.2.27 — When the Householder Has Plenty But Does Not Give

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बहुं परघरे अत्थि, विविहं खाइमसाइमं ।
ण तत्थ पंडिओ कुप्पे, इच्छा दिज्ज परो ण वा ॥५.२.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.27 ||
bahuṃ paraghare atthi, vivihaṃ khāimasāimaṃ |
ṇa tattha paṃḍio kuppe, icchā dijja paro ṇa vā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There may be much in another's house — varied solid foods (khādya) and tasty foods (svādya). The wise (monk) should not become angry there; (let him reflect that) the other person, by his own wish, may give or may not give.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **बहुं** (bahuṃ) = much
- **परघरे** (paraghare) = in another's house (i.e., the householder's)
- **अत्थि** (atthi) = there is
- **विविहं** (vivihaṃ) = various
- **खाइमसाइमं** (khāimasāimaṃ) = solid food (fruit) and tasty food (relishes / sweets)
- **ण तत्थ कुप्पे** (ṇa tattha kuppe) = should not become angry there
- **पंडिओ** (paṃḍio) = the wise (monk)
- **इच्छा** (icchā) = (according to his) wish
- **दिज्ज परो ण वा** (dijja paro ṇa vā) = the other (giver) may give or not

### 5. COMMENTARY

The scene is exact and humanly familiar: the monk enters a wealthy home; he can see fruit, sweets, prepared dishes filling the kitchen; the householder offers him nothing, or only a fraction. A small flame of resentment is the natural reaction — "they have so much, and yet…" The verse shuts that flame down before it can spread.

The reasoning is short but complete: *icchā dijja paro ṇa vā* — "by their own wish, the other may give or may not." The food belongs to the householder. Their giving is a free act, not a debt owed. The monk who forgets this turns alms into a transaction, and the moment alms become a transaction, monkhood ends.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even if a household has plenty and gives little (or nothing), the monk should not become angry. The choice belongs to the giver, not to the asker.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone has much and shares little, where does my anger come from? Whose freedom am I quietly resenting?

## SUTRA 5.2.28 — No Anger When Bedding, Seat, Cloth, Food, or Water Is Refused

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सयणासणवत्थं वा, भत्तपाणं च संजए ।
अदिंतस्स ण कुपिज्जा, पच्चक्खे वि य दीसओ ॥५.२.२८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.28 ||
sayaṇāsaṇavatthaṃ vā, bhattapāṇaṃ ca saṃjae |
adiṃtassa ṇa kupijjā, paccakkhe vi ya dīsao ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The restrained monk should not become angry with one who does not give — bedding (śayana), seat (āsana), cloth (vastra), or food and drink (bhakta-pāna) — even when (the items) are visibly present right before his eyes.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सयण आसण वत्थं** (sayaṇa āsaṇa vatthaṃ) = bedding, seat, cloth
- **भत्तपाणं** (bhattapāṇaṃ) = food and water
- **संजए** (saṃjae) = the restrained monk
- **अदिंतस्स** (adiṃtassa) = with one not giving
- **ण कुपिज्जा** (ṇa kupijjā) = should not become angry
- **पच्चक्खे वि य दीसओ** (paccakkhe vi ya dīsao) = even when (items are) visible / right in front of him

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verse handled food alone; this one extends the principle to every category of monastic need. The list — bedding, seat, cloth, food, water — covers nearly all the items a monk depends on a householder to provide. The hardest part of the rule is the closing phrase: *paccakkhe vi ya dīsao* — "even when visible right before his eyes." It is one thing to remain composed when the household claims to have nothing; it is another to remain composed when the items are sitting on the next shelf and the householder still declines.

The verse gently reminds the monk that the householder's home is not a public storeroom. What is visible there is not therefore available. Ownership is real; refusal is a right; the monk's task is only to ask once, accept the answer, and walk on without stain.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even when a householder visibly has bedding, seats, cloth, food, or water and still refuses the monk — the monk must not become angry. The household's possessions are theirs to give or withhold.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What do I quietly believe is owed to me when I see it within reach? When does seeing turn into expecting, and expecting into resentment?

## SUTRA 5.2.29 — Do Not Beg from Those Who Salute, Do Not Speak Harshly to Those Who Refuse

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इत्थियं पुरिसं वावि, डहरं वा महल्लगं ।
वंदमाणं ण जाइज्जा, णो य णं फरुसं वए ॥५.२.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.29 ||
itthiyaṃ purisaṃ vāvi, ḍaharaṃ vā mahallagaṃ |
vaṃdamāṇaṃ ṇa jāijjā, ṇo ya ṇaṃ pharusaṃ vae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A woman, a man, a child, or an elder — when they are bowing in salutation (vandamāna) — (the monk) should not beg from them; nor should he speak any harsh word to one who does not give.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इत्थियं** (itthiyaṃ) = woman
- **पुरिसं** (purisaṃ) = man
- **डहरं** (ḍaharaṃ) = child / young
- **महल्लगं** (mahallagaṃ) = elder / aged / great
- **वंदमाणं** (vaṃdamāṇaṃ) = (one who is) bowing / saluting
- **ण जाइज्जा** (ṇa jāijjā) = should not beg / make a request
- **णो य णं फरुसं वए** (ṇo ya ṇaṃ pharusaṃ vae) = nor should he speak harshly to him (who does not give)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Two refinements appear in this single verse. First: when a layperson — of whatever age or sex — is in the act of bowing in respect, do not turn that moment of devotion into a moment of asking. The vandanā belongs to them; let it complete in its own dignity. To request food while someone is still mid-bow is to convert reverence into transaction; the *bhāvārtha* of the Gujarati commentary is precise on this — *yācanā na karavī* — no request is to be made just then.

Second: when someone declines to give, do not punish them with a sharp word. *Pharusaṃ vae* — "speaks harshly" — is forbidden categorically. A monk who curses or scolds a householder for not giving has, in that instant, ceased to be a monk in the relevant sense. The discipline of asking and the discipline of not-asking-back are two halves of one self-control.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't make requests from someone who is in the middle of saluting you. And if anyone — woman, man, child, or elder — declines to give, never speak harshly to them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone honors me, can I receive it cleanly without immediately asking for more? When someone declines me, can I let the "no" remain a free word in their mouth?

## SUTRA 5.2.30 — Equanimity Toward the Honoring and the Indifferent

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे ण वंदे ण से कुप्पे, वंदिओ ण समुक्कसे ।
एवमण्णेसमाणस्स, सामण्णमणुचिट्ठइ ॥५.२.३०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.30 ||
je ṇa vaṃde ṇa se kuppe, vaṃdio ṇa samukkase |
evamaṇṇesamāṇassa, sāmaṇṇamaṇuciṭṭhai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If someone does not bow (does not salute), the monk should not become angry with him; if he is bowed to, he should not swell up (with pride). For one who seeks (alms) thus equably, *śrāmaṇya* — monkhood — remains intact.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जे ण वंदे** (je ṇa vaṃde) = the (householder) who does not bow
- **ण से कुप्पे** (ṇa se kuppe) = (the monk) should not become angry with him
- **वंदिओ** (vaṃdio) = (when he is) bowed to
- **ण समुक्कसे** (ṇa samukkase) = should not exalt himself / not become proud
- **एवं** (evaṃ) = thus
- **अण्णेसमाणस्स** (aṇṇesamāṇassa) = of one seeking (alms)
- **सामण्णं** (sāmaṇṇaṃ) = monkhood / śramaṇa-hood
- **अणुचिट्ठइ** (aṇuciṭṭhai) = remains intact / is preserved

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the great single-verse summaries of the alms-discipline's inner work. Two reactions are forbidden, and they are the mirror images of one another: getting angry at the indifferent, and getting puffed up by the reverent. Both come from the same root — staking one's self-worth on the householder's response. The cure is also single: become indifferent to *both* responses, and the monkhood lives.

The Sanskrit commentary glosses *evam aṇṇesamāṇassa* with the technical phrase *evam anveṣamāṇasya* — "of one searching in this way." The wording matters: the monk's job is to *search* (anveṣaṇa), not to *secure*. When the search itself, conducted in equanimity, becomes the practice, then whether the search yields fruit or not, śrāmaṇya is preserved.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Don't get angry at householders who don't bow to you. Don't get proud when they do. Stay even-minded through both — and your monkhood stays whole.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What honors do I secretly count, and what slights do I secretly tally? If I stopped doing both, what would my self look like?

## SUTRA 5.2.31 — The Monk Who Hides a Tasty Find

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिया एगइओ लद्धुं, लोभेण विणिगूहइ ।
मामेयं दाइयं संतं, दट्ठूणं सयमायए ॥५.२.३१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.31 ||
siyā egaio laddhuṃ, lobheṇa viṇigūhai |
māmeyaṃ dāiyaṃ saṃtaṃ, daṭṭhūṇaṃ sayamāyae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It may happen that a certain (monk), having obtained (some tasty food), conceals it out of greed (thinking): "Lest someone, seeing this — given to me, present in my bowl — should take it for himself."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया** (siyā) = it may be / kadāchit
- **एगइओ** (egaio) = a certain (monk) — egāntar, of low intelligence
- **लद्धुं** (laddhuṃ) = having obtained (tasty alms)
- **लोभेण** (lobheṇa) = out of greed
- **विणिगूहइ** (viṇigūhai) = hides / covers up (under bland food)
- **मा मेयं** (mā meyaṃ) = "lest this (food) of mine"
- **दाइयं संतं** (dāiyaṃ saṃtaṃ) = (which has been) given, being present (in the bowl)
- **दट्ठूणं** (daṭṭhūṇaṃ) = on seeing it
- **सयमायए** (sayamāyae) = (someone) take it for himself / claim it

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse names a quiet monastic vice. A monk goes for alms; he is given some unusually delicious food; he begins to fear that if his guru, ācārya, or fellow monks see it, they will ask for a portion or simply take it. So he covers the tasty food at the bottom of the bowl and piles bland food on top. The deceit is small in scale, but it strikes at three things at once: anāsakti (he is now attached to the food), gurukula-vinaya (he is hiding from the very superiors who structure his life), and saṅghārpaṇa (he has refused the spirit of communal sharing). The Gujarati commentator names this clearly as the first of two forms of *māyācāra* (deceitfulness) practiced for the sake of food.

The sutra here begins a long block (31-41) about the *māyāvī sādhu* — the deceitful monk — and his slow ruin. Read this verse as the seed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A weak-willed monk gets some specially tasty food, fears that others will share it with him, and quietly hides it under blander food in his bowl. That hiding is the first step of a long fall.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What small thing have I "hidden in the bottom of my bowl" today, hoping no one would notice? Why was sharing it harder than concealing it?

## SUTRA 5.2.32 — The Selfish Monk Never Reaches Liberation

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अत्तट्ठा गुरुओ लुद्धो, बहुं पावं पकुव्वइ ।
दुत्तोसओ य से होइ, णिव्वाणं च ण गच्छइ ॥५.२.३२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.32 ||
attaṭṭhā guruo luddho, bahuṃ pāvaṃ pakuvvai |
duttosao ya se hoi, ṇivvāṇaṃ ca ṇa gacchai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Heavy-laden with selfishness (atta-aṭṭhā guruo) and greedy (luddha), he commits much sin (bahuṃ pāvaṃ). He becomes hard to satisfy (duttosao); and he does not reach liberation (nirvāṇa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अत्तट्ठा** (attaṭṭhā) = for one's own sake / out of self-interest
- **गुरुओ** (guruo) = heavy / weighed down
- **लुद्धो** (luddho) = greedy
- **बहुं पावं पकुव्वइ** (bahuṃ pāvaṃ pakuvvai) = commits much sin
- **दुत्तोसओ** (duttosao) = hard to satisfy / never content
- **से होइ** (se hoi) = he becomes
- **णिव्वाणं ण गच्छइ** (ṇivvāṇaṃ ṇa gacchai) = he does not reach liberation

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse is structured as a cascading consequence. Selfishness produces greed; greed produces accumulated sin; accumulated sin produces a temperament that no amount of food, honor, or comfort can ever satisfy; that temperament produces a self that cannot reach nirvāṇa. Each link is a small step, but the chain is fatal.

The word *duttosao* — "hard to satisfy" — is striking. The deceitful monk thinks he is securing his pleasure by hiding choice food, but in fact he is teaching his own mind to be permanently dissatisfied. Every concealment trains the appetite to expect more, to fear loss, to grasp harder next time. And a mind so trained simply cannot relax into the equanimity that liberation requires.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The selfish, greedy monk piles up sin, becomes impossible to satisfy, and never reaches liberation. The hidden food in the bowl is a small thing; the consequences are not small.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What part of me is hard to satisfy today? Did that quality come from somewhere? What has it cost me already?

## SUTRA 5.2.33 — Eating the Tasty on the Way, Bringing the Bland to the Lodging

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिया एगइओ लद्धुं, विविहं पाणभोयणं ।
भद्दगं भद्दगं भोच्चा, विवण्णं विरसमाहरे ॥५.२.३३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.33 ||
siyā egaio laddhuṃ, vivihaṃ pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ |
bhaddagaṃ bhaddagaṃ bhoccā, vivaṇṇaṃ virasamāhare ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

It may happen that a certain (monk), having obtained various foods and drinks, eats up the choice (tastiest) portions one by one (along the way), and brings to the lodging only the colourless, tasteless (bland) remainder.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिया एगइओ** (siyā egaio) = it may be that some (monk)
- **लद्धुं** (laddhuṃ) = having obtained
- **विविहं पाणभोयणं** (vivihaṃ pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ) = various food and drink
- **भद्दगं भद्दगं** (bhaddagaṃ bhaddagaṃ) = the good (tasty) parts, one by one
- **भोच्चा** (bhoccā) = having eaten
- **विवण्णं** (vivaṇṇaṃ) = colourless / unattractive
- **विरसं** (virasaṃ) = tasteless
- **आहरे** (āhare) = brings (to the upāśraya / lodging)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the second species of *māyācāra* the commentator promised. Where 5.2.31 hid the tasty food at the bottom of the bowl, 5.2.33 eats the tasty food on the road and brings only bland leftovers home. The strategy is opposite, but the motive is identical: secure the choice food for oneself, secure the appearance of austerity for the community.

What is monstrous about this is its cleverness. The deceitful monk has converted one of monkhood's central acts — sharing alms with the community — into a private feast. He has used the cover of monastic propriety to commit a kind of theft. Many later traditions of monastic literature (Jain and Buddhist alike) treat this as one of the gravest internal corruptions, precisely because it cannot be detected from outside.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Some monks eat all the tasty food along the way back from alms, and bring only the dry, bland items to the lodging. The community thinks they are austere; in truth, they have stolen the meal.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What pleasures do I secretly take "on the way" so that the community sees only my austerity? Whose trust am I quietly spending?

## SUTRA 5.2.34 — The Disguise: Other Monks Praise Him as Self-Disciplined

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जाणंतु ता इमे समणा, आययट्ठी अयं मुणी ।
संतुट्ठो सेवए पंतं, लूहवित्ती सुतोसओ ॥५.२.३४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.34 ||
jāṇaṃtu tā ime samaṇā, āyayaṭṭhī ayaṃ muṇī |
saṃtuṭṭho sevae paṃtaṃ, lūhavittī sutosao ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(He thinks): "Let these monks know that *this* monk is one bent on the highest goal (āyayaṭṭhī — āyatārthī, seeking liberation); content, he resorts to the leftover (paṃta — inferior food); his way of life is bland (lūha-vitti); he is easily satisfied (sutosao)."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जाणंतु ता** (jāṇaṃtu tā) = "let (them) know"
- **इमे समणा** (ime samaṇā) = these (fellow) monks
- **आययट्ठी** (āyayaṭṭhī) = āyatārthī — one whose aim is the liberation-goal (the long-term good)
- **अयं मुणी** (ayaṃ muṇī) = "this monk (i.e., I)"
- **संतुट्ठो** (saṃtuṭṭho) = content
- **सेवए पंतं** (sevae paṃtaṃ) = uses leftover / inferior food
- **लूहवित्ती** (lūhavittī) = whose way of life is rough / bland (lūkha)
- **सुतोसओ** (sutosao) = easily satisfied

### 5. COMMENTARY

Verse 34 lifts the curtain on the deceit's whole purpose. The monk in 5.2.33 — who ate the tasty food on the way and brought only the bland items home — was not just being secretly greedy. He was actively staging an image. He wants the other monks to see him eating poor food and admiring him for it. The four words he wants applied to himself — *āyatārthī, saṃtuṣṭa, lūkhavṛtti, sutoṣaka* — are precisely the most prestigious labels of monastic virtue. Liberation-bound, content, austere, easily satisfied. To wear these labels falsely is to commit *māyāśalya* — the dart of deceit lodged in the soul.

The verse is also psychologically acute. It shows that the worst hypocrisy is not the bare lie but the curated impression: each gesture, each refusal of good food in public, each humble word, all chosen to produce an effect. Spiritual life, the verse implies, dies fastest under exactly this kind of polished performance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deceitful monk wants his fellow monks to think: "Look how austere he is! He's content with leftovers, lives rough, never asks for more." Behind this praise is a private, well-fed lie.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What labels am I quietly trying to attract? Which of my refusals are real, and which are performances?

## SUTRA 5.2.35 — Hungry for Worship, Honor, and Reputation

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पूयणट्ठी जसोकामी, माणसम्माणकामए ।
बहुं पसवइ पावं, मायासल्लं च कुव्वइ ॥५.२.३५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.35 ||
pūyaṇaṭṭhī jasokāmī, māṇasammāṇakāmae |
bahuṃ pasavai pāvaṃ, māyāsallaṃ ca kuvvai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Desiring worship (pūjaṇaṭṭhī), craving fame (yaśas-kāmī), longing for honour and respect (māna-saṃmāna-kāmaka) — (such a monk) generates much sin and impales himself with the dart of deceit (māyā-śalya).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पूयणट्ठी** (pūyaṇaṭṭhī) = one desirous of worship / pūjā
- **जसोकामी** (jasokāmī) = one craving fame / yaśas
- **माणसम्माणकामए** (māṇasammāṇakāmae) = one longing for honour (māna — through bodily gestures) and respect (saṃmāna — through gifts of clothes, vessels, etc.)
- **बहुं पावं** (bahuṃ pāvaṃ) = much sin
- **पसवइ** (pasavai) = brings forth / accumulates
- **मायासल्लं** (māyāsallaṃ) = the dart / thorn of deceit
- **कुव्वइ** (kuvvai) = practices / commits

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the closing verse of the five-verse cluster on the deceitful monk. It diagnoses the source: not appetite, but vanity. The hidden food and the staged poverty all serve a single appetite — the appetite for being seen as holy. Three nouns name the prize: *pūjā* (formal worship), *yaśa* (popular fame), *māna-saṃmāna* (esteem expressed in honors and material gifts). Each of these is, in itself, a worldly value the layperson chases; the monk, by dressing his pursuit of these in spiritual clothes, multiplies the sin rather than diminishing it.

The image of *māyā-śalya* — "the dart of deceit" — is exact. A śalya is a foreign object embedded in the body that, if not removed, festers. Deceit lodged in the practitioner's mind cannot simply be ignored; it slowly poisons the spiritual organism from within. This is why the alms discipline takes seemingly small acts of concealment so seriously.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk who wants to be worshipped, famous, and honored will pile up sin and lodge in himself the deep splinter of deceit — even while looking, on the surface, like a saint.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What part of my hunger for respect, fame, or honor is so old that I no longer notice it? What dart have I been carrying without removing?

## SUTRA 5.2.36 — Total Prohibition of Intoxicants

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुरं वा मेरगं वावि, अण्णं वा मज्जगं रसं ।
ससक्खं ण पिवे भिक्खू, जसं सारक्खमप्पणो ॥५.२.३६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.36 ||
suraṃ vā meragaṃ vāvi, aṇṇaṃ vā majjagaṃ rasaṃ |
sasakkhaṃ ṇa pive bhikkhū, jasaṃ sārakkhamappaṇo ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A monk should not drink — with himself as witness (sasakṣaṃ — i.e., before his own conscience and that of the Kevalī) — surā (grain-based liquor), meraka (mahuā-flower liquor), or any other intoxicating juice (madya-rasa) — guarding his own reputation and self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुरं** (suraṃ) = surā (grain/flour-based liquor, e.g., from piṣṭa)
- **मेरगं** (meragaṃ) = meraka (mahuā-flower liquor)
- **अण्णं वा मज्जगं रसं** (aṇṇaṃ vā majjagaṃ rasaṃ) = or any other intoxicating juice (bhāṅg, cāras, etc.)
- **ससक्खं** (sasakkhaṃ) = with self-witness / with the Kevalī as witness
- **ण पिवे** (ṇa pive) = should not drink
- **भिक्खू** (bhikkhū) = the monk
- **जसं सारक्खमप्पणो** (jasaṃ sārakkhamappaṇo) = guarding his (own) reputation / fame / self-restraint

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Sthānāṅga Sūtra calls intoxication a cause of the hellish destinies, and the present sutra-author re-affirms the prohibition by listing two specific drinks (*surā*, made from grain or flour; *meraka*, made from the mahuā flower) and then closing the door on every other intoxicant by the catch-all *aṇṇaṃ vā majjagaṃ rasaṃ*. The monastic life requires a *sāttvika* diet; *tāmasic* and intoxicating substances inflame buried desires, fog discrimination, and open the door to a hundred other faults that would otherwise stay closed.

The phrase *sasakkhaṃ* (Skt. svasākṣyam / sasākṣyaṃ) is the verse's moral hinge. The commentator unfolds it two ways: with one's own self as witness (the conscience), and with the Kevalī (the omniscient Jina) as witness. The teaching is: any sin committed in secret is still committed before two unfailing witnesses, and a monk who remembers this cannot drink even alone.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk must never drink any intoxicant — surā, meraka, or anything else — even in private. Two witnesses are always present: his own conscience, and the all-seeing Jina.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What act of mine relies on the assumption that no one is watching? Who is always watching, even then?

## SUTRA 5.2.37 — The Fallen Monk Who Drinks in Secret

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पियए एगओ तेणो, ण मे कोइ वियाणइ ।
तस्स पस्सह दोसाइं, णियडिं च सुणेह मे ॥५.२.३७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.37 ||
piyae egao teṇo, ṇa me koi viyāṇai |
tassa passaha dosāiṃ, ṇiyaḍiṃ ca suṇeha me ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Some fallen monk thinks): "I am drinking alone, like a thief; no one knows of me." See his faults, and hear from me of his deceit.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पियए** (piyae) = drinks (alcohol)
- **एगओ** (egao) = alone, in solitude
- **तेणो** (teṇo) = like a thief (a violator of Bhagavān's command)
- **ण मे कोइ वियाणइ** (ṇa me koi viyāṇai) = "no one knows me / no one sees me"
- **तस्स पस्सह दोसाइं** (tassa passaha dosāiṃ) = see his faults
- **णियडिं च** (ṇiyaḍiṃ ca) = and (his) deceit / the second fold of deceit (the lie that hides the drinking)
- **सुणेह मे** (suṇeha me) = hear from me

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra adopts a striking voice. The teacher introduces the figure of the *teṇa* — the thief — and says, in effect: "Listen, I will name his hiding places one by one." A thief here is anyone who violates the command of the Jina; drinking alcohol in secret is precisely this kind of theft. The phrase *ṇa me koi viyāṇai* — "no one sees me" — is the thief's reassurance to himself, and the sutra exposes it as self-deception.

The word *ṇiyaḍi* (Skt. *niḥkṛti*) is the verse's punchline. It refers to a *second* layer of deceit on top of the first: the drinker drinks (deceit one), and then lies to cover the drinking (deceit two). One transgression always breeds another; concealment is always parasitic on a deeper concealment. Verses 38-41 will now trace what happens when this layered deceit is allowed to take root.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The secret drinker says, "no one knows." But he is doubly deceitful: once for drinking, once for hiding it. Now hear what becomes of him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What second lie do I tell to protect a first one? How quickly does concealment become its own appetite?

## SUTRA 5.2.38 — Attachment, Falsehood, Disrepute, and Endless Restlessness

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वड्ढइ सुंडिया तस्स, मायामोसं च भिक्खुणो ।
अयसो य अणिव्वाणं, सययं च असाहुया ॥५.२.३८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.38 ||
vaḍḍhai suṃḍiyā tassa, māyāmosaṃ ca bhikkhuṇo |
ayaso ya aṇivvāṇaṃ, sayayaṃ ca asāhuyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For that monk, addiction (śauṇḍikā / drinker's craving) grows; deceit-and-falsehood (māyā-mṛṣā) grows; disrepute (ayaśas) grows; non-peace (aniḥnirvāṇa — no liberation, no even worldly tranquillity); and continually, "non-monkhood" (asādhutā) grows.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तस्स भिक्खुणो** (tassa bhikkhuṇo) = of that drunken monk
- **सुंडिया** (suṃḍiyā) = the addict's craving / drunkenness
- **वड्ढइ** (vaḍḍhai) = increases
- **मायामोसं** (māyāmosaṃ) = deceit and lying combined (māyā-mṛṣā)
- **अयसो** (ayaso) = ill-fame / disrepute
- **अणिव्वाणं** (aṇivvāṇaṃ) = no peace, no liberation (in this world or the next)
- **सययं** (sayayaṃ) = continually / unbroken
- **असाहुया** (asāhuyā) = un-saintliness / loss of monkhood

### 5. COMMENTARY

Five negative growths are listed, each one feeding on the previous. The drinker's craving expands; the deceit needed to sustain the craving thickens; the inevitable leakage into the world produces disrepute; the inner state loses any chance of peace; and the very identity of monkhood drains away, day by day, until what remains is a shape without substance. Each item here is a direct consequence, not a moral threat — the verse describes a mechanism, not a punishment.

The compound *māyā-mṛṣā* — "deceit and lie together" — is itself one of Jainism's named *mahā-doṣas*, identified as a hybrid evil more dangerous than either deceit or lying alone. The drinker, by definition, lives inside this compound.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The secret-drinker's craving keeps growing; his deceits multiply; his reputation crumbles; his peace is lost; and his very monkhood quietly dies — every day a little more.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in my life is "growing in the dark" that I have not yet named?

## SUTRA 5.2.39 — Like a Worried Thief, Even at Death He Cannot Hold Restraint

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिच्चुव्विग्गो जहा तेणो, अत्तकम्मेहिं दुम्मइ ।
तारिसो मरणंते वि, ण आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.३९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.39 ||
ṇiccuvviggo jahā teṇo, attakammehiṃ dummai |
tāriso maraṇaṃte vi, ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as a thief is constantly anxious because of his own (criminal) acts (*atta-kammehiṃ dummai*), so too the foolish (drunken) monk: even at the very end of life, he cannot accomplish *saṃvara* — the dharma of self-restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णिच्चुव्विग्गो** (ṇiccuvviggo) = constantly agitated / unsettled
- **जहा तेणो** (jahā teṇo) = just as a thief
- **अत्तकम्मेहिं** (atta-kammehiṃ) = on account of his own (bad) actions
- **दुम्मइ** (dummai) = of corrupted mind / dejected
- **तारिसो** (tāriso) = such (a monk)
- **मरणंते वि** (maraṇaṃte vi) = even at the end of life / on death-bed
- **ण आराहेइ संवरं** (ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ) = cannot accomplish the saṃvara-dharma (the cessation of karmic inflow)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The image is sharp. A thief, having stolen, can never settle. Every shadow startles him; every glance feels like an accusation; he cannot enjoy what he has stolen. Just so, a monk who has sustained a hidden vice for years has lost the inner ground on which death-time *saṃvara* — the deliberate, peaceful cessation of karmic intake — would normally stand.

This matters spiritually because in Jain teaching the moments before death carry tremendous weight. A peaceful, *saṃvara*-filled death can dramatically lighten the soul's onward trajectory. But peaceful saṃvara at death requires a mind already trained in stillness; the lifelong drunken-deceiver's mind is the opposite of still. So even the great moment passes without yielding its great gift.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The drunken monk's mind is restless like a thief's. So restless that even on his deathbed, when restraint matters most, he cannot find it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If today were the last day of my practice, would the long habits of my mind let me settle? Or would something keep nagging at me from underneath?

## SUTRA 5.2.40 — Even the Householders Despise Him

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयरिए णाराहेइ, समणे यावि तारिसो ।
गिहत्था वि णं गरिहंति, जेण जाणंति तारिसं ॥५.२.४०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.40 ||
āyarie ṇārāhei, samaṇe yāvi tāriso |
gihatthā vi ṇaṃ garihaṃti, jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He cannot serve the ācāryas, nor the (other) monks. Even householders despise him — because they recognize what kind of (man) he is.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयरिए** (āyarie) = the ācāryas / preceptors
- **ण आराहेइ** (ṇa ārāhei) = he cannot serve / propitiate / win the favor of
- **समणे यावि तारिसो** (samaṇe yāvi tāriso) = nor the monks (does such a one serve)
- **गिहत्था वि णं गरिहंति** (gihatthā vi ṇaṃ garihaṃti) = even householders despise / blame him
- **जेण जाणंति तारिसं** (jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ) = because they have come to know him as such

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse traces the social collapse that mirrors the spiritual collapse of 5.2.39. Three relational circles, named in widening order: ācāryas, śramaṇas, householders. The drunken monk has lost his standing in all three. He cannot give the kind of service to his ācāryas (vinaya, vaiyāvṛtya) that builds spiritual closeness, because he is too distracted by his vice. He cannot stand cleanly among fellow monks, because they sense the deceit. And — this is the cruelest sentence — even the householders, on whom monks depend for alms, see through him and despise him.

The reasoning is precise: *jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ* — "because they have come to know him as such." Hidden faults do not stay hidden indefinitely; the social world is slow but eventually accurate. The fall from monkhood is not first noticed by the monk himself, then by his teachers, then by the society. It tends to be noticed by the society first.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The drunken monk has lost it on every side: he can't serve his teachers, fellow monks see through him, and even the laity look down on him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the order in which a hidden fault becomes known? Who notices first, and how long can I keep telling myself otherwise?

## SUTRA 5.2.41 — The Vice-Seeker Who Rejects Virtues Cannot Hold Restraint Even at Death

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं तु अगुणप्पेही, गुणाणं च विवज्जए ।
तारिसो मरणंते वि, ण आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.४१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.41 ||
evaṃ tu aguṇappehī, guṇāṇaṃ ca vivajjae |
tāriso maraṇaṃte vi, ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus the seeker-of-faults (aguṇa-prekṣī), who casts away the virtues (guṇa) — even at the end of life such a one does not accomplish *saṃvara*.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवं तु** (evaṃ tu) = thus
- **अगुणप्पेही** (aguṇappehī) = one who looks for / clings to faults; the absorbed-in-pramāda type
- **गुणाणं विवज्जए** (guṇāṇaṃ vivajjae) = casts off (sammyak-jñāna-darśana-cāritra-kṣamā etc.) virtues
- **तारिसो** (tāriso) = such (a man)
- **मरणंते वि** (maraṇaṃte vi) = even at the end of life
- **ण आराहेइ संवरं** (ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ) = cannot accomplish saṃvara

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse closes the long block (31-41) on the deceitful, drunken, fallen monk. It generalises the argument: the specific case (drinking in secret) is one example of a wider species — the *aguṇa-prekṣī*, the man whose attention is permanently turned toward faults rather than virtues. He notices the lapses of others; he indulges his own; he rejects the established virtues — right knowledge, right faith, right conduct, forgiveness, obedience to the Jina's command. Such an orientation, sustained over a life, does not magically reverse at the moment of death.

The repetition of the closing line from 5.2.39 — *tāriso maraṇaṃte vi, ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ* — frames the block. Verse 39 began the lament; verse 41 closes it. The two together announce a single doctrine: *saṃvara* at the moment of death is not a gift; it is the ripening of a long discipline. The fallen monk gets nothing in his last hour because he has banked nothing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The fault-seeker who throws away virtues spends his life shrinking his soul. At the end, when restraint matters most, he has none left to draw on.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where is my attention habitually turned — toward faults or toward virtues? In a hundred small, daily choices, what am I quietly storing up for my last hour?

## SUTRA 5.2.42 — The Wise Monk: Austerity, Renouncing Tasty Drink, Free from Intoxication

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तवं कुव्वइ मेहावी, पणीयं वज्जए रसं ।
मज्जप्पमायविरओ, तवस्सी अइउक्कोसो ॥५.२.४२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.42 ||
tavaṃ kuvvai mehāvī, paṇīyaṃ vajjae rasaṃ |
majjappamāyavirao, tavassī aiukkoso ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The wise (medhāvī) monk practices austerity (tapas); he renounces rich/oily/tasty (paṇīta) juices and dishes; abstaining from intoxication and (its) negligence (madya-pramāda), the ascetic attains the highest excellence (atyutkarṣa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **मेहावी** (mehāvī) = the wise / disciplined monk
- **तवं कुव्वइ** (tavaṃ kuvvai) = practices austerity
- **पणीयं रसं** (paṇīyaṃ rasaṃ) = rich, oily, tasty juice / dish
- **वज्जए** (vajjae) = renounces
- **मज्जप्पमायविरओ** (majjappamāyavirao) = abstaining from intoxication and the negligence (pramāda) it causes
- **तवस्सी** (tavassī) = the austere one
- **अइउक्कोसो** (aiukkoso) = atyutkarṣa — the highest excellence; ascending the path of growth

### 5. COMMENTARY

After the long, sober warning of 31-41, the sutrakāra now lifts the gaze to the opposite figure: the wise monk who actually walks the discipline. Three things define him: he does austerity (tapas — fasts, restrictions, the long work of training the body); he gives up *paṇīta* foods (rich, fatty, juicy, especially tasty ones — the kind of food that fattens the senses); and he stays clear of intoxication and the *pramāda* (heedlessness) that follows from it.

Note the compound *majja-pamāya*. The Gujarati commentary clarifies that *madya* and *pramāda* are not two parallel evils here but a single causal chain: madya causes pramāda. The verse therefore says: by abstaining from the *cause*, the monk also abstains from the *effect*. This is the structure of all skillful asceticism — cut the root, and the branches do not have to be fought one by one.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The wise monk does austerity, gives up rich and tasty food, stays away from intoxicants and the carelessness they bring. That ascetic rises to the highest excellence.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What single root, if cut, would relieve me of a dozen branches I have been struggling against?

## SUTRA 5.2.43 — Witness His Welfare: Worshipped, Endowed With Vast Meaning

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स पस्सह कल्लाणं, अणेगसाहुपूइयं ।
विउलं अत्थसंजुत्तं, कित्तइस्सं सुणेह मे ॥५.२.४३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.43 ||
tassa passaha kallāṇaṃ, aṇegasāhupūiyaṃ |
viulaṃ atthasaṃjuttaṃ, kittaissaṃ suṇeha me ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Behold his welfare (kallāṇa) — worshipped by many monks (aṇegasāhupūita), vast (vipula), endowed with the great Goal (artha-saṃyukta — the goal of liberation). I shall extol (his virtues); hear them from me.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तस्स** (tassa) = of him (the wise, austere monk)
- **पस्सह कल्लाणं** (passaha kallāṇaṃ) = behold the welfare / blessedness
- **अणेगसाहुपूइयं** (aṇegasāhupūiyaṃ) = worshipped by many monks
- **विउलं** (viulaṃ) = vast / abundant
- **अत्थसंजुत्तं** (atthasaṃjuttaṃ) = endowed with (the supreme) artha — i.e., the goal of mokṣa
- **कित्तइस्सं** (kittaissaṃ) = I shall narrate / praise
- **सुणेह मे** (suṇeha me) = hear from me

### 5. COMMENTARY

The teacher's voice returns in the same tone he used at 5.2.37 to introduce the fallen monk — but with the polarity reversed. There he said *tassa passaha dosāiṃ* (see his faults); here he says *tassa passaha kallāṇaṃ* (see his welfare). The framing makes the contrast explicit: two paths, two outcomes, one teacher narrating both so that the disciple can see clearly which one to take.

The phrase *vipulam artha-saṃyuktam* deserves notice. In the discipline literature *artha* often slides between two meanings — practical wealth (worldly artha) and the supreme goal (mokṣa-artha). Here the latter is meant. The wise monk is welfare *because his life is bound to the great Goal*, and that binding is what gives all the other virtues their weight.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Now look at his good fortune: the wise monk is worshipped by many monks, his life is full of meaning, and it is bound to the highest goal. Listen as I describe his virtues.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "great Goal" is my life bound to? Could everything I do be traced back to that one thread?

## SUTRA 5.2.44 — The Virtue-Seeker Holds Restraint Even at Death

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं तु गुणप्पेही, अगुणाणं च विवज्जए ।
तारिसो मरणंते वि, आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.४४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.44 ||
evaṃ tu guṇappehī, aguṇāṇaṃ ca vivajjae |
tāriso maraṇaṃte vi, ārāhei saṃvaraṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus the virtue-seeker (guṇa-prekṣī), who casts off the faults (aguṇa) — even at the end of life such a one accomplishes *saṃvara* (self-restraint).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **गुणप्पेही** (guṇappehī) = one who looks-toward / cultivates virtues
- **अगुणाणं विवज्जए** (aguṇāṇaṃ vivajjae) = casts off the faults / vices
- **तारिसो** (tāriso) = such (a monk)
- **मरणंते वि** (maraṇaṃte vi) = even at the moment of death
- **आराहेइ संवरं** (ārāhei saṃvaraṃ) = accomplishes saṃvara — the cessation of karmic inflow

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is the exact mirror of 5.2.41. There the *aguṇa-prekṣī* could not hold *saṃvara* even at death; here the *guṇa-prekṣī* does hold it, even at death. The mirror is deliberate, and the lesson is the same lesson read in two directions: what I attend to becomes what I am, and what I am at the moment of death is what I have been training myself to be.

The verse is also the second of three almost identical closing-formulas (5.2.39 / 5.2.41 / 5.2.44). Three repetitions of one phrase frame the entire question of life-direction around the test of death-time saṃvara. The Jain teacher knows that the disciple may take many things lightly, but the moment of death is one moment he cannot take lightly — and so the entire teaching is anchored to that point.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The virtue-seeker who casts off vices keeps his restraint even at the moment of death. His life and his death are made of one cloth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If my last moment will be a concentrated drop of every other moment that came before, what am I distilling today?

## SUTRA 5.2.45 — Honored by Ācāryas, by Monks, Even by Householders

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आयरिए आराहेइ, समणे यावि तारिसो ।
गिहत्था वि णं पूयंति, जेण जाणंति तारिसं ॥५.२.४५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.45 ||
āyarie ārāhei, samaṇe yāvi tāriso |
gihatthā vi ṇaṃ pūyaṃti, jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He serves (ārādhayati — wins the favor of) the ācāryas, and likewise the (fellow) monks. Even householders worship him — because they recognize what kind of (man) he is.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आयरिए आराहेइ** (āyarie ārāhei) = serves / wins favor of the ācāryas
- **समणे यावि तारिसो** (samaṇe yāvi tāriso) = and the monks too, such (a one) serves
- **गिहत्था वि णं पूयंति** (gihatthā vi ṇaṃ pūyaṃti) = even the householders worship him
- **जेण जाणंति तारिसं** (jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ) = because they know him as such

### 5. COMMENTARY

The mirror of 5.2.40. There the fallen monk could not satisfy any of the three circles (ācāryas, monks, householders); here the virtuous monk wins all three. The same word *tārisa* — "of such kind" — carries opposite weight in the two verses. The whole moral psychology of the alms discipline rests on this contrast: in either direction, the inner orientation eventually becomes a public fact.

The verb *ārāhei* — "serves," "satisfies," "propitiates" — is the same word used for accomplishing *saṃvara* in the previous verse. The double use is deliberate. The monk who *ārāhei saṃvaram* (accomplishes restraint) is also the one who *ārāhei ācāryān* (satisfies preceptors): the same disposition that earns spiritual fruit also earns social grace, because both flow from the same character.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The virtuous monk satisfies his teachers, lives well among his fellow monks, and is even revered by householders — because they all see, in time, what he truly is.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If my real character is what eventually becomes visible to ācāryas, peers, and society alike, what am I building today that will be on display tomorrow?

## SUTRA 5.2.46 — The Four-Fold Thief: Of Tapas, Vows, Form, and Conduct

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तवतेणे वयतेणे, रूवतेणे य जे णरे ।
आयारभावतेणे य, कुव्वइ देवकिव्विसं ॥५.२.४६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.46 ||
tavateṇe vayateṇe, rūvateṇe ya je ṇare |
āyārabhāvateṇe ya, kuvvai devakivvisaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The man who is a thief of austerity (tapas-stena), a thief of vows (vrata-stena), a thief of form (rūpa-stena — i.e., violator of the monk's outward marks of conduct), and a thief of inner conduct (ācāra-bhāva-stena) — produces (for himself) the *kilbiṣika*-deva (state) — the lowest, most despised order of deva.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तवतेणे** (tavateṇe) = thief of tapas (one who is given the strength but neglects fasts, study, service)
- **वयतेणे** (vayateṇe) = thief of vows (one who keeps the five mahā-vratas only loosely)
- **रूवतेणे** (rūvateṇe) = thief of monastic form (violates the rules about mukha-vastrikā, rajoharaṇa, wood/clay vessels, alms-style, dress code, beard/hair, loca, etc.)
- **आयारभावतेणे** (āyārabhāvateṇe) = thief of conduct (violates the *samācārī* — pratikramaṇa, pratilekhana, svādhyāya, ten-fold samācārī, abstention from night-eating and bathing, scrupulosity in samiti and gupti)
- **कुव्वइ देवकिव्विसं** (kuvvai devakivvisaṃ) = produces / earns the state of a *kilbiṣika*-deva

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sutra now elevates its diagnosis. Drinking in secret was one symptom; the deeper disease has four faces, and each face is named here as a kind of *theft*. The metaphor is sharp: just as a thief takes something that does not belong to him, so the monk who does not perform what his vows obligate is appropriating the *prestige* of monkhood without paying its price. He keeps the title without doing the work — a theft of identity itself.

The four thefts together cover (1) the outer austerities, (2) the great vows, (3) the bodily marks of monkhood, and (4) the inner samācārī. Together they leave nothing of the monastic edifice unguarded. The destiny named — *kilbiṣika-deva* — is a deva-birth, but a low and despised one: technically heavenly but spiritually contemptible. Verses 47-48 will unfold this destiny.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A man who is a thief of austerity, vows, monastic form, and inner conduct earns himself the lowest, most contemptible deva-birth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where am I keeping the title without doing the work? What identity am I claiming whose price I have not paid?

## SUTRA 5.2.47 — Born a Despised Deva, He Cannot Even Recognize Why

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लद्धूण वि देवत्तं, उववण्णो देवकिव्विसे ।
तत्थावि से ण याणाइ, किं मे किच्चा इमं फलं ॥५.२.४७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.47 ||
laddhūṇa vi devattaṃ, uvavaṇṇo devakivvise |
tatthāvi se ṇa yāṇāi, kiṃ me kiccā imaṃ phalaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Although he has obtained deva-hood, having been born among the *kilbiṣika*-devas — even there, he does not understand: "By what action of mine did I obtain *this* (kind of) fruit?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लद्धूण वि देवत्तं** (laddhūṇa vi devattaṃ) = even having obtained deva-hood
- **उववण्णो देवकिव्विसे** (uvavaṇṇo devakivvise) = born among the kilbiṣika-devas
- **तत्थावि** (tatthāvi) = even there (in that deva-state)
- **से ण याणाइ** (se ṇa yāṇāi) = he does not know
- **किं मे किच्चा इमं फलं** (kiṃ me kiccā imaṃ phalaṃ) = "by what (action) of mine did I (obtain) this fruit?"

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse names a special tragedy. The fallen monk does become a deva — that is the surface result of his outwardly monastic life. But because his inner life was corrupted by *mithyātva* (false view), the deva-state he obtains is one of the lowest classes, the *kilbiṣikas*: heavenly beings who are despised even by other devas, of low influence and low merit. And because *mithyātva* clouds memory of past lives more than *samyaktva* does, this kilbiṣika-deva cannot even *understand* how he got here. He sees only that he is in a degraded position; he cannot trace it back to the cause.

This loss of self-understanding is, in some ways, worse than the degradation itself. The kilbiṣika cannot reform what he cannot remember. Hence the tradition's verdict: even a heavenly birth is not, by itself, a sign of spiritual progress. The kind of birth and the kind of awareness within it together determine the soul's actual position.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The fallen monk does become a deva — but a despised, low-grade one. And because his vision was distorted, even there he cannot understand: "What did I do to deserve this?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What "rewards" in my life have I received without understanding the cause? Could I trace any of them back honestly, even if I tried?

## SUTRA 5.2.48 — From There, He Falls into Mute-Goat, Hell, Animal Births, Where Awakening Is Rare

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्तो वि से चइत्ताणं, लब्भइ एलमूयगं ।
णरगं तिरिक्खजोणिं वा, बोही जत्थ सुदुल्लहा ॥५.२.४८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.48 ||
tatto vi se caittāṇaṃ, labbhai elamūyagaṃ |
ṇaragaṃ tirikkhajoṇiṃ vā, bohī jattha sudullahā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

And from there too, falling away (from that deva-state), he obtains (rebirth as) a mute goat (eḍa-mūka — a dumb sacrificial goat); or hell (naraka); or an animal womb (tiryañc-yoni) — wheresoever the gaining of *bodhi* is exceedingly rare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तत्तो वि** (tatto vi) = from there too (from the kilbiṣika deva-state)
- **से चइत्ताणं** (se caittāṇaṃ) = he, falling / departing
- **लब्भइ एलमूयगं** (labbhai elamūyagaṃ) = obtains (the womb of) a mute goat (one of the most pitiable births)
- **णरगं तिरिक्खजोणिं वा** (ṇaragaṃ tirikkhajoṇiṃ vā) = or hell, or animal womb
- **बोही जत्थ सुदुल्लहा** (bohī jattha sudullahā) = where (right) awakening is exceedingly hard to obtain

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse closes the four-step descent. (1) The man stole his own monkhood; (2) he became a *kilbiṣika*-deva; (3) even as deva he could not reform; (4) and after that deva-life ends, he tumbles into mute-goat births, hells, or animal wombs — exactly those states where the seed of right insight (*bodhi*) almost cannot germinate. This is the cost of *māyā-mṛṣā*-charioted monkhood. It does not merely waste one life; it sets up an entire onward chain of lives in which liberation grows further and further away.

The phrase *bohī sudullahā* — "bodhi exceedingly rare" — is one of the great refrains of Jain literature. Right insight, the kind that turns a soul toward liberation, is not freely distributed across realms. Some realms make it almost impossible. Hence the urgency of using a human birth — with its rare combination of intelligence, freedom, and access to teaching — for serious practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the kilbiṣika-deva-life ends, he falls again — into mute-goat births, hells, or animal wombs, where the chance of spiritual awakening is almost gone.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What conditions make awakening *possible* for me right now that may not be available later? Am I treating this opportunity as if it were rare?

## SUTRA 5.2.49 — Knowing This, the Wise Should Renounce Even an Atom of Deceit

एयं च दोसं दट्ठूणं, णायपुत्तेण भासियं ।
अणुमायं पि मेहावी, मायामोसं विवज्जए ॥५.२.४९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.49 ||
eyaṃ ca dosaṃ daṭṭhūṇaṃ, ṇāyaputteṇa bhāsiyaṃ |
aṇumāyaṃ pi mehāvī, māyāmosaṃ vivajjae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having seen this fault — declared by the son of the Jñātrikas (Mahāvīra) — the wise (medhāvī) should renounce *māyā-mṛṣā* (deceit-and-falsehood) even to the measure of an atom (aṇumātram).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एयं च दोसं** (eyaṃ ca dosaṃ) = and this fault (just expounded)
- **दट्ठूणं** (daṭṭhūṇaṃ) = having seen / understood
- **णायपुत्तेण** (ṇāyaputteṇa) = by the son of Jñātṛ (Mahāvīra)
- **भासियं** (bhāsiyaṃ) = (has been) declared
- **अणुमायं पि** (aṇumāyaṃ pi) = even (to the measure of) an atom
- **मेहावी** (mehāvī) = the wise (one)
- **मायामोसं विवज्जए** (māyāmosaṃ vivajjae) = should renounce deceit-cum-falsehood

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing teaching of the long fall-of-the-deceitful-monk arc. The verse explicitly attributes the doctrine to *Jñātaputra* — Mahāvīra himself — giving the warning the highest possible authority. And the closing instruction is striking: *aṇumātram api* — "even to the measure of an atom." Not "renounce great deceit"; not "renounce visible lies"; but: *do not allow even an atom* of māyā-mṛṣā into your life.

The reasoning of the previous verses is the foundation for this severity. A small deceit unleashes a slow chain reaction: first concealment, then drinking-or-its-equivalent, then social isolation, then loss of saṃvara, then a degraded heavenly birth, then animal births, then near-impossible *bodhi*. The chain begins with "an atom." Therefore the discipline targets the atom.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Mahāvīra has shown you this whole chain of harm. So the wise monk renounces even the smallest atom of deceit and lying.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the smallest deceit I currently allow myself? If I traced its ten-step downstream consequence honestly, what would I see?

## SUTRA 5.2.50 — The Closing Instruction of Adhyayana 5, Uddesha 2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिक्खिऊण भिक्खेसणसोहिं, संजयाण बुद्धाण सगासे ।
तत्थ भिक्खु सुप्पणिहिंदिए, तिव्वलज्जगुणवं विहरिज्जासि ॥त्ति बेमि॥५.२.५०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.2.50 ||
sikkhiūṇa bhikkhesaṇasohiṃ, saṃjayāṇa buddhāṇa sagāse |
tattha bhikkhu suppaṇihiṃdie, tivvalajjaguṇavaṃ viharijjāsi ||tti bemi||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having learned the purification of *bhikṣaiṣaṇā* — the discipline of seeking alms — in the proximity (sa-gāse) of the restrained ones (saṃyatas) and the awakened (buddhas) — there (in that very discipline), let the monk, with senses well-held (su-praṇihit-indriya), endowed with intense moral shame (tīvra-lajjā) and (other) virtues, dwell and move. — Thus I declare. (Iti bravīmi.)

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सिक्खिऊण** (sikkhiūṇa) = having learned (well)
- **भिक्खेसणसोहिं** (bhikkhesaṇasohiṃ) = the purification of *bhikṣa-eṣaṇā* (the alms-seeking discipline)
- **संजयाण** (saṃjayāṇa) = of the restrained ones (saṃyatas)
- **बुद्धाण** (buddhāṇa) = of the awakened ones / those who know the truths (tattvajña, ācāryas)
- **सगासे** (sagāse) = in the proximity (sakāśa) of
- **तत्थ** (tattha) = there (i.e., in that very discipline of eṣaṇā)
- **भिक्खु** (bhikkhu) = the monk
- **सुप्पणिहिंदिए** (suppaṇihiṃdie) = with senses well-controlled (su-praṇihita-indriya)
- **तिव्वलज्ज** (tivvalajja) = with intense lajjā (moral shame at any unworthy act)
- **गुणवं** (guṇavaṃ) = endowed with virtues
- **विहरिज्जासि** (viharijjāsi) = let him move / dwell / live
- **त्ति बेमि** (tti bemi) = "thus I declare" (the closing formula)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Uddesha closes with the standard *upasaṃhāra* — the summarizing instruction. Three verbs structure the sentence. *Sikkhiūṇa* — having learned: not from books or guesswork, but in the *sakāśa* (close presence) of *saṃjaya-buddhas*, the restrained and the awakened. The discipline of *bhikṣaiṣaṇā* is too subtle to pick up on one's own; it must be transmitted by life-long practitioners to a disciple who lives close to them. *Tattha* — there: the monk does not "learn" this once and then move on to other practice; the very alms-discipline becomes his ongoing field of life. *Viharijjāsi* — let him move: the spiritual life is described not as "stationing oneself in" but as "moving / dwelling / wandering through" the discipline.

Three qualities are named for that movement: *suppaṇihit-indriya* (senses well-restrained), *tīvra-lajjā* (intense moral shame at the very thought of an unworthy act), and *guṇavān* (richly virtuous). The last term sums up everything the Uddesha has taught — the four-fold purity of seeking, the equanimity in receiving, the absolute refusal of deceit, the vigilance about every grain of sentient food. *Iti bravīmi*: "thus I declare." The teacher closes; the disciple carries the entire discourse forward into the moving life of begging, eating, sharing, and disposing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Learn the alms-discipline closely from restrained, awakened teachers. Then, with senses controlled, with deep moral sensitivity, with virtue, *live* inside that discipline as your way of moving through the world. — Thus I declare.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to *live inside* a discipline rather than merely to *follow* one? Whose proximity am I learning from, and is that learning still alive in my daily moving?
