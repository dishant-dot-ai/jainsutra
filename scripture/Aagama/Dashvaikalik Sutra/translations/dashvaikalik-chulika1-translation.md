# Dashvaikalik Sutra — Pratham Chulika
# Rativakya (રતિવાક્યા) — Words That Restore Delight in the Path

**Pratham (First) Chulika of the Dashvaikalik Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Dashvaikalik Sutra, printed pages 473–489 (PDF pages 514–521)*
*Total Sutras in this Chulika: 1 prose sutra (with 18 contemplation-points) + 17 gathas*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The word *chulika* (Skt. *cūlikā*) means a "crest," a "peak," a "topknot" — the crown that completes the figure. Just as the tuft of hair atop the head completes the form of a person, just as the spire of a mountain completes its shape, the two chulikas of the Dashvaikalik Sutra are not extra material but the natural completion of the work. The chapter intro records that the twelfth Aṅga (the *Dṛṣṭivāda*) is similarly capped by chulika sections.

The first chulika is named **Rativakya** — "Words that produce *rati* (delight, attachment, love) in the disciplined life." Its purpose is sharply pastoral. The Dashvaikalik proper has set out the conduct of monastic life across ten chapters; but a monk in his actual living may still find that *arati* — disgust, weariness, restlessness with the discipline — rises in his heart. He may begin to wonder: was renouncing the household worth it? Should I go back? Is the suffering of the discipline really worth the unseen liberation? This chulika is the answer the Jina prepared for that exact moment.

The chulika opens with a beautiful three-fold simile: just as a horse needs a bit, an elephant needs a goad, and a ship needs a rudder to be steadied — so the wavering monk needs *eighteen contemplation-points* (*aṭṭhārasa-ṭhāṇāiṃ*) to steady his heart back into the path. These eighteen points are then set out in a long prose sutra. Each one lets the practitioner see the householder's life clearly — its scarcity, its short-lived pleasures, its deceitful relationships, its bondage, its inseparability from sin, the sharing of its joys with many strangers, the unbridgeable individuality of each soul's karma, the fragility of human life, and the impossibility of escaping karma except by enduring or burning it. To meditate on these eighteen, the chulika says, is to settle the wavering mind back into firm delight in the discipline.

After the prose sutra, seventeen gathas follow that return to and elaborate the same teaching in verse. Several of them use vivid similes — the disgraced Indra fallen to earth, a king dethroned, a merchant beggared, a man choking on his own vomit — to picture what it really means to abandon the great achievement of the monastic life for a brief return to household pleasure. The chulika is, in short, the Jina's pastoral address to every monk who, in the small hours, has wondered whether to go back. It says: *don't*. And it gives reason after reason after reason.

The closing line of the chulika is the seal: *chaijja dehaṃ na hu dhamma-sāsaṇaṃ* — "I will give up this body, but I will not give up the teaching of the dharma." That single line is the chulika's gift to every wavering practitioner.

## CHULIKA-1.1 — The Eighteen Contemplation-Points (Prose Sutra)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इह खलु भो ! पव्वइएणं उप्पण्णदुक्खेणं संजमे अरइसमावण्णचित्तेणं, ओहाणुप्पेहिणा अणोहाइएणं चेव, हयरस्सि गयंकुस पोयपडागाभूयाइं, इमाइं अट्ठारसट्ठाणाई सम्मं संपडिलेहियव्वाई भवंति; तं जहा—

हं भो ! दुस्समाए दुप्पजीवी ॥१॥ लहुस्सगा इत्तरिया गिहीणं कामभोगा ॥२॥ भुज्जो य साइबहुला मणुस्सा ॥३॥ इमे य मे दुक्खे ण चिरकालोवट्ठाई भविस्सइ ॥४॥ ओमजणपुरक्कारे ॥५॥ वंतस्स य पडियायणं ॥६॥ अहरगइवासोवसंपया ॥७॥ दुल्लहे खलु भो ! गिहीणं धम्मे, गिहीवासमज्झे वसंताणं ॥८॥ आयंके से वहाय होइ ॥९॥ संकप्पे से वहाय होइ ॥१०॥ बंधे गिहवासे, मोक्खे परियाए ॥११॥ सोवक्कसे गिहवासे, णिरुवक्कसे परियाए ॥१२॥ सावज्जे गिहवासे, अणवज्जे परियाए ॥१३॥ बहुसाहारणा गिहीणं कामभोगा ॥१४॥ पत्तेयं पुण्णपावं ॥१५॥ अणिच्चे खलु भो ! मणुयाण जीविए, कुसग्गजलबिंदुचंचले ॥१६॥ बहुं च खलु भो ! पावं कम्मं पगडं ॥१७॥ पावाणं च खलु भो कडाण कम्माणं पुव्विं दुच्चिण्णाणं दुप्पडिक्कंताणं, वेइत्ता मोक्खो, णत्थि अवेइत्ता, तवसा वा झोसइत्ता ॥१८॥ अट्ठारसमं पयं भवइ। भवइ य इत्थ सिलोगो—

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.1 ||
iha khalu bho ! pavvaieṇaṃ uppaṇṇadukkheṇaṃ saṃjame araisamāvaṇṇa-citteṇaṃ, ohāṇuppehiṇā aṇohāieṇaṃ ceva, haya-rassi gayaṃkusa poyapaḍāgābhūyāiṃ, imāiṃ aṭṭhārasa-ṭhāṇāiṃ sammaṃ saṃpaḍilehiyavvāiṃ bhavaṃti; taṃ jahā—

(1) haṃ bho ! dussamāe duppajīvī. (2) lahussagā ittariyā gihīṇaṃ kāmabhogā. (3) bhujjo ya sāibahulā maṇussā. (4) ime ya me dukkhe ṇa cirakālovaṭṭhāī bhavissai. (5) omajaṇapurakkāre. (6) vaṃtassa ya paḍiyāyaṇaṃ. (7) aharagaivāsovasaṃpayā. (8) dullahe khalu bho! gihīṇaṃ dhamme, gihīvāsa-majjhe vasaṃtāṇaṃ. (9) āyaṃke se vahāya hoi. (10) saṃkappe se vahāya hoi. (11) baṃdhe gihavāse, mokkhe pariyāe. (12) sovakkase gihavāse, ṇiruvakkase pariyāe. (13) sāvajje gihavāse, aṇavajje pariyāe. (14) bahu-sāhāraṇā gihīṇaṃ kāmabhogā. (15) patteyaṃ puṇṇa-pāvaṃ. (16) aṇicce khalu bho! maṇuyāṇa jīvie, kusagga-jala-biṃdu-caṃcale. (17) bahuṃ ca khalu bho! pāvaṃ kammaṃ pagaḍaṃ. (18) pāvāṇaṃ ca khalu bho kaḍāṇa kammāṇaṃ puvviṃ duccinnāṇaṃ duppaḍikkaṃtāṇaṃ, veittā mokkho, ṇatthi aveittā, tavasā vā jhosaittā. aṭṭhārasamaṃ payaṃ bhavai. bhavai ya ittha siloga —

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Here, indeed, O (disciples)! For the one who has gone forth, in whom suffering has arisen, whose mind has fallen into displeasure with the discipline, who is *contemplating return* (to the household) but has not yet actually returned — for such a one, like the bit for the horse, the goad for the elephant, the rudder-flag for the ship, these eighteen contemplation-points should be properly examined; namely:—

(1) Ah O (soul)! In this evil age, life is hard to live. (2) The household sense-pleasures are insubstantial and short-lived. (3) Moreover, men are full of much deceit. (4) "This suffering of mine will not last forever." (5) (In the household) one must humble oneself even before lowly people. (6) (Returning) is like swallowing what was vomited. (7) (Returning) is the embrace of a low-rebirth dwelling. (8) Hard indeed, O! is dharma for householders, for those living in the midst of the household. (9) Sickness arises for the householder's destruction. (10) Painful mental constructions (anxieties) arise for the householder's destruction. (11) The household is bondage; the (monk's) life-state is liberation. (12) The household is full of distress; the (monk's) life-state is free of distress. (13) The household is sin-filled; the (monk's) life-state is sin-free. (14) Household pleasures must be shared with many. (15) Each soul's merit and demerit is its own. (16) Impermanent indeed, O! is human life — trembling like a water-drop on the tip of a kuśa-grass blade. (17) I have, indeed, done much sinful karma. (18) Of (those) sinful karmas already done, badly behaved in the past, badly atoned for — there is liberation only by enduring (their fruit), not by avoiding it; or else by burning them up through austerity. — This is the eighteenth point. And here there is a verse:—

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इह खलु भो** (iha khalu bho) = here in this Jina-teaching, O (mumukṣus)
- **पव्वइएणं** (pavvaieṇaṃ) = by one who has gone forth (initiated)
- **उप्पण्णदुक्खेणं** (uppaṇṇadukkheṇaṃ) = in whom suffering has arisen
- **संजमे अरइसमावण्णचित्तेणं** (saṃjame arai-samāvaṇṇa-citteṇaṃ) = whose mind has fallen into *arati* (displeasure) with the discipline
- **ओहाणुप्पेहिणा** (ohāṇuppehiṇā) = (one who is) thinking of giving up (the discipline)
- **अणोहाइएणं चेव** (aṇohāieṇaṃ ceva) = but has not yet actually given it up
- **हयरस्सि गयंकुस पोयपडागाभूयाइं** (haya-rassi gayaṃkusa poyapaḍāgābhūyāiṃ) = (which become) like the bit (rein) for a horse, the goad for an elephant, the rudder-flag for a ship
- **इमाइं अट्ठारसट्ठाणाई** (imāiṃ aṭṭhārasa-ṭhāṇāiṃ) = these eighteen points (sthāna)
- **सम्मं संपडिलेहियव्वाई भवंति** (sammaṃ saṃpaḍilehiyavvāiṃ bhavaṃti) = should be well-examined / well-contemplated
- *(The eighteen points then follow as numbered above; key terms within them:)*
- **दुस्समा** (dussamā) = the duṣama (degenerate, evil) age — the fifth ara of avasarpiṇī
- **लहुस्सगा** (lahussagā) = light, insubstantial
- **इत्तरिया** (ittariyā) = brief, short-lived
- **साइबहुला** (sāibahulā) = full of much *sāti* (deceit, untrustworthiness)
- **ओमजण** (omajaṇa) = lowly persons (those of small status)
- **पुरक्कारे** (purakkāre) = obeisance, having to honour
- **वंतस्स पडियायणं** (vaṃtassa paḍiyāyaṇaṃ) = swallowing back what was vomited
- **अहरगइवासोवसंपया** (aharagai-vāsovasaṃpayā) = the embrace of a low-rebirth dwelling
- **आयंके** (āyaṃke) = sickness, disease
- **वहाय** (vahāya) = (becomes) for the destruction (of the householder's well-being)
- **संकप्पे** (saṃkappe) = mental constructions, anxious thoughts
- **बंधे गिहवासे** (baṃdhe gihavāse) = the household is bondage
- **मोक्खे परियाए** (mokkhe pariyāe) = the monastic life-state is liberation
- **सोवक्कसे** (sovakkase) = filled with kleśa (distress); **णिरुवक्कसे** (ṇiruvakkase) = free of kleśa
- **सावज्जे** (sāvajje) = sin-filled; **अणवज्जे** (aṇavajje) = sinless
- **बहुसाहारणा** (bahu-sāhāraṇā) = held in common with many (shared with thieves, fornicators, family, government, etc.)
- **पत्तेयं पुण्णपावं** (patteyaṃ puṇṇa-pāvaṃ) = each (being's) merit and demerit is its own
- **कुसग्गजलबिंदुचंचले** (kusagga-jala-biṃdu-caṃcale) = trembling like a water-drop on the tip of a blade of kuśa grass
- **वेइत्ता मोक्खो** (veittā mokkho) = liberation (from karma) only by enduring (its fruit)
- **णत्थि अवेइत्ता** (ṇatthi aveittā) = there is no (liberation) without enduring
- **तवसा वा झोसइत्ता** (tavasā vā jhosaittā) = or by burning (it) up through austerity

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the heart of the chulika and addresses, with extraordinary directness, the wavering monk. The opening sentence diagnoses the condition: a monk has been initiated; suffering has arisen in his life; his mind has fallen into *arati* (displeasure) with the discipline; he is now *contemplating return* to the household but has not yet actually broken the vow. This is exactly the moment the chulika addresses. Not the monk who has fallen, but the monk who is on the brink. To him, the sutra offers the bit, the goad, the rudder.

The eighteen points are then a structured contemplation. They fall into rough groups. (1)–(7) compare the state being contemplated (return to the household) to the state being held (the monastic discipline) and find the householder's life wanting on every count: hard living in this evil age; pleasures small and brief; companions deceitful; one's own suffering not actually permanent; one would have to bow to lowly people; the act of return is itself like swallowing one's own vomit; it is the embrace of a low-rebirth abode. (8)–(13) make the philosophical case: dharma is hard to practice in the household; sickness and anxieties strike the householder for his destruction; the household is bondage / sorrow / sin-stained; the monastic life-state is liberation / freedom from sorrow / sin-free.

Points (14)–(18) drive deeper. (14): household pleasures are not yours alone — you share them with kings, thieves, employees, family — so the satisfaction is divided. (15): each soul faces its own karma; you cannot share that. So returning to the household to seek pleasure does not solve your karmic situation. (16): human life itself is fragile — a water-drop on a kuśa-grass tip, ready to fall at any breath of wind. (17): I have already done much sinful karma. (18): and there is no escape from karma except by enduring its fruit or by burning it through austerity. The implicit conclusion is irresistible: since karma must be faced anyway, face it where the burning can occur — in the discipline, not in the household where new karma is being added every hour.

The eighteen points are not abstract doctrine but a meditation script. The chulika prescribes this script for the moment of crisis. The Sutra acknowledges, more honestly than almost any text in the tradition, that the path is hard and that the practitioner will sometimes want to leave it. Its response is not condemnation but contemplation. *Sammaṃ saṃpaḍilehiyavvāiṃ* — "to be examined well." Sit with these eighteen, the Sutra says; let them work on you; and when they have done their work, you will not return.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk's heart has filled with sorrow, he has come to dislike the discipline, and he is starting to think about going back to household life — though he has not actually broken his vows yet — there are eighteen contemplation-points he should examine carefully. These are like the bit that steers a horse, the goad that steers an elephant, the rudder-flag that steers a ship. They are: (1) life in this evil age is hard; (2) household pleasures are tiny and brief; (3) people are full of deceit; (4) "this suffering of mine will not last forever"; (5) in the household one has to bow even to small people; (6) going back is like swallowing your own vomit; (7) going back is the embrace of a low-rebirth life; (8) practising dharma as a householder is very difficult; (9) sickness comes to destroy the householder; (10) anxious thoughts come to destroy him; (11) the household is bondage, monastic life is liberation; (12) the household is full of distress, monastic life is free of it; (13) the household is sin-filled, monastic life is sinless; (14) household pleasures are shared with many; (15) each soul's karma is its own; (16) human life is as fragile as a water-drop on a grass-tip; (17) "I have already done a lot of sinful karma"; (18) the only way out of karma is to endure its fruit or burn it with austerity. Sit with these eighteen.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you are tempted to go back to a discipline you have abandoned, are you tempted by the thing itself, or only by the imagined sweetness of escape from your present discomfort?

## CHULIKA-1.2 — The Ignorant One Who Trades Discipline for Pleasure

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य चयइ धम्मं, अणज्जो भोगकारणा ।
से तत्थ मुच्छिए बालो, आयइं णावबुज्झइ ॥१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.2 ||
jayā ya cayai dhammaṃ, aṇajjo bhogakāraṇā |
se tattha mucchie bālo, āyaiṃ ṇāvabujjhai ||1||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When the ignoble one (an-ārya) gives up dharma for the sake of (sense) enjoyment, he, being there infatuated, the fool — does not understand the future (consequences).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जया** (jayā) = when
- **चयइ धम्मं** (cayai dhammaṃ) = gives up the dharma (cāritra-dharma — monastic conduct)
- **अणज्जो** (aṇajjo) = the an-ārya (ignoble one — anyone whose conduct is without discrimination, like a mleccha)
- **भोगकारणा** (bhogakāraṇā) = for the sake of (sense) enjoyments
- **से तत्थ मुच्छिए** (se tattha mucchie) = he, there infatuated (in those enjoyments)
- **बालो** (bālo) = the fool, the spiritually-immature
- **आयइं** (āyaiṃ) = the future (consequence), one's own welfare, or one's own dignity
- **ण अवबुज्झइ** (ṇa avabujjhai) = does not understand, does not awaken to

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first verse of the gatha-portion frames the entire problem in a single picture. A monk who returns to the household for the sake of sense-pleasure is, despite whatever learning he may have, *aṇajja* — an-ārya, ignoble. The Jain tradition's use of *ārya* is not racial but ethical: an Ārya is one whose conduct is governed by discriminative wisdom, an Anārya is one whose conduct is not. To give up the great vow for the small reward of sense-pleasure is by definition the work of someone whose discrimination has failed.

The verse calls such a one *bāla* — a child, a fool. The Jain technical sense of *bāla* is not chronological youth but spiritual immaturity. A child puts whatever is in front of it into its mouth without thought of consequence; the *bāla* monk grasps at sense-pleasure without thinking of *āyai* — the future, his own welfare, the dignity of his soul. The verse thus diagnoses the inner state of the one who returns: it is not that he has reasoned and decided; it is that he has been *infatuated* (mucchie) and has lost the capacity to consider. The chulika is therefore aimed at restoring discrimination *before* infatuation can take hold.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When an ignoble person gives up the dharma for the sake of sense-pleasures, he becomes infatuated with those pleasures, and like a foolish child he simply does not see what is coming next.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time pleasure made you stop thinking about consequences?

## CHULIKA-1.3 — Like Indra Fallen From Heaven

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया ओहाविओ होइ, इंदो वा पडिओ छमं ।
सव्वधम्मपरिब्भट्ठो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.3 ||
jayā ohāvio hoi, iṃdo vā paḍio chamaṃ |
savva-dhamma-paribbhaṭṭho, sa pacchā paritappai ||2||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the monk) has fled (the discipline) — like Indra fallen to the earth — that one, fallen from all dharma, afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जया** (jayā) = when
- **ओहाविओ होइ** (ohāvio hoi) = (he) has run away (from cāritra-dharma)
- **इंदो वा** (iṃdo vā) = like Indra (the king of heavens)
- **पडिओ छमं** (paḍio chamaṃ) = fallen on the earth
- **सव्वधम्मपरिब्भट्ठो** (savva-dhamma-paribbhaṭṭho) = fallen from all dharma (the entire conduct-dharma)
- **स पच्छा परितप्पइ** (sa pacchā paritappai) = he afterwards burns (with remorse, paritāpa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The next four verses give four similes for the fallen monk. The first is the most exalted: Indra, king of the gods, who lives in the celestial realms in inconceivable splendour. When his merit is exhausted and he must take rebirth on earth as a human, the contrast between what he was and what he has become tortures him. The verse uses this image for the monk who has abandoned the discipline. The fallen monk in his new life will sometimes look back and remember what he was — the great muni, the disciplined one, the bearer of the great vows — and the contrast will burn him.

The verse subtly insists that there is no escape from this remorse. The decision to leave looks, in the moment, like relief. But *pacchā* — afterwards — the burning comes. The chulika is thus an appeal not only to the seeker's wisdom but to his self-interest: *do not do this thing, because afterwards you will burn*. The four similes that follow elaborate the same pattern.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk runs away from the discipline — like Indra falling to the earth from heaven — he loses every part of his dharma, and afterwards he burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A choice that looks like relief now — what does it look like one year later?

## CHULIKA-1.4 — From Worshipped to Despised

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य वंदिमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अवंदिमो ।
देवया व चुया ठाणा, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.4 ||
jayā ya vaṃdimo hoi, pacchā hoi avaṃdimo |
devayā va cuyā ṭhāṇā, sa pacchā paritappai ||3||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the monk) was once worthy of salutation, but afterwards becomes unworthy of salutation — like a deity fallen from her station — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **वंदिमो** (vaṃdimo) = worthy of salutation, vandanīya
- **अवंदिमो** (avaṃdimo) = unworthy of salutation, fit only for contempt
- **देवया व** (devayā va) = like a goddess (or like the queen of Indra, the goddess Indrāṇī)
- **चुया ठाणा** (cuyā ṭhāṇā) = fallen from (her) station

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second simile turns to social stature. As a monk, the practitioner was bowed to by the world. Lay people came for darshan, asked for blessing, regarded him with reverence. As one fallen from the discipline, the same world now turns its face. He has become an object not of reverence but of disregard — even of mild contempt — for he is the one who could not hold his vow. The verse compares this to a *devī* — a goddess, perhaps Indrāṇī herself — who, when her merit exhausts and she must descend to the human realm, loses the worship of all the lesser deities who once bowed to her. The fall is doubly painful: the loss of the height, and the awareness of the bowing now denied.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk who used to be worshipped becomes someone people no longer salute — like a goddess fallen from her station in heaven — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of what you call your dignity depends on what you have already given up to earn it?

## CHULIKA-1.5 — From King to Dethroned

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य पूइमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अपूइमो ।
राया व रज्जपब्भट्ठो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.5 ||
jayā ya pūimo hoi, pacchā hoi apūimo |
rāyā va rajja-pabbhaṭṭho, sa pacchā paritappai ||4||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the monk) was once worthy of worship (pūjā), but afterwards becomes unworthy of worship — like a king fallen from his kingdom — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पूइमो** (pūimo) = worthy of pūjā (formal worship and reverence)
- **अपूइमो** (apūimo) = unworthy of pūjā
- **राया व** (rāyā va) = like a king
- **रज्जपब्भट्ठो** (rajja-pabbhaṭṭho) = fallen from (his) kingdom

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third simile sharpens the second. The previous verse spoke of the loss of *vandana* (salutation); this verse speaks of the loss of *pūjā* (formal worship — the offering of flowers, lamps, water at the feet). As a monk, even kings would come to make pūjā at his feet. As a fallen monk, even ordinary people withdraw their offerings. The simile is the king who is dethroned. He keeps the memory of the throne; he keeps the remembered taste of being bowed to; but the throne is gone. Every commoner who once approached on his knees now passes him without a glance. The pain of the dethroned king is sharper than the pain of one who never had a throne.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk who was once formally worshipped becomes someone no one worships any more — like a king fallen from his kingdom — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The reverence that comes to one who keeps the discipline is itself a kind of wealth. What is it actually worth, and what would you lose by losing it?

## CHULIKA-1.6 — From Honoured Merchant to Beggar

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य माणिमो होइ, पच्छा होइ अमाणिमो ।
सेट्ठिव्व कब्बडे छूढो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.6 ||
jayā ya māṇimo hoi, pacchā hoi amāṇimo |
seṭṭhivva kabbaḍe chūḍho, sa pacchā paritappai ||5||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the monk) was once held in honour (māna), but afterwards becomes unhonoured — like a great merchant (śreṣṭhin) cast into a wretched village (kabbaḍa) — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **माणिमो** (māṇimo) = held in honour (māna — esteem)
- **अमाणिमो** (amāṇimo) = unhonoured, dishonoured
- **सेट्ठिव्व** (seṭṭhivva) = like a śreṣṭhin (great merchant, head of a guild)
- **कब्बडे छूढो** (kabbaḍe chūḍho) = cast into a wretched / poor little village

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth simile is mercantile. A great merchant, accustomed to a wealthy city's commerce, festivals, society, and stature, is one day cast (perhaps by misfortune, perhaps by his own folly) into a poor backwater village. He still has the memory of the city. He still has the bearing of one who was great. But in this village no one knows him; no one cares for his stature; he is just one more poor man. The verse uses this image for the loss of *māna* — the honour formerly enjoyed. The previous three similes used celestial / royal / governmental images; this one uses the everyday image of the wealthy merchant who has lost his standing. The point is the same: what was once is no longer, and the contrast is what burns.

The four similes together — Indra fallen, goddess descended, king dethroned, merchant beggared — serve to flood the wavering monk's imagination with the *afterwards*. Whatever the imagined pleasure of return, this is what awaits.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk who was once held in great honour becomes one no one honours — like a great merchant who has been cast into a wretched little village — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you imagine giving something up, do you imagine only the relief — or do you also imagine, honestly, what you will look like to yourself a year later?

## CHULIKA-1.7 — Old Age Caught on the Hook

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य थेरओ होइ, समइक्कंतजुव्वणो ।
मच्छुव्व गलं गिलित्ता, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.7 ||
jayā ya thero hoi, samaikkaṃta-juvvaṇo |
macchuvva galaṃ gilittā, sa pacchā paritappai ||6||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the fallen monk) becomes old, his youth gone past — like a fish that has swallowed the hook — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **थेरओ** (thero) = an old man (sthavira)
- **समइक्कंतजुव्वणो** (samaikkaṃta-juvvaṇo) = (one) whose youth has fully passed
- **मच्छुव्व** (macchuvva) = like a fish (matsya)
- **गलं गिलित्ता** (galaṃ gilittā) = having swallowed the hook (gala — the iron hook on the angler's line)
- **स पच्छा परितप्पइ** (sa pacchā paritappai) = he afterwards burns with regret

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fifth simile shifts from social fall to bodily decline. The monk who left the discipline in youth, drawn by sense-pleasure, eventually grows old. His youth has passed. His body is no longer fit for the pleasures that lured him. But he is now too far from the discipline to return to it, and the householder's life — which only made sense as a vehicle for those pleasures — has nothing left for him. The image is the fish that, attracted by the small piece of meat on the hook, swallows the hook itself. The momentary pleasure of the meat is followed by being torn from the water and dying. The momentary pleasure of the abandoned discipline is followed by old age in which the soul is impaled, unable to enjoy and unable to return.

The Jain commentary notes the precision of the simile: the fish does not see the hook, only the bait. The fallen monk did not see the old age, only the immediate pleasure. By the time the hook is felt, it is too late.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the fallen monk grows old and his youth is gone — like a fish that has swallowed the angler's hook (lured by a tiny bait, now caught and dying) — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The piece of bait you are reaching for — what hook is hidden inside it?

## CHULIKA-1.8 — Bound Like an Elephant in Family Troubles

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य कुकुडुंबस्स, कुतत्तीहिं विहम्मइ ।
हत्थी व बंधणे बद्धो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.8 ||
jayā ya kukuḍuṃbassa, kutattīhiṃ vihammai |
hatthī va baṃdhaṇe baddho, sa pacchā paritappai ||7||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When (the fallen monk) is afflicted by the wretched anxieties of a wretched family — like an elephant bound in chains — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कुकुडुंबस्स** (kukuḍuṃbassa) = of a wretched / troubled family (ku-kuṭumba)
- **कुतत्तीहिं** (kutattīhiṃ) = by wretched anxieties (ku-cintā — the household worries)
- **विहम्मइ** (vihammai) = is struck, is afflicted
- **हत्थी व** (hatthī va) = like an elephant
- **बंधणे बद्धो** (baṃdhaṇe baddho) = bound in chains, in bondage

### 6. COMMENTARY

The sixth and seventh similes form a pair drawn from the elephant — the great free creature of the forest now reduced to bondage. The fallen monk, having returned to the household, finds himself entangled in the petty worries of a wretched family — financial troubles, illnesses of relatives, marriages and deaths and quarrels of children, the constant management of a thousand small matters. He came back for pleasure; what he gets is anxiety. The image is the wild elephant captured by men, lured into a pit, then chained and trained to a domesticated life of carrying loads. He was once a king of the forest; now he labours under a mahout's stick. The fall from the dignity of monastic life to the daily harness of household worry is described in this image with great force.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the fallen monk is afflicted by the petty worries of a troubled family — like an elephant captured and bound in chains — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How free do you really feel inside the things you have voluntarily taken on?

## CHULIKA-1.9 — Surrounded by Family, Like an Elephant Stuck in Mud

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुत्तदारपरिकिण्णो मोहसंताणसंततो ।
पंकोसण्णो जहा नागो, स पच्छा परितप्पइ ॥८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.9 ||
puttadāra-parikiṇṇo, moha-saṃtāṇa-saṃtato |
paṃkosaṇṇo jahā nāgo, sa pacchā paritappai ||8||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Surrounded by sons and wife, continuously enmeshed in the chain of delusion — like an elephant sunk in mud — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पुत्तदारपरिकिण्णो** (puttadāra-parikiṇṇo) = surrounded by son(s) and wife (putra-dāra)
- **मोहसंताणसंततो** (moha-saṃtāṇa-saṃtato) = continuously bound in the chain (saṃtāna) of delusion (moha)
- **पंकोसण्णो** (paṃkosaṇṇo) = sunk in mud (paṅka — slimy mud)
- **जहा नागो** (jahā nāgo) = like an elephant (nāga)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The seventh simile is the famous image of the thirsty elephant. In the burning days of summer, when the lakes are nearly dry, the great elephant comes to a shrinking pool. The water that remains is too far across the wide expanse of mud at the lake's edge. The elephant, desperate for water, steps into the mud believing he will reach the water; but with each step the mud grows deeper, and finally he is stuck — unable to reach the water, unable to climb back to the bank. There he stands, exhausted, dying of thirst within sight of the water that drew him in.

This is the picture of the fallen monk surrounded by the family he came back to enjoy. The wife, the son, the household — these were the water he reached for. The mud he is sunk in is the *moha-saṃtāna* — the unbroken chain of delusion that comes with these attachments. He cannot enjoy them as he hoped (the water never reached); he cannot return to the discipline (the bank is no longer accessible). He stands stuck, in growing despair. The chulika's images grow steadily darker as it forces the wavering monk to see the *afterwards*.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Surrounded by his wife and children, continuously bound in the chain of delusion — like a thirsty elephant who stepped into the muddy edge of a shrinking lake and is now stuck, unable to reach the water and unable to climb back — he afterwards burns with regret.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The relationships you ran toward — are any of them now mud you cannot get out of?

## CHULIKA-1.10 — "I Could Have Been an Acharya"

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अज्जाहं गणी हुंतो, भाविअप्पा बहुस्सुओ ।
जइ हं रमंतो परियाए, सामण्णे जिणदेसिए ॥९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.10 ||
ajjāhaṃ gaṇī huṃto, bhāviappā bahussuo |
jai haṃ ramaṃto pariyāe, sāmaṇṇe jiṇadesie ||9||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Today I would have been an Acharya (gaṇī), a soul ripened by contemplation, deeply learned in scripture — if I had remained delighting in the monastic life of śramaṇa-hood as taught by the Jina."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अज्ज** (ajja) = today
- **अहं** (ahaṃ) = I
- **गणी हुंतो** (gaṇī huṃto) = would have been an Acharya (gaṇī — head of a gaṇa, monastic group)
- **भाविअप्पा** (bhāviappā) = with self ripened by contemplation (bhāvitātmā)
- **बहुस्सुओ** (bahussuo) = deeply learned in scripture (bahu-śruta)
- **जइ हं** (jai haṃ) = if I (had)
- **रमंतो परियाए** (ramaṃto pariyāe) = (had) delighted (remained absorbed) in the monastic state (paryāya)
- **सामण्णे जिणदेसिए** (sāmaṇṇe jiṇadesie) = in śramaṇa-hood as taught by the Jina

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most heart-wrenching verses in the chulika because it places the burning regret in the mouth of the fallen monk himself. The verse is in the first person: "Today, *I* would have been an Acharya, *I* would have been a soul ripened by contemplation, *I* would have been deeply learned." The "if" makes it lethal: *jai haṃ ramaṃto* — "if I had only remained." The fallen monk imagines, in the household, what he might now be. He sees in his mind the great Acharya he could have become; the great learning he could have mastered; the great contemplative ripening he could have attained. Instead, he is here, in a household, with nothing.

The verse is constructed with deliberate cruelty. It does not preach. It simply lets the fallen monk hear his own future regret. The chulika's pastoral wisdom shows itself: it is not telling the wavering monk what others have suffered; it is letting him hear himself, twenty years from now, lamenting the choice he is contemplating today.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"Today I would have been an Acharya — a soul deeply ripened by meditation, deeply learned in the scriptures — *if* I had only stayed in the monastic life that the Jina taught." This is what the fallen monk says to himself, looking back.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Listen to your own voice, twenty years from now, looking back at today. What is it asking you to do?

## CHULIKA-1.11 — The Same Discipline: Heaven for Some, Hell for Others

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

देवलोगसमाणो उ, परियाओ महेसिणं ।
रयाणं अरयाणं तु, महानरयसारिसो ॥१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.11 ||
devaloga-samāṇo u, pariyāo mahesiṇaṃ |
rayāṇaṃ arayāṇaṃ tu, mahā-naraya-sāriso ||10||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the great seers (mahā-ṛṣis) who delight (in it), the monastic state is like the world of the gods (devaloka); but for those who do not delight (in it), it is like the great hell (mahā-naraka).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **देवलोगसमाणो** (devaloga-samāṇo) = like the world of the gods (devaloka)
- **परियाओ** (pariyāo) = the monastic state (paryāya)
- **महेसिणं** (mahesiṇaṃ) = of the great seers (mahā-ṛṣīṇām)
- **रयाणं** (rayāṇaṃ) = of those who delight in it (rata)
- **अरयाणं तु** (arayāṇaṃ tu) = but of those who do not delight in it (a-rata)
- **महानरयसारिसो** (mahā-naraya-sāriso) = like the great hell (mahā-naraka)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse offers a profound psychological observation. The same monastic discipline — the same fasting, the same study, the same alms-rounds, the same celibacy — is *devaloka* (heaven) for one practitioner and *mahā-naraka* (great hell) for another. What makes the difference is not the discipline itself but the inner orientation: *rata* (delight in it) versus *arata* (no delight in it). The monk whose mind has truly fallen in love with the discipline experiences each austerity as joy; the monk whose mind has not — for whom the discipline is mere obligation — experiences the same austerities as torment.

The teaching is both diagnostic and prescriptive. Diagnostically: if your monastic life feels like hell, the problem is not the monastic life — it is your relation to it. Prescriptively: cultivate *rati* (delight). When delight is restored, the same path becomes the path to bliss. The Bhagavati Sutra is paraphrased in the commentary: a monk in his first month already exceeds the bliss of certain lower deities; in his first year, exceeds the bliss of all the heavens including the Anuttara Vimanas. This is not poetic exaggeration; it is a description of the inner state of one who has found delight in the discipline.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For the great sages who genuinely delight in the discipline, the monastic life is like heaven itself. But for those who have lost delight in it — who keep it only as a burden — that very same monastic life feels like the great hell.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If two people walk the same path and one experiences heaven and the other hell, where exactly is the heaven and the hell?

## CHULIKA-1.12 — Therefore, Cultivate Delight in the Path

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अमरोवमं जाणिय सुक्खमुत्तमं, रयाणं परियाए तहा अरयाणं ।
नरओवमं जाणिय दुक्खमुत्तमं, रमिज्ज तम्हा परियाय पंडिए ॥११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.12 ||
amarovamaṃ jāṇiya sukkhamuttamaṃ, rayāṇaṃ pariyāe tahā arayāṇaṃ |
naraovamaṃ jāṇiya dukkhamuttamaṃ, ramijja tamhā pariyāya paṇḍie ||11||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowing (the bliss of those who delight in the discipline to be) the highest joy, like that of the immortals (gods); and (knowing the suffering of) those who do not delight in the discipline to be the highest suffering, like (that of) hell — therefore the wise one (paṇḍita) should delight in the monastic state.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अमरोवमं** (amarovamaṃ) = like that of the immortals (gods)
- **जाणिय** (jāṇiya) = having known
- **सुक्खमुत्तमं** (sukkhamuttamaṃ) = the highest joy
- **रयाणं परियाए** (rayāṇaṃ pariyāe) = of those who delight in the (monastic) state
- **तहा अरयाणं** (tahā arayāṇaṃ) = and of those who do not delight in it
- **नरओवमं** (naraovamaṃ) = like (that of) hell
- **दुक्खमुत्तमं** (dukkhamuttamaṃ) = the highest suffering
- **रमिज्ज** (ramijja) = should delight, should rejoice
- **तम्हा** (tamhā) = therefore
- **परियाय** (pariyāya) = in the monastic state
- **पंडिए** (paṇḍie) = the wise one

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse draws the conclusion of the previous one as direct counsel. Having seen that the same monastic life produces god-like bliss for those who delight in it and hell-like suffering for those who do not, the *paṇḍita* (the wise one) is given the imperative: *ramijja* — "delight in it." The wisdom here is not abstract. The same conditions produce opposite experiences depending on inner attitude. Therefore the practitioner is to cultivate the inner attitude that produces the bliss. The verse uses the word *paṇḍie* — wise — to emphasize that this is not arbitrary will but considered understanding. The wise one, having seen the two outcomes clearly, simply chooses the one that leads to bliss.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Knowing that for those who delight in monastic life it brings the highest joy — like that of the gods — and knowing that for those who do not delight in it, it brings the highest suffering — like that of hell — the wise one should therefore cultivate delight in the monastic state.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If your discipline could be either heaven or hell depending on how you hold it, why would you not choose to hold it as heaven?

## CHULIKA-1.13 — The Fallen Monk Despised Like a Defanged Snake

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धम्माउ भट्ठं सिरिओववेयं, जण्णग्गिविज्झायमिवप्पतेयं ।
हीलंति णं दुव्विहियं कुसीला, दाढुड्ढियं घोरविसं व नागं ॥१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.13 ||
dhammāu bhaṭṭhaṃ siri'ovavveyaṃ, jaṇṇaggi-vijjhāyamivappateyaṃ |
hīlaṃti ṇaṃ duvvihiyaṃ kusīlā, dāḍhuḍḍhiyaṃ ghora-visaṃ va nāgaṃ ||12||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one fallen from dharma, devoid of (spiritual) fortune, dim of radiance like a yajña-fire that has gone out — that misconducted (monk) is despised even by people of bad conduct (kusīla), like a venomous snake whose fangs have been pulled out.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **धम्माउ भट्ठं** (dhammāu bhaṭṭhaṃ) = fallen from dharma (the conduct-dharma)
- **सिरिओववेयं** (siri'ovavveyaṃ) = devoid of (spiritual) prosperity (śrī — the splendour of austerity)
- **जण्णग्गिविज्झायमिव** (jaṇṇaggi-vijjhāyamiva) = like a yajña-fire that has gone out (vidhyāta — extinguished)
- **अप्पतेयं** (appateyaṃ) = of dim radiance (alpa-tejas)
- **हीलंति णं** (hīlaṃti ṇaṃ) = (people) despise him
- **दुव्विहियं** (duvvihiyaṃ) = ill-conducted (durvihita — one who improperly observes the conduct)
- **कुसीला** (kusīlā) = (even) those of bad conduct (kuśīla)
- **दाढुड्ढियं** (dāḍhuḍḍhiyaṃ) = (one whose) fangs have been removed
- **घोरविसं व नागं** (ghora-visaṃ va nāgaṃ) = like a fearfully venomous snake (whose fangs are gone)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Two parallel similes carry this verse. First: the *yajña-agni* — the sacred ritual fire. While it burns, even the wealthy come to feed it ghee and honey, bow before it, regard it with reverence. The moment it is extinguished, all of that ends. The cold ash on the altar is dishonoured by the same hands that worshipped the flame. Second: the *nāga* — the cobra — whose fangs are full of venom. Everyone fears it. But once its fangs are pulled, even children play with it; it is despised, kicked, mocked. This is the picture of the fallen monk. While he held the discipline, he was the burning fire that received the worship of all and the great cobra that even kings approached with care. Once he has fallen — once his fangs (his discipline) are gone, once his fire (his austerity) is extinguished — even the lowly, even the ill-conducted, despise him.

The verse uses the cutting phrase *kusīlā hīlaṃti* — "even kuśīlas (people of bad conduct) despise him." The point: people who themselves have no discipline still look down on a monk who has lost his. The fall is universal in its disgrace.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The one who has fallen from dharma — emptied of spiritual splendour, dim like a yajña-fire that has gone out — even people of bad conduct despise that ill-conducted monk. He is like a venomous cobra whose fangs have been pulled: once feared, now mocked.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The respect that comes from holding a vow disappears the moment the vow is broken. Is the broken vow worth losing all of it?

## CHULIKA-1.14 — Loss of Reputation, Birth in Low Realms

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इहेव धम्मो अयसो अकित्ती, दुण्णामधिज्जं च पिहुज्जणम्मि ।
चुयस्स धम्माउ अहम्मसेविणो, संभिण्णवित्तस्स य हेट्ठओ गई ॥१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.14 ||
iheva adhammo, ayaso akittī, duṇṇāma-dhijjaṃ ca pihujjaṇammi |
cuyassa dhammāu ahamma-seviṇo, saṃbhiṇṇa-vittassa ya heṭṭhao gaī ||13||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the one fallen from dharma, given over to a-dharma, whose conduct is shattered: in this very life — un-righteousness, dis-fame, dis-repute, an evil name among the common people; and afterwards, a low rebirth (heṭṭha-gati).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इहेव** (iheva) = in this very (life / world)
- **अधम्मो** (adhammo) = un-dharma, accumulated negative karma
- **अयसो** (ayaso) = ill-fame
- **अकित्ती** (akittī) = ill-repute, loss of reputation
- **दुण्णामधिज्जं** (duṇṇāma-dhijjaṃ) = an evil name (durnāma)
- **पिहुज्जणम्मि** (pihujjaṇammi) = among the common (folk)
- **चुयस्स धम्माउ** (cuyassa dhammāu) = of the one fallen from dharma
- **अहम्मसेविणो** (ahamma-seviṇo) = (of one) given over to a-dharma
- **संभिण्णवित्तस्स** (saṃbhiṇṇa-vittassa) = (of one) whose conduct (vṛtta) is shattered
- **हेट्ठओ गई** (heṭṭhao gaī) = a downward / low rebirth (heṣṭha-gati)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse counts the costs of the fall in two columns. In *this* life: dis-fame (ayaśa), dis-repute (akīrti), an evil name (durnāma) among the common people. Lay people who pass each other on the street will say, "Did you hear? That one took the great vow and then came back. What a coward — what a hypocrite." His name itself becomes a curse-word. In *the next* life: heṭṭha-gati — a downward rebirth, descent into lower realms (animal, hell, lower human). The accumulated bad karma of returning to violence and greed and untruth and desire — having known better — has fruit. The verse insists on both consequences. The fall is not just internal; it is reputational here, and karmic hereafter.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For the one who has fallen from dharma, given himself over to a-dharma, with his conduct broken: in this very life he gets accumulated bad karma, ill-fame, ill-repute, and an evil name on every common tongue. And after this life — a rebirth in the low realms.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The reputation that is lost in a moment took a lifetime to build. Is the moment worth the lifetime?

## CHULIKA-1.15 — Pleasure Now, Suffering Later, Bodhi Lost

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

भुंजित्तु भोगाइं पसज्झ चेयसा, तहाविहं कट्टु असंजमं बहुं ।
गई च गच्छे अणहिज्झियं दुहं, बोही य से णो सुलहा पुणो पुणो ॥१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.15 ||
bhuṃjittu bhogāiṃ pasajjha cetasā, tahāvihaṃ kaṭṭu asaṃjamaṃ bahuṃ |
gaī ca gacche aṇahijjhiyaṃ duhaṃ, bohī ya se ṇo sulahā puṇo puṇo ||14||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having intensely enjoyed sense-pleasures with mind given over to them, having committed many acts of un-restraint of various kinds — he goes to a destination (gati) that is undesired and full of suffering; and bodhi (right awakening) does not come easily to him, again and again (in many births).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भुंजित्तु भोगाइं** (bhuṃjittu bhogāiṃ) = having enjoyed (sense-)pleasures
- **पसज्झ चेयसा** (pasajjha cetasā) = with mind given over to (with deeply attached attention)
- **तहाविहं कट्टु** (tahāvihaṃ kaṭṭu) = having performed of-such-kind
- **असंजमं बहुं** (asaṃjamaṃ bahuṃ) = much un-restraint (i.e., many sinful actions)
- **गई च गच्छे** (gaī ca gacche) = and goes to a (next) destination
- **अणहिज्झियं** (aṇahijjhiyaṃ) = un-desired, unwished-for
- **दुहं** (duhaṃ) = full of suffering
- **बोही** (bohī) = bodhi (right awakening, the dawning of correct understanding leading to liberation)
- **से** (se) = for him
- **णो सुलहा पुणो पुणो** (ṇo sulahā puṇo puṇo) = does not come easily again and again (in life after life)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse traces the karmic chain. The fallen monk, having returned to enjoy sense-pleasures with full attachment (*pasajjha cetasā*), commits many acts of un-restraint. These acts produce karma. The karma sends him in the next life to an undesired destination — animal birth, lower realms, painful human birth. And — most devastating — *bohī ya se ṇo sulahā puṇo puṇo* — "and bodhi does not come easily to him, again and again." That is, the rare opportunity of right awakening, which he had received in this life and threw away, does not return easily in subsequent lives. He may circle the wheel of samsara for many births before another opportunity arises.

This is the sharpest blade of the chulika. The chance you have *now* — to be a human being, with the Jina's teaching available, with the discipline before you — is itself the result of immense good karma. To throw it away for momentary pleasure is to lose not just this opportunity but to make the next opportunity exceedingly hard to recover.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After enjoying sense-pleasures with all his attention given over to them, and committing many sinful acts of un-restraint, the fallen monk goes to an undesired destination full of suffering. And the rare gift of right awakening — which he once had — does not come easily to him again, even across many lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The opportunity you have right now — how rare is it really? And how long might it take, if lost, to come around again?

## CHULIKA-1.16 — Compared to Hell, This Discipline's Suffering Is Brief

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इमस्स ता णेरइयस्स जंतुणो, दुहोवणीयस्स किलेसवत्तिणो ।
पलिओवमं झिज्जइ सागरोवमं, किमंग पुण मज्झ इमं मणोदुहं ॥१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.16 ||
imassa tā ṇeraiyassa jaṃtuṇo, duhovaṇīyassa kilesa-vattiṇo |
paliovamaṃ jhijjai sāgarovamaṃ, kim aṃga puṇa majjha imaṃ maṇoduhaṃ ||15||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For this hell-being (nāraka-jīva), brought into suffering, full of distress — even the palya-of-time and sāgara-of-time (immense ages) eventually run out. So what indeed (kim aṅga) is this little mental suffering of mine (in the discipline) compared to that?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इमस्स ता** (imassa tā) = of this (hell-being) indeed
- **णेरइयस्स** (ṇeraiyassa) = of the hell-dweller (nāraka)
- **जंतुणो** (jaṃtuṇo) = of the (suffering) creature
- **दुहोवणीयस्स** (duhovaṇīyassa) = brought (forced) into suffering
- **किलेसवत्तिणो** (kilesa-vattiṇo) = whose existence is one of distress (kleśa)
- **पलिओवमं झिज्जइ** (paliovamaṃ jhijjai) = the palyopama (a vast measure of time) ends
- **सागरोवमं** (sāgarovamaṃ) = the sāgaropama (a still vaster measure — many billions of years)
- **किमंग पुण** (kim aṃga puṇa) = how much more, how trifling
- **मज्झ इमं मणोदुहं** (majjha imaṃ maṇoduhaṃ) = this mental suffering of mine

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse and the next are the chulika's direct address to the wavering monk in the moment of his suffering. The argument is mathematical and brutal. Hell-beings (nāraka) in the lower hells suffer for *palyopamas* and *sāgaropamas* — measures of time so vast they are computed by analogy (a palyopama is the time it would take to empty a vast pit of finely-cut hairs at one hair every hundred years; a sāgaropama is countless palyopamas). And yet — *jhijjai* — even those vast measures eventually run out. The torment of hell, however long, is not infinite; it ends.

So, the verse asks: *kim aṅga puṇa majjha imaṃ maṇoduhaṃ* — "how much more so, how trifling, is this mental suffering of mine?" My current discomfort in the discipline is not a palyopama. It is not a sāgaropama. It is at most a few decades of mental restlessness. If the unimaginable suffering of hell ends, surely this brief inconvenience will end. The verse uses the most extreme comparison precisely to bring perspective: your present suffering, however acute it feels, is small.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even the hell-being, dragged into suffering and living a life of utter distress — even his torment, measured in unimaginable ages of palyopamas and sāgaropamas, eventually ends. So how trivial, by comparison, is this little mental discomfort of mine in the discipline?

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When your current suffering feels unbearable, ask honestly: how does it compare to what others bear? And how long, really, will it last?

## CHULIKA-1.17 — My Suffering Will Pass, My Cravings Will Pass, My Body Will Pass

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण मे चिरं दुक्खमिणं भविस्सइ, असासया भोगपिवास जंतुणो ।
ण चे सरीरेण इमेण अविस्सइ, अविस्सइ जीवियपज्जवेण मे ॥१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| Chulika-1.17 ||
ṇa me ciraṃ dukkhamiṇaṃ bhavissai, asāsayā bhoga-pivāsa jaṃtuṇo |
ṇa ce sarīreṇa imeṇa avissai, avissai jīviya-pajjaveṇa me ||16||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

This suffering of mine will not last long. The thirst for pleasures, in any (embodied) creature, is not eternal (it too will end). And if (this suffering) does not depart with this very body, then it will certainly depart at the end of (this) life.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ण मे चिरं** (ṇa me ciraṃ) = (will) not (last) long for me
- **दुक्खमिणं** (dukkhamiṇaṃ) = this suffering
- **भविस्सइ** (bhavissai) = will (not) be / will not last
- **असासया** (asāsayā) = impermanent, non-eternal
- **भोगपिवास** (bhoga-pivāsa) = the thirst for pleasures
- **जंतुणो** (jaṃtuṇo) = of the (embodied) creature
- **ण चे** (ṇa ce) = if not
- **सरीरेण इमेण** (sarīreṇa imeṇa) = with this (very) body
- **अविस्सइ** (avissai) = (will) depart, (will) cease
- **जीवियपज्जवेण मे** (jīviya-pajjaveṇa me) = at the end of my life

### 5. COMMENTARY

The seventeenth verse is the chulika's logical and consoling closing. It instructs the wavering monk in the inner argument he should make to himself in the moment of his discomfort. *Ṇa me ciraṃ dukkhamiṇaṃ bhavissai* — "this suffering of mine will not last long." Karma matures and exhausts; suffering that arises will fade. And — *asāsayā bhoga-pivāsa jaṃtuṇo* — the thirst for sense-pleasures that drives me to abandon the discipline is itself impermanent. It is a fever; it will break. It will not last forever; it lasts only as long as youth, and even within youth, only when fed.

The closing line is the masterstroke: *ṇa ce sarīreṇa imeṇa avissai, avissai jīviya-pajjaveṇa me* — "if (this suffering) does not depart with this body, it will certainly depart at the end of life." Nothing lasts beyond the body. The body itself will end. So whatever suffering, whatever craving, whatever discomfort you are now bearing, it has at most this body's lifetime. The body itself is a water-bubble (verse 16 of the prose sutra). To break a great vow for a temporary craving in a temporary body is to trade the eternal for the momentary. The chulika thus closes with the most reasonable argument it can offer: *wait*. The suffering will pass; the craving will pass; and even if neither passes, both will end with the body — which itself will end.

The chapter ends here with this counsel. The Bije Chulika (next file) takes up a different theme — the conduct of solitary monks.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This suffering of mine will not last long. The thirst for sense-pleasures in any embodied creature is not permanent — it too will pass. And even if my current suffering does not leave with this very body, it will certainly leave at the end of this life. Either way, it will end.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the longest your current suffering can possibly last? And what is the great thing you might trade for it that would last much, much longer?

## END OF PRATHAM CHULIKA

॥ પ્રથમ ચૂલિકા સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Pratham Chulika sampurna — "The First Chulika is complete.")*

The Rativakya — "Words that produce delight in the discipline" — closes here. Its purpose was simple and pastoral: to address the monk who, in the actual living of his vow, has come to a moment of wavering, and to give him eighteen contemplation-points and seventeen verses with which to settle his heart back into the path. The chulika's closing teaching is that all suffering is temporary, all craving is temporary, and the body itself is temporary; therefore the wavering monk should not trade the eternal liberation for a temporary relief.

The traditional final word of the chulika tradition — placed at the very end of the gathas — is the line *chaijja dehaṃ na hu dhamma-sāsaṇaṃ*: "I will give up this body, but I will not give up the teaching of the dharma." That is the seal of the chulika and the orientation it asks every practitioner to make their own.

What follows in the Bije Chulika (Vivikta-Charya) is no longer pastoral counsel for the wavering monk but a portrait of the conduct of the most advanced practitioners — those who have risen to the discipline of solitary forest-dwelling.

Jai Jinendra.
