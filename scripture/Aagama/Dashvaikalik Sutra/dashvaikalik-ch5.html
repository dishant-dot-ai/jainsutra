The Fifth Adhyayan — the Search for Food. The most extensive treatment of monastic alms-conduct in the Jain canon. In two uddeshas: the going-for-alms and the eating-after-alms.

Adhyayan 5: Piṇḍeṣaṇā

About This Adhyayan

The Daily Conduct of Receiving

The fifth adhyayan is the largest single section of the Dashvaikalik — 112 PDF pages devoted entirely to the monastic alms-tour and the meal that follows. Uddesha 1 (Piṇḍeṣaṇā — 'the search for food') walks the monk through every step: when to leave for alms, which paths to take, which households to enter and which to avoid, what food to accept and what to refuse, the famous 42 doshas (faults) — 16 udgama (donor-side), 16 utpādana (monk-side), 10 eṣaṇā (acceptance).

Uddesha 2 covers what happens after the alms are returned to the upashraya: how to eat, how to share with co-monks, how to handle leftovers (parishtāpanika), how to maintain inner detachment from taste even while the body is being nourished. Together the two uddeshas form the practical core of the Jain monastic life: receiving without taking, eating without grasping, sustaining the body without strengthening attachment to it.

5.1.1

When and How to Set Out for Alms

संपत्ते भिक्खकालंमि, असंभंतो अमुच्छिओ । इमेण कमजोगेण, भत्तपाणं गवेसए ॥५.१.१॥

When the time for alms-seeking has arrived, free from agitation and free from grasping attachment, the monk should search for food and drink by the following step-by-step method.

The opening verse fixes the moment from which everything else flows: when the right time has come. Jain monastic tradition teaches that the day of a monk is divided into four parts; the first is for self-study, the second for meditation, the third for going out to seek alms, and the fourth for further study. This third quarter — when householders' meals are naturally finished and food is no longer being cooked — is the proper time. To go before this is to disturb the household; to go after it is to find no food, or to imply impatience. The text quietly names this time without specifying a clock, because in different regions the householders' meal-time will fall at different hours; the monk learns to read the rhythm of the place he is in. But arrival of the time is not enough. The monk must arrive at the time inwardly as well: free from agitation (asambhanto) and free from grasping (amucchio). Agitation here means anxious haste — the nervous monk who fears that other monks will have collected the best food first, or who rushes from house to house without inner composure. Grasping here is more subtle: it is the inward leaning of the senses toward tasty food. The monk who walks with a mental note of which house gives sweets has already broken the vow of non-attachment, even before any food is touched. Both states must fall away. Only the unhurried, unattached walker is fit to investigate the purity of food.

Simply Put: When the proper time for collecting food has come, the monk should set out calmly, without any rush in his mind or any greed for tasty food, and follow the step-by-step rules that are about to be explained.

5.1.2

Walking Calmly Toward the Village

से गामे वा णगरे वा, गोयरग्ग गओ मुणी । चरे मंदमणुव्विग्गो, अव्वक्खित्तेण चेयसा ॥५.१.२॥

That monk, having gone out for alms in a village or city, should walk slowly, free from inner disturbance, with an undistracted, single-pointed mind.

The very word for the monk's alms-walk — gocharya (Prakrit goyara, "cow-tip") — is itself a teaching. The cow grazing in a meadow does not stand in one place stripping a single bush bare; she takes a tuft here, a tuft there, never enough from any one place to deplete it, never showing preference for one patch of grass over another, moving steadily without favour. Just so, the monk passes from house to house, taking only a small amount from each, never enough to inconvenience any one family, treating rich and poor houses alike, harbouring no preference for tasty food over plain. The image was chosen, the commentators note, with great care: it dignifies the act, removes any taint of beggary, and makes the alms-walk into an act of spiritual symmetry. Two qualities are demanded of the walking monk. First, mandam — slowness. The hurried walker cannot watch his step, and the careful watching of every footfall (irya-samiti, the discipline of walking) is itself one of the five cardinal disciplines of Jain monastic life. To walk slowly is not laziness; it is the visible form of inner mindfulness. Second, avvakkhitteṇa ceyasā — with an undistracted mind. The mind, like a calf, easily runs after sights and sounds. The text gives the lovely example of the merchant's calf: the merchant's daughter-in-law was so absorbed in feeding the calf one day that, even though she was dressed in her finest clothes and jewels, the calf paid no attention to her ornaments — its mind was wholly on the food. The monk's mind too should be wholly on the search for faultless food, with no glance to the side at the world's distractions.

Simply Put: When the monk goes out to collect food in a town or city, he should walk slowly, with a peaceful mind, and pay attention only to the food-search — not to anything else around him.

5.1.3

Watching the Path

पुरओ जुगमायाए, पेहमाणो महिं चरे । वज्जंतो बीयहरियाइं, पाणे य दगमट्टियं ॥५.१.३॥

He should walk while looking ahead at the ground a yoke's-length in front, avoiding seeds and green plants, living creatures, and water and damp earth.

This is the practical application of irya-samiti — the discipline of watchful walking. The text fixes the visual range with precision: a yuga — the length of an ox-cart's yoke, roughly four hastas (the distance from elbow-tip to fingertip times four), or about the height of a man. To look much further ahead is to lose sight of the ground at one's feet; to look only at one's feet is to be unable to anticipate. The yoke-length compromise — looking neither too near nor too far — allows the monk to see two-sense and three-sense small beings, to notice green sprouts, damp patches and insects in time to step around them. The four categories named here are no random list. Beeja (seeds) and hariya (sprouting plants) are one-sense plant-bodied beings. Pana (creatures) refers to two-sense beings and above — worms, ants, insects. Daga (water) is water-bodied beings; mattiya (wet earth) contains earth-bodied beings. In a single sentence the verse covers four of the six classes of living beings (the chhajjivanikaya of the previous chapter). The monk who walks watching the ground is therefore actively practising non-violence toward four whole categories of life with every step.

Simply Put: The monk should walk looking carefully at the ground about six feet in front, so he can step around seeds, sprouting plants, small creatures, water, and damp soil — because every one of these contains tiny living beings.

5.1.4

Avoiding Treacherous Paths

ओवायं विसमं खाणुं, विज्जलं परिवज्जए । संकमेण ण गच्छेज्जा, विज्जमाणे परक्कमे ॥५.१.४॥

He should avoid pits, uneven ground, tree-stumps and muddy paths. He should not walk over makeshift bridges (over water or pits) when an alternative way exists.

This verse is principally about the monk's own bodily safety — but its deeper purpose is to protect the discipline of non-violence. If the monk falls or slips on a pit, slippery mud or tree-stump, several harms follow at once: the body may be injured (self-violence), and falling crushes the small creatures and plants beneath (other-violence). A makeshift bridge laid by villagers across a stream or ditch is also unsafe — it may collapse, and crossing it may disturb water-bodied beings. So the rule is conditional: only when no proper path exists may such treacherous routes be used, and then only with extra care. The principle is wider than walking: when two paths exist, the monk always chooses the one that protects life.

Simply Put: The monk should avoid pits, uneven ground, tree-stumps, and muddy spots; and he should not cross temporary planks over water or holes if a normal path is available.

5.1.5

The Cost of a Slip

पवडंते व से तत्थ, पक्खलंते व संजए । हिंसेज्ज पाणभूयाइं, तसे अदुव थावरे ॥५.१.५॥

If that restrained monk falls or slips on such (uneven ground), he will harm living beings — whether mobile or immobile.

This verse states the consequence that justifies the previous one. A single slip — a stumble in a pit, a fall on slippery ground — kills not only one or two creatures but whole categories of them: the visible mobile beings (insects, worms) crushed by the falling body, and the invisible immobile beings (earth-bodied, water-bodied, plant-bodied) crushed beneath it. The monk's vow of non-violence is not a matter of intent only; even an accidental slip created by careless walking destroys life and binds karma. So the rule of avoiding bad ground is not bodily caution but spiritual integrity.

Simply Put: If the monk slips or falls on rough ground, he will harm both the visible little creatures and the invisible tiny lives in earth, water and plants — so even a stumble is a serious matter.

5.1.6

The Better Path Wins

तम्हा तेण ण गच्छेज्जा, संजए सुसमाहिए । सइ अण्णेण मग्गेण, जयमेव परक्कमे ॥५.१.६॥

Therefore, the well-composed restrained monk should not go by such a path; if another path exists, he should walk by it carefully.

This is the practical conclusion of the previous two verses. Where a safer alternative exists, the monk must take it. Only where no alternative exists may the dangerous path be walked, and even then with full mindfulness. The phrase jayameva — "with care alone" — is the sutra's signature word: nothing the monk does is to be done without yatana, the slow, deliberate, watchful awareness that protects every form of life.

Simply Put: So if a better path is available, the careful monk should always take it; he should only use a difficult path when no other choice exists, and even then very mindfully.

5.1.7

Avoiding Heaps That Soil the Foot

इंगालं छारियं रासिं, तुसरासिं च गोमयं । ससरक्खेहिं पाएहिं, संजओ तं णाइक्कमे ॥५.१.७॥

A heap of charcoal, of ash, of chaff, or of cow-dung — the restrained monk should not step on any of these with feet covered in soil-particles.

This is a finely observed rule. While walking, the monk's feet may pick up small particles of living earth (sachitta-raj). If he then steps on a heap of ash, charcoal, chaff, or dung, the moist earth-particles on his foot get pressed onto these heaps — and earth-bodied beings die. The verse therefore tells the monk: do not step on such heaps if your feet are dirty. If the path requires it, he should first carefully wipe his feet with the rajoharana (the soft woollen broom carried by Jain monks). The principle once again: even tiny earth-bodied lives are worth this much care.

Simply Put: If the monk has dust on his feet (which contains tiny earth-creatures), he should not step on heaps of charcoal, ash, chaff, or dung — because pressing the earth-creatures into those heaps would kill them.

5.1.8

Conditions Under Which Not to Go for Alms

ण चरेज्ज वासे वासंते, महियाए व पडंतीए । महावाए व वायंते, तिरिच्छसंपाइमेसु वा ॥५.१.८॥

The monk should not walk for alms when rain is falling, when fog is descending, when a great wind is blowing, or when flying creatures are crossing the path in numbers.

Each of the four conditions named is a circumstance in which an extra category of life would be harmed. Rain-water itself contains countless water-bodied beings; to walk through rain crushes them with every step, and water from the body or robes drips onto fresh creatures and earth. Fog is the same in finer form: its tiny droplets each contain a water-soul. A great wind whips up dust full of earth-bodied beings and forces them into the eyes, mouth, and ears of any walker; it also stirs up air-bodied beings. And when flying insects are dense in the air, walking forces them into the body and crushes them. In all four conditions, the rule is the same: the monk waits. Even hunger does not override the principle. This verse also teaches a deeper rhythm: the monk's life is shaped by the weather of the world, not against it. He moves with the conditions that allow life to flourish; he stays still when movement would cost lives. This responsiveness — this willingness to be inconvenienced for the sake of the smallest beings — is the inner heart of monastic non-violence.

Simply Put: The monk should not go for food when it is raining, foggy, very windy, or when many small flying insects are in the air — because walking through any of these would kill many invisible little beings.

5.1.9

Avoiding the Quarter of the Courtesans

ण चरेज्ज वेससामंते, बंभचेरवसाणुए । बंभयारिस्स दंतस्स, होज्जा तत्थ विसोत्तिया ॥५.१.९॥

The monk dedicated to the practice of celibacy should not walk near the dwellings of courtesans; for even a sense-controlled celibate monk may there encounter disturbance to his vow.

The discipline of the previous verses was about non-violence; the discipline now turns to the second great vow — celibacy (brahmacharya). The text speaks plainly: even the monk who has mastered his senses should not walk in the neighbourhood of courtesans, for the very air of such a place can disturb the inward current of restraint. The Prakrit word visottiya is striking — it means the outward turning of a stream that should be flowing inward. The monk's restraint is like a river flowing inward toward the soul; near such places, the current may be deflected outward. Even the strongest celibate cannot count on his strength here; the rule is to remove oneself from temptation rather than test it.

Simply Put: The monk should not even walk near the area where courtesans live, because even a self-controlled monk may find his vow of celibacy disturbed there.

5.1.10

The Doubts That Arise From Such Visits

अणायतणे चरंतस्स, संसग्गिए अभिक्खणं । होज्ज वयाणं पीला, सामण्णम्मि य संसओ ॥५.१.१०॥

For the monk who repeatedly walks in unsuitable places and keeps such company, there arises pain to his vows and doubt about his very monkhood.

The previous verse warned about a single walk; this one warns about the cumulative effect. Repeated exposure to wrong company gradually wears down the vows — like a soft drip of water that hollows even rock. Worse still, repeated exposure breeds doubt: the monk begins to question whether his life of restraint is worth it. The sutra-author names the danger precisely: not just vrata-pida (pain to the vows), but shramanya-samshaya (doubt about being a monk at all). Many a monk has fallen not by one great failure but by a hundred small associations whose effect was invisible until it was decisive.

Simply Put: A monk who keeps walking near and mixing with the wrong people will gradually find his vows weakening, and may even start to doubt his entire monastic life.

5.1.11

Therefore, Avoid Such Places

तम्हा एवं वियाणित्ता, दोसं दुग्गइवड्ढणं । वज्जए वेससामंतं, मुणी एगंतमस्सिए ॥५.१.११॥

Therefore, knowing this fault that increases (the chances of) bad rebirth, the monk dedicated to solitude should avoid the neighbourhood of courtesans.

This verse closes the small subsection on celibacy with a definite conclusion. The monk who has understood that even occasional walking near such areas weakens the vow and increases the danger of unfortunate rebirth must simply avoid such places altogether. The phrase egaṃtamassie is precise: the monk is one who has chosen ekanta — the lonely path, the path of single-pointed pursuit of liberation. Such a one cannot afford to be casual about what compromises that pursuit.

Simply Put: So once the monk understands how dangerous such walks are for both his vows and his future rebirths, he should completely avoid the courtesan quarter — for he has chosen a single-minded path to liberation.

5.1.12

Avoiding Dangerous Places on the Path

साणं सूइयं गाविं, दित्तं गोणं हयं गयं । संडिब्भं कलहं जुद्धं, दूरओ परिवज्जए ॥५.१.१२॥

The monk should keep at a distance from a (rabid) dog, a newly-calved cow, an enraged ox, a horse, an elephant, a place where children are at play, a quarrel, or a fight.

The text now lists eight specific dangers to avoid on the alms-route. The first five are animals capable of sudden violence: a stray or rabid dog may attack and bite; a newly-calved cow becomes ferociously protective and will charge anyone near her calf; an enraged bull, an excited horse or elephant — animals stronger than a human and unpredictable when agitated — can maim or kill. The sixth is more subtle: children absorbed in cricket or other games may collide with the monk, knock him over, or accidentally injure him. The seventh and eighth are human dangers: a verbal quarrel that the monk's presence may inflame, and a fight in which the monk could be drawn in or hurt as a bystander. The monk avoids all of these not because his life is more precious than another's, but because if he is hurt, killed, or drawn into combat, the discipline itself is lost. Self-protection here is not selfishness; it is preservation of the conditions under which the vow can continue.

Simply Put: The monk should walk well around dogs, newly-calved cows, angry bulls, horses, elephants, places where children play, quarrels, and fights — to protect himself and the discipline.

5.1.13

Inner Composure While Walking

अणुण्णए णावणए, अप्पहिट्ठे अणाउले । इंदियाणि जहाभागं, दमइत्ता मुणी चरे ॥५.१.१३॥

Neither held high nor bowed low, neither over-elated nor agitated, the monk should walk having subdued each of his senses according to its proper sphere.

This verse describes the inner posture that should accompany the outer alms-walk. The body should be neither held proudly upright nor bent in beggarly humility — the monk is neither superior nor inferior; he simply walks straight. The mind should not swing into elation when food is offered easily, nor into agitation when it is refused. Each of the five senses must be held in check: the eyes not roving toward attractive sights, the ears not chasing music or gossip, the nose not pulled by the smell of cooking, the tongue not anticipating taste, the body not seeking comfortable touch. Each sense, the verse says, should be subdued jahabhagam — within its own proper sphere of necessary activity, no more. The commentators note that the two pairs (high/low, elated/agitated) cover both physical bearing and emotional state. Anunnae applies both to the body held proudly aloft and to the inner pride of caste, learning, or austerity. Navanae applies both to the cringing posture and to the inner despondency that comes when one feels unworthy. Both are to be set aside; what remains is the steady, equable walking of one whose interior is balanced.

Simply Put: The monk should walk neither proudly nor too humbly, neither thrilled when things go his way nor upset when they don't, keeping each sense quiet and under control.

5.1.14

Manner of Walking and Bearing

दवदवस्स ण गच्छेज्जा, भासमाणो य गोयरे । हसंतो णाभिगच्छेज्जा, कुलं उच्चावयं सया ॥५.१.१४॥

The monk should not walk hurriedly, nor talking, nor laughing, while going for alms — and (he should go) always to households both high and low.

Three negative rules and one positive rule are combined in a single verse. The monk should not hurry — hurried walking damages the discipline of watching the path and creates the impression of impatience. He should not chat with other monks or with householders along the way — silence preserves the inwardness of the walk and keeps irya-samiti pure. He should not laugh — laughter, especially loud laughter, draws attention, breaks decorum, and undermines the seriousness of the alms-walk. And the positive rule: the monk should go to high and low households alike. He must not show favouritism — not avoid the poor because their food is plain, not seek out the rich because their food is varied. The cow does not graze only the lush patches; the monk too takes from all.

Simply Put: While going for food, the monk should not walk in a hurry, should not chat, should not laugh — and he must visit both rich and poor homes equally without preference.

5.1.15

Where Not to Look on the Way

आलोयं थिग्गलं दारं, संधिं दगभवणाणि य । चरंतो ण विणिज्झाए, संकट्ठाणं विवज्जए ॥५.१.१५॥

While walking, the monk should not stare at windows, holes (in walls), doorways, gaps, or water-houses (bathing rooms); he should avoid all places that may arouse suspicion.

The eyes of the monk reveal his inner state. If he is seen pausing to look at windows, doorways, gaps, or bathing rooms, householders will at once suspect him — they may take him for a thief casing the house, or a man of unclean motives gazing at women bathing or dressing. Either suspicion destroys his character in the eyes of others and harms the reputation of the entire monastic order. The principle is broader than mere appearance: the monk should not be looking at such places at all, because doing so shows that the eye and mind are not yet inward. The verse closes with the wider rule: avoid all places that may arouse suspicion. If even an innocent action could be misunderstood, the wise monk simply removes himself from the situation.

Simply Put: While walking, the monk must not stare at windows, holes in walls, doorways, gaps, or bathrooms — and he should stay away from any place where his behaviour could be misunderstood.

5.1.16

Avoid Royal Treasuries and Hidden Stores

रण्णो गिहवईणं च, रहस्सारक्खियाणि य । संकिलेसकरं ठाणं, दूरओ परिवज्जए ॥५.१.१६॥

The monk should keep at a great distance from the secret guarded places of the king and of wealthy householders — those places that produce mental disturbance.

The monk who is seen walking near or staring at royal treasuries, the storerooms of wealthy merchants, or the guarded places where weapons are kept, immediately becomes an object of suspicion. The guards may seize him on suspicion of espionage; he may be arrested, imprisoned, hauled before a king, or worse. Beyond the bodily danger, even the thought-disturbance produced by being near such treasures (the rising of envy, curiosity, or fear) undermines the monk's equanimity. So the rule: the monk must walk far from such places. The principle is identical to the previous verse — wherever an entanglement may arise, walk around it.

Simply Put: The monk should walk far away from royal treasuries, secret storerooms of wealthy people, or armouries — both because of suspicion that may fall on him and because such places agitate the mind.

5.1.17

Forbidden and Permitted Households

पडिकुट्ठं कुलं ण पविसे, मामगं परिवज्जए । अचियत्तं कुलं ण पविसे, चियत्तं पविसे कुलं ॥५.१.१७॥

The monk should not enter a forbidden household, should avoid one that has refused him; should not enter a household held in contempt — but should enter the household that is well-regarded.

This is the first explicit rule about which households the monk may enter. Three categories are forbidden: (1) Forbidden (paḍikuṭṭha) — households formally excluded by scripture. The Acharanga Sutra (2/1/2) lists twelve such kulas — those engaged in occupations involving violence, those of butcher-castes, brewers, fishermen, and so on. Among the four traditional varnas, the monks generally took alms from Brahmin, Kshatriya, and Vaishya households, avoiding these excluded ones. (2) Refusal-marked (māmaga) — a household where the master has said: "do not come to my house." The monk respects this and never returns there. (Alternatively, māmaga may mean "my own / familiar" house — a relative's house — which the monk also avoids to prevent emotional attachment.) (3) Disliked / disreputable (aciyatta) — homes with very bad reputation: those known for dishonest dealings, immoral conduct, courtesan-houses, or drunkards. To enter such houses harms the monk's own reputation and the order's standing in the community. But the fourth category — ciyatta, the well-regarded — is the one the monk should enter. These are households of good reputation, clean conduct, and proper food (vegetarian, conscientious). Among such, the monk goes from house to house without preference.

Simply Put: The monk should not enter scripturally forbidden homes, homes that have asked him not to come, or disreputable homes — but he should enter the homes of people known for good conduct.

5.1.18

Opening Doors Without Permission

साणी पावारपिहियं, अप्पणा णावपंगुरे । कवाडं णो पणुल्लिज्जा, उग्गहंसि अजाइया ॥५.१.१८॥

A door covered with a sackcloth or curtain — the monk should not lift it himself; he should not push open a closed door without first asking permission.

This verse teaches the social-spiritual courtesy of approach. The household belongs to the householder, and even the smallest action — pushing open a door, lifting a curtain — is an intrusion if done without permission. If the householder is bathing or sleeping, a sudden uncovering by the monk causes embarrassment and may even cause the householder to speak harshly. From that single discourtesy, mistrust of the entire monastic order can grow. So the rule: the monk waits at the door, gives an audible cough or signal, and enters only when invited. The principle is once again the protection of the conditions under which the alms-relationship can continue.

Simply Put: The monk should not push open a closed door or lift a curtain at the entrance of a house himself — he must first ask the householder's permission.

5.1.19

Bodily Needs Before Going

गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य, वच्चमुत्तं ण धारए । ओगासं फासुयं णच्चा, अणुण्णविय वोसिरे ॥५.१.१९॥

The monk who has entered upon the alms-walk should not suppress the urge for stool or urine; finding a faultless place and obtaining permission, he should release them.

A practical and humane rule. If during the alms-walk a natural urge arises, the monk must not suppress it — suppression damages the body and weakens the eyes and life-force (the verse quoted: "by suppressing urine the eyes weaken, by suppressing stool the very life is shortened"). But the release must be in a faultless place — earth that does not contain visible sprouts or insects — and the monk must ask the owner's permission to use it. Even bodily relief is to be done with non-violence and with social respect. Indeed, the verse implies that bodily needs should be attended to before setting out for alms; only if a need arises mid-walk should the monk pause to address it.

Simply Put: If the monk feels the need to relieve himself during the alms-walk, he must not hold it in — but he should find a clean spot, ask the owner's permission, and only then relieve himself.

5.1.20

Avoiding Dark and Low Doorways

णीयदुवारं तमसं, कुट्टगं परिवज्जए । अचक्खुविसओ, जत्थ पाणा दुप्पडिलेहगा ॥५.१.२०॥

The monk should avoid storerooms with low doors and full of darkness, where the eye cannot see and where small creatures cannot be properly inspected.

A dark, low-doored storeroom is dangerous on three counts: the monk cannot see the small creatures that may be on the floor and so cannot keep irya-samiti; he cannot examine the food properly for faults; and a stooping entry through a low door leaves him vulnerable to falls or to suspicions of pilferage. Where vision fails, every layer of the alms-discipline fails. So the monk simply does not enter such places.

Simply Put: The monk should avoid storerooms with low doors and dark interiors, because he cannot see the small creatures or properly check the food.

5.1.21

Avoiding Spaces with Scattered Seeds or Fresh Plaster

जत्थ पुप्फाइं बीयाइं, विप्पइण्णाइं कोट्ठए । अहुणोवलित्तं उल्लं, दट्ठूण परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२१॥

A storeroom where flowers and seeds are scattered, or which is freshly plastered and wet — seeing this, the monk should turn away.

Two kinds of unsafe entry are named. First, a place where flowers (one-sense plant beings still alive) or grains (potential one-sense plant beings) are scattered on the floor — to walk over them crushes them. Second, a place freshly plastered with damp cow-dung — the monk's feet would sink into the soft surface, kill the moisture-loving organisms, and dirty the householder's freshly cleaned space (which would then need re-plastering, killing more plant and earth beings). Where either condition is visible, the monk steps back.

Simply Put: If the monk sees flowers, scattered grains, or a freshly plastered wet floor at a household entrance, he should not enter — he would harm small lives.

5.1.22

Animals or Children Blocking the Doorway

एलगं दारगं साणं, वच्छगं वावि कोट्ठए । उल्लंघिया ण पविसे, विउहित्ताण व संजए ॥५.१.२२॥

If a goat (or ram), a child, a dog, or a calf is in the doorway of the storeroom, the restrained monk should not enter by stepping over them or by pushing them aside.

The doorway may be blocked by a small living being. The monk's options are limited and all the obvious ones are forbidden: he should not step over the creature (which may startle it into harming itself or him — the goat may butt the monk with its horns and break his alms-bowl; the dog may bite; the calf may kick; the child may fall and be injured, leading to his parents' anger toward the monk). Nor should he forcibly push the creature aside (which is itself a small violence, and may injure the child or animal). The implicit instruction: if the doorway is so blocked, simply wait — or move on to the next house. ⚠️ The Prakrit elaga is rendered as "ram/billy-goat" by some commentators and "lamb/kid" by others; either reading preserves the rule.

Simply Put: If a goat, child, dog, or calf is sitting in the doorway, the monk must not step over them or push them aside — he should wait or move on to another house.

5.1.23

How to Look Inside the House

असंसत्तं पलोएज्जा, णाइदूरावलोयए । उप्फुल्लं ण विणिज्झाए, णियट्टिज्ज अयंपिरो ॥५.१.२३॥

The monk should look (for the offering) without attachment, neither gazing too far nor staring with wide eyes; if no food is offered, he should turn back without speaking harshly or pleadingly.

This verse refines the discipline of the eyes at the householder's door. The monk's gaze should be even and impartial — neither lingering on the women of the household, nor on the ornaments and furnishings, nor on the food vessels with greedy interest. He should not stare deep into the house (which suggests prying) nor open his eyes wide in surprise at any rich sight (which suggests envy or amazement). And if the householder offers nothing — or offers only a little, or only stale food, or speaks harshly — the monk simply turns and leaves without a word: no angry retort, no pleading complaint, no plea for more. Equanimity in the face of refusal is itself a major spiritual victory.

Simply Put: The monk should glance into the house calmly, not stare into the distance or with wide eyes, and if no food is given he should leave without saying anything bitter or whining.

5.1.24

Honouring the Boundaries of the Household

अइभूमिं ण गच्छेज्जा, गोयरग्गगओ मुणी । कुलस्स भूमिं जाणित्ता, मियं भूमिं परक्कमे ॥५.१.२४॥

The monk on the alms-walk should not go beyond the permitted area; knowing the boundary of the household, he should walk only within the proper measured space.

Each household, by local custom, has an unspoken boundary up to which guests may proceed. Some homes welcome a visitor only at the doorway; some allow him into the front room; some — generally those of close family of the monastic community — into the kitchen itself. The monk learns these conventions and respects them. To go atibhumi — beyond the boundary — is a breach of trust: it makes the monk look like a trespasser, embarrasses the women of the household, and may be reported as misconduct. The discipline here is again about preserving the trust on which the entire alms-system rests.

Simply Put: The monk should learn how far inside each home he is allowed to go, and never go beyond that boundary uninvited.

5.1.25

Standing in the Right Place, Looking Away From the Wrong

तत्थेव पडिलेहेज्जा, भूमिभागं वियक्खणो । सिणाणस्स य वच्चस्स, संलोगं परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२५॥

There itself, the wise monk should examine the patch of ground (where he will stand); and he should keep out of sight of bathing-rooms and toilets.

When the monk arrives within the household's permitted boundary, he must stand on a clean patch of ground — not on grain spilt, not on living plants, not on insects. He performs pratilekhana (inspection) of the spot first. And he keeps his gaze averted from two specific directions: the bathing room (where someone may be undressed) and the latrine (which is unclean and may also have a person inside). Even an accidental glance toward such places destroys the propriety of the monk's bearing.

Simply Put: The wise monk first checks that the spot where he will stand is clean and free of living beings, and he keeps his gaze well away from any bathroom or toilet door.

5.1.26

Avoiding Paths Where Water and Earth Are Carried

दगमट्टियआयाणे, बीयाणि हरियाणि य । परिवज्जंतो चिट्ठेज्जा, सव्विंदिय समाहिए ॥५.१.२६॥

Avoiding the paths used for fetching water and earth, and (places strewn with) seeds and green plants, the monk should stand still, with all senses well composed.

The path on which servants of the house bring water (which may spill) or fresh wet clay or earth (which contains living beings) is forbidden as a standing place — to stand there is to be in the way of harm to water-bodied or earth-bodied creatures. Likewise, places where seeds, grains, or fresh vegetables have been laid out are forbidden — the monk's standing or walking there crushes plant-bodied beings. He chooses a clean spot, stands still, and keeps every sense composed and inward. The stillness and the inwardness together form the perfect posture of the monk waiting for alms.

Simply Put: The monk should not stand on paths used to bring water or earth, or where grains or vegetables are spread out — he should find a clean spot and stand there with his senses fully calm.

5.1.27

Accept Only What Is Permissible

तत्थ से चिट्ठमाणस्स, आहरे पाणभोयणं । अकप्पियं ण इच्छेज्जा, पडिगाहेज्ज कप्पियं ॥५.१.२७॥

When the monk is standing there (in the proper place) and food and drink are brought, he should not desire what is impermissible (akalpaniya) and should accept only what is permissible (kalpaniya).

This verse opens the section on grahaneshana — the rules of acceptance. The monk has now arrived, has stood properly, and the householder is bringing food and drink. The single rule: distinguish. What is kalpaniya — fit for a Jain monk to accept — is taken; what is akalpaniya — unfit — is refused. The Prakrit kalpa means rule, conduct, ordinance, propriety. Food obtained without violating any of the 42 doshas is kalpaniya; food touched even by one of these faults is akalpaniya. This is the entire test, applied to every offering.

Simply Put: When food and drink are brought to him, the monk should accept only what is faultless according to the rules, and refuse what is faulty.

5.1.28

Refuse Food Brought Spilling

आहरंती सिया तत्थ, परिसाडेज्ज भोयणं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.२८॥

If the woman bringing the food (along the way) spills the food (or drink), the monk should refuse her offer, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

This is the famous chhardita-dosha — the "spilling fault." When the giver brings the food in such a way that drops fall along the path, three harms follow: (1) the spilled food may attract ants and insects, who will be killed when stepped on; (2) the spilled liquid harms the air-bodied beings as it falls; (3) the carelessness shows that the giver herself is not attentive. So the monk refuses politely: "such food does not suit my discipline." The refusal is gentle but firm. There is no scolding, no anger; just the simple statement of the rule.

Simply Put: If the person bringing food is spilling some of it on the way, the monk should politely refuse: "I cannot accept food given in this way."

5.1.29

Refuse If Beings Are Crushed Underfoot

सम्मद्दमाणी पाणाणि, बीयाणि हरियाणि य । असंजमकरिं णच्चा, तारिसं परिवज्जए ॥५.१.२९॥

If the giver, while bringing the food, crushes underfoot living creatures, seeds or green plants, the monk — knowing this to be an unrestrained act — should refuse such food.

This is another donor-side fault. If the woman bringing the food walks over insects, seeds or fresh plants on her way to the monk, those beings are killed because of the monk's request — and accepting the food makes the monk a co-author of that violence. So even though the food itself may be pure, the mode of bringing it has tainted it. Refusal here is itself the highest form of non-violence.

Simply Put: If the person bringing food walks over insects, seeds, or fresh plants, the monk should refuse the food — because accepting it would make him a partner in those small killings.

5.1.30-31

Sahriya, Nikkhitta, and Udaka-Doshas

साहट्टु णिक्खिवित्ताणं, सचित्तं घट्टियाणि य । तहेव समणट्ठाए, उदगं संपणुल्लिया ॥५.१.३०॥ ओगाहइत्ता चलइत्ता, आहरे पाणभोयणं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.३१॥

If, for the sake of the monk, the giver — by combining (mixed-with-living-beings food in one place), by setting it aside (placing other things over living beings), by touching living beings, or by stirring or wading through (water containing living beings) — brings the food and drink, the monk should refuse her offer, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

These two verses together describe four interconnected donor-side faults that all destroy the purity of the food: (1) Sahriya (sahṛta) dosha — combining: pouring milk soaked with living water-beings from one vessel to another, mixing sachitta (living) and achitta (lifeless) substances together, gathering items from many vessels into one to look generous, etc. Wherever the combining itself harms life, the food is unfit. (2) Nikkhitta (nikṣipta) dosha — setting-aside: placing the lifeless food temporarily on top of living things (such as fresh grain or vegetation) before giving; or, having brought the food, dropping any lifeless object onto a living being while serving. (3) Sachittaṃ ghaṭṭitam — touching living beings: while serving, brushing the hand against fresh seeds, vegetables, salt-pieces, or even a fire. The touch alone is enough to taint the food. (4) Udaka-doshas — water faults (verse 31 amplifies): pouring or pushing fresh sachitta water aside; wading into a puddle of fresh rainwater to bring the food; stirring up a pool. All these involve the killing of water-bodied beings for the monk's sake. In each case, the food may be physically pure but spiritually impure — the monk cannot accept what required violence to deliver. The refusal-formula is the same: "such food is not permissible for me."

Simply Put: If the giver, in serving the monk, mixes living things together with the food, sets the food on top of living things, touches living beings, or wades through fresh water — the monk must refuse, because all of this kills tiny lives for his sake.

5.1.32

Refuse If the Hand or Vessel Is Pre-Wet (Purakarma Dosha)

पुरेकम्मेण हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.३२॥

If the giver brings the food with a hand, ladle, or vessel that has just been (washed in fresh water — i.e., affected by the fault of "preceding action"), the monk should refuse, saying: "Such food is not permissible for me."

The purakarma dosha is the "preceding-action fault." If the householder, on seeing the monk arrive, washes her hands or the serving ladle in fresh (sachitta) water before serving — that washing kills water-beings, and the killing is for the monk's sake. So the food carried in such a freshly-wet hand or vessel is unfit. The monk politely refuses. The principle: even pre-action done in preparation for the monk counts as violence done for him.

Simply Put: If the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel has just been freshly washed in water (which would have killed water-beings for the monk's sake), the monk must refuse the food.

5.1.33-34

The Many Forms of "Lipta-Dosha" (Smeared Hand or Vessel)

एवं उदुल्ले ससिणिद्धे, ससरक्खे मट्टिआऊसे । हरियाले हिंगुलए, मणोसिला अंजणे लोणे ॥५.१.३३॥ गेरुय वण्णिय सेढिय, सोरट्ठिय पिट्ठु कुक्कुसकए य । उक्किट्ठमसंसट्ठे, संसट्ठे चेव बोधव्वे ॥५.१.३४॥

In the same way (the monk should refuse food brought by a hand or vessel) that is wet, smeared with moist substance, covered with living dust, smeared with mud or salt-earth, with yellow orpiment, with vermilion, with red arsenic, with collyrium, with salt, with red ochre, with chalk-clay, with white clay, with alum (gopichandan), with flour, with husk-bran — whether thinly smeared or thickly smeared, all these are to be understood as faulty.

These two verses enumerate the many specific substances which, if present on the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel, render the food unfit for acceptance. The list covers: water-soaked or water-greasy hands; hands covered in living dust (earth-bodied beings); mud, salt-earth (which contains earth-beings); various mineral pigments and dyes (orpiment, vermilion, realgar, collyrium, ochre, paint-clays, alum/gopichandan — many of these are used for body decoration, religious marks, or wall painting); salt (which is sachitta in its raw form); freshly ground flour and husk-bran (fresh food-particles still containing plant-life). Whether the substance is thinly present or thickly coating the hand, the food is faulty in either case. This is one of the most detailed lists in the entire chapter, and shows just how meticulously the monk examines what touches the food before he accepts it.

Simply Put: If the giver's hand, ladle, or vessel has any living moisture, dust, mud, salt, mineral paints, dyes, or fresh flour-powder on it — the monk must understand that the food is unfit and should refuse it.

5.1.35

Pashchat-Karma Dosha (Subsequent-Action Fault)

असंसट्ठेण हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा । दिज्जमाणं ण इच्छेज्जा, पच्छाकम्मं जहिं भवे ॥५.१.३५॥

Even if the food is given with a hand, ladle, or vessel that is not smeared (and so seemingly clean), the monk should not accept it if it would lead to a "subsequent-action" fault (the giver washing the hand or vessel afterwards, killing water-beings).

Here is the mirror of the purakarma fault (verse 32). Even if the hand and vessel are clean now, the monk must refuse if he knows that the giver will, after serving him, wash that hand or vessel in fresh sachitta water — killing water-beings on his behalf. The monk's responsibility extends both before the giving (purakarma) and after (pashchatkarma). The Antagada Sutra cites the example of Queen Devaki who, after offering modaks, deliberately left the plate as it was (un-washed) precisely to spare the monks this fault. The teaching for the householder is also quiet but profound: serve the monk in such a way that the vessel can simply be set aside or used for the family's own meal, not specially cleaned for the monk's sake.

Simply Put: Even if the giver's hand or ladle looks clean, the monk should refuse the food if he knows the giver will wash that hand or ladle in fresh water afterwards — because that washing would kill water-beings for the monk's sake.

5.1.36

When the Hand Is Already Smeared With Acceptable Food

संसट्ठेण य हत्थेण, दव्वीए भायणेण वा । दिज्जमाणं पडिच्छिज्जा, जं तत्थेसणियं भवे ॥५.१.३६॥

If, on the other hand, food is given by a hand, ladle, or vessel that is smeared with already-permissible (lifeless) food-substance — the monk should accept it, provided the food itself is faultless.

This is the constructive complement of the previous verses. If the giver's hand or vessel is already coated with the same kind of (lifeless, permissible) food she is now serving — for example, her hand has rice on it because she just served her own family — the monk should accept. Why? Because here there is no purakarma fault (no fresh washing was done before) and no pashchatkarma fault (the householder will not need to wash her hand in fresh water afterwards — the smear is the same food, and she will simply continue eating or the smear will dry naturally). The whole logic of the previous verses is preserved. This principle expresses a beautiful balance in the alms-tradition: the monk accepts food that is naturally given as part of the household's normal life, and refuses food that requires the household to do anything special — anything violent — for him.

Simply Put: If the giver's hand or vessel is already smeared with the same kind of permissible food (because she was just serving her family), the monk should accept — there's no extra harm done for his sake.

5.1.37

When Two Are Eating, One Invites

दुण्हं तु भुंजमाणाणं, एगो तत्थ णिमंतए । दिज्जमाणं ण इच्छेज्जा, छंदं से पडिलेहए ॥५.१.३७॥

When two persons are eating together (or co-own the food), and one of them invites the monk to take some — the monk should not (immediately) accept what is being offered, but should first try to read the intent (and consent) of the other.

This is the famous anisrishta dosha — the "without-co-owner's-consent" fault. When food is jointly owned (two brothers running a shop together; spouses sharing a household; partners in a business), the consent of all owners is required for the gift to be valid. If one offers but the other is silent or visibly reluctant, accepting the food violates the second owner's third great vow (non-stealing) by proxy — the monk would be receiving what was not freely given by all rightful owners. So the monk first observes the second person's facial expression, body language, eye-contact — to read whether there is genuine consent or hidden displeasure. Only if both clearly consent should the monk accept.

Simply Put: If two people share the food and only one invites the monk, the monk should pause and read the other person's expression — only if both seem willing should he accept.

5.1.38

When Both Invite

दुण्हं तु भुंजमाणाणं, दोवि तत्थ णिमंतए । दिज्जमाणं पडिच्छिज्जा, जं तत्थेसणियं भवे ॥५.१.३८॥

When two persons are eating (or jointly own the food), and both invite the monk — the monk should accept what is being offered, provided the food itself is faultless.

The constructive complement of the previous rule. When both joint owners freely consent and explicitly offer — the consent is complete, the third great vow is preserved, and the monk may accept (as long as the food itself meets all the other tests of faultlessness). This pair of verses (37-38) together teaches a deep principle about gifts: a gift requires the willing consent of all who own what is being given.

Simply Put: When two people own the food and both willingly invite the monk, he should accept — provided the food itself is faultless.

5.1.39

Food Prepared for a Pregnant Woman

गुव्विणीए उवण्णत्थं, विविहं पाणभोयणं । भुंजमाणं विवज्जेज्जा, भुत्तसेसं पडिच्छए ॥५.१.३९॥

The various foods and drinks that have been specially prepared for a pregnant woman (to satisfy her cravings) — the monk should refuse them while she is still eating; he may accept what remains after she has finished and there is genuine surplus.

If a special meal has been prepared to satisfy the cravings (dohad) of a pregnant woman, the monk must not take from it before she eats — to do so could leave her unsatisfied, harm both mother and unborn child, and constitute a subtle violation of the great vow of non-violence. But if she has eaten her fill and food genuinely remains as surplus, the monk may accept that remainder. The principle: the monk's hunger is never to come at the cost of another's nourishment, especially not the nourishment of a vulnerable life.

Simply Put: Special food made for a pregnant woman should not be taken by the monk while she is still eating; only what is genuinely left over after her meal may be accepted.

5.1.40-41

Food Given by a Pregnant Woman in Late Stages

सिया य समणट्ठाए, गुव्विणी कालमासिणी । उट्ठिया वा णिसीइज्जा, णिसण्णा वा पुणुट्ठए ॥५.१.४०॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.४१॥

If, for the sake of the monk, a pregnant woman in her ninth month — having been standing — sits down to give, or, having been seated, stands up to give: such food is impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse the giver, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

The compassion here is precise. A woman in the ninth month of pregnancy is heavy, fragile, and any sudden movement — standing up after sitting, sitting down after standing — risks injury both to herself and to the unborn child. If she makes such a movement for the monk's sake (to get up and serve, or to settle and serve), the monk shares in that risk and that violence. So the rule: he refuses food given by such a woman in the act of changing posture. He may accept only if she serves him from the same posture she was already in. The Sthavira-kalpa tradition is even more cautious: from the seventh month onward, the monk avoids taking food specifically from pregnant women altogether; the Jina-kalpa tradition begins this caution from the fifth month. The principle that runs through all these gradations is the same: the monk's nourishment must never become the smallest source of distress to a vulnerable being.

Simply Put: If a woman in her last month of pregnancy gets up or sits down specifically to serve the monk, he must refuse — because the movement itself could harm her and the baby.

5.1.42-43

Food Given by a Mother Nursing a Child

थणगं पिज्जमाणी, दारगं वा कुमारियं । तं णिक्खिवित्तु रोयंतं, आहरे पाणभोयणं ॥५.१.४२॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.४३॥

If a woman who is breastfeeding a baby boy or girl sets the crying child down (on the bare ground) and brings food and drink — that food is impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse the giver, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

The same principle of compassion is now applied to the nursing mother. If she puts down the baby — interrupting the feeding, perhaps onto a hard surface — and the baby cries from hunger or fear, then the food brought to the monk has come at the cost of the baby's distress and potentially harm. The monk's first great vow (non-violence) extends even to that smallest cry. So he refuses politely. The text gives gentle conditions under which the monk may accept: (1) if the mother gives leftover food after nursing is complete; (2) if she serves while remaining seated and the baby is content; (3) if the baby is already accepting other food besides milk, will not cry on being put down, and the mother gives without disturbing the child.

Simply Put: If a mother sets down a hungry, crying baby just to serve the monk, he must refuse the food — the baby's distress is too high a price.

5.1.44

Refuse Food About Which You Are in Doubt

जं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, कप्पाकप्पंमि संकियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.४४॥

Whatever food and drink may be doubtful — uncertain whether permissible or not — the monk should refuse the giver, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

This is the famous shankita dosha — the "doubt fault." If after careful inspection and inquiry the monk still cannot determine whether the food is permissible or not — for example, he is uncertain whether the salt was raw (sachitta) or treated; whether the water touching the food was fresh or boiled; whether the giver's hair fell into the food; whether the food contains any tiny living creatures, etc. — then doubt itself is the deciding factor. Where doubt remains, refusal is the rule. The principle is profound. In the spiritual discipline of non-violence, the burden of proof is on the food, not on the monk. The food must be known faultless before it is accepted. If even after the monk's inquiry the doubt remains, he must refuse — for to accept under doubt is itself a fault. The fault is not in the food but in the act of accepting under uncertainty.

Simply Put: If after looking and asking, the monk is still not sure whether the food is faultless or faulty, he must simply refuse it — because doubt itself is enough reason to say no.

5.1.45-46

Pihita and Udbhinna Doshas (Covered Vessels)

दगवारेण पिहियं, णिसाए पीढएण वा । लोढेण वावि लेवेण, सिलेसेण व केणइ ॥५.१.४५॥ तं च उब्भिंदिया देज्जा, समणट्ठाए व दावए । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.४६॥

A vessel that is covered — by a water-pot, by a grinding stone, by a stool, by a heavy stone slab, by a clay-paste seal, or by any other lac-seal: if (the giver) breaks open such a seal for the monk's sake or arranges for someone else to do so, the monk should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

This is the udbhinna dosha — the "breaking-open fault" — combined with the pihita dosha ("covered fault") which is the 12th of the 16 udgama doshas. If a vessel of food is sealed shut by a heavy water-pot, a grindstone, a stool, a millstone, a clay seal, or a wax/lac seal, and the householder breaks the seal specifically to serve the monk, several violences result: lifting heavy stones risks crushing earth-bodied beings beneath; breaking clay or lac seals kills earth-, water-, fire- (in the case of warm wax), or plant-bodied beings; the act of unsealing for the monk's sake itself constitutes a special preparation that violates the principle of "no extra effort for the monk." So the monk refuses. If, however, the cover comes off easily (no heavy lifting needed) and the seal involves no living substance — the monk may accept. The rule is again about preventing violence done expressly for the monk.

Simply Put: If the food is in a vessel sealed shut by heavy stones or clay or wax, and the giver breaks the seal just to feed the monk, he must refuse — because the breaking itself causes harm.

5.1.47-48

Refusing Food Made Specifically for Charity (Danartha)

असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । जं जाणेज्ज सुणेज्जा वा, दाणट्ठा पगडं इमं ॥५.१.४७॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.४८॥

If the monk knows or hears that food, drink, chewables, or flavour-fresheners have been prepared specifically for the purpose of giving away as charity — such food and drink are impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

This verse names a profound principle. Food prepared specifically for charitable distribution — as part of a public almsgiving event, a charitable feeding of the poor, or any kind of organised giving — is not for the Jain monk. Why? Because such food has been prepared for those who need it: the genuinely hungry, the destitute, the needy who depend on charity to eat. If the monk takes from such a stock, he diminishes what is left for those who truly need it. Worse, the food was prepared with the intention of giving — and the act of preparation itself involves violence (cooking, gathering, etc.) done for the recipients, which the monk must not co-author. The Jain monk's begging tradition is unique in this respect: it is bhiksha-vritti — going from house to house and taking only the smallest portion of the household's normal food. It is not charity-feeding. The two are spiritually different acts: the monk shares the family's meal in tiny measure; the charitable giver feeds the destitute. The monk does not draw from the second.

Simply Put: If food has been specially made for handing out as charity to the needy, the monk should refuse it — that food belongs to those who need it more.

5.1.49-54

Refusing Food Made for Merit, Beggars, or Other Renunciants

असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । जं जाणेज्ज सुणेज्जा वा, पुण्णट्ठा पगडं इमं ॥५.१.४९॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.५०॥ असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । जं जाणेज्ज सुणेज्जा वा, वणिमट्ठा पगडं इमं ॥५.१.५१॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.५२॥ असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । जं जाणेज्ज सुणेज्जा वा, समणट्ठा पगडं इमं ॥५.१.५३॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.५४॥

(49-50) If the monk knows or hears that food, drink, chewables or flavour-fresheners have been prepared for the purpose of earning religious merit (e.g., for ritual feeding of Brahmins, shraddha rites, etc.) — such food is impermissible; he should refuse. (51-52) If prepared for ordinary beggars and supplicants — such food is impermissible; he should refuse. (53-54) If prepared for non-Jain renunciants (samana — wandering ascetics of other traditions) — such food is impermissible; he should refuse.

These three pairs of verses extend the principle of 5.1.47-48 to three additional categories of "prepared-for-someone-else" food. The Uddesha names four situations in total in which prepared-for-charity food is forbidden: danartha (prepared for general charity, 47-48), punyartha (prepared for religious merit-making, 49-50), vanima-artha (prepared for ordinary beggars, 51-52), and samana-artha (prepared for other renunciants, 53-54). The underlying logic in all four cases is identical: the food was prepared with a particular recipient in mind. To take from it diminishes what reaches that intended recipient (causing antaraya — the obstruction of another's nourishment, a serious karmic fault). And the cooking-violence associated with it was undertaken on behalf of someone else; for the monk to share in that food is to share in violence not done for him but which still benefits him — a subtle form of complicity. The fourth case (samana-artha) is especially worth noting: the Jain monk does not even take from food prepared for fellow-renunciants of other traditions. Each renunciant tradition keeps its own discipline; the Jain monk does not "borrow" charity meant for others. This rigorous independence is part of what makes the Jain alms-tradition unique.

Simply Put: If food was made specifically for religious merit, for ordinary beggars, or for non-Jain monks and ascetics — the Jain monk must refuse it, because that food belongs to those for whom it was prepared.

5.1.55

The Seven Forbidden Categories Summarised

उद्देसियं कीयगडं, पूइकम्मं च आहडं । अज्झोयर पामिच्चं, मीसजायं विवज्जए ॥५.१.५५॥

The monk should avoid food that is uddeshika (prepared with the monk in mind), kritakrita (purchased for the monk), putikarma (mixed with even a particle of adhakarmika food), ahrita (specially fetched from elsewhere), adhyavatara (made in extra quantity for the monk), pramitya (borrowed for the monk), and mishrajata (made jointly for the household and the monk).

This single verse compresses seven of the sixteen famous udgama doshas (donor-side faults) into one summary. Each of the seven names a specific way in which the food has been prepared with the monk in mind, even subtly: (1) Uddeshika — the family cooks something extra "in case a monk comes." This is a special intent. (2) Kritakrita — the family purchases something specifically to offer the monk. (3) Putikarma — the food itself is pure, but a single spoonful of "adhakarmika" (food cooked specifically for monks) was mixed in. Like a drop of impurity in clean water, the whole becomes impure. (4) Ahrita — the food is carried from one's own home to the upashraya (monk's lodging) or another village specifically to give. (5) Adhyavatara — while cooking for the family, more rice or vegetables are added to the pot once a monk is sighted, "so there will be enough." (6) Pramitya — the family borrows a bowl of ghee or lentils from a neighbour just to be able to offer the monk something nice. (7) Mishrajata — from the very beginning, the family cooked both for themselves and for the monks they expected. In every case, the violence of cooking, fetching, purchasing, or borrowing was undertaken at least partly for the monk. The monk's discipline of eating only what is "naturally" available (as if grazing like the cow on the meadow) is broken. So all seven are forbidden.

Simply Put: The monk must refuse food that was made or bought or borrowed or fetched or added or mixed with him in mind — he should only take what the family was making for themselves.

5.1.56

Inquiring About Origin When in Doubt

उग्गमं से य पुच्छेज्जा, कस्सट्ठा केण वा कडं । सोच्चा णिस्संकियं सुद्धं, पडिगाहेज्ज संजए ॥५.१.५६॥

The restrained monk should inquire about the origin (of the food), asking: "For whose sake was this prepared?" and "By whom was it made?" — and having heard the answer, only if it is doubt-free and pure should he accept.

This verse gives the monk's investigative method when the seven faults of the previous verse are suspected. He may ask the giver two simple questions: "For whose sake was this food prepared?" and "Who prepared it?" The answers reveal whether any of the seven udgama doshas applies. If the answer indicates that the food was prepared as part of the family's normal meal, by family members, with no special intent toward the monk — the monk may accept. If any doubt remains after inquiry, the previous rule (5.1.44, shankita-dosha) applies: refuse. The monk does not interrogate aggressively or in a way that embarrasses the householder. The inquiry is brief, gentle, and aimed solely at protecting his own discipline — never at testing the giver.

Simply Put: If in doubt, the monk should simply ask: "Who is this food made for?" and "Who made it?" — and accept only if the answers leave no doubt that the food is faultless.

5.1.57-58

Food Mixed with Flowers, Seeds, or Fresh Plants

असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । पुप्फेसु हुज्ज उम्मीसं, बीएसु हरिएसु वा ॥५.१.५७॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.५८॥

If food, drink, chewables, or flavour-fresheners are mixed with (live) flowers, with seeds, or with fresh green plants — such food is impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

If the food contains living substances mixed in — fresh flowers used as garnish (which are still alive), whole grains (which contain sleeping plant-life), or fresh herbs and shoots — then eating it would mean killing those living beings inside the food. So the monk refuses. The principle: food must be entirely lifeless (achitta) at the moment of acceptance. Even the smallest living substance mixed in renders it impermissible.

Simply Put: If the food has fresh flowers, live seeds, or green herbs mixed in, the monk must refuse — eating it would kill those living things.

5.1.59-60

Food Set on Fresh Water or Insect-Holes

असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । उदगम्मि हुज्ज णिक्खित्तं, उत्तिंग पणगेसु वा ॥५.१.५९॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.६०॥

If food, drink, chewables, or flavour-fresheners have been set down upon (sachitta) water, or upon insect-burrows or upon fresh moss/mould — such food is impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

This is another nikshipta dosha — the "set-down fault" — applied to specific living substrates. If the giver has temporarily placed the food vessel on top of fresh water, or on top of an insect-burrow (where many small creatures live), or on a patch of moss or mould (one-sense plant-bodied beings), then the food has come into direct contact with those living substances, the substrate has been crushed by the weight of the vessel, and the food is impermissible. The monk politely refuses.

Simply Put: If the food has been placed on fresh water, on an insect-hole, or on a patch of moss or mould, the monk must refuse — these are all living things being harmed.

5.1.61-62

Food on Fire or Touching Fire

असणं पाणगं वावि, खाइमं साइमं तहा । तेउम्मि हुज्ज णिक्खित्तं, तं च संघट्टिया दए ॥५.१.६१॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.६२॥

If food, drink, chewables, or flavour-fresheners have been placed upon fire, or are given after touching the fire — such food is impermissible for the restrained monk; he should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

Fire itself is a living being (tejaskaya — fire-bodied). If the food vessel rests on a fire (typical of cooking pots over a stove), or if the giver brings food that has just brushed against the fire, the contact has destroyed many fire-beings for the monk's sake. So the food is impermissible.

Simply Put: If the food has been on the fire, or the giver's vessel has just touched fire, the monk must refuse — fire itself is a form of life that has been disturbed for him.

5.1.63-64

The Nine "Fire-Touching" Doshas

एवं उस्सक्किया ओसक्किया, उज्जालिया पज्जालिया णिव्वाविया । उस्सिंचिया णिस्सिंचिया, उववत्तिया ओवारिया दए ॥५.१.६३॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.६४॥

In the same way (the food is impermissible if), the giver gives after — moving fuel toward the fire, withdrawing fuel from the fire, lighting the fire, fanning the fire to greater flame, extinguishing the fire, ladling out (food) from the cooking vessel, sprinkling water (on the food), tilting the vessel to one side to pour, or removing the vessel from the fire altogether.

These nine acts — all of which are typical kitchen actions performed when serving — each cause violence to the fire-bodied beings, and several also cause violence to water- and earth-beings. If any of them is performed specifically for the monk (to keep food warm, to make it ready to serve, to pour it out, etc.), the food becomes impermissible. The principle: the monk's discipline of taking only what would have happened anyway in the household's normal routine is violated whenever such "extra" actions are performed for his sake.

Simply Put: If the giver, just to serve the monk, adds fuel, removes fuel, lights or blows on the fire, puts out the fire, ladles, sprinkles, tilts, or removes the cooking pot — the monk must refuse, because all of this disturbs fire-beings for his sake.

5.1.65-66

Avoiding Unstable Walking Surfaces

हुज्ज कट्ठं सिलं वावि, इट्टालं वावि एगया । ठवियं संकमट्ठाए, तं च होज्ज चलाचलं ॥५.१.६५॥ ण तेण भिक्खू गच्छेज्जा, दिट्ठो तत्थ असंजमो । गंभीरं झुसिरं चेव, सर्विंदिय समाहिए ॥५.१.६६॥

If sometimes a piece of wood, a stone slab, or a brick has been placed (across a puddle or pit) as a passing-bridge, and it is wobbly and unstable — a monk in command of all his senses should not walk over it; for there is visible danger of unrestraint, and the place beneath it may be deep and hollow.

This returns to the rules of walking — specifically the situation of crossing puddles or pits during the monsoon season when villagers lay temporary planks, stones, or bricks across them. If the makeshift bridge wobbles, the monk slips: he falls, his bones may break, the householders' regard for the monk-tradition decreases, and water-beings or other living beings beneath get crushed. So even when it is the only available path, the monk should not use it if it is unstable — better to wait or take a longer route.

Simply Put: If a temporary plank, stone, or brick has been placed across a puddle and it wobbles, the monk should not walk on it — both his safety and the lives beneath are at risk.

5.1.67-69

Refusing Food from Climbed Heights (Malapahrita Dosha)

णिस्सेणिं फलगं पीढं, उस्सवित्ताणमारुहे । मंचं कीलं च पासायं, समणट्ठाए व दावए ॥५.१.६७॥ दुरूहमाणी पवडेज्जा, हत्थं पायं व लूसए । पुढविजीवे वि हिंसेज्जा, जे य तण्णिस्सिया जगे ॥५.१.६८॥ एयारिसे महादोसे, जाणिऊण महेसिणो । तम्हा मालोहडं भिक्खं, ण पडिगिण्हंति संजया ॥५.१.६९॥

If — for the monk's sake — the giver climbs a ladder, plank, stool, raised platform, post, or upper storey, and brings down (food from a height): she may fall while climbing, injure her hand or foot, kill earth-bodied beings (where she falls), and harm whatever creatures live in connection with that earth. Knowing such great faults, the great seers — the restrained monks — do not accept food brought from a height (mala-apahrita-dosha).

This is the famous mala-apahrita dosha — the "fetched-from-storage-above" fault. If the giver must climb a ladder, plank, stool, post, or mount to the top floor of the house to fetch food kept up there, several harms become possible: she may fall and break a bone; her own life may be endangered; she may crush insects or earth-beings on her landing or in her route; the entire faith in monks may be damaged if she is hurt while serving one. The great seers have thought of all this, and so refuse such food. There are three kinds of mala-apahrita: urdhva (fetched from above by climbing), adho (fetched from a basement / underground store by descending), tiryak (fetched with difficulty from a tall vessel or hard-to-reach side place). All three are forbidden. But where the household has ordinary stable stairs and the giver has no risk in fetching, the food may be accepted — the rule applies specifically to risky climbs.

Simply Put: If the giver has to climb a ladder, plank, post, or upper floor to fetch food just for the monk, he must refuse — because she might fall and hurt herself or others.

5.1.70

Refusing Living Plant Substances (Sachitta Vegetation)

कंदं मूलं पलंबं वा, आमं छिण्णं व सण्णिरं । तुंबागं सिंगबेरं च, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.१.७०॥

The monk should refuse: tubers, roots, fruits/seeds — whether raw, sliced, or moistened (still alive); gourds and ginger and the like, in any unprocessed (sachitta) state.

A foundational rule: the monk does not take sachitta vegetation — plants still containing their living plant-soul. Tubers, roots, seeds, gourds, ginger, fruits — any of these in a raw state still hold one or more plant-bodied beings. Even slicing them does not kill the soul; only application of "shastra-parinata" (an instrument that destroys life — fire, salt, drying, prolonged separation, etc.) renders them lifeless and therefore acceptable. This single verse contains the entire Jain restriction on tubers (kanda-mool) — the famous prohibition against root vegetables that distinguishes Jain dietary practice. The deepest reason: a single tuber may contain infinite (anantakaya) souls, while a fruit contains only one or a limited number. So the karmic weight of consuming a tuber is exponentially greater.

Simply Put: The monk must refuse raw tubers, roots, fruits, seeds, gourds, and ginger — anything still alive that has not been fully processed by fire or other means.

5.1.71-72

Refusing Food Sold in Open Shops

तहेव सत्तुचुण्णाइं, कोलचुण्णाइं आवणे । सक्कुलिं फाणियं पूयं, अण्णं वावि तहाविहं ॥५.१.७१॥ विक्कायमाणं पसढं, रएण परिफासियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.७२॥

In the same way: barley-flour, jujube-powder displayed in shops; sesame cakes, jaggery-paste, cooked sweets, and other such goods — if displayed openly for sale and (therefore) covered with road-dust, the monk should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

The monk does not normally take food from a shopkeeper at his sale-counter. Why? Because the food displayed openly for sale collects road-dust (full of earth-beings, and on which insects crawl), and customers' constant handling makes it neither pure nor traceable in origin. Furthermore, accepting from a shopkeeper risks lowering the monk's reputation — he may appear to be diverting goods meant for paying customers. The principle of begging from family kitchens, not market-stalls, is preserved.

Simply Put: The monk must refuse food displayed openly in shops for sale — it is dust-covered and not the same as fresh family cooking.

5.1.73-74

Refusing Food with Too Much Discardable Material

बहुअट्ठियं पुग्गलं अणिमिसं वा बहुकंटयं । अत्थियं तिंदुयं बिल्लं उच्छुखंडं व सिंबलिं ॥५.१.७३॥ अप्पे सिया भोयणजाए, बहुउज्झियधम्मिए । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.७४॥

(Such items as): meat full of bones, pineapple full of thorns, the bony fruit of the tinduka tree, the bel-fruit, sugarcane pieces, the flowers/seeds of the silk-cotton tree — if the edible portion is little and the discardable portion is much, the monk should refuse, saying: "Such (food) is not permissible for me."

This is the bahu-ujjhita-dharma dosha — the "much-must-be-discarded fault." If the proportion of inedible material (bones, thorns, pits, husks) is high relative to the edible portion, the monk refuses for two reasons: (1) the discarded material has to be thrown somewhere, where insects will gather and where the monk's discarding itself causes harm; (2) the discarded material may attract crawling beings. So the food is not "clean" food.

Simply Put: If a food item has very little to eat and a lot to throw away (like bel-fruit, sugarcane bits with much fibre, fruits with big pits), the monk should refuse — because the discards cause more harm.

5.1.75

Examining Wash-Water (Dhovan)

तहेवुच्चावयं पाणं, अदुवा वारधोयणं । संसेइमं चाउलोदगं, अहुणाधोयं विवज्जए ॥५.१.७५॥

In the same way, water (offered in various qualities) — including water used to wash a vessel, water used to wash boiled vegetables, water used to wash uncooked rice — if just freshly washed (still containing living water-beings), the monk should refuse it.

Jain monks traditionally drink only "dhovan" — the water used for washing utensils or vegetables in the household. The reason is subtle: such water is no longer fresh sachitta water (since it has been used and "spent") but it is still potable. However — and this is the key — if it has been just now poured (still freshly drawn from the tap or well), it contains living water-beings and is impermissible. Only after some time has passed (so that, by traditional understanding, the water-beings have died and the water is achitta) may it be accepted. The next two verses elaborate.

Simply Put: The monk should refuse water that has just been freshly drawn — even if it's only wash-water — because it still contains living water-beings.

5.1.76

Inquire About How Long Ago the Water Was Drawn

जं जाणेज्ज चिराधोयं, मईए दंसणेण वा । पडिपुच्छिऊण सुच्चा वा, जं च णिस्संकियं भवे ॥५.१.७६॥

If the monk knows by his own discernment, by direct observation, or by inquiring of the householder and hearing the answer, that the water has been drawn long ago — and his mind is fully free of doubt — then (he may accept it).

The monk's three means of verification: his own judgment (does the water look fresh or has it been sitting?); his own direct observation (did he see it being drawn?); and inquiring of the householder. If after all three the monk is certain the water was drawn long enough ago to be lifeless, he may accept it.

Simply Put: The monk should determine — by judgment, by looking, or by asking — whether the water was drawn long ago, and accept it only if he is fully sure.

5.1.77

Knowing It Is Lifeless, Accept; If In Doubt, Taste

अजीवं परिणयं णच्चा, पडिगाहेज्ज संजए । अह संकियं भवेज्जा, आसाइत्ताण रोयए ॥५.१.७७॥

Knowing the water to have become lifeless, the restrained monk may accept it; but if doubt arises, he should taste a little and decide.

If the monk is certain the water is now lifeless, he accepts. If doubt remains even after observation and inquiry, he is permitted a taste-test: living water has its own taste; spent dhovan has another. By tasting a small amount, he can determine whether the water is fit for the body (drinkable, thirst-quenching) and free of lingering doubt.

Simply Put: If the monk is sure the water is now lifeless, he may accept it; if uncertain, he is allowed to taste a little to check.

5.1.78-79

Asking for a Taste, and Refusing What Is Sour or Bitter

थोवमासायणट्ठाए, हत्थगम्मि दलाहि मे । मा मे अच्चंबिलं पूयं, णालं तिण्हं विणित्तए ॥५.१.७८॥ तं च अच्चंबिलं पूयं, णालं तिण्हं विणित्तए । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.१.७९॥

(The monk says:) "Pour a little into my hand for tasting, so that I may judge — lest the water be too sour or too foul (in which case) it cannot quench my thirst." If, on tasting, the water is found to be too sour or foul such that it cannot quench thirst, the monk should refuse the giver, saying: "Such (water) is not permissible for me."

These two verses give the practical procedure for the taste-test of verse 77. The monk politely says: "Please pour a little into my hand so I may taste — I want to know whether this water can quench my thirst." If on tasting the water is found to be excessively sour, fermented, foul, or bitter (such water existed in many traditional forms — fermented rice-wash, vegetable-wash with souring, etc.), the monk refuses. Why? Because such water cannot serve its purpose (quenching thirst), so accepting it serves no genuine bodily need; and the monk does not accept what serves no purpose.

Simply Put: The monk asks: "Pour a little in my hand to taste — I need to know if this will actually quench my thirst." If it tastes too sour or foul to drink, he politely refuses.

5.1.80

Mistakenly Accepted, Yet Not Drunk Nor Given Away

तं च होज्ज अकामेणं, विमणेणं पडिच्छियं । तं अप्पणा ण पिबे, णो वि अण्णस्स दावए ॥५.१.८०॥

If such (improper water) has been accidentally accepted — without intent, in absent-mindedness — the monk should neither drink it himself nor give it to another (monk) to drink.

If the monk, by lapse of attention, has actually accepted impermissible water (or food), he must not compound the error by drinking it (causing more harm to himself, his discipline) or by passing it to another monk (which would spread the error). The next verse explains what to do with such accidentally-accepted water.

Simply Put: If the monk has accidentally accepted improper water without paying attention, he should not drink it himself or pass it to another monk.

5.1.81

Disposing of Accidentally Accepted Water

एगंतमवक्कमित्ता, अचित्तं पडिलेहिया । जयं परिट्ठवेज्जा, परिट्ठप्प पडिक्कमे ॥५.१.८१॥

Going to a solitary place, having inspected a lifeless spot (free of living beings), with care he should set down (the water there); and after setting it down, he should perform the pratikramana (atonement-formula) for the careless walking involved.

The disposal of accidentally-accepted improper water is itself a careful spiritual act. The monk goes alone to a solitary spot, inspects it carefully to confirm it is lifeless (no creatures, no plants, no living surfaces), then with full awareness pours out the water there. Then he performs the iryapathika pratikramana — the formal atonement for any unintended harm caused by his walking to and from that spot. The principle: even error must be corrected through ritual and inner attention; nothing is glossed over.

Simply Put: The monk should take the water to a clean, lonely spot with no living things, pour it out carefully, and then perform the prescribed atonement for any harm caused by his walk there.

5.2.1

Eating Without Leaving a Residue

पडिग्गहं संलिहित्ताणं, लेवमायाए संजए । दुगंधं वा सुगंधं वा, सव्वं भुंजे ण छड्डुए ॥५.२.१॥

The restrained monk, having wiped the alms-bowl clean with his finger down to the last smear of food-residue, should eat all of it — whether ill-smelling or sweet-smelling — and not throw away even the smallest portion.

This first verse of the second Uddesha sets the keynote of the whole chapter: when the monk eats, he must finish what he has accepted — no leftover, no food thrown away, no preference shown. The image is precise. The monk wipes the inner curve of the bowl with his finger so that not even a smear of food clings to it; whatever was placed in the bowl by the householder is consumed in full. Two reasons stand behind this discipline. The first is the protection of life: any food carelessly discarded becomes a breeding ground for ants, flies, and microscopic beings, and so the monk who throws food away creates injury where there was none. The second is the discipline of taste. The terms "ill-smelling" and "pleasant-smelling" here are not literal — the commentators clarify that they stand for the whole spectrum of agreeable and disagreeable food that a monk receives. By eating both with the same care, the monk refuses to let his tongue dictate his choices. To leave the disagreeable food and finish only the agreeable would be to surrender to the senses; the alms-tour was not undertaken to please the body but to sustain it. There is a third, subtler implication. The monk who throws away food disrespects the householder's gift, displays distaste in front of others, and brings disrepute to the order. Cleanliness of bowl and equanimity of palate together form a single discipline — the outer act and the inner attitude reinforce each other.

Simply Put: After taking food from a household, the monk should eat every last bit of it — even wiping the bowl clean with his finger — whether the food tastes good or bad, and never throw any of it away.

5.2.2-3

When a Second Alms-Tour Becomes Necessary

सेज्जा निसीहियाए, समावण्णो य गोयरे । अयावयट्ठा भोच्चाणं, जइ तेणं ण संथरे ॥५.२.२॥ तओ कारणसमुप्पण्णे, भत्तपाणं गवेसए । विहिणा पुव्वुत्तेणं, इमेण उत्तरेण य ॥५.२.३॥

(2) If the monk, having returned with alms to the resting place or the study-place and eaten what he has obtained, finds that the food was insufficient and his sustenance is not maintained by it — (3) Then, when such a need has arisen, he should go in search of food and drink again, by the procedure already taught (in the first Uddesha) and by the further procedure to be taught here.

The general rule is one alms-tour per day. But the verse acknowledges that life does not always cooperate with the rule. The monk may, on a particular day, have brought back too little food — perhaps fewer households gave, or what was given did not last, or the monk underestimated what his fellows would need. In that case, having eaten what he has, hunger is not pacified and bodily strength is not restored. The verse then permits a second alms-tour, but only on condition: a legitimate cause must have arisen. This is not a loophole for greed; it is a concession for genuine need. The commentary lists possible reasons — illness in the community, an unexpected guest, severe hunger, weather that interrupted the first tour, fasting practitioners who need a small amount of food to break their austerity, travel ahead that requires energy, and so on. In all such cases the monk does not simply walk out again; he applies the same discipline taught in the first Uddesha — the forty-two faults to be avoided, the proper time, the careful inspection of food and place — and additionally observes the further rules that this second Uddesha is about to teach. The structure of these two verses is therefore a hinge between the two Uddeshas: the first taught the basic procedure, the second teaches the extension. The monk's freedom is real but disciplined — he may go again, but only under cause, and only by procedure.

Simply Put: If after one alms-round and eating the food the monk still does not have enough to sustain him, only then — and following all the same careful rules — may he go out for a second round.

5.2.4

The Right Time for Alms

कालेण णिक्खमे भिक्खू, कालेण य पडिक्कमे । अकालं च विवज्जित्ता, काले कालं समायरे ॥५.२.४॥

The monk should go out (for alms) at the right time and return at the right time; avoiding the wrong time, he should perform each task at its appropriate time.

Time-consciousness is a deep theme in monastic life. The monk's day is divided into segments — study, meditation, alms-tour, eating, rest — and each has its appointed slot. To collect alms, the monk should go when householders are normally cooking and eating, because food is then naturally being prepared and the gift causes no extra trouble. Going too early disturbs sleeping families; going too late catches them with leftovers or with no food at all, putting the householder in awkwardness. The verse also has a second, broader meaning. "Right time" includes not only the clock-time of the village but also the right circumstance: weather, the monk's own physical condition, the rhythm of the lodging's other duties. A monk who tries to do everything regardless of timing — alms in monsoon-rain, study at dinner-hour, rest when others are wakeful — burdens both himself and the community. The verse's repeated use of the word "kāla" (time) is deliberate; the monk is being asked to live in harmony with the unfolding of the day rather than impose his will upon it.

Simply Put: The monk should go out for food at the proper time, come back at the proper time, and avoid wrong times — doing each thing when it is naturally meant to be done.

5.2.5

The Cost of Wrong Timing

अकाले चरसि भिक्खू, कालं ण पडिलेहसि । अप्पाणं च किलामेसि, सण्णिवेसं च गरिहसि ॥५.२.५॥

O monk! If you wander (for alms) at the wrong time, you do not consider the proper time; you exhaust your own self, and you (end up) blaming the very village (that gave nothing).

Where the previous verse stated the rule positively, this verse states it as a warning, addressed almost in the second-person voice of a teacher correcting a young disciple. Three consequences follow from going at the wrong time. First, bodily exhaustion — the monk walks the village and returns with little or nothing, having spent his strength for no result. Second, failure of mindfulness — by ignoring the time, he has already broken the fundamental discipline of being present in the right activity at the right moment. Third, and subtlest, slander of the village — finding no alms, the monk's mind drifts into "this place gave nothing, these people are without faith," when in fact the fault was his own timing. This is a profound psychological observation. When we fail because of our own miscalculation, we are deeply tempted to blame the circumstance. The verse holds the monk accountable: if you went at the wrong time, the empty bowl is not the village's fault, it is yours; do not slander the very people whose hospitality keeps the order alive. To preserve right speech and right view, one must first take responsibility for one's own timing.

Simply Put: If a monk goes out for food at the wrong time, he tires himself for nothing and then unfairly blames the town that had nothing to give him.

5.2.6

Equanimity When Alms Are Not Received

सइ काले चरे भिक्खू, कुज्जा पुरिसकारियं । अलाभो त्ति ण सोएज्जा, तवो त्ति अहियासए ॥५.२.६॥

The monk should go out at the proper time and make full effort; (yet) if there is no gain, he should not grieve over it — bearing it (instead) as an austerity.

This verse is among the most quoted in Jain monastic teaching, because it captures the heart of the monk's relationship to food. The procedure is two-step: do everything in your power, then accept whatever comes. Step one is purisakāra — go at the right time, walk through the village, knock at doors, make the full effort. Step two is anavishada — if despite all effort the bowl remains empty, do not be downcast. The reframing is the masterstroke. What looks like a failure (no alms received) is recast as a success of a different kind: an unplanned austerity (tapas) has spontaneously arrived. The monk who in another context might have undertaken a one-day fast as a chosen discipline now receives that very fast as a gift of circumstance. Seen this way, there is nothing to grieve — the body's hunger today is the soul's purification. This is not denial of pain. The verse explicitly uses the word ahiyāsae — "endure," "bear with steadiness." Hunger is real, the body does feel it. But the monk's attitude transforms the experience: the same hunger that breeds frustration in one mind breeds equanimity in another. The Jain insistence on bhavana (mental disposition) as the deciding factor in the karmic effect of any event finds clear expression here.

Simply Put: The monk should go out at the right time and try his best, but if he gets no food he should not be sad — he should accept the hunger as a kind of fast quietly given to him.

5.2.7

Avoiding Animals Gathered for Their Own Food

तहेवुच्चावया पाणा, भत्तट्ठाए समागया । तं उज्जुयं ण गच्छेज्जा, जयमेव परक्कमे ॥५.२.७॥

Likewise, when small and large creatures have gathered (in some place) for the sake of food, the monk should not go straight (through that place); only with mindful care should he proceed.

The monk on his alms-tour walks through a living world. The verse imagines a scene that would be ordinary in any village: birds and small animals have gathered at a spot to eat their own food — perhaps grain scattered for them, perhaps water in a trough, perhaps fruit fallen from a tree. The monk's path crosses this gathering. What should he do? The instruction is precise. He should not walk straight through the gathering, because his approach would frighten the birds into flight, the animals into scattering, and would interrupt their meal. To break their meal is itself a form of antaraya — placing an obstacle between a being and its food, which Jain ethics regards as a serious harm. Either some other path should be chosen, or, if none exists, the monk should walk with extreme mindfulness — slowly, quietly, taking a route around them, so that the creatures' meal is not disturbed. This rule is not about kindness in the sentimental sense. It is the ahimsa of detail: a great vow of non-harm sustained by the smallest acts of attention. The monk who would never strike an animal must also never frighten one; the monk who would never withhold his own food must also never deny another creature theirs.

Simply Put: If the monk sees small or large animals gathered somewhere to eat, he should not walk straight through them — he should take another path or move around them very carefully so as not to disturb their meal.

5.2.8

Not Sitting and Not Preaching at the Householder's Door

गोयरग्गपविट्ठो य, ण णिसीएज्ज कत्थई । कहं च ण पबंधेज्जा, चिट्ठित्ताण व संजए ॥५.२.८॥

Having entered (the house) for alms, the restrained monk should not sit down anywhere there; nor, while standing, should he begin a long discourse.

The monk's purpose at the householder's door is narrow and clear: receive the alms, then leave. The verse forbids two natural temptations. The first is sitting down — settling into a chair or a corner of the home invites familiarity, prolongs the visit, increases the householder's burden, and may expose the monk to sights, sounds, and conversations that erode the discipline of detachment. The second is giving a long sermon — even while standing in the doorway, the monk should not turn the alms-encounter into an extended teaching session. Religious discourse has its proper place (the upashraya, the gathering of disciples) and proper time; the doorstep during the householder's mealtime is neither. This is a delicate teaching. The monk is a teacher, and the householder may even invite a discourse out of devotion. But the verse reminds the monk that the alms-tour is a discipline of austerity, not an opportunity for influence. To linger and teach at every door would convert the begging-bowl into a stage and the discipline into a performance. The monk takes only food; he does not take attention. The verse also implicitly protects the householder. A long visit interrupts cooking, eating, and family life. The monk's restraint — receiving the alms, blessing the household, and moving on — is itself a form of respect.

Simply Put: When the monk has entered a house for alms, he should not sit down anywhere or, even while standing, give a long sermon — he is there only for food.

5.2.9

Not Leaning on Anything in the Householder's Home

अग्गलं फलिहं दारं, कवाडं वावि संजए । अवलंबिया ण चिट्ठिज्जा, गोयरग्गगओ मुणी ॥५.२.९॥

The restrained monk who has gone for alms should not stand leaning on a door-bolt, a cross-bar, a doorway, or even a door-panel.

The detail of this verse is striking. Four kinds of household fixtures are named — the small bolt, the cross-bar, the door-frame itself, the door-panel — and the monk is told to lean on none of them. The point is universal: while waiting at a householder's home, the monk should stand free, supported only by his own legs. Why such precision? Three reasons emerge. First, modesty of bearing: a monk leaning casually against a doorway looks restless, distracted, even attached to comfort. The household will form an impression that does not befit the order. Second, possible damage: the bolt or door-panel may be loose, and if the monk's weight breaks it, the householder suffers a loss for which the monk is responsible — and if the broken piece falls and injures a small creature, there is jiva-virodhana as well. Third, inner attitude: leaning on something is a small sign of having relaxed one's vigilance; the monk's body should remain alert because the discipline is alert. Even a few minutes at a door is part of the practice. This is a verse that demonstrates how thoroughly the Jain code of conduct extends into the smallest gestures. Standing without support is not arbitrary fastidiousness; it is the bodily expression of an inner stance — self-supported, attentive, present.

Simply Put: While waiting for alms at someone's house, the monk should not lean against the door, the doorframe, the bolt, or any part of the entrance — he should stand straight on his own.

5.2.10-11

Yielding to Other Mendicants at the Door

समणं माहणं वावि, किविणं वा वणीमगं । उवसंकमंतं भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१०॥ तमइक्कमित्तु ण पविसे, ण चिट्ठे चक्खुगोयरे । एगंतमवक्कमित्ता, तत्थ चिट्ठिज्ज संजए ॥५.२.११॥

(10) (When) a (Buddhist) monk, a brahmin, a destitute person, or a beggar is approaching (a house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water, the restrained monk — (11) — should not bypass that one and enter (the house first); nor should he stand within their line of sight; he should withdraw to a place apart and stand there.

These two verses paint a scene that must have been common in ancient India. A householder's door, and at the same moment several seekers of food converging on it — a wandering Buddhist or other ascetic, a brahmin priest expecting his daily portion, a poor man, a beggar, and the Jain monk himself. What is the right thing to do? The instruction is to yield. The Jain monk should not push ahead of the others, should not overtake them, should not even stand where they can see him competing for the same food. He should withdraw to a place apart and stand there, waiting his turn until the other supplicants have left. The reasons reach into both ethics and prudence. From the side of ethics, every other being also has a need for food, and the monk who races them to the door violates the spirit of aparigraha (non-grasping). From the side of prudence, if a Jain monk is seen competing aggressively for food, the laypeople will lose respect for the order and the householder may feel pressured to favour one over the others, breeding ill-will. From the side of compassion, those other seekers may need the food more than the monk — they may have nowhere else to turn, while the Jain monk has many other doors. To stand aside, out of sight, is to acknowledge their priority quietly, without asking for credit. The phrase na chiṭṭhe cakkhugoyare ("should not stand within their line of sight") is especially fine. Even the appearance of competing must be avoided. The monk's discipline is so subtle that it watches not only what he does but what others see him doing.

Simply Put: If another monk, brahmin, poor person, or beggar is approaching a house for food or water, the Jain monk should not race ahead of them or even stand where they can see him — he should quietly step aside and wait until they are finished.

5.2.12

Why Yielding Matters

वणीमगस्स वा तस्स, दायगस्सुभयस्स वा । अप्पत्तियं सिया हुज्जा, लहुत्तं पवयणस्स वा ॥५.२.१२॥

(Otherwise) of that beggar, or of the giver, or of both, displeasure may arise; or there may be a lessening (of esteem) of the (Jain) teaching itself.

This verse explains the reasoning behind the previous one. Three possible harms follow if the Jain monk pushes ahead of the other beggar. First, the beggar feels displeasure — humiliated at being overtaken, perhaps angry at losing food he was about to receive. Second, the householder feels displeasure — caught in an uncomfortable competition between two needy seekers, and forced to choose. Third, the order itself is brought low — observers will say "look how these Jain monks behave, no different from any common scrambler for food," and the pravachana (the teaching, the order, the public face of Jain dharma) suffers a loss of dignity. The verse displays a particularly mature ethical sensibility: an action is wrong not only when it directly harms someone, but also when it generates displeasure, breaks social trust, or diminishes the credibility of the spiritual community. The monk does not live in isolation; everything he does either polishes or tarnishes the reputation of the entire order. That awareness of the wider impact — of one's tiny act on the pavayana — is the mark of a mature practitioner.

Simply Put: If the monk pushes ahead of another beggar, the beggar will feel hurt, the householder will feel awkward, and people will think badly of the entire Jain teaching — so the small act of yielding protects everyone.

5.2.13

When the Other Beggar Has Left

पडिसेहिए व दिण्णे वा, तओ तम्मि णियत्तिए । उवसंकमेज्ज भत्तट्ठा, पाणट्ठाए व संजए ॥५.२.१३॥

When that (other) beggar has either been refused or has been given (alms), and has thereupon turned away from that place, then the restrained monk may approach (the house) for the sake of food or for the sake of water.

This verse completes the etiquette begun two verses earlier. The Jain monk has stepped aside out of sight; the other beggar has now finished his interaction with the householder — either he was refused (perhaps the household had nothing to give) or he received something. In either case, he has left the doorway. Only now does the Jain monk approach. The instruction is so simple that it might seem almost over-cautious, but it carries real wisdom. By waiting until the previous transaction is complete and the other person has departed, the monk avoids any appearance of having capitalized on the rejection or competed for the gift. The two visits remain entirely separate. The householder is not pressured by the simultaneous presence of two requests; the previous beggar's experience is not coloured by feeling watched; the monk's request stands on its own. This three-verse sequence (10-11-12-13) is a small masterpiece of monastic etiquette: the situation is observed, the right action is named, the reasons are given, and the timing of resumption is specified. Few ethical codes combine such practical precision with such delicate sensitivity to the feelings of others.

Simply Put: Once the other beggar has left the door — whether they were given food or refused — only then may the Jain monk go up to the house for his own alms.

5.2.14-15

Food Offered with Sentient Flowers (Plucked)

उप्पलं पउमं वावि, कुमुयं वा मगदंतियं । अण्णं वा पुप्फसच्चित्तं, तं च संलुंचिया दए ॥५.२.१४॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.१५॥

If a (giver) plucks a blue lotus, a (red) lotus, or a white moon-lotus, or a magadanti (jasmine-like) flower, or any other sentient flower, and (with hands so soiled by the plucking) offers food — that food and drink would be unfit (akalpya) for restrained monks. (The monk) should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

These two verses form a single instruction. The image is precise: a householder, wishing to offer food to the visiting monk, plucks a fresh lotus or flower — perhaps to garnish the offering, perhaps as a gesture of welcome — and with that same act, sentient life has been destroyed. The food itself may be perfectly cooked and otherwise pure; but the giver's hands, having just torn living petals, now carry the trace of that violence into the dish. For a Jain monk, the food becomes inadmissible. The monk's response is gentle but firm. He is not silent (which would leave the giver puzzled) and not accusatory (which would shame them). He simply explains: "such (food) does not befit me." The phrasing places the limit on himself, not on the giver — a small linguistic kindness that preserves the householder's dignity while protecting the rule.

Simply Put: If someone plucks a fresh flower and then offers you food with the same hands, the food is no longer pure for a Jain monk. He politely declines: "this isn't allowable for me."

5.2.16-17

Food Offered After Crushing Sentient Flowers

उप्पलं पउमं वावि, कुमुयं वा मगदंतियं । अण्णं वा पुप्फसच्चित्तं, तं च सम्मद्दिया दए ॥५.२.१६॥ तं भवे भत्तपाणं तु, संजयाण अकप्पियं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.१७॥

If a (giver) crushes / treads upon a blue lotus, a red lotus, a white moon-lotus, a magadanti, or any other sentient flower, and (so) offers food — that food and drink would be unfit for restrained monks. He should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

This pair extends the previous rule with a different mode of harm. There the violence was plucking (saṃluñcana); here it is crushing (saṃmardana). The flower may have already fallen, or be lying scattered on the floor where the householder walks; the giver, hurrying to serve, treads on it or wipes it aside with a foot or hand. Even this passive, almost incidental harm is enough to render the food unfit. The repetition is deliberate. The text could have said "plucked or crushed" in a single line, but instead the two cases are given full and parallel verses. This is the sutra's way of saying: every distinct mode of injury matters; do not assume that what is forbidden by one verb is allowed by another. Awareness must be specific.

Simply Put: If someone crushes or tramples a sentient flower while serving you, the food cannot be accepted either. The monk again declines politely.

5.2.18-19

Sachitta Roots, Stems, Shoots and Greens

सालुयं वा विरालियं, कुमुयं उप्पलणालियं । मुणालियं सासवणालियं, उच्छुखंडं अणिव्वुडं ॥५.२.१८॥ तरुणगं वा पवालं, रुक्खस्स तणगस्स वा । अण्णस्स वावि हरियस्स, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.१९॥

A lotus-bulb (sāluya), a palāsha-root (virāliya), the stalk of a white night-lotus or of a blue lotus, a lotus-fibre (muṇāliya), a stalk of mustard greens, a sugarcane segment — if not yet rendered acittic (aṇivvuḍa, not yet "cooled" / processed by fire or other means) — likewise tender shoots or sprouts of a tree or of grass, or of any other green vegetation — being raw / sentient (āmaga) — (the monk) should completely avoid.

The teaching now lists, with a botanist's care, the kinds of plant matter that remain sentient until processed. Roots, bulbs, stalks, fibres, fresh segments of cane, tender shoots — anything green and uncooked is still living. The Jain category here is aṇivvuḍa / āmaga: vegetation that has not yet been rendered acittic by fire, drying, salt, prolonged time, or some other transformation. As long as the life-force is still present, the matter cannot enter the monk's bowl. The verses do not moralize; they enumerate. This is characteristic of the alms-discipline literature: precise lists protect against the rationalizations that vague principles invite. A monk who hears "avoid sentient food" might reason his way around an exception; a monk who has memorized "lotus-bulb, palāsha-root, mustard-stalk, sugarcane piece" simply recognizes them and steps back.

Simply Put: Lotus bulbs, fresh stalks, lotus fibres, raw sugarcane segments, tender shoots and any uncooked green vegetation are still alive. The monk avoids all of these completely.

5.2.20

Tender Pods Roasted Only Once

तरुणियं वा छिवाडिं, आमियं भज्जियं सइं । दिंतियं पडियाइक्खे, ण मे कप्पइ तारिसं ॥५.२.२०॥

(If the giver offers) tender pods (chivāḍi — pods of mung, gram, peanut, etc.) which are still raw, or which have been roasted only once — (the monk) should refuse the giver: "Such (food) is not allowable for me."

The verse names a food that looks ready but is not yet acittic. Tender pods whose seeds have not hardened, or pods that have only been parched once briefly, retain enough life-force inside the seed-cavity to still be classified as sentient. A second, thorough roasting (or other processing) is required before such pods become acceptable. The phrase "roasted only once" (bhajjiyaṃ saiṃ) is a small technical detail with a large implication: half-processed food is not the same as processed food. The monk does not approximate; he waits for the full transformation, or he declines.

Simply Put: Tender or only-once-roasted pods — whose seeds inside may still be alive — are not yet pure for the monk. He politely refuses such food.

5.2.21

Berries, Bamboo Shoots, and Wood-Apple Pulp

तहा कोलमणुस्सिण्णं, वेलुयं कासवणालियं । तिलपप्पडगं णीमं, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२१॥

Likewise berries (kola — jujube/ber) not (sufficiently) heated, bamboo shoots, kāsava-stalks (a water-grass kanda or shrīparṇī fruit), tilapapaḍa (sesame-flatbread / fresh peanut/coconut chunks), nīma (kadamba-fruit) — being raw — (the monk) should avoid.

The list continues with specific items the monk is likely to encounter on the alms route. Each is a foodstuff that the householder may genuinely think of as "prepared," but each retains a residual sentience until fully transformed. Jujube berries that have not been thoroughly heated; bamboo shoots; certain water-plant stalks; sesame patties made from raw paste; kadamba fruit — all are common, all are inviting, and all are off-limits while still raw. Reading the list, one feels the monk's careful eye traveling over the dish before it enters the bowl. This is not paranoia; it is upayoga — applied awareness — turned upon the very surface of daily food.

Simply Put: Berries not thoroughly heated, bamboo shoots, raw tilapapaḍī, kadamba fruit, and similar partly-prepared foods are still sentient. The monk avoids them all.

5.2.22

Raw Flours, Bran, and Lukewarm Water

तहेव चाउलं पिट्ठं, वियडं वा तत्ताणिव्वुडं । तिलपिट्ठं पूइपिण्णागं, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२२॥

Likewise raw rice-flour (cāulaṃ piṭṭhaṃ); or hot/lukewarm water that has not been fully boiled (viyaḍaṃ tattāṇivvuḍaṃ); raw sesame-flour (tilapiṭṭhaṃ); rotten / fermented oil-cake bran (pūipiṇṇāgaṃ) — being raw / sentient — (the monk) should avoid.

The technical term tattāṇivvuḍa deserves attention. Water that has been heated but not boiled long enough — or that has been boiled but has stood beyond its prescribed window of time — is, by Jain analysis, no longer fully acittic. It has either not yet died as water-life, or it has begun to come back to life. Either way, it is sentient again, and it must be avoided. This sensitivity to the life-window of processed substances is one of the most distinctive features of Jain monastic practice. The same logic applies to flours and to spoiled bran: each is examined not for whether it is "edible" by ordinary standards, but for whether life still inhabits it.

Simply Put: Raw rice flour, water that has been only partly boiled, raw sesame flour, and rotten oil-cake bran are all still sentient (or have become so again) and must be refused.

5.2.23

Raw Wood-Apple, Citron, and Roots

कविट्ठं माउलिंगं च, मूलगं मूलगत्तियं । आमं असत्थपरिणयं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ॥५.२.२३॥

Wood-apple (kapittha), citron (matuluṅga / bijoru), radish (mūlaka), or radish-segments — being raw and not (yet) transformed by any (sterilizing) instrument (asatthapariṇayaṃ) — he should not desire even mentally.

This verse goes one important step further than the previous ones. There the monk was told to avoid raw foods physically; here he is told not to desire them even mentally. The discipline reaches inward. It is not enough to keep the bowl clean if the mind keeps asking, "if only that wood-apple were dry, if only that radish were cooked." Such longing is itself a fault, because it conditions the monk to seek opportunities where none should exist. This is a recurring move in Jain ethics: bodily restraint is the first wall, mental restraint is the second, and the second is harder. Maṇasā vi ṇa patthae — "do not desire even in mind" — is one of the discipline's most demanding instructions.

Simply Put: Raw wood-apple, citron, radish, and similar foods are not even to be wished for, let alone accepted. The discipline closes the door on the mind, not just on the bowl.

5.2.24

Raw Fruit-Flours, Seed-Powders, and Wild Fruits

तहेव फलमंथूणि, बीयमंथूणि जाणिया । विहेलगं पियालं च, आमगं परिवज्जए ॥५.२.२४॥

Likewise fruit-flours (powders of jujube etc.), seed-flours (powders of wheat, gram, barley etc.) — knowing (them to be raw); also vibhītaka (one of the trifala fruits) and piyāla (chironji-fruit / rāyaṇ-fruit / grape) — being raw — he should avoid.

The list of "raw vegetal foods to refuse" closes here. The verses 18-24 together form a small reference catalogue covering bulbs, stalks, fibres, shoots, pods, berries, flours, brans, fruits, and roots — each named with enough specificity that no monk will mistake the rule for vagueness. The closing instruction jāṇiyā — "having known / having recognized" — places the burden on the monk's discrimination: the rule does not protect those who do not learn the categories. The seven verses (18-24) together teach a single unspoken lesson: knowing what counts as alive is itself a spiritual skill, and a monk who shrinks from such knowledge will fail the discipline despite his good intentions.

Simply Put: Powders of raw fruits or seeds, vibhītaka fruit, chironji or grape — when raw — must be avoided. The monk learns to recognize each one by name.

5.2.25

Samudānika: Begging Equally from All Households

समुयाणं चरे भिक्खू, कुलं उच्चावयं सया । णीयं कुलमइक्कम्म, ऊसढं णाभिधारए ॥५.२.२५॥

A monk should always beg in the samudānika manner — going to households both high (rich) and low (poor) — without bypassing a poor house in order to head straight for a wealthy one.

Samudānika bhikṣā-vṛtti — "begging from all alike" — is one of the most beautiful disciplines of the Jain monk. The rule is simple: walk a continuous route, take whatever each household freely offers, and never skip a poor house in order to reach a rich one. The discipline serves two purposes at once. It guards the monk's anāsakti (non-attachment), since the bowl ceases to be a tool of preference. And it protects the dignity of the poor, who are not made to feel that the holy man considered their offering beneath him. Two temptations are being named here: the temptation of luxury (always heading to the rich) and the temptation of relationship (cultivating wealthy patrons whose support becomes habitual). Both, when followed, slowly hollow out the monkhood from within.

Simply Put: The monk should beg from rich and poor houses alike, in their natural order. He must never skip a humble home in order to reach a wealthy one.

5.2.26

Without Despondency, Without Attachment

अदीणो वित्तिमेसिज्जा, ण विसीएज्ज पंडिए । अमुच्छिओ भोयणम्मि, मायण्णे एसणा रए ॥५.२.२६॥

Without despondency (adīno), the wise (paṇḍita) monk should seek his livelihood (vṛtti / alms); he should not become depressed (when little is found). Unattached to food, knowing the right measure, he should be devoted to the rules of eṣaṇā (the discipline of seeking).

The verse names the inner posture that the alms-discipline aspires to. Two extremes are forbidden. On one side, dīnatā — feeling diminished or pitiful when alms are scarce, or putting on a show of poverty before householders to extract sympathy. On the other side, mūrcchā — getting engrossed in food when it comes plentiful and tasty. Either extreme corrupts the alms-walk; the true posture is steady between them. Two further qualities are added: māyaṇṇa (knowing the right measure — taking only as much as is appropriate, not more, not less) and esaṇā rae (loving the rules of seeking — not resenting the discipline as a burden but embracing it as the very form of one's freedom). Together these four — adīna, amūrcchita, māyaṇṇa, esaṇā-rae — describe the spiritual signature of the Jain alms-walker.

Simply Put: The wise monk seeks alms without self-pity when little comes and without greed when much comes. He takes the right measure, and he loves the very discipline of seeking.

5.2.27

When the Householder Has Plenty But Does Not Give

बहुं परघरे अत्थि, विविहं खाइमसाइमं । ण तत्थ पंडिओ कुप्पे, इच्छा दिज्ज परो ण वा ॥५.२.२७॥

There may be much in another's house — varied solid foods (khādya) and tasty foods (svādya). The wise (monk) should not become angry there; (let him reflect that) the other person, by his own wish, may give or may not give.

The scene is exact and humanly familiar: the monk enters a wealthy home; he can see fruit, sweets, prepared dishes filling the kitchen; the householder offers him nothing, or only a fraction. A small flame of resentment is the natural reaction — "they have so much, and yet…" The verse shuts that flame down before it can spread. The reasoning is short but complete: icchā dijja paro ṇa vā — "by their own wish, the other may give or may not." The food belongs to the householder. Their giving is a free act, not a debt owed. The monk who forgets this turns alms into a transaction, and the moment alms become a transaction, monkhood ends.

Simply Put: Even if a household has plenty and gives little (or nothing), the monk should not become angry. The choice belongs to the giver, not to the asker.

5.2.28

No Anger When Bedding, Seat, Cloth, Food, or Water Is Refused

सयणासणवत्थं वा, भत्तपाणं च संजए । अदिंतस्स ण कुपिज्जा, पच्चक्खे वि य दीसओ ॥५.२.२८॥

The restrained monk should not become angry with one who does not give — bedding (śayana), seat (āsana), cloth (vastra), or food and drink (bhakta-pāna) — even when (the items) are visibly present right before his eyes.

The previous verse handled food alone; this one extends the principle to every category of monastic need. The list — bedding, seat, cloth, food, water — covers nearly all the items a monk depends on a householder to provide. The hardest part of the rule is the closing phrase: paccakkhe vi ya dīsao — "even when visible right before his eyes." It is one thing to remain composed when the household claims to have nothing; it is another to remain composed when the items are sitting on the next shelf and the householder still declines. The verse gently reminds the monk that the householder's home is not a public storeroom. What is visible there is not therefore available. Ownership is real; refusal is a right; the monk's task is only to ask once, accept the answer, and walk on without stain.

Simply Put: Even when a householder visibly has bedding, seats, cloth, food, or water and still refuses the monk — the monk must not become angry. The household's possessions are theirs to give or withhold.

5.2.29

Do Not Beg from Those Who Salute, Do Not Speak Harshly to Those Who Refuse

इत्थियं पुरिसं वावि, डहरं वा महल्लगं । वंदमाणं ण जाइज्जा, णो य णं फरुसं वए ॥५.२.२९॥

A woman, a man, a child, or an elder — when they are bowing in salutation (vandamāna) — (the monk) should not beg from them; nor should he speak any harsh word to one who does not give.

Two refinements appear in this single verse. First: when a layperson — of whatever age or sex — is in the act of bowing in respect, do not turn that moment of devotion into a moment of asking. The vandanā belongs to them; let it complete in its own dignity. To request food while someone is still mid-bow is to convert reverence into transaction; the bhāvārtha of the Gujarati commentary is precise on this — yācanā na karavī — no request is to be made just then. Second: when someone declines to give, do not punish them with a sharp word. Pharusaṃ vae — "speaks harshly" — is forbidden categorically. A monk who curses or scolds a householder for not giving has, in that instant, ceased to be a monk in the relevant sense. The discipline of asking and the discipline of not-asking-back are two halves of one self-control.

Simply Put: Don't make requests from someone who is in the middle of saluting you. And if anyone — woman, man, child, or elder — declines to give, never speak harshly to them.

5.2.30

Equanimity Toward the Honoring and the Indifferent

जे ण वंदे ण से कुप्पे, वंदिओ ण समुक्कसे । एवमण्णेसमाणस्स, सामण्णमणुचिट्ठइ ॥५.२.३०॥

If someone does not bow (does not salute), the monk should not become angry with him; if he is bowed to, he should not swell up (with pride). For one who seeks (alms) thus equably, śrāmaṇya — monkhood — remains intact.

This is one of the great single-verse summaries of the alms-discipline's inner work. Two reactions are forbidden, and they are the mirror images of one another: getting angry at the indifferent, and getting puffed up by the reverent. Both come from the same root — staking one's self-worth on the householder's response. The cure is also single: become indifferent to both responses, and the monkhood lives. The Sanskrit commentary glosses evam aṇṇesamāṇassa with the technical phrase evam anveṣamāṇasya — "of one searching in this way." The wording matters: the monk's job is to search (anveṣaṇa), not to secure. When the search itself, conducted in equanimity, becomes the practice, then whether the search yields fruit or not, śrāmaṇya is preserved.

Simply Put: Don't get angry at householders who don't bow to you. Don't get proud when they do. Stay even-minded through both — and your monkhood stays whole.

5.2.31

The Monk Who Hides a Tasty Find

सिया एगइओ लद्धुं, लोभेण विणिगूहइ । मामेयं दाइयं संतं, दट्ठूणं सयमायए ॥५.२.३१॥

It may happen that a certain (monk), having obtained (some tasty food), conceals it out of greed (thinking): "Lest someone, seeing this — given to me, present in my bowl — should take it for himself."

The verse names a quiet monastic vice. A monk goes for alms; he is given some unusually delicious food; he begins to fear that if his guru, ācārya, or fellow monks see it, they will ask for a portion or simply take it. So he covers the tasty food at the bottom of the bowl and piles bland food on top. The deceit is small in scale, but it strikes at three things at once: anāsakti (he is now attached to the food), gurukula-vinaya (he is hiding from the very superiors who structure his life), and saṅghārpaṇa (he has refused the spirit of communal sharing). The Gujarati commentator names this clearly as the first of two forms of māyācāra (deceitfulness) practiced for the sake of food. The sutra here begins a long block (31-41) about the māyāvī sādhu — the deceitful monk — and his slow ruin. Read this verse as the seed.

Simply Put: A weak-willed monk gets some specially tasty food, fears that others will share it with him, and quietly hides it under blander food in his bowl. That hiding is the first step of a long fall.

5.2.32

The Selfish Monk Never Reaches Liberation

अत्तट्ठा गुरुओ लुद्धो, बहुं पावं पकुव्वइ । दुत्तोसओ य से होइ, णिव्वाणं च ण गच्छइ ॥५.२.३२॥

Heavy-laden with selfishness (atta-aṭṭhā guruo) and greedy (luddha), he commits much sin (bahuṃ pāvaṃ). He becomes hard to satisfy (duttosao); and he does not reach liberation (nirvāṇa).

The verse is structured as a cascading consequence. Selfishness produces greed; greed produces accumulated sin; accumulated sin produces a temperament that no amount of food, honor, or comfort can ever satisfy; that temperament produces a self that cannot reach nirvāṇa. Each link is a small step, but the chain is fatal. The word duttosao — "hard to satisfy" — is striking. The deceitful monk thinks he is securing his pleasure by hiding choice food, but in fact he is teaching his own mind to be permanently dissatisfied. Every concealment trains the appetite to expect more, to fear loss, to grasp harder next time. And a mind so trained simply cannot relax into the equanimity that liberation requires.

Simply Put: The selfish, greedy monk piles up sin, becomes impossible to satisfy, and never reaches liberation. The hidden food in the bowl is a small thing; the consequences are not small.

5.2.33

Eating the Tasty on the Way, Bringing the Bland to the Lodging

सिया एगइओ लद्धुं, विविहं पाणभोयणं । भद्दगं भद्दगं भोच्चा, विवण्णं विरसमाहरे ॥५.२.३३॥

It may happen that a certain (monk), having obtained various foods and drinks, eats up the choice (tastiest) portions one by one (along the way), and brings to the lodging only the colourless, tasteless (bland) remainder.

This is the second species of māyācāra the commentator promised. Where 5.2.31 hid the tasty food at the bottom of the bowl, 5.2.33 eats the tasty food on the road and brings only bland leftovers home. The strategy is opposite, but the motive is identical: secure the choice food for oneself, secure the appearance of austerity for the community. What is monstrous about this is its cleverness. The deceitful monk has converted one of monkhood's central acts — sharing alms with the community — into a private feast. He has used the cover of monastic propriety to commit a kind of theft. Many later traditions of monastic literature (Jain and Buddhist alike) treat this as one of the gravest internal corruptions, precisely because it cannot be detected from outside.

Simply Put: Some monks eat all the tasty food along the way back from alms, and bring only the dry, bland items to the lodging. The community thinks they are austere; in truth, they have stolen the meal.

5.2.34

The Disguise: Other Monks Praise Him as Self-Disciplined

जाणंतु ता इमे समणा, आययट्ठी अयं मुणी । संतुट्ठो सेवए पंतं, लूहवित्ती सुतोसओ ॥५.२.३४॥

(He thinks): "Let these monks know that this monk is one bent on the highest goal (āyayaṭṭhī — āyatārthī, seeking liberation); content, he resorts to the leftover (paṃta — inferior food); his way of life is bland (lūha-vitti); he is easily satisfied (sutosao)."

Verse 34 lifts the curtain on the deceit's whole purpose. The monk in 5.2.33 — who ate the tasty food on the way and brought only the bland items home — was not just being secretly greedy. He was actively staging an image. He wants the other monks to see him eating poor food and admiring him for it. The four words he wants applied to himself — āyatārthī, saṃtuṣṭa, lūkhavṛtti, sutoṣaka — are precisely the most prestigious labels of monastic virtue. Liberation-bound, content, austere, easily satisfied. To wear these labels falsely is to commit māyāśalya — the dart of deceit lodged in the soul. The verse is also psychologically acute. It shows that the worst hypocrisy is not the bare lie but the curated impression: each gesture, each refusal of good food in public, each humble word, all chosen to produce an effect. Spiritual life, the verse implies, dies fastest under exactly this kind of polished performance.

Simply Put: The deceitful monk wants his fellow monks to think: "Look how austere he is! He's content with leftovers, lives rough, never asks for more." Behind this praise is a private, well-fed lie.

5.2.35

Hungry for Worship, Honor, and Reputation

पूयणट्ठी जसोकामी, माणसम्माणकामए । बहुं पसवइ पावं, मायासल्लं च कुव्वइ ॥५.२.३५॥

Desiring worship (pūjaṇaṭṭhī), craving fame (yaśas-kāmī), longing for honour and respect (māna-saṃmāna-kāmaka) — (such a monk) generates much sin and impales himself with the dart of deceit (māyā-śalya).

This is the closing verse of the five-verse cluster on the deceitful monk. It diagnoses the source: not appetite, but vanity. The hidden food and the staged poverty all serve a single appetite — the appetite for being seen as holy. Three nouns name the prize: pūjā (formal worship), yaśa (popular fame), māna-saṃmāna (esteem expressed in honors and material gifts). Each of these is, in itself, a worldly value the layperson chases; the monk, by dressing his pursuit of these in spiritual clothes, multiplies the sin rather than diminishing it. The image of māyā-śalya — "the dart of deceit" — is exact. A śalya is a foreign object embedded in the body that, if not removed, festers. Deceit lodged in the practitioner's mind cannot simply be ignored; it slowly poisons the spiritual organism from within. This is why the alms discipline takes seemingly small acts of concealment so seriously.

Simply Put: A monk who wants to be worshipped, famous, and honored will pile up sin and lodge in himself the deep splinter of deceit — even while looking, on the surface, like a saint.

5.2.36

Total Prohibition of Intoxicants

सुरं वा मेरगं वावि, अण्णं वा मज्जगं रसं । ससक्खं ण पिवे भिक्खू, जसं सारक्खमप्पणो ॥५.२.३६॥

A monk should not drink — with himself as witness (sasakṣaṃ — i.e., before his own conscience and that of the Kevalī) — surā (grain-based liquor), meraka (mahuā-flower liquor), or any other intoxicating juice (madya-rasa) — guarding his own reputation and self.

The Sthānāṅga Sūtra calls intoxication a cause of the hellish destinies, and the present sutra-author re-affirms the prohibition by listing two specific drinks (surā, made from grain or flour; meraka, made from the mahuā flower) and then closing the door on every other intoxicant by the catch-all aṇṇaṃ vā majjagaṃ rasaṃ. The monastic life requires a sāttvika diet; tāmasic and intoxicating substances inflame buried desires, fog discrimination, and open the door to a hundred other faults that would otherwise stay closed. The phrase sasakkhaṃ (Skt. svasākṣyam / sasākṣyaṃ) is the verse's moral hinge. The commentator unfolds it two ways: with one's own self as witness (the conscience), and with the Kevalī (the omniscient Jina) as witness. The teaching is: any sin committed in secret is still committed before two unfailing witnesses, and a monk who remembers this cannot drink even alone.

Simply Put: A monk must never drink any intoxicant — surā, meraka, or anything else — even in private. Two witnesses are always present: his own conscience, and the all-seeing Jina.

5.2.37

The Fallen Monk Who Drinks in Secret

पियए एगओ तेणो, ण मे कोइ वियाणइ । तस्स पस्सह दोसाइं, णियडिं च सुणेह मे ॥५.२.३७॥

(Some fallen monk thinks): "I am drinking alone, like a thief; no one knows of me." See his faults, and hear from me of his deceit.

The sutra adopts a striking voice. The teacher introduces the figure of the teṇa — the thief — and says, in effect: "Listen, I will name his hiding places one by one." A thief here is anyone who violates the command of the Jina; drinking alcohol in secret is precisely this kind of theft. The phrase ṇa me koi viyāṇai — "no one sees me" — is the thief's reassurance to himself, and the sutra exposes it as self-deception. The word ṇiyaḍi (Skt. niḥkṛti) is the verse's punchline. It refers to a second layer of deceit on top of the first: the drinker drinks (deceit one), and then lies to cover the drinking (deceit two). One transgression always breeds another; concealment is always parasitic on a deeper concealment. Verses 38-41 will now trace what happens when this layered deceit is allowed to take root.

Simply Put: The secret drinker says, "no one knows." But he is doubly deceitful: once for drinking, once for hiding it. Now hear what becomes of him.

5.2.38

Attachment, Falsehood, Disrepute, and Endless Restlessness

वड्ढइ सुंडिया तस्स, मायामोसं च भिक्खुणो । अयसो य अणिव्वाणं, सययं च असाहुया ॥५.२.३८॥

For that monk, addiction (śauṇḍikā / drinker's craving) grows; deceit-and-falsehood (māyā-mṛṣā) grows; disrepute (ayaśas) grows; non-peace (aniḥnirvāṇa — no liberation, no even worldly tranquillity); and continually, "non-monkhood" (asādhutā) grows.

Five negative growths are listed, each one feeding on the previous. The drinker's craving expands; the deceit needed to sustain the craving thickens; the inevitable leakage into the world produces disrepute; the inner state loses any chance of peace; and the very identity of monkhood drains away, day by day, until what remains is a shape without substance. Each item here is a direct consequence, not a moral threat — the verse describes a mechanism, not a punishment. The compound māyā-mṛṣā — "deceit and lie together" — is itself one of Jainism's named mahā-doṣas, identified as a hybrid evil more dangerous than either deceit or lying alone. The drinker, by definition, lives inside this compound.

Simply Put: The secret-drinker's craving keeps growing; his deceits multiply; his reputation crumbles; his peace is lost; and his very monkhood quietly dies — every day a little more.

5.2.39

Like a Worried Thief, Even at Death He Cannot Hold Restraint

णिच्चुव्विग्गो जहा तेणो, अत्तकम्मेहिं दुम्मइ । तारिसो मरणंते वि, ण आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.३९॥

Just as a thief is constantly anxious because of his own (criminal) acts (atta-kammehiṃ dummai), so too the foolish (drunken) monk: even at the very end of life, he cannot accomplish saṃvara — the dharma of self-restraint.

The image is sharp. A thief, having stolen, can never settle. Every shadow startles him; every glance feels like an accusation; he cannot enjoy what he has stolen. Just so, a monk who has sustained a hidden vice for years has lost the inner ground on which death-time saṃvara — the deliberate, peaceful cessation of karmic intake — would normally stand. This matters spiritually because in Jain teaching the moments before death carry tremendous weight. A peaceful, saṃvara-filled death can dramatically lighten the soul's onward trajectory. But peaceful saṃvara at death requires a mind already trained in stillness; the lifelong drunken-deceiver's mind is the opposite of still. So even the great moment passes without yielding its great gift.

Simply Put: The drunken monk's mind is restless like a thief's. So restless that even on his deathbed, when restraint matters most, he cannot find it.

5.2.40

Even the Householders Despise Him

आयरिए णाराहेइ, समणे यावि तारिसो । गिहत्था वि णं गरिहंति, जेण जाणंति तारिसं ॥५.२.४०॥

He cannot serve the ācāryas, nor the (other) monks. Even householders despise him — because they recognize what kind of (man) he is.

The verse traces the social collapse that mirrors the spiritual collapse of 5.2.39. Three relational circles, named in widening order: ācāryas, śramaṇas, householders. The drunken monk has lost his standing in all three. He cannot give the kind of service to his ācāryas (vinaya, vaiyāvṛtya) that builds spiritual closeness, because he is too distracted by his vice. He cannot stand cleanly among fellow monks, because they sense the deceit. And — this is the cruelest sentence — even the householders, on whom monks depend for alms, see through him and despise him. The reasoning is precise: jeṇa jāṇaṃti tārisaṃ — "because they have come to know him as such." Hidden faults do not stay hidden indefinitely; the social world is slow but eventually accurate. The fall from monkhood is not first noticed by the monk himself, then by his teachers, then by the society. It tends to be noticed by the society first.

Simply Put: The drunken monk has lost it on every side: he can't serve his teachers, fellow monks see through him, and even the laity look down on him.

5.2.41

The Vice-Seeker Who Rejects Virtues Cannot Hold Restraint Even at Death

एवं तु अगुणप्पेही, गुणाणं च विवज्जए । तारिसो मरणंते वि, ण आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.४१॥

Thus the seeker-of-faults (aguṇa-prekṣī), who casts away the virtues (guṇa) — even at the end of life such a one does not accomplish saṃvara.

This verse closes the long block (31-41) on the deceitful, drunken, fallen monk. It generalises the argument: the specific case (drinking in secret) is one example of a wider species — the aguṇa-prekṣī, the man whose attention is permanently turned toward faults rather than virtues. He notices the lapses of others; he indulges his own; he rejects the established virtues — right knowledge, right faith, right conduct, forgiveness, obedience to the Jina's command. Such an orientation, sustained over a life, does not magically reverse at the moment of death. The repetition of the closing line from 5.2.39 — tāriso maraṇaṃte vi, ṇa ārāhei saṃvaraṃ — frames the block. Verse 39 began the lament; verse 41 closes it. The two together announce a single doctrine: saṃvara at the moment of death is not a gift; it is the ripening of a long discipline. The fallen monk gets nothing in his last hour because he has banked nothing.

Simply Put: The fault-seeker who throws away virtues spends his life shrinking his soul. At the end, when restraint matters most, he has none left to draw on.

5.2.42

The Wise Monk: Austerity, Renouncing Tasty Drink, Free from Intoxication

तवं कुव्वइ मेहावी, पणीयं वज्जए रसं । मज्जप्पमायविरओ, तवस्सी अइउक्कोसो ॥५.२.४२॥

The wise (medhāvī) monk practices austerity (tapas); he renounces rich/oily/tasty (paṇīta) juices and dishes; abstaining from intoxication and (its) negligence (madya-pramāda), the ascetic attains the highest excellence (atyutkarṣa).

After the long, sober warning of 31-41, the sutrakāra now lifts the gaze to the opposite figure: the wise monk who actually walks the discipline. Three things define him: he does austerity (tapas — fasts, restrictions, the long work of training the body); he gives up paṇīta foods (rich, fatty, juicy, especially tasty ones — the kind of food that fattens the senses); and he stays clear of intoxication and the pramāda (heedlessness) that follows from it. Note the compound majja-pamāya. The Gujarati commentary clarifies that madya and pramāda are not two parallel evils here but a single causal chain: madya causes pramāda. The verse therefore says: by abstaining from the cause, the monk also abstains from the effect. This is the structure of all skillful asceticism — cut the root, and the branches do not have to be fought one by one.

Simply Put: The wise monk does austerity, gives up rich and tasty food, stays away from intoxicants and the carelessness they bring. That ascetic rises to the highest excellence.

5.2.43

Witness His Welfare: Worshipped, Endowed With Vast Meaning

तस्स पस्सह कल्लाणं, अणेगसाहुपूइयं । विउलं अत्थसंजुत्तं, कित्तइस्सं सुणेह मे ॥५.२.४३॥

Behold his welfare (kallāṇa) — worshipped by many monks (aṇegasāhupūita), vast (vipula), endowed with the great Goal (artha-saṃyukta — the goal of liberation). I shall extol (his virtues); hear them from me.

The teacher's voice returns in the same tone he used at 5.2.37 to introduce the fallen monk — but with the polarity reversed. There he said tassa passaha dosāiṃ (see his faults); here he says tassa passaha kallāṇaṃ (see his welfare). The framing makes the contrast explicit: two paths, two outcomes, one teacher narrating both so that the disciple can see clearly which one to take. The phrase vipulam artha-saṃyuktam deserves notice. In the discipline literature artha often slides between two meanings — practical wealth (worldly artha) and the supreme goal (mokṣa-artha). Here the latter is meant. The wise monk is welfare because his life is bound to the great Goal, and that binding is what gives all the other virtues their weight.

Simply Put: Now look at his good fortune: the wise monk is worshipped by many monks, his life is full of meaning, and it is bound to the highest goal. Listen as I describe his virtues.

5.2.44

The Virtue-Seeker Holds Restraint Even at Death

एवं तु गुणप्पेही, अगुणाणं च विवज्जए । तारिसो मरणंते वि, आराहेइ संवरं ॥५.२.४४॥

Thus the virtue-seeker (guṇa-prekṣī), who casts off the faults (aguṇa) — even at the end of life such a one accomplishes saṃvara (self-restraint).

This verse is the exact mirror of 5.2.41. There the aguṇa-prekṣī could not hold saṃvara even at death; here the guṇa-prekṣī does hold it, even at death. The mirror is deliberate, and the lesson is the same lesson read in two directions: what I attend to becomes what I am, and what I am at the moment of death is what I have been training myself to be. The verse is also the second of three almost identical closing-formulas (5.2.39 / 5.2.41 / 5.2.44). Three repetitions of one phrase frame the entire question of life-direction around the test of death-time saṃvara. The Jain teacher knows that the disciple may take many things lightly, but the moment of death is one moment he cannot take lightly — and so the entire teaching is anchored to that point.

Simply Put: The virtue-seeker who casts off vices keeps his restraint even at the moment of death. His life and his death are made of one cloth.

5.2.45

Honored by Ācāryas, by Monks, Even by Householders

आयरिए आराहेइ, समणे यावि तारिसो । गिहत्था वि णं पूयंति, जेण जाणंति तारिसं ॥५.२.४५॥

He serves (ārādhayati — wins the favor of) the ācāryas, and likewise the (fellow) monks. Even householders worship him — because they recognize what kind of (man) he is.

The mirror of 5.2.40. There the fallen monk could not satisfy any of the three circles (ācāryas, monks, householders); here the virtuous monk wins all three. The same word tārisa — "of such kind" — carries opposite weight in the two verses. The whole moral psychology of the alms discipline rests on this contrast: in either direction, the inner orientation eventually becomes a public fact. The verb ārāhei — "serves," "satisfies," "propitiates" — is the same word used for accomplishing saṃvara in the previous verse. The double use is deliberate. The monk who ārāhei saṃvaram (accomplishes restraint) is also the one who ārāhei ācāryān (satisfies preceptors): the same disposition that earns spiritual fruit also earns social grace, because both flow from the same character.

Simply Put: The virtuous monk satisfies his teachers, lives well among his fellow monks, and is even revered by householders — because they all see, in time, what he truly is.

5.2.46

The Four-Fold Thief: Of Tapas, Vows, Form, and Conduct

तवतेणे वयतेणे, रूवतेणे य जे णरे । आयारभावतेणे य, कुव्वइ देवकिव्विसं ॥५.२.४६॥

The man who is a thief of austerity (tapas-stena), a thief of vows (vrata-stena), a thief of form (rūpa-stena — i.e., violator of the monk's outward marks of conduct), and a thief of inner conduct (ācāra-bhāva-stena) — produces (for himself) the kilbiṣika-deva (state) — the lowest, most despised order of deva.

The sutra now elevates its diagnosis. Drinking in secret was one symptom; the deeper disease has four faces, and each face is named here as a kind of theft. The metaphor is sharp: just as a thief takes something that does not belong to him, so the monk who does not perform what his vows obligate is appropriating the prestige of monkhood without paying its price. He keeps the title without doing the work — a theft of identity itself. The four thefts together cover (1) the outer austerities, (2) the great vows, (3) the bodily marks of monkhood, and (4) the inner samācārī. Together they leave nothing of the monastic edifice unguarded. The destiny named — kilbiṣika-deva — is a deva-birth, but a low and despised one: technically heavenly but spiritually contemptible. Verses 47-48 will unfold this destiny.

Simply Put: A man who is a thief of austerity, vows, monastic form, and inner conduct earns himself the lowest, most contemptible deva-birth.

5.2.47

Born a Despised Deva, He Cannot Even Recognize Why

लद्धूण वि देवत्तं, उववण्णो देवकिव्विसे । तत्थावि से ण याणाइ, किं मे किच्चा इमं फलं ॥५.२.४७॥

Although he has obtained deva-hood, having been born among the kilbiṣika-devas — even there, he does not understand: "By what action of mine did I obtain this (kind of) fruit?"

The verse names a special tragedy. The fallen monk does become a deva — that is the surface result of his outwardly monastic life. But because his inner life was corrupted by mithyātva (false view), the deva-state he obtains is one of the lowest classes, the kilbiṣikas: heavenly beings who are despised even by other devas, of low influence and low merit. And because mithyātva clouds memory of past lives more than samyaktva does, this kilbiṣika-deva cannot even understand how he got here. He sees only that he is in a degraded position; he cannot trace it back to the cause. This loss of self-understanding is, in some ways, worse than the degradation itself. The kilbiṣika cannot reform what he cannot remember. Hence the tradition's verdict: even a heavenly birth is not, by itself, a sign of spiritual progress. The kind of birth and the kind of awareness within it together determine the soul's actual position.

Simply Put: The fallen monk does become a deva — but a despised, low-grade one. And because his vision was distorted, even there he cannot understand: "What did I do to deserve this?"

5.2.48

From There, He Falls into Mute-Goat, Hell, Animal Births, Where Awakening Is Rare

तत्तो वि से चइत्ताणं, लब्भइ एलमूयगं । णरगं तिरिक्खजोणिं वा, बोही जत्थ सुदुल्लहा ॥५.२.४८॥

And from there too, falling away (from that deva-state), he obtains (rebirth as) a mute goat (eḍa-mūka — a dumb sacrificial goat); or hell (naraka); or an animal womb (tiryañc-yoni) — wheresoever the gaining of bodhi is exceedingly rare.

The verse closes the four-step descent. (1) The man stole his own monkhood; (2) he became a kilbiṣika-deva; (3) even as deva he could not reform; (4) and after that deva-life ends, he tumbles into mute-goat births, hells, or animal wombs — exactly those states where the seed of right insight (bodhi) almost cannot germinate. This is the cost of māyā-mṛṣā-charioted monkhood. It does not merely waste one life; it sets up an entire onward chain of lives in which liberation grows further and further away. The phrase bohī sudullahā — "bodhi exceedingly rare" — is one of the great refrains of Jain literature. Right insight, the kind that turns a soul toward liberation, is not freely distributed across realms. Some realms make it almost impossible. Hence the urgency of using a human birth — with its rare combination of intelligence, freedom, and access to teaching — for serious practice.

Simply Put: When the kilbiṣika-deva-life ends, he falls again — into mute-goat births, hells, or animal wombs, where the chance of spiritual awakening is almost gone.

5.2.49

Knowing This, the Wise Should Renounce Even an Atom of Deceit

एयं च दोसं दट्ठूणं, णायपुत्तेण भासियं । अणुमायं पि मेहावी, मायामोसं विवज्जए ॥५.२.४९॥

Having seen this fault — declared by the son of the Jñātrikas (Mahāvīra) — the wise (medhāvī) should renounce māyā-mṛṣā (deceit-and-falsehood) even to the measure of an atom (aṇumātram).

The closing teaching of the long fall-of-the-deceitful-monk arc. The verse explicitly attributes the doctrine to Jñātaputra — Mahāvīra himself — giving the warning the highest possible authority. And the closing instruction is striking: aṇumātram api — "even to the measure of an atom." Not "renounce great deceit"; not "renounce visible lies"; but: do not allow even an atom of māyā-mṛṣā into your life. The reasoning of the previous verses is the foundation for this severity. A small deceit unleashes a slow chain reaction: first concealment, then drinking-or-its-equivalent, then social isolation, then loss of saṃvara, then a degraded heavenly birth, then animal births, then near-impossible bodhi. The chain begins with "an atom." Therefore the discipline targets the atom.

Simply Put: Mahāvīra has shown you this whole chain of harm. So the wise monk renounces even the smallest atom of deceit and lying.

5.2.50

The Closing Instruction of Adhyayana 5, Uddesha 2

सिक्खिऊण भिक्खेसणसोहिं, संजयाण बुद्धाण सगासे । तत्थ भिक्खु सुप्पणिहिंदिए, तिव्वलज्जगुणवं विहरिज्जासि ॥त्ति बेमि॥५.२.५०॥

Having learned the purification of bhikṣaiṣaṇā — the discipline of seeking alms — in the proximity (sa-gāse) of the restrained ones (saṃyatas) and the awakened (buddhas) — there (in that very discipline), let the monk, with senses well-held (su-praṇihit-indriya), endowed with intense moral shame (tīvra-lajjā) and (other) virtues, dwell and move. — Thus I declare. (Iti bravīmi.)

The Uddesha closes with the standard upasaṃhāra — the summarizing instruction. Three verbs structure the sentence. Sikkhiūṇa — having learned: not from books or guesswork, but in the sakāśa (close presence) of saṃjaya-buddhas, the restrained and the awakened. The discipline of bhikṣaiṣaṇā is too subtle to pick up on one's own; it must be transmitted by life-long practitioners to a disciple who lives close to them. Tattha — there: the monk does not "learn" this once and then move on to other practice; the very alms-discipline becomes his ongoing field of life. Viharijjāsi — let him move: the spiritual life is described not as "stationing oneself in" but as "moving / dwelling / wandering through" the discipline. Three qualities are named for that movement: suppaṇihit-indriya (senses well-restrained), tīvra-lajjā (intense moral shame at the very thought of an unworthy act), and guṇavān (richly virtuous). The last term sums up everything the Uddesha has taught — the four-fold purity of seeking, the equanimity in receiving, the absolute refusal of deceit, the vigilance about every grain of sentient food. Iti bravīmi: "thus I declare." The teacher closes; the disciple carries the entire discourse forward into the moving life of begging, eating, sharing, and disposing.

Simply Put: Learn the alms-discipline closely from restrained, awakened teachers. Then, with senses controlled, with deep moral sensitivity, with virtue, live inside that discipline as your way of moving through the world. — Thus I declare.
