The Seventh Adhyayan — Purity of Speech. The comprehensive treatise on right speech: four kinds of language, what to avoid, what to say, and how to use words carefully about plants, beings, and conditions.

Adhyayan 7: Suvākyaśuddhi

About This Adhyayan

What to Say and What to Hold Back

The seventh adhyayan is the most complete teaching on speech in the Jain canon. It begins by classifying language into four types: satya (truthful), asatya (false), satya-asatya miśra (mixed), and asatya-anubhaya (neither, i.e. neutral). The first and fourth are permissible to the monk; the second and third are forbidden. Beyond classification, the chapter teaches careful word-choice: never declarative speech about uncertain matters, never harsh or hurtful speech, never deceptive speech, never speech that predicts the future without omniscient warrant.

The chapter is famous for its precision about plants and beings: the monk should not call a banyan tree 'the great tree' (which praises it and may invite cutting); should not call a calf 'meaty' (which suggests slaughter); should not call clouds 'about to rain' (which presumes prediction). Right speech, in Jain practice, is mita-bhāṣaṇa — measured, beneficial, true, and clear. The chapter closes with verses on the discipline of the wise speaker.

The Sutras of Adhyayan 7

Prakrit original with English translation, commentary, and the simple explanation.

7.1

The Four Languages and the Wise Monk's Choice

चउण्हं खलु भासाणं, परिसंखाय पण्णवं । दोण्हं तु विणयं सिक्खे, दो न भासिज्ज सव्वसो

The wise monk, having fully understood the four (kinds of) languages, should learn the discipline of two of them and should not speak the (other) two at all.

The chapter opens by giving the monk a map. Speech, the sutrakar says, comes in only four flavours: Satya (true), Asatya (false), Mishra (mixed — partly true, partly false), and Vyavahara (conventional / transactional, which is neither true nor false in essence but operates by social agreement, e.g., greetings, instructions, directions). The monk's task is not to "tell the truth" in the naive sense — it is to discriminate which of these four serves liberation and which obstructs it. Of the four, two must be wholly renounced: Asatya (whatever is contrary to fact, whether spoken from passion or carelessness) and Mishra (whatever blends truth with untruth — because the untruthful element contaminates the whole). The remaining two, Satya and Vyavahara, are permissible — but only when used with vinaya, the discipline of considered, gentle, beneficial speech. Mere permissibility is not enough; even a true sentence must be examined for its effect before it leaves the mouth. This opening verse therefore establishes the entire architecture of the chapter. Everything that follows — the rules about predicting the future, about addressing women, about describing animals, about giving commands — is an unfolding of this single principle: the monk must know speech before he uses speech.

Simply Put: Speech comes in four kinds — true, false, mixed, and merely conventional. A wise monk learns how to use only the two safe kinds (true and conventional) with care, and completely gives up the other two (false and mixed).

7.2

Even True Speech May Be Unspeakable

जा य सच्चा अवत्तव्वा, सच्चामोसा य जा मुसा । जा य बुद्धेहिं णाइण्णा, न तं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

That (speech) which, though true, is unspeakable; that which is mixed (truth-and-falsehood) and that which is false; and that which has not been sanctioned by the Enlightened Ones — the wise monk should not speak any of these.

The first sutra established that two of the four languages are renounced. This second sutra delivers the more subtle teaching: even within the permissible category of Satya, there are sentences that must not be spoken. Avaktavya satya — "the unspeakable truth" — is truth that, when uttered, causes pain, increases hatred, fuels passion, dishonours the worthy, or simply binds karma without serving any liberatory purpose. The classic example: "this man is one-eyed" — factually correct, but cruel; or "let us cut this tree for timber" — practically true, but it incites destruction of plant-bodied beings. The category buddhehim na ainna ("not practised by the awakened") is a catch-all: any sentence form that the Tirthankaras have not sanctioned in scripture is to be avoided, even if the speaker cannot articulate exactly why. This places the monk under the authority of tradition — speech is not to be invented case by case but learned from the lineage. The teaching is profound: truth itself does not justify speech. Speech is justified only when it is true and harmless and beneficial and sanctioned. Three of those four conditions can be satisfied by a sentence that nevertheless fails the fourth, and such a sentence must not be spoken.

Simply Put: A wise monk does not speak: (1) a truth that hurts, (2) a half-truth, (3) a lie, or (4) any sentence that the awakened teachers have not approved.

7.3

The Four Qualities of Permissible Speech

असच्चमोसं सच्चं च, अणवज्जमकक्कसं । समुप्पेहमसंदिद्धं, गिरं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

The wise monk should speak speech that is conventional (neither false nor mixed) or true, that is sinless, gentle, well-considered, and unambiguous.

This is the operational core of the chapter — the four qualities (chatur-guna) that every spoken sentence must possess. Anavadya (sinless): the words must not provoke a sinful action in anyone, must not wound any heart. Akarkasha (gentle): not sharp, not stinging — soft to the ear and warm to the heart. Samupreksha (well-considered): the monk thinks before speaking — "what gain or loss will follow? is this fit for me to say? will it hurt the listener?" Only after this internal review does the sentence emerge. Asandigdha (unambiguous): clear, definite, conveying knowledge without confusion or double meaning. These four qualities are like a sieve. Even within the two permissible categories (true and conventional), a sentence must pass through all four. If it is true but harsh — it fails. If it is conventional but ambiguous — it fails. Only the sentence that survives all four filters is fit for monastic utterance.

Simply Put: When the monk does speak, the words must be: true or conventional, harmless, gentle, thought-out beforehand, and absolutely clear.

7.4

The Unclear Mixed Speech is Forbidden

एयं च अट्ठमण्णं वा, जं तु णामेइ सासयं । स भासं सच्चमोसं च, तंपि धीरो विवज्जए

That (speech) which obscures this meaning or another, which conceals the eternal/conclusive sense — that mixed (truth-and-falsehood) speech also the steadfast (monk) should completely avoid.

This sutra targets a more sophisticated kind of impermissible speech: language that, though not outright false, fails to make its meaning clear. Two readings are possible: (1) speech whose terms are so ambiguous that the listener cannot reach a definite conclusion about the topic, and (2) speech that, even if locally true, contradicts or obscures the path of liberation taught by the Jinas. The classic example given by commentators: someone says "ashvattha hanyatam" — which can mean either "kill the ashvattha tree" or "Ashvattha (a man's name) should be killed." Such double-edged sentences create confusion in the listener and karmic risk for the speaker. The monk's speech must be straight-grained: one meaning, one direction, one outcome.

Simply Put: Speech whose meaning is unclear, or that confuses people about the path to liberation, must be completely avoided — even if some part of it is true.

7.5

Even Mistaken Speech Carries Sin

वितहं पि तहामुत्तिं, जं गिरं भासए नरो । तम्हा सो पुट्ठो पावेण, किं पुणं जो मुसं वए

The man who, mistakenly (taking one form for another), speaks of something that is otherwise as if it were so — even he is touched by sin. How much more, then, the one who speaks an outright lie?

This sutra introduces a striking principle: even unintentional falsehood carries karmic stain. The example: a woman dressed as a man passes by, and the monk, seeing the form, calls her "a man" — though the statement matches the visual evidence, it is factually false. The monk has been deceived by appearances, but the karmic consequence still attaches because false words have left the lips. If unintentional falsehood already binds karma, then deliberate falsehood (musavaada) binds it incomparably more. The verse uses the rhetorical kim punaṃ ("how much more, then?") to drive home the gravity. This teaching expands the monk's vigilance from "do not lie" to "do not speak with certainty about anything you have not verified" — the foundation of the next several sutras on doubt and the future.

Simply Put: Even speaking falsely by mistake — such as calling a disguised woman a man because of how she looks — earns sin. Outright lying earns vastly more.

7.6

Forbidden Speech About the Future

तम्हा गच्छामो वक्खामो, अमुगं वा णं भविस्सइ । अहं वा णं करिस्सामि, एसो वा णं करिस्सइ

(Therefore, the kind of speech such as:) "We will go," "We will speak/explain," "Such-and-such will happen," "I will do this," "He will do this" —

This sutra (paired with 7.7) presents a list of categorically definite future-tense sentences that the monk must not speak. The reason is not that these statements are currently false, but that the future is unknown — circumstances change, illness intervenes, karma matures, and the speaker may not be able to fulfil what was asserted with certainty. To speak the future as if it were guaranteed is to cross from speech-discipline into a kind of arrogance that pretends to omniscience. Only the Kevali (omniscient one) can speak the future with certainty. Every other being must hedge — "I intend to," "I will try to," "circumstances permitting" — because every other being is subject to change.

Simply Put: Saying things like "we will go," "I will do this," "he will do that" — flatly, without qualification — is forbidden because no one but the omniscient knows what tomorrow holds.

7.7

Doubtful Speech About Past, Present, or Future

एवमाइ उ जा भासा, एसकालम्मि संकिया । संपयाइयमट्ठे वा, तंपि धीरो विवज्जए

Such speech and the like, when uncertain regarding the future, or regarding present and past matters — the steadfast monk should also avoid.

The previous verse listed the future-tense forms; this verse extends the prohibition: any sentence asserting certainty about a matter of which the speaker is genuinely uncertain — whether that matter lies in the past, present, or future — must be avoided. The discipline is one of epistemic honesty: the monk speaks only what he knows. Where knowledge is partial, language must reflect the partiality.

Simply Put: Any definite statement about something uncertain — past, present, or future — must be avoided.

7.8

Do Not Assert What You Do Not Know

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए । जमट्ठं तु न जाणेज्जा, एवमेयंति णो वए

Regarding past, present, or future time — whatever matter the (monk) does not know, he should not say "this is so" about it.

This is the positive formulation of the previous prohibition. Not knowing is a perfectly honourable state for any being below the Kevali. The vice is not in not-knowing but in speaking as if one knows. The monk's tongue must mirror his actual cognition: certain when certain, silent or qualified when uncertain.

Simply Put: About anything in past, present, or future that you do not actually know — do not say "it is like this."

7.9

Where Doubt Exists, Do Not Assert

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए । जत्थ संका भवे तं तु, एवमेयंति णो वए

Regarding past, present, or future time — wherever there is doubt, the (monk) should not say "it is thus."

Where 7.8 addressed ignorance, 7.9 addresses doubt — a slightly different state, in which the monk has some information but not enough to commit. The rule is the same: do not assert. Even partial information, if conveyed as definite, becomes a form of falsehood when the underlying reality turns out otherwise.

Simply Put: Wherever the monk is in doubt — past, present, or future — he must not declare "it is so."

7.10

Where Certainty Exists, Speak Definitely

अईयम्मि य कालम्मि, पच्चुप्पण्णमणागए । निस्संकियं भवे जं तु, एवमेयं तु णिद्दिसे

Regarding past, present, or future time — whatever is free from doubt, that he should declare "it is thus."

The four-verse sequence (7.7–7.10) closes with the positive principle: speech-discipline is not silence in the face of all matters but precision matched to certainty. Where the monk truly knows — by direct experience, scriptural authority, or unambiguous perception — he should speak clearly and definitely. The faults of definite speech (listed in commentary): (1) violation of bhasha-samiti, (2) loss of the Jina's command, (3) loss of the layfolk's faith if a confident statement turns out wrong, (4) decline of devotion to dharma, (5) belittlement of the order. To avoid these, the monk distinguishes precisely between "I know" and "I am not sure," and lets his sentence-form follow. The chapter at this point also gives the practitioner a translation table of impermissible-into-permissible forms: instead of "I will go," say "I have the intention to go" or "I will try to go"; instead of "He will do this," say "He has the capacity to do this" or "He intends to do this." Speech becomes a mirror of inner knowledge rather than a projection of inner desire.

Simply Put: Where there is no doubt — past, present, or future — the monk may speak with definite certainty. Where there is doubt, he must hedge.

7.11

Even True Speech, If Harsh, Is Forbidden

तहेव फरुसा भासा, गुरुभूओवघाइणी । सच्चा वि सा न वत्तव्वा, जओ पावस्स आगमो

Likewise, harsh speech that gravely injures (other) beings — though true, it is not to be spoken, because from it sin enters (the soul).

This sutra explicitly drives home what 7.2 stated in principle: a true sentence is not automatically a permissible sentence. If the words are harsh — sharp-edged, cutting, designed or destined to wound — the truth-content does not redeem them. The reason is karmic: harsh speech is itself a sinful activity (savadya yoga), and the influx (asrava) of sin attaches the moment the words leave the mouth, regardless of factual accuracy. This is one of the most ethically demanding teachings in the chapter. The world frequently rewards "telling it like it is"; Jainism explicitly forbids it when "it" wounds.

Simply Put: A harsh sentence that wounds another being must not be spoken — even if it happens to be true. Sin attaches the moment such words are uttered.

7.12

Do Not Name People by Their Defects

तहेव काणं काणे त्ति, पंडगं पंडगे त्ति वा । वाहियं वावि रोगि त्ति, तेणं चोरे त्ति णो वए

Likewise, do not address the one-eyed as "one-eyed," the eunuch as "eunuch," the sick as "diseased," the thief as "thief."

A direct application of 7.11: each of these words is factually accurate but socially and personally wounding. The monk does not deny that the one-eyed person has one eye — he simply refuses to make that defect the form of address. To call a person "thief" turns a single past act into a permanent identity, and to call a person "diseased" reduces their humanity to their illness. The monk's mouth refuses to participate in such reduction.

Simply Put: Even if someone is one-eyed, infertile, sick, or has stolen — the monk must never address them by these labels.

7.13

The Wise Monk Knows the Faults of Speech

एएणण्णेण अट्ठेण, परो जेणुवहम्मइ । आयारभावदोसण्णू, न तं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

By any other meaning of this kind by which another (being) is hurt — the monk who knows the faults of conduct (in speech), who is wise, should not speak it.

This sutra closes the section on harsh and wounding speech with a generalization: not just the four labels listed (one-eyed, eunuch, sick, thief), but any sentence by any name that wounds another, the wise monk avoids. The two qualifying epithets — acharbhava-dosannu (knower of faults of speech-conduct) and pannava (wise) — establish that this discrimination is itself a developed faculty, learned through scripture, the guru's instruction, and constant self-watching. An ordinary intellect cannot make these distinctions; the monk must cultivate them.

Simply Put: Any other sentence — beyond the four listed — that hurts a being must equally be avoided by the wise monk.

7.14

Forbidden Insulting Forms of Address

तहेव होले गोले त्ति, साणे वा वसुले त्ति य । दमए दुहए वा वि, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

Likewise (do not address anyone as): "Fool!" "Bastard!" "Dog!" or "Outcaste!" "Wretch!" "Beggar!" — the wise monk should never speak so.

Having forbidden truthful-but-wounding labels in 7.12, the sutrakar now forbids derogatory forms of address that may not even be factually true — "fool," "bastard," "dog," etc. These are apabda (cuss-words, abusive epithets), the verbal equivalent of striking another being. They originate from kashaya (passion) and create more kashaya in both speaker and listener. The monk who has taken the great vow against violence cannot allow such words on his tongue.

Simply Put: The monk must never address anyone as fool, bastard, dog, outcaste, beggar, wretch, or any such insulting term.

7.15-16

Forbidden Familial Forms of Address to Women

अज्जिए पज्जिए वा वि, अम्मो माउस्सियत्ति य । पिउस्सिए भाइणेज्ज त्ति, धूए नत्तुणिए त्ति य हले हले त्ति अण्णे त्ति, भट्टे सामिणि गोमिणि । होले गोले वसुले त्ति, इत्थियं णेवमालवे

(Do not address a woman as): "Grandmother!" "Great-grandmother!" "Mother!" "Maternal aunt!" "Paternal aunt!" "Niece!" "Daughter!" "Granddaughter!" — nor as "Hey!" "Hey you!" "O dear one (of low birth)!" "Madam!" "Mistress!" "Cattle-keeper's wife!" — nor as "Fool!" "Bastard!" "Outcaste!" — the monk should not call a woman by such terms.

These two paired verses cover a wide range of forbidden modes of addressing women. The first verse (7.15) lists familial terms — "mother," "aunt," "daughter," "granddaughter" — which a household member would naturally use, but which the monk must not, because such usage signals worldly attachment, raga, and the persistence of family-bonds in a heart that has formally renounced them. The second verse (7.16) lists casual or commanding terms ("hey!", "madam," "mistress") and abusive terms ("fool," "bastard"). All are forbidden for different reasons: the casual terms are intimate and familiar in a way unbecoming of a celibate; the commanding terms presume authority over the listener; the abusive terms wound. The principle: the monk's address to any woman must be neither familiar (which suggests attachment), nor authoritative (which suggests hierarchy), nor abusive (which suggests passion). What remains is the neutral, name-or-gotra-based mode prescribed in the next verse.

Simply Put: The monk must not call a woman by family terms (mother, daughter, aunt), by casual or commanding terms (hey, madam, mistress), or by insults (fool, bastard, outcaste).

7.17

The Permitted Way to Address a Woman

नामधिज्जेण णं बूया, इत्थीगोत्तेण वा पुणो । जहारिहमभिगिज्झ, आलविज्ज लविज्ज वा

(The monk) should address (a woman) by her (personal) name, or by her gotra (family-lineage name), or — having considered (her) suitability appropriately — should call her once or call her repeatedly.

The positive prescription: when a monk must address a woman (e.g., during alms-collection or instruction), he should use her name or her family-lineage (gotra) name — neutral, respectful, free from familial attachment or social hierarchy. If neither is known, the jaharihamabhigijjha clause permits a context-appropriate form ("O lay-sister," "O Shravika," "O dharma-sheela") chosen with care. The address may be uttered once or multiple times depending on the practical need to draw attention.

Simply Put: The right way for a monk to address a woman is by her personal name or her family-lineage name, or — failing that — by some respectful, situation-appropriate term, used as often as needed.

7.18-19

Forbidden Familial and Insulting Forms of Address to Men

अज्जए पज्जए वा वि, बप्पो चुल्लपिउ त्ति य । माउला भाइणेज्ज त्ति, पुत्ते नत्तुणिय त्ति य हे हो हले त्ति अण्णे त्ति, भट्टा सामिय गोमिए । होल गोल वसुले त्ति, पुरिसं णेवमालवे

(Do not address a man as): "Grandfather!" "Great-grandfather!" "Father!" "Uncle!" "Maternal uncle!" "Nephew!" "Son!" "Grandson!" — nor as "Hey!" "O!" "Hey you!" "O dear boy!" "Sir!" "Master!" "Cattle-keeper!" — nor as "Fool!" "Wretch!" "Outcaste!" — the monk should not address a man so.

This pair mirrors 7.15-16 but for male addressees. The same threefold prohibition applies: no familial terms (which suggest attachment), no casual or commanding terms (which suggest familiarity or hierarchy), no abusive terms (which wound). The symmetry is deliberate — the monk's discipline of speech treats men and women alike, and the neutral mode prescribed in the next sutra (name or gotra) applies to both equally.

Simply Put: The monk must not call a man by family terms, casual or commanding terms, or insults — exactly as for a woman.

7.20

The Permitted Way to Address a Man

नामधिज्जेण णं बूया, पुरिसगोत्तेण वा पुणो । जहारिहमभिगिज्झ, आलविज्ज लविज्ज वा

(The monk) should address (a man) by his (personal) name, or by his gotra; having considered what is appropriate, should call him once, or repeatedly.

Parallel to 7.17. The permissible address for a man is by name, by lineage-name, or by a context-appropriate respectful form. The example given in commentary: when Gautama Swami met Devananda the Brahmin lady, who was overcome with motherly affection, he asked Bhagavan Mahavira about the cause — and Mahavira used the relational word "mama ammaya" ("she is my mother") to explain her past-life karmic bond. Such relational speech is permitted when it serves understanding, not attachment.

Simply Put: A monk addresses any man by his personal name, his lineage-name, or a respectful situation-appropriate form.

7.21

Speech About Sex and Pancendriya Beings

पंचिंदियाण पाणाणं, एस इत्थी अयं पुमं । जाव णं न वियाणेज्जा, ताव जाइ त्ति आलवे

Regarding five-sensed beings: until one definitely knows "this is female, this is male," until then one should speak only by reference to the species/type.

When the monk encounters an animal (or any five-sensed being other than a clearly identified human), he must not assign sex unless it is definitely known. To say "this dog is a female" or "this cow is male" without certainty is to assert what one does not know — the same fault as 7.8 and 7.9. The corrective: refer to the being by its species ("a dog," "a cow," "a bird") rather than guessing its sex. The principle applies equally to humans whose sex is not visually clear (e.g., a person in disguise, or seen at a distance).

Simply Put: About any five-sensed being whose sex you do not definitely know, just call it by its species-name — not "male" or "female."

7.22

Do Not Describe Animals as Fit for Slaughter

तहेव मणुस्सं पसुं, पक्खिं वा वि सरीसवं । थूले पमेइले वज्झे, पाइमे त्ति य णो वए

Likewise, seeing a human, a beast, a bird, or a reptile, (the monk) should not say "this is fat," "this is well-fleshed," "this is fit for killing," "this is fit for cooking."

This is one of the most ethically advanced teachings in the chapter. The monk does not directly kill the animal; but if his casual remark — "what a fat cow!" — falls on the ear of a butcher, the monk has supplied the butcher with motive. Speech that enables violence is itself a form of violence. The monk's mouth must not become an instrument by which slaughter is suggested, even unintentionally. The four words listed escalate in complicity: "fat" (an admiring observation), "well-fleshed" (an evaluation in food-terms), "fit to kill" (an explicit endorsement), "fit to cook" (anticipation of the killed product). All four are forbidden. The monk who sees a fat cow either says nothing or speaks of the animal in some other respect entirely.

Simply Put: When the monk sees any animal, he must not say "it is fat," "it has good meat," "it is fit to kill," or "it is fit to cook" — such words can incite violence even when the speaker does not intend it.

7.23

Forbidden Speech About Mature Animals

परिवुड्ढे त्ति णं बूया, बूया उवचिए त्ति य । संजाए पीणिए वा वि, महाकाए त्ति आलवे

(Of an animal) one should say "fully grown" / "well developed" / "mature" / "well nourished" / "of large body" (rather than the slaughter-suggesting terms forbidden in the previous verse).

This is the positive complement to 7.22. The monk may describe the animal — these words ("fully grown," "well-nourished," "of large body") are factually similar to the forbidden ones, but they are framed in terms of the animal's own well-being and natural development rather than in terms of its usefulness to a killer or cook. The discrimination is fine but real: "fat" implies food; "well-grown" implies a being that has flourished. Same animal, two different worlds.

Simply Put: When the monk does describe an animal, he should use words like "fully grown," "well-developed," "mature," "well-nourished," or "of large body" — words about the animal's own wellbeing, not about its meat-value.

7.24

Do Not Describe Cattle as Useful for Labour

तहेव गाओ दुज्झाओ, दम्मा गोरहग त्ति य । वाहिमा रहजोगि त्ति, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

Likewise, "this cow is fit for milking," "these are bullocks fit to be tamed," "this is fit for carrying loads," "this is fit to be yoked to a chariot" — the wise monk should not so speak.

The same principle as 7.22, applied to draught animals. To say "this cow is good for milking" or "this bull can pull a cart" sounds harmless, but it converts the animal into a tool, and supplies prospective users with the recommendation. The monk neither owns animals nor uses them — and his speech must reflect that distance from the economy of exploitation. Such evaluative language is savadya bhasha (sin-bearing speech), and the wise monk avoids it.

Simply Put: The monk must not say "this cow gives good milk," "these bulls can be trained," "this animal can carry loads," "this one can pull a chariot" — such words make the animal into a tool.

7.25

Permitted Descriptions of Cattle

जुवं गवे त्ति णं बूया, धेणुं रसदय त्ति य । रहस्से महल्लए वा वि, वए संवहणे त्ति य

(Of cattle) one should say "this is a young bullock," "this is a milch-cow / juice-yielding cow," "this is small," "this is large," "this is mature," "this is sturdy."

The positive complement to 7.24. The monk may describe cattle by their natural state — young, mature, large, small, sturdy — but not by their utility-to-humans. The line is fine but consequential: "this cow gives good milk" speaks from the dairy-farmer's frame; "this is a milch-cow" is closer to the cow's own form. The Gujarati commentary stresses: the meaning of these neutral words must be understood from the explanations already given for the previous sutras.

Simply Put: The monk may say of cattle: "this is a young bull," "this is a milch-cow," "this is small / large / mature / sturdy" — words about their nature, not their usefulness.

7.26-29

Forbidden Speech About Trees

तहेव गंतुमुज्जाणं, पव्वयाणि वणाणि य । रुक्खा महल्ल पेहाए, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं अलं पासायखंभाणं, तोरणाणं गिहाण य । फलिहग्गलणावाणं, अलं उदगदोणिणं पीढए चंगबेरे य, णंगले मइयं सिया । जंतलट्ठी व नाभी वा, गंडिया व अलं सिया आसणं सयणं जाणं, होज्जा वा किंचुवस्सए । भूओवघाइणिं भासं, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

Likewise, having gone to a garden, mountains, or forests and seen great trees, the wise monk should not speak (thus): "(These are) fit for palace pillars, fit for arches and houses, fit for door-bolts and boats, fit for water-troughs; fit for stools, basins, plough-handles or harrows; fit for press-poles, axles, anvils — fit for seats, beds, vehicles, or any (need) of the lodging." Such being-injurious speech the wise monk should not speak.

This four-verse sequence (7.26-29) covers a single theme: the monk's response to trees. Walking through forests and gardens, he will inevitably see grand trees. The temptation is to admire them in functional terms — "what fine timber that would make!" The sutrakar prohibits this entirely. To say "this tree would make a great pillar" is verbal enabling of arboricide — even if the monk himself never lifts an axe, his words supply the woodcutter with motive. The list (palace pillars, arches, doors, boats, water-troughs, stools, basins, ploughs, harrows, mill-poles, axles, anvils, seats, beds, vehicles, monastic-quarter fittings) is exhaustive precisely to forestall every category of utilitarian appraisal. Bhutopaghatini bhasha — "speech that injures beings" — is the technical name for this category, because plants are ekendriya jiva (one-sensed beings) whose lives are extinguished by every felling. The monk's tongue must not become an instrument of even indirect violence to them.

Simply Put: When the monk sees great trees in gardens, mountains, or forests, he must never describe them in terms of what they could be turned into — pillars, beams, doors, boats, troughs, stools, ploughs, axles, anvils, seats, beds, vehicles — because such talk encourages their destruction.

7.30-31

Permitted Speech About Trees

तहेव गंतुमुज्जाणं, पव्वयाणि वणाणि य । रुक्खा महल्ल पेहाए, एवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं जाइमंता इमे रुक्खा, दीहवट्टा महालया । पयायसाला विडिमा, वए दरिसणि त्ति य

Likewise, having gone to a garden, mountains, or forests and seen great trees, the wise monk should speak (thus): "These trees are of noble species, tall and rounded, large; spreading-branched, full of small twigs, beautiful to behold."

The positive form: when the monk must speak of trees, he describes them in their own glory — noble species, tall, round, vast, spreading, branching, beautiful. This vocabulary praises the tree as a tree, not as potential lumber. The discrimination preserves the tree's dignity in language and removes any incitement to harm.

Simply Put: When the monk does describe trees, he may say: "noble species," "tall and round," "great," "spreading-branched," "thick with twigs," "beautiful to behold."

7.32

Forbidden Speech About Fruits

तहा फलाइं पक्काइं, पायखज्जाइं णो वए । वेलोइयाइं टालाइं, वेहिमाइत्ति णो वए

Likewise, (the monk) should not say of fruits: "they are ripe," "they are fit to be cooked and eaten," "they are over-ripe (and so fit to be plucked)," "they are tender (without hard pits)," "they are fit to be cut in two / fit to be processed."

The same principle as the trees, applied to their fruits. To pronounce "these mangoes are ripe" or "these are ready to be cooked" is to invite plucking and processing — and plucking a fruit is itself violence (even if the fruit is destined to fall, the act of plucking ends the fruit's natural completion). The monk avoids all evaluative language that would supply such motive.

Simply Put: The monk should not say of fruits: "they are ripe," "fit to cook and eat," "over-ripe so pluck them," "tender (no stone)," or "fit to cut and process."

7.33

Permitted Speech About Fruits

असंथडा इमे अंबा, बहुणिव्वडिमा फला । वएज्ज बहुसंभूया, भूयरूव त्ति वा पुणो

(One should say:) "These mango-trees are unable (to bear their load), the fruits are clustered in many bunches" — should say "they are abundantly fruited," "they are beautiful in many forms."

Positive form: the monk may admire the fruit-tree as a flourishing being — "these mangoes are so heavy with fruit the branches cannot bear it"; "the fruit grows in abundance"; "they look beautiful." The tree is praised for its own being, not for what can be taken from it.

Simply Put: The monk may say: "these mango-trees are bowed under their weight," "the fruits hang in many clusters," "they are abundantly fruited," "they look beautiful."

7.34

Forbidden Speech About Crops

तहेवोसहिओ पक्काओ, णीलियाओ छवीइय । लाइमा भज्जिमाओ त्ति, पिहुखज्ज त्ति णो वए

Likewise, (of grains and crops) the monk should not say: "they are ripe," "they are green / soft-skinned," "they are fit to be reaped," "they are fit to be roasted in a pan," "they are fit to be parched (and eaten as poha)."

The same teaching applied to grains and crops. Each phrase forbidden is one that would prompt the householder to begin a violent activity — reaping, threshing, parching, processing — that destroys the lives in the grain (which are ekendriya jiva) and the many small lives caught up in agricultural work. The monk's speech must remain neutral.

Simply Put: The monk should not say of crops: "they are ripe," "still green," "in pod," "fit to harvest," "fit to roast," "fit to parch into poha."

7.35

Permitted Speech About Crops

रूढा बहुसंभूया, थिरा ऊसढा वि य । गब्भियाओ पसूयाओ, ससाराओ त्ति आलवे

(The monk) may say: "they have sprouted," "they are abundantly grown," "they have become firm," "they have risen up," "they are bearing within (in seed)," "they have brought forth," "they have come to fruition (full-grained)."

This is one of the most botanically precise verses in the chapter. The sutrakar gives the monk a seven-stage vocabulary for the natural development of a crop, all of which is permissible because none of it suggests harvest or use. Each word names a phase of the plant's own life-process, not a phase of human exploitation. The monk who must speak of crops uses these terms.

Simply Put: The monk may describe crops in seven natural stages: just sprouted, well-grown, firm, risen up, bearing grain within, having brought forth grain, full-grained — all words about the plant's own development.

7.36

Do Not Praise Feasts, Slaughter, or River-Crossings

तहेव संखडिं णच्चा, किच्चं कज्जं ति णो वए । तेणगं वावि वज्झे त्ति, सुतित्थ त्ति य आवगा

Likewise, knowing of a feast (sankhadi), the monk should not say "this is a worthy / proper / meritorious act"; nor of a (caught) thief should he say "he is fit to be killed"; nor of a river should he say "this is a good ford."

Three diverse cases joined by a single principle: the monk's praise creates karmic complicity. (1) A sankhadi (mass feast) involves great violence — large-scale cooking, milling of grain, killing of countless small lives in fire and water-use. To praise it is to endorse the violence. (2) Of a captured thief, to say "he deserves to die" is to endorse capital violence. (3) Of a river, to say "this is a beautiful ford to cross" is to encourage water-borne lives' disturbance and to endorse activity (crossing) that may not be necessary. The monk's silence around these matters is not callousness — it is refusal to lend the moral weight of his words to acts of violence, however socially normalized.

Simply Put: The monk must not call a feast "a worthy event," a thief "fit to be killed," or a river "a fine crossing point" — each such phrase fuels violence.

7.37

Permitted Neutral Speech in Such Cases

संखडिं संखडिं बूया, पणियट्ठ त्ति तेणगं । बहुसमाणि तित्थाणि, आवगाणं वियागरे

(If he must speak,) the monk should call a feast "a feast," a thief "one who has staked his life (for goods)"; should describe river-banks as "very level."

The neutral substitutes given here preserve communicability without endorsement. Sankhadi called sankhadi — a flat description, no praise. The thief is named not by the moral verdict ("villain," "deserves death") but by the existential fact — paniyattha, "one who has wagered his life for goods" — which is more compassionate, more accurate, and morally non-prescriptive. The river-bank is called "very level" — descriptive, without inviting or recommending the crossing.

Simply Put: If the monk must mention these things, he should call a feast simply "a feast," a thief "one who staked his life for goods," and a river-bank "very level" — neutral words without judgment.

7.38

Do Not Speak Casually of Rivers

तहा णईओ पुण्णाओ, कायतिज्ज त्ति णो वए । णावाहिं तारिमाओ त्ति, पाणिपेज्ज त्ति णो वए

Likewise, of rivers (the monk) should not say: "they are full," "they can be crossed by the body / swimming," "they can be crossed by boat," "their water can be drunk from the bank."

Each of these four phrases is in itself accurate, but each contains an implicit endorsement of an activity that injures water-bodied beings (apkaya jiva). To say "this river is full" prompts crossing; to say "swimmable" or "boat-crossable" actively recommends the means; to say "drinkable from the bank" recommends consuming raw, sentient water. The monk's tongue must not give any of these recommendations.

Simply Put: The monk must not say of rivers: "they are full," "swimmable," "boat-crossable," or "the water can be drunk from the bank."

7.39

Permitted Neutral Speech About Rivers

बहु वाहडा अगाहा, बहुसलिलुप्पिलोदगा । बहुवित्थडोदगा यावि, एवं भासेज्ज पण्णवं

The wise monk may speak (of rivers thus): "they have abundant water," "they are deep / unfathomable," "their waters are spreading / flowing forcefully," "they have widely-spread waters."

The monk's permitted vocabulary describes the river as it is, in its own grandeur — full, deep, forceful, broad — without prompting any human action upon it. The river is named as a being, not as a route or a beverage.

Simply Put: The monk may say of rivers: "they bear much water," "they are deep," "their waters surge across the banks," "they spread widely" — descriptions of the river itself, with no recommendation.

7.40

Do Not Endorse Sinful Activities

तहेव सावज्जं जोगं, परस्सट्ठाए णिट्ठियं । कीरमाणं ति वा णच्चा, सावज्जं ण लवे मुणी

Likewise, knowing that a sinful activity has been completed for another's sake, or is being performed (now), the monk should not utter sin-bearing speech (about it).

The principle generalized: any sinful act — completed for someone, currently being done, or planned — must not draw approving speech from the monk. Approval (anumodana) is one of the three modes by which karma binds (the other two being doing and causing-to-be-done). The monk who himself does not act and does not commission still binds karma if his words endorse what others have done.

Simply Put: About any sinful activity that has been completed, is being done, or will be done by householders — the monk must not utter approving words.

7.41

Do Not Praise Cooked Food

सुकडे त्ति सुपक्के त्ति, सुच्छिण्णे सुहडे मडे । सुणिट्ठिए सुलट्ठे त्ति, सावज्जं वज्जए मुणी

"Well-prepared!" "Well-cooked!" "Well-trimmed!" "Well-de-bittered!" "Well-seasoned with ghee!" "Well-finished!" "Wonderfully delicious!" — such sin-bearing speech the monk shall avoid.

A direct application of 7.40 to food. The household has just prepared a meal involving fire-violence, water-violence, vegetable-violence, and (sometimes) cooking-of-living-jivas. To praise the result — "what wonderful cooking!" — is to endorse the violence. The monk who comes for alms must not become the food-critic of the kitchen. He receives, eats with detachment, and is silent on the merits of the preparation.

Simply Put: About food, the monk must not say: "well-prepared," "well-cooked," "well-cut," "the bitterness is well removed," "well-seasoned with ghee," "made by perfect method," "absolutely delicious." Such praise endorses violence.

7.42

Speak of Acts as Effortful, Not as Praiseworthy

पयत्तपक्क त्ति व पक्कमालवे, पयत्तछिण्ण त्ति व छिण्णमालवे । पयत्तलट्ठि त्ति व कम्महेउयं, पहारगाढ त्ति व गाढमालवे

Of a cooked thing, (one may) say "it has been cooked with effort"; of a cut thing, say "it has been cut with effort"; of a karma-binding action (or skilfully made object), say it has been "done with effort"; of a heavy blow (struck on someone), say "a forceful blow was inflicted."

The positive complement to 7.41. When the monk must mention an act that involved violence (cooking, cutting, striking), he frames it as effortful rather than excellent. The phrase "cooked with effort" acknowledges the labour without praising the result; the phrase "a forceful blow was struck" describes the violence factually, without taking the side of either striker or struck. The monk neither approves nor condemns; he merely describes — without raga (attachment) or dvesha (aversion).

Simply Put: If the monk must speak of cooked, cut, or made things, he should say "made with effort" — not "made well." Of a violent blow, he should say "a forceful blow was struck" — without picking sides.

7.43

Do Not Use Superlatives or Inexpressibles

सव्वुक्कसं परग्घं वा, अउलं णत्थि एरिसं । अचक्कियमवत्तव्वं, अचिंतं चेव णो वए

"This is the very best of all," "this is most precious," "incomparable, nothing like it exists," "indescribable," "unutterable," "unthinkable" — (such words) the monk should not say.

This sutra forbids hyperbole and superlatives. The reasons: (1) such language is rarely literally true — most "best in the world" claims are exaggerations and therefore musavaada; (2) such language inflates the value of worldly things and stokes attachment in the listener; (3) such absolute language is reserved (in Jain teaching) for the Tirthankaras, the Siddhas, and liberated states — to apply it to mundane objects is a kind of sacrilege. The monk's speech remains measured, precise, and free of inflation.

Simply Put: The monk must not say: "this is the very best," "the most precious," "unmatched," "indescribable," "unutterable," "unthinkable" — such inflated speech is forbidden.

7.44

Do Not Promise to Convey Every Word

सव्वमेयं वइस्सामि, सव्वमेयं ति णो वए । अणुवीइ सव्वं सव्वत्थ, एवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

"I will tell (your) entire (message)," "this is exactly all" — (the monk) should not say so. The wise monk should speak everything, everywhere, only after due reflection (anuvichi).

If asked to carry a message, the monk must not promise "I'll tell them everything you said" or "this is the complete message" — because he cannot guarantee that he will remember every word, that the receiver will be available, that no detail will be omitted. The promise itself becomes a falsehood the moment any portion is forgotten or modified. Instead, the monk uses the word "all" (when he must) only after careful prior reflection on what is truly conveyable.

Simply Put: The monk must not promise "I'll tell them everything" or "this is exactly all" — speech that uses "all" must be preceded by careful consideration.

7.45

Do Not Speak the Language of Commerce

सुक्कीयं वा सुविक्कीयं, अकिज्जं किज्जमेव वा । इमं गिण्ह इमं मुंच, पणियं णो वियागरे

"You bought this well," "you sold this well," "this is not fit to do," "this is fit to do," "take this," "give this up" — (the monk) should not so speak in matters of commerce.

The monk has renounced trade and possessions. Therefore his speech must not enter the commercial frame at all. To advise "you bought well," "sell this," "buy that" is to enter the very domain he has renounced — and to give such advice is to participate karmically in the gains and losses, the joys and sorrows, of the transaction. The monk speaks neither praise nor blame nor recommendation in matters of buying, selling, owning.

Simply Put: The monk must not say in trade matters: "you bought well," "you sold well," "this deal is not good," "this is a good deal," "buy this," "sell this." All commercial speech is renounced.

7.46

When Speech About Trade is Necessary, Speak Without Endorsement

अप्पग्घे वा महग्घे वा, कए वा विक्कए वि वा । पणियट्ठे समुप्पण्णे, अणवज्जं वियागरे

In matters of low-priced or high-priced (goods), buying or selling — when (specific) commercial necessity arises, (the monk) should speak (only) sinless (descriptive) words.

A practical concession: there are circumstances where a monk may need to describe a transaction (for instance, when explaining a dharmic point about wealth, or in the limited domain of monastic alms-economics). In such cases, the monk speaks descriptively — "this is low-priced," "this is high-priced," "this was bought," "this was sold" — without adding the evaluative endorsement forbidden in 7.45. The line: factual reference is permitted; commercial recommendation is not.

Simply Put: If the monk genuinely must mention a transaction, he may describe it neutrally — "this is cheap," "this is expensive," "this was bought," "this was sold" — without saying it was "good" or "bad."

7.47

Do Not Issue Commands to Householders

तहेवासंजय धीरो, आस एहि करेहि वा । सयं चिट्ठ वयाहि त्ति, णेवं भासिज्ज पण्णवं

Likewise, the steadfast wise (monk) should not say to an unrestrained (householder): "sit here," "come here," "do this," "lie down," "stand," "go (there)."

This sutra extends the monk's discipline to the imperative mood itself. To command the householder — "sit," "come," "do," "go" — is to enter into the householder's domain of activity. The householder's actions are governed by asanyam (lack of restraint) and inevitably involve subtle violence; for the monk to direct those actions is to become karmically responsible for them, even if the householder's act was already going to happen. The Gujarati commentary suggests permissible alternatives: rather than "sit here," say "you may sit nearby if you wish, no need to stand"; rather than "come back later," say "we are at our alms-time now, please come at a more convenient hour for both of us." The principle: the monk does not command — he describes circumstances and lets the householder choose.

Simply Put: The monk must not order householders: "sit here," "come here," "do this," "lie down," "stand," "go there." Such commanding speech makes the monk responsible for the household activity.

7.48

Do Not Call the Unworthy "Sadhu"

बहवे इमे असाहू, लोए वुच्चंति साहुणो । ण लवे असाहुं साहुत्ति, साहुं साहुत्ति आलवे

Many in this world who are not (true) sadhus are called sadhus. (The monk) should not call a non-sadhu "sadhu"; he should call a (true) sadhu "sadhu."

A precise teaching about religious titles. The world is loose with the word "sadhu" — applying it to anyone in robes, anyone who claims spiritual authority, anyone with a following. The monk must not be loose: to call an unworthy person "sadhu" is itself a form of falsehood (musavaada), because it asserts qualities that are not present. It also misleads the laity into giving devotion where devotion is not warranted, weakening the community's discrimination. The next sutra (7.49) gives the four marks by which a true sadhu is identified.

Simply Put: The world calls many people "sadhu" who are not really sadhus. The monk must not extend that label to the unworthy — he calls a true sadhu "sadhu" only.

7.49

The Four Marks of a True Sadhu

णाणदंसणसंपण्णं, संजमे य तवे रयं । एवं गुणसमाउत्तं, संजयं साहुमालवे

(One who is) endowed with right knowledge and right perception, and devoted to self-restraint and austerity — being thus equipped with (these four) qualities, the (true) self-restrained one — call him "sadhu."

The four-fold criterion is precise: samyag-jnana + samyag-darshana + samyam + tapas. This is the classical definition of the path to liberation (the same four named in the Tattvartha Sutra's samyag-darshana-jnana-charitra-tapasi marga). Where these four are present, "sadhu" is a true name; where they are absent, "sadhu" is a lie. The commentary notes a subtlety: the monk's discipline is more strict toward Jain monks (who must be evaluated by these four qualities) than toward non-Jain ascetics (where conventional forms of address may apply, since the monk is not bound to recognize Jain vows in non-Jain contexts). But within the Jain order, no slack is permitted. A Jain monk who neglects loch (hair-pulling), pratikraman (daily confession), restricted travel, or proper monastic dress, has crossed the line and should not be called sadhu — silence is the right response in such cases.

Simply Put: A true sadhu has four marks: right knowledge, right perception, devotion to self-restraint, and devotion to austerity. Call only such a one "sadhu."

7.50

Do Not Predict Outcomes of Wars

देवाणं मणुयाणं च, तिरियाणं च वुग्गहे । अमुगाणं जओ होउ, मा वा होउ त्ति णो वए

In conflicts among gods, humans, or animals — "may such-and-such party win," "may such-and-such party not win" — (the monk) should not say so.

Wars and conflicts are sites of enormous violence. To wish either side victory is to endorse the violence on that side; to wish either side defeat is to endorse the violence done to them. The monk's tongue stays out of partisan conflict entirely. Beyond that, predicting outcomes is forbidden by the same logic as 7.6-7.10: only the omniscient knows who will win. To predict and be wrong is to lose credibility for the dharma; to predict and be right is to nourish the speaker's pride and the laity's superstition.

Simply Put: In any conflict — among gods, humans, or animals — the monk must not say "may this side win" or "may this side lose." No partisan or predictive speech.

7.51

Do Not Predict Weather, Health, or Prosperity

वाओ वुटुं च सीउण्हं, खेमं धायं सिवं ति वा । कयाणु हुज्ज एयाणि, मा वा होउ त्ति णो वए

"When will (favourable) wind, rain, cool, heat, peace, abundant harvest, prosperity arise?" or "may they not arise" — (the monk) should not say so.

The monk does not predict weather, public health, harvest, or general welfare. The reason is twofold: (1) such predictions are speculative — only the omniscient knows; if the prediction proves false, the layperson loses faith in the monk and in the dharma; (2) such talk is useless prattle (nirarthak pravartan) — speaking of natural phenomena does not change them, and the speaker accumulates either complacency (if right) or shame (if wrong) without spiritual gain. The monk's silence on such matters preserves both his dignity and his focus.

Simply Put: The monk must not predict or wish about weather, public health, harvests, peace, or prosperity — neither "may these come" nor "may these not come."

7.52

Do Not Deify Clouds, Sky, or People

तहेव मेहं व नहं व माणवं, ण देव देव त्ति गिरं वएज्जा । संमुच्छिए उण्णए वा पओए, वएज्ज वा वुट्टे बलाहए त्ति

Likewise, of a cloud, the sky, or a (great) human, the monk should not utter speech (calling them) "god, god." (Of a cloud one) may say "the cloud has gathered," "it has risen high," or "this cloud-mass has rained."

A precise theological teaching. Ordinary religion deifies natural phenomena — clouds become rain-gods, sky becomes sky-god, charismatic people become incarnations. Jain doctrine refuses all such deification: clouds are pudgala (matter), the sky is ajiva-dravya (a non-living substance), and even the most miraculous human is still a samsari jiva bound to embodiment. To call any of them "god" is mithyatva (false belief) and musavaada (falsehood) at once. The monk speaks of clouds in their actual physics: gathered, risen, raining. The wonder remains; the false reverence is removed.

Simply Put: The monk must not call a cloud, the sky, or any extraordinary human "god." Of a cloud, he may simply say "it has gathered," "it has risen high," "this cloud has rained."

7.53

Permitted Speech About Sky and Powerful People

अंतलिक्खे त्ति णं बूया, गुज्झाणुचरियत्ति य । रिद्धिमंतं णरं दिस्स, रिद्धिमंतं ति आलवे

(Of the sky) one should say "antariksha" or "the path traversed by the celestials"; seeing a person of (worldly) prosperity, one should say "(this is) a prosperous person."

The neutral, non-deifying vocabulary. The sky is antariksha — the in-between space — or "the path by which the unseen devas travel" (a poetic but factually neutral description). The wealthy or powerful person is riddhimanta — "endowed with worldly prosperity" — a description of what they have, not a deification of what they are. The monk preserves accuracy and refuses inflation simultaneously.

Simply Put: The monk may call the sky "antariksha" or "the path the devas travel"; a wealthy person, simply "wealthy / endowed."

7.54

Closing Instruction: Avoid All Sin-Endorsing or Wounding Speech

तहेव सावज्जणुमोयणी गिरा, ओहारिणी जा य परोवघाइणी । से कोह लोह भयसा व माणवो, ण हासमाणो वि गिरं वइज्जा

Likewise, the speech that endorses sin, that asserts certainty (where there is none), that injures another being — the (wise) man should not utter it under (the influence of) anger, greed, fear, or pride; not even while jesting.

This is the great summary verse. Three categories of speech are banned in absolute terms: (1) savadya-anumodani — that which endorses sin; (2) avadharini / oharini — that which asserts unwarranted certainty; (3) parovaghatini — that which wounds others. And four inner states are named as the roots from which such speech most often springs: krodha (anger), lobha (greed), bhaya (fear), mana (pride). Plus a fifth — hasamana (jest, joking) — which the monk often imagines is harmless but which is included here because even in jest, sin-endorsing or wounding speech accumulates karma.

Simply Put: The monk must never utter speech that endorses sin, asserts false certainty, or wounds others — not under anger, greed, fear, pride, or even in jest.

7.55

The Reward of Pure Speech

सुवक्कसुद्धिं समुपेहिया मुणी, गिरं च दुट्ठं परिवज्जए सया । मियं अदुट्ठं अणुवीइ भासए, सयाण मज्झे लहइ पसंसणं

The monk who has well examined the purification of well-spoken words, who always avoids corrupted speech, who speaks measured (limited), uncorrupted speech with due reflection — finds praise among the company of the noble.

The first of three closing summary verses. The monk who has actually internalized the chapter's teaching — who has examined the purity of his own speech, who consistently rejects faulty utterance, and who speaks only measured, uncorrupted, well-considered words — receives the natural reward: praise among the noble. This is not mere social standing; it is the recognition by the spiritually mature that this monk has achieved one of the rarest of disciplines. To be praised by the wise is itself a confirmation that the path is bearing fruit.

Simply Put: The monk who studies the purity of speech, avoids faulty speech, and speaks only measured, clean, well-considered words — earns praise among the truly noble.

7.56

The Discipline of the Six Restraints

भासाइ दोसे य गुणे य जाणिया, तीसे य दुट्ठे परिवज्जए सया । छसु संजए सामणिए सया जए, वएज्ज बुद्धे हियमणुलोमियं

Knowing well the faults and virtues of speech, always rejecting its corruptions, restrained in the (protection of) the six (kinds of life-bodies), always vigilant in the monastic state — the awakened (monk) should speak that which is beneficial and pleasing (to all beings).

The second summary verse expands the principle to the full monastic life. Speech-purity is not an isolated discipline; it sits inside chasu samjae (the six-fold restraint, protecting the six kinds of jivas) and samaniye saya jae (constant vigilance in the monastic state). The monk who has all three layers — knowing faults and virtues of speech, rejecting all faulty speech, and being constantly restrained in the larger monastic discipline — is qualified to speak. And what he speaks is hita (beneficial) and anulomik (agreeable, in harmony with the listener) — words that genuinely help and that fall pleasingly on the ear.

Simply Put: The awakened monk — knowing speech's faults and virtues, always avoiding corrupted speech, restrained toward all six kinds of beings, vigilant in monastic life — should speak only what is beneficial and agreeable to all.

7.57

Closing Verse: The Fruit of the Discipline (||ti bemi||)

परिक्खभासी सुसमाहिइंदिए, चउक्कसायावगए अणिस्सए । स णिद्धुणे धुण्णमलं पुरेकडं, आराहए लोगमिणं तहा परं ॥ ॥त्ति बेमि॥

The (monk) who speaks only after examination, whose senses are well-controlled, who has overcome the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed), who is unattached — he shakes off the karmic dust accumulated from previous (deeds), and accomplishes (the welfare of) this world and the next. — Thus do I declare.

The closing verse names four qualities that, taken together, complete the discipline of speech: (1) parikshabhashi — speaks only after careful examination; (2) susamahit-indriya — has all five senses under control; (3) chatur-kashaya-apagata — has overcome anger, pride, deceit, and greed; (4) anissaya — is unattached, free of worldly support and dependence. When these four are present, the monk's speech itself becomes a force of purification: it shakes off the accumulated karma-dust of past deeds, and accomplishes welfare in both this world and the next. The closing ti bemi ("thus I declare") is the canonical signature of Sudharma Swami, the fifth Gandhara of Bhagavan Mahavira. Through him the teaching reached his disciple Jambu Swami, and from there to all subsequent ages. The phrase certifies that this entire chapter is not the composition of the editor but the direct teaching of the Tirthankara. The chapter's deepest message: pure speech is not a mere social courtesy or even a moral discipline — it is a karma-shaking practice. Every sentence the monk would have spoken in fault is a karmic bond that does not form; every sentence spoken in care is a small act of liberation. The fruit, named here as loga imanam taha param ("this world and the next"), is the sequence of progressively purer rebirths that culminates in the final liberation.

Simply Put: The monk who speaks only after careful thought, whose senses are calm, who has conquered the four inner passions, and who is free from worldly attachment — shakes off the karmic dust of his past deeds, and wins the welfare of this life and the next. Thus I declare.
