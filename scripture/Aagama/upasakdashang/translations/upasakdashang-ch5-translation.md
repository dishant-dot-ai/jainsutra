# Upasakdashang Sutra — Chapter 5
# Shramanopasak Chullashatak (શ્રમણોપાસક ચુલ્લશતક)

**Chapter 5 of the Upasakdashang Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Upasakdashang Sutra, printed pages 108–112 (PDF pages 170–174)*
*Total Sutras: 6*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

This is the fifth chapter of the Upasakdashang Sutra, titled **Chullashatak** — the story of the lay devotee (shramanopasak) Chullashatak. Chullashatak was a householder of Aalibhika city in northern India, a prosperous and influential man. His wife's name was Bahula. He possessed six crore gold coins in each of three categories (deposits, revenue, trade), six types of livestock, and large cattle herds. He lived in comfort and prosperity, leading a virtuous householder life.

Chapter 5 continues the escalating pattern of the Upasakdashang's middle chapters. Each of the three sons is now cut into seven pieces each (three in Ch3, five in Ch4, seven in Ch5), continuing the systematic progression. But the novel element in Chapter 5 is entirely different from Chapters 3 and 4: after killing the sons, the deva does not threaten the mother (Ch3) or the body with disease (Ch4), but threatens to take all of Chullashatak's wealth — his entire fortune of 18 crore gold coins and all his material possessions — and scatter them at every crossroads, intersection, and major road of Aalibhika city.

This threat tests attachment to wealth. The three previous chapters tested attachment to family (sons, mother) and to the body. Chapter 5 tests attachment to material possessions — arguably the most common form of worldly attachment among householders, and perhaps the most socially defining one in a culture where wealth equaled status, security, and social standing.

Chullashatak endures all, briefly experiences the same thought of seizing the deva, his wife Bahula guides him to atonement exactly as Dhanya guided Suradeva, and he completes the full arc of lay practice: twenty years, eleven pratimas, sallekhana, and rebirth in the Saudharma heaven in the "Arunsiddha" celestial vehicle with a lifespan of four paliopamas. Liberation follows in Mahavideha.

## SUTRA 5.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उक्खेवो पंचमस्स अज्झयणस्स । एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं आलिभिया णामं णयरी । संखवणे उज्जाणे । जियसत्तू राया । चुल्लसयए गाहावई अट्टे जाव छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ, छ वुड्डिपउत्ताओ, छ पवित्थरपउत्ताओ, छ वया, दस गो-साहसिसएणं वएणं । बहुला भारिया ।

सामी समोसढे । जहा आणंदो तहा गिहिधम्मं पडिवज्जइ । सेसं जहा कामदेवो जाव धम्म पणत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्तांणं विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ukkhevo paṃcamassa ajjhayanassa | evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ ālibhiyā ṇāmaṃ ṇayarī | saṃkhavaṇe ujjāṇe | jiyasattū rāyā | cullasayae gāhāvaī aṭṭe jāva cha hiraṇṇakoḍīo ṇihāṇapauттāo, cha vuḍḍipauттāo, cha pavitthara-pauттāo, cha vayā, dasa go-sāhasisaeṇaṃ vaeṇaṃ | bahulā bhāriyā |

sāmī samosaḍhe | jahā āṇaṃdo tahā gihidhammaṃ paḍivajjai | sesaṃ jahā kāmadevo jāva dhamma paṇattiṃ uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Beginning of the fifth chapter. Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that period, there was a city named Aalibhika. The garden was called Shankhavan. King Jiyasattu ruled. Chullashatak was a householder, wealthy — up to: six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade goods, six types of livestock, and wealth of ten thousand cattle herds. Bahula was his wife.

The Lord (Mahavira) arrived. Just as Ananda accepted the householder's dharma, so did he. The rest was the same as with Kamadev — up to: living in accordance with the dharma teaching.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उक्खेवो पंचमस्स** = beginning of the fifth (chapter)
- **आलिभिया णामं णयरी** = a city named Aalibhika (distinct from Varanasi — northern India, separate setting for Ch5)
- **संखवणे उज्जाणे** = the garden called Shankhavan (Shankhavana = conch-shell forest/garden)
- **चुल्लसयए गाहावई** = Chullashatak was the householder (culla = small/junior, shatak = hundred — perhaps a clan name)
- **बहुला भारिया** = Bahula was his wife (bahula = abundant, plentiful)
- **जहा आणंदो तहा गिहिधम्मं पडिवज्जइ** = just as Ananda, so he accepted the householder's dharma
- **सेसं जहा कामदेवो** = the rest was the same as Kamadev

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fifth chapter shifts its setting from Varanasi (used in Chapters 1, 2, 3, and 4) to Aalibhika — a city in northern India. This change of location signals a shift in the chapter's character. The garden is called Shankhavan ("conch-shell garden"), a new name not seen in earlier chapters.

Chullashatak's wealth profile is the same as Kamadev's and Suradeva's — six crore in each category. His wife Bahula's name means "abundant" or "plentiful" — a name with prosperous connotations. Like all the previous lay followers, he hears Lord Mahavira's teaching and accepts the full lay vows.

The introduction is compressed to the absolute minimum using double abbreviation: "just as Ananda" for the householder dharma acceptance, "just as Kamadev" for all the initiation details. The chapter's focus is entirely on what makes Chullashatak's ordeal unique.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In the city of Aalibhika lived Chullashatak, a wealthy householder with his wife Bahula. When Lord Mahavira came, he accepted the teaching and took all the vows of a devoted lay follower.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your sense of security comes from your material wealth or possessions? What would you be without them?

## SUTRA 5.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स चुल्लसयगस्स समणोवासयस्स अंतिए पुव्व-रत्तावरतकाल-समयंसि एगे देवे पाउब्भूए जाव असिं गहाय एवं वयासी– हं भो चुल्लसयगा समणोवासया! जाव ण भंजेसि तो ते अज्ज जेट्ठुं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि । एवं जहा चुलणीपिय, णवरं एक्केक्के सत्त मंससोल्लया जाव कणीयसं जाव आयंचामि । तए णं से चुल्लसयए समणोवासए जाव विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa cullasayagassa samaṇovāsayassa aṃtie puvva-rattāvaratakāla-samayaṃsi ege deve pāubbhūe jāva asiṃ gahāya evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho cullasayagā samaṇovāsayā ! jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi to te ajja jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi | evaṃ jahā culaṇīpiya, ṇavaraṃ ekkekke satta maṃsasollayā jāva kaṇīyasaṃ jāva āyaṃcāmi | tae ṇaṃ se cullasayae samaṇovāsae jāva viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, at the previous early night period, a celestial being manifested in the presence of the lay follower Chullashatak — holding a sword — and spoke thus: "Ho there, lay follower Chullashatak! If you do not break your vows, then I shall today bring your eldest son from your house." — the same as Chunilipita's account, except now seven pieces of flesh for each son — up to the youngest — up to: and smeared me. Then the lay follower Chullashatak continued to abide accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवं जहा चुलणीपिय** = the same as Chunilipita's account (full narrative abbreviated)
- **णवरं एक्केक्के सत्त मंससोल्लया** = except seven pieces of flesh for each (the escalation: 3 in Ch3, 5 in Ch4, 7 in Ch5)
- **जाव कणीयसं** = up to the youngest son (all three sons)
- **जाव आयंचामि** = up to: and smeared me
- **जाव विहरइ** = continued to abide (in his practice — fearless)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The three sons are killed and the pattern from Chapter 3 is followed, except for the escalation to seven pieces per son. This progression — three pieces, five pieces, seven pieces — follows an odd-number sequence (3, 5, 7) and creates an escalating sense of the ordeal's severity across the chapters.

The sutra compresses everything through "just as Chunilipita" — the reader who has followed the Upasakdashang in sequence knows exactly what happened: the deva brings each son from the house, kills him before the father, cuts him into seven pieces, boils them in a cauldron, and smears the father's body with flesh and blood. Each time, Chullashatak remains in fearless meditation. The last phrase — "jāva viharai" (continued to abide) — confirms his equanimity.

This compression is itself a literary achievement. By now in the Upasakdashang, the pattern of ordeal-and-equanimity is so well established that the full description is unnecessary. The reader has been trained to fill in the details. The text trusts its audience.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

At night, a celestial being appeared and killed each of Chullashatak's three sons before him — this time cutting each into seven pieces (more than any previous chapter) — and smeared his body with their remains. Each time, Chullashatak remained fearless and steady.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a point at which the escalation of suffering becomes impossible to endure? Or is the capacity for equanimity truly unlimited for those who are deeply established in it?

## SUTRA 5.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे चुल्लसयगं समणोवासयं चउत्थं पि एवं वयासी– हं भो चुल्लसयगा समणोवासया ! जाव ण भंजेसि तो ते अज्ज जाओ इमाओ छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ, छ वुड्डिपउत्ताओ, छ पवित्थरपउत्ताओ, ताओ साओ गिहाओ णीणिमि, णीणेत्ता आलिभियाए णयरीए सिंघाडय-तिय-चउक्क-चच्चर-चडुम्हु-महापहपरेसु सव्वेसा समंता विप्पहिरामि, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुट्टु-वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve cullasayagaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ cautthampī evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho cullasayagā samaṇovāsayā ! jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi to te ajja jāo imāo cha hiraṇṇakoḍīo ṇihāṇapauттāo, cha vuḍḍipauттāo, cha pavitthara-pauттāo, tāo sāo gihāo ṇīṇimi, ṇīṇettā ālibhiyāe ṇayarīe siṃgāḍaya-tiya-caukkа-caccara-caḍumhu-mahāpahparēsu savvesā samaṃtā vippahirāmi, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-duṭṭhu-vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being addressed the lay follower Chullashatak for the fourth time: "Ho there, lay follower Chullashatak! If you still do not break your vows, then I shall today take all these — your six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade goods — take them from your house and scatter them everywhere at every triangular crossroads, quadrilateral crossroads, main intersections, four-way junctions, all major roads and their sides throughout the entire city of Aalibhika — so that you, tormented by extreme distress and grief, may be made to depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउत्थं पि** = for the fourth time (after the three son-threats)
- **छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ, छ वुड्डिपउत्ताओ, छ पवित्थरपउत्ताओ** = six crore in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade (the full 18 crore total)
- **साओ गिहाओ णीणिमि** = I shall take from your own house
- **सिंघाडय** = triangular crossroads (singham = triangular junction)
- **तिय** = at the three-way intersection
- **चउक्क** = at the four-way intersection
- **चच्चर** = at the main market intersection/town square
- **चडुम्हु** = at the four-way junction (variant term)
- **महापहपरेसु** = at all the major roads and their sides
- **सव्वेसा समंता विप्पहिरामि** = I shall scatter everywhere on all sides
- **विप्पहिरामि** = I will scatter, throw about, disperse

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is Chapter 5's unique contribution to the Upasakdashang: the threat to scatter all of Chullashatak's wealth at the crossroads of Aalibhika city. Where previous chapters tested attachment to children, attachment to the mother, attachment to the body's health, Chapter 5 tests attachment to wealth.

The enumeration of where the wealth will be scattered is detailed and systematic — every triangular crossroads, every four-way junction, every main intersection, every major road and side road. The image is of a total and public humiliation: Chullashatak's entire fortune — the foundation of his social standing, his family's security, his livelihood — thrown about at every corner of his city for anyone to take or trample. In ancient Indian society, wealth was not merely material comfort; it was identity, social honor, family legacy, and the basis of one's ability to perform religious duties and charitable acts.

The Jain philosophical teaching embedded in this threat is that attachment to wealth is one of the most persistent obstacles to spiritual liberation. The soul accumulates karma through the greed, deception, and violence associated with acquiring and protecting wealth. A practitioner who truly understands the nature of wealth — that it is borrowed temporarily, that it will leave at death regardless, that it is not the soul's true possession — should be no more disturbed by its loss than by the movement of clouds.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deva's fourth threat was completely different: if Chullashatak still wouldn't break, it would take all of his 18 crore gold coins and scatter them at every crossroads and road in the entire city — publicly destroying his entire fortune.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you lost all your material wealth overnight — savings, property, possessions — how much of your sense of security, identity, and stability would remain?

## SUTRA 5.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से चुल्लसयए समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे अभीए जाव विहरइ । तए णं से देवे चुल्लसयगं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि तहेव भणइ जाव ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se cullasayae samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe abhīe jāva viharai | tae ṇaṃ se deve cullasayagaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā doccaṃpi taccaṃpi taheva bhaṇai jāva vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Chullashatak, having been thus addressed by that celestial being, remained fearless — and continued to conduct himself accordingly. Then that celestial being, seeing him fearless, addressed him a second and third time with the same words — up to: you will depart from life.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभीए जाव विहरइ** = fearless, and continued in his practice
- **अभीयं जाव पासइ** = seeing him fearless
- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = a second and third time
- **तहेव भणइ जाव ववरोविज्जसि** = spoke the same, up to: you will depart from life

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chullashatak's response to the threat of total wealth destruction is the same as to all previous threats: fearless, continuing in practice. The deva repeats the threat of wealth-scattering two more times and receives the same unbroken response each time.

This is the teaching crystallized: the progression of threats across four chapters has moved from family (sons) to extended family (mother) to body (sixteen diseases) to wealth. Chullashatak has faced the wealth-threat and found it no more disturbing than any other. For a Jain practitioner who has truly understood non-possessiveness (aparigraha) as one of the fundamental vows, the loss of wealth is already, in principle, accepted. The vow of aparigraha involves deliberately limiting one's possessions and cultivating detachment from what one does hold. A mind already practicing non-attachment to wealth cannot be destabilized by its loss.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chullashatak remained completely fearless even at the threat of losing all his wealth. The deva repeated the threat two more times — and still Chullashatak remained steady in his practice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the four threatened losses across Chapters 3–5 — sons, mother, bodily health, all wealth — would be hardest for you personally? What does your answer reveal about your deepest attachments?

## SUTRA 5.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स चुल्लसयगस्स समणोवासयस्स तेणं देवेणं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि एवं वुत्तस्स समाणस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए जाव समुप्पणे– अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए जहा चुलणीपिया तहा चिंतेइ जाव आयंचइ, जाओ वि य णं इमाओ ममं छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ, छ वुड्डिपउत्ताओ, छ पवित्थरपउत्ताओ, ताओ वि य णं इच्छइ ममं साओ गिहाओ णीणेत्ता आलिभियाए णयरीए सिंघाडग जाव विप्पहिरित्ते, तं सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए सि कट्टु उद्दाइए, जहा सुरादेवो । तहेव भारिया पुच्छइ, तहेव कहेइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa cullasayagassa samaṇovāsayassa teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi evaṃ vuttassa samāṇassa imeyārūve ajjhithae jāva samuppaṇe— aho ṇaṃ ime purīse aṇārie jahā culaṇīpiyā tahā ciṃtei jāva āyaṃcai, jāo vi ya ṇaṃ imāo mamaṃ cha hiraṇṇakoḍīo ṇihāṇapauттāo, cha vuḍḍipauттāo, cha pavitthara-pauттāo, tāo vi ya ṇaṃ icchei mamaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇettā ālibhiyāe ṇayarīe siṃgāḍaga jāva vippahiritte, taṃ seyaṃ khalu mamaṃ eyaṃ purīsaṃ giṇhittae si kaṭṭu uddāiye, jahā surādevo | taheva bhāriyā pucchai, taheva kaheī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, having been addressed a second and third time by that celestial being, this thought arose in the lay follower Chullashatak's mind: "Ah! This person is ignoble — just as Chunilipita thought — up to: smeared me with remains — and who also desires to take my six crore in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade goods from my house and scatter them at the triangular crossroads throughout Aalibhika city. Therefore it is best for me to seize this person." Deciding thus, he leapt up — just as Suradeva did. His wife then questioned him in the same way, and he told her in the same way.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए जहा चुलणीपिया तहा चिंतेइ** = just as Chunilipita thought, so he thought — this person is ignoble
- **जाव आयंचइ** = up to: smeared me with remains (abbreviated reference to the son-killings)
- **सिंघाडग जाव विप्पहिरित्ते** = scatter at the crossroads, up to: scatter throughout (abbreviated)
- **तं सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए** = it is best for me to seize this person
- **जहा सुरादेवो** = just as Suradeva (leapt up, deva escaped, grasped pillar, made uproar)
- **तहेव भारिया पुच्छइ, तहेव कहेइ** = his wife questioned him in the same way, and he told her in the same way

### 5. COMMENTARY

The pattern completes once more. When the wealth-threat is repeated a second and third time, a thought arises — the same thought of recognition and righteous impulse to seize the deva that arose in Chunilipita (Ch3) and Suradeva (Ch4). The text now makes explicit what has been implicit: "just as Chunilipita thought" and "just as Suradeva did." The three middle chapters form a deliberate trilogy, each one building on the previous pattern.

The thought that arises in Chullashatak is the same in structure: recognition of the deva as ignoble, recognition of all the acts committed (the son-killings abbreviated with "up to smeared me"), recognition of the wealth-scatter threat, and the decision to seize the being. He leaps, the deva escapes, uproar ensues.

Then: wife questions him, he tells her — abbreviated with "just as Suradeva." The wife (Bahula) plays the same role as Dhanya (Ch4) and Bhadra Sarthavahi (Ch3): spiritual guide who identifies the ordeal, names the disturbance, and guides her husband to atonement. The entire resolution is compressed into two phrases.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the deva repeated the threat against his wealth, a thought arose in Chullashatak — the same thought as in the previous two chapters — and he leapt up to seize the deva. The deva escaped. His wife Bahula came and asked what happened, and he told her everything.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can a thought that arises without your choosing it be considered a spiritual failing? Or is the failing only in whether you act on it and how you respond afterward?

## SUTRA 5.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सेसं जहा चुलणीपियस्स जाव सोहम्मे कप्पे अरुणसिद्धे विमाणे उवण्णे । चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई ।

से णं भंते ! चुल्लसयए ताओ देवलोगाओ आउक्खएणं, भवक्खएणं, ठिइक्खएणं अणंतरं चयं चइता कहिं गमिहिइ ? कहिं उववज्जिहइ ? गोयमा ! महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ । णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

sesaṃ jahā culaṇīpiyassa jāva sohame kappe aruṇasiddhe vimāṇe uvavaṇṇe | cattāri paliōvamāiṃ ṭhiī ||

se ṇaṃ bhaṃte ! cullasayae tāo devalōgāo āukkhaēṇaṃ, bhavakkhaēṇaṃ, ṭhiikkaēṇaṃ aṇaṃtaraṃ cayaṃ caitā kahiṃ gamihiī ? kahiṃ uvavajjihiī ? goyamā ! mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihai | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The rest was the same as Chunilipita's — up to: he was born in the Saudharma heaven, in the Arunsiddha celestial vehicle. His lifespan is four paliopamas.

"O Venerable One! Chullashatak, having exhausted his lifespan, his existence, and his allotted time in that heavenly realm — having departed, where will he go? Where will he be reborn?" "O Gautama! In the Mahavideha realm he will attain liberation." The conclusion is the same as the first chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सेसं जहा चुलणीपियस्स** = the rest was the same as Chunilipita's account (atonement, twenty years practice, eleven pratimas, sallekhana, equanimous departure)
- **अरुणसिद्धे विमाणे** = in the Arunsiddha celestial vehicle ("aruṇa" = dawn-red, "siddha" = perfected/accomplished — dawn of perfection)
- **चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई** = lifespan of four paliopamas
- **से णं भंते** = O Venerable One! (Gautama Swami asking Lord Mahavira)
- **आउक्खएणं, भवक्खएणं, ठिइक्खएणं** = having exhausted lifespan, existence-cycle, and allotted time
- **अणंतरं चयं चइता** = having immediately thereafter departed (from the heavenly realm)
- **महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ** = in the Mahavideha realm he will attain liberation

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chapter 5 concludes with the now-familiar arc. Bahula's guidance leads Chullashatak to atonement; he then practices for twenty years with the same dedication as all previous lay followers, completes all eleven pratimas, undertakes sallekhana (one-month voluntary fast), and departs in equanimity. He is born in the Saudharma heaven in the "Arunsiddha" celestial vehicle — the name is significant: "aruṇa" means dawn-red (the same root as Ch3's Arunprabha and Ch4's Arunakant), while "siddha" means accomplished or perfected. Each chapter's celestial vehicle has a slightly different name, all sharing the "Aruna" (dawn/reddish dawn) element, suggesting a family of celestial vehicles in the Saudharma heaven.

The dialogue between Gautama Swami and Lord Mahavira appears explicitly in this chapter (in the other chapters it was implied through the jāva abbreviation). Gautama asks: "After Chullashatak exhausts his heavenly life — where does he go next?" This question is the standard Agamic inquiry about the soul's final destination. Mahavira's answer: "Mahavideha — liberation." This is the same answer as for all ten lay followers in the Upasakdashang.

The Gujarati commentary concludes with the insight that the story of Chullashatak is particularly relevant for modern people because attachment to wealth is perhaps the most universal obstacle to spiritual liberation. Vows, fasting, and meditation are difficult enough; but releasing attachment to wealth — truly, not merely in words — is the hardest test for a householder. Chullashatak's story shows it is possible.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After the ordeal, Chullashatak performed atonement with his wife's guidance, practiced diligently for twenty years, completed all eleven stages of lay practice, and underwent the final voluntary fast. He was reborn in the Saudharma heaven with a four-paliopama lifespan — and will ultimately attain complete liberation in the Mahavideha realm.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The Upasakdashang shows ten different lay followers each testing a different dimension of attachment. Which dimension of attachment — children, parents, body, or wealth — do you feel the most work still remains to be done in your own life?
