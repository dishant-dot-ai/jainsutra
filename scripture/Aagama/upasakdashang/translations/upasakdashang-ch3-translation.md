# Upasakdashang Sutra — Chapter 3
# Shramanopasak Chunilipita (શ્રમણોપાસક ચુલનીપિતા)

**Chapter 3 of the Upasakdashang Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Upasakdashang Sutra, printed pages 90–101 (PDF pages 152–163)*
*Total Sutras: 26*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

This is the third chapter of the Upasakdashang Sutra, titled **Chunilipita** — the story of the lay devotee (shramanopasak) Chunilipita. Chunilipita was a householder of Varanasi (modern-day Banaras, Uttar Pradesh), one of the most sacred cities of ancient India. His wife's name was Shyama. He was a man of extraordinary wealth — possessing eight crore gold coins in deposits, eight crore in investments, eight crore in trade, along with eight types of livestock and large herds of cattle. He was as respected in his time as the famous Ananda and Kamadeva — a counsel to all who sought advice.

The defining characteristic of this chapter is the nature of the divine ordeal (upsarg) inflicted upon Chunilipita. Where Kamadev in Chapter 1 faced a demon, an elephant, and a serpent, and Kamadeva in Chapter 2 faced three ordeals from a single deva, Chunilipita faces a deva who kills his three sons — one by one, before his eyes. Each son is brought from the house, murdered, cut into pieces, dropped in boiling oil, and his flesh and blood smeared on Chunilipita's body. When even this does not break his spiritual resolve, the deva threatens his own mother. The chapter explores the most heart-wrenching test in the Upasakdashang: whether the love of a parent for their child — and a child's love for his parent — can be weaponized against spiritual steadfastness.

Chunilipita endures all, practices the eleven stages (pratimas) of the lay follower's path over twenty years, fasts for sixty meals (santhara), and attains rebirth in the Saudharma heaven. He will ultimately attain liberation (moksha) in the Mahavideha realm.

## SUTRA 3.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उक्खेवो तइयस्स अज्झयणस्स । एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं वाणारसी णामं णयरी । कोट्टुए चेइए । जियसत्तू राया ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ukkhevo taiyassa ajjhayanassa | evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ vāṇārasī ṇāmaṃ ṇayarī | koṭṭue ceie | jiyasattū rāyā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Beginning of the third chapter. Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that period, there was a city named Varanasi. The shrine was called Koshtuk. King Jiyasattu was the ruler.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उक्खेवो तइयस्स** = beginning/commencement of the third (chapter)
- **एवं खलु जंबू** = thus indeed, O Jambu (the disciple)
- **तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं** = at that time, in that period
- **वाणारसी णामं णयरी** = a city named Varanasi
- **कोट्टुए चेइए** = the shrine/temple called Koshtuk
- **जियसत्तू राया** = King Jiyasattu (was the ruler)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third chapter opens with the standard Jain Agamic formula, addressed to Jambu — the disciple of Sudharma Swami, who is narrating these accounts in the chain of oral transmission. The chapter is set in Varanasi, one of the most ancient and spiritually significant cities in India, situated on the banks of the Ganga river. In Jain tradition, Varanasi (also called Kashi) appears in multiple Agamic texts as the setting for significant spiritual events.

King Jiyasattu ruled there — the same king mentioned in Ch1 and Ch2, suggesting these chapters are roughly contemporaneous, taking place during Lord Mahavira's ministry. The local shrine is called Koshtuk, which served as the assembly place (samavasarana) when Lord Mahavira visited.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The story begins in the great ancient city of Varanasi, during the time when Lord Mahavira was traveling and teaching.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which city or place in your life has been the setting for your most significant spiritual moments?

## SUTRA 3.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थ णं वाणारसीए णयरीए चुलणीपिया णामं गाहावई परिवसइ, अट्टे जाव अपरिभूए । सामा भारिया । अट्टु हिरण्ण-कोडीओ णिहाण-पउत्ताओ, अट्टु वुड्डिपउत्ताओ, अट्टु पावित्थर-पउत्ताओ, अट्ट वया, दस-गो-साहसिसएणं वएणं । जहा आणंदो राईसर जाव सव्व-कज्ज-वड्डावए यावि होत्था । सामी समोसढे । परिसा णिग्गया । चुलणीपिया वि जहा आणंदो तहा णिग्गओ । तहेव गिहि-धम्मं पडिवज्जइ । गोयम-पुच्छा । तहेव सेसं जहा कामदेवस्स जाव पोसह सालाए पोसिहए बंभयारी समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स अंतियं धम्मपणत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्तांणं विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tattha ṇaṃ vāṇārasīe ṇayarīe culaṇīpiyā ṇāmaṃ gāhāvaī parivasai, aṭṭe jāva aparibhūe | sāmā bhāriyā | aṭṭu hiraṇṇa-koḍīo ṇihāṇa-pauттāo, aṭṭu vuḍḍipauттāo, aṭṭu pāvitthara-pauттāo, aṭṭa vayā, dasa-go-sāhasisaeṇaṃ vaeṇaṃ | jahā āṇaṃdo rāīsara jāva savva-kajja-vaḍḍāvae yāvi hotthā | sāmī samosaḍhe | parisā ṇiggayā | culaṇīpiyā vi jahā āṇaṃdo tahā ṇiggao | taheva gihi-dhammaṃ paḍivajjai | goyama-pucchā | taheva sesaṃ jahā kāmadevassa jāva posaha sālāe posihaē baṃbhayārī samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa aṃtiyaṃ dhammappaṇattiṃ uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There in the city of Varanasi lived a householder named Chunilipita, wealthy, up to undefeatable. Shyama was his wife. He possessed eight crore gold coins in deposits, eight crore in revenue, eight crore in trade goods, eight types of livestock, and wealth of ten thousand cattle herds. Like Ananda the royal counselor, he was an advisor in all matters. The Lord (Mahavira) arrived. The assembly came out. Chunilipita also went out just as Ananda did. He likewise accepted the householder's dharma. Gautama's questioning (followed). The rest was the same as with Kamadev — up to: in the poshadha hall he observed celibacy, and living in accordance with the teaching of the ascetic Lord Mahavira, he conducted himself accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चुलणीपिया णामं गाहावई** = a householder named Chunilipita
- **अट्टे जाव अपरिभूए** = wealthy, up to undefeatable/unassailable
- **सामा भारिया** = Shyama was his wife
- **अट्टु हिरण्ण-कोडीओ** = eight crore gold coins (8 = aṭṭu, more than Kamadev's 6)
- **जहा आणंदो राईसर** = like Ananda, a royal counselor
- **सामी समोसढे** = the Lord (Mahavira) arrived
- **गिहि-धम्मं पडिवज्जइ** = accepted the householder's dharma (lay vows)
- **गोयम-पुच्छा** = Gautama's questioning (standard narrative element)
- **पोसिहए बंभयारी** = observing celibacy during the poshadha vow
- **धम्मपणत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्तांणं विहरइ** = living in accordance with the dharma teaching

### 5. COMMENTARY

This long compound sutra establishes Chunilipita's background and his acceptance of Lord Mahavira's teaching using the standard abbreviation technique of referring to previous chapters. His wealth is slightly greater than Kamadev's — eight crore in each category versus six — emphasizing his even greater prosperity. His wife Shyama is mentioned by name. He is likened to the great Ananda — the first and most prominent of the ten lay followers in the Upasakdashang — as an advisor to all.

The structure of his initiation parallels Kamadev's exactly: the Lord arrives in Varanasi, the assembly gathers, Chunilipita goes out to hear the teaching, Gautama questions the Lord about Chunilipita's spiritual future (which is the standard Agamic way of establishing that a person's liberation is prophesied by the Tirthankara), and Chunilipita accepts all the vows of a lay follower, including the poshadha vow with celibacy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In Varanasi lived a very wealthy and respected householder named Chunilipita with his wife Shyama. When Lord Mahavira came to the city, Chunilipita heard his teaching and took all the vows of a devoted lay follower.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it take for you to commit fully to a spiritual path — not just admiring it, but actually living by it every day?

## SUTRA 3.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स चुलणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स पुव्व रत्तावरतकाल-समयंसि एगे देवे अंतियं पाउब्भूए ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa culaṇīpiyassa samaṇovāsayassa puvva rattāvaratakāla-samayaṃsi ege deve aṃtiyaṃ pāubbhūe ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, at a previous time during the early night period, a celestial being manifested in the presence of the lay follower Chunilipita.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं तस्स** = then, of that
- **चुलणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स** = of the lay follower Chunilipita
- **पुव्व रत्तावरतकाल-समयंसि** = at a previous time, during the early night period
- **एगे देवे** = one celestial being
- **अंतियं पाउब्भूए** = manifested in his presence

### 5. COMMENTARY

The divine ordeal begins. A celestial being appears in the early night — a time associated with heightened spiritual vulnerability and quiet meditation. In the Jain Agamas, ordeals often occur at night, when the practitioner is alone with his practice and his inner life. This sutra is the gateway to the most emotionally devastating test in the entire Upasakdashang. What follows is not a test of physical courage against a demon or a beast, but a test of spiritual equanimity against the most powerful human attachments: love for one's children and love for one's mother.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

One night, a celestial being appeared before the lay follower Chunilipita to test his spiritual resolve.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are you more afraid of physical threats, or of threats to those you love?

## SUTRA 3.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे एगं महं णीलुप्पल जाव असिं गहाय चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी– हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! जहा कामदेवो जाव ण भंजेसि, तो ते अहं अज्ज जेट्ठुं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि, णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएमि, घाएत्ता आदाण-भिरियंसि कडाहयंसि अद्धेमि, अद्धेत्ता तव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचामि, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुहट्टु वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve egaṃ mahaṃ nīluppala jāva asiṃ gahāya culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! jahā kāmadevo jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te ahaṃ ajja jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi, ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāemi, ghāettā ādāṇa-bhiriyaṃsi kaḍāhayaṃsi addhemi, addhettā tava gāyaṃ maṃseṇa ya soṇieṇa ya āyaṃcāmi, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-duhaṭṭhu vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, seizing a large blue-lotus-like sharp sword, spoke thus to the lay follower Chunilipita: "Ho there, lay follower Chunilipita! Just as with Kamadev — if you do not break your vows, then I shall today bring your eldest son from your house, bring him before you, kill him, cut him into pieces, boil him in a cauldron of liquid, and smear your body with his flesh and blood — so that you, tormented by extreme pain and grief, may be made to depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एगं महं णीलुप्पल जाव असिं** = a large, sharp sword (described as blue like a lotus — nīluppala is a blue lotus, here used to describe the color/sheen of the blade)
- **जेट्ठुं पुत्तं** = your eldest son
- **साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि** = I will bring from your own house
- **तव अग्गओ घाएमि** = I will kill before you
- **आदाण-भिरियंसि कडाहयंसि** = in a boiling cauldron of liquid
- **अद्धेमि** = I will boil/cook
- **तव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य** = your body with his flesh and blood
- **आयंचामि** = I will smear/anoint
- **अट्टु-दुहट्टु वसट्टे** = tormented by extreme pain and grief
- **अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि** = you will be made to depart from life before your time (untimely death)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra contains one of the most disturbing threats in the entire Jain Agamic literature. The deva does not merely threaten Chunilipita with personal harm — it threatens to kill his eldest son before his eyes, in the most gruesome manner possible: bringing him from the house, murdering him, cutting him into three pieces, boiling those pieces, and smearing Chunilipita's body with his own son's flesh and blood.

The reference to "just as with Kamadev" is the standard abbreviation — the deva repeats the same opening warning that was given to Kamadev, and then escalates with this specific threat. The choice of the eldest son is deliberate and culturally significant. In ancient Indian society, the eldest son carried the weight of the family lineage, the continuation of the clan, and was the primary emotional attachment for a father. If anything could break a man's spiritual equanimity, it would be the murder of his eldest child.

The phrase "before your time" (akāle) is important — the goal of the deva's torture is not just pain but the complete disruption of spiritual practice. A person dying in agony and despair, rather than in equanimous meditation, falls far from the spiritual trajectory they had built.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The celestial being appeared holding a sword and threatened Chunilipita: "If you don't break your spiritual vows, I will bring your eldest son from your house, kill him before your eyes, and smear you with his blood — until you die in agony."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there anyone in your life whose suffering would break your most deeply held convictions? What does that tell you about where your true attachments lie?

## SUTRA 3.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से चुलणीपिया समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे अभीए जाव विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe abhīe jāva viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Chunilipita, having been thus addressed by that celestial being, remained fearless — and continued to conduct himself accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेणं देवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे** = having been thus addressed by that celestial being
- **अभीए** = fearless, without fear
- **जाव विहरइ** = and continued to conduct himself (in spiritual practice)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita's response to this horrifying threat is a single word: abhīe — fearless. He does not bargain, does not waver, does not plead. He simply remains in his state of spiritual practice. The Agamic brevity here is deliberate — the threat is described in extensive detail, and the response is compressed into one quality: fearlessness. This contrast emphasizes that spiritual equanimity does not need elaborate words. It is a state of being, not a statement.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Despite this terrifying threat against his eldest son, Chunilipita remained completely fearless and continued his spiritual practice without flinching.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does true fearlessness look like? Is it the absence of fear, or the presence of something stronger than fear?

## SUTRA 3.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी– हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! तं चेव भणइ जाव विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā doccaṃpi taccaṃpi culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! taṃ ceva bhaṇai jāva viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, seeing the lay follower Chunilipita fearless, addressed him a second time and a third time with the same words — and he (Chunilipita) continued to conduct himself accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभीयं जाव पासइ** = seeing him fearless
- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = a second time and a third time
- **तं चेव भणइ** = spoke the same thing (repeated the same threat)
- **जाव विहरइ** = and he continued to conduct himself (in practice)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The deva repeats the threat two more times. This triple repetition is standard in the Agamic tradition — each repetition represents an escalation of psychological pressure. The first time, the threat is a shock. The second time, it is a challenge. The third time, it is a declaration of intent. Each time, Chunilipita remains unmoved. The deva is not just testing whether the threat itself will break him — it is testing whether the extended pressure of repeated threats, the sustained weight of anticipatory dread, will erode his resolve. It does not.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deva repeated the same threat two more times — but each time Chunilipita remained completely steady in his spiritual practice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is your spiritual resolve strong enough not just to survive a single shock, but to remain steady under sustained, repeated pressure?

## SUTRA 3.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासित्ता आसुरत्ते चुलणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स जेट्ठुं पुत्तं गिहाओ णीणेइ, णीणेत्ता अग्गओ घाएइ, घाएत्ता तओ मंससोल्लए करेइ, करेत्ता आदाणभिरियंसि कडाहयंसि अद्धेइ, अद्धेत्ता चुलणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsittā āsuratte culaṇīpiyassa samaṇovāsayassa jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ gihāo ṇīṇei, ṇīṇettā aggao ghāei, ghāettā tao maṃsasolle karei, karettā ādāṇabhiriyaṃsi kaḍāhayaṃsi addhei, addhettā culaṇīpiyassa samaṇovāsayassa gāyaṃ maṃseṇa ya soṇieṇa ya āyaṃcai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, seeing the lay follower Chunilipita remain fearless, became enraged. He brought Chunilipita's eldest son from the house, killed him before Chunilipita, cut him into three pieces, put him in a cauldron of boiling liquid, boiled him, and smeared Chunilipita's body with his flesh and blood.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आसुरत्ते** = enraged, furious
- **जेट्ठुं पुत्तं** = the eldest son
- **गिहाओ णीणेइ** = brought from the house
- **अग्गओ घाएइ** = killed before him
- **तओ मंससोल्लए करेइ** = cut into three roasted/mangled pieces
- **आदाणभिरियंसि कडाहयंसि** = in a boiling cauldron of liquid
- **अद्धेइ** = boiled/cooked
- **गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचइ** = smeared his (Chunilipita's) body with the flesh and blood

### 5. COMMENTARY

The deva carries out the threat. This is one of the most viscerally horrifying passages in the Upasakdashang. A father watches his eldest son dragged from their home, murdered before his eyes, dismembered, boiled, and the remains smeared on his own body. The text does not soften this — it is described with stark, unflinching specificity.

Why does the Agama include such graphic content? Because it is precisely the extremity of the test that establishes the extremity of the achievement. The Upasakdashang is not a text about easy virtue — it is a text about what human spiritual capacity can endure. Chunilipita's steadfastness in the face of this is not stoicism or numbness. The Gujarati commentary clarifies that his heart was not unmoved — but he was in the state of dharmadhyana (absorption in the truth of the dharma) so deeply that the deva's cruelty could not dislodge him from it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Enraged that Chunilipita wouldn't break, the deva carried out his threat — bringing his eldest son before him, killing him, cutting him up, boiling him, and smearing Chunilipita's body with the remains.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone you love is suffering because of your convictions, how do you hold both the love and the conviction at the same time?

## SUTRA 3.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से चुलणीपिया समणोवासए तं उज्जलं जाव अहियासेइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsae taṃ ujjalaṃ jāva ahiyāsei ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Chunilipita endured that intense (pain) with equanimity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तं उज्जलं** = that intense (pain/affliction) — ujjala means bright, intense, blazing
- **जाव** = up to (abbreviation — implies the full sequence of bearing pain)
- **अहियासेइ** = endured, bore with equanimity

### 5. COMMENTARY

Again, the contrast: the previous sutra describes an act of horrifying cruelty in meticulous detail. The response is three words — "he endured that intense pain." The word "ujjala" (bright/blazing) is used for the pain, conveying its searing quality. The word "ahiyāsei" means not merely passive endurance but active, conscious equanimous acceptance.

In Jain thought, this is the practice of titiksha — patient forbearance without inner resentment, without reactive hatred toward the perpetrator. Chunilipita is not gritting his teeth and suppressing his grief. He is in dharmadhyana — the meditation on the nature of reality, the impermanence of the body, the reality of karma, the path of the soul. In that state, even the murder of his beloved son becomes an ordeal that cannot sever his connection with truth.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita bore even this most extreme pain with complete inner equanimity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the difference between suppressing grief and transcending it? Which one is genuine spiritual practice?

## SUTRA 3.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी–हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! अपत्थिय-पत्थिया जाव ण भंजेसि, तो ते अहं अज्ज मज्झिमं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि, णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएमि जहा जेट्ठुं पुत्तं तहेव भणइ, तहेव करेइ । एवं तच्चंपि कणियसं जाव अहियासेइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā doccaṃpi taccaṃpi culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! apatthiya-patthiyā jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te ahaṃ ajja majjhimaṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi, ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāemi jahā jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ taheva bhaṇai, taheva karei | evaṃ taccaṃpi kaṇiyasaṃ jāva ahiyāsei ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, seeing the lay follower Chunilipita remain fearless, addressed him a second and third time: "Ho there, lay follower Chunilipita! One who seeks what should not be sought — if you still do not break your vows, then I shall today bring your middle son from your house and kill him before you" — and he spoke and did exactly as he had with the eldest son. And likewise the youngest son — and Chunilipita endured it all.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अपत्थिय-पत्थिया** = one who seeks what should not be sought; the deva is calling Chunilipita "seeker of the wrong path"
- **मज्झिमं पुत्तं** = the middle son
- **जहा जेट्ठुं पुत्तं** = just as with the eldest son
- **तहेव भणइ, तहेव करेइ** = spoke the same, did the same
- **तच्चंपि कणियसं** = and likewise the youngest (son)
- **जाव अहियासेइ** = and he endured it all

### 5. COMMENTARY

The deva proceeds to murder the middle son and then the youngest son — each time following the same gruesome procedure as with the eldest, and each time Chunilipita endures it with equanimity. All three sons are killed, dismembered, boiled, and their remains smeared on their father's body.

This is the Upasakdashang's most extensive portrayal of an ordeal. Three sons lost, one by one, in the most brutal manner, while Chunilipita remains in meditation. The Jain tradition is very clear: Chunilipita's ability to endure this is not an endorsement of emotional numbness or the suppression of parental love. It is a demonstration of what happens when the soul has rooted itself so deeply in the recognition of the true nature of reality — that attachment to bodily forms, however beloved, cannot override the commitment to liberation.

The word "apatthiya-patthiya" (one who seeks what should not be sought) is the deva's insult, attempting to provoke a reaction of ego or defensiveness. It also fails.

## SUTRA 3.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता चउत्थंपि चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी–हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! अपत्थिय-पत्थिया जाव ण भंजेसि, तओ अहं अज्ज जा इमा तव माया भद्दा सत्थवाही देवगुरुजणणी, दुक्कर-दुक्कर-कारिया, तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि, णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएमि, घाएत्ता तओ मंससोल्लए करेमि, करेत्ता आदाणभिरियंसि कडाहयंसि अद्धेमि, अद्धेत्ता तव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचामि, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुक्खु-वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā cautthampī culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! apatthiya-patthiyā jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, tao ahaṃ ajja jā imā tava māyā bhaddā satthavāhī devagurujaṇaṇī, dukkara-dukkara-kāriayā, taṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi, ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāemi, ghāettā tao maṃsasolle karemi, karettā ādāṇabhiriyaṃsi kaḍāhayaṃsi addhemi, addhettā tava gāyaṃ maṃseṇa ya soṇieṇa ya āyaṃcāmi, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-dukkhu-vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, seeing the lay follower Chunilipita remain fearless, addressed him a fourth time: "Ho there, lay follower Chunilipita! You who seek what should not be sought — if you still do not break your vows, then I shall today bring your mother Bhadra Sarthavahi — the one who is like a god and a guru to you, who has performed the most difficult of deeds — from her house, bring her before you, kill her, cut her into pieces, boil her in a cauldron of liquid, and smear your body with her flesh and blood — so that you, tormented by extreme pain and grief, may be made to depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउत्थंपि** = for the fourth time
- **माया भद्दा सत्थवाही** = your mother Bhadra Sarthavahi (the caravan leader)
- **देवगुरुजणणी** = one who is like a god and a guru to you — the one who gave you birth; "devaguru" means as worthy of reverence as a god and a guru
- **दुक्कर-दुक्कर-कारिया** = one who has performed the most difficult of difficult deeds (i.e., raised you through great hardship)
- **साओ गिहाओ** = from her own house

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having killed all three sons, the deva now escalates to the final and most potent threat: the mother. After sons, it is the mother — the one who in Indian culture is revered as equal to a god and a guru. The text uses the extraordinary compound "deva-guru-janani" — she is simultaneously a deity, a spiritual teacher, and the one who gave birth. This triple title places the mother at the absolute apex of human reverence in Indian thought.

The phrase "dukkar-dukkar-kariya" (performer of the most difficult of deeds) refers to the immense sacrifices of motherhood — the pain of childbirth, the years of raising a child, the devotion that cannot be repaid. The deva is deliberately invoking every layer of Chunilipita's deepest bonds: first his sons, now the woman whose suffering gave him life.

This is the ultimate test. In ancient Indian society, allowing one's mother to come to harm — especially when one has the apparent choice to prevent it — was considered the worst possible failure of dharma. The deva is exploiting this cultural weight to its maximum.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Still not breaking, the deva threatened one more time: "I will bring your own mother — the one who is as sacred to you as a god and a teacher — and kill her the same way I killed your sons."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

We speak of honoring our parents as a duty. But is there a level of personal conviction that transcends even this duty — and if so, how do you know when you've reached it?

## SUTRA 3.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स चुलणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स तेणं देवेणं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि एवं वुत्तस्स समाणस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए जाव समुप्पणे– अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए अणारियबुद्धी, अणारियाई पावाई कम्माई समायरइ, जेणं ममं जेट्ठुं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेइ जाव कणियसं जाव आयंचइ। जा वि य णं इमा ममं माया भद्दा सत्थवाही देवगुरु-जणणी, दुक्कर-दुक्कर-कारिया त पि य णं इच्छइ साओ गिहाओ णीणेत्ता ममं अग्गओ घाएत्तए, त सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए ति कट्टु उद्दाइए, से वि य आगासे उप्पइए, तेणं च खंभे आसाइए, महया-महया सद्देणं कोलाहले कए ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa culaṇīpiyassa samaṇovāsayassa teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi evaṃ vuttassa samāṇassa imeyārūve ajjhithae jāva samuppaṇe— aho ṇaṃ ime purīse aṇārie aṇāriyabuddhi, aṇāriyāi pāvāi kammāi samāyarai, jeṇaṃ mamaṃ jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇei jāva kaṇiyasaṃ jāva āyaṃcai | jā vi ya ṇaṃ imā mamaṃ māyā bhaddā satthavāhī devaguru-jaṇaṇī, dukkara-dukkara-kāriyā ta pi ya ṇaṃ icchei sāo gihāo ṇīṇettā mamaṃ aggao ghāettae, ta seyaṃ khalu mamaṃ eyaṃ purisaṃ giṇhittae ti kaṭṭu uddāiye, se vi ya āgāse uppiye, teṇaṃ ca khaṃbhe āsāiye, mahayā-mahayā saddeṇaṃ kolāhale kae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, having been addressed a second and third time by that celestial being, this thought arose in the lay follower Chunilipita's mind: "Ah! This person is ignoble, of ignoble intellect, performing ignoble sinful deeds — who took my eldest son from my house, up to the youngest, and smeared me with their remains. And now he also desires to bring my mother Bhadra Sarthavahi — the one who is like a god and a guru to me, who has performed the most difficult deeds — from her house and kill her before me. Therefore, it is better that I seize this person." Deciding thus, he sprang up. The deva also leapt into the sky. He (Chunilipita) seized a column/pillar for support and made a great uproar.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अज्झिथए जाव समुप्पणे** = this thought arose in him
- **अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए** = ah! this person is ignoble/unworthy
- **अणारियबुद्धी** = of ignoble intellect
- **अणारियाई पावाई कम्माई** = performing ignoble sinful deeds
- **त सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए** = therefore it is best for me to seize this person
- **उद्दाइए** = sprang up, leapt
- **आगासे उप्पइए** = (the deva) leapt into the sky
- **खंभे आसाइए** = grasped/seized a column for support
- **महया-महया सद्देणं कोलाहले कए** = made a great and mighty uproar/commotion

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the pivotal turning point. After enduring three sons being killed, Chunilipita — when faced with the same fate for his mother — has a moment of thought-arising: he recognizes the deva as ignoble and decides to seize him. He leaps up. The deva escapes into the sky. Chunilipita grabs a pillar and shouts.

It is critical to understand what this moment means in the Jain Agamic tradition. This is not a moment of spiritual failure. Chunilipita's thought-arising is not one of hatred or revenge — it is one of righteous recognition: this being is performing sinful actions. His impulse to "seize" the deva is a protective one, motivated by the desire to prevent harm to his mother, not by personal anger.

However, the Gujarati commentary (upsanhar) notes that this brief moment — where his attention moved from dharmadhyana to the deva — represents a slight perturbation. Not a breaking of vows, but a momentary shift in focus. This is what his mother later asks him to atone for. The pratikraman (confession) and prayashchitta (atonement) that follow are not for any moral failing, but for this momentary distraction from the highest state of meditation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

## SUTRA 3.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं अहं तेणं पुरिसेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे अभीए जाव विहरामि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ ahaṃ teṇaṃ purīseṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe abhīe jāva vīharāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Chunilipita continues:) "Then, having been thus addressed by that person, I remained fearless — and continued to conduct myself accordingly."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अहं** = I (Chunilipita is now speaking in first person to his mother)
- **तेणं पुरिसेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे** = having been thus addressed by that person
- **अभीए जाव विहरामि** = I remained fearless and continued in practice

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita confirms to his mother: he remained fearless. The shift to first person ("ahaṃ" — I) is notable here. Until now the narrative was in third person. From sutra 3.15 onward, Chunilipita is narrating directly. This shift creates an intimacy — we hear him speaking to his mother, describing his own experience of the ordeal. He names his own state: abhīe — fearless. He is telling her not just what happened but what he was internally.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita told his mother: "Despite those threats, I remained fearless and kept on with my spiritual practice."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a difference between reporting your inner state to someone you trust and performing a version of yourself for their approval?

## SUTRA 3.17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से पुरिसे ममं अभीयं जाव विहरमाणं पासइ, पासित्ता ममं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि एवं वयासी– हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! जाव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se purīse mamaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva viharamāṇaṃ pāsai, pāsittā mamaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! jāva gāyaṃ maṃseṇa ya soṇieṇa ya āyaṃcai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Chunilipita continues narrating:) "Then that person, seeing me remain fearless and continuing in practice, addressed me a second and third time — and smeared my body with flesh and blood."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ममं अभीयं जाव विहरमाणं पासइ** = seeing me remaining fearless and in practice
- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = a second and third time
- **जाव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचइ** = up to: smeared my body with flesh and blood (abbreviated — the full sequence of murder and smearing is implied)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita continues his report to his mother — describing how the threats were repeated and how the acts of violence were carried out. He is speaking plainly and directly. There is no dramatization, no self-pity, no anger in his retelling. This equanimity in the very act of narrating the ordeal is itself a sign of his spiritual state. He is not re-traumatizing himself by recounting it; he is simply stating what happened.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita continued: "The person repeated the threats and carried out the attacks — smearing my body with flesh and blood."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it take for you to be able to describe your worst ordeal to another person with complete calm, as a simple statement of fact?

## SUTRA 3.18

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं अहं तं उज्जलं, विउलं, कक्कसं, पगाढं, चंडं, दुक्खं, दुरिहयासं वेयणं सम्मं सहामि, खमामि, तितिक्खामि, अहियासेमि । एवं तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा कहेइ जाव अहियासेमि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ ahaṃ taṃ ujjalaṃ, viulaṃ, kakkasaṃ, pagāḍhaṃ, caṃḍaṃ, dukkhaṃ, durihayāsaṃ veyaṇaṃ sammaṃ sahāmi, khamāmi, tititkkhāmi, ahiyāsemi | evaṃ tiṇṇi vi uvasaggā kaheī jāva ahiyāsemi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Chunilipita continues:) "Then I bore that intense, vast, harsh, deep-set, fierce, painful, and difficult-to-endure sensation with proper equanimity — I bore it, I forgave, I endured with patience, I endured it fully. And thus all three ordeals he narrated — and said: I endured it all."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उज्जलं** = intense, blazing
- **विउलं** = vast, extensive
- **कक्कसं** = harsh, rough
- **पगाढं** = deep-set, profound
- **चंडं** = fierce, violent
- **दुक्खं** = painful
- **दुरिहयासं** = difficult to endure
- **सम्मं सहामि** = I bore it properly, with equanimity
- **खमामि** = I forgave (there is no resentment)
- **तितिक्खामि** = I endured with patient forbearance
- **अहियासेमि** = I endured it fully
- **तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा** = all three ordeals (the three sons)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra contains one of the most beautiful passages in the Upasakdashang. Chunilipita describes his internal experience of the ordeal using seven precise adjectives for the pain — intense, vast, harsh, deep-set, fierce, painful, difficult-to-endure — and then four verbs for his response: sahāmi (I bore), khamāmi (I forgave), tititkkhāmi (I endured with patience), ahiyāsemi (I endured it fully).

These four verbs describe distinct but related states. "Bearing" (sahāmi) is accepting the physical reality. "Forgiving" (khamāmi) is the absence of resentment or desire for revenge. "Patient forbearance" (titikkhā) is a technical Jain term for the specific practice of enduring without inner reactivity. "Enduring fully" (ahiyāsemi) is the complete embracing of the experience without rejection.

This sequence is not the numbness of suppression — it is the fullness of conscious, free, equanimous acceptance. He narrates this to his mother as a complete statement of his inner life during the ordeal.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita told his mother how he faced the pain: "That intense, fierce, vast suffering — I bore it, I forgave, I endured it with patience, I endured it completely."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the four responses — bearing, forgiving, patient endurance, and complete acceptance — which one do you find hardest? Why?

## SUTRA 3.19

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से पुरिसे ममं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता ममं चउत्थंपि एवं वयासी– हं भो चुलणीपिया समणोवासया ! अपत्थिय-पत्थिया जाव ण भंजेसि, तो ते अज जा इमा माया देवगुरु जणणी दुक्कर-दुक्करकारिया, तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि, णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएमि जहा जेट्ठुं पुत्तं तहेव भणइ, तहेव करेइ । एवं कणियसं जाव आयंचइ, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुक्खु-वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se purīse mamaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā mamaṃ cautthampī evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsayā ! apatthiya-patthiyā jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te aja jā imā māyā devaguru jaṇaṇī dukkara-dukkarakāriyā, taṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi, ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāemi jahā jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ taheva bhaṇai, taheva karei | evaṃ kaṇiyasaṃ jāva āyaṃcai, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-dukkhu-vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Chunilipita continues narrating:) "Then that person, seeing me remain fearless, addressed me a fourth time: 'You who seek what should not be sought — if you still do not break, then I shall bring your mother — the one like a god and guru to you, who has performed difficult deeds — from her house, and kill her just as I killed your eldest son.' He spoke and did the same, up to the youngest — and he said: so that you may depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउत्थंपि** = for the fourth time
- **माया देवगुरु जणणी** = your mother, the one like a god and guru
- **दुक्कर-दुक्करकारिया** = who has performed the most difficult deeds
- **जहा जेट्ठुं पुत्तं तहेव भणइ तहेव करेइ** = spoke the same, did the same as with the eldest son

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita is now describing to his mother — who is physically present before him — how the deva threatened to do exactly to her what he did to the sons. The scene has an extraordinary emotional quality: a son, covered in the remains of his murdered sons, is narrating to his living mother how a supernatural being threatened to murder her too. The mother is listening. Neither weeps, neither flees. What follows is a mother instructing her son in spiritual practice — one of the most remarkable parent-child exchanges in the entire Agamic literature.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita told his mother: "The fourth time, he threatened to bring you — my mother, who is sacred to me as a god and teacher — and kill you the same way he killed my sons."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Has anyone ever demonstrated their love for you not through comfort, but by holding you to your highest spiritual standards?

## SUTRA 3.22

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तेणं तेणं पुरिसेणं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि ममं एवं वुत्तस्स समाणस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए जाव समुप्पणे– अहो णं ! इमे पुरिसे अणारिए अणारियबुद्धी, अणारियाई पावाई कम्माई समायरइ, जेणं ममं जेट्ठुं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ तहेव जाव कणियसं जाव आयंचइ, त सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए ति कट्टु उद्दाइए । से वि य आगासे उप्पइए, मए वि य खंभे आसाइए, महया-महया सद्देणं कोलाहले कए ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ teṇaṃ teṇaṃ purīseṇaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi mamaṃ evaṃ vuttassa samāṇassa imeyārūve ajjhithae jāva samuppaṇe— aho ṇaṃ ! ime purīse aṇārie aṇāriyabuddhi, aṇāriyāi pāvāi kammāi samāyarai, jeṇaṃ mamaṃ jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo taheva jāva kaṇiyasaṃ jāva āyaṃcai, ta seyaṃ khalu mamaṃ eyaṃ purīsaṃ giṇhittae ti kaṭṭu uddāiye | se vi ya āgāse uppiye, mae vi ya khaṃbhe āsāiye, mahayā-mahayā saddeṇaṃ kolāhale kae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Chunilipita continues:) "Then, having been thus addressed a second and third time, this thought arose in my mind: 'Ah! This person is ignoble, of ignoble intellect, performing ignoble sinful deeds — who took my eldest son from my house, up to the youngest, and smeared me with their remains. Therefore it is best for me to seize this person.' Deciding thus, I leapt up. He also leapt into the sky. I too grasped a column for support and made a great and mighty uproar."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए** = this kind of thought arose
- **अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए** = ah! this person is ignoble
- **त सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए** = it is best for me to seize this person
- **उद्दाइए** = I leapt up (first person here — Chunilipita narrating)
- **मए वि य खंभे आसाइए** = I also grasped a column

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita now narrates his own moment of disturbance. The thought that arose — "this person is ignoble, it is best to seize him" — is what breaks the pure state of dharmadhyana. It is a righteous thought, not a vindictive one. But in the Jain framework of spiritual practice, even a righteous thought directed outward (toward the deva, toward action in the world) is a departure from inward absorption in dharmadhyana.

This is a subtle but profound teaching. Chunilipita did not break his vows. He did not feel anger or hatred toward the deva. He felt recognition of wrongdoing and a protective impulse. Yet this impulse — however justified — represents a perturbation of the highest state of meditative absorption. The ensuing pratikraman (atonement) is for this momentary shift, not for any moral transgression.

The fact that this moment was enough to prompt his mother to instruct him in atonement demonstrates the exacting standard of the Jain path. Even good impulses, if they arise from ego-identification rather than pure awareness, require acknowledgment and purification.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita told his mother: "When the same thing happened again, a thought arose in me — 'this person is sinful, I should seize him.' I leapt up, he escaped into the sky, I grabbed a pillar and cried out."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a difference between a reaction that comes from genuine care and one that comes from inner disturbance? Can both be present at the same time?

## SUTRA 3.23

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सा भद्दा सत्थवाही चुलणीपियं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी– जो खलु केइ पुरिसे तव जाव कणियसं साओ गिहाओ णीणेइ णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएत्ए, एस णं केइ पुरिसे तव उवसग्गं करेइ, एस णं तुमे विदिरसणे दिट्टु । तं णं तुमं भगव्वए भग्गणियमे विदिरसणे हेरण्फए जाव पडिवज्जाइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sā bhaddā satthavāhī culaṇīpiyaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— jo khalu kei purīse tava jāva kaṇiyasaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇei ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāettae, esa ṇaṃ kei purīse tava uvasaggaṃ karei, esa ṇaṃ tume vidirasaṇe diṭṭhu | taṃ ṇaṃ tumaṃ bhagavvae bhagga-ṇiyame vidirasaṇe heraṃphae jāva paḍivajjāi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Bhadra Sarthavahi said to the lay follower Chunilipita: "Whoever this person was who brought your sons from your house and killed them before you — this person was inflicting an ordeal upon you. You have seen this frightening sight. Therefore, since your vow was shaken and you saw this terrifying vision — you should now perform atonement, confession, and prayashchitta accordingly."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एस णं केइ पुरिसे तव उवसग्गं करेइ** = this person was inflicting an ordeal (upsarga) upon you
- **विदिरसणे दिट्टु** = you have seen a frightening/terrifying sight (vidirasana = terrifying vision)
- **भगव्वए भग्गणियमे** = since your vow/discipline was shaken (bhagga = broken/shaken, niyama = vow/discipline)
- **विदिरसणे** = frightening, disturbing
- **हेरण्फए** = ⚠️ frightening, disturbing (similar meaning — term uncertain)
- **जाव पडिवज्जाइ** = you should accept and undertake (atonement)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Bhadra Sarthavahi's response to her son's account is extraordinary. She does not weep for her murdered grandchildren. She does not panic. She does not congratulate her son on his steadfastness. She does precisely what a spiritually mature mother would do: she identifies what happened with precision, names it (an upsarga — a divine ordeal), recognizes that Chunilipita's vow was briefly shaken, and instructs him to perform atonement.

The phrase "bhagga-niyame" (shaken vow) is not an accusation — it is an accurate spiritual assessment. Chunilipita's vow was not broken in the moral sense. His reaction — the thought of seizing the deva — was a momentary deviation from the highest state of absorption. His mother, with the clarity of an experienced practitioner, sees this immediately and guides him to the appropriate remedy: atonement (pratikraman) and prayashchitta (penannce-purification).

This scene elevates the role of the mother to that of a spiritual guide — a guru. The title "devaguru-janani" given to her earlier in the chapter is now shown to be literally true: she is acting as a guru to her son.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita's mother said to him: "Whoever that being was, he was testing you. You saw a terrifying sight, and your resolve was briefly shaken. Now you must perform atonement and confession to purify yourself."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Who in your life can see the small spiritual deviations you make — the ones you might excuse or overlook — and gently call you back to your highest standard?

## SUTRA 3.24

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से चुलणीपिया समणोवासए अम्मयाए भदाए सत्थवाहीए 'तह' ति एयमट्ट विणओणं पडिसुणेइ, पडिसुणेत्ता तस्स ठाणस्स आलोइत जाव पडिवज्जइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se culaṇīpiyā samaṇovāsae ammayāe bhadāe satthavāhīe 'taha' ti eyamaṭṭha viṇaoṇaṃ paḍisuṇei, paḍisuṇettā tassa ṭhāṇassa āloita jāva paḍivajjai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Chunilipita, having humbly listened to his mother Bhadra Sarthavahi with "so be it," and having heard this counsel with reverence, performed atonement for that lapse — and accepted (the prayashchitta) accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अम्मयाए भदाए सत्थवाहीए** = from his mother Bhadra Sarthavahi
- **'तह' ति** = "so be it" — the word of complete acceptance
- **विणओणं पडिसुणेइ** = listened with humility and reverence
- **तस्स ठाणस्स आलोइत** = performed atonement/confession for that moment (that state/lapse)
- **जाव पडिवज्जइ** = accepted and undertook (the prayashchitta)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chunilipita accepts his mother's counsel with the same word — "taha" (so be it) — that he used when accepting Lord Mahavira's vows. He listens with humility (viṇayoṇaṃ — with reverence/humble attentiveness). He performs ālochana — the confessional introspection in which a practitioner consciously examines a lapse and brings it before a spiritual superior for absolution. Then he accepts the prayashchitta — the appropriate expiation.

This sequence — ālochana, pratikraman, prayashchitta — is the standard Jain framework for purifying the effect of any lapse in practice, however small. The Upasakdashang's inclusion of this sequence even for Chunilipita — who endured more than any other lay practitioner in the text — shows that the Jain path does not demand the impossible. It demands honesty, clarity, and the willingness to purify whatever arose.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Chunilipita humbly accepted his mother's advice with "so be it," and performed the proper confession and atonement for the moment he was briefly distracted.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is the willingness to acknowledge and atone for a small spiritual slip actually a sign of spiritual strength, not weakness?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पढमं उवासगपडिमं** = the first pratima (stage) of the lay follower
- **उवसंपज्जित्तांणं विहरइ** = took up and began living accordingly
- **अहासुत्तं** = as prescribed by scripture (aha = as, suttaṃ = sutra/scripture)
- **जहा आणंदो** = just as Ananda (Chapter 1, the model layperson)
- **जाव एक्कारसमं वि** = up to the eleventh as well

### 5. COMMENTARY

Following the atonement, Chunilipita embarks on the progressive path of the eleven pratimas (stages of lay follower discipline). These are a graduated series of increasingly intensive vows and practices that a layperson takes as they progress toward renunciation:

1. Samyak-darshana (right vision)
2. Vratadhara (vow-holder)
3. Samayika (equanimous meditation practice)
4. Poshadha (fasting on prescribed days)
5. Brahmacharya (celibacy) — progressively
6-11: Increasing stages of renunciation up to the threshold of monastic life

The reference "just as Ananda" again uses the first chapter as the canonical model for the complete practice of all eleven stages.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After the ordeal, Chunilipita took up the progressive stages of spiritual practice one by one — just as the model layperson Ananda had — completing all eleven stages of the lay follower's path.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it look like to approach your own spiritual life as a series of progressive commitments — each one deeper than the last?

## SUTRA 3.26

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से चुलणीपिया समणोवासए तेणं उरालेणं जहा कामदेवो बहूहिं सीलव्वय-गुण-वेरमण-पच्चक्खाण-पोसहोववासेहिं अप्पाणं भावेत्ता, बीसं वासाइं समणोवासग-परियाय पाउणित्ता, एक्कारस य उवासग-पडिमाओ सम्मं कारणं फासित्ता, मासियाए सलेहणाए अत्ताणं झूसित्ता, सट्ठिं भत्ताइं अणसणाए छेदेत्ता, आलोइय-पडिक्कंतें, समाहिपत्ते कालमासे कालं किच्चा सोहम्मे कप्पे सोहम्मवडिंसगस्स महाविमाणस्स उत्तर-पुरित्थमेणं अरुणप्पभे विमाणे देवत्ताए उववण्णे चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई पण्णत्ता। महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ । णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स ।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Chunilipita, through that noble practice — just as Kamadev — purifying himself through many virtuous vows, qualities, renunciations, practices, and poshadha fasting; having completed twenty years in the practice of a lay follower; having properly practiced and touched all eleven stages of the lay follower; having worn away the self through one-month long sallekhana; having broken (his fast) with sixty meal-skippings in final fasting; having performed ālochana (confession) and pratikraman (repentance); having attained equanimity; having departed at the proper time, he was born as a deity in the Saudharma heaven — in the Arunprabha celestial vehicle, in the northeastern direction of the great Saudharma celestial mansion. His lifespan there is declared to be four paliopamas. He will attain liberation in the Mahavideha realm. The conclusion of this chapter is the same as the first.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेणं उरालेणं** = through that noble/great practice
- **जहा कामदेवो** = just as Kamadev (Chapter 2)
- **बीसं वासाइं समणोवासग-परियाय** = twenty years in the practice of a lay follower
- **एक्कारस उवासग-पडिमाओ** = all eleven stages of the lay follower
- **मासियाए सलेहणाए** = through one-month sallekhana (the conscious gradual fast)
- **सट्ठिं भत्ताइं अणसणाए** = sixty meal-skippings (= 30 days of complete fasting, alternating)
- **आलोइय-पडिक्कंतें** = having performed confession and repentance
- **समाहिपत्ते** = having attained equanimity/samadhi
- **सोहम्मे कप्पे** = in the Saudharma heaven (first heavenly realm)
- **अरुणप्पभे विमाणे** = in the Arunprabha celestial vehicle (aruṇa = reddish-dawn colored)
- **चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई** = lifespan of four paliopamas (a paliopama is an incomprehensibly long unit of time)
- **महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ** = will attain liberation in the Mahavideha realm

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chapter 3 concludes with the full arc of Chunilipita's spiritual life. After the divine ordeal, the atonement, and the acceptance of the eleven pratimas, he practices for twenty years as a lay follower with extraordinary dedication — multiple vows, renunciations, fasting practices, and poshadha days. Then, when death approaches, he undertakes sallekhana — the conscious voluntary fast — through sixty meal-skippings (approximately thirty days of complete fasting). He performs final confession and repentance (ālochana-pratikraman), attains inner equanimity (samādhi), and departs.

He is reborn in the Saudharma heaven — the first and most accessible of the Jain heavenly realms, where beings with accumulated merit are born. Specifically, he is born in the Arunprabha ("dawn-red") celestial vehicle, in the northeastern sector of the great Saudharma palace-world. His lifespan there will be four paliopamas — an enormous stretch of divine existence.

But the ultimate goal is stated clearly: "mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihai" — he will attain liberation in the Mahavideha realm. In Jain cosmology, Mahavideha is a realm where Tirthankaras are always present, making liberation possible at any point. Chunilipita's soul will eventually reach that realm and attain moksha — complete and final liberation from all karma.

The closing line "the conclusion is the same as the first chapter" refers to the standardized ending formula of the Upasakdashang, linking all ten chapters as variations of the same fundamental journey: from worldly prosperity, through spiritual commitment, through divine ordeal, through steadfast practice, to liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After practicing for twenty years, completing all eleven stages of lay practice, and undergoing a final voluntary fast with full confession and equanimity, Chunilipita departed from life and was born as a divine being in heaven. He will ultimately attain complete liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you knew that the arc of your life would eventually lead to liberation — regardless of the ordeals along the way — would that change how you face your current difficulties?
