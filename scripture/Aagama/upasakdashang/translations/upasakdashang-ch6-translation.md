# Upasakdashang Sutra — Chapter 6
# Shramanopasak Kundkolik (શ્રમણોપાસક કુંડકોલિક)

**Chapter 6 of the Upasakdashang Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Upasakdashang Sutra, printed pages 113–121 (PDF pages 175–183)*
*Total Sutras: 16*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

This is the sixth chapter of the Upasakdashang Sutra, titled **Kundkolik** — the story of the lay devotee (shramanopasak) Kundkolik. Kundkolik was a householder of Kampillyapur, a prosperous city. His wife's name was Pusha. He was a man of great material wealth — possessing six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in investments, six crore in trade, six types of livestock, and ten thousand head of cattle. He lived in abundance yet was deeply drawn to the spiritual path.

The chapter narrates how Kundkolik came to hear Lord Mahavira's teachings, undertook the vows of a lay follower, practiced spiritual discipline amidst household life, and ultimately attained a noble end through his steadfast adherence to the twelve-fold dharma of a shravak.

## SUTRA 6.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

छट्ठस्स उक्खेवओ । एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं कंपिल्लपुरे णयरे सहस्संबवणे उज्जाणे । जियसत्तू राया । कुंडकोलिए गाहावई । पूसा भारिया ।
छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ, छ वुट्ठिपउत्ताओ, छ पविठ्ठरपउत्ताओ, छ वया, दस गोसहस्सिसएणं वरणं ॥६.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

chaṭṭhassa ukkhevao | evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ kampillapure ṇayare sahassambavaṇe ujjāṇe | jiyasattū rāyā | kuṇḍakolie gāhāvaī | pūsā bhāriyā |
cha hiraṇṇakoḍīo ṇihāṇapauттāo, cha vuṭṭhipauттāo, cha paviṭṭharapauттāo, cha vayā, dasa gosahassisaeṇaṃ varaṇaṃ || 6.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Beginning of the sixth chapter. Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that period, in the city of Kampillyapur, in the garden called Sahasramravan, King Jiyasattu ruled. Kundkolik was a householder. Pusha was his wife. He possessed six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade goods, six types of livestock, and wealth equivalent to ten thousand cattle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **छट्ठस्स उक्खेवओ** = beginning/commencement of the sixth (chapter)
- **एवं खलु जंबू** = thus indeed, O Jambu (the disciple being addressed)
- **तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं** = at that time, in that period
- **कंपिल्लपुरे णयरे** = in the city of Kampillyapur
- **सहस्संबवणे उज्जाणे** = in the garden called Sahasramravan (Thousand-tree garden)
- **जियसत्तू राया** = King Jiyasattu (was the ruler)
- **कुंडकोलिए गाहावई** = Kundkolik was the householder
- **पूसा भारिया** = Pusha was (his) wife
- **छ हिरण्णकोडीओ णिहाणपउत्ताओ** = six crore gold coins deposited in storage
- **छ वुट्ठिपउत्ताओ** = six crore in revenue/investments
- **छ पविठ्ठरपउत्ताओ** = six crore in active trade
- **छ वया** = six types of livestock/vehicles
- **दस गोसहस्सिसएणं वरणं** = wealth of ten thousand head of cattle

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening sutra establishes the setting in the traditional Jain narrative format — beginning with Bhagavan Mahavira's disciple Sudharma Swami narrating the account to his own disciple Jambu. The phrase "thus indeed, O Jambu" signals this chain of oral transmission.

The city of Kampillyapur (modern-day Kampil in Uttar Pradesh) was a prosperous settlement. King Jiyasattu ruled there. In this city lived Kundkolik, a wealthy householder, whose wife was named Pusha. The elaborate description of his wealth — six crore in deposits, six crore in investments, six crore in trade, along with livestock and cattle — follows the standard Jain Agamic pattern of depicting the material prosperity of a shravak before their spiritual transformation. This device demonstrates that the path of renunciation is chosen not out of deprivation, but from the heights of worldly abundance — making the spiritual choice all the more meaningful.

The name "Kundkolik" likely derives from "kunda" (a type of jasmine flower or a pit/well) and "kolik" (related to the weaver or family clan). His story follows the same narrative arc as the five previous chapters: each upasaka is introduced in full worldly splendor before the encounter with Lord Mahavira transforms their life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In the city of Kampillyapur, there lived a very wealthy householder named Kundkolik with his wife Pusha — he had enormous riches in gold, trade, and cattle.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you define yourself by what you possess, or by what you are willing to let go of?

## SUTRA 6.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सामी समोसढे । जहा कामदेवो तहा कुंडकोलिए गाहावई ॥६.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

sāmī samosaḍhe | jahā kāmadevo tahā kuṇḍakolie gāhāvaī || 6.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The Lord (Mahavira) arrived. Just as with Kamadev (the householder of Chapter 2), so too with the householder Kundkolik.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सामी** = the Lord, the Master (Bhagavan Mahavira)
- **समोसढे** = arrived, descended, came to (the city/assembly)
- **जहा कामदेवो** = just as (in the case of) Kamadev
- **तहा** = so too, in the same way
- **कुंडकोलिए गाहावई** = (with) the householder Kundkolik

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is an important structural sutra that uses the standard Jain Agamic abbreviation technique. Rather than repeating the entire description of Lord Mahavira's arrival — his divine assembly, the celestial arrangements, the gathering of monks, nuns, laypeople, and celestials — the text simply says "just as with Kamadev." This refers back to Chapter 2 (Kamadev adhyayana), where the full elaborate description of Bhagavan's arrival was given in detail. The reader is expected to mentally supply all those details here.

This literary device, common in the Agamas, is both a memory aid for oral transmission and a way to keep the text concise while maintaining the full grandeur of the scene. The arrival of a Tirthankara in a city was described as a cosmic event — gods descending to prepare the assembly, divine canopies appearing, celestial music playing, and the earth itself becoming purified. All of this is implied in this single compact sutra.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lord Mahavira came to the city — and everything that happened when he visited the householder Kamadev in an earlier chapter happened here too.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When truth arrives at your doorstep, are you ready to receive it, or do you need repeated invitations?

## SUTRA 6.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कुण्डकोलिए समणोवासए अण्णया कयाइ पुव्वावरहकालसमयंसि जेणेव असोगवणिया, जेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता गोसालस्स मंखलिपुत्तस्स धम्मपण्णत्ती– णत्थि उज्जाणे इ वा, कम्मे इ वा, बले इ वा, वीरिए इ वा, पुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे इ वा, णिययासव्वभावा, मंगुली णं समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स धम्मपण्णत्ती– अत्थि उट्टाणे इ वा जाव अणिययासव्वभावा, तं ते मिच्छा ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsae aṇṇayā kayāi puvvāvarahakālasamayaṃsi jeṇeva asogavaṇiyā, jeṇeva uvāgacchai, uvāgacchittā gosālassa maṃkhaliputtassa dhammappaṇṇattī– ṇatthi ujjāṇe i vā, kamme i vā, bale i vā, vīrie i vā, purisakkkāra-parakkame i vā, ṇiyayāsavvabhāvā, maṃgulī ṇaṃ samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa dhammappaṇṇattī– atthi uṭṭāṇe i vā jāva aṇiyayāsavvabhāvā, taṃ te micchā || 6.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Kundkolik, on a certain occasion, at a previous time, went to the Ashoka garden. Having gone there, he encountered the doctrine of Gosala Mankhaliputta which states: "There is no effort, no action, no strength, no energy, no human exertion — everything is governed by fate." But the doctrine of the ascetic Lord Mahavira declares: "There is effort, there is action, there is energy, there is human endeavor — things are not determined solely by fate." That (first doctrine of Gosala) is false.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से** = then indeed, he (Kundkolik)
- **कुण्डकोलिए समणोवासए** = the lay follower Kundkolik
- **अण्णया कयाइ** = on a certain occasion, once
- **पुव्वावरहकालसमयंसि** = at a previous time period
- **जेणेव असोगवणिया** = went to the Ashoka garden
- **गोसालस्स मंखलिपुत्तस्स** = of Gosala Mankhaliputta (the Ajivika teacher)
- **धम्मपण्णत्ती** = the doctrine/teaching proclaimed
- **णत्थि उज्जाणे** = there is no effort (according to Gosala)
- **कम्मे** = action/karma
- **बले** = strength
- **वीरिए** = energy/virility
- **पुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे** = human exertion and endeavor
- **णिययासव्वभावा** = everything governed by inherent nature/fate (niyati)
- **समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स** = of the ascetic Lord Mahavira
- **अत्थि उट्टाणे** = there is effort (according to Mahavira)
- **तं ते मिच्छा** = that (Gosala's view) is false/wrong

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra presents a pivotal philosophical confrontation. Before encountering Lord Mahavira's teaching, Kundkolik had been exposed to the doctrine of Gosala Mankhaliputta, the founder of the Ajivika school — one of the prominent rival sects during Mahavira's time. Gosala taught absolute fatalism: that nothing anyone does matters because everything is predetermined by fate. There is no effort, no action, no strength, no energy, no human endeavor — all beings cycle through existence mechanically until they are eventually freed after a fixed number of births.

Lord Mahavira's teaching directly contradicts this. The Jain position affirms human agency: effort exists, action matters, strength has purpose, energy produces results, and human endeavor makes a real difference. The soul's bondage is caused by karma accumulated through one's own choices and actions, and liberation is achieved through one's own disciplined effort — not by waiting for fate to run its course.

The sutra says plainly: Gosala's teaching is "false" (micchā). This is not merely an intellectual disagreement — it is a fundamental assertion that individual moral responsibility exists. The entire ethical edifice of Jainism — the vows, restraints, penances, and spiritual practices — rests on this foundation that what you do truly matters.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Kundkolik had heard a teacher named Gosala say that nothing matters because everything is fated — but Lord Mahavira taught that your actions do matter, and Gosala's teaching was wrong.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you believe your efforts matter, or do you secretly use "fate" as an excuse for inaction?

## SUTRA 6.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे कुंडकोलिए उपसगं–पिसाय रूपं दुपट्टो, विणिच्छिट्ट इ वा, कम्मे इ वा, बले इ वा, वीरिए इ वा, पुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे इ वा, अणिययासव्वभावा ॥६.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve kuṇḍakolie uvasagaṃ–pisāya rūpaṃ dupaṭṭo, viṇicchiṭṭa i vā, kamme i vā, bale i vā, vīrie i vā, purisakkāra-parakkame i vā, aṇiyayāsavvabhāvā || 6.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then a celestial being tested Kundkolik by creating an apparition, having determined to examine whether effort, action, strength, energy, and human endeavor truly exist or whether everything is governed by inherent nature/fate.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से देवे** = then indeed, that celestial being (deva)
- **कुंडकोलिए** = (directed at) Kundkolik
- **उपसगं** = an obstacle/test/affliction (upsarga)
- **पिसाय रूपं** = in the form of a demon/frightening apparition
- **दुपट्टो** = having assumed, taking the form of
- **विणिच्छिट्ट** = having determined/decided
- **कम्मे, बले, वीरिए, पुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे** = action, strength, energy, human endeavor
- **अणिययासव्वभावा** = not governed solely by fate

### 5. COMMENTARY

A celestial being — a deva — decides to test Kundkolik's spiritual resolve. This is a common motif in the Jain Agamas: after a layperson accepts the true teaching and begins practicing, a celestial being creates obstacles (upsarga) to test whether the faith is genuine and unshakable.

The deva takes a frightening demonic form. The test is fundamentally about the very philosophical question raised in the previous sutra: does human effort matter, or is everything predetermined? By creating a terrifying obstacle, the deva is essentially asking: "Will you rely on your own spiritual strength and discipline, or will you collapse and prove that individual effort is meaningless?"

This pattern of divine testing appears in every chapter of the Upasakdashang — it represents the universal truth that genuine spiritual commitment will always be tested, whether by external circumstances, temptations, or fears.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A heavenly being decided to test Kundkolik by appearing as a terrifying demon — to see if his faith and spiritual strength were real.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When life tests your deepest convictions, do you hold firm or do you discover they were only surface-level beliefs?

## SUTRA 6.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे णाममुद्दगं च उत्तरिज्जगं च पुढविसिला-पट्टयाओ गेण्हइ, गेणिहत्ता अंतलिक्ख-पडिवण्णे एवं वयासी– हं भो गेणिहत्ता कुंडकोलिए ! तं चेव सव्वं जाव से पूणं गोसालगमंखलिपुत्तं णगिहाय एवं वुत्ते ॥६.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve ṇāmamuddagaṃ ca uttarijjagaṃ ca puḍhavisilā-paṭṭayāo geṇhai, geṇihattā aṃtalikkha-paḍivaṇṇe evaṃ vayāsī– haṃ bho geṇihattā kuṇḍakolie ! taṃ ceva savvaṃ jāva se pūṇaṃ gosālagamaṃkhaliputtaṃ ṇagihāya evaṃ vutte || 6.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being seized the stone slabs bearing the name-inscription and the upper covering, and having risen into the sky, spoke thus: "Ho there, Kundkolik!" — and repeated all that had been said before, refuting the doctrine of Gosala Mankhaliputta.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से देवे** = then that celestial being
- **णाममुद्दगं** = the name-inscription (name-seal/plate)
- **उत्तरिज्जगं** = the upper covering/canopy
- **पुढविसिला-पट्टयाओ** = stone slabs of the earth/ground
- **गेण्हइ** = seized, took hold of
- **अंतलिक्ख-पडिवण्णे** = having risen into the sky, stationed in mid-air
- **एवं वयासी** = spoke thus
- **हं भो** = ho there! (an exclamation of address)
- **गोसालगमंखलिपुत्तं णगिहाया** = refuting Gosala Mankhaliputta
- **एवं वुत्ते** = thus was spoken

### 5. COMMENTARY

The deva escalates the test. He seizes the inscribed stone slabs — perhaps memorial or dedication stones — and the canopy above, and levitates into the sky. From mid-air, the celestial being calls out to Kundkolik and repeats the philosophical argument against Gosala's fatalism.

This dramatic scene serves multiple purposes. First, the supernatural display demonstrates that there are forces beyond ordinary human perception — the deva literally rises into the air. Second, by repeating the refutation of Gosala's doctrine, the narrative reinforces the central teaching: human effort is real and meaningful.

The deva's ability to seize physical objects and fly demonstrates the power that comes from previous meritorious actions — itself a refutation of fatalism. If everything were predetermined with no role for action, how could a being accumulate the merit to be born as a powerful deva? The deva's very existence as a powerful celestial being is proof that karma — the fruit of one's own actions — is real.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The celestial being flew into the sky carrying stone slabs, called out to Kundkolik, and argued against the idea that fate controls everything.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What truths have you heard from unexpected sources, delivered in ways you never anticipated?

## SUTRA 6.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए अण्णया कयाई पुव्वावरहकालसमयंसि जेणेव असोगवणिया, जेणेव अगवण्णिया— तं देवं पाउब्भविस्था ।
अपुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे इ वा, जाव अणिययासव्वभावा ॥६.६॥

tae ṇaṃ se kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsae aṇṇayā kayāī puvvāvarahakālasamayaṃsi jeṇeva asogavaṇiyā, jeṇeva agavaṇṇiyā— taṃ devaṃ pāubbhavisthā |
apurisakkāra-parakkame i vā, jāva aṇiyayāsavvabhāvā || 6.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Kundkolik, on a certain occasion at a previous time, in the Ashoka garden, encountered that celestial being who had manifested — and rejected the doctrine that denies human effort and claims everything is governed by fate.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए** = then the lay follower Kundkolik
- **अण्णया कयाई** = on a certain occasion
- **पुव्वावरहकालसमयंसि** = at a previous time
- **असोगवणिया** = in the Ashoka garden
- **तं देवं पाउब्भविस्था** = that deva who had manifested/appeared
- **अपुरिसक्कार-परक्कमे** = denying human effort and endeavor
- **अणिययासव्वभावा** = (claiming) everything governed by inherent fate

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkolik encounters the deva's test and stands firm. He rejects the fatalistic doctrine. This is the turning point in the test — despite the dramatic supernatural display, Kundkolik does not waver from his conviction that human effort matters. He has accepted Lord Mahavira's teaching that action has consequences, that individual endeavor leads to spiritual progress, and that fatalism is a false doctrine.

The significance is profound: a layperson, not a monk, is demonstrating philosophical clarity and spiritual firmness. The Upasakdashang repeatedly shows that householders — people living ordinary lives with families and businesses — can achieve remarkable spiritual conviction. One does not need to renounce the world to have firm and correct understanding. What matters is the clarity of one's view and the sincerity of one's practice.

Despite the deva's dramatic test, Kundkolik held firm in his belief that human effort truly matters and rejected the idea that everything is just fate.

## SUTRA 6.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से सद्दालपुत्ते आजीविओवासगस्स अंतिए एगं देवे पाउब्भविस्था ॥६.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se saddālaputte ājīviovāsagassa aṃtie egaṃ deve pāubbhavisthā || 6.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then near the Ajivika follower Saddalaputra, a celestial being manifested.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से** = then indeed
- **सद्दालपुत्ते** = Saddalaputra (a follower of the Ajivika sect) ⚠️
- **आजीविओवासगस्स** = of the Ajivika lay follower
- **अंतिए** = near, in the presence of
- **एगं देवे** = one celestial being
- **पाउब्भविस्था** = manifested, appeared

### 5. COMMENTARY

The narrative briefly shifts to another character — Saddalaputra, who is a follower of the Ajivika sect (Gosala's school). A celestial being appears near him as well. This parallel testing serves a narrative purpose: both a Jain layperson (Kundkolik) and an Ajivika follower (Saddalaputra) are tested, and the contrast in their responses will demonstrate which philosophical view is correct.

The Ajivika sect, founded by Makkhali Gosala (who was once associated with Mahavira before their paths diverged), taught that all beings are destined to cycle through exactly 8,400,000 great cycles of birth before automatically attaining liberation. No action could speed up or slow down this process. This deterministic worldview was one of the most significant philosophical rivals to Jainism in ancient India.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A celestial being also appeared near Saddalaputra, who was a follower of the rival fatalistic school — setting up a comparison between the two believers.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 6.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे अंतलिक्ख-पडिवण्णे एवं वयासी— हं भो कुंडकोलिए ! तं ते मिच्छा ।
शब्दार्थ :— मिच्छा = मिथ्या, उत्थान चवसि = कहो छो. ⚠️

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve aṃtalikkha-paḍivaṇṇe evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho kuṇḍakolie ! taṃ te micchā || 6.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being, stationed in mid-air, spoke thus: "Ho there, Kundkolik! That (doctrine of yours) is false!"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से देवे** = then that celestial being
- **अंतलिक्ख-पडिवण्णे** = stationed in mid-air, hovering in the sky
- **एवं वयासी** = spoke thus
- **हं भो कुंडकोलिए** = ho there, Kundkolik!
- **तं ते मिच्छा** = that (belief) of yours is false

### 5. COMMENTARY

The deva now directly confronts Kundkolik by declaring his faith in Mahavira's teaching to be "false." This is the core of the test: a powerful supernatural being — hovering in the sky in a display of otherworldly power — is telling Kundkolik that everything he believes is wrong. The temptation to doubt is immense. After all, this being has just demonstrated abilities beyond human capacity. Surely such a powerful entity must know the truth?

But this is precisely the test. Spiritual conviction must not be swayed by displays of power. A being with supernatural abilities is not necessarily a being with correct knowledge. In Jain philosophy, even celestial beings (devas) can hold wrong views. Only a fully omniscient being — a Kevali or Tirthankara — can be relied upon for absolute truth. Power and knowledge are not the same thing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The celestial being hovered in the sky and told Kundkolik: "Your beliefs are wrong!" — testing whether he would abandon his faith under pressure.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you tell the difference between power and truth? Just because someone is powerful, does that make them right?

## SUTRA 6.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए इमीसे कहाए लद्धट्ठे हट्ट-तुट्ठे जाव तहा कामदेवो तहा ते कुंडकोलिए ⚠️ ॥६.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsae imīse kahāe laddhaṭṭhe haṭṭa-tuṭṭhe jāva tahā kāmadevo tahā te kuṇḍakolie || 6.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Kundkolik, having understood the meaning of this teaching, became delighted and joyful — and just as it was with Kamadev (in Chapter 2), so it was with Kundkolik.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं से कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए** = then the lay follower Kundkolik
- **इमीसे कहाए** = by this teaching/narrative
- **लद्धट्ठे** = having attained/understood the meaning
- **हट्ट-तुट्ठे** = delighted and joyful
- **जाव** = up to, as far as (abbreviation marker)
- **तहा कामदेवो तहा ते कुंडकोलिए** = as with Kamadev, so with Kundkolik

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkolik passes the test. He does not waver. Having understood the teaching — that individual effort matters, that karma is real, that one's own actions determine one's destiny — he is filled with joy and conviction. The text again uses the abbreviation formula "just as Kamadev," meaning that all the details described in Chapter 2 about how the layperson received the teaching, took vows, and began practicing apply equally to Kundkolik.

The phrase "delighted and joyful" (hatta-tuttha) is significant. True spiritual understanding brings joy — not the fleeting pleasure of sensory gratification, but the deep satisfaction of knowing the truth and having the courage to live by it. Kundkolik's joy comes from certainty: he knows that his efforts on the spiritual path are not futile, that his choices matter, and that liberation is achievable through his own disciplined practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Kundkolik understood the teaching, remained firm in his faith despite the test, and was filled with deep joy — following the same path as the devoted layperson Kamadev before him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time understanding a truth brought you genuine, deep joy — not just intellectual satisfaction, but a feeling of coming home?

## SUTRA 6.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं सामी समोसढे ॥६.१०॥

teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ sāmī samosaḍhe || 6.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सामी** = the Lord, the Master (Bhagavan Mahavira)
- **समोसढे** = arrived, descended

### 5. COMMENTARY

Lord Mahavira arrives in the region where Kundkolik lives. This sutra marks the transition from the preliminary testing phase to the actual encounter with the Tirthankara. Everything that happened before — the philosophical confusion, the deva's test, Kundkolik's steadfastness — was preparation for this moment.

In the Jain tradition, the arrival of a Tirthankara in a region is considered an event of cosmic significance. It is not merely a holy person visiting a city; it is the descent of omniscient truth into a locality. The entire atmosphere transforms — the teaching assembly (samavasarana) is established, celestial beings descend to attend, and all beings in the vicinity have the opportunity to hear the truth directly from the most authoritative source possible.

Through the divine sound (divya-dhvani), the Tirthankara's teaching reached each being in their own language and at their own level of understanding. For Kundkolik, who had already been tested and had demonstrated his philosophical clarity, the discourse would have confirmed and deepened his understanding.

## SUTRA 6.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कुंडकोलिया ! ति समणे भगवं महावीरे कुंडकोलिसएणं एवं वयासी— से पूणं कुंडकोलिए ॥६.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

kuṇḍakoliyā ! ti samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre kuṇḍakolisaeṇaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— se pūṇaṃ kuṇḍakolie || 6.12 ||

- **ति** = thus (quotation marker)
- **समणे भगवं महावीरे** = the ascetic Lord Mahavira
- **कुंडकोलिसएणं** = to/with Kundkolik

Lord Mahavira personally addresses Kundkolik by name. In the Agamic tradition, when the Tirthankara directly calls a layperson by name, it signifies a special relationship and recognition. By calling Kundkolik by name, Mahavira acknowledges him as one who is ready to take the formal vows of a shravak.

The phrase "that same Kundkolik" emphasizes identity and continuity — this is the same person who was tested by the deva, who withstood the challenge to his faith, who rejected fatalism and affirmed the reality of human effort. ## SUTRA 6.13

ई समणे भगवं महावीरे समणे णिग्गंथीओ य समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स तहा कामदेवो तहा ते कुंडकोलिए ⚠️ ॥६.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ī samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre samaṇe ṇiggaṃthīo ya samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa tahā kāmadevo tahā te kuṇḍakolie || 6.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus the ascetic Lord Mahavira, along with the Nirgrantha monks and nuns — just as it was with Kamadev, so it was with Kundkolik.

- **समणे णिग्गंथीओ** = the Nirgrantha monks (and nuns)

By referencing Kamadev's chapter, the text implies that Kundkolik undertook the same commitments: the five minor vows (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibity/fidelity, non-possessiveness), the three auxiliary vows (limiting the area of activity, limiting possessions, purposeful meditation), and the four disciplinary vows (equanimous death, fasting, limiting consumables, and offering food/support to monks).

## SUTRA 6.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं समणा णिग्गंथा य समणस्स भगवओ तहा कुंडकोलिए समणोवासएणं एवं वुत्ते— 'तह' ति कट्टु विहरइ ॥६.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ samaṇā ṇiggaṃthā ya samaṇassa bhagavao tahā kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsaeṇaṃ evaṃ vutte— 'taha' ti kaṭṭu viharai || 6.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the Nirgrantha monks, along with the Lord's order, having thus instructed the lay follower Kundkolik — he accepted with "so be it" and began to live accordingly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं** = then
- **समणा णिग्गंथा** = the Nirgrantha monks
- **समणस्स भगवओ** = of the Lord (Mahavira)
- **कुंडकोलिए समणोवासएणं** = the lay follower Kundkolik
- **एवं वुत्ते** = thus having been instructed
- **'तह' ति** = "so be it" / "yes, I accept"
- **कट्टु** = having done/accepted
- **विहरइ** = lives, conducts himself, abides

### 5. COMMENTARY

The single word "taha" ("so be it") carries immense weight. When Kundkolik says "taha," he is accepting the complete dharma of a lay follower — all the vows, all the restraints, all the commitments. It is a word of total acceptance.

The phrase "viharai" (lives/abides) means that this was not merely a verbal acceptance but a lived reality. Kundkolik began conducting his life in accordance with the vows. He continued as a householder — running his business, living with his family — but now every action was governed by spiritual discipline. This is the essence of the Upasakdashang's teaching: spiritual transformation does not require abandoning worldly responsibilities, but it does require transforming how one engages with them.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Kundkolik accepted all the teachings by saying "so be it" and began living his life according to the vows he had taken.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would change in your daily life if you truly said "so be it" to your deepest spiritual convictions?

## SUTRA 6.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए भगवं महावीरे वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता, पुच्छित्ता पुच्छित्ता अट्टमादिवय, अट्टमादिवय चेइयवंदणं पज्जुवासइ जाव तत्थ गमणाणं ण विहरइ ॥६.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsae bhagavaṃ mahāvīre vaṃdittā ṇamaṃsittā, pucchittā pucchittā aṭṭhamādivaya, aṭṭhamādivaya ceiyavaṃdaṇaṃ pajjuvāsai jāva tattha gamaṇāṇaṃ ṇa viharai || 6.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Kundkolik, having bowed to and saluted Lord Mahavira, asking questions again and again, observed the eighth-day fasting vow, worshipped at the shrine, and served (the Lord) — and did not depart from that place (of practice).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **भगवं महावीरे वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता** = having bowed to and saluted Lord Mahavira
- **पुच्छित्ता पुच्छित्ता** = asking questions again and again
- **अट्टमादिवय** = the eighth-day fasting (ashtami fasting vow)
- **चेइयवंदणं** = worship at the temple/shrine
- **पज्जुवासइ** = serves, attends upon, practices devotion
- **तत्थ गमणाणं ण विहरइ** = does not depart from there (remains steadfast in practice)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra describes Kundkolik's active spiritual practice after taking the vows. He is not a passive believer — he bows to the Tirthankara, asks questions repeatedly (showing his desire to understand deeply), observes the fasting vow on the eighth day of each lunar fortnight, visits the shrine for worship, and serves the monastic community.

The phrase "asking questions again and again" is noteworthy. In the Jain tradition, questioning is not a sign of doubt but of sincere engagement. A true seeker asks, reflects, asks again, and deepens understanding progressively. Kundkolik was not content with surface-level acceptance — he wanted thorough comprehension.

The eighth-day fast (atthami) is a significant lay practice: on the eighth day of each lunar half, the layperson fasts completely, spending the day in meditation and scriptural study. The fact that Kundkolik maintained this discipline regularly shows the seriousness of his commitment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Kundkolik became a devoted practitioner — he bowed to Lord Mahavira, asked many questions, fasted regularly, worshipped at the temple, and remained steadfast in his spiritual life.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is your spiritual practice something you do occasionally, or is it woven into the rhythm of your life?

## SUTRA 6.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स कुंडकोलिए समणोवासयस्स बहूहिं सील जाव कुंडकोलिए समणोवासए धम्मपव्वतिं-धम्म साधणामां लीण थई ।
कुंडकोलिए सामाधिमरणं कइ ⚠️ ॥६.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsayassa bahūhiṃ sīla jāva kuṇḍakolie samaṇovāsae dhammappavattiṃ-dhamma sādhaṇāmāṃ līṇa thaī |
kuṇḍakolie sāmādhimaraṇaṃ kai || 6.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Kundkolik, endowed with many virtues, remained absorbed in the practice of dharma and spiritual discipline. Kundkolik attained a death in meditation (samadhimaran — a peaceful, spiritually conscious death).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं तस्स** = then, of that
- **कुंडकोलिए समणोवासयस्स** = of the lay follower Kundkolik
- **बहूहिं सील** = endowed with many virtues/ethical qualities
- **धम्मपव्वतिं** = the practice/propagation of dharma
- **धम्म साधणामां** = in the spiritual discipline of dharma
- **लीण थई** = remained absorbed, was established
- **सामाधिमरणं कइ** = attained death in meditation (peaceful conscious death)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter concludes with the culmination of Kundkolik's spiritual journey as a householder. After living a life of discipline, virtue, and sincere practice, he attains the highest achievement possible for a layperson in this life: samadhimaran — death in a state of complete equanimity and spiritual awareness.

Samadhimaran is not suicide or the seeking of death. It is the natural conclusion of a life lived in spiritual awareness. When death approaches — whether through illness, old age, or circumstance — the practitioner faces it without fear, without attachment, without aversion. The mind remains in a state of equanimous meditation. There is no clinging to life, no terror of what comes next, no regret for what was left undone. There is only peace, clarity, and conscious surrender.

For Kundkolik, this meant that despite living as a householder with all its entanglements — wealth, family, business, social obligations — he had purified his inner life to such a degree that when death came, his mind was undisturbed. This is the ultimate message of the Upasakdashang: it is possible to live in the world and still achieve profound spiritual realization.

The entire arc of Kundkolik's story — from philosophical confusion about fatalism versus free will, through divine testing, to conviction, practice, and ultimately peaceful death — demonstrates the Jain ideal of a lay spiritual life: one that combines worldly engagement with inner transformation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After living a life of virtue and spiritual practice, Kundkolik died peacefully in a state of meditation — the highest spiritual achievement possible for a householder.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you knew this was your last day, would you face it with peace and equanimity — or with clinging and fear?
