# Upasakdashang — Chapter 9: Shramanopasak Nandinipita

**Setting:** Shravasti (Sāvatthī), Koshtuk shrine, King Jiyasattu  
**Protagonist:** Nandinipita (ṇandīṇīpiyā) — "one who has found joy in the abode"  
**Wealth:** 12 crore gold coins (4 deposited, 4 in trade, 4 in household); 4 gokulas × 10,000 cows = 40,000 cows  
**Wife:** Ashvini (Assinī)  
**Key feature:** No divine ordeal (upsarg) — entirely peaceful practice  
**Practice:** 14 years active + 6 years retirement (nivritti) = 20 years total, with sallekhana  
**Afterlife:** Saudharma heaven, Arunagav viman (vehicle), 4 paliopamas, then Mahavideha → liberation

---

## Sutra 1

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
नवमस्स उक्खेवो । एवं खलु जम्बू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं सावत्थी नयरी । कोट्टुए चेइए । जियसत्तु राया । तत्थ णं सावत्थीए णयरीए णंदीणीपिया णामं गाहावई परिवसइ । अट्ठे जाव बहुजणस्स अपरिभूए । चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ गिण्हाणपट्टाओ, चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ वुट्टिपट्टाओ, चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ पवित्थरपट्टाओ, चत्तारि वया, दस गोसाहसिएणं वण्णं । अस्सिणी भारिआ ।

### Roman Transliteration
Navamass ukkhevo. Evam khalu Jambū! Teṇam kāleṇam teṇam samayeṇam Sāvatthī nayrī. Koṭṭhue ceiae. Jiyasattu rāyā. Tattha ṇam Sāvatthīe nayrīe Ṇandīṇīpiyā nām gāhāvaī parīvasaī. Aṭṭhe jāv bahujanas aparibhūe. Cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo giṇhāṇapaṭṭāo, cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo vuṭṭhipaṭṭāo, cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo paviṭṭharapaṭṭāo, cāttāri vayā, dasa go-sāhasieṇam vaṇṇam. Assinī bhāriā.

### Literal English Translation
The opening of the ninth chapter. Thus it was, O Jambū! At that time and in that era, the city of Shravasti existed. There was the Koshtuk shrine. King Jiyasattu ruled. In that city of Shravasti, there lived a householder named Nandinipita. He was wealthy — (and so on) up to: revered by many people. He held four crore gold coins in deposited form, four crore in circulating trade, and four crore in household assets; he had four gokulas, each with ten thousand cows. His wife's name was Ashvini.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Navamass ukkhevo* — opening/setting of the ninth (chapter)
- *Evam khalu Jambū* — thus indeed, O Jambū
- *teṇam kāleṇam teṇam samayeṇam* — at that time, in that era
- *Sāvatthī nayrī* — the city of Shravasti
- *Koṭṭhue ceiae* — the Koshtuk shrine
- *Jiyasattu rāyā* — King Jiyasattu (ruled)
- *Ṇandīṇīpiyā nām gāhāvaī* — a guildmaster/householder named Nandinipita
- *Aṭṭhe jāv bahujanas aparibhūe* — wealthy, (full description) up to: not disrespected by many people
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo giṇhāṇapaṭṭāo* — four crore gold coins in deposited treasury
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo vuṭṭhipaṭṭāo* — four crore in circulating business
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo paviṭṭharapaṭṭāo* — four crore in household assets
- *cāttāri vayā* — four gokulas (cattle herds)
- *dasa go-sāhasieṇam vaṇṇam* — ten-thousand cows (in each)
- *Assinī bhāriā* — wife named Ashvini

### Commentary
The ninth chapter opens with the now-familiar setting — Shravasti, the Koshtuk shrine, King Jiyasattu — the stage shared by all ten chapters of the Upasakdashang. Nandinipita ("one who has found joy in the abode") was a prosperous merchant-guildmaster whose 12 crore gold coins were prudently divided: four crore kept secure in the royal treasury, four crore actively deployed in trade, and four crore as household wealth. He also maintained four cattle stations, each with 10,000 cows — a mark of agricultural affluence. His wife was Ashvini, named after the first lunar asterism associated with physicians and healers.

What distinguishes this chapter from those that came before is the complete absence of any divine ordeal. No deva appears to kill his children, destroy his wealth, or afflict his body. Nandinipita's story is one of quiet, unbroken devotion — a portrait of the householder path when walked with steady sincerity, undramatic but no less complete.

### Simply Put
The ninth chapter begins in Shravasti with the same setting as all prior chapters. Nandinipita was a wealthy and respected guildmaster with 12 crore gold coins and 40,000 cows. His wife was named Ashvini. Unlike many of the earlier disciples, no ordeal ever tested him — his entire spiritual journey unfolded in undisturbed peace.

## Sutra 2

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
सामी समोसढे । जहा आणंदो तहेव गिहिधम्मं पडिवज्जइ । सामी बहिया विहरढे ।

### Roman Transliteration
Sāmī samosaḍhe. Jahā Āṇamdo taheva giṇhidhammam paḍivajjai. Sāmī bahiyā viharḍhe.

### Literal English Translation
The Lord arrived and established his samavaran assembly. Just as Ananda (received and took his lay dharma), so too did (Nandinipita) accept the householder's dharma. The Lord then departed to wander elsewhere.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Sāmī samosaḍhe* — the Lord (Mahavir) arrived and set up samavaran (omniscient assembly hall)
- *Jahā Āṇamdo taheva* — just as Ananda (did), so too in the same way
- *giṇhidhammam paḍivajjai* — he accepted/took the householder's dharma (twelve vows)
- *Sāmī bahiyā viharḍhe* — the Lord went to wander elsewhere (outside)

### Commentary
Bhagavan Mahavir arrived in Shravasti and established his samavaran — the divine assembly that gathers spontaneously in his presence, drawing devotees from all directions. Among the faithful who came to hear was Nandinipita. The teachings moved him deeply, and he took the twelve vows of a shravak (Jain layperson) — the same vows taken by Ananda in the first chapter: five anuvratas (minor ethical vows), three gunavratas (qualifying vows), and four shikshavratas (educational vows). After initiating Nandinipita, Bhagavan continued his wandering ministry elsewhere.

The sutra's brevity — just three phrases — signals that Nandinipita's initiation was straightforward and unencumbered. No elaborate ceremony, no dramatic conversion. He heard, he understood, he accepted.

### Simply Put
Mahavir arrived in Shravasti. Nandinipita went to hear his teachings and — just like Ananda in the first chapter — took the twelve vows of a Jain layperson. Mahavir then continued his journey.

## Sutra 3

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
तए णं से णंदीणीपिया समणोवासए जाव विहरढे ।

### Roman Transliteration
Tae ṇam se Ṇandīṇīpiyā samaṇovāsae jāv viharḍhe.

### Literal English Translation
Thereupon Nandinipita became a shramanopasak (Jain lay follower) and lived (practicing the dharma in full).

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Tae ṇam* — thereupon, at that point
- *se Ṇandīṇīpiyā* — that Nandinipita
- *samaṇovāsae* — (became) a shramanopasak (lay devotee of the shramana path)
- *jāv viharḍhe* — and lived (accordingly, the full description understood via yāvat)

### Commentary
Having taken his vows, Nandinipita began living the life of a committed shramanopasak — observing his anuvratas with care, practicing samayik (equanimity meditation), observing poshadha (periodic fasting), and progressively deepening his practice through the stages of pratima. The yāvat (jāv) here is the Agamic shorthand indicating that the full description of his diligent, growing practice is identical to what was narrated for earlier disciples and need not be repeated.

There is a quiet dignity in this sutra. Nandinipita did not need divine pressure to live his faith. He simply became, and lived.

### Simply Put
Nandinipita became a devoted Jain lay follower and lived out his daily life in keeping with his vows — practicing meditation, fasting, and the progressive disciplines of the householder path.

## Sutra 4

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
तए णं तस्स णंदीणीपियस्स समणोवासयस्स बहूहिं सीलव्वयगुण जाव भावेमाणस्स चोद्दस संवच्छराइं वइक्कंताइ । तहेव जेट्ठु पुत्तं ठवेइ । धम्मपण्णत्तिं । वीसं वासाइं परियागं । णाणत्ते — अरुणगवे विमाणे उववाओ महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ । णिक्खेवओ जहा पढमस्स ।

### Roman Transliteration
Tae ṇam tassa Ṇandīṇīpiyassa samaṇovāsayassa bahūhim sīlavvayaguṇa jāv bhāvemāṇassa coḍasa samvacchrāi vaikkamtāi. Taheva jeṭṭhu puttam ṭhavei. Dhammpaṇṇattim. Vīsam vāsāi pariyāgam. Ṇāṇatte — aruṇagave vimāṇe uvavāo mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihaī. Ṇikhkhevao jahā paḍhamass.

### Literal English Translation
Then, as the shramanopasak Nandinipita was nurturing himself through many virtues, minor vows, and spiritual qualities — (full description) up to — fourteen years passed. Just as (earlier disciples did), he then installed his eldest son (in charge of household affairs). (He received) the proclamation of dharma. His total renunciation period was twenty years. Special note: he was born in the Arunagav celestial vehicle; he will attain liberation while dwelling in Mahavideha. The concluding verse is as in the first chapter.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *bahūhim sīlavvayaguṇa jāv bhāvemāṇassa* — cultivating himself with many virtues, vows, and qualities (full description via yāvat)
- *coḍasa samvacchrāi vaikkamtāi* — fourteen years passed/elapsed
- *jeṭṭhu puttam ṭhavei* — installed (placed) his eldest son (as household head)
- *Dhammpaṇṇattim* — the dharma proclamation (accepted/understood)
- *Vīsam vāsāi pariyāgam* — twenty years of renunciation/practice total
- *Ṇāṇatte* — the special distinction/difference (from earlier chapters)
- *aruṇagave vimāṇe uvavāo* — born in the Arunagav celestial vehicle
- *mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihaī* — will attain liberation while dwelling in Mahavideha
- *Ṇikhkhevao jahā paḍhamass* — the closing is as in the first chapter

### Commentary
Over fourteen years of active householder practice, Nandinipita cultivated all the spiritual qualities befitting a shramanopasak — observing anuvratas, performing samayik, practicing poshadha, progressing through the stages of pratima (the eleven graduated stages of lay discipline). At this natural turning point, he followed the established pattern of the great lay disciples: he transferred all family and social responsibilities to his eldest son Jayesh, fully withdrawing from worldly affairs.

The remaining six years were spent in complete retirement (nivritti) — residing in a poshdhashala (monastic meditation hall), deepening his practice without any household obligations. This retirement culminated in sallekhana (santhara) — the Jain practice of voluntarily thinning the body through progressive fasting as death approaches, embraced consciously and with equanimity. His total practice spanned twenty years.

The *ṇāṇatte* (special note) marks the one distinctive element of this chapter: the Arunagav celestial vehicle. Unlike Chunilipita (Ch3: Arunprabha), Suradeva (Ch4: Arunakant), or Chullashatak (Ch5: Arunsiddha), Nandinipita is reborn in the Arunagav viman in the Saudharma heaven, with a lifespan of four paliopamas. After exhausting his heavenly life, he will be born in the Mahavideha region — where liberation is always attainable — and attain final mukti.

The defining lesson of this chapter: the absence of ordeal is not the absence of depth. Nandinipita's uninterrupted twenty years of practice — steady, complete, unhurried — is its own testimony. Not every soul needs fire to be purified.

### Simply Put
Nandinipita practiced his vows for fourteen active years, then handed household duties to his eldest son and spent six more years in full retirement, culminating in a conscious voluntary fast unto death. His twenty years of completely undisturbed, peaceful practice earned him rebirth in the Arunagav heaven. After four paliopamas there, he is destined for final liberation in Mahavideha.
