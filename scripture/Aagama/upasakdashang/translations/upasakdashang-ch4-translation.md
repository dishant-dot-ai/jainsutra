# Upasakdashang Sutra — Chapter 4
# Shramanopasak Suradeva (શ્રમણોપાસક સુરાદેવ)

**Chapter 4 of the Upasakdashang Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Upasakdashang Sutra, printed pages 102–107 (PDF pages 164–169)*
*Total Sutras: 7*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

This is the fourth chapter of the Upasakdashang Sutra, titled **Suradeva** — the story of the lay devotee (shramanopasak) Suradeva. Suradeva was a householder of Varanasi, the same great city of the previous chapter. His wife's name was Dhanya. He possessed six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade, six types of livestock, and large cattle herds — the same wealth profile as Kamadev in Chapter 2.

Chapter 4 builds upon the pattern established in Chapter 3 (Chunilipita) with two significant progressions. First, the three sons are cut into five pieces each (compared to three pieces in Chapter 3), indicating an escalating severity of the ordeal across chapters. Second, after the sons, the novel threat is not against a family member but against Suradeva's own body: the deva threatens to inflict sixteen great diseases simultaneously on him, enumerating each one — breathing difficulties, coughing, fever, burning, stomach pain, fistula, hemorrhoids, indigestion, eye pain, headache, skin disorders, ear pain, itching, abdominal swelling, and leprosy.

The chapter's resolution follows the exact pattern of Chapter 3: Suradeva's thought of seizing the deva briefly disturbs his dharmadhyana, his wife Dhanya guides him to atonement, he practices twenty years, completes eleven pratimas, undertakes sallekhana, and is reborn in the Saudharma heaven in the Arunakant celestial vehicle with a lifespan of four paliopamas. Liberation follows in the Mahavideha realm.

## SUTRA 4.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उक्खेवओ चउत्थस्स अज्झयणस्स । एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं वाणारसी णामं णयरी । कोट्टुए चेइए । जियसत्तू राया । सुरादेवे गाहावई अट्टे जाव अपरिभूए । छ हिरण्ण-कोडीओ णिहाण-पउत्ताओ, छ वुड्डि-पउत्ताओ, छ पवित्थर-पउत्ताओ । छ वया, दस-गो-साहसिसएणं वएणं । धण्णा भारिया । सामी समोसढे । जहा आणंदो तहेव पडिवज्जए गिहिधम्मं । जहा कामदेवो जाव समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स धम्मपणत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्तांणं विहरइ ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ukkhevao cautthassa ajjhayanassa | evaṃ khalu jambū ! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ vāṇārasī ṇāmaṃ ṇayarī | koṭṭue ceie | jiyasattū rāyā | surādeve gāhāvaī aṭṭe jāva aparibhūe | cha hiraṇṇa-koḍīo ṇihāṇa-pauттāo, cha vuḍḍi-pauттāo, cha pavitthara-pauттāo | cha vayā, dasa-go-sāhasisaeṇaṃ vaeṇaṃ | dhaṇṇā bhāriyā | sāmī samosaḍhe | jahā āṇaṃdo taheva paḍivajjae gihidhammaṃ | jahā kāmadevo jāva samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa dhammappaṇattiṃ uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharai ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Beginning of the fourth chapter. Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that period, there was a city named Varanasi. The shrine was Koshtuk. King Jiyasattu ruled. Suradeva was a householder, wealthy, up to undefeatable. He possessed six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade goods, six types of livestock, and wealth of ten thousand cattle herds. Dhanya was his wife. The Lord (Mahavira) arrived. Just as Ananda (accepted the householder's dharma), so did he accept the householder's dharma. Just as Kamadev — up to: living in accordance with the teaching of the ascetic Lord Mahavira.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उक्खेवओ चउत्थस्स** = beginning of the fourth (chapter)
- **सुरादेवे गाहावई** = Suradeva was the householder
- **धण्णा भारिया** = Dhanya was his wife (dhanya = blessed, fortunate)
- **जहा आणंदो तहेव पडिवज्जए गिहिधम्मं** = just as Ananda, so he accepted the householder's dharma
- **जहा कामदेवो जाव** = just as Kamadev — up to (all the initiation details)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth chapter opens with the same Varanasi setting as Chapter 3 — same city, same shrine, same king. Suradeva's wealth is the same as Kamadev's (six crore each), which is slightly less than Chunilipita's (eight crore each). His wife Dhanya — a name meaning "blessed" or "fortunate" — will play a significant role at the chapter's climax, just as Bhadra Sarthavahi did in Chapter 3.

The initiation uses double abbreviation: "just as Ananda" for the acceptance of householder dharma, and "just as Kamadev" for the full details of Lord Mahavira's arrival, teaching, and Suradeva's initiation. This compressed beginning signals that the chapter's focus is on what makes Suradeva's ordeal unique, not on repeating the introductory material.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In Varanasi lived Suradeva, a wealthy householder with his wife Dhanya. When Lord Mahavira came, he heard the teaching and took all the vows of a devoted lay follower — just as the great lay followers before him had done.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you find it easier to follow a well-established path, or do you feel the need to discover everything anew? What is the wisdom in building on the example of those who walked before you?

## SUTRA 4.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स सुरादेवस्स समणोवासयस्स अंतिए पुव्व-रत्तावरतकाल-समयंसि एगे देवे पाउब्भविस्था । से देवे एगं महं णीलुप्पल जाव असिं गहाय सुरादेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी– हं भो सुरादेवा समणोवासया ! अपत्थिय-पत्थिया जाव जइ णं तुमं सीलाई जाव ण भंजेसि, तो ते अहं अज्ज जेट्ठुं पुत्तं साओ गिहाओ णीणेमि, णीणेत्ता तव अग्गओ घाएमि, घाएत्ता पंच सोल्लए करेमि, करेत्ता आदाणभिरियंसि कडाहयंसि अद्धेमि, अद्धेत्ता तव गायं मंसेण य सोणिएण य आयंचामि, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुहट्टु वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि । एवं मज्झिमयं, कणीयसं; एक्केक्के पंच सोल्लया । तहेव करेइ जहा चुलणीपियस्स, णवरं एक्केक्के पंच सोल्लया ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa surādevassa samaṇovāsayassa aṃtie puvva-rattāvaratakāla-samayaṃsi ege deve pāubbhavisthā | se deve egaṃ mahaṃ nīluppala jāva asiṃ gahāya surādavaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho surādavā samaṇovāsayā ! apatthiya-patthiyā jāva jai ṇaṃ tumaṃ sīlāiṃ jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te ahaṃ ajja jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ sāo gihāo ṇīṇemi, ṇīṇettā tava aggao ghāemi, ghāettā paṃca solle karemi, karettā ādāṇabhiriyaṃsi kaḍāhayaṃsi addhemi, addhettā tava gāyaṃ maṃseṇa ya soṇieṇa ya āyaṃcāmi, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-duhaṭṭhu vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi | evaṃ majjhimayaṃ, kaṇīyasaṃ; ekkekke paṃca sollayā | taheva karei jahā culaṇīpiyassa, ṇavaraṃ ekkekke paṃca sollayā ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, at the previous early night period, a celestial being manifested in the presence of the lay follower Suradeva. That celestial being, seizing a great sharp sword, spoke thus to the lay follower Suradeva: "Ho there, lay follower Suradeva! You who seek what should not be sought — if you do not break your vows, then I shall today bring your eldest son from your house, kill him before you, cut him into five pieces, boil him in a cauldron of liquid, and smear your body with his flesh and blood — so that you, tormented by extreme pain, may be made to depart from life before your time." Likewise for the middle son and the youngest son — five pieces each. And he did so just as he had with Chunilipita's sons, except now five pieces for each.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एगे देवे पाउब्भविस्था** = a celestial being manifested
- **एगं महं णीलुप्पल जाव असिं** = a great sword (blue-lotus-bright blade)
- **पंच सोल्लए करेमि** = I will cut into five pieces (vs three pieces in Ch3)
- **एवं मज्झिमयं, कणीयसं** = likewise the middle son, the youngest son
- **एक्केक्के पंच सोल्लया** = five pieces each (the escalation: 3 pieces in Ch3, 5 pieces here)
- **तहेव करेइ जहा चुलणीपियस्स** = did so just as with Chunilipita
- **णवरं** = except (the only difference: five pieces instead of three)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourth chapter's divine ordeal parallels Chapter 3 precisely — but with one deliberate escalation: each son is now cut into five pieces instead of three. This progression (three pieces in Ch3, five pieces in Ch4, and as we will see seven pieces in Ch5) is a literary pattern across the Upasakdashang demonstrating increasing severity. Each successive chapter presents a more extreme version of the same test.

The text uses the Agamic abbreviation technique masterfully here: rather than repeating all of Chunilipita's Chapter 3 narrative in full, it states "just as with Chunilipita, except five pieces each." The reader familiar with Chapter 3 knows exactly what happened — the bringing from the house, the killing before the father, the boiling, the smearing. The single word "navarāṃ" (except) carries the entire weight of the escalation.

Suradeva endures all three sons' murders with the same equanimity that Chunilipita demonstrated. The pattern of spiritual steadfastness is identical; only the ordeal's severity increases.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

At night, a celestial being appeared and threatened Suradeva: it killed each of his three sons before his eyes, cutting each into five pieces (more than in the previous chapter), boiling them, and smearing Suradeva's body with their remains.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How does witnessing someone endure an ordeal slightly greater than the one before it change what you believe is possible for the human spirit?

## SUTRA 4.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे सुरादेवं समणोवासयं चउत्थं पि एवं वयासी– हं भो सुरादेवा समणोवासया ! अपत्थिय-पत्थिया जाव ण भंजेसि, तो ते अज्ज सरीरंसि जमग-समगं सोलस रोगायंके पक्खिवामि, तं जहा– सासे, कासे, जरे, दाहे, कुच्छिसूले, भगंदरे, अरिसए, अजीरिए, डिट्ठिसूले, मुढसूले, अंकारिए, अच्छियवेणा, कण्णवेणा, कंडुए, उदरे, कोढे, जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुहट्टु-वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se deve surādavaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ cautthampī evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho surādavā samaṇovāsayā ! apatthiya-patthiyā jāva ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te ajja sarīraṃsi jamaga-samagaṃ solasa rogāyaṃke pakkhivāmi, taṃ jahā— sāse, kāse, jare, dāhe, kucchisūle, bhaṃgandare, arisae, ajīrie, diṭṭhisūle, muḍhasūle, aṃkārie, acchiyaveṇā, kaṇṇaveṇā, kaṃḍue, udare, koḍhe, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-duhaṭṭhu-vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that celestial being addressed the lay follower Suradeva for the fourth time: "Ho there, lay follower Suradeva! You who seek what should not be sought — if you still do not break your vows, then I shall today inflict upon your body, all at once simultaneously, sixteen great diseases, namely: breathing difficulties, coughing, fever, burning inflammation, stomach/intestinal pain, fistula, hemorrhoids/piles, indigestion, eye pain, piercing headache, unknown ailment, eye-vein pain, ear pain, severe itching, abdominal swelling, and leprosy — so that you, tormented by extreme pain and distress, may be made to depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चउत्थं पि** = for the fourth time (after the three son-threats)
- **सरीरंसि** = on/into your body
- **जमग-समगं** = all at once, simultaneously (jama + samaga = at the same time together)
- **सोलस रोगायंके पक्खिवामि** = I will inflict sixteen diseases
- **सासे** = breathing difficulties (asthma, respiratory illness)
- **कासे** = coughing (bronchial/lung)
- **जरे** = fever
- **दाहे** = burning inflammation/heat
- **कुच्छिसूले** = intestinal/stomach pain (colic)
- **भगंदरे** = fistula (painful anal/rectal condition)
- **अरिसए** = hemorrhoids/piles
- **अजीरिए** = indigestion/digestive failure
- **डिट्ठिसूले** = eye pain/piercing eye ailment
- **मुढसूले** = headache/piercing head pain
- **अंकारिए** = ⚠️ uncertain (possibly a skin/joint ailment)
- **अच्छियवेणा** = eye-nerve pain/optic pain
- **कण्णवेणा** = ear pain
- **कंडुए** = severe itching/skin irritation
- **उदरे** = abdominal swelling/liver enlargement
- **कोढे** = leprosy/severe skin disease

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chapter 4's unique contribution to the Upasakdashang is this enumeration of sixteen simultaneous diseases. Where previous ordeals targeted external relationships (sons in Ch3, mother in Ch3, sons in Ch4), this fourth threat targets the practitioner's own body directly. The sixteen diseases are enumerated systematically, covering major organ systems: respiratory (breathing, coughing), metabolic (fever, inflammation), digestive (stomach pain, indigestion), structural (fistula, hemorrhoids), sensory (eye pain, ear pain), skin (itching, leprosy), and internal organs (abdominal swelling).

The word "jamaga-samaga" (all at once, simultaneously) is the key intensifier. One disease is suffering. Sixteen simultaneously, each at its acute worst, would render a person completely incapacitated. The deva is essentially threatening total systemic physical collapse while the practitioner is still alive — the maximally torturous non-lethal assault on the body.

This threat represents a shift in strategy: from attacking what you love (sons, mother) to attacking the instrument through which you experience all of life — your own body. In Jain philosophy, the body is understood as a temporary vehicle for the soul's journey. A practitioner who truly understands this will be no more attached to the body than to a rented house.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deva made a fourth threat: if Suradeva still wouldn't break his vows, it would simultaneously inflict all sixteen major diseases on his body at once — breathing illness, coughing, fever, burning, stomach pain, hemorrhoids, indigestion, eye pain, ear pain, itching, leprosy, and more — all together, all at once.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If your body were in extreme pain, would your spiritual resolve hold? What does your answer reveal about your relationship with your body?

## SUTRA 4.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से सुरादेवे समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे अभीए, अतत्थे, अणुविग्गे, अकुहिए, अचलिए, असंभंते, तुसिणीए धम्मझाणोवगए विहरइ । एवं देवो दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि भणइ जइ णं तुमं अज्ज सीलाई, वयाई, वेरमणाई, पच्चक्खाणाई, पोसहोववासाई ण छड्डेसि, ण भंजेसि, तो ते अहं अज्ज सरीरंसि जमग-समगं सोलस रोगायंके पक्खिवामि जहा णं तुमं अट्टु-दुहट्टु-वसट्टे अकाले चेव जीविआओ ववरोविज्जसि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se surādeve samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe abhīe, atatthe, aṇuvigge, akuhie, acalie, asaṃbhaṃte, tusiṇīe dhammajhāṇovagae viharai | evaṃ devo doccaṃpi taccaṃpi bhaṇai jai ṇaṃ tumaṃ ajja sīlāiṃ, vayāiṃ, veramaṇāiṃ, paccakkhāṇāiṃ, posahōvavāsāiṃ ṇa chaḍḍesi, ṇa bhaṃjesi, to te ahaṃ ajja sarīraṃsi jamaga-samagaṃ solasa rogāyaṃke pakkhivāmi jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ aṭṭhu-duhaṭṭhu-vasaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviāo vavorovijjasi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Suradeva, having been thus addressed by that celestial being, remained: fearless, untroubled, undisturbed, unmoved, unshaken, unperturbed — silent, absorbed in the meditation of dharmadhyana. And the deva addressed him a second time and a third time: "If today you do not abandon and break your virtuous vows, renunciations, observances, and poshadha fasting — then I shall today inflict upon your body simultaneously sixteen great diseases — so that you may depart from life before your time."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभीए** = fearless
- **अतत्थे** = untroubled (not in a state of agitation)
- **अणुविग्गे** = undisturbed, not agitated
- **अकुहिए** = unmoved (not perturbed within)
- **अचलिए** = unshaken, not displaced from one's position
- **असंभंते** = unperturbed, not panicked
- **तुसिणीए** = silent (in meditative quiet)
- **धम्मझाणोवगए** = absorbed in dharmadhyana (the meditation on the nature of dharma/reality)
- **सीलाई, वयाई, वेरमणाई, पच्चक्खाणाई, पोसहोववासाई** = virtuous vows, renunciations, abstinences, and poshadha fasting (the five categories of lay follower practice)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is remarkable for its series of seven negative adjectives (abhīe, atatthe, aṇuvigge, akuhie, acalie, asaṃbhaṃte, tusiṇīe) describing Suradeva's inner state. Each describes a different facet of equanimity: not frightened, not troubled, not agitated, not displaced internally, not shaken externally, not panicked, silent. Together they paint a portrait of complete inner stillness in the face of escalating external threat.

The final term — "dhammajhāṇovagae" (absorbed in dharmadhyana) — is the root cause of all the others. Dharmadhyana is the meditational state of continuous awareness of the true nature of reality: the impermanence of all conditions, the reality of karma and liberation, the soul's ultimate independence from all external circumstances. From this state, nothing can move the practitioner — not the death of sons, not threats against the body.

The deva repeats the disease-threat a second and third time, and receives the same unbroken silence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Suradeva remained completely fearless, undisturbed, silent, absorbed in meditation — even as the deva repeated the threat of sixteen diseases two more times.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Seven different words describe Suradeva's equanimity — fearless, untroubled, undisturbed, unmoved, unshaken, unperturbed, silent. Which of these is hardest for you to maintain when you are threatened or challenged?

## SUTRA 4.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स सुरादेवस्स समणोवासयस्स तेणं देवेणं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि एवं वुत्तस्स समाणस्स इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए जाव समुप्पणे– अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए जाव समायरइ, जेणं ममं जेट्ठुं पुत्तं जाव आयंचइ, जो वि य इमे सोलस रोगायंका, ते वि य इच्छइ ममं सरीरंसि पक्खिवित्ते, तं सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए ति कट्टु उद्दाइए । से वि य आगासे उप्पइए । तेणं य खंभे आसाइए, महया-महया सद्देणं कोलाहले कए ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ tassa surādevassa samaṇovāsayassa teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi evaṃ vuttassa samāṇassa imeyārūve ajjhithae jāva samuppaṇe— aho ṇaṃ ime purīse aṇārie jāva samāyarai, jeṇaṃ mamaṃ jeṭṭhuṃ puttaṃ jāva āyaṃcai, jo vi ya ime solasa rogāyaṃkā, te vi ya icchei mamaṃ sarīraṃsi pakkhivitte, taṃ seyaṃ khalu mamaṃ eyaṃ purīsaṃ giṇhittae ti kaṭṭu uddāiye | se vi ya āgāse uppiye | teṇaṃ ya khaṃbhe āsāiye, mahayā-mahayā saddeṇaṃ kolāhale kae ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, having been addressed a second and third time by that celestial being, this thought arose in the lay follower Suradeva's mind: "Ah! This person is ignoble, performing ignoble sinful deeds — who took my eldest son, up to: smeared me with remains — and who also desires to inflict sixteen diseases on my body. Therefore it is best for me to seize this person." Deciding thus, he leapt up. The deva also leapt into the sky. He grasped a column for support and made a great and mighty uproar.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इमेयारूवे अज्झिथए** = this kind of thought arose
- **अहो णं इमे पुरिसे अणारिए** = ah! this person is ignoble
- **जेणं ममं जेट्ठुं पुत्तं जाव आयंचइ** = who took my eldest son, up to smeared me
- **सोलस रोगायंका** = sixteen diseases (the escalated threat unique to this chapter)
- **तं सेयं खलु ममं एयं पुरिसं गिण्हित्तए** = therefore it is best for me to seize this person
- **उद्दाइए** = leapt up

### 5. COMMENTARY

The pattern from Chapter 3 repeats exactly: when the deva escalates to the final, most personal threat — here, the threat against Suradeva's own body with sixteen simultaneous diseases — a thought arises in Suradeva's mind. This thought of seizing the deva represents the brief departure from pure dharmadhyana. Not a moral failing, not a breaking of vows, not a reaction of hatred — but a righteous recognition and protective impulse that momentarily shifts awareness from inward absorption to outward action.

The commentary in the source text notes that Suradeva remained firm through the killing of his sons (the external ordeals) but was momentarily disturbed when his own body was threatened. This is philosophically interesting: attachment to children could be called love for another; attachment to one's own body is a more fundamental form of self-identification. The deva found the leverage point. Suradeva's equanimity did not break entirely, but it shifted enough to generate a thought and a physical impulse.

His wife Dhanya, like Bhadra Sarthavahi before her, will identify this moment precisely and guide him back.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the deva repeated the threat of sixteen diseases, a thought arose in Suradeva: "This sinful being wants to destroy my sons and afflict my body — I should seize him." He leapt up, but the deva escaped into the sky, and Suradeva was left grasping a pillar, crying out.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

We often endure suffering on behalf of those we love more easily than we endure threats to ourselves. What does this reveal about where our deepest sense of self resides?

## SUTRA 4.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं सा धण्णा भारिया कोलाहलं सोच्चा-णिसम्म, जेणेव सुरादेवे समणोवासए तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता एवं वयासी– किण्णं देवाणुप्पिया ! तुब्भेहिं महया-महया सद्देणं कोलाहले कए ?

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ sā dhaṇṇā bhāriyā kolāhalaṃ soccā-ṇisamme, jeṇeva surādeve samaṇovāsae teṇeva uvāgacchai, uvāgacchittā evaṃ vayāsī— kiṇṇaṃ devāṇuppiyā ! tubbhehiṃ mahayā-mahayā saddeṇaṃ kolāhale kae ? ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Dhanya, his wife, having heard that great uproar, went to where the lay follower Suradeva was and said to him: "Why, beloved! Why have you made such a great and mighty uproar?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **धण्णा भारिया** = the wife Dhanya (dhanya = blessed, fortunate)
- **कोलाहलं सोच्चा-णिसम्म** = having heard and recognized the uproar
- **किण्णं देवाणुप्पिया** = why, beloved? (devāṇuppiya = dear one; literally "dear to the gods" — a term of affectionate address between spouses)
- **तुब्भेहिं** = by you (plural honorific)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Dhanya comes to her husband, just as Bhadra Sarthavahi came to Chunilipita in Chapter 3. But where Bhadra addressed her son as "putta" (son), Dhanya addresses her husband as "devāṇuppiya" (beloved, dear one — literally "one dear to the gods"). This term of affectionate address between spouses is a standard Agamic formula. It conveys warmth, intimacy, and respect simultaneously.

Dhanya's role in this chapter mirrors Bhadra's precisely: she will hear the account, correctly identify the divine ordeal, recognize the brief disturbance in Suradeva's practice, and guide him toward atonement. The Upasakdashang repeatedly shows that spiritual companions — whether mother or wife — are not just emotional support; they are active participants in each other's spiritual journey.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Suradeva's wife Dhanya heard the uproar, came to him, and asked: "Beloved, why are you making such a loud commotion?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Who in your life knows your spiritual practice well enough to notice when something has disrupted your inner state — before you even tell them?

## SUTRA 4.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से सुरादेवे समणोवासए धण्णं भारियं एवं वयासी– एवं खलु देवाणुप्पिया! के वि पुरिसे, तहेव तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा कहेइ जहा चुलणीपिया । धण्णा वि पडिभणइ– णो खलु देवाणुप्पिया ! तुब्भं के वि पुरिसे सरीरंसि जमग-समगं सोलस रोगायंके पक्खिवइ, एस णं के वि पुरिसे तुब्भं उवसग्गं करेइ । सेसं जहा चुलणीपियस्स तहा भणइ ।

एवं सेसं जहा चुलणीपियस्स णिरवसेसं जाव सोहम्मे कप्पे अरुणकंते विमाणे उवण्णे । चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई । महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ । णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tae ṇaṃ se surādeve samaṇovāsae dhaṇṇaṃ bhāriyaṃ evaṃ vayāsī— evaṃ khalu devāṇuppiyā ! ke vi purīse, taheva tiṇṇi vi uvasaggā kaheī jahā culaṇīpiyā | dhaṇṇā vi paḍibhaṇai— ṇo khalu devāṇuppiyā ! tubbhaṃ ke vi purīse sarīraṃsi jamaga-samagaṃ solasa rogāyaṃke pakkhivai, esa ṇaṃ ke vi purīse tubbhaṃ uvasaggaṃ karei | sesaṃ jahā culaṇīpiyassa tahā bhaṇai ||

evaṃ sesaṃ jahā culaṇīpiyassa ṇiravasesaṃ jāva sohame kappe aruṇakante vimāṇe uvavaṇṇe | cattāri paliōvamāiṃ ṭhiī | mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihai | ṇikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the lay follower Suradeva said to his wife Dhanya: "Thus it is, beloved! A certain person — and he narrated all three ordeals just as Chunilipita had." Dhanya then replied: "Indeed, beloved! Whoever this person was who desired to inflict sixteen great diseases simultaneously on your body — this person was inflicting an ordeal upon you." The rest was the same as what Chunilipita had said.

And the rest was the same as Chunilipita's account in full — up to: he was born in the Saudharma heaven, in the Arunakant celestial vehicle. His lifespan is four paliopamas. He will attain liberation in the Mahavideha realm. The conclusion is the same as the first chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तहेव तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा कहेइ जहा चुलणीपिया** = he narrated all three ordeals just as Chunilipita had (full narrative repeated by abbreviation)
- **धण्णा वि पडिभणइ** = Dhanya also replied
- **सरीरंसि जमग-समगं सोलस रोगायंके पक्खिवइ** = who desired to inflict sixteen diseases simultaneously on your body (Dhanya correctly names the unique threat)
- **एस णं के वि पुरिसे तुब्भं उवसग्गं करेइ** = this was a person inflicting an ordeal on you
- **सेसं जहा चुलणीपियस्स तहा भणइ** = the rest was the same as Chunilipita's (atonement, practice, etc.)
- **अरुणकंते विमाणे** = in the Arunakant celestial vehicle (note: different name from Ch3's Arunprabha — "kanta" = beloved/beautiful vs "prabha" = radiance)
- **चत्तारि पलिओवमाइं ठिई** = lifespan of four paliopamas
- **महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ** = will attain liberation in the Mahavideha realm

### 5. COMMENTARY

Chapter 4 concludes in compressed form, with its resolution following Chapter 3's pattern exactly. Suradeva tells Dhanya the complete account of the three ordeals; she identifies each one correctly as a divine ordeal (upsarga), specifically naming the novel fourth threat — the sixteen simultaneous diseases — by its exact content. Just as Bhadra Sarthavahi in Ch3 instructed her son in atonement, Dhanya instructs her husband.

The rest follows: Suradeva performs ālochana and pratikraman (confession and repentance), takes up the eleven pratimas, practices for twenty years, undertakes sallekhana, and departs in equanimity. He is born in the Saudharma heaven in the "Arunakant" (dawn-beloved) celestial vehicle — slightly different name from Ch3's "Arunprabha" (dawn-radiance), but in the same Saudharma realm. His lifespan and liberation trajectory are identical.

The key lesson emphasized in the Gujarati commentary is the role of the wife (dharmapatni) as spiritual corrector. Just as the practitioners of the Upasakdashang are models for lay followers, their spouses are models for spiritual partners: aware, calm, spiritually literate, and willing to hold each other to the highest standard.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Suradeva told Dhanya everything that happened. Dhanya, just like Chunilipita's mother, identified it as a divine ordeal and guided her husband to atonement. Suradeva then lived twenty years in dedicated practice, completed all eleven stages, and ultimately departed to be reborn in heaven — on the path to final liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to be a spiritual partner to someone — not just an emotional support, but someone who can see their spiritual state clearly and guide them back when they drift?
