# Upasakdashang — Chapter 10: Shramanopasak Salihipita

**Setting:** Shravasti (Sāvatthī), Koshtuk shrine, King Jiyasattu  
**Protagonist:** Salihipita (Sālihīpiyā) — "father of rice/grain"; wealthy and radiant guildmaster  
**Wealth:** 12 crore gold coins (4 deposited, 4 in trade, 4 in household); 4 gokulas × 10,000 cows = 40,000 cows  
**Wife:** Phalguni (Fagghuṇī) — named after the auspicious Phalguni lunar month  
**Key feature:** No divine ordeal (upsarg) — follows Kamadeva's pattern most closely  
**Practice:** 11 pratimas completed; poshdhashala maintained within home compound  
**Afterlife:** Saudharma heaven, Arunakil viman (vehicle), 4 paliopamas, then Mahavideha → liberation  
**Significance:** Final chapter; contains the closing colophon of the entire Upasakdashang

---

## Sutra 1

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
दसमस्स उक्खेवो । एवं खलु जम्बू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं सावत्थी णयरी । कोट्टुए चेइए । जियसत्तु राया । तत्थ णं सावत्थीए णयरीए सालिहीपिया णामं गाहावई परिवसइ, अट्ठे दित्ते जाव अपरिभूए । चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ गिण्हाणपट्टाओ, चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ वुट्टिपट्टाओ, चत्तारि हिरण्णकोडीओ पवित्थरपट्टाओ, चत्तारि वया, दस-गो-साहसिएणं वण्णं । फग्गुणी भारिआ ।

### Roman Transliteration
Dasamass ukkhevo. Evam khalu Jambū! Teṇam kāleṇam teṇam samayeṇam Sāvatthī nayrī. Koṭṭhue ceiae. Jiyasattu rāyā. Tattha ṇam Sāvatthīe nayrīe Sālihīpiyā nām gāhāvaī parīvasaī, aṭṭhe ditte jāv aparibhūe. Cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo giṇhāṇapaṭṭāo, cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo vuṭṭhipaṭṭāo, cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo paviṭṭharapaṭṭāo, cāttāri vayā, dasa-go-sāhasieṇam vaṇṇam. Fagghuṇī bhāriā.

### Literal English Translation
The opening of the tenth chapter. Thus it was, O Jambū! At that time and in that era, the city of Shravasti existed. There was the Koshtuk shrine. King Jiyasattu ruled. In that city of Shravasti, there lived a householder named Salihipita — wealthy, radiant (generous), (and so on) up to: revered by many people. He held four crore gold coins in deposited form, four crore in circulating trade, and four crore in household assets; he had four gokulas, each with ten thousand cows. His wife's name was Phalguni.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Dasamass ukkhevo* — opening/setting of the tenth (chapter)
- *Evam khalu Jambū* — thus indeed, O Jambū
- *teṇam kāleṇam teṇam samayeṇam* — at that time, in that era
- *Sāvatthī nayrī* — the city of Shravasti
- *Koṭṭhue ceiae* — the Koshtuk shrine
- *Jiyasattu rāyā* — King Jiyasattu (ruled)
- *Sālihīpiyā nām gāhāvaī* — a guildmaster named Salihipita
- *aṭṭhe ditte jāv aparibhūe* — wealthy, radiant/generous, (full description) up to: not disrespected
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo giṇhāṇapaṭṭāo* — four crore in deposited treasury
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo vuṭṭhipaṭṭāo* — four crore in circulating business
- *cāttāri hiraṇṇakoḍīo paviṭṭharapaṭṭāo* — four crore in household assets
- *cāttāri vayā* — four gokulas (cattle herds)
- *dasa-go-sāhasieṇam vaṇṇam* — ten thousand cows (in each)
- *Fagghuṇī bhāriā* — wife named Phalguni

### Commentary
The tenth and final chapter of the Upasakdashang opens with the same setting that has anchored all ten stories — Shravasti, the Koshtuk shrine, King Jiyasattu. This repetition is intentional: the Agamic tradition uses it to signal that all ten shramanopasaks belong to the same world, the same era, the same spiritual community gathered around Bhagavan Mahavir.

Salihipita ("father of rice" or "grain-keeper") was a dhanadhya (wealthy) and prabhavshali (radiant, influential) guildmaster — the Gujarati text adds *deep-purna* (full of light), suggesting someone whose material success was matched by personal dignity. His wealth structure exactly mirrors Nandinipita's: 12 crore gold coins in three equal portions and 40,000 cows in four gokulas. His wife Phalguni is named after the lunar month of Phalguna (February-March), an auspicious time for festivals and new beginnings — befitting the wife of the final shramanopasak whose story closes the entire Anga.

Like Nandinipita before him, no divine ordeal ever came to test Salihipita. The last two chapters form a quiet, assured coda to the Upasakdashang — eight disciples who faced trials, two who did not, all arriving at the same destination.

### Simply Put
The final chapter begins in Shravasti. Salihipita was a wealthy, radiant, and influential guildmaster with 12 crore gold coins and 40,000 cows in four herds. His wife was named Phalguni. Like the preceding chapter, no divine ordeal ever tested him — his path was one of steady, uninterrupted practice.

## Sutra 2

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
सामी समोसढे । जहा आणंदो तहेव गिहिधम्मं पडिवज्जइ । जहा कामदेवो तहा जेट्ठु पुत्तं ठवेत्ता पोसहसालाए समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स धम्मपण्णत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरढे । णोवरं ठिरुवसग्गाओ । एक्कारस विं उवासगडिमाओ तहेव भाणियव्वाओ । एवं कामदेवं गणेयं जाव सोहम्मे कप्पे अरुणकीले विमाणे देवत्ताए उवववण्णे । चत्तारि पलिओमाइ ठिई । महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहइ ।

### Roman Transliteration
Sāmī samosaḍhe. Jahā Āṇamdo taheva giṇhidhammam paḍivajjai. Jahā Kāmadevo tahā jeṭṭhu puttam ṭhavettā poshasālāe samaṇassa bhagavo Mahāvīrassa dhammpaṇṇattim uvasampajjittāṇam viharḍhe. Ṇovaram ṭhiruvasaggāo. Ekkāras viṃ uvāsagaḍimāo taheva bhāṇiyavvāo. Evam Kāmadevam gaṇeyam jāv Sohme kappe aruṇakīle vimāṇe devattāe uvavvaṇṇe. Cāttāri paliomāi ṭhiī. Mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihaī.

### Literal English Translation
The Lord arrived and established his samavaran assembly. Just as Ananda (received and took the dharma), so too (Salihipita) accepted the householder's dharma. Just as Kamadeva — after installing his eldest son (as household head) and (taking up residence) in the poshdhashala — he devoted himself to the dharma proclamation of Shramana Bhagavan Mahavira. Special note: there was no divine ordeal. The eleven stages of lay practice are to be described (as for earlier disciples). His life is to be understood as Kamadeva's. He was born as a deva in the Arunakil celestial vehicle in the Saudharma heaven. His lifespan there is four paliopamas. He will attain liberation while dwelling in Mahavideha.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Sāmī samosaḍhe* — the Lord (Mahavir) arrived and set up samavaran
- *Jahā Āṇamdo taheva giṇhidhammam paḍivajjai* — just as Ananda accepted householder dharma, so too (did Salihipita)
- *Jahā Kāmadevo tahā* — just as Kamadeva (did), so too
- *jeṭṭhu puttam ṭhavettā* — after installing his eldest son (as household head)
- *poshasālāe* — in the poshdhashala (monastic meditation and study hall)
- *samaṇassa bhagavo Mahāvīrassa dhammpaṇṇattim uvasampajjittāṇam viharḍhe* — devoted to the dharma proclamation of Shramana Bhagavan Mahavira, he lived (in renunciant practice)
- *Ṇovaram ṭhiruvasaggāo* — special note: no divine ordeal (upsarg) came
- *Ekkāras viṃ uvāsagaḍimāo taheva bhāṇiyavvāo* — the eleven pratimas are to be described as for (earlier disciples)
- *Evam Kāmadevam gaṇeyam* — his life should be understood as Kamadeva's
- *Sohme kappe aruṇakīle vimāṇe devattāe uvavvaṇṇe* — born as a deva in the Arunakil vehicle in the Saudharma heaven
- *Cāttāri paliomāi ṭhiī* — lifespan of four paliopamas there
- *Mahāvidehe vāse sijjhihaī* — will attain liberation dwelling in Mahavideha

### Commentary
Salihipita's spiritual biography is the most concisely rendered of all ten — a single sutra covers his initiation, his practice period, his retirement, and his afterlife. The sutra instructs the reader to understand his life through two reference points: Ananda (Ch1) for the act of taking vows, and Kamadeva (Ch2) for the manner of retirement.

This distinction matters. The Vivechan notes: from chapters 3 through 10, the disciples' retirement follows Kamadeva's pattern, not Ananda's. Ananda, in the fifteenth year of his retirement, traveled far from home to a distant poshdhashala (monastic meditation hall) belonging to another community. Kamadeva, by contrast, established an independent poshdhashala within or adjacent to his own home compound, and remained there. Salihipita — like Kamadeva and the other eight shravoaks — stayed within the boundary of his own household, creating a private sacred space for his final years of practice.

Having installed his eldest son Jayesh as head of household, Salihipita fully withdrew from worldly responsibilities and devoted himself to the dharma — attending Bhagavan's teachings, practicing the eleven pratimas, observing poshadha fasts, and deepening his samayik. The sutra's *Ṇovaram ṭhiruvasaggāo* ("special note: no ordeal came") is significant: it is stated explicitly, as if to affirm that an untroubled path is just as valid as one forged through trials.

The celestial vehicle — Arunakil (dawn-blue/dawn-dark) — completes a subtle set in the Upasakdashang's afterlife destinations: Arunprabha (dawn-light), Arunakant (dawn-beloved), Arunsiddha (dawn-accomplished), Arunagav (dawn-cow/dawn-radiance), Arunakil (dawn-blue peak). All in Saudharma heaven; all with four paliopamas; all bound for Mahavideha liberation.

### Simply Put
When Mahavir came to Shravasti, Salihipita took his vows like Ananda did, then followed Kamadeva's path — handing worldly duties to his son and setting up a personal meditation hall at home to devote himself fully to dharma. No ordeal ever came to test him. He completed all eleven stages of lay practice, died peacefully in samadhi, and was born in the Arunakil heaven. After four paliopamas there, he is destined for final liberation in Mahavideha.

## Sutra 3 — The Closing Colophon of the Upasakdashang

### Prakrit (Devanagari)
एवं खलु जम्बू ! समणेणं जाव संपत्तेणं सत्तमस्स अंगस्स उवासगदसाणं दसमस्स अज्झयणस्स अयमट्टे पण्णत्ते ॥

### Roman Transliteration
Evam khalu Jambū! Samaṇeṇam jāv sampateṇam sattamass amgass uvāsagdasāṇam dasamass ajjhayaṇass ayamatte paṇṇatte.

### Literal English Translation
Thus indeed, O Jambū! By the Shramana (Mahavir) — (transmitted down) up to one who has attained liberation — this is the meaning of the tenth chapter of the seventh Anga, the Upasakdashana, as proclaimed.

### Anvayartha (Word-by-Word)
- *Evam khalu Jambū* — thus indeed, O Jambū
- *Samaṇeṇam* — by the Shramana (Bhagavan Mahavir)
- *jāv sampateṇam* — up to (Sudharmaswami) who has attained liberation (the chain of transmission)
- *sattamass amgass* — of the seventh Anga
- *uvāsagdasāṇam* — of the Upasakdashana (Upasakdashang)
- *dasamass ajjhayaṇass* — of the tenth chapter
- *ayamatte paṇṇatte* — this meaning/import has been proclaimed

### Commentary
This brief sutra is the formal closing seal of the entire Upasakdashang — one of the twelve Angas of the Jain Agamic canon. Sudharmaswami, the direct disciple of Bhagavan Mahavir and the primary transmitter of the Agamas, addresses his own disciple Jambuswamiand declares the tenth chapter — and with it the entire scripture — complete.

The phrase *jāv sampateṇam* ("up to one who has attained liberation") is the Agamic acknowledgment of the unbroken chain of transmission: Mahavir → Sudharmaswami → Jambu → subsequent generations. Sudharmaswami himself attained liberation (moksha), and this sutra marks the moment just before that final passage, as he completes the handing-on of sacred knowledge.

The Vivechan notes an important structural observation about the Upasakdashang as a whole: the third through tenth shramanopasaks' experiences of receiving vows are described as "like Ananda's joy," while their retirement practices are described as "like Kamadeva's." This is because Ananda, in his fifteenth year of retirement, traveled to a distant poshdhashala outside his home — while Kamadeva and the subsequent nine shravoaks each maintained a private poshdhashala within their own home boundaries, practicing without leaving their domestic sphere.

The Upasakdashang thus closes as it opened: with the listener's name called, the teacher's presence acknowledged, and the transmission confirmed. Ten lives, one teaching. One dharma, infinite paths.

### Simply Put
Sudharmaswami closes the entire Upasakdashang by declaring to Jambu: "This is what the liberated Mahavir taught — the tenth and final chapter of the seventh Anga, the Upasakdashana." The scripture is now complete. Ten lay disciples, ten lives of devotion, one unbroken teaching handed down from the Tirthankara to us.
