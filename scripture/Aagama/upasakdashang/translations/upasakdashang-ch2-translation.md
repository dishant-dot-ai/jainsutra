## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

This is the second chapter of the Upasakdashang Sutra, titled **Kamadeva** — the story of the lay devotee (shramanopasak) Kamadeva. Kamadeva was a householder of Champanagari (modern Bhagalpur, Bihar), a city ruled by King Jiyasattu. His wife's name was Bhadra. He was a man of enormous wealth — six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in revenue, six crore in trade, and six herds of cattle each of ten thousand head.

The central drama of this chapter is a threefold divine ordeal (upsarg) — a mithyadrishti (wrong-visioned) deity who tests Kamadeva's spiritual steadfastness by taking three successive terrifying forms: a horrific demon (pishacha), a ferocious divine elephant, and a massive divine serpent. In each form, the deity threatens Kamadeva with death, then finally attacks him physically — yet Kamadeva remains completely unshaken in dharma-dhyana throughout.

After the deity reveals its true divine form and Indra (Sakkendra) himself praises Kamadeva with an extensive eulogy, Kamadeva completes his pratima vrata. Bhagavan Mahavira then arrives at Champanagari and confirms the three ordeals before his assembly, using Kamadeva's example to inspire monks and nuns. Kamadeva then practices all eleven pratimas of the lay path over twenty years, dies with a month-long fast, and is reborn in the Arunabha vimana in Saudharma heaven with a four-palya lifespan. When asked by Gautama, Mahavira declares that Kamadeva will ultimately attain liberation in Mahavideha.

## SUTRA 2.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जइ णं भंते ! समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं जाव संपत्तेणं सत्तमस्स अंगस्स उवासगदसाणं पढमस्स अज्झयणस्स अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते, दोच्चस्स णं भंते ! अज्झयणस्स के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते ? ॥२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jaī ṇaṃ bhaṃte! samaṇeṇaṃ bhagavayā mahāvīreṇaṃ jāva saṃpatteṇaṃ sattamassa aṃgassa uvāsagadasāṇaṃ paḍhamassa ajjhayaṇassa ayamaṭṭhe paṇṇatte, doccassa ṇaṃ bhaṃte! ajjhayaṇassa ke aṭṭhe paṇṇatte? || 2.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O Venerable One! If the meaning of the first chapter of the Upasakdashang — the seventh Anga — as taught by the Accomplished Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, has been set forth, then O Venerable One, what is the meaning set forth in the second chapter?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **जइ णं भंते** = if indeed, O Venerable One
- **समणेणं भगवया महावीरेणं** = by Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira
- **जाव संपत्तेणं** = who is accomplished (yāvat — full epithet implied)
- **सत्तमस्स अंगस्स** = of the seventh Anga
- **उवासगदसाणं** = of the Upasakdashang (Ten Lay Followers)
- **पढमस्स अज्झयणस्स** = of the first chapter
- **अयमट्ठे पण्णत्ते** = this meaning has been set forth
- **दोच्चस्स अज्झयणस्स** = of the second chapter
- **के अट्ठे पण्णत्ते** = what meaning has been set forth?

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the standard opening question that bridges the chapters of the Upasakdashang. It follows the oral transmission framework: the disciple Jambu Swami asks his teacher Arya Sudharma Swami about the content of the second chapter, just as Sudharma Swami received it from Bhagavan Mahavira himself.

The phrase "jāva sampatteṇaṃ" (yāvat accomplished) is a canonical abbreviation. It stands for the full laudatory epithet of Mahavira that appears in full at the opening of the first chapter: "siddhe ayariya..." — describing him as one who has attained perfection, who is the teacher of teachers, who has crossed the ocean of existence. This shorthand device (yāvat = "up to") is used throughout Jain Agamic literature to avoid repeating long formulaic passages.

The structure of the Upasakdashang mirrors that of Jambu-Sudharma transmission found across multiple Angas: each chapter opens with Jambu asking about the next lay follower's story, and Sudharma narrating it in detail. This framing device authenticates the teaching by establishing the unbroken chain of oral transmission from Mahavira down to the final compiler.

The question itself is simple but structurally important — it signals that this is not an independent treatise but the second in a series of ten related narratives, each about a different exemplary lay follower.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Jambu asks his teacher: "You've explained the first chapter about lay follower Ananda — now what is the second chapter about?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Who are the teachers in your own life from whom you have received wisdom — and how faithfully do you pass that wisdom on to others?

## SUTRA 2.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं खलु जंबू ! तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं चंपा णामं णयरी होत्था। पुण्णभद्दे चेइए। जियसत्तू राया। कामदेवे गाहावई। भद्दा भारिया। छ हिरण्ण-कोडीओ णिहाण-पत्ताओ, छ वुड्ढि-पत्ताओ, छ पवित्थर-पत्ताओ, छ वया दस-गो-साहस्सिएणं वएणं। समोसरणं। जहा आणंदो तहा णिग्गओ, तहेव सावगधम्मं पडिवज्जइ।
सा चेव वत्तव्या जाव जेट्ठु-पुत्तं, मित्त-णाई आपुच्छित्ता, जेणेव पोसहसालं पमज्जित्ता उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता पोसहसालं पमज्जित्ता उच्चारपासवणभूमिं पडिलेहेइ, पडिलेहित्ता, दब्भसंथारयं संथरइ, संथरेत्ता दब्भसंथारयं दुरुहइ, दुरुहित्ता पोसहसालाए पोसिहए दब्भसंथारोवगए समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स धम्मपण्णत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरइ। ॥२.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ khalu jaṃbū! teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ campā nāmaṃ ṇaYarī hotthā. puṇṇabhadde ceie. jiyasattū rāyā. kāmadeve gāhāvaī. bhaddā bhāriyā. cha hiraṇṇa-koḍīo ṇihāṇa-pattāo, cha vuḍḍhi-pattāo, cha pavitthara-pattāo, cha vayā dasa-go-sāhassieṇaṃ vaeṇaṃ. samosaraṇaṃ. jahā āṇaṃdo tahā ṇiggao, taheva sāvagadhammaṃ paḍivajjai.
Sā ceva vattavyā jāva jeṭṭhu-puttaṃ, mitta-ṇāī āpucchittā, jeṇeva posaha-sālaṃ pamajjittā uvāgacchati, uvāgacchittā posaha-sālaṃ pamajjittā uccāra-pāsavaṇa-bhūmiṃ paḍilehei, paḍilehittā dabbha-saṃthārayaṃ saṃtharai, saṃtharetta dabbha-saṃthārayaṃ duruhai, duruhittā posaha-sālāe posihae dabbha-saṃthārovagae samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa dhamma-paṇṇattiṃ uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharai. || 2.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus indeed, O Jambu! At that time, in that era, there was a city called Champa. There was a garden called Purnahbhadra. King Jiyasattu ruled. Kamadeva was a householder. Bhadra was his wife. He had six crore gold coins in deposits, six crore in income, six crore in trade goods, and six herds of cattle with ten thousand head each. Lord Mahavira arrived and established his divine assembly. Just as Ananda (of the first chapter) had done, so too Kamadeva left his house and accepted the householder's dharma. He then took permission from his eldest son, friends, and relatives, came to the poshashala, swept it clean, inspected the ground for living beings, spread a sacred grass mat, sat upon it, and dwelling in the poshashala on the grass mat, engaged in the practice of Dharma as set forth by Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं** = at that time, in that period
- **चंपा णामं णयरी** = a city named Champa
- **पुण्णभद्दे चेइए** = the Purnahbhadra garden (chaitya)
- **जियसत्तू राया** = King Jiyasattu
- **कामदेवे गाहावई** = Kamadeva the householder
- **भद्दा भारिया** = Bhadra was (his) wife
- **छ हिरण्ण-कोडीओ णिहाण-पत्ताओ** = six crore gold coins in deposits
- **छ वुड्ढि-पत्ताओ** = six crore in income/investments
- **छ पवित्थर-पत्ताओ** = six crore in trade
- **छ वया दस-गो-साहस्सिएणं वएणं** = six herds of ten thousand cattle each
- **समोसरणं** = Bhagavan's divine assembly (samosorana)
- **जहा आणंदो तहा णिग्गओ** = just as Ananda (did), so (Kamadeva) went forth
- **सावगधम्मं पडिवज्जइ** = accepted the shravaka dharma
- **जेट्ठु-पुत्तं मित्त-णाई आपुच्छित्ता** = having taken permission from eldest son, friends, relatives
- **पोसहसालं पमज्जित्ता** = having swept the poshashala
- **उच्चारपासवणभूमिं पडिलेहेइ** = inspected the area for small creatures
- **दब्भसंथारयं** = sacred grass mat
- **धम्मपण्णत्तिं उवसंपज्जित्ताणं** = taking up the dharma as proclaimed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra establishes the setting and introduces the protagonist. Champanagari (modern Bhagalpur in Bihar) was one of the six major cities of ancient India mentioned frequently in Jain and Buddhist texts. The Purnahbhadra garden was a customary assembly point for Mahavira's discourses — the same location is mentioned across several Angas.

The wealth description follows the precise formula used for all ten lay followers in the Upasakdashang: six crore in deposits (nihana-patta), six crore in investments (vruddhi-patta), six crore in trade goods (pravishtar-patta), and six herds of cattle (vaya). This is not mere literary embellishment — it establishes that Kamadeva's subsequent spiritual choice is made from the heights of material security, not from poverty or deprivation. The Jain tradition emphasizes that genuine renunciation is chosen, not forced.

The phrase "jahā āṇaṃdo tahā" (just as Ananda had done) is a canonical cross-reference to Chapter 1. It means: the entire process by which Ananda heard Mahavira's discourse, accepted the shravak dharma, and began practicing poshadha — the same sequence applies here to Kamadeva without needing full repetition. This yāvat technique keeps the text concise while preserving completeness.

The poshashala (poshadha hall) is the dedicated space for poshadha observance — the Jain equivalent of an intensified vow period where a lay follower withdraws from household activities, observes a fast, and focuses entirely on meditation and dharma. Kamadeva is depicted sweeping the hall, inspecting the ground (to avoid harming insects — a direct expression of ahimsa), spreading the sacred grass mat, and sitting in complete absorption. This is the scene when the divine ordeal begins.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

In the city of Champa lived an extremely wealthy householder named Kamadeva with his wife Bhadra. When Lord Mahavira visited, Kamadeva heard his teachings, took the vows of a lay follower, and went to his prayer hall to practice a holy fast in deep meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have material security and comfort, does your spiritual practice deepen — or does abundance make practice feel less urgent?

## SUTRA 2.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं तस्स कामदेवस्स समणोवासयस्स अंतिए पुव्वरत्तावरत्त-काल-समयंसि एगे देवे मायीमिच्छदिट्ठी पाउब्भूए। ॥२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ tassa kāmadevassa samaṇovāsayassa aṃtie puvvaratāvaratta-kāla-samayaṃsi ege deve māyī-miccha-diṭṭhī pāubhūe. || 2.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, in the latter half of the midnight period, a deceitful and wrong-visioned deity appeared before the shramanopasak Kamadeva.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तए णं** = then indeed
- **तस्स कामदेवस्स** = of that Kamadeva
- **समणोवासयस्स अंतिए** = near/before the shramanopasak
- **पुव्वरत्तावरत्त-काल-समयंसि** = in the latter part of midnight (literally: in the time of the second-half of the night)
- **एगे देवे** = a certain deity
- **मायीमिच्छदिट्ठी** = deceitful and of wrong-vision (mithyadrishti)
- **पाउब्भूए** = appeared, became manifest

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra marks the beginning of the chapter's central drama — the threefold divine ordeal (upsarg). The timing is significant: "the latter half of midnight" (puvvaratāvaratta-kāla) is the deepest and darkest part of the night, when normal mental defenses are at their lowest. This is when Kamadeva's test begins.

The deity is described with two key qualities: "māyī" (deceitful, using illusion) and "mithyadrishti" (of wrong vision — meaning the deity has not accepted the true nature of reality as taught in Jain philosophy; it does not accept the validity of the Nirgranth path). This is crucial — the ordeal is not merely a test of physical courage, but a spiritual confrontation between a being invested in illusion and a lay follower grounded in right knowledge.

In Jain cosmology, deities of certain categories can harbor wrong views. The mithyadrishti dev is motivated either by jealousy of Kamadeva's spiritual progress, or by a desire to prove that householder practitioners cannot truly remain steady. The testing of lay followers by celestial beings who doubt the depth of householder spirituality is a recurring theme in the Upasakdashang — each of the ten chapters follows this pattern. The message is consistent: genuine spiritual steadfastness is accessible not only to monks but also to householders who have truly internalized the teaching.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

At midnight, a powerful but deceitful deity with misguided beliefs appeared in front of Kamadeva, who was deep in meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In the darkest moments of your life — when fear and doubt press in — what is the quality of your inner ground? Does it hold?

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity created a great Pishacha (demon) form. The description of that deity's Pishacha form is as follows: Its head was shaped like a grass basket; its hair was like sheaves of paddy stalks, tawny and bristling; its forehead was large and shaped like a broken pot; its eyebrows were like a mongoose's tail — thick, shaggy, and horrible to behold; its eyes were like inverted fire-pots, frightening to look at; its ears were like winnowing fans — flat and horrible; its nostrils were like the bellows of a forge; its nostril-chambers were shaped like a double-chambered stove; its moustache was like a horse's tail, tawny and horrid; its teeth were long and drooping like a camel's; its tongue was like a winnowing fan-cutter — horrible; its jaw was shaped like a plough; its cheeks were rough, cracked, tawny, coarse, and enormous; its neck was shaped like a mrdanga drum; both upper arms were shaped like the doors of a city gate; both forearms were shaped like grinding millstones; the fingers of its hands were shaped like revolving spindles; both its breasts hung far down; its belly was round like an iron ball; its navel was like black water; its loins were shaped like a bird's nest; both thighs were shaped like double doorposts; its kneecaps were badly bent; its calves were covered with coarse hair; both feet were shaped like wooden platforms; its toenails were shaped like conch-shell halves.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पिसायरूवं विउव्वइ** = created/manifested a Pishacha form (vaikriya — supernatural shape-shifting power)
- **गो-किलंज-संठाण-संठियं** = shaped like a go-kilanj (grass basket used for cattle)
- **सालिहसेल्ल-सिरिसा** = like sheaves of paddy stalks
- **कविल** = tawny, russet-brown
- **महल्ल-उडिया-कभल्ल** = large, broken-pot shaped
- **मुगुंस-पुच्छं** = like a mongoose's tail
- **सुप्प-कत्तर** = winnowing fan / cutting implement — ⚠️ (exact implement uncertain; suppa = winnowing fan; kattara may refer to a cropping/cutting knife)
- **उरंभ-पुड** = like bellows of a forge
- **मुइंगाकारोवमं** = shaped like a mrdanga drum
- **पुरक्खरकवाड** = like the doors of a city gate
- **अयकोडुओ** = iron ball
- **बिस्कगासंठाण** = shaped like a bird's nest
- **अहरींसंठाण** = shaped like a platform/plank
- **सिंपिपुड** = like a conch-shell half

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is one of the longest and most visually dramatic descriptions in the entire Upasakdashang. The Jain canonical tradition uses these elaborate body-horror descriptions not for shock value but to illustrate the full extent of a test: a being so terrifying in appearance that merely seeing it would shatter ordinary courage.

The Pishacha (demon) is a class of being in Indian cosmology — associated with darkness, the night, cremation grounds, and unrefined appetites. By taking this form, the deity signals that it is willing to use the full arsenal of psychological fear. The description proceeds systematically from head to toe, using a series of similes: each body part is compared to something coarse, brutal, distorted, or grotesque. The head like a basket, the teeth drooping like a camel's, the tongue like a harvesting knife, the belly like an iron ball — together these create an image of something that looks almost human but is violently wrong in every proportion and quality.

In the Jain philosophical framework, this scene demonstrates a key teaching: external appearance cannot shake a consciousness grounded in right knowledge. The practitioner's fearlessness in the face of a being this terrifying is not bravado — it is the natural consequence of being rooted in dharmadhyana (the meditation on the nature of the self and the teachings). When the soul knows itself clearly, no external threat can disturb it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deity transformed itself into a horrific demon — a nightmare creature, grotesque from head to toe — and appeared before Kamadeva who was deep in prayer.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something truly frightening enters your awareness — a sudden crisis, a dark thought, a moment of real danger — where does your mind go? What is the center it returns to?

## SUTRA 2.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लंडहमहाजापुए, विगय-भंगर-भुग्ग-भुमए, अवदालिय-वयणविवर-णिल्ललिय-गीहे, सरड-कय-मालियाए, उंदुरमाल-परिणद्ध-सुकए-विंधे, णजल-कय-कप्पूरे, सप्प-कयवेगंच्छे, अप्फोडंते, अधिगज्जंते, भीममुक्कट्टहासे, जाणाविपंचवण्णेहिं लोमेहिं उवचिय एगं महं णीलुप्पल-गवल-गुलिय-अयिसकुसुमप्पगासं खुरधारं असिं गहाय, जेणेव पोसहसाला, जेणेव कामदेवे समणोवासए, तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता आसुरे, रुट्ठे, कुविए, मिसिमिसीयमाणे कामदेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी— हं भो कामदेवा समणोवासया ! अपत्थियपत्थिया, दुरंतपंतलक्खणा, हीण-पुण्ण-चाउद्दसिया, हिरि-सिरि-धिइ-किंति-परिवज्जिया, धम्मकामया, पुण्णकामया, धम्मकिखया, मोक्खकामया, धम्मपिवासिया, पुण्णपिवासिया, मोक्खपिवासिया, णो खलु कप्पइ तव देवाणुपिया ! जं सीलाइं, वयाइं, वेरमणाइं, पच्चक्खाणाइं, पोसहोवासाइं चालित्तए वा, खोभित्तए वा, खंडित्तए वा, भंजित्तए वा, उज्झित्तए वा, परिच्छइत्तए वा। तं जइ णं तुमं अज्ज सीलाइं, वयाइं, वेरमणाइं, पच्चक्खाणाइं, पोसहोवासाइं भंजेसि, तो तं अहं अज्ज इमेणं णीलुप्पल-गवल-गुलिय-अयिस-कुसुमप्पगासेहिं खुरधारेण असिणा खंडाखंडि करेमि, जहा णं तुमं देवाणुपिया ! अट्टुहट्टुवस्सट्टे अकाले चेव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि। ॥२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Laṃḍaha-mahājāpue, vigaya-bhaṃgara-bhugga-bhumae, avadāliya-vayaṇa-vivara-ṇillaliya-gīhe, saraḍa-kaya-māliyāe, uṃdura-māla-pariṇaddha-sukae-viṃdhe, ṇajala-kaya-kappūre, sappa-kayavegiṃcche, apphoḍaṃte, adhigajjaṃte, bhīma-mukkaṭṭahāse, jāṇāvi-paṃca-vaṇṇehiṃ lomehiṃ uvaciya egaṃ mahaṃ nīluppala-gavala-guliya-ayisa-kusumappagāsaṃ khura-dhāraṃ asiṃ gahāya... evaṃ vayāsī— haṃ bho kāmadevā samaṇovāsayā! apatthiya-patthiyā, duraṃta-paṃta-lakkhaṇā, hīṇa-puṇṇa-cāuddasi-yā, hiri-siri-dhii-kiṃti-parivajjiyā... || 2.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That demon — its thighs smeared with filth, its body contorted in wild and broken postures, with its mouth gaping wide and its chin hanging loose, garlanded with lizards, its body bound with garlands of mice, smeared with the fat of snakes, clapping its hands, roaring thunderously, laughing a fearsome and terrible laugh, its hair standing erect in five colors — took hold of a great sword that shone like the combined light of a blue lotus, buffalo horn, iron filings, and the sirisha flower, and which bore a razor-sharp edge. It came to the poshashala, came to where Kamadeva the shramanopasak was, and spoke to him in fury, rage, anger, and simmering wrath, saying: "O shramanopasak Kamadeva! You who seek what is undesirable, whose marks are of an ill-fated end, who have kept no proper fourteenth-day observance, who are devoid of shame, beauty, steadiness, and fame — you who lust for dharma, who thirst for merit, who crave liberation, who long for dharma, who are drunk on it — it is not proper for you, O dear one, to shake, disturb, break, destroy, abandon, or give up your vows, restraints, abstinences, and poshadha-fasts! If today you break your vows, I shall cut you to pieces with this razor-sharp sword that gleams like blue lotus and sirisha blossoms — so that you will be expelled from life at the wrong time in the eight-twelfth-sixteenth year!"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लंडहमहाजापुए** = ⚠️ (compound: laṃḍaha = filth/excrement; mahā-jāpu = big thighs smeared; sense: its thighs covered in filth)
- **अवदालिय-वयणविवर-णिल्ललिय-गीहे** = with mouth cavity wide open and chin hanging loose
- **सरड-कय-मालियाए** = garlanded with lizards
- **उंदुरमाल** = garlands of mice/rats
- **सप्प-कयवेगंच्छे** = smeared with serpent fat
- **अप्फोडंते** = clapping its hands
- **अधिगज्जंते** = thunderously roaring
- **भीममुक्कट्टहासे** = laughing a terrible, frightful laugh
- **णीलुप्पल-गवल-गुलिय-अयिस-कुसुमप्पगासं** = shining like blue lotus + buffalo horn + iron filings + sirisha flower combined
- **खुरधारं असिं** = a razor-edged sword
- **अपत्थियपत्थिया** = one who seeks/desires what should not be desired
- **दुरंतपंतलक्खणा** = one bearing the marks of an ill-fated end
- **हिरि-सिरि-धिइ-किंति-परिवज्जिया** = devoid of shame, prosperity, steadiness, and fame
- **सीलाइं वयाइं वेरमणाइं** = vows, restraints, abstinences
- **पोसहोवासाइं** = poshadha-fasts
- **खंडाखंडि करेमि** = shall cut to pieces
- **अट्टुहट्टुवस्सट्टे** = ⚠️ (compound referring to dying at age 8, 12, or 16 — before one's natural lifespan)
- **अकाले चेव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि** = will be expelled from life at the wrong time (untimely death)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra contains the demon's direct speech — a long, elaborate threat designed to intimidate Kamadeva into abandoning his spiritual practice. The demon first adorns itself with revolting accessories (lizard garlands, rat garlands, snake-fat) and makes a terrifying entrance, then delivers a speech that is remarkable for what it reveals about the demon's own confusion.

The demon's insults are ironically self-defeating. It calls Kamadeva someone who "seeks what is undesirable" — but in Jain understanding, Kamadeva's "undesirable" pursuit is liberation itself. It calls him someone "devoid of shame, beauty, steadiness, and fame" — yet the entire narrative proves the opposite: Kamadeva will display extraordinary steadiness. The demon is in fact accusing him of precisely the qualities that make him spiritually powerful.

The threat is vivid: the demon brandishes a sword that glows with the combined brilliance of blue lotus, buffalo horn, iron, and sirisha flowers — a poetic description of something both beautiful and deadly. It threatens to cut Kamadeva to pieces and kill him prematurely ("at the wrong time"). This is the archetypal threat of death — the most fundamental fear any human faces.

That Kamadeva does not respond to any of this reveals a key Jain teaching: the practitioner who has truly taken up dharma has already, in a sense, transcended the fear of death. When one understands that the soul is eternal and that actions in this life determine the trajectory of future lives, death itself loses its power to intimidate.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The terrifying demon — dressed in rotting garlands of lizards and rats, roaring and laughing horribly — marched into Kamadeva's prayer hall with a sword and threatened to kill him if he didn't give up his religious vows.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When pressure mounts and something or someone demands that you compromise your deepest values — what is the source of your ability to say no?

## SUTRA 2.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं पिसाय-रूवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे, अभीए, अतत्थे, अणुविग्गे, अक्खुभिए, अचलिए, असंभंते, तुसिणीए धम्मज्झाणोवगए विहरइ। ॥२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ pisāya-rūveṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe, abhīe, attatthe, aṇuvigge, akkhu-bhie, acalie, asaṃbhaṃte, tusiṇīe dhammajjhāṇovagae viharai. || 2.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, when shramanopasak Kamadeva was addressed thus by the deity in Pishacha form, he remained fearless, unfrightened, undisturbed, unshaken, unmoved, unperturbed, silent, and completely absorbed in religious meditation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अभीए** = fearless (abhīta)
- **अतत्थे** = unfrightened (a-trasta — one who has not been shaken)
- **अणुविग्गे** = undisturbed, without agitation (an-udvigna)
- **अक्खुभिए** = unshaken (a-kṣubhita — without agitation like a still pond)
- **अचलिए** = unmoved, steadfast (a-calita)
- **असंभंते** = unperturbed (a-sambhraṃta — without confusion or alarm)
- **तुसिणीए** = in silence, silently (tuṣṇīṃ — in quietude)
- **धम्मज्झाणोवगए** = absorbed in dharmadhyana (dharmādhyāna — the meditation on the soul's true nature and the teachings)
- **विहरइ** = remained, dwelled, continued

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the pivot of the entire ordeal narrative. Six words describe Kamadeva's inner state when confronted with the demon's threat: fearless, unfrightened, undisturbed, unshaken, unmoved, unperturbed. These are not mere synonyms — each targets a different dimension of psychological disturbance. The tradition is precise: there are multiple ways a mind can be destabilized, and Kamadeva's mind demonstrates immunity to all of them.

The final phrase — "tusiṇīe dhammajjhāṇovagae" (silent and absorbed in dharmadhyana) — is particularly significant. Kamadeva does not argue with the demon, does not try to reason with it or pray loudly or call for help. He simply continues what he was doing: meditating. This is the response of complete integration — the dharma is not just believed intellectually, it has become the ground of Kamadeva's being.

Dharmadhyana in Jain philosophy refers specifically to meditation on the nature of the self (ātmā), the teachings (āgama), the path (magga), and the goal (moksha). It is one of four types of dhyana described in the Jain canon, and the one prescribed for householders at the shravaka level. By remaining in dharmadhyana, Kamadeva is not passive — he is actively aligned with the highest truth, and that alignment makes him impervious to threats.

This sutra will be repeated (with variations) after each of the six physical attacks across the three forms. Its repetition is intentional — each time reinforcing that no matter what the demon does, Kamadeva's inner state does not change.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the terrifying demon finished its threats, Kamadeva didn't move, didn't speak, didn't tremble — he simply remained in silent, peaceful meditation, completely unafraid.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is your spiritual practice something you do when conditions are favorable — or something you can remain in even when everything around you is threatening?

## SUTRA 2.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे पिसायरूवे कामदेवं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव विहरमाणं पासइ, पासित्ता दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि कामदेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी जाव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि। ॥२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve pisāya-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva viharamāṇaṃ pāsai, pāsittā doccaṃpi taccaṃpi kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī jāva jīviyāo vavarōvijjasi. || 2.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity in Pishacha form, seeing shramanopasak Kamadeva fearless and continuing in meditation (yāvat), addressed shramanopasak Kamadeva a second and then a third time with the same words — up to "you will be expelled from life."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पासइ** = saw, observed
- **पासित्ता** = having seen
- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = a second time and a third time
- **जाव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि** = yāvat (up to) "you will be expelled from life" — the full threat of sutra 2.5 is implied by this abbreviation

### 5. COMMENTARY

The demon's repetition reveals escalating frustration. In the first round, the threat had no effect. Rather than accepting this and departing, the demon returns with identical threats a second and a third time — the canonical number of attempts before a different approach must be tried. This pattern (three verbal threats followed by three physical attacks) mirrors the traditional Jain narrative structure used for testing lay followers.

The three repetitions are not redundancy — they represent a deepening test. The first threat catches the practitioner off-guard; the second tests whether the calm was genuine or just a stunned reaction; the third tests whether the calm can be maintained under persistent pressure. Kamadeva passes all three. The demon's failure at the verbal level shows that Kamadeva's steadfastness is not fragile — it cannot be eroded by repetition.

This also teaches something about the nature of fear itself: most fear is generated not by a single overwhelming event but by sustained, grinding pressure. A practitioner who can hold their ground through the third repetition of a threat — when hope of relief fades and the mind starts to tire — has demonstrated a genuine quality of spiritual strength.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The demon threatened Kamadeva again — a second time, then a third time — with the exact same words. Kamadeva didn't react at all, any of the three times.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone or something repeatedly attacks your peace — hour after hour, day after day — what sustains your stillness? Or does it eventually erode?

## SUTRA 2.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि एवं वुत्ते समाणे, अभीए जाव विहरइ। ॥२.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ doccaṃpi taccaṃpi evaṃ vutte samāṇe, abhīe jāva viharai. || 2.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva, addressed thus a second and third time by the deity, remained fearless (yāvat) and continued in dharma-meditation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तेणं देवेणं** = by that deity
- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = a second and third time
- **एवं वुत्ते समाणे** = being addressed thus
- **अभीए जाव विहरइ** = remained fearless — yāvat (up to) absorbed in dharmadhyana — as fully described in sutra 2.6

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is a paired mirror of sutra 2.6: where that sutra described Kamadeva's inner state after the first threat, this sutra confirms that the same inner state was maintained after the second and third threats. The brevity is deliberate — the teaching does not require repetition. The reader already knows the six qualities of Kamadeva's stillness. The yāvat shorthand simply signals: nothing changed.

This consistency is itself the spiritual teaching. Many people can maintain composure once — under the right conditions, with enough initial resolve. But maintaining the same quality of stillness three times in succession, under identical pressure, reveals something deeper: the stillness is not a performance or a momentary state. It is structural. It is who Kamadeva is at this moment of practice.

The Upasakdashang uses this paired sutra structure (threat → response, repeated threat → same response) six times across this chapter. The cumulative effect is to demonstrate that spiritual steadfastness is not a single dramatic act but a pattern — an orientation of the being that holds across time and repetition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Even after the demon threatened him a second and third time, Kamadeva remained completely still and undisturbed in his meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the difference between patience that wears thin under pressure and equanimity that actually deepens the more it is tested?

## SUTRA 2.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे पिसायरूवे कामदेवं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव विहरमाणं पासइ, पासित्ता आसुरत्ते जाव कामदेवस्स सरसरस्स कायं दुरहइ, दुरहित्ता पच्छिमेणं भाएणं तिक्खुत्तो गीवं वेढेइ, वेढिता तिक्खाहिं विसपरिगयाहिं दाढाहिं उरंसि चेव णिकुट्टेइ। ॥२.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve pisāya-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva viharamāṇaṃ pāsai, pāsittā āsuratthe jāva kāmadevassa sarasarassa kāyaṃ durahai, durahittā pacchiimeṇaṃ bhāeṇaṃ tikkhutto gīvaṃ veḍhei, veḍhitā tikkhāhiṃ visa-parigayāhiṃ dāḍhāhiṃ uraṃsi ceva ṇikuṭṭei. || 2.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the demon in Pishacha form, seeing shramanopasak Kamadeva fearless and continuing in meditation (yāvat), became enraged (yāvat) and grabbed Kamadeva's slender body. Having grabbed him, it coiled around his neck three times from behind, then with its sharp, venom-filled fangs, it pierced him in the chest.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आसुरत्ते** = in a demonic fury, enraged
- **जाव** = yāvat — shorthand indicating the full description of demonic anger (extremely furious, frothing, wild, beyond reason — as described in the full passage)
- **सरसरस्स कायं** = (Kamadeva's) slender, delicate body
- **दुरहइ** = grabbed, seized
- **पच्छिमेणं भाएणं** = from behind
- **तिक्खुत्तो गीवं वेढेइ** = coiled three times around the neck
- **तिक्खाहिं विसपरिगयाहिं दाढाहिं** = with sharp, venom-filled fangs
- **उरंसि चेव णिकुट्टेइ** = pierced him in the chest

### 5. COMMENTARY

When verbal threats fail completely, the demon escalates to direct physical violence. This is the transition from psychological warfare to bodily harm. The demon grabs Kamadeva, wraps around his neck three times — a strangling coil — and then bites him in the chest with poison-filled fangs. This is a serious physical attack: the three-coil grip around the neck threatens asphyxiation; the venom-filled bite threatens poisoning.

The description of the body as "sarasara" (slender, delicate) is deliberately chosen here. Kamadeva is a householder, not a trained ascetic who has hardened his body through years of physical austerity. His body is described as vulnerable — which makes the demonstration of inner strength all the more remarkable. He is not enduring this through physical toughness. He endures it through the strength of dharmadhyana alone.

The Jain tradition uses this escalation to teach a hierarchy of pressures. Verbal threats represent the lowest tier — the attempt to destabilize through fear of the future. Physical violence represents a higher tier — the attempt to destabilize through actual present pain. The question the narrative is asking is: when the pain is real and the body is genuinely suffering, can the soul maintain its orientation? The next sutra answers this.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When threats didn't work, the demon attacked — it grabbed Kamadeva's body, wrapped itself around his neck three times, and bit him in the chest with its poison fangs.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When pain is not hypothetical but real and present in your body — what quality of awareness can you still bring to it?

## SUTRA 2.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए तं उज्जलं जाव अहियासेइ। ॥२.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae taṃ ujjalaṃ jāva ahiyāsei. || 2.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva endured all of that bright, sharp, intense (yāvat) suffering with complete equanimity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तं उज्जलं** = that bright/intense (the suffering — described by this adjective indicating it was extreme, vivid, undeniable)
- **जाव** = yāvat — shorthand for the full description: "उज्जलं, विउलं, कक्कसं, पगाढं, चंड, दुक्खं, दुरियासं वेयणं सम्मं सहइ, खमइ, तितिक्खइ, अहियासेइ" (bright, vast, harsh, deep, fierce, painful, difficult-to-bear — he bore it, endured it, tolerated it, withstood it)
- **अहियासेइ** = endured, withstood (the highest level of patient endurance)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most powerful sutras in the chapter — and one of the most compressed. In a single line, it describes Kamadeva enduring actual physical torture with complete equanimity. The word "ujjala" (bright, intense) applied to suffering indicates that the pain was real, sharp, and fully present — not numbed, not blocked, not escaped. And yet: "ahiyāsei" — he endured it.

The full passage implied by yāvat lists four verbs of endurance: sahati (bore it), khamai (endured it), titikkhati (tolerated it), ahiyāsei (withstood it). These four form an ascending scale of endurance in Jain thought. To merely "bear" something is the first level; to fully "withstand" it with equanimity is the highest. Kamadeva reaches the highest level.

This is the culminating demonstration of the first round of the ordeal. The demon has failed at all three verbal attempts, and now its physical attack has also failed to shake Kamadeva's dharmadhyana. Kamadeva is still in silence, still in meditation. The demon's entire toolkit — psychological, verbal, and physical — has been exhausted without result.

The Jain tradition points to this verse when teaching about the distinction between pain and suffering. Pain is the physical sensation — unavoidable, present, real. Suffering is the mental response — the contraction, the fear, the resistance, the "this should not be happening." Kamadeva experiences pain; he does not create suffering around it. Dharmadhyana is the practice that makes this possible.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Despite being physically attacked and in real pain, Kamadeva bore all of it calmly — completely unbroken in his meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you distinguish between pain — which is unavoidable — and suffering — which is the mind's resistance to pain? What would it take to experience pain without creating suffering around it?

## SUTRA 2.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे पिसायरूवे कामदेवं समणोवासयं जाहे णो संचाएइ णिग्गंथाओ पावयणाओ चालित्तए वा, जाव दिव्यं हत्थि-रूवं विप्पजहइ, विप्पजहित्ता एगं महं दिव्यं हत्थिरूवं विउव्वइ, उग्गविसं, चंडविसं, घोरविसं, महाकायं, मसी-मूसा-कालगं, णयण-विस-रोस-पुण्णं, अंजण-पुंज-णिगरण-गासं, रत्तछ लोहिय-लोयणं, जमल-जुयल-चंचल-जीहं, धरणीयल-वेणीभूयं, उक्कड-फुड-कुडिल-जडिल-कक्कस-वियड-फुडाडोव-करण-दच्छं, लोहागर-धम्मण-धमधमेतघोसं, अणागलिय-तिव्व-चंड-रोस-सप्परूवं विउव्वइ। सुजायं पुरओ उदगंप, पिट्ठेओ वराहं, अयाकुच्छि, अलंकुच्छि, पलंब-लंबोदरागर-करं, म-भुग्गर-मल्लिला-महल-धवल-दंतं, आणामिय-चाव-लालिय-संवेललिय-गओ, सोहं-पडिपुण्णं-षलणं, वीसइं-पुच्छं, गुलगुलेंत। ॥२.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve pisāya-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ jāhe ṇo saṃcāei ṇiggaṃthāo pāvaṇāo cālittae vā, jāva divvaṃ hatthi-rūvaṃ vippajaha, vippajahistā egaṃ mahaṃ divvaṃ hatthi-rūvaṃ viuvvai, ugga-visaṃ, caṃḍa-visaṃ, ghora-visaṃ, mahā-kāyaṃ, masī-mūsā-kālagaṃ, ṇayaṇa-visa-rosa-puṇṇaṃ, aṃjaṇa-puṃja-ṇigaraṇa-gāsaṃ, rattacha lohiya-loyaṇaṃ, jamala-juyal-caṃcala-jīhaṃ, dharaṇīyala-veṇī-bhūyaṃ... viuvvai. || 2.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity in Pishacha form, seeing that it could not dislodge Kamadeva from the Nirgranth teachings (yāvat), abandoned the Pishacha form and created a great divine elephant form. That divine elephant had fierce venom, raging venom, terrifying venom; an enormous body; black as ink and an iron crucible; eyes full of venomous fury; glowing like heaped masses of black collyrium; red and blood-filled eyes; a paired, darting tongue; coiled flat on the ground like a plait of hair; with rough, cracked, curly, matted, harsh, expanded, flaring ears; with a sound like the thunderous roar of a great forge; unrestrained, intense, fierce — it took the form of an infuriated divine elephant. Beautiful in front like a water-buffalo, like a wild boar from behind; with an iron belly and a decorated belly; long-hanging, pendulous belly; great, white tusks like the maces of champion wrestlers; flexible as a drawn bow, beautifully swaying with a full gait; with twenty tails; roaring thunderously.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णिग्गंथाओ पावयणाओ चालित्तए** = to dislodge/shake from the Nirgranth teachings
- **दिव्यं हत्थिरूवं विउव्वइ** = created a divine elephant form (vaikriya power)
- **उग्गविसं, चंडविसं, घोरविसं** = fierce venom, raging venom, terrifying venom
- **मसी-मूसा-कालगं** = black as ink (masi) and an iron crucible (musa)
- **णयण-विस-रोस-पुण्णं** = eyes full of venomous anger
- **अंजण-पुंज-णिगरण-गासं** = shining like heaped masses of black collyrium (anjana)
- **जमल-जुयल-चंचल-जीहं** = paired darting tongue
- **धरणीयल-वेणीभूयं** = flat on the ground, like a hair-plait
- **लोहागर-धम्मण-धमधमेतघोसं** = thunderous sound like a great iron furnace/forge
- **वीसइं-पुच्छं** = twenty tails ⚠️ (characteristic of divine celestial elephants in Jain cosmology)
- **गुलगुलेंत** = roaring, rumbling

### 5. COMMENTARY

The escalation from Pishacha to divine elephant is significant. The Pishacha form was ghastly and monstrous — something designed to produce revulsion and terror. The divine elephant form is different: it is a being of enormous power. The description mixes terrifying qualities (poison, blood-red eyes, thunderous roar) with qualities of grandeur and cosmic scale (the divine elephant of Indra's court, white tusks like warriors' maces, a beautiful full-gait, twenty tails — a marker of celestial elephants in Jain cosmology).

The shift in strategy mirrors something true in how adversity works. The first form of testing is pure ugliness and horror — designed to break a person through disgust and fear. When that fails, the test shifts to raw power and intimidation. This is the difference between someone threatening you with something loathsome versus someone demonstrating that they have the force to simply crush you. The first appeals to revulsion; the second to the awareness of one's own smallness and vulnerability.

The elephant form's physical description also creates a sharp contrast: Kamadeva is described earlier as having a "slender, delicate" body. The divine elephant is enormous, its tusks like warrior weapons, its sound like a furnace. The visual and auditory contrast is extreme — yet as the next sutras will show, this disparity in scale does nothing to Kamadeva's inner state.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When threats in demon form failed, the deity abandoned that form and instead became a massive, terrifying divine elephant — enormous, roaring, with enormous white tusks, dark as ink, with eyes blazing with fury.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Has the nature of a challenge you face ever changed forms — shifting from one kind of pressure to a completely different kind? How did you meet it?

## SUTRA 2.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विउव्वित्ता जेणेव पोसहसाला, जेणेव कामदेवे समणोवासए, तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता कामदेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी— हं भो कामदेवा समणोवासया ! तहेव भणइ जाव भंजेसि, तो ते अज्ज अहं सोंडाए गिण्हेइ, गेण्हेत्ता णीलि-पोसहसालाओ णीणिमि, उड्डं वेहासं डाहि, उड्डविहित्ता तिक्खेहिं दंतमुसलेहिं पडिच्छामि, पडिच्छित्ता अहे धरणितलंसि तिक्खुत्तो पायसु लोलेमि, जहा णं तुमं देवाणुपिया ! अट्ट-दुहट्ट-वस्सट्टे अकाले चेव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि। ॥२.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Viuvvittā jeṇeva posaha-sālā, jeṇeva kāmadeve samaṇovāsae, teṇeva uvāgacchati, uvāgacchittā kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī — haṃ bho kāmadevā samaṇovāsayā! taheva bhaṇai jāva bhaṃjesi, to te ajja ahaṃ soṃḍāe giṇhei, geṇhettā nīli-posaha-sālāo nīṇimi, uḍḍaṃ vehāsaṃ ḍāhi, uḍḍavihittā tikkhehiṃ daṃta-musalehiṃ paḍicchāmi, paḍicchittā ahe dharaṇitalaṃsi tikkhutto pāyasu lolemi, jahā ṇaṃ tumaṃ devāṇupiyā! aṭṭa-duhaṭṭa-vassaṭṭe akāle ceva jīviyāo vavarōvijjasi. || 2.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having taken that form, it came to the poshashala, came to where shramanopasak Kamadeva was, and spoke to him as before (yāvat) — the same words up to "if you break your vows today, I will seize you with my trunk, pull you out of the poshashala, throw you high into the sky, catch you on my sharp mace-like tusks as you fall, and drag you three times along the ground with my feet — so that you will be expelled from life prematurely in terrible pain."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सोंडाए गिण्हेइ** = seizes with (its) trunk
- **णीलि-पोसहसालाओ णीणिमि** = drags/pulls out of the poshashala
- **उड्डं वेहासं डाहि** = throws high into the sky (vehasa = sky, air)
- **तिक्खेहिं दंतमुसलेहिं** = on sharp, mace-like tusks
- **पडिच्छामि** = will receive/catch (on the tusks, coming down)
- **अहे धरणितलंसि** = below, on the surface of the earth
- **तिक्खुत्तो पायसु लोलेमि** = drags three times with the feet (rolling/crushing)
- **अकाले चेव जीवियाओ ववरोविज्जसि** = will be expelled from life prematurely

### 5. COMMENTARY

The elephant's threat is specific and vivid: seize with trunk, tear from poshashala, hurl into sky, impale on tusks, drag along ground. This is not a general death-threat but a described sequence of tortures. The specificity is deliberate — it is designed to trigger the imagination, to make the threatened person picture exactly what is about to happen to them.

The detail of being torn out of the poshashala itself is worth noting. The poshashala is Kamadeva's sacred space for the duration of the poshadha observance. To be ripped from it physically is also a desecration of the vow — the ordeal attacks not just the body but the sacred container of the practice.

The imagery of being thrown into the sky, caught on tusks, and dragged along the ground combines three kinds of torment: the disorientation of weightlessness, the piercing of impact, and the grinding of prolonged contact. The demon knows the full anatomy of physical suffering and uses it in its threat. Yet as the narrative demonstrates, the one who is fully absorbed in dharmadhyana does not respond to any of it — because the consciousness has moved to a place where these threats simply have no grip.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The elephant marched to Kamadeva's prayer hall and threatened him: "If you don't abandon your vows, I'll grab you with my trunk, throw you into the sky, impale you on my tusks, and drag you along the ground until you die."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it take for you to remain calm when threatened with something so vivid and specific that your imagination can already feel the pain?

## SUTRA 2.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए तेणं देवेणं हत्थिरूवेणं एवं वुत्ते समाणे अभीए जाव विहरइ। ॥२.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae teṇaṃ deveṇaṃ hatthi-rūveṇaṃ evaṃ vutte samāṇe abhīe jāva viharai. || 2.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva, addressed thus by the deity in elephant form, remained fearless (yāvat) and continued in dharma-meditation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **हत्थिरूवेणं** = in elephant form
- **अभीए जाव विहरइ** = fearless, yāvat — all six qualities of stillness as in sutra 2.6 — absorbed in dharmadhyana

### 5. COMMENTARY

The response is identical in substance to sutra 2.6 — the same unshakeable stillness. This is the power of the repetitive structure in Jain canonical narrative: it conditions the reader/listener to expect and recognize that Kamadeva's state does not change regardless of the form in which the threat comes. The form changes; the response does not.

In spiritual training traditions worldwide, this is a key milestone: when inner stillness becomes form-independent — when it no longer depends on external conditions being favorable but simply is, regardless of what appears — that is when it has become genuinely integrated.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The elephant's threats had no more effect on Kamadeva than the demon's had — he remained completely still in meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Does your inner peace depend on external conditions being calm? What would it take for it to hold even when conditions are extreme?

## SUTRA 2.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे हत्थिरूवे कामदेवं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव विहरमाणं पासइ, पासित्ता दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि कामदेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी— हं भो ! कामदेवा ! जाव विहरइ। ॥२.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve hatthi-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva viharamāṇaṃ pāsai, pāsittā doccaṃpi taccaṃpi kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī — haṃ bho! kāmadevā! jāva viharai. || 2.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity in elephant form, seeing shramanopasak Kamadeva fearless and continuing in meditation, addressed him a second and third time with the same words. But Kamadeva (yāvat) remained in dharma-meditation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दोच्चंपि तच्चंपि** = second time and third time
- **हं भो ! कामदेवा !** = the same opening of the speech — the elephant addresses Kamadeva again
- **जाव विहरइ** = yāvat — remained as described (fearless, in dharmadhyana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The elephant, like the Pishacha before it, repeats its threats three times before escalating to physical action. The repetition establishes that this is a complete cycle: three verbal attempts followed by physical attack. The fact that Kamadeva does not respond to the second and third repetitions either shows that his steadfastness is not merely a response to novelty or a first-time resolve — it is consistent. The three repetitions test whether Kamadeva's stillness will crack under persistence, and it does not.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The elephant threatened Kamadeva a second and third time, but Kamadeva remained completely unmoved each time.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone keeps pushing, keeps repeating the same demand or threat — how do you hold your ground without hardening into anger?

## SUTRA 2.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से देवे हत्थिरूवे कामदेवं समणोवासयं अभीयं जाव पासइ, पासित्ता आसुरत्ते जाव कामदेवं समणोवासयं सोंडाए गिण्हेइ, गेण्हेत्ता उड्डं वेहासं उड्डविहइ, उड्डविहित्ता तिक्खेहिं दंतमुसलेहिं पडिच्छइ, पडिच्छित्ता अहे धरणितलंसि तिक्खुत्तो पायसु लोलेइ। ॥२.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve hatthi-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ abhīyaṃ jāva pāsai, pāsittā āsuratthe jāva kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ soṃḍāe giṇhei, geṇhettā uḍḍaṃ vehāsaṃ uḍḍavahai, uḍḍavahittā tikkhehiṃ daṃta-musalehiṃ paḍicchati, paḍicchittā ahe dharaṇitalaṃsi tikkhutto pāyasu lolei. || 2.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity in elephant form, seeing shramanopasak Kamadeva fearless and continuing in meditation (yāvat), became enraged (yāvat) and seized shramanopasak Kamadeva with its trunk, threw him high into the sky, caught him on its sharp mace-like tusks as he fell, and dragged him three times along the ground with its feet.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **आसुरत्ते** = in demonic fury, enraged (literally: in the state of being a demon/asura — extreme uncontrolled anger)
- **सोंडाए गिण्हेइ** = seized with the trunk
- **उड्डं वेहासं उड्डविहइ** = threw him high into the sky
- **तिक्खेहिं दंतमुसलेहिं पडिच्छइ** = caught him on sharp mace-like tusks
- **अहे धरणितलंसि** = on the ground below
- **तिक्खुत्तो पायसु लोलेइ** = dragged three times with the feet

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra describes exactly what was threatened in sutra 2.12 — now carried out. The threat has become reality. The sequence is brutal: the trunk seizes the fragile human body, hurls it into the air, allows it to fall onto massive pointed tusks, then the enormous feet drag the already-injured body along the earth three times. This is a sustained, sequential torment — not a single blow but a methodical infliction of damage.

What is especially powerful is that the text does not pause here to describe Kamadeva's pain or shock or cries. The narrative moves directly from the physical action to Kamadeva's response in the next sutra — which is the same: he endured. There is no gap between the violence and the equanimity. The equanimity is simultaneous with the suffering, not sequential to it.

This is the second complete round of the ordeal: elephant form, three verbal threats, one physical attack. The pattern will be mirrored once more with the serpent form.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The enraged elephant did exactly what it threatened: it grabbed Kamadeva with its trunk, threw him into the sky, caught him on its tusks, and dragged his body along the ground three times.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a difference between enduring pain and being defeated by it? What keeps those two things separate?

## SUTRA 2.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए तं उज्जलं जाव अहियासेइ। ॥२.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae taṃ ujjalaṃ jāva ahiyāsei. || 2.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva endured all of that bright, intense (yāvat) suffering with complete equanimity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

Same as sutra 2.10 — the same formula of endurance applied to the suffering caused by the elephant form.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The second round ends here — complete cycle, same result. Pishacha form: three threats, one attack, endured. Elephant form: three threats, one attack, endured. The cumulative picture is of a practitioner whose inner stability is simply not available to be shaken. The form of the test changes; the depth of the stillness does not.

The narrative efficiency here is itself a teaching device: by using the same formula twice, the text prevents the reader from placing too much emphasis on the novelty of the threat. What matters is not the nature of the attack but the nature of the response. And the response — ujjala jāva ahiyāsei — is constant.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Despite being physically tortured by the divine elephant, Kamadeva bore all of it with the same complete calm as before.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you look back at a time when you endured something very difficult — what carried you through it?

## SUTRA 2.17

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se deve hatthi-rūve kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ jāhe ṇo saṃcāei ṇiggaṃthāo pāvaṇāo cālittae vā, jāva divvaṃ hatthi-rūvaṃ vippajaha, vippajahistā egaṃ mahaṃ divvaṃ sappa-rūvaṃ viuvvai, ugga-visaṃ, caṃḍa-visaṃ, ghora-visaṃ, mahā-kāyaṃ, masī-mūsā-kālagaṃ, ṇayaṇa-visa-rosa-puṇṇaṃ... sappa-rūvaṃ viuvvai. || 2.17 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the deity in elephant form, seeing that it still could not dislodge Kamadeva from the Nirgranth teachings (yāvat), abandoned the elephant form and created a great divine serpent form — with fierce venom, raging venom, terrifying venom; enormous body; black as ink and an iron crucible; eyes full of venomous fury; glowing like heaped black collyrium; red and blood-filled eyes; paired, darting tongue; coiled flat on the ground like a plait; with rough, cracked, curly, matted, harsh, expanded, roaring scales; thunderous like a great forge; unrestrained, intense, fierce, raging — it manifested as a great divine serpent. It came to the poshashala where Kamadeva was and said: "O shramanopasak Kamadeva! [same as before] If you do not break your vows today, I will seize your slender body, coil around your neck three times from behind, and pierce your chest with my sharp venom-filled fangs — so that you will be expelled from life prematurely!"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **दिव्यं सप्प-रूवं** = divine serpent form
- **सरसरस्स कायं** = slender body
- **दुरहामि** = will seize, grab
- **पच्छिमेणं भाएणं** = from behind
- **तिक्खुत्तो गीवं वेढेमि** = will coil three times around the neck
- **तिक्खाहिं विसपरिगयाहिं दाढाहिं** = with sharp venom-filled fangs
- **उरंसि चेव णिकुट्टेमि** = will pierce in the chest

### 5. COMMENTARY

The third and final form is the serpent (sarpa). The choice of forms has a logic: Pishacha (demonic ugliness), elephant (divine power and scale), serpent (venom and constriction — a different kind of intimate, slow killing). Each form appeals to a different primal fear. The Pishacha evokes revulsion and the fear of darkness; the elephant evokes the terror of overwhelming force; the serpent evokes the fear of poison and the helplessness of being bound.

The serpent is also the closest of the three to human scale — it can coil around a person's neck and crush them, bite and poison them. There is something particularly personal about this threat. And yet the serpent form carries the same description of enormous divine power: ugravisha (fierce venom), chandavisha (raging venom), ghoravisha (terrifying venom) — three grades of escalating lethality.

The three forms also map onto the three categories of human fear that Jain teachers identify: fear of ugliness/the uncanny, fear of power/force, and fear of dissolution/poisoning. Together they represent a complete test of Kamadeva's capacity for fearlessness.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After the elephant form failed, the deity abandoned it and became an enormous divine serpent — black, coiled, dripping with venom — and again threatened to kill Kamadeva by wrapping around his neck and biting him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Different forms of threat activate different fears. Which kind of adversity — public humiliation, physical danger, loss of security, or slow dissolution — is hardest for you to face with equanimity?

- **दिव्यं देव-रूवं विउव्वइ** = manifested the true divine form

- **दिव्वेणं वण्णेणं ... दिव्वाए लेसाए** = with divine color, fragrance, beauty, touch, gait, prosperity, radiance, light, shadow, fire, brilliance, luster
- **दस दिसाओ उज्जोवेमाणे** = illuminating all ten directions
- **पासाइयं दरिसणिज्जं** = pleasing and beautiful to behold
- **धण्णसि** = you are blessed/fortunate
- **संपुण्णे, कयत्थे, कयलक्खणे** = complete, accomplished, of excellent marks

This sutra marks the turning point of the chapter — the revelation. The transformation from terrifying to beautiful is complete and instantaneous. The same being that was a grotesque Pishacha, then a fury-filled elephant, then a venom-dripping serpent, now shines with twelve categories of divine light, illuminating all ten directions.

The deity's words of praise to Kamadeva are remarkable for their sincerity. "You are blessed" — not ironically, but genuinely. "Well-gained is the fruit of your human birth" — this is the central Jain teaching about the preciousness of human life: it is the one form of existence in which liberation is possible, in which the full range of ethical and spiritual practice can be undertaken. The deity is acknowledging that Kamadeva has used his human birth properly — that he has proven himself worthy of it.

The phrase "the Nirgranth teaching is such that it cannot be shaken or turned aside" is the deity's own recognition of the correctness of the path. The very being that tried to dislodge Kamadeva from this path now praises it. This is the tradition's way of demonstrating that even adversity, when fully met, ultimately confirms the truth of the dharma.

The twelve categories of divine radiance (color, fragrance, beauty, touch, gait, prosperity, radiance, light, shadow, fire, brilliance, luster) are standard descriptions of celestial beings in Jain canonical literature. They signal that this being, in its true form, is genuinely beautiful and powerful — which retroactively reveals that the entire ordeal was conducted by a being of real spiritual potency, not a weak or trivial force. Kamadeva was not tested by something small.

Evaṃ khalu devāṇupiyā! sakke devinda devārāyā, vajjapāṇī, puraṃdare, sayakkau, sahassakkhe, maghavaṃ, pāgasāsaṇe, dāhiṇaḍḍha-logā-hivai, battīsa-vimāṇa-saya-sahassāhivai, erāvaṇavāhaṇe, suriṃde... || 2.22 ||

"Thus indeed, O dear one! I am Shakra (Indra), king of the gods, the ruler of the devas: I am Vajrapani (holder of the thunderbolt), Purandra (destroyer of citadels), Shatakarma (hundred-actioned), Sahasraksha (thousand-eyed), Maghava, the chastiser of Paka, the overlord of the southern half of the world, the lord over thirty-two hundred thousand celestial vehicles, the one borne by Airavana the elephant, the foremost deva — wearing celestial clothes as his sky-garment, his crown adorned with ornaments, his face beaming with golden radiance, dwelling in Saudharma heaven in the Saudharma-vatamsaka, amid the thirty-three Trayastrimsha devas, surrounded by four world-guardian devas and their retinues, amid eight chief queens and their retinues, three divine assemblies, seven armies and seven army-chiefs, and surrounded by four times four thousand divine bodyguard devas — amid all these many gods and goddesses — O dear one! And Kamadeva replied: "Yes, Bhagavan, it was so."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सक्के देविंदे देवराया** = Shakra, king of the gods, Indra
- **वज्जपाणी** = holder of the vajra (thunderbolt)
- **पुरंदरे** = Purandra, destroyer of cities (epithet of Indra)
- **सयक्कउ** = Shatakarma, the hundred-actioned
- **सहस्सक्खे** = Sahasraksha, thousand-eyed
- **मघवं** = Maghava (another Indra epithet, the bountiful)
- **पागसासणे** = chastiser of Paka (the demon)
- **एरावणवाहणे** = borne/riding the Airavana elephant
- **बत्तीस-विमाण-सय-सहस्साहिवई** = lord over 32 hundred thousand celestial vehicles

- **धण्णसि** = you are blessed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the chapter's grand climax — Indra (Sakkendra) himself, the king of all the gods of Saudharma heaven, identifying himself and delivering a formal eulogy to Kamadeva. The self-introduction of Indra is a set-piece that appears multiple times across the Upasakdashang — each time with the same sequence of his titles and epithets. This is intentional: Indra's identity is established in full, so the weight of his praise is fully felt. This is not a minor deity offering compliments — it is the overlord of the entire first heaven bearing witness.

The significance of Indra's praise extends beyond personal compliment. In the Jain theological framework, Indra is a being of extraordinary merit but also one who has not yet attained liberation — he is still in the cycle of rebirth, though at its highest mundane level. For such a being to recognize and praise a lay householder's spiritual accomplishment is a reversal of the usual hierarchy. The world expects monks and renunciants to be spiritually admirable; the Upasakdashang argues that lay practitioners who have truly internalized the dharma can demonstrate equal or even greater steadfastness.

The question at the end — "Was this indeed so, Kamadeva?" and Kamadeva's simple affirmation "Yes, it was so" — is also significant. Indra already knows what happened. He was watching. The question is not for Indra's benefit but for Kamadeva's: it is an invitation to consciously recognize what has just occurred, to integrate the experience and claim it. Kamadeva's "Yes" is his quiet acknowledgment of what he has passed through.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have gone through something very difficult and come out the other side — do you take a moment to consciously recognize what you endured and what it revealed about you?

## SUTRA 2.24

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेणं कालेणं तेणं समएणं समणे भगवं महावीरे जाव जेणेव चंपा णयरी, जेणेव पुण्णभद्दे चेइए, तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता अहापडिरूवं ओग्गहं ओगिण्हित्ता संजमेणं तवसा अप्पाणं भावेमाणे विहरइ। ॥२.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Teṇaṃ kāleṇaṃ teṇaṃ samaeṇaṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre jāva jeṇeva campā ṇaYarī, jeṇeva puṇṇabhadde ceie, teṇeva uvāgacchati, uvāgacchittā ahā-paḍirūvaṃ oggahaṃ ogiṇhittā saṃjameṇaṃ tavasā appāṇaṃ bhāvemāṇe viharai. || 2.24 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that time, in that era, Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira (yāvat) came to Champanagari, came to the Purnahbhadra Chaitya, accepted the appropriate shelter, and dwelling there, engaged in purifying his soul through restraint and austerity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **समणे भगवं महावीरे** = Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira
- **अहापडिरूवं ओग्गहं ओगिण्हित्ता** = having accepted the appropriate, proper shelter
- **संजमेणं तवसा** = through restraint (samyama) and austerity (tapas)
- **अप्पाणं भावेमाणे** = engaging in purifying/refining the soul
- **विहरइ** = remained, dwelled, continued

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra marks the beginning of the second half of the chapter's narrative — the meeting between Kamadeva and Mahavira. The ordeal is over; now comes the confirmation, the teaching, and the celebration.

The description of Mahavira's own practice — "through restraint and austerity, purifying the soul" — is important as a counterpoint to Kamadeva's test. While Kamadeva was practicing the householder's poshadha and enduring the upsarg, Mahavira was independently engaged in his own continuous self-purification. The two practices are different in form but aligned in orientation: both are processes of turning toward the true nature of the self.

The arrival of Mahavira at Champa creates the context for Kamadeva's next act — visiting him, seeking confirmation, asking questions. The Upasakdashang frequently uses this structure: the lay follower is tested in private, then meets the teacher who both confirms what happened and draws a larger teaching from it for the entire assembly.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Around this time, Lord Mahavira arrived in Kamadeva's city of Champa and stayed at the Purnahbhadra garden, continuing his own spiritual practice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When have you sought the counsel of a true teacher after going through something difficult — and what did that meeting illuminate that you could not have seen alone?

## SUTRA 2.25

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं समाए कहाए लद्धट्टे समाणे एव भगवं महावीरे जाव विहरइ। तं सेयं खलु मम समणं भगवं महावीरं वंदित्ता, णमंसित्ता ताओ पडिणियत्तस्स पोसहं पारित्तए ति कट्टु एव संपेहेइ, संपेहेत्ता सुद्धप्पावेसाइं वत्थाइं पवर-परिहाए जेणेव समणे भगवं महावीरे, तेणेव उवागच्छइ, उवागच्छित्ता तिक्खुत्तो आयाहिणं पयाहिणं करेइ, करेत्ता वंदइ, णमंसइ, वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता तिविहाए पज्जुवासणाए पज्जुवासइ। ॥२.२५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ samāe kahāe laddhaṭṭhe samāṇe eva bhagavaṃ mahāvīre jāva viharai. taṃ seyaṃ khalu mama samaṇaṃ bhagavaṃ mahāvīraṃ vaṃdittā, ṇaṃsittā tāo paḍiṇiyattassa posahan pārittae ti kaṭṭu eva saṃpehei, saṃpehettā suddha-ppāvesāiṃ vatthāiṃ pavara-parihāe jeṇeva samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre, teṇeva uvāgacchati, uvāgacchittā tikkhutto āyāhiṇaṃ payāhiṇaṃ karei, karette vaṃdai, ṇaṃsai, vaṃdittā ṇaṃsittā tivihāe pajjuvāsaṇāe pajjuvāsai. || 2.25 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Kamadeva, hearing that Bhagavan Mahavira (yāvat) was there, thought: "It would be best for me to first bow and pay respects to Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, and then return and complete the poshadha." Having reflected thus, he put on pure and auspicious clothes of fine quality and came to where Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira was. He walked around him three times clockwise, bowed and paid respects, and then served him in the threefold manner (with body, speech, and mind).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लद्धट्टे** = having learned, having received the news
- **पडिणियत्तस्स** = having returned back
- **पोसहं पारित्तए** = to complete/fulfill the poshadha
- **सुद्धप्पावेसाइं वत्थाइं** = pure, auspicious clothes appropriate for entering (a sacred presence)
- **तिक्खुत्तो आयाहिणं पयाहिणं** = three times circumambulation clockwise (pradakshina)
- **तिविहाए पज्जुवासणाए** = threefold attendance/service (physical, verbal, mental)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kamadeva's decision here reveals a beautiful quality: even after enduring the most extreme ordeal of his life — three supernatural attacks, venom, being hurled and dragged — his first thought upon learning Mahavira has arrived is not "I need to rest" or "I need to process this." It is: "I should go see my teacher."

The orderly sequence — put on proper clothes, walk to the teacher, circumambulate three times, bow, offer threefold service — reflects the Jain tradition's understanding that the meeting with a liberated teacher (or in this case, a teacher on the path to liberation) is itself a form of practice that must be conducted with full attention and care.

The decision to visit Mahavira before completing the poshadha is also notable. The text presents this as the right decision — going to the teacher takes priority over finishing the formal observance, because the teacher's presence is itself the highest form of dharma-contact.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Learning that Lord Mahavira had arrived nearby, Kamadeva put on his best clothes, walked to Mahavira's assembly, bowed to him with deep reverence, and sat attentively to serve him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have just been through something profound — a test, a loss, a revelation — do you seek the presence of someone wiser to help you understand what you experienced?

## SUTRA 2.26

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं समणे भगवं महावीरे कामदेवस्स समणोवासयस्स तीसे य जाव धम्मकहा समत्ता। ॥२.२६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre kāmadevassa samaṇovāsayassa tīse ya jāva dhamma-kahā samattā. || 2.26 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira gave a religious discourse (dharmakatha) to shramanopasak Kamadeva and to the assembly (yāvat), and the discourse was completed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तीसे य** = and to the assembly (tīse = the assembly, gathering)
- **जाव** = yāvat — the full dharmakatha as given in detail elsewhere
- **धम्मकहा समत्ता** = the religious discourse was completed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the most compressed sutra in the post-ordeal section — the entire teaching event summarized in a single sentence. The yāvat formula points to the full content of a dharmakatha as given elsewhere in the Agamic literature: covering the four essential topics (jīva, ajīva, āśrava, saṃvara — the soul, the non-soul, the inflow of karma, and the cessation of karma), the nature of right knowledge, right faith, and right conduct, and the path to liberation.

The brevity here is appropriate: the dharmakatha itself is not the dramatic focus of this chapter. The focus is on Kamadeva's ordeal and its meaning. Mahavira's discourse is the formal teaching context that enables the next sutra — the confirmation of the ordeal and the drawing out of its significance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lord Mahavira gave a complete religious teaching to Kamadeva and the gathered assembly.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

After a profound personal experience, how does receiving formal teaching help you integrate and understand what you went through?

## SUTRA 2.27

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कामदेवा ! तिं समणे भगवं महावीरे कामदेवं समणोवासयं एवं वयासी— सेणूणं कामदेवा ! तुब्भं अंतिए पुव्व-रत्तावरत्त-काल-समयंसि एगे देवे पाउब्भूए। तए णं से देवे एगं महं दिव्यं पिसाय-रूवं विउव्वइ, एवं तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा कहेइ जाव जामेव दिसं पाउब्भूए तामेव दिसं पिंडगाए। से णूणं कामदेवा ! अट्ठे समट्ठे ? हंता, अत्थि। ॥२.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Kāmadevā! tiṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre kāmadevaṃ samaṇovāsayaṃ evaṃ vayāsī — seṇūṇaṃ kāmadevā! tubbhaṃ aṃtie puvva-ratta-varatta-kāla-samayaṃsi ege deve pāubhūe. tae ṇaṃ se deve egaṃ mahaṃ divvaṃ pisāya-rūvaṃ viuvvai, evaṃ tiṇṇi vi uvasaggā kahe jāva jāmeva disaṃ pāubhūe tāmeva disaṃ piṃḍagāe. se ṇūṇaṃ kāmadevā! aṭṭhe samaṭṭhe? haṃtā, atthi. || 2.27 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O Kamadeva! Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira spoke to shramanopasak Kamadeva thus: "O Kamadeva! A deity appeared before you in the midnight period. That deity created a great divine Pishacha form" — and he described all three ordeals — "up to, it returned in the direction from which it had come. Was this indeed so, O Kamadeva?" And Kamadeva replied: "Yes, Bhagavan, it was so."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सेणूणं** = know then (seyanunam — introductory word for confirmation)
- **तुब्भं अंतिए** = before you, in your presence
- **एगे देवे पाउब्भूए** = a certain deity appeared
- **तिण्णि वि उवसग्गा** = all three ordeals
- **अट्ठे समट्ठे** = is this meaning true? Is this correctly understood?
- **हंता, अत्थि** = yes, it is (Kamadeva's affirmation)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Bhagavan Mahavira's act of naming what happened — publicly, before the assembly — is itself a form of spiritual witness. He recounts the three ordeals not because Kamadeva needs to be told (he lived through them) but because naming them in the presence of the teacher and the assembly transforms a private ordeal into a shared teaching.

The question "Was this so?" is a crucial element. Mahavira does not simply declare what happened. He invites Kamadeva to confirm it — to actively claim his own experience. Kamadeva's "Yes" (haṃtā) is his act of witness to himself. He does not minimize it, does not say "it wasn't so bad." He simply confirms: yes, this happened, and I was there.

Mahavira's omniscience (kevalajñāna) — his ability to perceive events without being physically present — is here demonstrated naturally: he knew about the three ordeals without having witnessed them with physical eyes. The ability of a kevalin to know the experiences of others directly is a foundational claim of Jain theology, and scenes like this one in the Agamas serve to demonstrate it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lord Mahavira described to Kamadeva everything that had happened during the night — all three ordeals — as if he had been watching the entire time. He asked Kamadeva: "Was this so?" And Kamadeva said: "Yes, Bhagavan, exactly as you say."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone who truly sees you names your experience accurately — not diminishing it, not dramatizing it, just seeing it clearly — what does that recognition feel like?

## SUTRA 2.28

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अज्जो ! तिं समणे भगवं महावीरे बहवे समणे णिग्गंथे य णिग्गंथीओ य आमंतेत्ता एवं वयासी— जइ ताव, अज्जो ! समणोवासगा, गिहिणो, गिहमज्झावसंता दिव्व-माणुस-तिरिक्खजोणिए उवसग्गे सम्मं सहंति, तितिक्खंति अहियासेंति, सक्का पुणाइं, अज्जो ! समणेहिं णिग्गंथेहिं दुवालसंगं गणिपिडगं अहिज्जमाणेहिं दिव्व-माणुस-तिरिक्खजोणिए उवसग्गे सम्मं सहित्तए, तितिक्खित्तए, अहियासित्तए। ॥२.२८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ajjo! tiṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre bahave samaṇe ṇiggaṃthe ya ṇiggaṃthīo ya āmaṃtettā evaṃ vayāsī — jai tāva, ajjo! samaṇovāsagā, gihiṇo, giha-majjhāvasaṃtā divva-māṇusa-tirikkhajōṇie uvasagge sammaṃ sahaṃti, titikkhaṃti ahiyāseṃti, sakkā puṇāiṃ, ajjo! samaṇehiṃ ṇiggaṃthehiṃ duvālasaṃgaṃ gaṇi-piḍagaṃ ahijjamāṇehiṃ divva-māṇusa-tirikkhajōṇie uvasagge sammaṃ sahittae, titikkhittae, ahiyāsittae. || 2.28 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O Noble Ones! Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira then addressed the many monks and nuns: "O Noble Ones! If lay followers (shramanopasaks), who are householders living amid household life, can properly bear, endure, and withstand ordeals from divine, human, and animal sources — then, O Noble Ones, surely Nirgranth monks who are studying and mastering the twelve-fold canonical scriptures (Dvadashangi Ganapitaka) are capable of properly bearing, enduring, and withstanding ordeals from divine, human, and animal sources."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अज्जो** = O Noble Ones (address to monks and nuns, term of respect)
- **गिहिणो, गिहमज्झावसंता** = householders, living in the midst of household life
- **दिव्व-माणुस-तिरिक्खजोणिए उवसग्गे** = ordeals caused by divine, human, and animal beings
- **सम्मं सहंति, तितिक्खंति अहियासेंति** = properly bear, endure (titiksha = patient tolerance), withstand
- **दुवालसंगं गणिपिडगं** = the twelve-fold canonical scriptures (Dvadashangi — the twelve Angas of the Jain canon)
- **अहिज्जमाणेहिं** = who are studying/mastering these

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the chapter's explicit teaching moment — where the story becomes a lesson. Mahavira turns Kamadeva's private ordeal into instruction for the monastic community. The logic is a compelling a-fortiori argument: if a householder — who has not renounced the world, who lives with family, wealth, and worldly concerns — can endure such extreme ordeals with complete equanimity, then surely monks and nuns, who have renounced everything and who study the full Dvadashangi canon daily, should be capable of at least as much.

The structure is not a criticism of monks but an inspiration. Mahavira is effectively saying: "You have the teachings, you have the community, you have renounced the world's distractions — and Kamadeva has shown you what is possible even without all those supports. What, then, should be possible for you?"

The three categories of ordeal — divine (deva), human (manushya), and animal/sub-human (tiryach) — are the standard Jain typology for sources of external adversity. All three types have now been demonstrated in this chapter through the deity's three forms. The chapter has, in effect, provided a complete curriculum of how to face all categories of challenge.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Lord Mahavira turned to his monks and nuns and said: "If a householder like Kamadeva — living with family and worldly responsibilities — could endure all of that without wavering, then you who have given up the world and study the scriptures every day should certainly be able to do the same."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you see an ordinary person display extraordinary courage or steadfastness, does it inspire you or make you feel the gap between your ideals and your practice?

## SUTRA 2.29

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तओ ते बहवे समणा णिग्गंथा य णिग्गंथीओ य समणस्स भगवओ महावीरस्स तह ति एयमट्टु विणएणं पडिसुणेंति। ॥२.२९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tao te bahave samaṇā ṇiggaṃthā ya ṇiggaṃthīo ya samaṇassa bhagavao mahāvīrassa taha ti eya-maṭṭhu viṇaeṇaṃ paḍisuṇeṃti. || 2.29 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then the many monks and nuns of Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira accepted this teaching with the words "So it is, O Bhagavan!" and received it with reverence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **तह ति** = "so it is" — the canonical response of acceptance
- **एयमट्टु** = this meaning/teaching
- **विणएणं** = with humility, with reverence (vinaya)
- **पडिसुणेंति** = they hear and accept, they receive reverently

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monks' and nuns' response of "taha ti" (so it is) is the canonical formula of acceptance in Jain Agamic literature. It signals not just intellectual acknowledgment but volitional acceptance — a commitment to receive the teaching into practice. Vinaya (humility/reverence) is named as the quality with which the teaching is received, indicating that the response is not merely obedient but deeply appreciative.

The inclusion of both monks (nirgranth) and nuns (nirgranthi) in the acceptance is significant. The teaching applies equally to those of all genders within the renunciant community.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monks and nuns heard Mahavira's teaching and accepted it with deep reverence, saying "Yes, Bhagavan, so it is."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you receive a teaching that challenges you to be better — what is the quality of your inner acceptance? Is it genuine, or does part of you resist?

## SUTRA 2.30

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं कामदेवे समणोवासए हटु जाव समणं भगवं महावीरं पिसणाई पुच्छइ, अट्टुमादियइ। समणं भगवं महावीरं वंदइ णमंसइ, वंदित्ता णमंसित्ता जामेव दिसं पाउब्भूए, तामेव दिसं पिंडगाए। ॥२.३०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ kāmadeve samaṇovāsae haṭṭu jāva samaṇaṃ bhagavaṃ mahāvīraṃ pisaṇāiṃ pucchati, aṭṭumādiyai. samaṇaṃ bhagavaṃ mahāvīraṃ vaṃdai ṇaṃsai, vaṃdittā ṇaṃsittā jāmeva disaṃ pāubhūe, tāmeva disaṃ piṃḍagāe. || 2.30 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva was overjoyed (yāvat) and asked Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira various questions, receiving answers. He then bowed and paid reverence to Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, and having done so, departed in the direction from which he had come.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **हटु** = overjoyed, pleased (haṭṭa — elated)
- **जाव** = yāvat — the full description of Kamadeva's joy: "delighted, pleased, with hair standing on end, thrilled in every limb" — the canonical formula
- **पिसणाई पुच्छइ** = asked various questions
- **अट्टुमादियइ** = obtained resolution/answers
- **जामेव दिसं पाउब्भूए, तामेव दिसं पिंडगाए** = departed in the direction from which he had come (canonical formula for departure)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The overjoyed state (haṭṭa) of Kamadeva after the meeting with Mahavira stands in contrast to his composed silence during the ordeal. In the ordeal, he was still, unmoved, silent — the quality of dharmadhyana. After the meeting with the teacher, he is joyful — filled with the natural happiness of contact with genuine wisdom. These two states — inner stillness under pressure and natural joy in wisdom's presence — are not contradictory but complementary aspects of spiritual maturity.

The act of asking questions is itself honored here. Kamadeva does not simply receive the teaching passively — he engages with it, asks for clarification, seeks deeper understanding. The questions and answers are not spelled out (they are implied by the yāvat formula), but their occurrence is noted. A genuine student practices by asking.

The departure formula — returning in the direction from which one came — is a respectful acknowledgment that Kamadeva did not linger unnecessarily in the teacher's presence. He received what he needed, expressed his reverence, and went back to his life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Filled with joy, Kamadeva asked Lord Mahavira his questions and received answers. Then he bowed respectfully and went home.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

After a meaningful meeting with a teacher or mentor, do you ask the questions you really need to ask — or do you hold back out of diffidence?

## SUTRA 2.31

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं समणे भगवं महावीरे अण्णया कयाइं चंपाओ पडिणिक्खमइ, पडिणिक्खमित्ता बहिया जणवय-विहारं विहरइ। ॥२.३१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ samaṇe bhagavaṃ mahāvīre aṇṇayā kayāiṃ campāo paḍiṇikkhami, paḍiṇikkhami bahiyā jaṇavaya-vihāraṃ viharai. || 2.31 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then Shraman Bhagavan Mahavira, at some other time, departed from Champa city and began wandering through other regions (janpada-vihara).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **अण्णया कयाइं** = at some other time, on some occasion
- **चंपाओ पडिणिक्खमइ** = departed from Champa
- **जणवय-विहारं** = wandering through regions and communities (janpada-vihara — the itinerant teaching practice of a Jain ascetic)
- **विहरइ** = continued, traveled

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra marks the transition from the public teaching event to Kamadeva's private spiritual life over the coming years. Mahavira moves on — his role in this chapter's narrative is complete. The teacher who confirmed the ordeal, gave the teaching, and inspired the monastic community now continues his own itinerant mission.

The departure of the teacher is not an abandonment but a natural rhythm. In the Jain tradition, the teacher does not remain permanently with the student — the student is expected to internalize the teaching and practice independently. The next three sutras describe Kamadeva doing exactly this over twenty years: practicing progressively, steadily deepening, until the end of his life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

At some point, Lord Mahavira left Champa to continue his traveling mission to teach other communities.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a teacher, mentor, or guiding presence leaves your life — how do you carry what you received from them?

## SUTRA 2.32

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं कामदेवे समणोवासए पढमं उवासग-पडिमं उवसंपज्जित्ताणं विहरइ। जाव एक्कारसमं उवासगपडिमं सम्मं आराहेइ, एवं जहा आणंदे जाव भत्त-पाण-पडियाइंखाए कालं अणवकंखमाणे विहरइ। ॥२.३२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ kāmadeve samaṇovāsae paḍhamaṃ uvāsaga-paḍimaṃ uvasaṃpajjittāṇaṃ viharai. jāva ekkārasamaṃ uvāsagapaḍimaṃ sammaṃ ārāhei, evaṃ jahā āṇaṃde jāva bhatta-pāṇa-paḍiyāiṃkhāe kālaṃ aṇavakhaṃkhamāṇe viharai. || 2.32 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva took up the first stage (pratima) of the lay follower's path and dwelt in it. (Yāvat) He properly fulfilled all eleven stages, just as Ananda had done (yāvat), and remained, having renounced food and water (bhatta-pana-pratakhyana), awaiting his end without anxiety.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उवासग-पडिमं** = pratima — the progressive stages of lay practice in Jain tradition (eleven in total)
- **उवसंपज्जित्ताणं** = having taken up, having undertaken
- **एक्कारसमं** = the eleventh (final stage)
- **सम्मं आराहेइ** = properly practiced/fulfilled
- **जहा आणंदे** = just as Ananda had done (cross-reference to Chapter 1)
- **भत्त-पाण-पडियाइंखाए** = having renounced food and water (the final fast preceding liberation from the body)
- **कालं अणवकंखमाणे** = awaiting the end without anxiety, without wishing for death

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eleven pratimas of the Jain shravaka (lay follower) path represent a progressive movement from ordinary householder life toward something approaching monastic renunciation — without formally taking the monastic vows. They include: the Darshana pratima (establishing right faith), Vrata pratima (taking vows), Samayika pratima (regular equanimity practice), Proshadha pratima (full poshadha observance), Sacchita-tyaga pratima (giving up raw food), Ratri-bhukta-tyaga pratima (giving up eating at night), Brahmacharya pratima (celibacy), Arambha-tyaga pratima (giving up business), Parigraha-tyaga pratima (giving up possessions), Anumati-tyaga pratima (giving up involvement in worldly decisions), and the final Uddishtha-tyaga pratima (completely severing attachment to the household and entering near-monastic practice).

Kamadeva proceeds through all eleven — a complete spiritual journey from householder vows to the threshold of full renunciation. The final stage culminates in bhatta-pana-pratyakhyana — the voluntary renunciation of food and water, which in the Jain tradition is the practice of santhara (or sallekhana) — a prepared, meditative, non-violent departure from life. This is not suicide but the culmination of a lifetime of practice: the practitioner, no longer able to serve their spiritual practice through the body, consciously lets go of bodily sustenance while maintaining equanimity of mind.

The phrase "awaiting the end without anxiety" (kālaṃ aṇavakhāṃkhamāṇe) is the canonical formula. It captures the ideal: not rushing toward death, not fleeing from it, simply remaining present and at peace as the body's processes naturally slow and cease.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After Mahavira's visit, Kamadeva committed fully to his spiritual practice — progressively taking up all eleven stages of the lay path, just as Ananda had done before him — and at the end, he fasted and waited peacefully for death.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A spiritual life is not built in a single dramatic moment but in years of steady, progressive practice. What does steady commitment to daily practice look like in your own life?

## SUTRA 2.33

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तए णं से कामदेवे समणोवासए बहुइं सीलव्वय-गुण-वेरमण-पच्चक्खाण-पोसहवासेहिं अप्पाणं भावेत्ता वीसं वासाइं समणोवासगपरियायं पाउणित्ता, एक्कारसमं उवासगपडिमाओ सम्मं लेहणाए अप्पाणं झुसित्ता, भत्ताइं अणसणाए छेदेत्ता, आलोइयपडिक्कंते, समाहिपत्ते, कालमासे काल किच्चा, सोहम्मं कप्पे सोहम्मावडिसए महाविमानस्स उत्तरपुरित्थमेणं अरुणाभे विमाणे देवत्ताए उववण्णे। तत्थ णं अत्थेगइयाणं देवाणं चत्तारि पलिओवमाई ठिई पण्णत्ता। कामदेवस्स वि देवस्स चत्तारि पलिओवमाई ठिई पण्णत्ता। ॥२.३३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tae ṇaṃ se kāmadeve samaṇovāsae bahuiṃ sīlvvaya-guṇa-veramaṇa-paccakkhāṇa-posahavāsehiṃ appāṇaṃ bhāvettā vīsaṃ vāsāiṃ samaṇovāsaga-pariyāyaṃ pāuṇittā, ekkārasamaṃ uvāsaga-paḍimāo sammaṃ lehṇāe appāṇaṃ jhusittā, bhattāiṃ aṇasaṇāe chedettā, āloiya-paḍikkaṃte, samāhi-patte, kālamāse kāla kiccā, sohammaṃ kappe sohamm-āvaḍisae mahā-vimānassa uttara-puritthameṇaṃ aruṇābhe vimāṇe devattāe uvavaṇṇe. tattha ṇaṃ atthegaiyāṇaṃ devāṇaṃ cattāri paliomāī ṭhiī paṇṇattā. kāmadevassa vi devassa cattāri paliomāī ṭhiī paṇṇattā. || 2.33 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then shramanopasak Kamadeva, having purified his soul through many vows, virtues, renunciations, abstinences, and poshadha-fasts, completed twenty years of the shramanopasak's path. He properly fulfilled and completed the eleventh pratima in its full form. He terminated food through fasting (anapana), performed confession and repentance (alochana-pratikramana), attained equanimity (samadhi), and at the proper time, departed from this life. He was reborn as a deity in Saudharma heaven (Saudharma Kalpa), in the Saudharma-vatamsaka (crown jewel) Mahavimana, in the northeast quarter, in the celestial vehicle called Arunabha. The lifespan assigned to some deities there is four palya. The lifespan of the deity Kamadeva is also four palya.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सीलव्वय-गुण-वेरमण-पच्चक्खाण-पोसहवासेहिं** = through vows (shila-vrata), virtues (guna), renunciations (virama), abstinences (pratakhyana), and poshadha-fasts
- **अप्पाणं भावेत्ता** = having refined/purified the soul
- **वीसं वासाइं** = twenty years
- **समणोवासगपरियायं** = the shramanopasak period/path
- **लेहणाए अप्पाणं झुसित्ता** = completing/purifying the self in its entirety
- **भत्ताइं अणसणाए छेदेत्ता** = having terminated food through fasting (anapana — a specific type of santhara)
- **आलोइयपडिक्कंते** = having performed alochana (confession) and pratikramana (repentance)
- **समाहिपत्ते** = having attained samadhi (meditative equanimity at the time of death)
- **कालमासे काल किच्चा** = at the proper time, having performed the final act (death)
- **सोहम्मं कप्पे** = in Saudharma heaven (the first of the twelve heavens in Jain cosmology)
- **सोहम्मावडिसए** = Saudharma-vatamsaka — the "crown jewel" or chief vimana of Saudharma heaven
- **अरुणाभे विमाणे** = in the celestial vehicle named Arunabha (meaning: red-dawn-luminous)
- **देवत्ताए उववण्णे** = reborn as a deity
- **चत्तारि पलिओवमाई** = four palya (a palya is an inconceivably long unit of time in Jain cosmology; four palya is the lifespan in this heavenly level)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra provides the complete arc of Kamadeva's spiritual practice life in one dense passage. Twenty years of steady, progressive shramanopasak practice. Completion of all eleven pratimas. And finally, the conscious, meditative departure from life — through voluntary fasting, confession, repentance, and the attainment of samadhi at death.

The four-step sequence at death — anapana (termination of food), alochana-pratikramana (confession/repentance for any remaining faults), samadhi (meditative equanimity), and kalakriya (natural death with awareness) — is the Jain ideal for a practitioner's death. It is not passive — it is the most intensive spiritual act of a lay practitioner's life, requiring extraordinary equanimity, clarity, and resolve.

The destination — Saudharma heaven, in the Arunabha vimana in the northeast corner of the Saudharma-vatamsaka Mahavimana — is specific in Jain cosmology. Saudharma is the first and most meritorious of the twelve heavens. The northeast corner (uttara-puristthama) is specifically associated with high merit. The Arunabha vimana's name means "possessing the radiance of the rising sun" — beautiful and apt for a soul that has shone with such steadfast spiritual light.

Four palya is the assigned lifespan of the deity Kamadeva in this heavenly realm — an extraordinarily long celestial life that is, in Jain teaching, the natural consequence of such an advanced level of lay practice and such resolute endurance of the upsargas.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After twenty years of deep spiritual practice — taking all eleven stages of the lay path, fasting, confessing, and dying in perfect meditative peace — Kamadeva was reborn as a radiant deity in Saudharma heaven, the highest level of the first heaven, with a lifespan of four palya.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to approach your own death the way Kamadeva did — as the final and most important act of a life spent practicing?

## SUTRA 2.34

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

से णं भंते ! कामदेवे ताओ देवलोगाओ आउक्खएणं ठिइक्खएणं अंजरं चयं चत्ता, कहिं गमिहिइ, कहिं उवज्जिहिइ ? गोयमा ! महाविदेहे वासे सिज्झिहिइ। णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स। ॥२.३४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Se ṇaṃ bhaṃte! kāmadeve tāo devalogāo āukkhaeṇaṃ ṭhiikkhaeṇaṃ aṃjaraṃ cayaṃ cattā, kahiṃ gamihiī, kahiṃ uvajjihiī? Goyamā! mahāvideha vāse sijjhihiī. Nikkhevo jahā paḍhamassa. || 2.34 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O Venerable One! When Kamadeva, having spent his lifespan and exhausted his divine existence, departs from that celestial realm — where will he go? Where will he be born?" Bhagavan replied: "O Gautama! He will attain liberation in Mahavideha." The closing verse (nikshepa) is the same as that of the first chapter.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **से णं भंते** = O Venerable One (Gautama asking Mahavira)
- **ताओ देवलोगाओ** = from that heavenly realm
- **आउक्खएणं ठिइक्खएणं** = when the lifespan is exhausted and the allotted time is exhausted
- **अंजरं चयं चत्ता** = having left/abandoned the non-deteriorating divine body
- **कहिं गमिहिइ** = where will he go?
- **कहिं उवज्जिहिइ** = where will he be reborn?
- **गोयमा** = O Gautama (Bhagavan addressing his chief disciple Gautama Swami)
- **महाविदेहे वासे** = in the Mahavideha realm (one of the five continents of Jambudvipa where liberated souls can be born and attain liberation)
- **सिज्झिहिइ** = will attain liberation (siddha — will become a perfected, liberated soul)
- **णिक्खेवो जहा पढमस्स** = the nikshepa (closing verse) is the same as in the first chapter

### 5. COMMENTARY

This final sutra brings the chapter to its complete conclusion with the ultimate Jain destination: liberation (siddhi/moksha). The trajectory of Kamadeva's soul is now fully described — from householder in Champa, through the ordeal and twenty years of practice, to a deity in Saudharma heaven, and ultimately to liberation in Mahavideha.

Mahavideha is one of the five continental regions of Jambudvipa in Jain cosmology. Unlike Bharatakshetra (our world), where liberation can only occur during certain cosmic time periods, Mahavideha always has a Tirthankara actively teaching — meaning liberation is always possible there. Kamadeva's future birth in Mahavideha and subsequent liberation is thus a statement of absolute certainty: his spiritual trajectory, once set in motion by his steadfast practice and confirmed by the ordeal, is now irreversible.

Gautama's question about Kamadeva's future is a standard element of the Upasakdashang's closing formula — each chapter ends with Gautama inquiring about the ultimate destiny of the lay follower. Mahavira's answer — liberation — is both a factual statement (from the Jain perspective of the omniscient teacher knowing future births) and a teaching: this is where genuine lay practice leads. Not just to better rebirths or heavenly pleasures, but ultimately to the final release from all rebirth.

The reference to "nikshepa (closing verse) same as the first chapter" uses the yāvat formula to invoke the full formal closing that was spelled out at the end of Chapter 1: the technical terms ukkhepa (opening) and nikshepa (closing) that frame each chapter as a complete unit within the larger Anga.

The chapter closes as it opened — with a formula that situates it within a larger transmission. What was asked at the beginning (what is the meaning of the second chapter?) has been fully answered: the meaning is the story of a soul who, through steadfast faith in the face of everything that can be thrown at it, charted a course from the deepest worldly prosperity to the threshold of liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Gautama asked Lord Mahavira: "After Kamadeva's heavenly life ends, what happens to him?" Mahavira replied: "He will be born in Mahavideha and attain complete liberation from all rebirth."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Every choice to hold your ground in dharma, every moment of genuine stillness in the face of difficulty — does it accumulate? Does it matter? Where do you believe your own spiritual life is ultimately headed?
