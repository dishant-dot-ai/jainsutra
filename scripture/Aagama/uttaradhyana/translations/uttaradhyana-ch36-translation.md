## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

Chapter 36 is the Classification of Living and Non-Living — the most philosophically detailed chapter in the Uttaradhyayana. It presents the complete Jain map of reality: the six fundamental substances, their divisions and sub-divisions, and their characteristics in terms of substance, region, time, and mode. The chapter includes classification trees and measurement charts showing how the entire universe breaks down into its parts — so the practitioner can understand clearly what is soul and what is not. This is the essential knowledge for liberation.

## SUTRA 36.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जीवाजीविभभत्ति, सुणेह मे एगमणा इओ ।
जं जाणिऊण भिक्खू, सम्मं जयइ संजमे ॥३६.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

jīvājīvibhabhatti, suṇeha me egamaṇā io |
jaṃ jāṇiūṇa bhikkhū, sammaṃ jayai saṃjame || 36.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Listen to the jīvājīva-vibhakti from me with a focused, one-pointed mind — knowing which, a monk strives correctly in restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jīvājīvibhabhatti** = jīvājīva-vibhakti — the classification/distinction of the living (jīva) and the non-living (ajīva)
- **suṇeha** = listen, hear
- **me** = from me
- **egamaṇā** = with one-pointed mind, with concentrated attention (eka-mana)
- **io** = from here, from now on
- **jaṃ** = which (knowledge)
- **jāṇiūṇa** = having known, upon knowing
- **bhikkhū** = a monk, a mendicant
- **sammaṃ** = correctly, rightly (samyak)
- **jayai** = strives, is vigilant, practises with care
- **saṃjame** = in restraint (saṃyama)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The opening sutra establishes the purpose of the entire chapter: knowledge of jīva and ajīva is the foundation of correct restraint (samyak saṃyama). The word **egamaṇā** — one-pointed mind — is the first instruction: this is not casual listening. The classification that follows is the Jain science of reality — precise, exhaustive, and directly connected to liberation. A monk who clearly understands what is jīva (soul) and what is ajīva (matter, space, time, etc.) knows exactly what to protect (the jīva) and what to refrain from harming (all jīvas in ajīva forms). This knowledge is samyag-jñāna — right knowledge — one of the three jewels.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Listen with full concentration to the classification of living and non-living — knowing this, a monk strives correctly in restraint.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I know clearly what is "me" (jīva) and what is "not me" (ajīva)? How would that clarity change how I live?

## SUTRA 36.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जीवा चेव अजीवा य, एस लोए वियाहिए ।
अजीव देसमागासे, अलोए से वियाहिए ॥३६.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

jīvā ceva ajīvā ya, esa loe viyāhie |
ajīva desamāgāse, aloe se viyāhie || 36.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

This universe (loka) is said to be composed of jīvas and ajīvas. That which is only one division (region) of ajīva — pure ākāśa without any other substance — is called aloka (non-universe).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jīvā ceva ajīvā ya** = jīvas (souls) and ajīvas (non-souls/matter, etc.)
- **esa** = this
- **loe** = in the loka (universe, the inhabited cosmos)
- **viyāhie** = is said to be, is defined as (vyākhyāta)
- **ajīva** = ajīva substance alone
- **desamāgāse** = one region/part (desa) of ākāśa (space)
- **aloe** = aloka (non-universe — the infinite space beyond the cosmos)
- **se viyāhie** = that is called, that is defined

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Jain cosmology divides all of existence into loka (the inhabited universe) and aloka (the infinite space beyond). The loka contains all six dravyas: jīva (souls), dharma, adharma, ākāśa, kāla, and pudgala. The aloka contains only one dravya: pure ākāśa (space) with nothing else in it — no souls, no matter, no time as an operative substance. This precise ontological division is fundamental to Jain physics. The universe is not infinite in all its fullness — beyond a certain boundary, there is only pure, empty space. Liberation means the jīva rises to the very top of the loka and rests there in siddha-śilā.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The universe (loka) consists of jīvas and ajīvas. Beyond the universe, only pure space exists — that is the aloka.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If the universe has a boundary — what lies beyond? And where does liberation take a soul?

## SUTRA 36.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दव्वओ खेत्तओ चेव, कालओ भावओ तहा ।
परूवणा तेसिं भवे, जीवाणमजीवाण य ॥३६.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

davvao khettao ceva, kālao bhāvao tahā |
parūvaṇā tesiṃ bhave, jīvāṇamajīvāṇa ya || 36.3 ||

### 4. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The description of those jīvas and ajīvas will be given in four ways: from dravya (substance), kṣetra (region/space), kāla (time), and bhāva (mode/quality).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **davvao** = from the perspective of dravya (substance — what it is in itself)
- **khettao** = from the perspective of kṣetra (space — how much space it occupies)
- **kālao** = from the perspective of kāla (time — how long it exists)
- **bhāvao** = from the perspective of bhāva (mode — in what modes/states it exists)
- **parūvaṇā** = description, explanation, analysis (prarūpaṇā)
- **tesiṃ** = of those (jīva and ajīva)
- **bhave** = will be, shall be
- **jīvāṇam** = of jīvas
- **ajīvāṇa ya** = and of ajīvas

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the methodological statement of the entire chapter. The four-perspective analysis — dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva — is the standard Jain analytical framework (called the caturaṅga or four-angled analysis). Each substance will be examined from all four perspectives:
- **Dravya**: its intrinsic nature (what kind and how many units)
- **Kṣetra**: the space it occupies or pervades
- **Kāla**: its temporal existence (how long it exists, when it is anādi/ananta)
- **Bhāva**: its qualitative modes (rūpī, arūpī, saparyāvasita, etc.)

This ensures a complete and non-redundant analysis of every substance in reality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The description of jīvas and ajīvas will be given in four ways: by substance, by space, by time, and by mode/quality.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I try to understand something deeply, do I examine it from multiple perspectives — or do I stop at the first angle I encounter?

## SUTRA 36.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रूविणो चेव अरूवी य, अजीवा दुविहा भवे ।
अरूवी दसहा वुत्ता, रूविणो उ चउव्विहा ॥३६.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

rūviṇo ceva arūvī ya, ajīvā duvihā bhave |
arūvī dasahā vuttā, rūviṇo u cauvvihā || 36.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Ajīvas are of two kinds: rūpī (with form) and arūpī (without form). The arūpī are said to be of ten kinds, and the rūpī are of four kinds.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **rūviṇo** = rūpī — those with form (perceivable qualities of colour, smell, taste, touch)
- **arūvī** = arūpī — those without form (no colour, smell, taste, touch)
- **ajīvā** = non-living substances
- **duvihā** = of two kinds
- **bhave** = are
- **arūvī** = the formless ajīvas
- **dasahā** = of ten kinds
- **vuttā** = are said to be, are described
- **rūviṇo** = the formed ajīvas
- **cauvvihā** = of four kinds

### 5. COMMENTARY

The primary division of ajīva: with form (rūpī) and without form (arūpī). This maps to the fundamental difference between pudgala (matter) — which has the four qualities of colour, smell, taste, and touch — and the other four ajīva substances (dharma, adharma, ākāśa, kāla) which have no such sensory qualities. The arūpī ten kinds arise because each of the three non-pudgala ajīvas (dharma, adharma, ākāśa) has three sub-divisions (skandha/whole, desa/part, pradeśa/point-unit) plus kāla — giving 3+3+3+1=10. The rūpī four kinds are: skandha, desa, pradeśa, and paramāṇu.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Ajīva substances are of two kinds: those with form and those without form. The formless are ten in type; those with form are four in type.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in my world is truly "formless" — beyond colour, smell, taste, and touch? Can I sense what is formless?

## SUTRAS 36.5–6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धम्मत्थिकाए तद्देसे, तप्पएसे य आहिए ।
अहम्मे तस्स देसे य, तप्पएसे य आहिए ॥३६.५॥

आगासे तस्स देसे य, तप्पएसे य आहिए ।
अद्धासमए चेव, अरूवी दसहा भवे ॥३६.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

dhammatthikāe tadese, tappaeese ya āhie |
ahamme tassa dese ya, tappaeese ya āhie || 36.5 ||

āgāse tassa dese ya, tappaeese ya āhie |
addhāsamae ceva, arūvī dasahā bhave || 36.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(5) The dharmāstikāya, its desa (partial region), and its pradeśa (point-unit) are named. The adharmāstikāya, its desa, and its pradeśa are named.
(6) The ākāśāstikāya, its desa, and its pradeśa are named. And the addhā-samaya (time-unit, kāla) — thus the arūpī ajīvas are said to be ten.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **dhammatthikāe** = dharmāstikāya — the medium of motion; the substance that assists movement
- **tadese** = its desa (a portion or region of it)
- **tappaeese** = its pradeśa (the ultimate point-unit)
- **āhie** = are named, are described
- **ahamme** = adharmāstikāya — the medium of rest; the substance that assists remaining still
- **āgāse** = ākāśāstikāya — space; that which provides room for all substances
- **addhāsamae** = addhā-samaya — time (kāla); the unit of time
- **arūvī dasahā bhave** = the formless (arūpī) ajīvas are thus ten in kind

### 5. COMMENTARY

The ten arūpī ajīvas enumerated:
1. Dharmāstikāya (the medium of motion — as a whole)
2. Dharmāstikāya-desa (a portion of it)
3. Dharmāstikāya-pradeśa (a point-unit of it)
4. Adharmāstikāya (the medium of rest — as a whole)
5. Adharmāstikāya-desa (a portion of it)
6. Adharmāstikāya-pradeśa (a point-unit of it)
7. Ākāśāstikāya (space — as a whole)
8. Ākāśāstikāya-desa (a portion of space)
9. Ākāśāstikāya-pradeśa (a point-unit of space)
10. Addhā-samaya = kāla (time) — time has no skandha/desa/pradeśa in the same way, as it is a continuous flow rather than an extended substance; hence only one subdivision

**Dharmāstikāya** is not "dharma" in the ethical sense — it is the medium that makes motion possible, like water makes fish swim. Without it, jīvas and pudgalas could not move.
**Adharmāstikāya** is the medium that makes rest possible, like earth makes a tired traveller stop and sit.
**Ākāśāstikāya** is the all-pervading space that provides room (avakāśa) for all substances.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The ten formless (arūpī) ajīvas: the medium of motion (dharma-astikāya) and its part and point; the medium of rest (adharma-astikāya) and its part and point; space (ākāśa-astikāya) and its part and point; and time (kāla). These make ten.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the "medium of rest" in my own life — what allows me to be still? And what is the "medium of motion" — what makes it possible for me to move?

## SUTRA 36.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धम्माधम्मे य दो चेव, लोगमित्ता वियाहिया ।
लोगालोगे य आगासे, समये समयखेत्तिए ॥३६.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

dhammādhamme ya do ceva, logamittā viyāhiyā |
logāloge ya āgāse, samaye samayakhettie || 36.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Dharmāstikāya and adharmāstikāya — these two are said to be loka-mātra (of the measure of the universe). Ākāśāstikāya spans both loka and aloka. Time (samaya) is contained within the samaya-kṣetra (the field of time — the universe).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **dhammādhamme** = dharmāstikāya and adharmāstikāya
- **ya do ceva** = those two indeed
- **logamittā** = loka-mātra — exactly the size of the universe (not larger, not smaller)
- **viyāhiyā** = are said to be
- **logāloge ya** = in both loka and aloka
- **āgāse** = ākāśāstikāya (space) — spans both
- **samaye** = samaya (time, kāla)
- **samayakhettie** = within the samaya-kṣetra (the space-field of time, which is the universe itself)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra describes the spatial extent of the four formless ajīvas:
- **Dharma and adharma**: exactly coextensive with the loka — they span the entire universe but no further. This is logical: their function (enabling motion/rest) is needed only where jīvas and pudgalas exist, which is within the loka.
- **Ākāśa (space)**: spans both loka and aloka — space is truly infinite, extending infinitely beyond the universe in all directions.
- **Kāla (time)**: exists only in the universe (samaya-kṣetra). This is the Jain understanding that time as an operational substance exists only where matter and souls change — in the aloka, there is no change, hence no operative time.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The media of motion and rest are exactly the size of the universe. Space spans both the universe and beyond. Time operates only within the universe.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to exist "beyond time"? The siddhas, liberated at the top of the universe, rest in a time-free existence — what is that?

## SUTRA 36.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

धम्माधम्मागासा, तिण्णि वि एए अणाइया ।
अपज्जविसया चेव, सव्वद्धं तु वियाहिया ॥३६.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

dhammādhamaāgāsā, tiṇṇi vi ee aṇāiyā |
apajjavisayā ceva, savvaddhaṃ tu viyāhiyā || 36.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Dharmāstikāya, adharmāstikāya, and ākāśāstikāya — these three are said by the Tīrthaṅkaras to be beginningless (anādi), without end (aparyavasita), and persisting throughout all time (sarvaddhā).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **dhammādhamaāgāsā** = dharmāstikāya, adharmāstikāya, and ākāśāstikāya
- **tiṇṇi vi ee** = all three of these
- **aṇāiyā** = anādi — beginningless (without a beginning in time)
- **apajjavisayā** = aparyavasita — without end, inexhaustible, infinite in extent
- **ceva** = indeed
- **savvaddhaṃ** = throughout all time (sarva-addhā)
- **viyāhiyā** = are said to be

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three of the four arūpī ajīva substances are eternal in the fullest sense: **anādi** (no beginning) and **aparyavasita** (no end). They have always existed and will always exist. This is fundamental to the Jain non-creationist ontology: the universe was not created, and the fundamental substances of which it is made are eternal. They were not produced by any God or first cause. The implication: liberation is possible because the dharma-substance (medium of motion) will always be there to allow the soul's upward movement; the ākāśa will always provide room for the liberated soul to rest in siddha-śilā.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Dharmāstikāya, adharmāstikāya, and ākāśāstikāya are all beginningless, endless, and exist throughout all time — they are eternal.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to live in a universe that was never created and will never be destroyed — only transformed?

## SUTRA 36.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समए वि संतइं पप्प, एवमेव वियाहिए ।
आएसं पप्प साईए, सपज्जविसिए वि य ॥३६.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

samae vi saṃtaiṃ pappa, evameva viyāhie |
āesaṃ pappa sāīe, sapajjavisie vi ya || 36.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Time (samaya-kāla) also, from the perspective of its flow (santati/stream), is similarly said to be beginningless and endless. But from the perspective of a specific instance (ādeśa), it is said to have a beginning (sādi) and an end (saparyavasita).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **samae vi** = time (samaya) also
- **saṃtaiṃ pappa** = from the perspective of the stream/flow (santati — continuity of time)
- **evameva** = in this same way
- **viyāhie** = is said to be (anādi and ananta)
- **āesaṃ pappa** = from the perspective of a specific instance (ādeśa — a particular moment or point)
- **sāīe** = sādi — with a beginning
- **sapajjavisie vi ya** = also saparyavasita — with an end

### 5. COMMENTARY

Time has a unique status in Jain ontology. As a **stream** (santati), time is beginningless and endless — moments of time have been flowing forever and will flow forever. But any **specific moment** (ādeśa — a particular instant, like "the moment a pot was made") has a beginning (when it starts) and an end (when it is over). This is the Jain resolution of the paradox: time itself is eternal, but every event within time has a start and end. This also explains why kāla is not grouped with the other three substances as unconditionally aparyavasita — because individual kāla-units (samaya) do have boundaries.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

As a stream, time is beginningless and endless. But each specific moment of time has a beginning and an end.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The moment you are in right now — it has a beginning and an end. What will you do with it before it ends?

## SUTRA 36.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खंधा य खंधदेसा य, तप्पएसा तहेव य ।
परमाणुणो य बोद्धव्वा, रूविणो य चउव्विहा ॥३६.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

khaṃdhā ya khaṃdhadesā ya, tappaeesā taheva ya |
paramāṇuṇo ya boddavvā, rūviṇo ya cauvvihā || 36.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The rūpī (formed/material) ajīvas are to be known as four: skandha (aggregate), skandha-desa (portion of an aggregate), skandha-pradeśa (point-unit of an aggregate), and paramāṇu (atom/ultimate particle).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **khaṃdhā** = skandha — an aggregate of paramāṇus (any material combination from 2 atoms upward)
- **khaṃdhadesā** = skandha-desa — a notional portion/section of a skandha
- **tappaeesā** = the pradeśa of that skandha — the ultimate indivisible space-point of a skandha
- **taheva ya** = and similarly, in the same way
- **paramāṇuṇo** = paramāṇu — the ultimate indivisible particle, the atom
- **boddavvā** = are to be known, should be understood
- **rūviṇo** = the formed (rūpī) ajīvas
- **cauvvihā** = of four kinds

### 5. COMMENTARY

The four types of rūpī (material/formed) ajīva — all belonging to the one substance pudgalāstikāya:
1. **Skandha**: any aggregate of two or more paramāṇus (from the smallest visible speck to the entire cosmos of matter)
2. **Skandha-desa**: a notional/conceptual division of a skandha (like saying "the upper half of this stone")
3. **Skandha-pradeśa**: the most refined analytical sub-division, the space-point unit associated with a skandha
4. **Paramāṇu**: the indivisible ultimate particle — it has colour, smell, taste, and one type of touch; it occupies exactly one space-point

The paramāṇu is the fundamental building block of all matter. It cannot be cut, cannot be divided, has no parts. All material phenomena — bodies, karma-matter, food, breath — are various forms of skandha (aggregates of paramāṇus).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The four types of formed (material) non-living substance are: aggregates of matter (skandha), portions of aggregates, point-units of aggregates, and the ultimate indivisible particle (paramāṇu).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If all matter is ultimately made of indivisible point-particles — what does that say about the nature of the "solid" world we experience?

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एगत्तेण पुहुत्तेण, खंधा य परमाणु य ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

egattena puhuttena, khaṃdhā ya paramāṇu ya |
loegadese loe ya, bhaiyavvā te u khettao || 36.11 ||

suhuma savva logammi, logadese ya bāyarā |
itto kālavibhāgaṃ tu, tesiṃ vuccha cauvvihaṃ || 36.12 ||

(11) Skandhas and paramāṇus — coming together (egatta) and separating (puhutta) — are found in a single region of the universe or throughout the entire universe. This is their description from the perspective of kṣetra (space).
(12) The subtle ones (suhuma/sūkṣma) pervade the entire universe; the gross ones (bāyara/bādara) exist in a specific region of the universe. From here I will describe their fourfold classification by time (kāla).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **egattena** = through coming together (eka-tva — union, combination)
- **puhuttena** = through separating (pṛthak-tva — division, separation)
- **loegadese** = in one region/section of the universe (loka-eka-desa)
- **loe ya** = and in the entire universe (loka — all of it)
- **bhaiyavvā** = are found, can be present (bhajyante — to be distributed)

- **suhuma** = subtle (sūkṣma) skandhas and paramāṇus

- **kālavibhāgaṃ** = the classification by kāla (time)

The spatial distribution of material substances:
- **Skandhas** are formed by union (egatta) of paramāṇus and can be dissolved (puhutta) back. They range in size from the smallest 2-atom combination to massive aggregates.
- **Sūkṣma (subtle) paramāṇus and skandhas** pervade the entire universe — they are present everywhere in the loka. The karma-body of a jīva is an example of subtle matter that pervades all space.

The next verse will describe the fourfold classification by time: anādi, sānta, sādi, saparyavasita.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Material skandhas and atoms, combining and separating, pervade either a region or the whole universe. Subtle ones are everywhere in the universe; gross ones occupy specific locations.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The karma attached to your soul is made of subtle matter that pervades all space. What does it mean to "purify" something so subtle and pervasive?

## SUTRA 36.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संतइं पप्प तेणाई, अपज्जविसया वि य ।
ठिइं पडुच्च साइया, सपज्जविसया वि य ॥३६.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

saṃtaiṃ pappa teṇāī, apajjavisayā vi ya |
ṭhiiṃ paḍucca sāiyā, sapajjavisayā vi ya || 36.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

From the perspective of their flow/stream (santati), skandhas and paramāṇus are beginningless (anādi) and without end (aparyavasita). From the perspective of their state/condition (sthiti), they are with a beginning (sādi) and with an end (saparyavasita).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **saṃtaiṃ pappa** = from the perspective of the stream/flow (santati — continuity)
- **teṇāī** = those skandhas and paramāṇus are anādi (beginningless)
- **apajjavisayā vi ya** = and also aparyavasita (without end)
- **ṭhiiṃ paḍucca** = from the perspective of sthiti (condition, state, resting-position)
- **sāiyā** = sādi — with a beginning
- **sapajjavisayā vi ya** = and saparyavasita — with an end

### 5. COMMENTARY

Parallel to the analysis of kāla (time) in sutra 36.9, material substances also have a dual nature regarding time:
- As a **stream** (santati): the flow of paramāṇus and skandhas through their transformations has been going on forever and will continue forever — the matter of the universe is anādi-ananta.
- As a **condition/state** (sthiti): any specific state a skandha is in (e.g., "being a rock," "being a karma-particle attached to this soul") has a beginning and an end.

This is why karma can be removed: the karma-state of a jīva (the specific condition of having karma attached) is sādi-saparyavasita — it has a beginning (when the karma was attracted) and it can be ended (through practice). The underlying matter continues, but no longer as karma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

As a stream, material substances are beginningless and endless. But each specific state or condition of matter has a beginning and an end.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Karma has a beginning (when it was created) and an end (when it is purified). If your karma can end — why wait?

## SUTRA 36.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असंखकालमुक्कोसं, इक्कं समयं जहण्णयं ।
अजीवाण य रूवीणं, ठिइं एसा वियाहिया ॥३६.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

asaṃkhakālamukkosaṃ, ikkaṃ samayaṃ jahaṇṇayaṃ |
ajīvāṇa ya rūvīṇaṃ, ṭhiiṃ esā viyāhiyā || 36.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For rūpī ajīvas (material substances), the maximum duration of any given state is asaṃkhyāta-kāla (innumerable time units), and the minimum duration is one samaya (one indivisible unit of time). This is said to be their sthiti (state-duration).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **asaṃkhakālam** = asaṃkhyāta-kāla — innumerable units of time (beyond calculation but finite)
- **ukkosaṃ** = maximum, at the most
- **ikkaṃ samayaṃ** = one samaya (one indivisible instant of time)
- **jahaṇṇayaṃ** = minimum, at the least
- **ajīvāṇa ya rūvīṇaṃ** = of rūpī ajīvas (material, formed non-living substances)
- **ṭhiiṃ** = sthiti — the duration of a state/condition
- **esā viyāhiyā** = this is said to be

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra establishes the temporal range of any specific state of material substance:
- **Minimum**: one samaya — the smallest possible unit of time in Jain philosophy, indivisible, the "atom" of time. Any state of a material substance lasts at least one moment.
- **Maximum**: asaṃkhyāta-kāla — an innumerable but finite number of time units. Note: it is not infinite (ananta) — any specific material state, however long-lasting, eventually changes.

This is philosophically significant for karma: karma can last for an extremely long time (asaṃkhyāta-kāla) — but it is not eternal. All karma, however old, will eventually be shed either through practice or through natural fruition. There is no karma so deeply embedded that it can never be removed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The maximum duration of any state of material substance is innumerable (but finite) time units; the minimum is one indivisible instant. No material state lasts forever.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even the deepest, oldest karma has a maximum lifespan — it is not permanent. How does that knowledge change your relationship to your own patterns?

## SUTRA 36.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणंतकालमुक्कोसं, इक्कं समयं जहण्णयं ।
अजीवाण य रूवीणं, अंतरेयं वियाहियं ॥३६.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

aṇaṃtakālamukkosaṃ, ikkaṃ samayaṃ jahaṇṇayaṃ |
ajīvāṇa ya rūvīṇaṃ, aṃtareyaṃ viyāhiyaṃ || 36.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For rūpī ajīvas, the maximum antarā (interval between returning to the same state) is anantakāla (infinite time), and the minimum is one samaya. This is said to be their antarā.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇaṃtakālam** = anantakāla — infinite time (truly without end)
- **ukkosaṃ** = maximum
- **ikkaṃ samayaṃ** = one samaya
- **jahaṇṇayaṃ** = minimum
- **ajīvāṇa ya rūvīṇaṃ** = of rūpī ajīvas
- **aṃtareyaṃ** = antarā — the interval (the gap between two occurrences of the same state)
- **viyāhiyaṃ** = is said to be

**Antarā** is the interval between two occurrences of the same state. For a skandha: if it is in a certain configuration, leaves that configuration, and returns to an identical configuration — the time in between is the antarā. This has a practical implication: a human body with capacity for liberation is a rare configuration that, once lost, may not recur for an infinite time.

The minimum interval between two occurrences of the same material state is one instant; the maximum is infinite time. Some configurations of matter, once lost, may never recur.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The human birth capable of liberation — if this configuration is lost, it may not recur for infinite time. What does that urgency call you to do?

## SUTRAS 36.20–21

iya phāsapariṇayā ee, puggalā samudāhiyā || 36.21 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(20) Those pudgalas that have undergone transformation in touch are said to be of eight kinds: rough (kakkhada), soft (mṛdu), heavy (guru), light (laghu).
(21) Cold (śīta), hot (uṣṇa), smooth/oily (snigdha), and dry (rūkṣa). These are declared to be the touch-transformed pudgalas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **phāsao pariṇayā** = transformed by/in touch (sparśa)
- **aṭṭhahā** = of eight kinds
- **kakkhaḍā** = kakkhada/karkaśa — rough, coarse
- **mauaā** = mṛdu — soft, gentle
- **guruyā** = guru — heavy
- **lahuyā** = laghu — light
- **sīyā** = śīta — cold
- **uṇhā** = uṣṇa — hot
- **ṇiddhā** = snigdha — smooth, oily, greasy

The eight types of touch (sparśa) in Jain physics are arranged in four pairs of opposites:
1. Rough ↔ Soft
2. Heavy ↔ Light
3. Cold ↔ Hot
4. Smooth/Oily ↔ Dry

These eight represent the full range of tactile experience. Every physical sensation of touch is a variation of these eight. The pairs **snigdha** (oily/smooth) and **rūkṣa** (dry) are particularly important in Jain physics: paramāṇus bond with each other only when one is snigdha and another is snigdha, or their rūkṣa-snigdha qualities allow bonding — this is the physical basis of all karma attachment.

- **āyayā** = āyata — rectangular/oblong (long-shaped)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Shape (saṃsthāna) is the fifth quality of pudgala — and it is unique to Jain physics to explicitly enumerate it as a fundamental property alongside colour, smell, taste, and touch. The five shapes represent the basic geometric forms:
1. **Parimandala**: perfectly spherical (the paramāṇu itself, and the ideally round)
2. **Vṛtta**: circular (flat-round, like a wheel)
3. **Trikoṇa**: triangular
4. **Catura-aṃśa**: square/quadrangular
5. **Āyata**: rectangular, oblong, elongated

Every material object has one of these basic forms (or a combination). Shape is treated as ontologically fundamental — it is not merely how matter appears, but a real quality that matter possesses.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Pudgalas that have shape are of five kinds: perfectly spherical, circular, triangular, square, and oblong/rectangular.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Shape is a fundamental property of matter. What is the shape of your life right now — circular (repeating patterns), triangular (moving toward a point), or oblong (stretched in one direction)?

रसओ फासओ चेव, भइए संठाणओ वि य ॥३६.२६॥

वण्णओ सुक्किले जे उ, भइए से उ गंधओ ।
रसओ फासओ चेव, भइए संठाणओ वि य ॥३६.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

vaṇṇao je bhave kiṇhe, bhaie se u gaṃdhao |
rasao phāsao ceva, bhaie saṃṭhāṇao vi ya || 36.23 ||

*(Sutras 24–27 follow the same pattern with: ṇīle / lohie / pīyae / sukkiale)*

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(23) A pudgala-skandha that is black in colour — in it, the qualities of smell, taste, touch, and shape are all distributed/fractionally present.
(24–27) The same applies to pudgala-skandhas that are blue, red, yellow, and white respectively.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vaṇṇao** = from/in colour
- **je bhave kiṇhe** = whichever is black
- **bhaie se u** = in it is present, are fractionally distributed (bhajyate)
- **gaṃdhao** = smell quality
- **rasao** = taste quality
- **phāsao** = touch quality
- **saṃṭhāṇao** = shape quality
- **vi ya** = also

### 5. COMMENTARY

**The 530 sub-types of rūpī ajīva begin here.** This series of sutras establishes the key principle: every quality of a pudgala-skandha co-exists with all other qualities. A black skandha has smell, taste, touch, and shape — but these qualities are **bhajya** (distributionally present), meaning they are distributed in fractional combinations. Each of the five colours gives rise to 20 combinations of the other qualities (2 smells × 5 tastes × 8 touches × 5 shapes = 400, but the actual Jain calculation for a black skandha gives 20 "bols" or items). The entire calculation of 530 types proceeds from this matrix of co-occurring qualities.

**Summary of the 20 bols for each colour-skandha:**
A skandha with a given colour has:
- 2 types of smell
- 5 types of taste
- 8 types of touch
- 5 types of shape
Total: 2 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 20 bhajya items (the qualities are listed, not multiplied here)

Five colours × 20 items = 100 items for colour-primary skandhas.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Any pudgala that is black (or blue, red, yellow, white) also has all the other qualities — smell, taste, touch, and shape — present and distributed within it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Everything we perceive has all five qualities simultaneously. What does that tell us about the nature of complete, holistic perception?

The exact derivation continues through the following sutras.

## SUTRAS 36.56–58: THE SIDDHA-ŚILĀ (ABODE OF THE LIBERATED)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कर्हिं पडिहया सिद्धा, कर्हिं सिद्धा पइट्ठिया ।
बारसहिं जोयणेहिं, सव्वट्टस्सुवरिं भवे ॥

पणयाल-सयहस्सा, जोयणाणं तु आयया ।
अट्टजोयण बाहल्ला, सा मज्झम्मि वियाहिया ॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The siddhas are established at the very top of the loka. They reside 12 yojanas above everything else. The siddha-śilā is 45 lakh yojanas wide, and 8 yojanas thick in the middle.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The siddha-śilā is described physically:
- **Location**: at the very summit of the loka (lokāgra), 12 yojanas above the highest point of the universe
- **Width/Length**: 45,00,000 (45 lakh) yojanas — an enormous expanse
- **Thickness**: 8 yojanas in the middle — it is shaped like an inverted umbrella (chatra), thicker in the center and tapering at the edges
- **Quality**: pure, luminous, white like conch shell and jasmine (described in subsequent verses)

The liberated souls exist here permanently, without any further movement or change. They possess **ananta-jñāna** (infinite knowledge), **ananta-darśana** (infinite perception), **ananta-sukha** (infinite bliss), and **ananta-vīrya** (infinite energy). They have no body, no karma, no birth — only pure consciousness in eternal perfection.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The siddha-śilā — the abode of the liberated — sits at the very top of the universe, 12 yojanas above all else. It is 45 lakh yojanas wide and 8 yojanas thick in the center.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

At the very top of reality, beyond all movement and change, sits pure consciousness in eternal bliss. That is where every soul is heading — is mine on the way?

## SUTRAS 36.59–65: SIDDHA CHARACTERISTICS

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अज्जुण सुवण्णगमई, सा पुढवी णिम्मला सहावेणं ।
संखंक कुंदसंकासा, पंडुरा णिम्मला सुहा ॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(59–65) The siddha-śilā is like pure white gold by nature — luminous and immaculate. Like conch shell and jasmine flower, it is bright, spotless, and blissful. The siddha's height is two-thirds of their last body's height. From the perspective of flow (santati), siddhas are with a beginning (sādi — they attained liberation at a specific moment) and without end (ananta — their liberated state never ceases).

### 5. COMMENTARY

Key characteristics of siddhas:
- **Form**: arūpī — completely formless. They have no material body, no colour, no smell.
- **Knowledge**: endowed with perfect jñāna (knowledge) and darśana (vision) — they know everything simultaneously.
- **Size/Space**: each siddha occupies a space slightly less than their final body — specifically, two-thirds of the height of the body in their last human birth.
- **Time**: sādi (with a beginning — they became siddha at a specific moment) but ananta (without end — once liberated, they never return to bondage).
- **Relation to loka**: they are in loka-eka-desa (one specific region of the loka — the siddha-śilā at the top).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The siddha-śilā is luminous white, immaculate, and blissful. Siddhas are formless, omniscient, occupy a space two-thirds of their last body, and their liberation has a beginning but no end.

## SUTRA 36.66: THE SIDDHA'S ETERNAL STATE

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एगत्तेण साइया, पुहत्तेण अणाइया ।
अपज्जवसिया वि य ।
अरूविणो ते सव्वे, णाणदंसण सण्णिया ॥३६.६६–६७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

egattena sāiyā, puhattena aṇāiyā |
apajjavasiyā vi ya |
arūviṇo te savve, ṇāṇadaṃsaṇa saṇṇiyā || 36.66–67 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Individually, siddhas are with a beginning (sādi). Collectively, they are beginningless (anādi — there has never been a time when no siddha existed) and without end (aparyavasita). All siddhas are formless, characterised by perfect knowledge and vision.

### 5. COMMENTARY

A profound ontological statement: while each individual siddha has a beginning (the moment of liberation), the collective state of siddhahood is anādi — there was never a moment in infinite past when there were zero siddhas. Jīvas have been attaining liberation since beginningless time. Similarly, there will never be a moment when there are no siddhas — the process continues forever. The word **ṇāṇadaṃsaṇa saṇṇiyā** defines the essential nature of the liberated soul: pure knowledge and pure vision. This is what remains when all karma is removed — not emptiness, but infinite knowing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Each siddha's liberation has a beginning, but collectively, siddhas have always existed and always will. All are formless, characterised by infinite knowledge and vision.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When all karma is removed, what remains is not nothing — it is infinite knowing. What is the soul behind all your habits, pains, and stories?

## SUTRA 36.69: THE SAṂSĀRĪ JĪVA CLASSIFICATION

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संसारत्था उ जे जीवा, दुविहा ते वियाहिया ।
तसा य थावरा चेव, थावरा तिविहा तहि ॥३६.६९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

saṃsāratthā u je jīvā, duvihā te viyāhiyā |
tasā ya thāvarā ceva, thāvarā tivihā tahi || 36.69 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The saṃsārī jīvas are said to be of two kinds: trasa (mobile — those that can move by their own volition) and sthāvara (immobile — those fixed in place). The sthāvara are of three kinds.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **saṃsāratthā** = saṃsārī — worldly beings, bound in the cycle
- **je jīvā** = those jīvas
- **duvihā** = of two kinds
- **te viyāhiyā** = they are said to be
- **tasā** = trasa — mobile beings (those possessing 2–5 senses and the ability to move)
- **thāvarā** = sthāvara — immobile beings (those with only 1 sense, fixed in place)
- **ceva** = indeed
- **tivihā** = of three kinds (earth-bodied, water-bodied, plant-bodied)
- **tahi** = among them

### 5. COMMENTARY

The great division of bound souls: **trasa** (mobile) and **sthāvara** (immobile). Trasa beings have 2 to 5 senses and the capacity for voluntary movement — they include insects (2-sense), ants (3-sense), bees/flies (4-sense), and animals/humans/celestials/hell-beings (5-sense). Sthāvara beings have only 1 sense (touch) and cannot move by their own volition — these include earth-bodied (pṛthvī-kāya), water-bodied (ap-kāya), fire-bodied (agni/tejas-kāya), air-bodied (vāyu-kāya), and plant-bodied (vanaspati-kāya). This classification is the foundation of Jain biology and the basis for the elaborate ethics of ahiṃsā.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Worldly jīvas are of two kinds: trasa (mobile — with 2 to 5 senses, capable of movement) and sthāvara (immobile — with 1 sense only, fixed in place).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even the earth beneath your feet, the water you drink, and the air you breathe contain living souls. How does knowing that change the way you walk through the world?

## SUTRA 36.70: STHĀVARA JĪVA — PṚTHVĪ-KĀYA (EARTH-BODIED)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पुढवी आउ जीवा य, सुहुमा बायरा तहा ।
इच्चेए थावरा तिविहा, तेसिं भेए सुणेह मे ॥३६.७०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

puḍhavī āu jīvā ya, suhumā bāyarā tahā |
iccee thāvarā tivihā, tesiṃ bhee suṇeha me || 36.70 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Earth-bodied, water-bodied, and plant-bodied — and each further divided into sūkṣma (subtle) and bādara (gross). Thus the sthāvara (immobile) jīvas are of three (main) types — listen as I describe their sub-divisions.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sthāvara (immobile) jīvas possess only one sense — touch (sparśa-indriya). They are classified into five kāya (body-types): pṛthvī-kāya (earth-bodied), ap-kāya (water-bodied), tejas-kāya (fire-bodied), vāyu-kāya (air-bodied), and vanaspati-kāya (plant-bodied). Each is further divided into sūkṣma (subtle — invisible, pervading the entire universe) and bādara (gross — visible, localised). Additionally, each has paryāpta (fully developed) and aparyāpta (undeveloped) states.

## SUTRAS 36.71–72: PṚTHVĪ-KĀYA (EARTH-BODIED SOULS)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुविहा तेउ जीवा य, सुहुमा बायरा तहा ।
पज्जत्तमपज्जत्ता, एवमेए दुहा पुणो ॥३६.७१॥

बायरा जे उ पज्जत्ता, दुविहा ते वियाहिया ।
सण्हा खरा य बोधव्वा, सपज्जवसिया तर्हि ॥३६.७२॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(71) Earth-bodied jīvas are of two types: sūkṣma and bādara. Each is further divided into paryāpta (developed) and aparyāpta (undeveloped).
(72) The bādara paryāpta earth-bodied are further known as two: soft (snigdha) and hard (khara). Their existence has an end (saparyavasita).

### 5. COMMENTARY

**Pṛthvī-kāya** includes all souls embodied in earth, stones, minerals, gems, metals, and related substances. The seven types of pṛthvī listed in the Vivecana: kāṇī (black clay), kālī (dark soil), sāfed (white earth), pīlī (yellow), pāṇḍuraṅgī (pale), fikāśa (scattered), pāṇḍuraṅgī subtypes. The Bhāvārtha clarifies: bādara (gross) pṛthvī-kāya jīvas are the ones we encounter in daily life — in soil, stone, gems. The sūkṣma are subtle, pervading all space, invisible.

## SUTRAS 36.73–84: FIVE KĀYA TYPES — SYSTEMATIC DESCRIPTION

*The text follows an identical pattern for each of the five kāya types. The key sutras for each:*

### AP-KĀYA (Water-Bodied Souls) — Sutras 73–77

किण्हा णीला य रुहिरा, हालिद्दा सुक्किला तहा ।
पंडु पणग मट्टिया, खरा छत्तीसई विहा ॥

**Summary**: Water-bodied jīvas include souls embodied in water, ice, dew, fog, mist, and other aqueous substances. Like pṛthvī-kāya, they are sūkṣma/bādara and paryāpta/aparyāpta.

### TEJAS-KĀYA (Fire-Bodied Souls) — Sutras 78–83

**Prakrit**: पुढवी य सक्करा वालुया, अयं तंब तउय सीसग, रूप्प सुवण्णग...

**Summary**: Fire-bodied jīvas include souls embodied in fire, lightning, hot coals, sparks, and all forms of combustion. The commentary lists: ordinary fire (agni), lightning (vidyut), coal-fire, forest fire, underground fire, and more. They too are sūkṣma/bādara and paryāpta/aparyāpta.

### VĀYU-KĀYA (Air-Bodied Souls) — Sutras 84–92

एए वण्णओ चेव, गंधओ रसओ फासओ ।
संठाणादेसओ वावि, विहाणाइ सहस्ससो ॥

**Summary**: Air-bodied jīvas include souls embodied in wind, breeze, cyclone, and all atmospheric movements. They are the most widely distributed of the sthāvara types after vanaspati.

### VANASPATI-KĀYA (Plant-Bodied Souls) — Sutras 93–106

दुविहा वणस्सइ जीवा, सुहुमा बायरा तहा ।
पज्जत्तमपज्जत्ता, एवमेए दुहा पुणो ॥

**Summary**: Plant-bodied jīvas are the most diverse of the sthāvara. They include:
- **Pratyeka vanaspati** (individual-body plants) — trees, shrubs, herbs, grasses where each plant has one soul
- **Sādhāraṇa vanaspati** (common-body plants) — organisms where an infinite number of souls share one body (like mould, algae, certain root-vegetables)

Each has sūkṣma/bādara and paryāpta/aparyāpta. The Vivecana provides extensive detail on plant classification, listing hundreds of types.

## SUTRAS 36.107–108: TRASA JĪVAS — AGNI-KĀYA AND VĀYU-KĀYA AS TRASA

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्चेए थावरा तिविहा, समासेण वियाहिया ।
इत्तो कालविभागं तु, तेसिं वुच्छ चउव्विहं ॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Thus the sthāvara jīvas have been described in summary. Now I shall describe the trasa (mobile) jīvas."

### 5. COMMENTARY

The transition from sthāvara to trasa. Trasa jīvas are those with 2 to 5 senses and the capacity for voluntary movement. They include:
- **Beindriya** (2-sense): worms, conch shells, leeches
- **Teindriya** (3-sense): ants, lice, certain insects
- **Chaurindriya** (4-sense): flies, bees, mosquitoes, butterflies
- **Panchendriya** (5-sense): all beings with five senses — animals, humans, hell-beings, celestials

## SUTRAS 36.109–126: TRASA JĪVA CLASSIFICATIONS

### BEINDRIYA (2-Sense Beings) — Sutras 109–113

बायरा जे उ पज्जत्ता, पंचहा ते पकित्तिया ।
किमिणो सोमंगला चेव, अलसा माइवाहया ।
वासीमुहा य सिप्पिया, संखा संखणगा तहा ॥

**Summary**: 2-sense beings include worms (kṛmi), conch-shells (śaṅkha), shells, oysters, leeches, and earthworms. They possess touch and taste only. They have bādara (gross) bodies.

### TEINDRIYA (3-Sense Beings) — Sutras 114–116

तिण्णेव सहस्साइं, वासाणुक्कोसिया भवे ।
आउठिई वाऊणं, अंतोमुहुत्तं जहण्णयं ॥

**Summary**: 3-sense beings (touch, taste, smell) include ants, lice, certain bugs, and termites. Maximum lifespan: 3,000 years.

### CHAURINDRIYA (4-Sense Beings) — Sutras 117–119

**Summary**: 4-sense beings (touch, taste, smell, sight) include flies, bees, wasps, butterflies, mosquitoes, and scorpions. Maximum lifespan: stated as varying by type.

### PANCHENDRIYA (5-Sense Beings) — Sutras 120–126

एएसिं वण्णओ चेव, गंधओ रस फासओ ।
संठाणादेसओ वावि, विहाणाइ सहस्ससो ॥

**Summary**: 5-sense beings (touch, taste, smell, sight, hearing) are the most complex category:
- **Nārakī** (hell-beings): 14 types in 7 hells
- **Tiryañca** (animals/plants with 5 senses): 20 types — jalacara (aquatic), sthalacara (terrestrial), khecara (aerial)
- **Manuṣya** (humans): 303 sub-types — garbhaja (womb-born: 202) and saṃmūrchima (spontaneously born: 101)
- **Deva** (celestials): 4 main types — bhavanavāsī, vyantara, jyotiṣka, vaimānika

## SUTRAS 36.127–131: TRASA JĪVA DETAILS — SENSE ORGANS AND BODIES

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वासीमुहा य सिप्पिया, संखा संखणगा तहा ॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(127–131) The gross trasa jīvas are of four types: 2-sense, 3-sense, 4-sense, and 5-sense. [Then extensive listing of specific creatures in each category, their lifespans, locations, body types, and more.]

These sutras provide exhaustive zoological-style classifications for each sense-category of trasa jīvas, listing specific organisms, their maximum lifespans, the regions of the universe they inhabit, and whether they are paryāpta or aparyāpta.
