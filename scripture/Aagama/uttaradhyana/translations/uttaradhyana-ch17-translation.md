# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 17: Pāpasramaṇīya (પાપશ્રમણીય)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary (Shri Uttaradhyayan Sutra-1)
**Chapter:** Seventeenth Adhyayana (સત્તરમું અધ્યયન)
**Total Sutras:** 21
**PDF pages:** 393–404 (book pages 323–333)
**Theme:** The marks of a *pāpasramaṇa* — a fallen or sinful monk — one who has taken initiation (diksha) and wears the outward form of a śramaṇa, but whose actual conduct in knowledge, reverence, walking-care, inspection of belongings, emotional control, sleeping, eating, and community life falls far short of true monkhood. Twenty faults across ten domains are enumerated (Sutras 1–20), and the chapter closes with a single verse (Sutra 21) describing the *suvihat śramaṇa* — the virtuous monk who abandons all these faults and becomes worthy of veneration in both worlds. Ends with *iti bemi* (thus speaks the Lord).

---

## ज्ञानमां पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa in Knowledge (Sutras 1–3)

---

### SUTRA 17.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे केइ उ पव्वइए णियंठे, धम्मं सुणित्ता विणओववण्णे ।
सुदुल्लहं लहिंड बोहिलाभं, विहरेञ्ज पच्छा य जहासुहं तु ॥१७.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je keī u pavvaīe ṇiyaṃṭhe, dhammaṃ suṇittā viṇaovavaṇṇe |
sudullahaṃ lahiṃḍa bohilābhaṃ, vihareñja pacchā ya jahāsuhaṃ tu || 17.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having heard the dharma and become endowed with the vinay of knowledge, vision, and conduct — having obtained the extremely rare benefit of spiritual awakening — one who takes initiation as a nirgrantha yet afterward wanders entirely as he pleases is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *dhammaṃ* = shrut-charitra-rūpa dharma (dharma in the form of knowledge and conduct)
- *suṇittā* = having heard and understood
- *viṇaovavaṇṇe* = having become endowed with vinay of jnāna, darśana, and charitra
- *sudullahaṃ* = extremely rare
- *bohilābhaṃ* = the benefit of bodhi (spiritual awakening / right understanding)
- *lahiṃḍa* = having obtained
- *je keī* = whoever, some particular one
- *pavvaīe* = having taken initiation (diksha)
- *ṇiyaṃṭhe* = becomes a nirgrantha (naked/unbound monk)
- *tu* = but, however
- *pacchā* = afterward, after taking initiation
- *jahāsuhaṃ* = however one finds comfortable, as one pleases
- *vihareñja* = wanders, lives freely

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter opens not with a story but with a type — the practitioner who receives all the prerequisites for liberation and then squanders them. He has heard dharma taught by qualified teachers, become endowed with vinay (proper reverence and conduct), and — most critically — obtained the *bodhi-lābha*, the extremely rare gift of right understanding that is the seed of liberation. Yet after taking diksha, he lives exactly as he pleases, following his own desires and comforts, not the discipline of a nirgrantha. The Gujarati commentary emphasizes the word *sudullaha* (extremely rare): bodhi-lābha is not given to every soul in every birth; to receive it and then squander it by self-willed wandering is the first and deepest mark of a pāpasramaṇa. He has the form of renunciation but retains the heart of a householder.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: If someone receives the rarest gift — a genuine taste of what the spiritual path means — and then takes monastic vows, but afterward just lives however feels comfortable to them rather than actually following the discipline, they are a fallen monk.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever received clarity about what truly matters — and then quietly gone back to living as before?

---

### SUTRA 17.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सेज्जा दढा पाउरणं मे अत्थि, उप्पज्जइ भोतुं तहेव पाउं ।
जाणामि जं वट्टइ आउसु ति, किं णाम काहामि सुएण भंते ॥१७.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sejjā daḍhā pāuraṇaṃ me atthi, uppajjai bhotuṃ taheva pāuṃ |
jāṇāmi jaṃ vaṭṭai āusu ti, kiṃ ṇāma kāhāmi sueṇa bhaṃte || 17.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"O venerable Gurudev! I have a secure place to rest, I have clothing to cover myself, food and drink are available — I know what is happening right now; so what exactly will I gain by studying scripture?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *bhaṃte* = O venerable Gurudev (addressing the teacher respectfully)
- *sejjā daḍhā* = secure, comfortable shelter/abode
- *pāuraṇaṃ* = cloth for covering oneself
- *me atthi* = I have, is mine
- *bhotuṃ* = food for eating
- *pāuṃ* = water for drinking
- *uppajjai* = is obtained, becomes available
- *āusu* = O long-lived one (respectful address)
- *vaṭṭai* = what is happening, what goes on
- *jāṇāmi* = I know
- *ti* = then, after this
- *sueṇa* = through scripture-study, through learning
- *kiṃ ṇāma kāhāmi* = what exactly shall I accomplish, what will I gain

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra puts words directly in the pāpasramaṇa's mouth — a rare and striking technique. He rationalizes his neglect of study with material comforts: shelter is secured, clothing is at hand, food and water come without difficulty. He uses this comfort as a reason to stop learning. The Gujarati commentary identifies this as the logic of the *svaicchā-vihārī* — the self-indulgent wanderer who mistakes the absence of hardship for the achievement of the goal. The phrase *vaṭṭai jāṇāmi* ("I know what is going on") reveals his deeper error: he believes present sensory awareness is sufficient and that śāstra-study adds nothing he doesn't already have. This is not humility; it is the arrogance of the intellectually comfortable monk who has stopped growing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk thinks, "I have food, shelter, and clothing — I'm comfortable, so why bother studying and learning more? I know what I need to know," and uses comfort as an excuse to stop growing spiritually.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When life is comfortable and nothing is urgent, do you still pursue the difficult work of inner learning — or does comfort quietly become an excuse to stop?

### SUTRA 17.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे केइ उ पव्वइए, णिद्धासीले पगामसो ।
भोच्चा पेच्चा सुहं सुवइ, पावसमणे ति सुच्चइ ॥१७.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je keī u pavvaīe, niddhāsīle pagāmaso |
bhoccā peccā suhaṃ suvai, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti succai || 17.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whoever, having taken initiation, becomes excessively sleep-indulgent — eating and drinking and then sleeping comfortably — is said to be a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *je keī* = whoever, some one
- *pavvaīe* = having taken initiation
- *pagāmaso* = excessively, abundantly, greatly
- *niddhāsīle* = becoming deeply sleep-indulgent, addicted to sleep
- *bhoccā peccā* = having eaten and having drunk
- *suhaṃ* = comfortably, at ease
- *suvai* = goes to sleep, sleeps
- *pāvasaṃmaṇe ti succai* = is said to be a pāpasramaṇa

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary identifies four types of pāpasramaṇa in the first three sutras: (1) the self-willed wanderer who lives as he pleases; (2) the one who uses shameless speech to argue against study; (3) the one who is excessively sleep-indulgent; (4) the one who is comfort-seeking in eating and rest. Sutra 3 targets the third and fourth types directly. The word *niddhāsīle* (sleep-addicted) is pointed: sleep is not wrong for a monk in moderation, but when eating and comfort become the routine around which the monk organizes his day — sleep after food, rest after drink — the entire orientation of monastic life is reversed. Life is no longer organized around sādhana (practice); sādhana becomes the interruption to life.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk has basically turned into someone who eats, drinks, and sleeps comfortably — just with robes on — instead of actually doing the hard work of spiritual practice.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much of your day is structured around comfort — and how much around growth?

## विनयमां पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa in Vinay/Reverence (Sutras 4–5)

---

### SUTRA 17.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आयिरय उवज्झाएहिं, सुयं विणयं च गाहिए ।
ते चेव खिंसइ बाले, पावसमणे ति सुच्चइ ॥१७.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āyiraya uvajjhāehiṃ, suyaṃ viṇayaṃ ca gāhie |
te ceva khiṃsai bāle, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti succai || 17.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who has received scriptural knowledge and vinay (codes of conduct and reverence) from acharya and upadhyaya, yet slanders those very teachers — that fool is said to be a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *āyiraya uvajjhāehiṃ* = from the acharya and upadhyaya (the two primary teachers in Jain monastic life)
- *suyaṃ* = śāstra (scripture, sacred learning)
- *viṇayaṃ* = vinay (codes of reverence, proper conduct)
- *gāhie* = having received, having learned
- *te ceva* = those very same (teachers)
- *bāle* = the foolish one, the ignorant one
- *khiṃsai* = criticizes, slanders, speaks ill of
- *pāvasaṃmaṇe ti succai* = is said to be a pāpasramaṇa

**5. COMMENTARY**

Vinay — deep reverential conduct toward one's teachers — is considered the very root of the spiritual path in Jain monasticism. This sutra describes its precise violation: the monk who received everything from his teachers and then turns to slander them. The Gujarati commentary calls this *viveka-bhrashṭa* (the one who has lost discrimination) — because to slander the very source of one's formation is not merely ingratitude, it is evidence that nothing was truly absorbed. The learning passed through the student without transforming him. The word *bāle* (fool/ignorant one) is deliberately placed: slandering one's teacher is not a sign of independence or discernment, it is a sign of spiritual immaturity.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk learns everything from his teachers — knowledge, manners, discipline — and then turns around and speaks badly about those same teachers, which shows he never truly learned anything at all.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you honor the sources of your formation — or do you diminish them once you feel you've outgrown them?

---

### SUTRA 17.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आयिरय-उवज्झायाणं, सम्मं णो पडितप्पइ ।
अप्पडिपूयए थड्ढे, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āyiraya-uvajjhāyāṇaṃ, sammaṃ ṇo paḍitappai |
appaḍipūyae thaḍḍhe, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who does not properly serve the acharya and upadhyaya, does not honor or acknowledge their assistance, and remains arrogant — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *āyiraya-uvajjhāyāṇaṃ* = of the acharya and upadhyaya
- *sammaṃ* = properly, in the correct manner
- *ṇo paḍitappai* = does not serve, does not satisfy, does not attend to their needs
- *appaḍipūyae* = does not sing their praises, does not acknowledge their assistance (*apratipūjaka*)
- *thaḍḍhe* = arrogant, stiff, proud
- *pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai* = is called a pāpasramaṇa

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary explains *appaḍipūyae* (apratipūjaka) as one who does not: honor the guru with reverence, acknowledge the guru's service or assistance, praise the virtues of the teacher, or show gratitude in word or deed. Paired with *thaḍḍhe* (arrogance), the portrait is complete: this monk has made himself the center of his own universe, expecting to receive without giving, to be served without serving, to be honored without honoring. The commentary notes that this is distinct from the slanderer of Sutra 4 — this monk does not attack; he simply ignores and dismisses through his attitude of self-importance.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk doesn't bother to help or serve his teachers, never appreciates or acknowledges what they've done for him, and walks around with his nose in the air as if he already knows everything.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In the relationships where you receive the most — do you also give, serve, and genuinely acknowledge what you've been given?

## ईर्यासमितिमां पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa in Irya-Samiti (Care in Walking) (Sutras 6–8)

---

### SUTRA 17.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सम्मद्दमाणे पाणाणि, बीयाणि हरियाणि य ।
असंजए संजय मण्णमाणे, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sammaddamāṇe pāṇāṇi, bīyāṇi hariyāṇi ya |
asaṃjae saṃjaya maṇṇamāṇe, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who, while walking, crushes two-sensed and higher beings, seeds, and green vegetation — and who, though actually undisciplined, considers himself disciplined — is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *pāṇāṇi* = two-sensed and higher living beings (beindriyādi prāṇī)
- *bīyāṇi* = seeds (which contain life)
- *hariyāṇi* = green vegetation, living plants
- *sammaddamāṇe* = crushing, trampling, grinding underfoot while walking
- *asaṃjae* = being undisciplined, restrained in neither action nor attention
- *saṃjaya* = disciplined, restrained (saṃyamī)
- *maṇṇamāṇe* = considering himself, thinking of himself as

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Irya-samiti* is the first of the five samitis (carefulness-disciplines) — the specific mindfulness a Jain monk must maintain while walking, ensuring that no living being is harmed. This sutra describes its failure in two layers. The outer failure: the monk tramples beings, seeds, and green vegetation without care. The inner failure — and more dangerous — is that he still considers himself *saṃyata* (disciplined). The Gujarati commentary explains that the outer harm alone would be correctable; it is the inner self-assessment of being disciplined that makes the problem intractable, because a person who thinks he is already careful has no reason to become careful.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk walks carelessly and hurts small living beings — bugs, seeds, plants — but the real problem is that he thinks he's being careful, when he's actually not paying attention at all.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your life do you cause harm without noticing — and believe, in those same areas, that you are being careful?

---

### SUTRA 17.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

संथारं फलगं पीढं, णिसेज्जं पायकंबलं ।
अप्पमज्जिय-मारुहइ, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

saṃthāraṃ falakaṃ pīḍhaṃ, ṇisejjaṃ pāyakaṃbalaṃ |
appamajjiya-māruhai, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who, without first inspecting and purifying them, sits upon the bedding, plank, stool, study-seat, and foot-cloth — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *saṃthāraṃ* = straw/grass bedding (*sansaraka*)
- *falakaṃ* = a plank (for sitting or sleeping)
- *pīḍhaṃ* = a stool or low seat
- *ṇisejjaṃ* = the place designated for study and practice (*svādhyāya-sthāna*)
- *pāyakaṃbalaṃ* = the cloth used for wiping the feet
- *appamajjiya* = without inspecting (for living beings), without purifying
- *māruhai* = sits upon, uses

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Pratilekhana* (inspection of all implements before use) is central to ahimsa in Jain monastic life — every surface, every cloth, every implement must be carefully examined before use to ensure no living being is harmed. This sutra describes the beginning of neglect: using one's belongings without inspection. The Gujarati commentary clarifies that *appamajjiya* here implies not just neglecting to wipe or dust, but failing to look — to be visually and mentally present with the object before using it. The list moves from bedding to foot-cloth: every object a monk handles from sleep to waking has the potential to harbor life, and each requires the same reverence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk just sits on his mat, stool, or study cloth without first carefully checking whether any tiny living creatures might be on them — skipping the basic carefulness that protects small life.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How often do you act before you look — and what living things — literally or figuratively — might you be overlooking in your haste?

---

### SUTRA 17.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दवदवस्स चरइ, पमत्ते य अभिकखणं ।
उल्लंघणे य चंडे य, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

davadavassa carai, pamatte ya abhikkhaṇaṃ |
ullaṃghaṇe ya caṃḍe ya, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who walks noisily and in haste, who is repeatedly negligent in religious practice, who transgresses proper boundaries, and who is intensely angry — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *davadavassa* = noisily, hurriedly, with a dham-dham sound (stomping)
- *carai* = walks, moves
- *pamatte* = negligent in dharma-practice, heedless
- *abhikkhaṇaṃ* = repeatedly, again and again
- *ullaṃghaṇe* = in transgressing boundaries, violating norms of conduct
- *caṃḍe* = intensely angry, furious (particularly when given beneficial correction)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary identifies four defects described across this sutra: (1) walking with speed and noise (violating irya-samiti by both hurting beings and displaying unrestrained mind); (2) repeated negligence in sādhana; (3) transgression of conduct-boundaries; and (4) fierce anger. These four are interconnected: the hurrying walk reveals a mind that is already agitated; the negligence reveals a will that keeps choosing comfort over practice; the boundary-transgression reveals a contempt for the structure of monastic life; and the anger is the reaction when any of these are corrected. The commentary notes that the pāpasramaṇa described here becomes angry precisely at instruction meant to help him — the most self-defeating form of anger.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk stomps around carelessly, keeps slacking off in his practice, ignores the rules of conduct, and then gets furious when someone tries to correct him.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone corrects you for your own benefit, is your first reaction gratitude — or irritation?

## प्रतिलेखनमां पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa in Pratilekhana (Inspection of Belongings) (Sutras 9–10)

---

### SUTRA 17.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पडिलेहेइ पमत्ते, अवउज्झइ पायकंबलं ।
पडिलेहणा अणाउत्ते, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

paḍilehei pamatte, avaujjhai pāyakaṃbalaṃ |
paḍilehaṇā aṇāutte, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who performs pratilekhana negligently, places all religious implements carelessly wherever they happen to land, and does not maintain proper care and attention during pratilekhana — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *pamatte* = with negligence, without attentiveness (*pramāda*)
- *paḍilehei* = performs pratilekhana (the formal inspection of implements for living beings)
- *pāyakaṃbalaṃ* = the foot-wiping cloth and all other religious implements (*dharma-upakāraṇas*)
- *avaujjhai* = places haphazardly, puts down carelessly (*avyavasthita mūke*)
- *paḍilehaṇā* = in the activity of pratilekhana
- *aṇāutte* = does not maintain care, does not keep attentiveness (*upayoga*)

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Pratilekhana* is not merely inspection — it is the daily ritual renewal of ahimsa-consciousness, the monk's formal acknowledgment that every implement he owns is a borrowed relationship with living beings, not a possession. To perform it negligently is to hollow out this awareness. The Gujarati commentary notes two simultaneous failures: the monk does the action of pratilekhana (the form is present) but without *upayoga* (attentiveness — the soul's active engagement). He inspects without seeing. He goes through the motions while his mind is elsewhere. Then he places his belongings carelessly, compounding the harm. This sutra is about presence: the fault is not doing something wrong but being absent while appearing to do something right.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk goes through the motions of checking his belongings for tiny creatures but does it without actually paying attention — and then tosses everything wherever — so the whole practice becomes hollow and meaningless.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there practices or rituals in your life that you perform in form but not in presence — going through the motions while your mind is elsewhere?

---

### SUTRA 17.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पडिलेहेइ पमत्ते, से किंचि हु णिसामिया ।
गुरुं परिभावए णिच्चं, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

paḍilehei pamatte, se kiṃci hu ṇisāmiyā |
guruṃ paribhāvae ṇiccaṃ, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who carelessly performs pratilekhana, and whenever he notices anything while inspecting, always uses it as an occasion to disrespect, blame, or speak against the guru — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *pamatte* = with negligence, heedlessly
- *paḍilehei* = performs pratilekhana
- *se kiṃci hu* = something, some particular matter, any detail
- *ṇisāmiyā* = notices, perceives, becomes aware of (while inspecting)
- *ṇiccaṃ* = always, constantly
- *guruṃ paribhāvae* (*guru-paribhāsae*) = disrespects the guru, shames the guru, blames the guru, or speaks back against the guru
- *se* = that one

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary gives a vivid example of *guru-paribhāvae*: when a guru or teacher notices a flaw in a student's conduct during pratilekhana and points it out, the pāpasramaṇa does not receive the correction with humility — instead he deflects: "You taught me this way in the beginning; how can the fault be mine now?" He uses the occasion of inspection to argue, to blame, to reverse the direction of accountability. The commentary identifies three forms of *guru-paribhāsā*: (1) *vigrah* — physical challenge or dispute; (2) *vyudgrah* — stubborn false insistence; and (3) speaking in a manner that demeans or diminishes the guru. Notably, this fault appears in the middle of pratilekhana — the very practice meant to cultivate humility has become an arena for its opposite.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk does his daily inspection sloppily, and whenever his teacher points out a problem, he argues back and blames the teacher instead of taking correction gracefully.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone in authority over you points to a flaw, do you look at the flaw — or do you look for ways to redirect the blame?

## कषायभावोथी पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa from Kashaya-Emotions (Sutras 11–12)

---

### SUTRA 17.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

बहुमाई पमुहरे, थड्ढे लुद्धे अणिग्गहे ।
असंविभागी अचियते, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

bahūmāī pamuhare, thaḍḍhe luddhe aṇiggahe |
asaṃvibhāgī aciyate, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who is very deceitful, garrulous, arrogant, greedy, without control of the senses, who does not share food-distribution with co-residents, and who does not treat fellow monks with love and affection — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *bahūmāī* = very deceitful, given to much deception and cunning (*chhal-kapaṭa*)
- *pamuhare* = garrulous, talkative, loud-mouthed, speaks excessively
- *thaḍḍhe* = arrogant, proud, stiff with self-importance (*abhimānī*)
- *luddhe* = greedy, possessive, attached (*lobhī*)
- *aṇiggahe* = without control of the senses, unable to restrain the sense organs
- *asaṃvibhāgī* = one who does not distribute food properly, does not share alms with others (especially the sick, elderly, junior monks)
- *aciyate* = one who does not treat co-resident monks with love, warmth, and vatsalya (affection)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary identifies seven qualities of a worthy monk: (1) simplicity, (2) measured speech, (3) humility, (4) contentment, (5) sense-control, (6) sharing what one receives with others in the community, and (7) reverence for seniors. This sutra is a direct inversion of all seven. The *asaṃvibhāgī* label is particularly notable: *saṃvibhāga* (proper sharing of food and resources) is an explicit monastic duty — food received on alms-rounds is to be distributed among the guru, the elderly, the sick, and co-residents before one eats. A monk who eats without this distribution violates both the letter and spirit of community life. *Aciyate* (lack of affectionate treatment) completes the picture: the pāpasramaṇa has contracted inward — he takes from the community while giving nothing back in care or warmth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk is two-faced and dishonest, talks too much, is full of pride, is greedy, can't control his impulses, keeps the best food for himself rather than sharing, and treats his fellow monks coldly — basically the opposite of what a monk should be.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In your community — family, team, spiritual circle — do you receive generously and give sparingly, or do you give before you take?

### SUTRA 17.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

विवादं च उदीरेइ, अहम्मे अत्तपण्णहा ।
वुग्गहे कलहे रते, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vivādaṃ ca udīreī, ahamme attapaṇṇahā |
vuggahe kalahe rate, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who fans and inflames arguments, who destroys right understanding through immersion in irreligious thinking, and who is forever delighting in disputes and verbal conflicts — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *vivādaṃ* = arguments, stubborn controversies, contentious debates
- *udīreī* = stirs up, inflames, makes grow (*udīraṇa*)
- *ahamme* = in what is contrary to dharma, in irreligious matters
- *attapaṇṇahā* = one who destroys right understanding (*ātma-prajñā*), who corrupts the intellect with disordered thinking
- *vuggahe* = in physical fighting and disputation
- *kalahe* = in verbal quarreling, causing disturbance through speech
- *rate* = delighting in, absorbed in, given over to

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary gives three forms of *attapaṇṇahā* (destruction of right understanding): (1) *ātma-pragrahā* — corrupting right intelligence by absorbing the mind in non-dharmic matters without proper discernment; (2) *ātma-prasāhā* — promoting one's own worldly interests by corrupting others' right intelligence; and (3) *ātma-praśnāhā* — evading legitimate questions about the soul and dharma, or using clever speech to destabilize others' faith. The pāpasramaṇa does not merely engage in argument: he actively cultivates it, *rates* (delights) in it. Conflict has become his medium. This sutra is a sobering mirror for any practitioner whose spiritual energy is consumed by debate rather than transformation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk loves to start arguments, fills his mind with distracting and irreligious thoughts, and practically lives for conflict and quarreling — spending all his energy fighting instead of growing.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there any area of your life where conflict has become a habit — where you are more comfortable arguing than resting in clarity?

## आसन-शयन विषयक पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa in Seat and Sleep (Sutras 13–14)

---

### SUTRA 17.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अथिरासणे कुक्कुइए, जत्थ तत्थ णिसीयइ ।
आसणम्मि अणाउत्ते, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

athirāsaṇe kukkuīe, jattha tattha ṇisīyai |
āsaṇammi aṇāutte, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who sits unsteadily, who fidgets with hands and feet restlessly, who sits down anywhere and everywhere without discrimination, and who maintains no mindful care about how and where to sit — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *athirāsaṇe* = one who sits in an unstable manner, without steadiness (*asthira āsana*)
- *kukkuīe* = one who fidgets hands and feet restlessly, moves the limbs with restlessness (*kukkuṭa*-like, chicken-like fluttering)
- *jattha tattha* = wherever, anywhere and everywhere, without thought or discrimination
- *ṇisīyai* = sits down, takes a seat
- *āsaṇammi* = in matters related to the seat and posture
- *aṇāutte* = does not maintain attention, does not keep care (*upayoga*)

**5. COMMENTARY**

In Jain monastic life, *āsana* (the posture and manner of sitting) is not merely physical — it reflects the state of the inner life. Steadiness in sitting (*sthirāsana*) is associated with steadiness of mind. The pāpasramaṇa cannot sit still: his hands and feet move, his posture collapses, he seats himself wherever without examining the surface. The Gujarati commentary notes that *jattha tattha* (sitting anywhere) is connected both to ahimsa (sitting without inspecting may harm living beings) and to community norms (sitting in inappropriate places, on other monks' assigned seats, or in householders' spaces). The inability to sit still is both symptom and cause: a restless body feeds a restless mind.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk can't sit still — he fidgets constantly, plops himself down wherever he feels like without thinking, and shows no care or mindfulness about how or where he sits.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can you sit still — in body and in mind — for an extended period? What does your restlessness, when it arises, tell you about the state of your inner life?

---

### SUTRA 17.14

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ससरक्खपाए सुवइ, सेज्जं ण पडिलेहइ ।
संथारए अणाउत्ते, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sasarakkha-pāe suvai, sejjaṃ ṇa paḍilehei |
saṃthārāe aṇāutte, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.14 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who sleeps with feet bearing living dust (sachit particles), who does not inspect the bedding, and who maintains no care regarding the sleeping mat — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *sasarakkha-pāe* = with feet that still have living dust (*sachit* — living-particle-bearing dust)
- *suvai* = lies down to sleep, sleeps
- *sejjaṃ* = the bedding, sleeping place
- *ṇa paḍilehei* = does not inspect (for living beings), does not examine carefully
- *saṃthārāe* = regarding the bedding (*saṃstāraka*), the sleeping mat
- *aṇāutte* = does not maintain attention, does not keep care

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Sachit* in Jain thought refers to objects that contain life — dust that has not been kept away from living beings may carry micro-organisms or seeds of life. A monk is expected to wipe his feet with the foot-cloth (*pāyakambala*) that has been inspected, before lying down, so as not to transfer living particles onto the sleeping mat. This sutra describes the monk who skips this entirely — lying down with unwashed feet on an uninspected mat. The Gujarati commentary connects this to the deeper principle: even in rest, the monk's commitment to ahimsa does not pause. Sleep is not exempt from the consciousness of non-harm. The pāpasramaṇa treats sleep as a private zone — a time when the rules don't apply.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk just goes to bed without checking his mat for tiny creatures or cleaning his feet — because when no one is watching, he drops all the carefulness he's supposed to maintain even in small things.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are the values you hold in public maintained with the same care in private — when no one is watching and nothing is at stake?

## आहार अविवेकथी पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa from Careless Eating (Sutras 15–16)

---

### SUTRA 17.15

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दुद्ध-दहीविगईओ, आहारेइ अभिक्खणं ।
अरए य तवोकम्मे, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

duddha-dahī-vigaīo, āhāreī abhikkhaṇaṃ |
araye ya tavokamme, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.15 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who repeatedly eats stimulant foods such as milk and curd, yet has no inclination for or engagement in austerities — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *duddha-dahī-vigaīo* = milk, curd, and other *vikṛti* (stimulant foods, *vigaya* in Jain dietary classification)
- *abhikkhaṇaṃ* = repeatedly, again and again, habitually
- *āhāreī* = eats, consumes
- *tavokamme* = in austerities (*tapascarya*), in the practice of penance
- *araye* = has no interest, does not remain engaged, is disinclined

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary lists the nine *vigayas* (stimulant foods that heighten sensory pleasure and weaken celibacy): (1) milk (*dūdha*), (2) curd (*dahī*), (3) ghee (*ghī*), (4) butter (*navanīta*), (5) butter oil (*mākhṇa*), (6) oil (*tela*), (7) sweets made from sugar or jaggery (*mīṭhāī*), (8) honey (*madhu*), and (9) meat (*māṃsa*). Of these, honey and meat are entirely forbidden for Jain monks. The sutra specifically names milk and curd, the most commonly consumed. The fault is not their occasional use — it is the *repeated* consumption (*abhikkhaṇaṃ*) alongside complete indifference to austerity. Eating for bodily satisfaction while avoiding the discipline that sublimates the body is the precise inversion of monastic purpose.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk keeps eating rich, stimulating foods all the time but has zero interest in fasting or any other discipline that would actually develop inner strength — so he's feeding the body while starving the spirit.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your life do you feed the body's preferences while quietly avoiding the discipline that would deepen the soul?

---

### SUTRA 17.16

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अत्थंतमि य सूरिम्मि, आहारेइ अभिक्खणं ।
चोइओ पडिचोएइ, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

atthaṃtami ya sūrimmi, āhāreī abhikkhaṇaṃ |
coio paḍicoei, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.16 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who eats repeatedly from sunrise to sunset, and when corrected by the teacher, responds with disrespect and argument — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *sūrimmi* = the sun (*sūrya*)
- *atthaṃtami* = until it sets (*asta* — reaching the horizon at sunset)
- *abhikkhaṃ* = repeatedly, throughout the day
- *āhāreī* = eats, consumes
- *coio* = when instructed not to do so, when prompted or counseled
- *paḍicoei* = responds with a counter-argument, speaks back with disrespect, ignores the instruction and retorts

**5. COMMENTARY**

Jain monastic code specifies that eating should occur only within prescribed time windows during daylight hours; eating throughout the entire day — from sunrise to sunset repeatedly — violates these boundaries. But the deeper fault in this sutra is the response to correction. The Gujarati commentary recounts an illustrative example: a senior monk told a junior who was eating all day, "Brother, you eat all day long — is that right?" The junior responded not by reflecting but by retorting: "You're good at giving advice — why don't you practice it yourself?" This reversal — receiving instruction and responding with a counter-attack — is called *choio paḍicoei* (being counseled and counter-counseling). The inability to receive correction without defending oneself is one of the deepest obstructions to inner growth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk eats all day long from morning to night, and when his teacher points this out and tells him to stop, he argues back instead of listening — turning a chance to grow into a fight.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The last time someone gave you a correction that was true — did you hear it, or did you immediately find a way to argue back?

## अस्थिर चित्तथी पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa from Unstable Mind (Sutra 17)

---

### SUTRA 17.17

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आयिरय परिच्चाई, परपासंड सेवए ।
गाणंगणिए दुब्भूए, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āyiraya pariccāī, parapāsaṃḍa sevae |
gāṇaṃgaṇie dubbhūe, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.17 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who abandons his acharya, joins other sects, repeatedly changes his community and teacher, and is of blameworthy character — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *āyiraya pariccāī* = one who abandons his acharya (teacher, the head of one's monastic community)
- *parapāsaṃḍa sevae* = one who goes to other sects, joins other traditions
- *gāṇaṃgaṇie* = one who repeatedly changes his *gaṇa* (monastic group) and his guru (teacher)
- *dubbhūe* = one of blameworthy nature and conduct (*durbhūta* — base, vile, condemned by good people)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary defines *āyiraya pariccāī* as the monk who, finding the rigor of his own tradition too demanding, or seeking more comfort and convenience, abandons his acharya and takes refuge in a more accommodating sect. The word *gāṇaṃgaṇie* (changing groups repeatedly) is precise: this is not the rare case of genuine doctrinal discernment, but the habit of serially leaving when discipline becomes uncomfortable. The commentary notes that a monk who changes his gaṇa within six months without genuine cause is called *gāṇagaṇika*. The inner disorder here is an unstable mind (*asthira citta*) — the mind that cannot commit, cannot sustain, cannot remain with difficulty long enough for transformation to occur.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk keeps leaving his teacher and community whenever things get hard, hopping from one group to another looking for easier conditions, never really committing to the transformation that staying requires.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a relationship, practice, or commitment in your life that you keep leaving or scaling back — not because it is wrong, but because it demands something difficult of you?

## कुशील आचरणथी पापश्रमणता — Pāpasramaṇa from Misconduct (Sutras 18–19)

---

### SUTRA 17.18

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सयं गेहं परिच्चज्ज, परगेहंसि वावरे ।
णिमित्तेण य ववहरइ, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sayaṃ gehaṃ pariccajja, paragehaṃsi vāvare |
ṇimittteṇa ya vavaharai, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.18 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who, having renounced his own household, goes to householders' homes to perform their work — and engages in forecasting auspicious and inauspicious occasions — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *sayaṃ gehaṃ* = one's own home, one's own householder life
- *pariccajja* = having renounced, having left behind
- *paragehaṃsi* = in the homes of other householders
- *vāvare* = engages in activities, performs tasks, does householder work
- *ṇimittteṇa* = through *nimitta* — forecasting auspicious/inauspicious events (astrology, omens, predictions)
- *vavaharai* = carries out activity, conducts this business

**5. COMMENTARY**

The irony in this sutra is sharp: the monk renounces his *own* home precisely to be free of householder entanglements — and then becomes deeply involved in the homes of *others*. This is worse than never having renounced, because it adds to the social entanglement the pretense of renunciation. The Gujarati commentary identifies this monk as one who provides astrological predictions, announces auspicious times for events, performs rituals for householders, and generally makes himself useful in ways that are the domain of Brahmin priests, not śramaṇas. This is called *nimitta* (forecasting through signs and omens) and is explicitly forbidden in monastic codes. The monk who does this has not merely relapsed — he has built a new attachment-network while wearing the robes of detachment.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk leaves his own home to become a monk, but then ends up spending all his time doing household tasks for others and playing the role of a fortune-teller or astrologer — which is exactly what he was supposed to have given up.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you renounced something in principle — but found a way to re-engage with it in a different form, in someone else's context?

---

### SUTRA 17.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सण्णाइपिंडं जेमेइ, णेच्छइ सामुदाणियं ।
गिहिणिसेज्जं च वाहेइ, पावसमणे ति वुच्चइ ॥१७.१९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

saṇṇāipiṃḍaṃ jemeī, ṇecchai sāmudāṇiyaṃ |
gihiṇisejjaṃ ca vāheī, pāvasaṃmaṇe ti vuccai || 17.19 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who eats food only from the homes of his own caste-relatives, who does not wish to take general alms from the community at large, and who sits on householders' beds and chairs — that one is called a pāpasramaṇa.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *saṇṇāipiṃḍaṃ* = food (*piṃḍa*) from the homes of one's own caste-relatives and family connections (*svajāti-svajanon*)
- *jemeī* = eats, consumes
- *ṇecchai* = does not wish to take, is unwilling
- *sāmudāṇiyaṃ* = *sāmudāyika* alms — general alms collected from all types of households across the community
- *gihiṇisejjaṃ* = householders' beds, chairs, or seating (*gṛhastha-śayyā*)
- *vāheī* = sits on, uses, occupies

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Sāmudāyika* (community alms-collection) is the proper form of alms for a Jain monk: going from door to door across all households, rich and poor, without preference, accepting what is offered without seeking specific foods or specific homes. The Gujarati commentary defines it as collecting alms from every type of family — wealthy or modest — without discriminating. When a monk instead eats only from the homes of his own relatives, two faults arise: (1) attachment to familiar comfort and social networks persists under the guise of renunciation; and (2) the monk essentially becomes a guest at family meals rather than a genuine renunciant. Sitting on householders' beds compounds this — the monk occupies space and comfort that belongs to the world he was supposed to have left.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A fallen monk only eats from his own relatives' homes (keeping his old social ties) instead of collecting alms properly from everyone, and he sits on householders' chairs and beds like a visitor — never really having left the householder world behind.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there old networks, comforts, or identities from a previous stage of life that you are still quietly maintaining while telling yourself you have moved on?

## पापश्रमणनुं भविष्य — The Future of the Pāpasramaṇa (Sutra 20)

---

### SUTRA 17.20

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयारिसे पंचकूसीलसंवुडे, रूवंधरे मुणिपवराण हेट्ठिमे ।
अयंसि लोए विसमेव गरिहिए, ण से इहं णेव परत्थ लोए ॥१७.२०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyārise paṃcakūsīlasaṃvuḍe, rūvaṃdhare muṇipavāraṇa heṭṭhime |
ayaṃsi loe visameava garihie, ṇa se ihaṃ ṇeva paratthu loe || 17.20 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Such a one — filled with the five types of misconduct, wearing only the outward form of a monk, and of conduct inferior to the best monks — becomes blameworthy in this world like poison itself, and finds happiness in neither this world nor the next.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *eyārise* = such as these (referring to all the pāpasramaṇas described above)
- *paṃcakūsīlasaṃvuḍe* = one filled with the five types of *kuśīla* (misconduct): *pāsattha* (sectarian), *uṣaṇa* (lazy), *kuśīla* (of bad conduct), *saṃsatta* (attached to householders), *nitiya* (habitually lapsed)
- *rūvaṃdhare* = one who merely wears the outward appearance (*rūpa*) and form of a monk
- *muṇipavāraṇa heṭṭhime* = inferior in conduct compared to the best monks (*muṇipravara*)
- *ayaṃsi loe* = in this present world
- *visameava* = like poison (*viṣa-sama*)
- *garihie* = becomes blameworthy, is condemned, is despised
- *ṇa se ihaṃ* = neither in this world (does he find happiness or success)
- *ṇeva paratthu loe* = nor in the next world

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary identifies the *pañca-kuśīla* (five types of misconduct) as: (1) *pāsattha* — a monk aligned with a particular sectarian group but without genuine practice; (2) *uṣaṇa* — a lazy, comfort-seeking monk; (3) *kuśīla* — one of genuinely bad conduct; (4) *saṃsatta* — one deeply entangled with householders; (5) *nitiya* — one habitually lapsed in all or several of the above. A pāpasramaṇa may not fall into every category but will fall into some. The commentary is not merely descriptive: it is a diagnosis. The word *viṣa-sama* (like poison) is the most severe judgment the text delivers — the pāpasramaṇa's presence does not merely fail to help the community; like poison, his example and influence can harm those around him. And having squandered this birth, he loses the benefit of both worlds.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A person who wears the monk's robes but lives with all these failures is like poison — he fools himself and misleads others, and in the end gains nothing in this life or the next.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there any area of your life where your outward form — what you show — is significantly ahead of your inner reality — what you actually live?

## सुविहित श्रमणनी आराधना — The Virtuous Monk: The Suvihat Shramana (Sutra 21)

---

### SUTRA 17.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे वज्जए एए सया उ दोसे, से सुव्वए होइ मुणीण मज्झे ।
अयंसि लोए अमयं व पूइए, आराहए लोगामिणं तहा परं ॥२१॥
ति बेमि ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je vajjae ee sayā u dose, se suvvae hoi muṇīṇa majjhe |
ayaṃsi loe amayaṃ va pūie, ārāhae lōgāmiṇaṃ tahā paraṃ || 17.21 ||
ti bemi |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

A monk who forever abandons all these faults described above becomes the most excellent among monks, adorned with noble vows; in this world he is venerated like nectar, and he properly worships the Lord of both worlds — this world and the next. Thus speaks the Lord.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- *je* = whichever monk
- *ee dose* = these faults (all the faults described in Sutras 1–20)
- *sayā* = always, forever, in all circumstances
- *vajjae* = abandons, gives up completely
- *se* = that one
- *muṇīṇa majjhe* = among monks
- *suvvae* = of excellent vows (*suvrata*), the most outstanding one
- *hoi* = becomes
- *ayaṃsi loe* = in this world
- *amayaṃ va* = like nectar (*amṛta-sama*)
- *pūie* = is venerated, is worshipped, is honored
- *tathā* = in that same way
- *iṇaṃ* = this world
- *paraṃ lōgaṃ* = the next world (*paraloka*)
- *ārāhae* = properly worships, properly serves, brings about the benefit of both worlds
- *ti bemi* = thus speaks the Lord (Mahavira's own voice — the standard closing of Jain āgamic sutras)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Gujarati commentary draws the chapter to its close with a moment of elevation: after twenty portraits of what a monk must not be, the final sutra offers the single portrait of what a monk truly is. The *suvihat śramaṇa* is not defined by any positive list of achievements — he is defined simply by the absence of all the foregoing faults. The commentary notes that such a monk's renunciation is real (*satya-tyāga*): he has genuinely left the householder world, not reconstructed it in robes. He becomes *amṛta-sama* (like nectar) in this world — not merely tolerated or respected, but nourishing to those around him, a source of true benefit. The phrase *ārāhae lōgāmiṇaṃ* (properly worships the Lord of both worlds) carries a profound meaning: by keeping his sādhana pure, the monk *worships* — he participates in the ongoing reality of liberation that spans all worlds and all time. The chapter closes with *ti bemi* — the first-person voice of the liberated Lord, Mahavira: "Thus I speak." The teaching is his own.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk who truly avoids all these faults becomes the best kind of person — like nectar instead of poison — someone whose presence genuinely helps everyone around him, in this life and the next.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you abandoned — truly and permanently — the single fault you most recognize in yourself from this chapter, what would become possible in your life?
