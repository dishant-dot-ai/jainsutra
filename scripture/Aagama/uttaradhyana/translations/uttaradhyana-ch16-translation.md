# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 16: Brahmacharya Samadhistan (બ્રહ્મચર્ય સમાધિસ્થાન)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary (Shri Uttaradhyayan Sutra-1)
**Chapter:** Sixteenth Adhyayana (સોળમું અધ્યયન)
**Total Sutras:** 29 (Prose Sutras 1–12 + Verse Gāthās 1–17)
**Theme:** The ten supports (*samādhisṭhāna*) of brahmacharya — the foundations a monk must maintain to protect and sustain celibacy. The chapter is presented in two forms: first as Āgamic prose (question-and-answer canonical statements, S1–12), then restated in verse (gāthās 1–10), followed by summary, duty, glory, and conclusion verses (gāthās 11–17), ending with *iti bemi*.

## SECTION A — PROSE SUTRAS (1–12)

---

### SUTRA 16.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सुयं मे आउसं ! तेणं भगवया एवमक्खायं इह खलु थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं दस बंभचेर समाहिठाणा पण्णत्ता, जे भिक्खू सोच्चा, णिसम्म, संजमबहुले, संवरबहुले, समाहिबहुले, गुत्ते, गुत्तिंदिए, गुत्तबंभयारी सया अप्पमत्ते विहरेज्जा।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

suyaṃ me āusaṃ! teṇaṃ bhagavayā evamakkhāyaṃ iha khalu therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ dasa baṃbhacera samāhiṭhāṇā paṇṇattā, je bhikkhū soccā, ṇisamm'a, saṃjamabahule, saṃvarabahule, samāhibahule, gutte, guttindiye, guttabaṃbhayārī sayā appamatte viharjjā.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

I have heard, O long-lived one! — thus it has been declared by the Bhagavān: "In this (teaching), the venerable elders — the Bhagavants — have proclaimed ten foundations (*samādhisṭhāna*) of brahmacharya, which the bhikshu, having heard and having held in the heart, should practice — abounding in saṃyama, abounding in saṃvara, abounding in samādhi, guarded in mind-speech-body, guarded in the senses, guarded in brahmacharya, always vigilant."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*suyaṃ me* = I have heard | *āusaṃ* = O long-lived one (address to a senior monk) | *teṇaṃ bhagavayā* = by that Bhagavān | *evamakkhāyaṃ* = thus it has been declared | *therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ* = by the venerable elder Bhagavants (senior liberated teachers) | *dasa baṃbhacera samāhiṭhāṇā* = ten brahmacharya foundations/supports | *paṇṇattā* = have been proclaimed | *soccā* = having heard | *ṇisamm'a* = having held in the heart, having decided the meaning | *saṃjamabahule* = abounding in the growth of saṃyama | *saṃvarabahule* = abounding in the multiplicity of saṃvara | *samāhibahule* = abounding in the multiplicity of samādhi-bhāvanā | *gutte* = guarded by mind, speech and body | *guttindiye* = with senses guarded | *guttabaṃbhayārī* = guarded brahmacharya, guardian of brahmacharya | *sayā appamatte* = always vigilant, non-negligent | *viharjjā* = should wander (in saṃyama)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra is the canonical framing statement in Āgamic prose — it follows the standard Jain canonical formula "suyaṃ me āusaṃ" (I have heard, O long-lived one), which marks transmitted sacred teaching. The speaker (Sudharmā Swāmī, narrating to Jambū Swāmī) declares that the venerable elders have proclaimed ten foundations for protecting brahmacharya. The sutra does not list the ten yet — it first establishes the context and the fruits: a monk who hears and follows these ten will possess *saṃyama* (restraint), *saṃvara* (blocking of karmic inflow), *samādhi* (meditative stability), triple *gupti* (guarded mind-speech-body), sense-restraint, and perpetual vigilance. The fruit precedes the list — a pedagogical choice: first understand why, then receive the what.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: Lord Mahavira's senior disciples taught ten specific rules for protecting celibacy — and a monk who truly follows them will naturally grow in self-control, inner calm, and spiritual alertness.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Before you receive any teaching, do you first understand why it matters — and does understanding the purpose change how deeply you receive the instruction?

### SUTRA 16.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कयरे खलु ते थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं दस बंभचेरसमाहिठाणा पण्णत्ता? इमे खलु ते थेरेहिं भगवंतेहिं दस बंभचेरसमाहिठाणा पण्णत्ता — तं जहा:

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kayare khalu te therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ dasa baṃbhacerasamāhiṭhāṇā paṇṇattā? ime khalu te therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ dasa baṃbhacerasamāhiṭhāṇā paṇṇattā — taṃ jahā:

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Which indeed are those ten brahmacharya foundations proclaimed by the venerable elder Bhagavants?" — "These indeed are those ten brahmacharya foundations proclaimed by the venerable elder Bhagavants — namely:"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kayare* = which, what | *khalu* = indeed, certainly (particle of emphasis) | *te* = those | *therehiṃ bhagavaṃtehiṃ* = by the venerable elder Bhagavants | *dasa baṃbhacera samāhiṭhāṇā* = ten brahmacharya foundations | *paṇṇattā* = proclaimed, declared | *ime* = these (the answer begins) | *taṃ jahā* = namely, they are as follows

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra follows the standard canonical Q&A format — the question is posed ("which are the ten?") and immediately answered ("they are as follows"). This formal structure of question-and-answer is not rhetorical — it reflects the oral transmission tradition of the Jain Āgamas. The teaching was transmitted in dialogue form between teacher and student, and the text preserves that pedagogical structure intact. The word *khalu* — "indeed, certainly" — appears in both question and answer, lending the exchange a quality of affirmation: yes, these ten truly exist; yes, they were truly proclaimed.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: The student asks "what are the ten?" and the teacher answers "here they are" — this back-and-forth was how sacred knowledge was passed down for thousands of years.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you receive a teaching, do you truly ask the question first — or do you collect answers without first feeling the real urgency of the question?

### SUTRA 16.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(1) उपाश्रय विवेक: विवित्ताइं सयणासणाइं सेवित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे। णो इत्थी पसु पंडग संसत्ताइं सयणासणाइं सेवित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

तं कहिमिते चे? आयिरयाह — णिग्गंथस्स खलु इत्थीपसुपंडग संसत्ताइं सयणासणाइं सेवमाणस्स बंभयारिस्स बंभचेरे संका वा, कंखा वा, वितिगिच्छा वा समुप्पज्जिज्जा, भेयं वा लभेज्जा, उम्मायं वा पाउणिज्जा, दीहकालियं वा रोगायंकं हवेज्जा, केवलिपण्णत्ताओ वा धम्माओ भंसेज्जा। तम्हा खलु णो णिग्गंथे इत्थि पसुपंडग संसत्ताइं सयणासणाइं सेविज्जा।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vivittāiṃ sayaṇāsaṇāiṃ sevitta havai, se niggaṃthe. ṇo itthī pasu paṃḍaga saṃsattāiṃ sayaṇāsaṇāiṃ sevitta havai, se niggaṃthe. | taṃ kahimite ce? āyirayāha — niggaṃthas khalu itthīpasupaṃḍaga saṃsattāiṃ sayaṇāsaṇāiṃ sevamāṇas baṃbhayāriss baṃbhacere saṃkā vā, kaṃkhā vā, vitigicchā vā samuppajjijjā, bheyaṃ vā labhejjā, ummāyaṃ vā pāuṇijjā, dīhakāliyaṃ vā rogāyaṃkaṃ havejjā, kevalippaṇṇattāo vā dhammāo bhaṃsejjā. tamhā khalu ṇo niggaṃthe itthī pasu paṃḍaga saṃsattāiṃ sayaṇāsaṇāiṃ sevijjā.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The First Foundation — Upāśraya Viveka (Discernment of Dwelling): He who uses solitary beds and seats free from women, animals, and neuters — he is a nirgranth. He who uses beds and seats occupied by women, animals, and neuters — he is not a nirgranth. Why so? The teacher answers: For the nirgranth who is a brahmacharya practitioner, residing in places occupied by women, animals, and neuters may give rise to doubt (*śaṅkā*), desire (*kāṅkṣā*), or wavering (*vitigicchā*) regarding brahmacharya; or he may experience a fall (*bheda*); or he may fall into madness (*unmāda*); or he may contract a long-term disease; or he may fall away from the Dharma proclaimed by the Kevalis. Therefore the nirgranth must not use beds or seats occupied by women, animals, and neuters.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vivittāiṃ* = solitary, free from women and animals | *sayaṇāsaṇāiṃ* = beds and seats (by extension: dwelling places) | *sevitta* = using, residing in | *niggaṃthe* = he is a nirgranth | *itthī pasu paṃḍaga saṃsattāiṃ* = occupied by women, animals, neuters | *saṃkā* = doubt about brahmacharya | *kaṃkhā* = desire, craving for enjoyment | *vitigicchā* = wavering, uncertainty about brahmacharya fruit | *bheyaṃ* = fall, collapse, ruin | *ummāyaṃ* = madness, mental disturbance | *rogāyaṃkaṃ* = disease-inducing, long-term illness | *kevalippaṇṇattāo dhammāo bhaṃsejjā* = may fall away from the Dharma proclaimed by the Omniscient Ones

**5. COMMENTARY**

The first of the ten brahmacharya foundations is *upāśraya viveka* — discernment about where one sleeps and dwells. The monk must choose a dwelling free from women, animals, and neuters (*paṇḍaka*). The sutra gives seven specific dangers that arise from living in proximity to them: doubt (*śaṅkā*), desire (*kāṅkṣā*), wavering (*vitigicchā*), collapse (*bheda*), madness (*unmāda*), disease, and ultimately falling from the Dharma itself. These seven dangers are not hypothetical — they trace a precise psychological and physiological chain. Proximity to the opposite sex generates subtle mental agitation (śaṅkā), which becomes craving (kāṅkṣā), which creates inner conflict (vitigicchā), which eventually breaks brahmacharya (bheda), leading to mental disorder, physical disease, and finally spiritual ruin.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk must be careful about where he sleeps and rests — staying in places free from distraction is not uptightness, it's protection of his life's purpose.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What environments in your life support your deepest intentions — and which ones, if you're honest, work quietly against them?

### SUTRA 16.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(2) स्त्रीकथा संयम: णो इत्थीणं कहं किहित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

तं कहिमिते चे? आयिरयाह — णिग्गंथस्स खलु इत्थीणं कहं कहेमाणस्स, बंभयारिस्स बंभचेरे संका वा, कंखा वा, वितिगिच्छा वा समुप्पज्जिज्जा, भेयं वा लभेज्जा, उम्मायं वा पाउणिज्जा, दीहकालियं वा रोगायंकं हवेज्जा, केवलिपण्णत्ताओ वा धम्माओ भंसेज्जा। तम्हा खलु णो णिग्गंथे इत्थीणं कहं कहेज्जा।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo itthīṇaṃ kahaṃ kahitta havai, se niggaṃthe. | taṃ kahimite ce? āyirayāha — niggaṃthas khalu itthīṇaṃ kahaṃ kahemāṇass, baṃbhayāriss baṃbhacere saṃkā vā... tamhā khalu ṇo niggaṃthe itthīṇaṃ kahaṃ kahejjā.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Second Foundation — Strikathā Saṃyama (Restraint from Women's Talk): He who does not engage in talk about women — he is a nirgranth. Why so? For the nirgranth brahmacharya practitioner who engages in talk about women, there may arise doubt, desire, or wavering regarding brahmacharya; or he may experience a fall; or madness; or long-term disease; or he may fall away from the Dharma of the Kevalis. Therefore the nirgranth must not engage in talk about women.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*itthīṇaṃ kahaṃ* = talk about women — their form, appearance, laughter, movement, manner, caste, dress | *kahitta* = engaging in, speaking | *ṇo kahejjā* = must not speak

**5. COMMENTARY**

The second foundation addresses speech — specifically, conversation about women: their appearance, manners, dress, caste, beauty, humor. Even this seemingly innocent category of talk is identified as dangerous to brahmacharya. The reason is psychological: as Jain psychology understands it, attention and speech shape thought. When a monk lingers in descriptions of women's attractiveness — even in a storytelling context — the mind begins to be colored by those impressions. The same chain of seven dangers (śaṅkā → kāṅkṣā → vitigicchā → bheda → unmāda → disease → dharma-fall) applies here as in sutra 3. This is not prohibition for its own sake — it is recognition that where speech goes, attention follows; and where attention lingers, desire grows.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk avoids conversations about women — their looks, stories, or behavior — because he knows that where his words go, his mind follows.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you talk about most — and does the pattern of your conversation reflect the values you claim to hold?

### SUTRA 16.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(3) स्त्री साथे एकासन वर्जन: णो इत्थीहिं सद्धिं सणिसेज्जागए विहरित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

तं कहिमिते चे? आयिरयाह — णिग्गंथस्स खलु इत्थीहिं सद्धिं सणिसेज्जागयस्स, बंभयारिस्स बंभचेरे संका वा, कंखा वा, वितिगिच्छा वा समुप्पज्जिज्जा, भेयं वा लभेज्जा, उम्मायं वा पाउणिज्जा, दीहकालियं वा रोगायंकं हवेज्जा, केवलिपण्णत्ताओ वा धम्माओ भंसेज्जा। तम्हा खलु णो णिग्गंथे इत्थीहिं सद्धिं सणिसेज्जागए विहरेज्जा।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo itthīhiṃ saddhaṃ saṇisejjāgae viharittā havai, se niggaṃthe. | tamhā khalu ṇo niggaṃthe itthīhiṃ saddhaṃ saṇisejjāgae viharejjā.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Third Foundation — Avoidance of Shared Seating with Women: He who does not live having sat together on the same seat as women — he is a nirgranth. Why so? [Same seven dangers apply.] Therefore the nirgranth must not live having sat together on the same seat as women.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*itthīhiṃ saddhaṃ* = together with women | *saṇisejjāgaye* = sitting on the same seat | *viharittā* = living, abiding | *ṇo viharejjā* = must not abide (in that state) | The vivechana clarifies: this means (1) not sitting on the same seat as a woman at the same time; (2) not sitting even on a seat recently vacated by a woman within an *antarmuhūrta* (48 minutes)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The third foundation is physical proximity — specifically, shared seating. Even sitting on the same bench or mat as a woman is prohibited. The vivechana (commentary) gives two interpretations: first, the obvious one — do not sit simultaneously; second, a subtler one — do not sit on a seat a woman has just vacated, even if she is no longer there. The Jain psychology here is precise: the warmth, scent, and impression left by the body of another can itself be a stimulus. This may seem extreme to a modern reader, but it reflects a thorough understanding of how sense perception triggers association, and how association triggers desire. The point is not distrust of women — it is a monk's careful management of his own perceptual field.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk avoids sharing seats with women — not out of disrespect, but because he has learned through deep practice exactly how easily the mind can be pulled by physical proximity.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there physical environments or proximities in your life that you know, honestly, affect your inner state — and do you manage them with as much care as you claim to manage your thoughts?

### SUTRA 16.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(4) दृष्टि संयम: णो इत्थीणं इंदियाइं मणोहराइं, मणोरमाइं आलोएत्ता, णिज्झाएत्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

तं कहिमिते चे? आयिरयाह — णिग्गंथस्स खलु इत्थीणं इंदियाइं मणोहराइं, मणोरमाइं आलोए–माणस्स, णिज्झायमाणस्स बंभयारिस्स बंभचेरे संका वा, कंखा वा, वितिगिच्छा वा समुप्पज्जिज्जा, भेयं वा लभेज्जा, उम्मायं वा पाउणिज्जा, दीहकालियं वा रोगायंकं हवेज्जा, केवलिपण्णत्ताओ वा धम्माओ भंसेज्जा। तम्हा खलु णो णिग्गंथे इत्थीणं इंदियाइं मणोहराइं, मणोरमाइं आलोएज्जा, णिज्झाएज्जा।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo itthīṇaṃ indiyāiṃ maṇoharāiṃ, maṇoramāiṃ āloeттā, nijjhāettā havai, se niggaṃthe. | tamhā khalu ṇo niggaṃthe itthīṇaṃ indiyāiṃ maṇoharāiṃ, maṇoramāiṃ āloejjā, nijjhāejjā.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Fourth Foundation — Dṛṣṭi Saṃyama (Restraint of Gaze): He who does not gaze at or contemplate the beautiful and charming sense-organs of women — he is a nirgranth. Why so? [Same seven dangers apply.] Therefore the nirgranth must not gaze at or contemplate the beautiful and charming sense-organs of women.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*itthīṇaṃ indiyāiṃ* = the sense-organs of women — understood broadly as all limbs and parts of the body (nose, eyes, face, beauty) | *maṇoharāiṃ* = charming, mind-captivating | *maṇoramāiṃ* = mind-delighting, attractive | *āloeттā* = gazing at (brief looking) | *nijjhāettā* = contemplating, dwelling upon with the mind after seeing | *ṇo āloejjā* = must not gaze | *ṇo nijjhāejjā* = must not contemplate/dwell upon

**5. COMMENTARY**

The fourth foundation moves from space to perception — specifically the gaze. The sutra distinguishes two stages: *ālocanā* (looking, gazing) and *nijjhāyamāna* (contemplating, dwelling upon). A monk may unavoidably catch a glimpse — eyes receive images involuntarily — but the contemplation that follows looking is a choice. It is this contemplation, the lingering of attention on the pleasant form of another, that the fourth foundation prohibits. The Jain teachers understand that desire does not begin in action — it begins in sustained attention. What you gaze upon and dwell upon becomes what you want. Managing the gaze is therefore managing the first link in the chain of desire.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk doesn't stare at or dwell on attractive features of women — he knows that what the mind lingers on becomes what the heart wants.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you find yourself repeatedly looking at, or mentally dwelling on, that you know isn't serving your growth — and what does that pattern cost you?

### SUTRA 16.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(5) स्त्री शब्द संयम: णो इत्थीणं कूइयं, रुइयं, गीयं, हसियं, थणियं, कंदियं सोच्चा हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo itthīṇaṃ kūiyaṃ, ruiyaṃ, gīyaṃ, hasiyaṃ, thaṇiyaṃ, kaṃdiyaṃ soccā havai, se niggaṃthe.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Fifth Foundation — Strī Śabda Saṃyama (Restraint of Hearing Women's Sounds): He who does not listen to women's cooing, crying, singing, laughing, whispering, or calling — he is a nirgranth. [Same seven dangers apply.] Therefore the nirgranth must not listen to these sounds.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kūiyaṃ* = cooing, sweet murmuring | *ruiyaṃ* = crying, weeping | *gīyaṃ* = singing | *hasiyaṃ* = laughing | *thaṇiyaṃ* = whispering, hushed speaking | *kaṃdiyaṃ* = calling out, exclaiming

**5. COMMENTARY**

The fifth foundation targets the ear (śrotra-indriya). Six specific sounds are listed, each capable of stirring emotional attachment: sweet murmuring, weeping (which evokes protective instincts), singing, laughing, whispering (which creates intimacy), and calling out. The Vivecana explains that each sound bypasses rational thought and directly stimulates emotional response. Even incidental hearing should be managed — if a monk hears such sounds, he should redirect attention to dharma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk doesn't deliberately listen to women's cooing, crying, singing, laughing, whispering, or calling — each sound has the power to stir desire through the ear.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which sounds pull at your emotions most powerfully — and are you aware of their effect on you?

### SUTRA 16.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(6) भुक्त भोजन स्मरण संयम: णो इत्थीणं पुव्वभुत्तासियाणि, सहभुत्तासियाणि, ससयणासणभोयणपडिलेहणाणि समणुस्सरित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo itthīṇaṃ puvvabhuttāsiyāṇi, sahabhuttāsiyāṇi, sasayaṇāsaṇabhoyaṇapaḍilehaṇāṇi samaṇussarittā havai, se niggaṃthe.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Sixth Foundation — Bhukta Bhojana Smaraṇa Saṃyama (Not Recalling Past Pleasures): He who does not recall past indulgences with women, shared meals, shared beds, shared foods — he is a nirgranth.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*puvvabhuttāsiyāṇi* = memories of past indulgences | *sahabhuttāsiyāṇi* = memories of eating/enjoying together | *sasayaṇāsaṇabhoyaṇa* = sharing beds, seats, food | *samaṇussarittā* = does not recall, does not reminisce

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sixth foundation targets internal memory. Even in total physical isolation, the mind can replay past experiences with devastating effect. The Vivecana specifies: memories of past physical intimacy, shared meals (a deeply bonding activity), sitting together, and sleeping arrangements. Each memory carries emotional charge that can reactivate desire. The brahmacārī must be vigilant not just about present stimuli but about the mind's tendency to revisit the past.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk doesn't let his mind dwell on memories of past pleasures shared with women — past meals, past intimacy, past companionship.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which memories from your past keep resurfacing — and what power do they still hold over you?

---

### SUTRA 16.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(7) प्रणीत आहार वर्जन: णो पणीयं, खज्जभोयणं, सप्पिं माहणसे, मिट्ठे, कडुहारे, अइभोयणं भुंजित्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo paṇīyaṃ, khajjabhoyaṇaṃ, sappiṃ māhaṇase, miṭṭhe, kaḍuhāre, aibhoyaṇaṃ bhuṃjittā havai, se niggaṃthe.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Seventh Foundation — Praṇīta Āhāra Varjana (Avoiding Rich Food): He who does not eat rich, delicious food — butter, sweets, sharp spices, excessive food — he is a nirgranth.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The seventh foundation connects food to desire directly. The Vivecana identifies how stimulating food increases bodily heat and energy, which the body channels into sexual desire. Five problematic food categories: excessively sweet, excessively oily/buttery, excessively spiced, food eaten in excess, and food eaten at improper times. The monk eats for sustenance, not for pleasure. When food becomes a pleasure source, it becomes a gateway to other pleasures.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A monk avoids rich, stimulating food — sweets, ghee, heavy spices, overeating — because rich food directly inflames desire.

---

### SUTRA 16.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(8) पणीयं भत्तपाणं, आहारपरिमाणवं: धम्मलद्धं मियं काले, जत्तत्थं पणिहाणवं। णाइमत्तं तु भुंजेज्जा, बंभचेरे रओ सया।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammaladdhaṃ miyaṃ kāle, jattatthaṃ paṇihāṇavaṃ | ṇāimattaṃ tu bhuṃjejjā, baṃbhacere rao sayā ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Eighth Foundation — Proper Eating: Food obtained through dharma, measured in quantity, at the proper time, for sustenance, with mindful intention — only that much should a brahmacārī eat, never excessively.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The positive counterpart to sutra 9's prohibition. Five conditions for proper eating: (1) righteously procured alms (dharma-labdha), (2) measured quantity (mita), (3) proper time (kāla), (4) for sustenance only (yātrā-artha), (5) with mindful awareness (praṇidhāna). This is the Jain middle path for food: not starvation, not indulgence — mindful, purposeful eating.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: Eat only righteous food, in measured amounts, at proper times, for sustenance — never for pleasure, never excessively.

---

### SUTRA 16.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(9) विभूषा संयम: णो गत्तभूसणं, अंगुलीओ, कडगं, कुंडलं, कंचुयं, तिलगं, अंजणं, विलेवणं धारेत्ता हवइ, से णिग्गंथे।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo gattabhūsaṇaṃ, aṃgulīo, kaḍagaṃ, kuṃḍalaṃ, kaṃcuyaṃ, tilagaṃ, aṃjaṇaṃ, vilevṇaṃ dhārettā havai, se niggaṃthe.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Ninth Foundation — Vibhūṣā Saṃyama (Avoiding Adornment): He who does not wear body-ornaments, rings, bracelets, earrings, garments for display, tilaka, kohl, or paste — he is a nirgranth.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The ninth foundation addresses self-display. Adorning the body is an implicit invitation — it signals availability and attracts attention. The Vivecana lists specific ornaments and cosmetics. Even a monk who avoids women cannot maintain brahmacarya if he treats his own body as an object of beauty. The body is a vehicle for dharma, not a display case.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: No ornaments, no cosmetics, no body decoration — the monk's body serves dharma, not display.

---

### SUTRA 16.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

(10) इन्द्रिय विषय संयम: णो सद्दे, रूवे, गंधे, रसे, फासे — पंचविहे कामगुणे, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए, से णिग्गंथे।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo sadde, rūve, gaṃdhe, rase, phāse — paṃcavihe kāmaguṇe, ṇiccaso parivajjae, se niggaṃthe.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The Tenth Foundation — Indriya Viṣaya Saṃyama (Restraint of All Five Senses): He who perpetually avoids the five types of sense-pleasure — sound, form, smell, taste, and touch — he is a nirgranth.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The tenth and broadest station: guard all five senses simultaneously. Previous foundations addressed specific senses; this encompasses all five. The five kāmaguṇa (sense-pleasures) are the channels through which external objects stimulate internal passion. Each sense is a doorway; this foundation instructs: guard every doorway.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: Guard all five senses — hearing, sight, smell, taste, touch — against every form of sense-pleasure. Perpetually.

---

## SECTION B — VERSE GĀTHĀS (1–17)

*The following 17 gāthās present the ten samādhisthānas in verse form, adding poetic imagery, the praise of celestial beings, and the chapter's conclusion.*

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.1 (Verse)

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जं विवित्तमणाइण्णं, रहियं इत्थी जणोहिओ ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, सिंगारत्थं ण धारए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Secluded, free from distraction, separated from women — a monk devoted to brahmacarya should not maintain anything for the sake of adornment.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मण्णपल्हायजणाणिं, कामरागविवड्ढणिं ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, थीकहं तु विवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Talk about women — which pleases the mind and increases desire — a monk devoted to brahmacarya should completely abandon.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

समं च संथवं थीहिं, संकहं, बंभचेरे रओ ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Socializing with women, familiar conversation — a brahmacārī monk should perpetually avoid.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अंगपच्चंग-संठाणं, चारुल्लविय-पेहियं ।
बंभचेरे रओ थीणं, चक्खुगिज्झं विवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Beautiful forms of women's limbs — a brahmacārī must not gaze upon with craving eyes.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कूइयं रुइयं गीयं, हसियं थिणिय-कंदियं ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, सोयगिज्झं विवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Women's cooing, crying, singing, laughing, whispering, calling — a brahmacārī should not listen with ear-craving.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

हासं किड्डं रइ दप्पं, सहभुत्तासियाणि य ।
बंभचेरे रओ थीणं, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Fun, play, passion, pride, and memories of shared pleasures with women — a brahmacārī should perpetually avoid recalling.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पणीयं भत्तपाणं तु, खिप्पं मयविवड्ढणं ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Rich food and drink — which quickly increases desire — a brahmacārī monk should perpetually avoid.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मलद्धं मियं काले, जत्तत्थं पणिहाणवं ।
णाइमत्तं तु भुंजेज्जा, बंभचेरे रओ सया ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Righteous food, measured, at proper time, for sustenance, with mindful intention — only that much should a brahmacārī eat, never excessively.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

विभूसं परिवज्जेज्जा, सरीरपरिमंडणं ।
बंभचेरे रओ भिक्खू, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Body-adornment and decoration — a brahmacārī monk should perpetually avoid.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सद्दे रूवे य गंधे य, रसे फासे तहेव य ।
पंचविहे कामगुणे, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Sound, form, smell, taste, touch — all five sense-pleasures — should be perpetually guarded against.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.11–13: THE SUMMARY AND POISON SIMILE

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आलओ थीजणाइण्णो, थीकहा य मणोरमा ।
संथवो चेव णारीणं, तांसि इंदिय दरिसणं ॥

कूइयं रुइयं गीयं, हास भुत्तासियाणि य ।
पणीयं भत्तपाणं च, अइमायं पाणभोयणं ॥

गत्तभूसणमिहुं च, कामभोगा य दुज्जया ।
णरस्सउत्त गवेसिस्स, विसं तालउडं जहा ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Dwelling among women, delightful talk, socializing, gazing — cooing, crying, singing, laughter, memories — rich food, excessive eating — body-adornment, sexual indulgence: all of these, for the sense-seeker, are like poison in the palm-fruit.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The devastating simile: **visaṃ tālauḍaṃ jahā** — "like poison in the tāla (palm) fruit." The toddy fruit looks sweet and nourishing but conceals a slow poison. Each pleasure listed above appears harmless but gradually destroys the capacity for brahmacarya and liberation.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.14: DUTY DESPITE DIFFICULTY

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दुज्जए कामभोगे य, णिच्चसो परिवज्जए ।
दुज्जए कामभोगे य, संकाठाणि सव्वाणि, वज्जेज्जा पणिहाणवं ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Difficult-to-conquer sense-pleasures should be perpetually avoided. All dangerous situations should be abandoned with mindful intention.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.15: DHARMĀRĀMA — THE GARDEN OF DHARMA

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्माराणे चरे भिक्खू, धिइमं धम्मसारही ।
धम्माराणे रए दंते, बंभचेरे समाहिए ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Let the monk dwell in the garden of dharma — patient, a chariot of dharma. Delighting in dharma's garden, self-controlled — established in brahmacarya.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final station is a redirection: make dharma your source of joy. **Dharmārāma** — "the garden of dharma" — deliberately echoes the pleasure-gardens of kings and devas. Dharma is meant to be a source of genuine delight, not grim endurance. Study, meditation, austerity, contemplation — these are the flowers in the brahmacārī's garden.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.16: EVEN THE GODS BOW

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

देव-दाणव-गंधव्वा, जक्ख-रक्खस-किण्णरा ।
बंभयारिं णमंसंति, दुक्करं जे करंति तं ॥

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Devas, Dānavas, Gandharvas, Yakṣas, Rākṣasas, and Kiṃnaras — all bow to the brahmacārī. They honour those who do what is difficult.

**5. COMMENTARY**

Even the most powerful beings in the universe bow before the brahmacārī. Why? Because **dukkaraṃ** — what the brahmacārī does is genuinely difficult. The gods themselves are subject to desire. A human who conquers desire surpasses the gods.

---

### GĀTHĀ 16.17: THIS DHARMA IS ETERNAL

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एस धम्मे धुवे णिच्चे, सासए जिणदेसिए ।
सिद्धा सिज्झंति चाणेणं, सिज्झिस्संति तहावरे ॥ – ति बेमि ।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This dharma is eternal, permanent, everlasting — proclaimed by the Jina. Through it, siddhas have attained, are attaining, and will forever attain liberation. Thus I say.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The closing verse declares the universality and eternity of this dharma. It is **dhuve, ṇicce, sāsae**: firm, eternal, everlasting. The triple-time formula (past-present-future) emphasises timeless truth. **Ti bemi** — "Thus I say" — seals the chapter with Mahāvīra's authority.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The path is eternal. The method is proven. Others have walked it and arrived. Will you?
