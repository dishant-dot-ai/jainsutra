# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 30
# Tapomārga Gati (The Path of Austerity)

**Total Sutras:** 37
**Theme:** This chapter is a comprehensive exposition on tapas (austerity/asceticism) as the path to spiritual purification and liberation. It begins by establishing the twofold approach of renunciation and practice — turning away from sinful conduct while actively engaging in righteous conduct. The chapter then systematically covers the six external austerities (bāhya tapa): anaśana (fasting), avamodarikā (reduced intake), bhikṣācaryā (alms-seeking), rasa-parityāga (abandoning tastes), vivikta-śayyāsana (solitary sleeping/sitting), and kāya-kleśa (bodily mortification); and the six internal austerities (abhyantara tapa): prāyaścitta (expiation), vinaya (reverence), vaiyāvṛtya (service), svādhyāya (study), vyutsarga (abandoning the body), and dhyāna (meditation). It describes how austerity, properly practiced with right knowledge and right faith, burns away karmic bonds and leads to liberation.

## SUTRA 30.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एगओ विरइं कुज्जा, एगओ य पवत्तणं ।
असंजमे णियत्तै चे, संजमे य पवत्तणं ॥३०.१॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Egao viraiṃ kujjā, egao ya pavattaṇaṃ |
Asaṃjame ṇiyattai ce, saṃjame ya pavattaṇaṃ || 30.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One should practice renunciation on one hand and engagement on the other — turning away from non-restraint and engaging in restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **egao** — on one side / from one direction
- **viraiṃ** — renunciation / cessation (virati)
- **kujjā** — one should do / one should practice
- **egao** — on the other side
- **pavattaṇaṃ** — engagement / active practice (pravartana)
- **asaṃjame** — in non-restraint / in sinful conduct
- **ṇiyattai** — one turns away / one ceases (nivartate)
- **ce** — and
- **saṃjame** — in restraint / in self-discipline
- **pavattaṇaṃ** — engagement / active practice

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening sutra establishes the fundamental dual principle of the spiritual path: simultaneously withdrawing from what is harmful and advancing toward what is beneficial. The practitioner must do two things at once — *virati* (renunciation of sinful conduct) and *pravartana* (active engagement in righteous conduct). This is not merely passive avoidance but a dynamic, two-directional effort. One turns away from *asaṃyama* (non-restraint — the state of being bound by passions and careless conduct) while simultaneously turning toward *saṃyama* (restraint — the disciplined life of a spiritual seeker). This twofold approach forms the foundation for the entire chapter on austerity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The spiritual path requires two simultaneous efforts — stopping harmful behavior while actively doing what is right.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Am I only trying to stop bad habits, or am I also actively cultivating good ones? True growth requires both.

## SUTRA 30.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रागदोसे य दो पावे, पावकम्म-पवत्तणे ।
जे भिक्खू रुभइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.२॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Rāgadose ya do pāve, pāvakamma-pavattaṇe |
Je bhikkhū rubhai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Attachment and aversion are the two sinful causes that lead to sinful karma; the monk who constantly delights in them does not remain in the circle of the righteous.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **rāgadose** — attachment and aversion (rāga and dveṣa)
- **do** — two
- **pāve** — sinful / unwholesome (pāpa)
- **pāvakamma-pavattaṇe** — in the engagement of sinful karma
- **je** — which / whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk / mendicant
- **rubhai** — delights in / is attracted to
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly / always
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain / does not stay
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle / in the assembly (of the righteous)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having established the twofold principle, this sutra identifies the root causes that prevent spiritual progress: *rāga* (attachment) and *dveṣa* (aversion). These are the two fundamental passions (*pāva* — sinful forces) that drive the accumulation of sinful karma. A monk who constantly delights in (*rubhai*) these two passions — who feeds attachment and aversion rather than starving them through austerity — cannot remain in the *maṃḍala* (the sacred circle of the righteous community). The word *maṃḍala* here signifies not just a physical assembly but the spiritual orbit of those progressing toward liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Attachment and hatred are the two root causes of bad karma — a person who keeps indulging in them falls away from the path of righteousness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the two poisons — attachment or aversion — has a stronger grip on my daily actions?

## SUTRA 30.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रागदोसे य दो पावे, पावकम्म-पवत्तणे ।
जे भिक्खू वज्जइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.३॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Rāgadose ya do pāve, pāvakamma-pavattaṇe |
Je bhikkhū vajjai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Attachment and aversion are the two sinful causes of sinful karma; the monk who constantly avoids them — he does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **rāgadose** — attachment and aversion
- **do** — two
- **pāve** — sinful
- **pāvakamma-pavattaṇe** — in the engagement of sinful karma
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **vajjai** — avoids / abandons (varjayati)
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle (of saṃsāra)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is a deliberate counterpart to the previous one, using almost identical language but with one crucial change: where Sutra 2 said *rubhai* (delights in), this one says *vajjai* (avoids/abandons). The previous sutra warned that indulging in rāga and dveṣa keeps one trapped; this sutra promises that avoiding them frees one from the cycle. The *maṃḍala* here shifts meaning — in Sutra 2, the monk who delights in passions falls out of the circle of the righteous; here, the monk who avoids passions escapes the circle of saṃsāra. The pairing of these two sutras mirrors the opening sutra's twin principle of virati and pravartana.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who constantly avoids attachment and hatred breaks free from the cycle of rebirth — he does not remain trapped in worldly existence.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If attachment and aversion are the only two chains binding me to suffering, what would my life look like if I loosened just one of them?

## SUTRA 30.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दंडाणं गारवाणं च, सल्लाणं च तियं तियं ।
जे भिक्खू चयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.४॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Daṃḍāṇaṃ gāravāṇaṃ ca, sallāṇaṃ ca tiyaṃ tiyaṃ |
Je bhikkhū cayai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The three daṇḍas (instruments of sin), three gāravas (attachments to pride), and three śalyas (thorns) — the monk who constantly renounces these does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **daṃḍāṇaṃ** — of the daṇḍas (three instruments of sinful activity: mind, speech, body)
- **gāravāṇaṃ** — of the gāravas (three kinds of worldly attachment/pride)
- **ca** — and
- **sallāṇaṃ** — of the śalyas (three thorns: māyā/deceit, mithyātva/wrong belief, nidāna/worldly desire)
- **tiyaṃ tiyaṃ** — three and three (each group being threefold)
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **cayai** — renounces / abandons (tyajati)
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra identifies nine specific obstacles to liberation, organized in three groups of three. The *three daṇḍas* are the instruments of sinful activity through mind, speech, and body — these are the channels through which karma is accumulated. The *three gāravas* are attachments to pride or worldly regard — these keep the soul entangled in worldly concerns. The *three śalyas* (thorns) are māyā (deceit), mithyātva (wrong belief), and nidāna (desire for worldly reward) — these are deeply embedded impurities that prevent spiritual progress even when external conduct appears pure. A monk who systematically abandons all nine of these escapes the cycle of rebirth.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: There are nine specific spiritual obstacles — three harmful actions (of mind, speech, and body), three worldly attachments, and three inner thorns (deceit, wrong belief, and selfish desire). Letting go of all nine leads to freedom.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the three "thorns" — deceit, wrong belief, and desire for reward — which one is most deeply embedded in me, and how does it sabotage my spiritual effort?

## SUTRA 30.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दिव्वे य जे उवसग्गे, तहा तेरिच्छमाणुसे ।
जे भिक्खू सहइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.५॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Divve ya je uvasagge, tahā tericchāmāṇuse |
Je bhikkhū sahai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The afflictions that are divine, and likewise those from animals and humans — the monk who constantly endures them does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **divve** — divine / celestial (upasarga caused by devas)
- **ya** — and
- **je** — which
- **uvasagge** — afflictions / obstacles (upasarga)
- **tahā** — likewise / similarly
- **tericchāmāṇuse** — from animals and humans (tiryañc and manuṣya)
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **sahai** — endures / bears (sahate)
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra addresses the inevitability of external obstacles (*upasarga*) on the spiritual path and the importance of endurance. Three sources of affliction are identified: *divya* (divine — obstacles created by celestial beings testing the aspirant), *tairyagyona* (from animals — physical dangers and disturbances from creatures), and *mānuṣa* (from humans — persecution, ridicule, or physical harm from people). The key word is *sahai* (endures) — the monk does not merely avoid these afflictions but bears them with equanimity. This endurance is itself a form of tapas (austerity) that burns away karma. One who can maintain spiritual composure through all three categories of external affliction transcends saṃsāra.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who patiently endures all kinds of hardships — whether caused by supernatural forces, animals, or other people — escapes the cycle of suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When difficulties come from unexpected sources — nature, others, or life itself — do I see them as obstacles to my practice, or as the practice itself?

## SUTRA 30.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विगहा-कसाय-सण्णाणं, झाणाणं च दुयं तहा ।
जे भिक्खू वज्जइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.६॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Vigahā-kasāya-saṇṇāṇaṃ, jhāṇāṇaṃ ca duyaṃ tahā |
Je bhikkhū vajjai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The harmful types of speech, passions, perceptions, and the two harmful kinds of meditation — the monk who constantly avoids these does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vigahā** — harmful speech / disputatious talk (vigṛhyā)
- **kasāya** — passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed)
- **saṇṇāṇaṃ** — perceptions / cognitions (saṃjñā)
- **jhāṇāṇaṃ** — meditations (dhyāna)
- **ca** — and
- **duyaṃ** — two (the two harmful types of dhyāna)
- **tahā** — likewise
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **vajjai** — avoids
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra catalogs the internal enemies that must be abandoned. *Vigahā* (disputatious speech) refers to argumentative or contentious talk that inflames ego and attachment. *Kasāya* refers to the four fundamental passions — krodha (anger), māna (pride), māyā (deceit), and lobha (greed) — which color the soul's perception and generate karma. *Saṇṇā* (perceptions) here refers to harmful categories of cognition such as āhāra-saṃjñā (food-consciousness) and others that keep the mind attached to worldly concerns. The *two harmful dhyānas* are ārta-dhyāna (painful meditation born of suffering) and raudra-dhyāna (cruel meditation born of violence) — as opposed to the beneficial dharma-dhyāna and śukla-dhyāna. Avoiding all of these is essential for liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk must avoid harmful speech, the four destructive passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed), worldly perceptions, and two types of harmful meditation — these are the inner enemies that keep one trapped.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I sit in quiet reflection, does my mind gravitate toward peaceful contemplation or toward worried, angry, or scheming thoughts?

## SUTRA 30.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वएसु-इंदियत्थेसु, समिईसु किरियासु य ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.७॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Vaesu-iṃdiyatthesu, samiīsu kiriyāsu ya |
Je bhikkhū jayai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the five mahāvratas, the control of the five senses, the five samitis, and the three guptis — the monk who is constantly victorious does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vaesu** — in the vratas (vows / the five mahāvratas)
- **iṃdiyatthesu** — in sense objects / control of the five senses (indriya-artha)
- **samiīsu** — in the samitis (five types of careful conduct)
- **kiriyāsu** — in activities / the three guptis (restraints of mind, speech, body)
- **ya** — and
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **jayai** — conquers / is victorious (jayati)
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

After describing what must be abandoned (Sutras 2-6), this sutra turns to what must be mastered. The four domains of spiritual victory are enumerated: the *five mahāvratas* (great vows — ahiṃsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, aparigraha), the *five indriya-arthas* (mastery over the five sense objects — touch, taste, smell, sight, sound), the *five samitis* (careful conduct in walking, speaking, eating, placing objects, and disposing waste), and the *three guptis* (restraints of mind, speech, and body). Together these form the complete framework of monastic discipline. The word *jayai* (conquers) is significant — these are not merely followed but conquered, suggesting active spiritual warfare against one's own tendencies.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who masters the five great vows, controls the five senses, practices careful conduct in all activities, and restrains mind, speech, and body — such a person conquers the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In which area of spiritual discipline am I strongest, and in which am I most careless? True victory requires mastery across all fronts.

## SUTRA 30.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लेसासु छसु काएसु, छक्के आहार-कारणे ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.८॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Lesāsu chasu kāesu, chakke āhāra-kāraṇe |
Je bhikkhū jayai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the six leśyās, the six types of bodies, and the six reasons for food-gathering — the monk who is constantly victorious does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **lesāsu** — in the leśyās (six colorations of the soul reflecting karmic states)
- **chasu** — six
- **kāesu** — in the kāyas (six types of bodies: earth, water, fire, air, plant, mobile beings)
- **chakke** — six
- **āhāra-kāraṇe** — in the reasons/occasions for food-gathering
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **jayai** — conquers
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra addresses three additional domains of mastery, each grouped in sixes. The *six leśyās* are the soul-colorations that reflect one's karmic state — from the darkest kṛṣṇa-leśyā (black, indicating cruelty) through nīla (blue), kāpota (grey), tejas (fiery/yellow), padma (lotus/pink), to the brightest śukla-leśyā (white, indicating purity). A monk must understand and progressively purify these. The *six kāyas* represent the six types of living beings categorized by their bodies — understanding this deepens one's commitment to ahiṃsā. The *six reasons for āhāra* (food-gathering) refer to the proper occasions and conditions under which a monk may accept food, ensuring that even the basic act of eating is governed by spiritual discipline.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk must also understand and master the six soul-colorations (which reveal one's spiritual state), show care for all six types of living beings, and follow strict rules even about when and how to accept food.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I pay attention to the "coloration" of my inner state — the quality of my thoughts and intentions — or only to my outward actions?

## SUTRA 30.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पिंडोग्गह-पडिमासु, भिक्खू धम्मम्मि दसविहे ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.९॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Piṃḍoggaha-paḍimāsu, bhikkhū dhammammi dasavihe |
Je bhikkhū jayai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the rules of alms-collection, the progressive stages of spiritual practice, and the tenfold dharma — the monk who is constantly victorious does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **piṃḍoggaha** — piṇḍa-grahaṇa; alms-collection (the rules governing how monks receive food)
- **paḍimāsu** — in the pratimās (progressive stages/milestones of monastic practice)
- **bhikkhū** — for a monk
- **dhammammi** — in dharma / in righteousness
- **dasavihe** — tenfold (the ten aspects of dharma)
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **jayai** — conquers
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra completes the enumeration of spiritual domains by adding three more areas of mastery. *Piṇḍograha* (alms-collection) has its own elaborate rules ensuring that the monk's sustenance is obtained without himsa, attachment, or obligation. The *pratimās* are progressive stages of spiritual achievement that serve as milestones on the monastic path. The *tenfold dharma* (daśa-dharma) consists of: uttama kṣamā (supreme forgiveness), uttama mārdava (supreme humility), uttama ārjava (supreme straightforwardness), uttama śauca (supreme purity), uttama satya (supreme truthfulness), uttama saṃyama (supreme self-restraint), uttama tapas (supreme austerity), uttama tyāga (supreme renunciation), uttama ākiñcanya (supreme non-possessiveness), and uttama brahmacarya (supreme celibacy). Together with previous sutras, this covers the entire landscape of monastic spiritual practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk must also master the rules of collecting food, progress through the stages of spiritual practice, and embody the ten supreme virtues — including forgiveness, humility, honesty, and self-restraint.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the ten supreme virtues — forgiveness, humility, straightforwardness, purity, truthfulness, self-restraint, austerity, renunciation, non-possessiveness, and celibacy — which one do I most need to cultivate right now?

## SUTRA 30.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मएसु बंभगुत्तीसु, संजमम्मि जयइ य ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३०.१०॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Maesu baṃbhaguttīsu, saṃjamammi jayai ya |
Je bhikkhū jayai ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchai maṃḍale || 30.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the eight primary qualities of conduct, the nine types of brahmacarya fences, and in overall restraint — the monk who is constantly victorious does not remain in the circle of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **maesu** — in the mūla-guṇas (fundamental/primary qualities of conduct)
- **baṃbhaguttīsu** — in the brahmacarya-guptis (the nine fences/protections of celibacy)
- **saṃjamammi** — in saṃyama (overall restraint and self-discipline)
- **jayai** — conquers
- **ya** — and
- **je** — whoever
- **bhikkhū** — monk
- **jayai** — conquers
- **ṇiccaṃ** — constantly
- **se** — he
- **ṇa acchai** — does not remain
- **maṃḍale** — in the circle of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra highlights three additional pillars of spiritual mastery. The *mūla-guṇas* (fundamental qualities) are the eight basic qualities that form the foundation of monastic conduct — they represent the irreducible minimum of spiritual practice. The *nine brahmacarya-guptis* (fences of celibacy) are specific safeguards that protect the vow of brahmacarya, addressing mental, verbal, and physical dimensions of sexual restraint. Together with *saṃyama* (the overarching principle of self-restraint), these complete the comprehensive framework of spiritual discipline described across Sutras 7-10. It is tenfold: (1) *ālocanā* — confessing one's faults to the guru; (2) *pratikramaṇa* — formal repentance through prescribed rituals; (3) *tadubhaya* — combining confession and repentance; (4) *viveka* — discernment and separation from the cause of the fault; (5) *vyutsarga* — temporary renunciation as penance; (6) *tapas* — specific austerities assigned as penance; (7) *cheda* — reduction in monastic seniority; (8) *mūla* — complete restart of monastic initiation; (9) *anavashthāpyā* — temporary expulsion from the order; (10) *pārāñcikā* — permanent expulsion for irredeemable offenses. The purpose of all ten is *bhāva-pariśuddhi* — purification of inner disposition — not punishment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Expiation (prāyaścitta) is a tenfold process starting with honest confession and extending to serious consequences for grave faults. The goal is always inner purification — genuinely removing the stain of wrongdoing, not just performing a ritual.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I make mistakes, do I genuinely confess and work to purify the root cause, or do I simply try to move on without addressing what went wrong?
