# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 31
# Caraṇavidhi (The Rule of Right Conduct)

**Chapter 31 of the Uttaradhyayana Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Volume 2, printed pages 253–274 (PDF pages 307–328)*
*Total Sutras: 21 (covering 33 topics/bols of practice)*

---

## SUTRA 31.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चरणविहिं पवक्खामि, जीवस्स उ सुहावहं ।
जं चरित्ता बहू जीवा, तिण्णा संसार-सागरं ॥३१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

caraṇavihiṃ pavakkhāmi, jīvassa u suhāvahaṃ |
jaṃ carittā bahū jīvā, tiṇṇā saṃsāra-sāgaraṃ || 31.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I shall declare the rule of right conduct, which is beneficial to the soul — by following which many beings have crossed the ocean of worldly existence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **caraṇavihiṃ** = the rule/method of conduct (caraṇavidhi)
- **pavakkhāmi** = I shall declare, I shall describe
- **u** = indeed, verily
- **jīvassa** = for the soul/living being
- **suhāvahaṃ** = beneficial, welfare-bringing, bliss-giving
- **jaṃ** = which
- **carittā** = having practised, having followed
- **bahū** = many
- **jīvā** = souls, living beings
- **tiṇṇā** = have crossed (past participle)
- **saṃsāra-sāgaraṃ** = the ocean of worldly existence (saṃsāra)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening verse is the manifesto of the entire chapter. Caraṇavidhi means the disciplined, discerning practice of right conduct — the practical expression of cāritra, the third jewel. The text opens by establishing its purpose: to describe a conduct so powerful that many souls (bahū jīvā) have already used it to cross the ocean of saṃsāra — the endless cycle of births, deaths, and rebirths. The word "many" is significant: this is not speculation but a proven path, walked and confirmed. The chapter will enumerate 33 specific domains of practice across 21 gāthas, forming a complete curriculum for liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: I am going to describe the rules of right conduct — the way of living that countless souls have already used to escape the endless cycle of birth and death.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which habits in my daily life bring me closer to the shore of liberation, and which ones pull me deeper into the ocean?

## SUTRA 31.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एगओ विरइं कुज्जा, एगओ य पवत्तणं ।
असंजमे णियत्ति च, संजमे य पवत्तणं ॥३१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

egao viraïṃ kujjā, egao ya pavattaṇaṃ |
asaṃjame ṇiyatti ca, saṃjame ya pavattaṇaṃ || 31.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In one [statement]: perform withdrawal from one and engagement in the other — restrain from non-restraint, and engage in restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **egao** = in one (the first bol/topic)
- **viraïṃ** = withdrawal, cessation, abstinence
- **kujjā** = one should do, one should practise
- **egao ya** = and in one
- **pavattaṇaṃ** = engagement, activity, entry
- **asaṃjame** = from non-restraint (hiṃsā, asatya, etc.)
- **ṇiyatti** = withdrawal, restraint, stopping
- **ca** = and
- **saṃjame** = in restraint (ahiṃsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, aparigraha)
- **pavattaṇaṃ** = engagement, full entry

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the first of 33 topics (bols) that form the substance of this chapter. The most foundational teaching is a binary choice: stop what harms, start what heals. Non-restraint (asaṃyama) encompasses violence, falsehood, theft, sensual indulgence, and possessiveness — these 17 forms must be identified and stopped. Restraint (saṃyama) — the five mahāvratas and their 17 corresponding expressions — must be fully embraced. This one principle, applied to all 33 domains, is the engine of liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The first rule of right conduct is stop doing harmful things (hurting, lying, grasping) and start doing good ones (non-violence, truthfulness, restraint).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In which area of my life am I still engaging in the harmful when I already know the good?

## SUTRA 31.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

रागदोसे य दो पावे, पावकम्म-पवत्तणे ।
जे भिक्खू रुंभइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

rāgadose ya do pāve, pāvakamma-pavattaṇe |
je bhikkhū ruṃbhaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Attachment and aversion are the two sins that cause sinful karma to arise — the monk who always restrains these does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **rāgadose** = rāga (attachment) and dosa (aversion/hatred)
- **ya** = and
- **do** = two
- **pāve** = sins, evil roots
- **pāvakamma** = sinful karma
- **pavattaṇe** = that which causes, that which sets in motion
- **je** = the one who
- **bhikkhū** = monk, seeker
- **ruṃbhaï** = restrains, blocks, stops
- **ṇiccaṃ** = always, constantly
- **se** = that (monk)
- **ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale** = does not wander in the cycle (of saṃsāra)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The two root sins are rāga (attachment) and dveṣa (aversion). These are not merely emotions — they are the seeds from which all karma grows. Just as a tree with a living root can always sprout again, a soul entangled in rāga-dveṣa will continuously generate new karma. The monk who cuts these two at their root stops all new karmic accumulation and begins the journey to liberation. "Does not wander in the cycle" (ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale) is the refrain of this entire chapter — a vivid image of breaking free from the circular trap of saṃsāra.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Attachment and hatred are the two root causes of all bad karma — a monk who constantly checks these two will escape the endless cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where do I notice rāga and dveṣa arising most in me — what do I cling to, and what do I push away?

## SUTRA 31.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दंडाणं गारवाणं च, सल्लाणं च तियं तियं ।
जे भिक्खू चयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

daṃḍāṇaṃ gāravāṇaṃ ca, sallāṇaṃ ca tiyaṃ tiyaṃ |
je bhikkhū cayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The triple of punishments (daṇḍas), the triple of arrogances (gārvas), and the triple of thorns (śalyas) — the monk who always abandons these triples does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **daṃḍāṇaṃ** = of the three daṇḍas (harmful acts of mind, speech, body)
- **gāravāṇaṃ** = of the three gārvas (arrogances: ṛddhi, rasa, śātā)
- **sallāṇaṃ** = of the three śalyas (thorns: māyā, nidāna, mithyātva)
- **ca ... ca** = and ... and
- **tiyaṃ tiyaṃ** = triple by triple, three each
- **cayaï** = abandons, gives up
- **ṇiccaṃ** = always, ever

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three triads of inner poison are identified here. The three daṇḍas are harmful activities of mind, speech, and body — the very instruments through which karma is generated. The three gārvas are: ṛddhi-gārva (pride in worldly achievement), rasa-gārva (pride in pleasurable food), and śātā-gārva (pride in physical comfort). The three śalyas are the thorns lodged deep within the soul: māyā-śalya (inner deceit and cunning), nidāna-śalya (desire for future rewards even from austerity), and mithyātva-śalya (clinging to false belief). Until these nine are removed, liberation is impossible regardless of outward practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who gives up three types of harmful actions, three types of arrogance, and three types of inner thorns (hidden desires and false beliefs) will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the three śalyas — inner deceit, secret desire for reward, or wrong belief — do I carry most deeply?

## SUTRA 31.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दिव्वे य जे उवसग्गे, तहा तेरिच्छमाणुसे ।
जे भिक्खू सहइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

divve ya je uvasagge, tahā tericchamāṇuse |
je bhikkhū sahaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The afflictions that come from divine beings, from animals, and from humans — the monk who always endures these does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **divve** = divine, from devas (celestial beings)
- **ya** = and
- **je** = which
- **uvasagge** = afflictions, adversities, attacks (upasargas)
- **tahā** = similarly, also
- **tericcha** = from animals/tiryañca (non-human creatures)
- **māṇuse** = from humans
- **sahaï** = endures, bears with equanimity
- **ṇiccaṃ** = always, ever

### 5. COMMENTARY

Afflictions (upasargas) come from three sources: from devas (celestial beings who test ascetics out of jealousy, sport, or to probe their steadiness), from tiryañca (animals that harm through fear, aggression, or territorial instinct), and from manuṣya (human beings who cause pain through ridicule, exploitation, or hostility). The true monk meets all three with samabhāva — equanimity — not as passive acceptance but as active inner strength. To endure without rāga or dveṣa is to sever the chain that binds to saṃsāra. Every affliction endured becomes a purification of past karma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Whether trouble comes from gods, animals, or people, a monk who calmly endures it all without losing inner peace will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone causes me pain today, can I endure it with equanimity rather than reacting with anger or fear?

## SUTRA 31.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विगहा-कसाय-सण्णाणं, झाणाणं च दुयं तहा ।
जे भिक्खू वज्जइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

vigahā-kasāya-saṇṇāṇaṃ, jhāṇāṇaṃ ca duyaṃ tahā |
je bhikkhū vajjaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The four vain conversations (vikathās), the four passions (kaṣāyas), the four instincts (saṃjñās), and the two harmful meditations — the monk who always avoids these does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vigahā** = the four vikathās (vain conversations): strīkathā, bhojanakathā, deśakathā, rājakathā
- **kasāya** = the four kaṣāyas (passions): krodha, māna, māyā, lobha
- **saṇṇāṇaṃ** = the four saṃjñās (instinctual drives): āhāra, bhaya, maithuna, parigraha
- **jhāṇāṇaṃ** = meditations
- **duyaṃ** = two (the two harmful ones: ārta-dhyāna and raudra-dhyāna)
- **tahā** = also
- **vajjaï** = avoids, shuns, keeps away from

### 5. COMMENTARY

Four domains of avoidance are named here. The four vikathās scatter a monk's focus: vain talk about women, food, foreign lands, and kings pulls the mind toward worldliness. The four kaṣāyas (anger, pride, deceit, greed) are the most powerful generators of karma — the Jain tradition calls them "kap" (that which binds to saṃsāra) + "āya" (that which provides it). The four saṃjñās are instinctual cravings arising from mohanīya karma: for food, out of fear, for sexual contact, and for accumulation. The two harmful dhyānas — ārta (brooding anxiety over loss and craving) and raudra (meditation of cruelty and pleasure in harm) — are to be replaced by dharma-dhyāna and śukla-dhyāna.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who avoids four types of useless talk, four inner passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed), four animal instincts, and two harmful mental states will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the four kaṣāyas — anger, pride, deceit, or greed — do I most frequently entertain without noticing?

## SUTRA 31.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वएसु-इंदियत्थेसु, समीसु किरियासु य ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

vaïsu-iṃdiyatthesu, samīsu kiriyāsu ya |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the [five] vows, in the objects of the [five] senses, in the [five] regulations (samitis), and in the [five] activities (kriyās) — the monk who always conquers does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vaïsu** = in the vows (five mahāvratas: ahiṃsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, aparigraha)
- **iṃdiyatthesu** = in the objects of the senses (five: sound, form, smell, taste, touch)
- **samīsu** = in the samitis (five regulations: īryā, bhāṣā, eṣaṇā, ādāna-nikṣepa, pariṣṭhāpanikā)
- **kiriyāsu** = in the five activities/kriyās (kāyikī, ādhikaraṇikī, prādveṣikī, pāritāpanikī, prāṇātipātikī)
- **ya** = and
- **jayaï** = wins, conquers, is vigilant

### 5. COMMENTARY

Four sets of five form this sutra. The five mahāvratas are the foundation of monastic life — they must be upheld completely, not just in gross action but in mind and speech as well. The five sense-objects (sound, form, smell, taste, touch) are to be approached with equanimity, with neither attraction nor aversion. The five samitis are the five mindful regulations governing every movement of the monk's daily life — walking, speaking, begging, handling objects, and disposing of waste. The five kriyās are the categories of activity that bind karma. The monk who is "victorious" (jayaï) in all four sets is free from saṃsāra's pull.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who carefully follows five main vows, controls his five senses, follows five rules of mindful action, and avoids five types of harmful activity will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Among the five senses, which one pulls my attention most strongly, and how do I relate to its objects today?

## SUTRA 31.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लेसासु छसु काएसु, छक्के आहार कारणे ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

lesāsu chasu kāesu, chakke āhāra kāraṇe |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the six leśyās, among the six kāyas (categories of beings), and in the six causes of taking or abandoning food — the monk who always wins does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **lesāsu** = among the leśyās (soul-colorings/psychic states)
- **chasu** = six (kṛṣṇa, nīla, kāpota, tejo, padma, śukla)
- **kāesu** = among the kāyas (six categories of embodied beings)
- **chakke** = six
- **āhāra** = food (its taking and abandoning)
- **kāraṇe** = causes, reasons (six each)
- **jayaï** = is vigilant, wins

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three sets of six form this sutra. The six leśyās are states of the soul's coloring ranging from the black (kṛṣṇa — most impure, most passionate) to the white (śukla — completely pure). The lower three (kṛṣṇa, nīla, kāpota) are to be abandoned; the higher three (tejo, padma, śukla) cultivated. The six kāyas — earth-bodied, water-bodied, fire-bodied, air-bodied, plant-bodied, and mobile (trasa) beings — all have life and deserve protection. The six reasons for eating and six reasons for not eating (detailed in Chapter 26, Samācārī) govern the monk's mindful relationship with food, ensuring all consumption is purposeful and disciplined.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who cultivates a clear inner state, shows compassion toward all six types of living beings, and eats only for the right reasons will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the current coloring of my soul — closer to dark and restless, or bright and calm?

## SUTRA 31.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पिंडोग्गह-पडिमासु, भयट्ठाणेसु सत्तसु ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

piṃḍoggaha-paḍimāsu, bhayaṭṭhāṇesu sattasu |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the seven food-vows and the seven dwelling-vows (piṇḍa and avagraha pratimās), and in the seven stations of fear — the monk who always wins does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **piṃḍoggaha** = piṇḍa (food-related) + uggaha/avagraha (dwelling-related)
- **paḍimāsu** = in the pratimās (specific vows/commitments), seven each
- **bhayaṭṭhāṇesu** = in the stations/sources of fear
- **sattasu** = seven (each group)
- **jayaï** = wins, is vigilant, conquers

### 5. COMMENTARY

Two sets of seven cover the external disciplines, plus a third set of seven addresses the inner life. The seven piṇḍa-pratimās govern how food is sought, accepted, and consumed. The seven avagraha-pratimās govern a monk's use of dwelling places — specific forms of permission and limitation that protect non-possessiveness. The seven bhaya-sthānas are the seven stations of fear: fear of this life, the next, of livelihood loss, of illness, of death, of disrepute, and of sudden calamity. A monk grounded in these 21 specifics is free from the twin tyrannies of hunger and fear.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who follows seven rules about accepting food, seven rules about dwelling places, and who has overcome seven types of fear will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which fear holds the most power over my daily choices — the fear of loss, of pain, or of what others think of me?

## SUTRA 31.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मएसु बंभगुत्तीसु, भिक्खू-धम्मिम्म दसविहे ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

maïsu baṃbhaguttīsu, bhikkhū-dhammimma dasavihe |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the eight intoxications (madas), in the nine guardianships of celibacy (brahmacarya-guptis), and in the ten-fold monk's dharma — the monk who always wins does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **maïsu** = in the madas (forms of pride/intoxication): jāti, kula, bala, rūpa, tapa, śruta, lābha, aiśvarya (eight)
- **baṃbhaguttīsu** = in the brahmacarya-guptis (nine protective practices of celibacy)
- **bhikkhū-dhammimma** = in the bhikṣu-dharma (the monk's code/religion)
- **dasavihe** = ten-fold (ten qualities: kṣamā, mukti/nirlobhatā, ārjava, mārdava, laghu, satya, saṃyama, tapa, tyāga, brahmacarya)
- **jayaï** = wins, conquers, masters

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three more domains: the eight madas (intoxications of pride in birth, family, strength, beauty, austerity, learning, wealth, and power) arise when the māna-mohanīya karma asserts itself. They are to be abandoned for humility, softness, and simplicity. The nine brahmacarya-guptis are specific practices that protect celibacy at every level of interaction — guarding the senses, the body, the speech. The ten qualities of the bhikṣu-dharma (forgiveness, freedom from greed, straightforwardness, gentleness, lightness, truthfulness, self-restraint, austerity, renunciation, and celibacy) are the complete portrait of monastic virtue.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who gives up eight types of pride, follows nine rules to protect celibacy, and practises ten qualities of monkhood will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the eight madas — pride in my birth, intelligence, looks, or spiritual achievements — do I carry most subtly?

## SUTRA 31.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उवासग-पडिमासु, भिक्खूणं पडिमासु य ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

uvāsaga-paḍimāsu, bhikkhūṇaṃ paḍimāsu ya |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the eleven pratimās of the upāsaka (lay devotee) and the twelve pratimās of the monks — the monk who always wins does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **uvāsaga** = upāsaka, the lay disciple (śrāvaka)
- **paḍimāsu** = in the pratimās (stages of observance/vows)
- **bhikkhūṇaṃ** = of the monks
- **paḍimāsu ya** = and in the pratimās (twelve stages for monks)
- **jayaï** = is vigilant about, is conversant with

### 5. COMMENTARY

The eleven pratimās of the upāsaka (lay follower) range from basic samyaktva through increasingly demanding vows up to near-full renunciation — a complete graduated path for householders. The twelve pratimās of the monk are stages of intensifying ascetic practice, beginning with basic vows and culminating in the most demanding forms of meditation and fasting. A monk who is fully conversant with — and practices — both roadmaps understands the complete Jain soteriological path from householder to perfect ascetic. This breadth of knowledge deepens his compassion for lay followers and sharpens his own commitment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A monk who understands both the eleven stages for lay followers and the twelve stages for monks sees the complete path to liberation and will escape the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a gap between the stage I claim to be at and the stage my actual daily conduct reveals?

## SUTRA 31.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

किरियासु भूयगामेसु, परमाहम्मिएसु य ।
जे भिक्खू जयइ णिच्चं, से ण अच्छइ मंडले ॥३१.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

kiriyāsu bhūyagāmesu, paramāhammiesu ya |
je bhikkhū jayaï ṇiccaṃ, se ṇa acchhaï maṃḍale || 31.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the thirteen activities (kriyās), among the fourteen categories of living beings (bhūtagrāma), and regarding the fifteen supremely demoniac beings (paramadhārmika) — the monk who always wins does not wander in the cycle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kiriyāsu** = in the thirteen kriyās (activities that generate karma)
- **bhūyagāmesu** = among the bhūtagrāma (all categories of living beings — 14 types)

The 15 paramadhārmika beings are powerful asura-devas who revel in causing suffering to ascetics; a monk maintains samabhāva (equanimity) even toward them — neither reacting with dveṣa nor seeking their favor.
