# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 14: Ishukariya (ઈષુકારીય)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary (Shri Uttaradhyayan Sutra-1)
**Chapter:** Fourteenth Adhyayana (ચૌદમું અધ્યયન)
**Total Sutras:** 53
**Story:** Six souls who shared a divine abode are reborn together in the city of Ishukar. The priest Bhrigu's two sons remember their past lives and wish to renounce. Bhrigu argues using Vedic tradition; the sons refute philosophically. Bhrigu then joins them. His wife Yasha debates, then follows. Queen Kamalavati teaches King Ishukar through metaphors. All six attain final liberation.

---

## PART I — Introduction of the Six Souls (Sutras 1–3)

---

### SUTRA 14.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

देवा भवित्ताण पुरे भवम्मि, केई चुया एगविमाणवासी ।
पुरे पुराणे उसुयारणामे, खाए समिद्धे सुरलोगरम्मे ॥१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

devā bhavittāṇa pure bhavammi, keī cuyā egavimāṇavāsī |
pure purāṇe usuyāraṇāme, khāe samiddhe suraloga-ramme || 1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having been gods in a former existence, some who fell — dwelling-together-in-one-divine-abode — were born in the ancient, renowned, prosperous city named Ishukar, as beautiful as the realm of the gods.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pure* = in a former existence | *bhavammi* = in the realm | *devā* = gods | *bhavittāṇa* = having been/become | *egavimāṇavāsī* = dwelling together in one divine vehicle (vimāna) | *keī* = some beings (six beings) | *cuyā* = having fallen, having passed away from there | *purāṇe* = ancient | *khāe* = renowned, famous | *samiddhe* = prosperous, flourishing | *suraloga-ramme* = as beautiful as the divine realm | *usuyāra-nāme* = named Ishukar | *pure* = in the city | (they were born)

**5. COMMENTARY**

In their previous existence, six souls had lived together in one divine abode (a single vimāna in the heavenly realm). Upon exhausting their divine lifespan, they were born together in the ancient, renowned, and prosperous city of Ishukar — described as beautiful as the divine realm itself. The sutra opens mid-story, establishing that these six souls are not random strangers: their joint heavenly life created a karmic bond that pulls them together again in human form. The phrase *egavimāṇavāsī* — "dwelling in one divine abode" — is the seed of the entire chapter; shared merit, shared past, and now a shared opportunity for liberation.

📷 Visual Aid: Six luminous souls sharing a single radiant divine chariot (vimāna) in the heavens, with a bustling prosperous city visible below — showing their descent from a shared divine home to a shared human birth.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Six souls who had lived together as gods in the same heavenly home were born again together in a city called Ishukar — because their past connection pulled them back to each other.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Who in your life today shares a connection so deep it feels like it came from somewhere before this birth?

### SUTRA 14.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सकम्मसेसेण पुराकएणं, कुलेसुदग्गेसु य ते पसूया ।
णिव्विण्णसंसारभया जहाय, जिणिंदमग्गं सरणं पवण्णा ॥२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sakammasesen̥a purākaen̥aṃ, kulesu-dagghesu ya te pasūyā |
ṇivviṇṇa-saṃsāra-bhayā jahāya, jiṇinda-maggaṃ saraṇaṃ pavaṇṇā || 2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

By the fruit of their remaining previously-done karma, they were born in excellent families; becoming dispassionate and fearful of samsara, abandoning (sense pleasures), they took refuge in the path of the Jina Lord.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*te* = those (six) beings | *purākaen̥aṃ* = previously done (karma) | *sakammasesen̥a* = by the residue of their remaining good karmas | *dagghesu* = excellent, superior | *kulesu* = in families | *pasūyā* = were born | *saṃsāra-bhayā* = from fear of samsara | *ṇivviṇṇa* = having become dispassionate, wearied | *jahāya* = having abandoned (sense pleasures) | *jiṇinda-maggaṃ* = the path of the Jina Lord | *saraṇaṃ* = refuge | *pavaṇṇā* = attained, took

**5. COMMENTARY**

Their birth in Ishukar was not accidental — it was the direct result of the residue of their past good karmas (puṇya). They were born in the finest families in the city. But the more significant turn is the second half of the sutra: these souls did not simply enjoy their fortunate birth. They became *ṇivviṇṇa* — dispassionate, literally "turned away from" — fearing the cycle of samsara, and took shelter in the path of the Jina. The word *saraṇa* (refuge/shelter) is the same word used in the Navkar-rooted formula of taking three refuges. Their past good karma brought them a good birth; their dispassion moved them toward liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Because of good deeds done in past lives, these six souls were born into noble families — and instead of enjoying their good fortune, they turned away from the world's cycle of suffering and chose the path of the Jina.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Good fortune in life can be used to accumulate more, or to go deeper. Which direction are you moving?

### SUTRA 14.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुमत्तमागम्म कुमार दो वि, पुरोहिओ तस्स जसा य पत्ती ।
विसालकित्ती य तहेसुयारो, रायत्थ देवी कमलावई य ॥३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

pumatta-māgamma kumāra do vi, purohio tassa jasā ya pattī |
visāla-kittī ya tahesuyāro, rāyattha devī kamalavāī ya || 3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having taken human male form — two (became) princes (sons of the priest), the priest of that (city) named Bhrigu and his wife Yasha, the greatly renowned (king) named Ishukar, the king there, and the queen Kamalavati.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pumattaṃ* = human male form | *āgamma* = having come to, having been born into | *do vi* = both two | *kumāra* = princes, young men | *purohio* = the priest (named Bhrigu) | *tassa* = his | *jasā* = named Yasha | *pattī* = wife | *visāla-kittī* = of great renown | *isuyāro* = named Ishukar | *rāyattha* = the king there | *devī* = chief queen | *kamalavāī* = named Kamalavati

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra maps all six souls to their present identities. Two became the two sons (princes) of the brahmin priest Bhrigu. Bhrigu himself is the third. His wife Yasha is the fourth. The fifth became King Ishukar — the king whose name gives the chapter its title — a man of great renown (*visāla-kittī*). The sixth became his chief queen, Kamalavati. All six completed their divine lifespan and descended together into Ishukar through the thread of their shared karmic bond. The chapter's entire drama — philosopher-sons, debating husband, reasoning wife, teaching queen, learning king — is established in this single sutra.

📷 Visual Aid: A character map showing six figures arranged in the city of Ishukar — two young men, a brahmin priest, his wife, a king on a throne, and a queen — each labeled, illustrating how the six souls from the divine abode became these six specific people.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The six souls took on six different roles in Ishukar: two became the priest's sons, one became the priest, one became his wife, one became the king, and one became the queen.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The souls who challenge us most and teach us most — could they be the ones we are most anciently connected to?

## PART II — The Sons' Dispassion and Request to Renounce (Sutras 4–7)

---

### SUTRA 14.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जाई जरा मच्चु भयाभिभूया, बहिं विहाराभिणिवट्टु चित्ता ।
संसारचक्कस्स विमोक्खहंडा, दट्टूण ते कामगुणे विरत्ता ॥४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jāī jarā maccu bhayābhibhūyā, bahiṃ vihārābhiṇivaṭṭu cittā |
saṃsāra-cakkassa vimokkha-haṇḍā, daṭṭūṇa te kāma-guṇe virattā || 4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Overwhelmed by the fear of birth, old age, and death, with their minds fixed outward toward liberation, desiring release from the wheel of samsara, having seen (the Jain monks), they (the two sons) became dispassionate toward sense pleasures.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jāī jarā maccu* = birth, old age, and death | *bhayābhibhūyā* = overwhelmed by fear | *bahiṃ vihārābhiṇivaṭṭu cittā* = with minds turned outward — toward what is beyond (liberation), fixed on the path outside samsara | *saṃsāra-cakkassa* = from the wheel of samsara | *vimokkha-haṇḍā* = desiring release, wishing to be free | *daṭṭūṇa* = having seen (the Jain monks) | *te* = they (the two sons) | *kāma-guṇe* = toward sense pleasures | *virattā* = became dispassionate, turned away

**5. COMMENTARY**

The two sons had a direct encounter with Jain monks — and this encounter awakened their *jātismraṇa-jñāna* (memory of past lives). In those past lives, they had practiced tapas and restraint. The memory returned, and with it, dispassion. Three fears are named in sequence: birth (jagged with ignorance), old age (the gradual erosion of the body), and death (the final collapse). These three together constitute the existential weight of samsara. A mind that truly feels this weight turns *outward* — not outward into the world, but outward from the world, toward liberation.

📷 Visual Aid: Two young men stopping mid-path as they encounter passing Jain monks, their expressions shifting from distraction to sudden inner recognition — the moment of awakening triggered by the sight of renunciants.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When the two sons saw Jain monks and remembered their own past lives of discipline, they were suddenly overwhelmed by the reality of birth, aging, and death — and turned completely away from worldly pleasures.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When did you last truly sit with the fact of death — not as a concept, but as something that is already in motion?

### SUTRA 14.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पियपुत्तगा दोण्हि वि माहणस्स, सकम्मसीलस्स पुरोहियस्स ।
सिरित्तु पोराणिय तत्थ जाई, तहा सुचिण्णं तव संजमं च ॥५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

piya-puttakā doṇhi vi māhaṇassa, sakamma-sīlassa purohiyassa |
siritthu porāṇiya tattha jāī, tahā sucin̥ṇaṃ tava saṃjamaṃ ca || 5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Both beloved sons of the brahmin, the duty-devoted priest — recalled the ancient births (from past lives) and likewise the tapas and restraint that had been purely practiced there.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*māhaṇassa* = of the brahmin (Bhrigu) | *sakamma-sīlassa* = devoted to his own karma and duties | *purohiyassa* = of the priest | *doṇhi vi* = both | *piya-puttakā* = beloved sons | *porāṇiya* = ancient, prior | *jāī* = births, past lives | *tattha* = there (in those lives) | *sucin̥ṇaṃ* = purely practiced, rightly conducted | *tava saṃjamaṃ ca* = tapas and restraint | *siritthu* = recalled, remembered

**5. COMMENTARY**

Sutra 5 gives us the internal mechanism of the sons' awakening: they remembered their past lives (*jātismraṇa*) and, within that remembrance, they remembered the tapas and restraint they had purely practiced in those lives. The Gujarati commentary notes that the word *sucin̥ṇam* — purely practiced — is significant: the tapas in their past lives was not for worldly gain or name, but genuinely practiced. That purity created a residue that now surfaced as memory. Their father Bhrigu is described carefully here: he is *sakamma-sīla* — devoted to his own karmic duties (Vedic rites, priestly functions). His devotion is real. But it is precisely that devotion that will become the source of his coming argument with his sons.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The two sons suddenly remembered their past lives — and in those memories, they saw themselves practicing deep tapas and restraint with sincerity. That memory became their compass.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there something within you — a pull toward stillness, discipline, or inner life — that feels older than this birth?

### SUTRA 14.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ते कामभोगेसु असज्जमाणा, माणुस्सएसु जे याविं दिव्वा ।
मोक्खाभिकंखी अभिजायसड्ढा, तातं उवागम्म इमं उदाहु ॥६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

te kāma-bhogesu asajjamāṇā, māṇussaesu je yāviṃ divvā |
mokkha-bhikaṃkhī abhijāya-saḍḍhā, tātaṃ uvāgamma imaṃ udāhu || 6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those two, unattached to sense pleasures — whether human or divine — desiring liberation and possessed of natural faith (in dharma), came to their father and spoke thus:

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*te* = those two (sons) | *māṇussaesu* = among human (pleasures) | *divvā* = divine (pleasures) | *kāma-bhogesu* = in sense pleasures | *asajjamāṇā* = not attached | *mokkha-abhikaṃkhī* = desiring liberation | *abhijāya-saḍḍhā* = born with natural faith in dharma, with inherent confidence | *tātaṃ* = to their father | *uvāgamma* = having come, approaching | *udāhu* = spoke, said

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sutra captures the sons' internal state before they speak: they are unattached to pleasures in both realms — human and divine. This is notable because human pleasures are easier to renounce than divine ones. The fact that they are unattached to *both* signals a complete turning. *Abhijāya-saḍḍhā* — "naturally faithful" or "born-faithful" — describes the sons as possessing an inherent quality of faith that did not need external instruction; it arose from within due to their past-life cultivation. With this preparation, they approach their father. Whatever follows will be a dialogue between a son's inner awakening and a father's external tradition.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The two sons, already unattached to any pleasure — human or heavenly — and already inclined toward liberation from within, walked up to their father and began to speak.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the quality of your desire for freedom — is it a reaction to suffering, or something quieter and more innate?

### SUTRA 14.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

असासयं दट्टु इमं विहारं, बहुऽंतरायं ण य दीहमाउं ।
तम्हा गिहंसि ण रई लभामो, आमंतयामो चरिस्सामु मोणं ॥७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

asāsayaṃ daṭṭhu imaṃ vihāraṃ, bahu-aṃtarāyaṃ ṇa ya dīha-māuṃ |
tammhā gihaṃsi ṇa raī labhāmo, āmaṃtayāmo carissāmu moṇaṃ || 7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Seeing this existence to be impermanent, full of many obstacles, and with a short lifespan — therefore we find no delight in the household; we ask your permission and will practice the vow of restraint (mauna)."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*imaṃ vihāraṃ* = this (human) existence | *asāsayaṃ* = impermanent, momentary | *bahu-aṃtarāyaṃ* = full of many obstacles and hindrances | *ṇa ya dīha-māuṃ* = and the lifespan is not long, it is very short | *tammhā* = therefore | *gihaṃsi* = in the household | *raī* = delight, pleasure | *ṇa labhāmo* = we do not find | *moṇaṃ* = the vow of monastic life, of restraint and silence | *carissāmu* = we will practice | *āmaṃtayāmo* = we ask your permission, we inform you

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sons' opening words to their father are a philosophical statement, not an emotional one. They identify three facts about human existence: it is impermanent (*asāsayam*), it is full of obstacles (*bahu-aṃtarāya*), and its lifespan is short (*na dīha-māum*). These three facts, when truly seen, make the household — with its pleasures and comforts — appear insufficient as a purpose. *Moṇaṃ* here does not only mean silence; it refers to the entire monastic vow — the life of inner stillness, restraint, and renunciation. The sons are not fleeing; they are asking permission with respect. *Āmaṃtayāmo* — "we inform you, we ask your blessing." The request is humble even as the resolve is firm.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons said to their father: "Life is short, uncertain, and full of difficulty — we find no real joy in household life, so please give us your permission to become monks."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you truly accepted that your life is short and uncertain — not as a cliché but as a lived fact — what would you stop postponing?

## PART III — The Priest's Arguments Against Initiation (Sutras 8–11)

---

### SUTRA 14.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अह तायगो तत्थ मुणीण तेसिं, तवस्स वाघायकरं वयासी ।
इमं वयं वेयविवओ वयंति, जहा ण होइ असुयाण लोगो ॥८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

aha tāyago tattha muṇīṇa tesim̐, tavassa vāghāya-karaṃ vayāsī |
imaṃ vayaṃ veyavivao vayaṃti, jahā ṇa hoi asuyāṇa logo || 8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Then their father, speaking words that obstructed the tapas of those aspiring monks, said: "The knowers of the Vedas say this teaching — that those without sons have no good destination."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*aha* = then, at that time | *tāyago* = their father, priest Bhrigu | *tesim̐* = those | *muṇīṇa* = aspiring monks (bhāva-munis) | *tavassa* = in their tapas and restraint | *vāghāya-karaṃ* = obstacle-creating, obstructing | *vayāsī* = said, spoke | *veyavivao* = the knowers of the Vedas, the Veda-experts | *vayaṃti* = say, teach | *asuyāṇa* = of those without sons | *logo* = good destination, superior rebirth | *ṇa hoi* = does not come about

**5. COMMENTARY**

The father's first argument is not personal — it is traditional. He invokes Vedic authority: "the Veda-knowers say." The Vedic tradition holds that a man has three debts (*ṛṇa*) — to the seers (repaid by study), to the ancestors (repaid by producing sons), and to the gods (repaid by sacrifice). Without sons, the ancestral debt is unpaid, and without that repayment, no good destination is possible after death. This is not Bhrigu being unreasonable — he is a *sakamma-sīla* priest, and his tradition teaches this. The sons will now dismantle this argument sutra by sutra, showing that the Vedic framework of protection does not hold under philosophical scrutiny.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The father tried to stop his sons by quoting Vedic tradition: "Those who die without sons have no good afterlife — this is what our scripture teaches."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When is following tradition wisdom — and when is it a way of avoiding a deeper truth?

### SUTRA 14.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अहिज्ज वेए परिवेस्स विप्पे, पुत्ते परिट्टुप्प गिहंसि जाया ।
भोच्चाण भोए सह इत्थियाहिं, आरणगा होइ मुणी पसत्था ॥९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ahijja vee parivessa vippe, putte pariṭṭuppa gihaṃsi jāyā |
bhoccāṇa bhoe saha itthiyāhiṃ, āraṇagā hoi muṇī pasatthā || 9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"O sons, study the Vedas, feed the brahmins, establish sons in the household; having enjoyed pleasures with women — (then) become a forest-dweller, then a praised monk."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jāyā* = O sons! | *vee* = the Vedas | *ahijja* = having studied | *vippe* = the brahmins | *parivessa* = having fed | *putte* = sons | *pariṭṭuppa* = having established, handed over the household to them | *gihaṃsi* = in the household | *itthiyāhiṃ saha* = together with women | *bhoccāṇa* = having enjoyed pleasures | *āraṇagā* = forest-dweller (vānaprastha) | *pasatthā* = praised, excellent | *muṇī hoi* = become a monk

**5. COMMENTARY**

Bhrigu now lays out the four-stage Vedic life-path (*āśrama-dharma*): student (*brahmacārya*), householder (*gṛhastha*), forest-dweller (*vānaprastha*), and finally monk (*sannyāsa*). His argument: you cannot skip stages. You must study, marry, produce sons, enjoy life — and only when all that is done, in old age, may you renounce. This is a coherent system. The sons' counter-argument (beginning at S12) will not dismiss this path as wrong but will show it is not *sufficient* — that Vedic study, feeding brahmins, and producing sons do not actually provide the protection that matters.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The father gave his sons a roadmap: study the Vedas first, feed brahmins, have children, enjoy life with your wife — and only in old age should you renounce. Don't skip ahead.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there wisdom in honoring each stage of life fully — or can genuine awakening happen at any age?

### SUTRA 14.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सोयिग्गणा आयगुणिधणेणं, मोहाणिला पज्जलणाहिएणं ।
संततभावं परितप्पमाणं, लालप्पमाणं बहुहा बहुं च ॥१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

soyig-gaṇā āyaguṇi-dhaṇeṇaṃ, mohāṇilā pajjalaṇā-hieṇaṃ |
saṃtata-bhāvaṃ paritappamāṇaṃ, lālappamāṇaṃ bahuhā bahuṃ ca || 10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

(The priest,) his inner grief blazing like fire fueled by the wood of his self-qualities, fanned by the wind of delusion — continuously tormented, lamenting in many ways and greatly:

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*āyaguṇi-dhaṇeṇaṃ* = with (his) self-qualities as the fuel-wood (feeding the fire) | *mohāṇilā* = by the wind of delusion | *pajjalaṇā-hieṇaṃ* = greatly inflamed, blazing fiercely | *soyig-gaṇā* = by the fire of grief | *saṃtata-bhāvaṃ* = with continuous, unbroken emotion | *paritappamāṇaṃ* = his suffering heart, tormented | *bahuhā* = in many ways | *bahuṃ ca* = very greatly | *lālappamāṇaṃ* = lamenting, crying out

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is a portrait of a father in genuine anguish. The imagery is precise: grief is the fire; his natural love for his sons is the fuel-wood; and the wind of delusion (*moha*) makes the fire blaze uncontrollably. The Jain understanding is compassionate here — Bhrigu's grief is not wrong, but it is driven by *moha* (attachment-delusion). His self-qualities, which in another context might produce tapas, here produce sorrow because they are bound to attachment. The sutra does not mock the father. It describes him truthfully. The next sutra will show him trying again — pleading, coaxing, offering alternatives.

📷 Visual Aid: A grieving priest with hands outstretched toward his two sons, his torso depicted as a fire blazing from within — love as fuel, delusion as wind — illustrating the Jain metaphor for attachment-driven suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The father was burning inside — his love for his sons, twisted by attachment, made his grief fierce and continuous. He kept pleading with them in many ways.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When love becomes possessive, it burns both the one who loves and the one who is loved. How can love be both deep and free?

### SUTRA 14.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुरोहियं तं कमसोऽणुणंतं, णिमंतयंतं च सुए धणेणं ।
जहक्कमं कामगुणेहिं चेव, कुमारगा ते पसिमक्ख वक्कं ॥११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

purohiyaṃ taṃ kamaso-aṇuṇaṃtaṃ, ṇimaṃtayaṃtaṃ ca sue dhaṇeṇaṃ |
jahakkamaṃ kāma-guṇehiṃ ceva, kumāragā te pasimakkha vakkaṃ || 11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

To that priest who was gradually pleading, repeatedly luring them with (promises of) sons and wealth, with sense pleasures step by step — the princes spoke this deliberate word:

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*taṃ purohiyaṃ* = to that priest (Bhrigu) | *kamaso* = gradually, one after another | *aṇuṇaṃtaṃ* = pleading, entreating | *sue* = sons (the prospect of having sons) | *dhaṇeṇaṃ* = wealth | *ṇimaṃtayaṃtaṃ* = inviting, luring, tempting | *jahakkamaṃ* = step by step, in order | *kāma-guṇehiṃ ceva* = with sense pleasures | *te kumāragā* = those (two) princes | *pasimakkha* = thoughtfully, deliberately | *vakkaṃ* = this word, these words (spoke)

The father has tried every approach: tradition, love, grief, promises of sons, promises of wealth, promises of pleasure — layer by layer, *kamaso* (gradually), each inducement more pressing than the last. The sons are not provoked; they are thoughtful. *Pasimakkha* — "deliberately" — signals that what follows is not impulsive. They have considered everything their father has offered and will now respond with systematic philosophical arguments, not with emotion. The teacher of this chapter is not the father or the sons alone — it is the collision between Vedic convention and the Jain philosophical framework that follows.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The father tried everything — love, promises of wealth, pleasure, family — one by one, gradually, to change his sons' minds. They listened to everything, thought carefully, and then replied.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the difference between someone who disagrees with you out of stubbornness — and someone who has genuinely considered your argument and still says no?

## PART IV — The Sons' Philosophical Refutation (Sutras 12–15)

### SUTRA 14.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जाया य पुत्ता ण हवंति ताणं, को णाम ते अणुमण्णेज्ज एयं ॥१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

veyā ahīyā ṇa havaṃti tāṇaṃ, bhuttā diyā ṇiṃti tamaṃ tameṇaṃ |
jāyā ya puttā ṇa havaṃti tāṇaṃ, ko ṇāma te aṇumaṇṇejja eyaṃ || 12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"The Vedas, merely by being studied, do not become one's protection; the days enjoyed (by brahmins fed) lead only deeper into darkness; sons born are no protection — what wise person, then, would accept your statement?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*veyā* = the Vedas | *ahīyā* = merely by being studied | *tāṇaṃ* = protection, refuge | *ṇa havaṃti* = do not become | *diyā* = the (brahmin's fed) days/periods | *bhuttā* = having been fed/enjoyed | *tamaṃ tameṇaṃ* = into deeper and deeper darkness (greater ignorance) | *ṇiṃti* = lead | *jāyā ya puttā* = sons born also | *tāṇaṃ* = refuge, protection | *ṇa havaṃti* = do not become | *te* = your | *eyaṃ* = this statement | *aṇumaṇṇejja* = would accept, agree with | *ko ṇāma* = what wise person?

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sons launch their philosophical counterattack. They take each of the father's three arguments and invert them. (1) Vedic study: Learning the Vedas does not produce refuge — the real protection comes from *dharmic conduct*, not scholarly knowledge. The Vedic tradition itself says: *"śilpamadhyayanaṃ nāma, vṛttaṃ brāhmaṇa-lakṣaṇam"* — "study is the skill, but conduct is the mark of a brahmin." (2) Feeding brahmins: The commentary notes sharply that brahmins who accept food while engaging in himsa (animal sacrifice) are not elevating others — they are leading them deeper into *tama* (darkness). (3) Sons: A son cannot prevent anyone's fall into adharma or bad destinies. The question "what wise person would accept this?" is not disrespectful — it is a genuine philosophical challenge.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons replied: "Study of scriptures doesn't protect you. Feeding priests doesn't protect you. Having sons doesn't protect you. Who with real understanding would accept your arguments?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you trust as your protection — knowledge, family, social standing? Which of these has proven itself at the level of what really matters?

### SUTRA 14.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

संसारमोक्खस्स विपक्खभूया, खाणी अणत्थाण उ कामभोगा ॥१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Sense pleasures give momentary happiness and long-duration suffering; they are replete with pain and scarce in joy; opposed to liberation from samsara — sense pleasures are truly a mine of evils."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*khaṇimitta-sukhkhā* = giving happiness only for a moment | *bahukāla-dukhkhā* = giving suffering for a long time | *aṇigāma-sukhkhā* = in which happiness is very small/scarce | *pagāma-dukhkhā* = but suffering is very great and abundant | *saṃsāra-mokkkhassa* = for liberation from samsara | *vipakkha-bhūyā* = being an opponent, like an enemy | *aṇatthāṇa* = of evils, of meaningless harms | *u* = indeed, truly | *kāma-bhogā* = sense pleasures | *khāṇī* = a mine, a source

Having dismantled the father's positive arguments (protection through Vedas, feeding, and sons), the sons now turn to the nature of sense pleasures themselves. **6. SIMPLY PUT**

Sense pleasures give you a moment of happiness but a long stretch of suffering. They give little joy and much pain. They are not just useless — they actively work against your liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Look at a pleasure you regularly seek. What is the ratio of the happiness it gives to the suffering it costs — before, during, and after?

### SUTRA 14.14

All that effort, and still the destination is the same: death.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A person who never lets go of desires wanders through life burning day and night, always chasing wealth — and at the end, just reaches old age and death with nothing to show for it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are you circling in your life — returning to again and again without arriving anywhere? What would it take to stop circling?

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

While a person is busy thinking "this is mine, that is not mine, I must do this, I don't need to do that" — day and night are quietly stealing their life. How can anyone afford to be lazy about dharma in that situation?

How much of your mental life is occupied with "mine / not mine / must do / need not do" — and how much of it is occupied with what actually matters?

### SUTRA 14.17

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धणेण किं धम्मधुराहिगारे, सयणेण वा कामगुणेहिं चेव ।
समणा भविस्सामु गुणोहधारी, बहिंविहारा अभिगम्म भिक्खं ॥१७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhaṇeṇa kiṃ dhamma-dhurā-higāre, sayaṇeṇa vā kāma-guṇehiṃ ceva |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"What is the use of wealth for one charged with the burden of dharma? Or of kinsmen? Or of sense pleasures? We will become shramanas, bearers of virtues, wandering outside, subsisting on alms."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*dhamma-dhurā-higāre* = in the charge of the burden of dharma, in the matter of dharmic conduct | *dhaṇeṇa kiṃ* = what is the use of wealth? | *sayaṇeṇa vā* = or of kinsmen? | *kāma-guṇehiṃ ceva* = or of sense pleasures? | *guṇoha-dhārī* = bearers of the stream of virtues (right perception, knowledge, conduct) | *samaṇā* = shramanas (Jain monks) | *bhavissāmu* = we will become | *bahiṃ-vihārā* = wandering from village to village without attachment | *abhigamma* = practicing, going | *bhikkhaṃ* = living by pure begging (alms)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sons' answer is not defensive — it reframes the question entirely. When one is carrying the burden of dharma — meaning when one is seriously committed to the path of liberation — wealth, kinsmen, and sense pleasures are not benefits. They are weights. The metaphor *dhura* (a yoke or burden) applied to dharma is significant: dharma is not a light hobby. It is a full-weight responsibility. *Guṇoha-dhārī* — bearers of the stream of virtues — describes what they intend to become: carriers of right perception, right knowledge, and right conduct. The Vedic tradition's Upaniṣadic voice itself says: *"na prajayā na dhanena, tyāgenaikena amṛtatvam āṃśuḥ"* — "not through sons, not through wealth — only through renunciation does one reach immortality."

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons replied: "When your purpose is dharma and liberation, wealth, family, and pleasure are not assets — they're extra weight. We will become monks, carry virtues, wander freely, and live on alms."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What in your life — though genuinely valuable — is also weight on the path toward what you most deeply want to be?

### SUTRA 14.18

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जहा य अग्गी अरणी असंतो, खीरे घयं तेल्ल महातिलेसु ।
एमेव जाया सरीरंसि सत्ता, सम्मुच्छइ णासइ णाविचिट्ठे ॥१८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jahā ya aggī araṇī asaṃto, khīre ghayaṃ tella mahāti-lesu |
emeva jāyā sarīraṃsi sattā, sammucchai ṇāsai ṇāviciṭṭhe || 18 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Just as fire (exists latently) in the arani wood though not visible, as ghee in milk, as oil in sesame seeds — so the soul arises in the body, perishes (with it), and does not remain afterward."

⚠️ **Note:** This is the father Bhrigu's argument — a materialist-Vedic position denying the soul's independent persistence. The sons refute this in Sutra 19. This sutra does NOT represent the Jain view.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jāyā* = O sons! | *araṇī* = arani (fire-making) wood | *aggī* = fire | *asaṃto* = though not visible directly, arising only when conditions meet | *khīre* = in milk | *ghayaṃ* = ghee | *mahāti-lesu* = in sesame seeds | *tella* = oil | *emeva* = in this same way | *sarīraṃsi* = in this body | *sattā* = the soul/being | *sammucchai* = arises spontaneously from the combination of elements | *ṇāsai* = perishes when the body perishes | *ṇāviciṭṭhe* = does not remain afterward

**5. COMMENTARY**

Bhrigu now makes his most philosophical argument: the soul is like fire in wood or ghee in milk — a product of material conditions. When the body dies, the soul-fire is extinguished. There is no persistent soul that carries karma forward. This is a form of the Vedic-materialist position (influenced by the *Lokāyata* school). The father's motivation is clear: if the soul does not persist across births, there is no karmic continuity, and therefore no philosophical basis for seeking liberation through renunciation. The sons will demolish this in S19 by turning the same logic around: precisely *because* the soul is immaterial, it is *not* subject to material causation — and therefore it *is* eternal.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The father argued: "The soul is just like fire hidden in wood or butter hidden in milk — it arises from matter and disappears when the body dies. There's nothing to save or liberate."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What do you believe the "self" actually is — a product of the body, or something that uses the body? How does that belief shape how you live?

### SUTRA 14.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अज्झत्थहेउं णियतयस्स बंधो, संसारहेउं च वयंति बंधं ॥१९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo iṃdiya-gejjha amuttabhāvā, amuttabhāvā vi ya hoi ṇicco |
ajjhatthaheūṃ ṇiyatayassa bandho, saṃsāraheūṃ ca vayaṃti bandhaṃ || 19 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Because it is immaterial, the soul is not grasped by the senses — and precisely because it is immaterial, it is also eternal. The karmic binding arises from the soul's own inner causes, and the knowers call this binding the cause of samsara."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*amuttabhāvā* = because it is immaterial, non-physical | *ṇo iṃdiya-gejjha* = not graspable by the senses | *amuttabhāvā vi* = precisely because it is immaterial | *ṇicco hoi* = it is eternal | *ṇiyatayassa* = of the soul, certainly | *bandho* = karmic binding | *ajjhatthaheūṃ* = arising from the soul's own inner dispositions (adhyavasāya) | *saṃsāraheūṃ ca* = and (this binding) is the cause of samsara | *vayaṃti* = thus the great ones say

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sons' philosophical counter-argument is elegant: the father used the analogy of fire in wood to argue that the soul is a material product and perishes with the body. The sons turn this: if the soul were material, it could be perceived by the senses. But it cannot be perceived by the senses. Therefore it is *immaterial* (*amuttabhāva*). And precisely because it is immaterial, it is also *eternal* — not subject to the arising and perishing that material things undergo. The second half of the sutra introduces the Jain mechanism of karma: the soul's bondage to karma arises from its own internal dispositions (*adhyavasāya* — inner impulses of passion, attachment, aversion). This bondage — not physical death — is the real cause of samsara. Liberation therefore requires purifying those inner dispositions, not simply biological processes.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons replied: "The soul can't be touched or seen by the senses — that's exactly why it's not material, and not material means eternal. What keeps it trapped in samsara isn't the body dying — it's the internal passions that create karma."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If the soul is eternal and its suffering comes from its own inner impulses — then what is the most important work you could do?

## PART VI — The Night Dialogue and Final Decision (Sutras 21–28)

### SUTRA 14.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अब्भाहयंमि लोगंमि, सव्वओ परिवारिए ।
अमोहाहिं पडंतीहिं, गिहंसि ण रई लभे ॥२१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

abbhāhayaṃmi logaṃmi, savvao parivārie |
amohāhiṃ paḍaṃtīhiṃ, gihaṃsi ṇa raī labhe || 21 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"In a world struck down (by death), surrounded on all sides, with inescapable (forces) falling — I find no delight in the household."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*logaṃmi* = this world | *abbhāhayaṃmi* = struck down, afflicted from all sides | *savvao* = from every direction | *parivārie* = surrounded, enclosed | *amohāhiṃ* = by inescapable forces (unblockable, inexorable) | *paḍaṃtīhiṃ* = falling, coming down | *gihaṃsi* = in the household | *raī* = delight, pleasure | *ṇa labhe* = I do not find, I have no taste for

**5. COMMENTARY**

The dialogue shifts register. What follows (S21–25) is one of the most poetically structured exchanges in the chapter — a father asking what his sons mean, the sons explaining through a precise triple metaphor. The sons begin with the conclusion: I find no delight in the household. The reason is compressed into three images: the world is struck down (*abbhāhata*), surrounded (*parivārita*), and under inescapable falling forces (*amohāhiṃ paḍaṃtīhiṃ*). These three phrases are deliberately cryptic — the father will ask what they mean in S22, and the sons will explain in S23. The structure mirrors a teaching method: state the conclusion, invite the question, then illuminate.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons said: "This world is being struck down, surrounded, and there are forces falling on it that cannot be stopped — I find no joy in just living in a house in the middle of all this."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you become truly aware of what is happening to this life — the aging, the loss, the passage — does delight in ordinary things deepen, or does it change?

### SUTRA 14.23

### SUTRA 14.25

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जा जा वच्चइ रयणी, ण सा पडिणियत्तइ ।
धम्मं च कुणमाणस्स, सफला जंति राइओ ॥२५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jā jā vaccai rayaṇī, ṇa sā paḍiṇiyattai |
dhammaṃ ca kuṇamāṇassa, saphalā jaṃti rāio || 25 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Each night that passes does not return. For the one practicing dharma, those nights pass fruitfully.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

(Same as S24, with one change:) *dhammaṃ ca kuṇamāṇassa* = for the one practicing dharma | *saphalā* = fruitful, with fruit | *rāio jaṃti* = those nights pass

**5. COMMENTARY**

The exact mirror of S24 — same structure, same universal law of irreversible time, but opposite result. For the one practicing dharma, each passing night is *saphalā* — fruitful. This does not mean productive in a worldly sense. It means the time adds to the soul's liberation rather than to its bondage. The teaching embedded in the pairing of S24 and S25 is that time itself is neutral — it is what you do within it that determines whether each irreversible unit moves you toward liberation or away from it. The question is not whether time passes (it always does), but what direction it takes you.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The same irreversible nights — if you spend them in dharma, they become fruitful. The law of time doesn't change. Only the use of it does.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Tonight when you lie down — will this day have been saphalā or aphalā? Not by achievement, but by direction.

The father, now persuaded by his sons' arguments, made a different proposal: "Let's all live together first, following the layperson's vows, and then when we're old, we'll all renounce together and become monks."

### SUTRA 14.27

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जस्सत्थि मच्चुणा सक्खं, जस्स वऽत्थि पलायणं ।
जो जाणे ण मरिस्सामि, सो हु कंखे सुए सिया ॥२७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jassatthi maccuṇā sakkhaṃ, jassa v-atthi palāyaṇaṃ |
jo jāṇe ṇa marissāmi, so hu kaṃkhe sue siyā || 27 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"He who has a compact with death (an agreement of immunity), he who has the power to flee from it, he who knows 'I will not die' — only such a one might desire (to wait and do it) in the path of sons."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jassa* = the person who | *maccuṇā* = with death | *sakkhaṃ* = a compact, a friendship, immunity | *atthi* = has | *palāyaṇaṃ* = the power to flee from death | *atthi* = has | *jo jāṇe* = who knows | *ṇa marissāmi* = I will not die | *so hu* = truly that person alone | *sue siyā* = in the path involving sons (the Vedic path) | *kaṃkhe* = might wish, might defer to

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sons' rebuttal of the father's compromise is devastating in its clarity. The logic is watertight: only someone who (a) has made a deal with death, or (b) can flee from death, or (c) genuinely knows "I will not die" — only such an impossible person could rationally defer dharma to a later stage. The three conditions the sons enumerate are ironically impossible — precisely to show that deferral is never rational. Every person alive knows, at some level, that death can come at any time. The sons simply make this explicit.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The sons replied: "You can only afford to wait until old age if you have made a deal with death, can run away from it, or know for certain you won't die. Can you honestly say any of those are true?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are you deferring — in life, in practice, in what matters — and on what assumption about the future does that deferral rest?

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

"For one bereft of sons, O Vasishtha-daughter (Yasha), there is truly no fitting dwelling in the household — the purpose is now the life of a wandering mendicant. A tree attains peace through its branches — when they are cut, that very (tree) becomes the stump."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

**5. COMMENTARY**

The priest Bhrigu now speaks to his wife Yasha. He addresses her as *vāsiṭṭi* — daughter of the Vasishtha lineage — which in ancient tradition was the highest form of respectful address to a woman of priestly family. His message: now that the sons have gone, remaining in the household has no meaning. He supports this with the tree metaphor: a tree's beauty and purpose is in its branches (children). When the branches are gone, what remains is the stump. But — crucially — the stump is not ruined; it simply takes its essential form. This is not despair. It is a reframing: without children, the householder becomes the bare essential self, which is exactly what is needed for renunciation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The priest said to his wife Yasha: "Without our sons, staying in the household makes no sense anymore. A tree's glory is in its branches — but even when the branches are gone, the stump stands. Our time as a wandering monk and nun has come."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What roles or relationships define your identity? What would remain of "you" if they were removed?

### SUTRA 14.30

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

"Like a bird here without wings, like a king in battle without soldiers, like a merchant's boat without cargo — bereft of sons, I (Bhrigu) am the same."

### SUTRA 14.33

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

भुंजाहि भोगाइं मए समाणं, दुक्खं खु भिक्खायरियाविहारो ॥३३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

mā hu tumaṃ soyariyāṇa saṃbhare, juṇṇo va haṃso paḍisotta-gāmī |
bhuṃjāhi bhogāiṃ mae samāṇaṃ, dukkhaṃ khu bhikkhāyariyā-vihāro || 33 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Do not grieve remembering your companions, like an aged swan trying to swim upstream — enjoy pleasures together with me; truly, the wandering life of a mendicant is painful."

⚠️ **Note:** This is Yasha's husband (Bhrigu) now arguing *against* full renunciation for her — urging her to stay. This appears contradictory but reflects his genuine concern: he has resolved to renounce, but is not sure Yasha can bear the hardship of mendicant life. His argument here will be answered by Yasha in S34-36.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*va* = just as | *juṇṇo* = aged, weak | *haṃso* = a swan | *paḍisotta-gāmī* = going against the current (swimming upstream against the flow) | (suffers greatly) | *mā hu tumaṃ* = do not let you (also suffer so) | *soyariyāṇa* = of your family, companions | *saṃbhare* = by remembering | (grief will come) | *mae samāṇaṃ* = together with me | *bhogāiṃ bhuṃjāhi* = enjoy these available pleasures | *khu* = truly, indeed | *bhikkhāyariyā-vihāro* = the wandering life of a mendicant, the monastic path | *dukkhaṃ* = is very painful, very difficult

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra shows the complexity of Bhrigu's love for Yasha. He has resolved to go. But he is uncertain whether she should follow. The swan metaphor is compassionate: an aged, weak swan swimming against a strong current suffers needlessly. Bhrigu fears Yasha will suffer in the austerities of mendicant life. His advice — stay and enjoy — is not hypocrisy. It is love miscalculated. Yasha's response (S34-36) will show that she has already gone beyond the need for this protective caution. She is not a weak swan. She is the rohita fish of S35 — cutting through the net to freedom.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The priest told Yasha: "Don't torture yourself missing your family — like an old swan fighting the current. Stay with me and enjoy our remaining pleasures together. The monk's life is genuinely hard."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Sometimes the people who love us most protect us from the very growth we need. How do you distinguish between protection and limitation?

### SUTRA 14.34

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एमेए जाया पयहंति भोए, ते हं कहं णाणुगमिस्समेक्को ॥३४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jahā ya bhoi taṇuyaṃ bhuyaṃgo, ṇimmoyiṇaṃ hicca palei mutto |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"O blessed one, just as a serpent sheds its thin old skin and goes, free and without looking back — in this same way these sons are abandoning pleasures; how shall I alone not follow them?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*bhoi* = O blessed one (Yasha addressing her husband) | *jahā* = just as | *bhuyaṃgo* = a serpent | *taṇuyaṃ* = its own thin, old (skin) | *ṇimmoyiṇaṃ* = the cast-off skin | *hicca* = having shed, abandoned | *mutto* = free, without attachment | *palei* = goes, moves on (without even looking back) | *emee jāyā* = in this same way, my sons | *bhoe* = pleasures | *payahaṃti* = are abandoning | *te* = with them | *ahaṃ* = I | *kahaṃ* = how | *ṇa aṇugamissam-ekkho* = shall I not follow — what shall I do remaining alone?

**5. COMMENTARY**

Yasha's answer to her husband is one of the most beautiful moments in the chapter. She turns his own concern back at him with a natural image: the serpent. A serpent sheds its skin completely — no hesitation, no grief, no looking back at what was discarded. It simply goes. Her sons are doing exactly this. And she has seen it. How, she asks, can she remain alone? The question is rhetorical — its answer is already embedded in the asking. By framing it this way, Yasha shows she is not following out of weakness or loneliness. She is following because she has seen what true renunciation looks like and recognizes it as her own path.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Yasha replied: "Look at how a snake sheds its old skin and just goes — free, without even glancing back. That's what our sons are doing. How could I stay behind alone?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it feel like to let go of something — a role, a story about yourself, a comfort — completely, like a snake sheds its skin? Not with grief, but with freedom?

### SUTRA 14.35

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धोरेयसीला तवसा उदारा, धीरा हु भिक्खायरियं चरंति ॥३५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

chiditu jālaṃ abalaṃ va rohiyā, macchā jahā kāma-guṇe pahāya |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Like weak rohita fish cutting through the net — the fish having abandoned sense pleasures — the yoke-bearing, noble in tapas, the steadfast ones truly practice the wandering mendicant life."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā* = just as | *rohiyā* = rohita fish (a species known for breaking nets) | *macchā* = fish | *jālaṃ* = the net | *abalaṃ va* = though weak, though the net is worn | *chiditu* = having cut, broken through | *kāma-guṇe* = sense pleasures | *pahāya* = having abandoned | *hu* = indeed | *dhoreyasīlā* = like a thoroughbred bull bearing the yoke (steadfast, built for endurance) | *dhīrā* = the steadfast, resolute ones | *udārā* = noble, of great character | *tavasā* = with tapas | *bhikkhāyariyaṃ* = the mendicant path | *caraṃti* = they practice, they walk

Yasha's second metaphor — the rohita fish — completes her self-declaration. Even a weak fish can cut through a worn net if it moves with purpose. The net of sense pleasures may seem strong — and the individual renouncer may seem small — but the act of cutting is possible and real. The second half of the sutra shifts to the aspirational image: *dhoreyasīlā* — like a yoke-bearing bull, bred for endurance and purpose. The steadfast ones, noble in tapas, simply walk the mendicant path. Yasha is not just agreeing to follow. She is announcing who she intends to become.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Yasha continued: "Even a weak fish can break through a net. The resolute and noble ones, strong in their practice, cut through the net of pleasures and walk the path of the monk. That is what I intend to do."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the "net" that holds you most firmly? Is it actually as strong as it seems — or does it just need one decisive cut?

### SUTRA 14.36

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जहेव कुंचा समइक्कमंता, तयाणि जालाणि दलितु हंसा ।
पलेंति पुत्ता य पई य मझं, ते हं कहं णाणुगमिस्समेक्का ॥३६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jaheva kuṃcā samaikkhamaṃtā, tayāṇi jālāṇi dalitu haṃsā |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Just as cranes cross over and geese, having shattered the spread nets, fly away freely — my sons and my husband from my midst — how shall I, alone, not follow them?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*va* = just as | *kuṃcā* = krauñca birds (cranes) | *samaikkhamaṃtā* = crossing over many regions | (fly into the sky) | *tayāṇi* = those spread-wide | *jālāṇi* = nets | *dalitu* = having shattered, broken through | *haṃsā* = swans/geese | *paleṃti* = fly away freely of their own accord | *majjhaṃ* = from my midst, from among me | *puttā ya* = my sons | *paī ya* = and my husband | *te* = with them | *ahaṃ* = I | *kahaṃ* = how | *ṇa aṇugamissam-ekkā* = shall I alone not follow?

Yasha's third and final metaphor — now combining two species: cranes that simply fly over what would trap them, and geese (haṃsas) that break through nets and soar. It is a declaration. Yasha has completed her inner journey from reluctance (S31) to resistance (S33, countered by S34) to resolution (S35, S36).

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Yasha said: "Cranes fly right over — geese shatter the nets and soar free. My sons and my husband are already gone from my side. How could I possibly stay behind alone?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When everyone around you is moving toward truth, and you keep finding reasons to stay — what is it that you are actually protecting?

### SUTRA 14.37

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पुरोहियं तं ससुयं सदारं, सोच्चाऽभिणिक्खम्म पहाय भोए ।
कुडुंबसारं विउलुत्तमं च, रायं अभिक्खं समुवाय देवी ॥३७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

purohiyaṃ taṃ sasuyaṃ sadāraṃ, soccā-abhiṇikkhamm pahāya bhoe |
kuḍuṃba-sāraṃ viululuttamaṃ ca, rāyaṃ abhikkhaṃ samuvāya devī || 37 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having heard that the priest with his sons and wife had renounced, abandoned pleasures, and departed, leaving behind even the greatest household wealth — the queen repeatedly approached the king.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pahāya* = having abandoned | *sasuyaṃ* = his two sons | *sadāraṃ* = together with his wife | *taṃ purohiyaṃ* = that priest Bhrigu | *bhoe* = pleasures | *soccā* = having heard (that he had renounced) | *abhiṇikkhamma* = departing (from household life, taking initiation) | *viululuttamaṃ ca* = his vast and most excellent | *kuḍuṃba-sāraṃ* = family wealth and household | *rāyaṃ* = the king (Ishukar) | *abhikkhaṃ* = repeatedly | *samuvāya* = began to say | *devī* = Queen Kamalavati

**5. COMMENTARY**

The scene shifts to the palace. The queen Kamalavati — the sixth of the six souls — has heard that the entire priestly household has renounced. This event is the trigger for the chapter's final and most condensed philosophical teaching. An ancient custom (*paramparā*) is mentioned in the Gujarati commentary: when a householder renounces and leaves with no heir, the king has the right to the abandoned property. King Ishukar is apparently considering exercising this right. Kamalavati is about to teach him — not through sentiment, but through precise metaphorical argument — why this action reveals a fundamental confusion about the nature of wealth, life, and protection.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen heard that the priest's entire family had renounced and departed, leaving all their wealth behind. She immediately went to the king — repeatedly — to teach him something important.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone near you makes a radical spiritual choice, does it stir something in you — or do you find yourself focused on what they've left behind?

### SUTRA 14.38

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वंतासि पुरिसो रायं, ण सो होइ पसंसिओ ।
माहणेण परिच्चत्तं, धणं आदाडुमिच्छसि ॥३८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vaṃtāsi puriso rāyaṃ, ṇa so hoi pasaṃsio |
māhaṇeṇa parittaṃ, dhaṇaṃ ādāḍum-icchasi || 38 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"A person who eats what has been vomited, O king — that one is not praised. What was abandoned by the brahmin — do you wish to pick up that wealth?"

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rāyaṃ* = O king! | *māhaṇeṇa* = by the brahmin (Bhrigu) | *parittaṃ* = abandoned, renounced | *dhaṇaṃ* = wealth | *ādāḍuṃ* = to take, to receive | *icchasi* = you wish | *vaṃtāsi* = one who eats what was vomited | *puriso* = a person | *ṇa hoi* = does not become | *pasaṃsio* = praiseworthy — in fact, is criticized everywhere

**5. COMMENTARY**

The queen's first image is deliberately shocking: taking what a renouncer has abandoned is like eating vomit. The metaphor cuts through any rational justification. In the Jain and Vedic traditions alike, wealth that a renouncer has abandoned is considered spiritually charged — it is not merely unclaimed property, it is *tyakta* (renounced). To take it is to participate in the logic of attachment after someone has consciously transcended it. The king may have legal standing to claim the property. Kamalavati is not arguing law — she is arguing the nature of the act. A king who grasps what a saint has released is diminished, not enriched.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen said: "A person who eats what someone has vomited is not respected by anyone. The brahmin deliberately gave up that wealth — do you really want to take it?"

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have you ever found yourself wanting something specifically because someone else discarded it — and mistaken that wanting for actual value?

### SUTRA 14.39

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सव्वं जगं जइ तुहं, सव्वं वावि धणं भवे ।
सव्वं पि ते अपज्जत्तं, णेव ताणाय तं तव ॥३९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

savvaṃ jagaṃ jai tuhaṃ, savvaṃ vāvi dhaṇaṃ bhave |
savvaṃ pi te apajjattaṃ, ṇeva tāṇāya taṃ tava || 39 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Even if all the world were yours, even if all wealth were yours — even all of that would still be insufficient for you, and it cannot be your protection."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jagaṃ* = the world | *tuhaṃ* = yours | *jai* = if | *savvaṃ* = all | *dhaṇaṃ* = wealth | *bhave* = becomes | *vāvi* = even, or | *savvaṃ pi* = even all of it | *te* = for you | *apajjattaṃ* = insufficient, not enough | *taṃ* = this wealth | *tava* = yours, your | *tāṇāya* = for protection | *ṇeva* = cannot, does not

**5. COMMENTARY**

The queen escalates: even *total* wealth is not enough. The word *apajjattam* — insufficient — is the key. Desire, by its nature, is never satisfied by its object. The wanting always exceeds the having. But the deeper point is *tāṇāya* — protection. Wealth can accumulate endlessly and still not protect you from death, aging, or suffering. The queen is not making a spiritual argument against prosperity. She is making a logical argument: the function you are trying to fulfill (protection) cannot be fulfilled by wealth, regardless of how much you accumulate. If the king claims Bhrigu's wealth hoping it will make him more secure — he is pursuing the wrong instrument for the right need.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen said: "Even if you owned the entire world and all its wealth — it would still not be enough, and none of it would actually protect you."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are you accumulating in the belief that at some level it will make you secure? How much of it would be enough?

### SUTRA 14.40

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मिरिहिसि रायं जया तया वा, मणोरमे कामगुणे पहाय ।
इक्को हु धम्मो णरदेव ताणं, ण विज्जइ अण्णमिहेह किंचि ॥४०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ikko hu dhammo ṇaradeva tāṇaṃ, ṇa vijjai aṇṇam-iheha kiṃci || 40 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"You will die, O king, at whatever time — having abandoned these mind-delighting sense pleasures. Dharma alone is the protection of a man, O lord of men — nothing else can be found here."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jayā tayā vā* = at whatever time (death's time comes) | *maṇorame* = mind-delighting, beautiful | *kāma-guṇe* = sense pleasures | *pahāya* = having abandoned (forced to abandon at death) | *mirihisi* = you will certainly die | *rāyaṃ* = O king! | *iha* = here in this world | *hū* = certainly | *ikko* = one only | *dhammo* = dharma alone | *ṇaradeva* = O lord of men! | *tāṇaṃ* = protection, refuge | *aṇṇaṃ* = other | *kiṃci* = anything | *ṇa vijjai* = is not found

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the chapter's central teaching and its hero sutra — the verse chosen as the featured quote for the chapter page. The queen's statement has two parts: (1) the fact of death — you *will* die, regardless of what you possess, and at that moment every sense pleasure will be abandoned against your will; (2) the implication — the only thing that accompanies you through death is dharma. It is a precise philosophical claim: at the boundary of death, wealth, family, power, and pleasure are all equally unavailable. Dharma alone — the inner quality of the soul — crosses with you.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen said: "One day you will die, O king — and at that moment you will be forced to leave behind every pleasure you love. Only dharma will protect you then. Nothing else will be there."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If you knew you would die in one year, would you live differently — not in the things you would do, but in the quality of attention you would bring to each day?

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen then said: "I am like a bird in a cage in this palace — I find no real joy here. I am cutting my bonds and will become a nun: without possessions, without deception, without desire, without causing harm."

### SUTRA 14.42

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दविग्गणा जहा रण्णे, डज्झमाणेसु जंतुसु ।
अण्णे सत्ता पमोयंति, रागदोसवसंगया ॥४२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

davig-gaṇā jahā raṇṇe, ḍajjhamāṇesu jaṃtusu |
aṇṇe sattā pamoyaṃti, rāga-dosa-vasaṃgayā || 42 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Just as in the forest with wildfire spreading, while creatures are burning — other beings rejoice, overcome by passion and hatred."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā* = just as | *raṇṇe* = in the forest | *davig-gaṇā* = with wildfire spreading | *jaṃtusu* = creatures | *ḍajjhamāṇesu* = while burning | *aṇṇe* = other | *sattā* = beings | *rāga-dosa-vasaṃgayā* = overcome by passion and hatred | *pamoyaṃti* = rejoice, take pleasure

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra and S43 form a paired teaching through a single extended metaphor. Wildfire breaks out in a forest. Creatures are burning. And other creatures nearby — overcome by passion (desire) and hatred (aversion) — are rejoicing. The Gujarati commentary asks: why would they rejoice? Because driven by rāga-dveṣa, beings take pleasure in the suffering of those they dislike and in their own momentary safety. This is the nature of the deluded mind: it responds to the burning of the world with appetite rather than awareness.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Imagine a wildfire burning through a forest — while some animals are dying, others watch happily, driven by passion and hatred, unaware that the fire will reach them too.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When the world around you is burning — with conflict, change, loss — do you respond with awareness, or do you find yourself, despite yourself, invested in the drama?

### SUTRA 14.43

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवमेव वयं मूढा, कामभोगेसु मुच्छिया ।
डज्झमाणं ण बुज्झामो, रागद्दोसिग्गणा जगं ॥४३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evameva vayaṃ mūḍhā, kāma-bhogesu mucchhiyā |
ḍajjhamāṇaṃ ṇa bujjhāmo, rāg-adosa-ig-gaṇā jagaṃ || 43 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"In just this same way, we fools, intoxicated by sense pleasures — do not comprehend the world burning in the fire of passion and hatred."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evameva* = in just this same way | *vayaṃ* = we | *mūḍhā* = fools | *kāma-bhogesu* = in sense pleasures | *mucchhiyā* = intoxicated, entranced | *rāg-adosa-ig-gaṇā* = in the fire of passion and hatred | *jagaṃ* = the entire world | *ḍajjhamāṇaṃ* = burning | *ṇa bujjhāmo* = we do not understand, do not perceive

**5. COMMENTARY**

The application of the wildfire metaphor to the self is the crucial move. Kamalavati turns the image inward: we are those creatures watching others burn, intoxicated by our pleasures, too absorbed to notice that the same fire is already consuming us. *Mūḍhā* — fools — is the Jain term for those in the grip of moha (delusion). The wildfire here is not metaphorical danger: it is the actual suffering of samsara, the repeated burning of birth-age-death, of attachment-and-loss. The teaching is not pessimistic — it is diagnostic. You cannot address a problem you cannot see. Seeing the fire is the first act of intelligence.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 14.44

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

भोगे भोच्चा विमित्ता य, लहुभूयविहारिणो ।
आमोयमाणा गच्छंति, दिया कामकमा इव ॥४४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

bhoge bhoccā vimittā ya, lahubhūya-vihāriṇo |
āmoyamāṇā gacchaṃti, diyā kāmakamā iva || 44 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Having enjoyed pleasures and being freed from them, those who wander lightly — rejoicing they go, like birds flying step by step through the sky at their will."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*bhoge* = pleasures | *bhoccā* = having enjoyed | *vimittā* = freed, released | *lahubhūya-vihāriṇo* = wandering lightly, like the wind — free of attachment | *āmoyamāṇā* = rejoicing | *gacchaṃti* = they go | *iva* = like | *diyā* = birds | *kāmakamā* = flying at their own will through the sky (freely, wherever they wish)

**5. COMMENTARY**

After the fire metaphor (S42-43), this sutra is a breath of lightness: those who have genuinely enjoyed and then genuinely let go become like birds. *Lahubhūya* — light-natured — describes the monk's quality of being. Not heavy with possessions, obligations, or anxiety. Not struggling against the air but moving through it freely. *Kāmakamā* — "flying as they wish" — describes absolute inner freedom: no agenda imposed from outside, only the pure movement of the awakened soul through the world. This is Kamalavati's vision of what she and the king could become.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Those who enjoy life and then let it go become like birds flying freely wherever they want — light, joyful, unattached, moving through the world without weight.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would "lightness" feel like in your life — not as irresponsibility, but as genuine freedom from inner weight?

### SUTRA 14.45

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

इमे य बद्धा फंदंति, मम हत्थडज्झमागया ।
वयं च सत्ता कामेसु, भविस्सामो जहा इमे ॥४५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ime ya baddhā phaṃdaṃti, mama hattha-ḍajjhama-āgayā |
vayaṃ ca sattā kāmesu, bhavissāmo jahā ime || 45 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"These (pleasures/fish), though bound and struggling, have come to burn in my hands — we too are attached to pleasures; we shall become like these."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ajjaṃ* = O noble one (addressing the king) | *mama hattha* = (come) to my hands | *āgayā* = reached, arrived | *kāmesu* = in sense pleasures | *baddhā* = bound, though we try to hold them securely | *ime* = these pleasures | *phaṃdaṃti* = they slip away, they struggle free | *vayaṃ* = we | *sattā* = attached | *jahā ime* = just as these (pleasures leave others) | *bhavissāmo* = we shall become (similarly abandoned)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The queen now uses the image of a caught fish: it comes to your hands, but it is bound and struggling — and eventually it will slip free or die. Sense pleasures are like this: they come to you, you hold them, but they always go. The more tightly you grasp, the more they struggle. *Phaṃdaṃti* — "they struggle and escape" — is the word. Kamalavati is not arguing that pleasure is bad. She is pointing to its transience. "We too are attached — and we shall become like these people who had to let go." The question is whether you let go consciously with freedom, or are forced to let go with suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen said: "Look — pleasures come to your hands like a caught fish, but they keep struggling to get free. We're attached to them now, but we'll end up the same — being forced to let them go."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What pleasures or attachments in your life are you holding tightly — and what energy are you spending trying to keep them from slipping away?

### SUTRA 14.46

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सामिसं कुललं दिस्स, बज्झमाणं णिरामिसं ।
आमिसं सव्वमुज्झित्ता, विहरिस्सामि णिरामिसा ॥४६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sāmisaṃ kulalaṃ dissa, bajjhamāṇaṃ ṇirāmisaṃ |
āmisaṃ savvam-ujjhittā, viharissāmi ṇirāmisā || 46 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Having seen the baited (bird) in the trap being caught, while the baitless one is free — I shall abandon all bait and wander free of bait."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kulalaṃ* = as in a trap (snare for birds) | *sāmisaṃ* = the baited one (with flesh as bait) | *dissa* = having seen | *bajjhamāṇaṃ* = being caught | *ṇirāmisaṃ* = the baitless one (not troubled) | *āmisaṃ* = all bait (worldly attachments, sense lures) | *savvaṃ* = entirely | *ujjhittā* = having abandoned | *ṇirāmisā* = free of all bait, without lure | *viharissāmi* = I shall wander on the path of restraint

**5. COMMENTARY**

The trap metaphor: one bird is baited and caught; another bird without bait passes freely. The *āmisa* (bait) here stands for worldly attachment — property, pleasure, prestige. Kamalavati sees the trap clearly. She has seen the baited one (herself and the king, caught in royal life) and the baitless ones (Bhrigu and his family, who are now free). Her declaration — *āmisaṃ savvam-ujjhittā* — "I shall abandon all bait" — is total. Not a reduction of attachment. A complete abandonment. The Gujarati commentary on *ṇirāmisā* extends the metaphor: just as a bird without bait attracts no trapper, a soul without attachment attracts no karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen said: "I've seen which bird gets caught — the one with bait. And which one goes free — the one without. I'm going to give up all bait and wander free."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the bait that traps you most reliably? Recognition? Comfort? Security? What would it feel like to go without it?

### SUTRA 14.47

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गिद्धोवमे उ णच्चाणं, कामे संसारवड्ढणे ।
उरगो सुवण्णपासे व्व, संकमाणो तणुं चरे ॥४७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

giddhavame u ṇaccāṇaṃ, kāme saṃsāra-vaḍḍhaṇe |
urago suvaṇṇa-pāse vva, saṃkamāṇo taṇuṃ care || 47 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Knowing sense pleasures to be like the greedy vulture, as the increaser of samsara — move with a restrained body, like a serpent cautiously crossing a golden snare."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*giddhavame u* = like the greedy (vulture metaphor just used) | *kāme* = sense pleasures | *saṃsāra-vaḍḍhaṇe* = as the increaser of samsara | *ṇaccāṇaṃ* = having known, understood | *suvaṇṇa-pāse vva* = like a golden snare set for a serpent | *urago* = a serpent | *saṃkamāṇo* = moving cautiously, alertly | *taṇuṃ* = with a restrained/slender body | *care* = moves, goes

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in your life are you moving carelessly through territory that requires awareness? What would it mean to move through it more carefully — not with fear, but with attention?

**5. COMMENTARY**

The queen returns to the image of the greedy vulture (already introduced in S45-46) and adds a second: the serpent crossing a golden snare. Gold is beautiful and tempting — a serpent moving through golden rings might be drawn to linger. But a wise serpent moves through with a slender, contained body (*taṇuṃ*) — aware, alert, not entangled. Sense pleasures are golden snares. The world around them is beautiful. The Jain practitioner does not deny the beauty — they move through it with awareness and restraint. *Saṃkamāṇo* — cautiously, alertly — describes not fear but presence. The fully awake mind moves through the world without being caught.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen advised: "Know that sense pleasures are like a greedy vulture — they increase samsara. Move through life like a snake carefully passing through a golden trap — restrained, alert, not caught."

### SUTRA 14.48

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णागो व्व बंधणं छित्ता, अप्पणो वसिहिं वए ।
एयं पत्थं महारायं, उस्सुयारि ति मे सुयं ॥४८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāgo vva baṃdhaṇaṃ chittā, appoṇo vasihiṃ vae |
eyaṃ patthaṃ mahārāyaṃ, ussuyāri ti me suyaṃ || 48 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Like an elephant cutting its bonds and going freely to its own dwelling — this is the beneficial path, O great king of Ishukar, as I have heard it from the knowers."

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇāgo vva* = like an elephant | *baṃdhaṇaṃ* = bonds (chains) | *chittā* = having cut | *appoṇo* = its own | *vasihiṃ* = dwelling, home in nature (the forest) | *vae* = goes | *ussuyāri* = O King Ishukar! | *eyaṃ patthaṃ* = this path | *mahārāyaṃ* = O great king! | *ti me suyaṃ* = I have heard this from the knowers of truth

**5. COMMENTARY**

The queen's final teaching image is the most powerful: an elephant breaking its chains. An elephant in captivity is bound — not by weakness, but by chains it was trained not to break. The moment it chooses to break them, it is free, and it returns to its natural habitat. Kamalavati is telling the king: your chains are not external. They are habits of mind — attachment to wealth, power, pleasure. When you cut them, you return to the soul's natural state. *Ti me suyaṃ* — "as I have heard from the knowers" — is the queen's acknowledgment that this teaching is not her personal invention. It is the teaching of the liberated ones, transmitted across time.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The queen concluded: "Like an elephant that breaks its chains and walks freely back to its natural home — that is the path, O great king. This is what I have heard from those who know truth."

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the chain you keep — not because you cannot break it, but because you have stopped believing it can be broken? What would change if you genuinely tried?

### SUTRA 14.49

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

चइत्ता विउलं रज्जं, कामभोगे य दुच्चए ।
णिब्भिवसया णिरामिसा, णिण्णेहा णिप्परिग्गहा ॥४९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

caittā viulaṃ rajjaṃ, kāma-bhoge ya duccae |
ṇibhhi-vasayā ṇirāmisā, ṇiṇṇehā ṇipparigghahā || 49 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having abandoned the vast kingdom and the sense pleasures — difficult to abandon — (they became) free of sense objects, without lure, without affection, without possessions.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*viulaṃ* = vast | *rajjaṃ* = kingdom | *caittā* = having abandoned | *duccae* = difficult to abandon | *kāma-bhoge* = sense pleasures | *ṇibhhi-vasayā* = free from sense objects | *ṇirāmisā* = without lure (without attachment to sensory bait) | *ṇiṇṇehā* = without affection (without family attachment) | *ṇipparigghahā* = without possessions, non-possessive

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter turns toward completion. The king and queen have absorbed the teaching. *Duccae* — "difficult to abandon" — is honest. The Gujarati commentary confirms: a vast kingdom with all its pleasures is genuinely hard to give up. The sutra does not pretend otherwise. But the king and queen did it. The four qualities that follow — *ṇibhhi-vasayā*, *ṇirāmisā*, *ṇiṇṇehā*, *ṇipparigghahā* — are the four marks of complete internal renunciation: free from sensory pull, free from lure, free from family attachment, free from possessiveness. All four together constitute the state of *akiñcana* — the soul standing alone, needing nothing external.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The king and queen abandoned their vast kingdom and all their pleasures — as difficult as that was — and became completely free: no desire for sense objects, no lure, no family attachment, no possessions.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would you have to let go of for your soul to stand truly alone — dependent on nothing external for its sense of completeness?

### SUTRA 14.50

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सम्मं धम्मं वियाणित्ता, चिच्चा कामगुणे वरे ।
तवं पगिज्झऽहक्खायं, घोरं घोरपरक्कमा ॥५०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sammaṃ dhammaṃ viyāṇittā, ciccā kāma-guṇe vare |
tavaṃ paghijjha-hakkkhāyaṃ, ghoraṃ ghora-parakkamā || 50 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having rightly understood dharma, having abandoned the excellent sense pleasures, they took up the well-proclaimed tapas — fierce, with fierce valor.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sammaṃ* = rightly, correctly | *dhammaṃ* = dharma | *viyāṇittā* = having understood | *vare* = excellent, primary | *kāma-guṇe* = sense pleasures | *ciccā* = having abandoned | *ahakkkhāyaṃ* = well-proclaimed, as taught by the Tirthankara | *ghoraṃ* = fierce, intense, difficult | *tavaṃ* = tapas | *paghijjha* = having taken up | *ghora-parakkamā* = with fierce valor, performing intense austerities

**5. COMMENTARY**

The sequence here is precise and important: first, *right understanding* of dharma — not sentiment, not fear, but genuine comprehension; then, abandonment of pleasures; then, taking up tapas. In the Jain path, *samyag-darśana* (right understanding/faith) is always the first step. Without it, renunciation is either imitation or escapism. With it, tapas becomes *ghora* — fierce — because it is backed by complete conviction. *Ahakkkhāyaṃ* — "well-proclaimed" — refers to the tapas described in the Jain āgamas as practiced by the Tirthankaras themselves. The king and queen are not doing something invented — they are entering a tested and ancient path.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Having truly understood dharma and let go of excellent pleasures, they took up intense tapas — fierce and with complete determination.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the difference between doing something spiritual out of habit or social pressure — and doing it from right understanding? What would change?

### SUTRA 14.51

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं ते कमसो बुद्धा, सव्वे धम्मपरायणा ।
जम्म मच्चुभउविग्गा, दुक्खस्संतगवेसिणो ॥५१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ te kamaso buddhā, savve dhamma-parāyaṇā |
jamma-maccu-bhau-viggā, dukkha-saṃta-gavesiṇo || 51 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Thus they, gradually awakened — all devoted to dharma — agitated by fear of birth and death — became seekers of the complete end of suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*evaṃ* = thus | *te* = they (all six) | *kamaso* = gradually, one after another | *buddhā* = awakened, having attained understanding | *savve* = all | *dhamma-parāyaṇā* = devoted to dharma | *jamma-maccu-bhau-viggā* = agitated by fear of birth and death | *dukkha-saṃta-gavesiṇo* = seekers of the complete end of suffering

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Kamaso* — gradually. This single word carries the entire narrative arc: the two sons awakened first; their father was awakened by their arguments; Yasha was awakened by their departure; the queen was awakened by their example; and finally the king was awakened by the queen's teaching. Six awakenings, in sequence. All six arrived at the same recognition: *jamma-maccu-bhau-viggā* — agitated by the fear of birth and death, which is the Jain idiom for the existential urgency that motivates genuine spiritual practice. They did not renounce out of failure or despair — they renounced because they saw clearly and could not un-see.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

One by one, all six souls woke up. All became devoted to dharma. All were shaken by the reality of birth and death. All became seekers of the final end of all suffering.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it take for you to be truly *agitated* — not just intellectually aware — by the fact of birth, aging, and death?

### SUTRA 14.52

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सासणे विगयमोहाणं, पुव्विं भावणभाविया ।
अचिरेणेव कालेण, दुक्खस्संतमुवागया ॥५२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sāsaṇe vigaya-mohāṇaṃ, puvviṃ bhāvaṇa-bhāviyā |
acireṇeva kāleṇa, dukkha-saṃtam-uvāgayā || 52 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In the teaching of those (Tirthankaras) freed from delusion, previously cultivated in spiritual practice — in a short time indeed, they reached the end of suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vigaya-mohāṇaṃ* = of those who have conquered delusion (the Tirthankaras) | *sāsaṇe* = in the teaching, in the path | *puvviṃ* = from previous lives | *bhāvaṇa-bhāviyā* = cultivated through spiritual practice, imbued through prior bhāvanā (meditation and contemplation) | *acireṇeva* = in just a short time | *kāleṇa* = in time | *dukkha-saṃtaṃ* = the end of all suffering | *uvāgayā* = attained, reached

**5. COMMENTARY**

*Puvviṃ bhāvaṇa-bhāviyā* — "previously cultivated in practice" — is the key phrase. The six souls attained liberation quickly not because they were extraordinary talents of this lifetime, but because they had been preparing across many lifetimes. Their past-life tapas and restraint had created karmic thinning. In this life, a single encounter — with monks, with dharma, with each other — was enough to complete the ripening. The Gujarati commentary's *upasaṃhāra* (conclusion) makes this explicit: the influence of good spiritual companions (*satsaṃga*) is decisive. Their connection across lifetimes was itself the deepest form of spiritual companionship.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Because they had been spiritually practicing across past lives, in the teaching of the Tirthankaras, they reached the complete end of all suffering — in just a short time.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What spiritual work done in this life might be ripening something that will complete in ways you cannot foresee?

### SUTRA 14.53

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

राया सह देवीए, माहणो य पुरोहिओ ।
माहणी दारगा चेव, सव्वे ते परिणिव्वुडे ॥५३॥ — ति बेमि

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāyā saha devīe, māhaṇo ya purohio |
māhaṇī dāragā ceva, savve te pariṇivvuḍe || 53 || — ti bemi

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The king together with the queen, the brahmin and the priest, the brahmin-woman and the children as well — all of them attained final liberation (parinirvāṇa). — Thus I say.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rāyā* = the king (Ishukar) | *saha devīe* = together with the queen (Kamalavati) | *māhaṇo* = the brahmin | *purohio* = the priest (Bhrigu) | *māhaṇī* = the brahmin woman (Yasha) | *dāragā* = the children/sons (the two princes) | *ceva* = also, as well | *savve* = all | *te* = they | *pariṇivvuḍe* = attained final liberation, parinirvāṇa (moksha) | **ti bemi** = thus I say (the formula that closes each adhyayana of the Uttaradhyayana Sutra, attributed to Lord Mahavira)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final sutra names all six: the king, the queen, the priest (Bhrigu), the priest's wife (Yasha), the brahmin woman ⚠️ *(the exact referent of "māhaṇī dāragā" is slightly ambiguous — the Gujarati commentary confirms "māhaṇī" refers to Yasha, and "dāragā" to the two sons, making the count exactly six)*, and the two sons. All six attained *pariṇivvuḍe* — final liberation, the state of the perfected soul beyond all karma. The chapter closes with *ti bemi* — the seal of Mahavira's direct testimony. These words are not described as transmitted or reported. They are *bemi* — "I say." The living word of the living teacher, preserved for 2,500 years.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The king, the queen, the priest, the priest's wife, and both sons — all six — attained complete, final liberation. Thus says Mahavira.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Six souls — a king, a queen, a priest, his wife, and two young sons — all found the path together across lifetimes. What does it tell you about the role of the people in your life on your own path?
