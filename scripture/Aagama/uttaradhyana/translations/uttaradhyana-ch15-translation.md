# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 15: Sabhikshu (સભિક્ષુ)

**Translation by:** Dishant Shah
**Source:** Gujarati PDF commentary (Shri Uttaradhyayan Sutra-1)
**Chapter:** Fifteenth Adhyayana (પંદરમું અધ્યયન)
**Total Sutras:** 16
**Theme:** The qualities and conduct of a true bhikshu (Jain monk). Each sutra ends with the refrain "sa bhikkhū" — "he is a bhikshu." The chapter enumerates 16 defining characteristics of genuine monkhood: right resolve, equanimity, forbearance, contentment, absence of desire for praise, restraint from women, avoidance of forbidden arts, rejection of medical arts, avoidance of flattery, indifference to householder dealings, equanimity toward food refusal, non-craving for food, non-contempt for plain alms, fearlessness of sounds, equanimity toward doctrinal debates, and complete renunciation.

---

## PART I — The Resolve and Inner Qualities (Sutras 1–5)

---

### SUTRA 15.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मोणं चरिस्सामि समिच्च धम्मं, सहिए उज्जुकडे णियाणछिण्णे ।
संथवं जहिज्ज अकामकामे, अण्णायएसी परिव्वए स भिक्खू ॥१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

moṇaṃ carissāmi samicc'a dhammaṃ, sahie ujjukaḍe ṇiyāṇa-chinṇe |
saṃthavaṃ jahijja akāmakāme, aṇṇāya-esī parivvae sa bhikkhū || 1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having reflected upon the Dharma, he who resolves "I will practice monkhood," who is endowed with right knowledge and vision, who is straight and free of deceit, who has cut the cord of craving, who has abandoned attachment to relatives, who is free of desire for sense pleasures, and who wanders seeking alms in unknown households — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*samicc'a* = having reflected upon, having understood | *dhammaṃ* = the Dharma (both śruta and cāritra) | *moṇaṃ* = the conduct of a muni (monkhood) | *carissāmi* = I shall practice | *sahie* = endowed with right vision (samyag-darśana) and knowledge | *ujjukaḍe* = having become straight, free of māyā (deceit) | *ṇiyāṇa-chinṇe* = having severed niyāṇa — the cord of vasanā (craving/ulterior desires) | *saṃthavaṃ* = familiarity with, attachment to relatives and companions | *jahijja* = abandons | *akāmakāme* = free from desire for sense pleasures | *aṇṇāya-esī* = seeking alms in unknown, unfamiliar households | *parivvae* = wanders continuously | *sa* = he | *bhikkhū* = is called a bhikshu-muni

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening sutra establishes the inner resolve of a true monk. The word *moṇaṃ* is rich: it means not merely silence, but the total orientation of a muni — the entire way of being of one who has renounced. The phrase "I will practice monkhood" (*moṇaṃ carissāmi*) is the foundational vow, made after genuine reflection (*samicc'a*) upon the nature of Dharma. The true bhikshu is not one who merely wears robes, but one who has inwardly severed three things: the rope of craving (*niyāṇa*), the bond of family attachment (*saṃthava*), and the pull of sense pleasures (*akāmakāme*). He wanders in unknown households — not seeking the comfortable and familiar — because this detachment from preferred donors is itself a practice of non-attachment.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk is someone who has genuinely decided — after deep reflection — to live without craving, without attachment to family, and without desire for pleasure, wandering wherever alms may come without preferences.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How many of your daily actions are shaped by hidden desires for reward, recognition, or comfort — and what would it mean to act without any such motivation?

### SUTRA 15.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

राओवरयं चरेज्ज लाढे, विरए वेयवियाऽऽयरिक्खए ।
पण्णे अभिभूय सव्वदंसी, जे कम्हि वि ण मुच्छिए स भिक्खू ॥२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāo-varayaṃ carejja lāḍhe, virae veyaviyā-āyarikkhae |
paṇṇe abhibhūya savvadaṃsī, je kamhi vi ṇa mucchie sa bhikkhū || 2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who, free from rāga (attachment) and ovara (aversion), wanders in suitable territory, who is restrained from sin, learned in the scriptures and protective of the self, who is wise, who has overcome all trials, who sees all beings equally, and who is not attached to anything whatsoever — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rāo-varayaṃ* (rāgo-varayaṃ) = free from rāga (attachment) and aversion, free from night-eating and night-wandering | *lāḍhe* = in a suitable, worthy field (āryakṣetra) | *carejja* = wanders | *virae* = restrained from sin (virata) | *veyaviyā* = learned in the Vedas — i.e., knowledgeable in scriptures | *āyarikkhae* = self-protective, one who guards the self | *paṇṇe* = wise, intelligent | *abhibhūya* = having overcome, having endured all trials with equanimity | *savvadaṃsī* = seeing all beings as one's own self, keeping the goal of saṃyama in view | *kamhi vi* = in any thing whatsoever | *ṇa mucchie* = not attached | *sa bhikkhū* = he is a bhikshu

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra describes the monk in motion — moving through the world with complete detachment. He has overcome rāga and dveṣa (attraction and repulsion), the two engines of karmic bondage. He is called *veyaviyā* — one who knows the scriptures deeply — and *āyarikkhae* — one who is his own protector, not dependent on anyone else for his spiritual safety. The phrase *savvadaṃsī* — "one who sees all" — means he has overcome rāga and dveṣa toward all beings and keeps the purpose of saṃyama always in view. Most significantly: he is attached to nothing. Not to comfort, not to acclaim, not to any object or person.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk moves through the world with no likes or dislikes pulling him — he is learned, self-sufficient, kind to all beings, and completely unattached to anything.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there any person, thing, or outcome in your life that you are so attached to that it would pull you away from your deepest values?

### SUTRA 15.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अक्कोस वहं विइत्तु धीरे, मुणी चरे लाढे णिच्चमायगुत्ते ।
अव्वग्गमणे असंपिहट्टे, जे किसिणं अहियासए स भिक्खू ॥३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

akkosa vahaṃ viittu dhīre, muṇī care lāḍhe ṇiccamāyagutte |
avvaggamaṇe asaṃpihaṭṭe, je kisiṇaṃ ahiyāsae sa bhikkhū || 3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The steadfast muni who, having understood harsh words and blows as the fruit of his own past karma, wanders in worthy territory, always guarding his soul, never agitated in mind, never elated, and who endures all hardships with equanimity — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*akkosa* = harsh speech, insult | *vahaṃ* = beating, physical blow | *viittu* = having known, having accepted as the fruit of past karma | *dhīre* = steadfast, patient | *muṇī* = the muni | *lāḍhe* = in worthy territory | *care* = wanders | *ṇicca* = always | *māyagutte* = guarding the soul, self-protective | *avvaggamaṇe* = not bringing agitation or distress to the mind | *asaṃpihaṭṭe* = not becoming over-elated in favorable conditions | *kisiṇaṃ* = all hardships, all afflictions | *ahiyāsae* = endures with equanimity | *sa bhikkhū* = he is a bhikshu

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra addresses the monk's response to adversity — specifically insult and physical violence. The key word is *viittu*: "having known." The monk does not merely tolerate abuse; he has understood it philosophically — recognizing it as the fruition of his own past karma, not as something done to him by an external enemy. This reframing transforms suffering into a spiritual opportunity. He remains *avvaggamaṇe* — unagitated — and *asaṃpihaṭṭe* — unelated. These two qualities together describe perfect equanimity: he neither collapses in pain nor soars in pleasure. Both extremes bind; steadiness liberates.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk accepts insults and even physical harm calmly — not by suppressing feelings, but because he understands that what happens to him is a result of his own past actions, not someone else's fault.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone criticizes or hurts you, do you look outward at who wronged you, or inward at what this moment is calling you to learn?

### SUTRA 15.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

पंतं सयणासणं भइत्ता, सीउण्हं विविहं च दंसमसगं ।
अव्वग्गमणेऽसंपिहट्टे, जे किसिणं अहियासए स भिक्खू ॥४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

paṃtaṃ sayaṇāsaṇaṃ bhaitta, sīuṇhaṃ vivihaṃ ca daṃsamasagaṃ |
avvaggamaṇe'saṃpihaṭṭe, je kisiṇaṃ ahiyāsae sa bhikkhū || 4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who accepts inferior bedding and seating with contentment, who endures cold, heat, and various kinds of insect bites — never agitated in mind, never elated — and who endures all hardships with equanimity — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*paṃtaṃ* = inferior, ordinary | *sayaṇāsaṇaṃ* = bedding and seating (śayyā and āsana) | *bhaitta* = accepts, receives with contentment | *sīuṇhaṃ* = cold and heat | *vivihaṃ* = various kinds | *daṃsamasagaṃ* = mosquito bites, insect bites | *avvaggamaṇe* = not bringing distress to the mind | *asaṃpihaṭṭe* = not becoming elated | *kisiṇaṃ* = all hardships | *ahiyāsae* = endures with equanimity

**5. COMMENTARY**

Where sutra 3 addressed inner trials (insult, violence), this sutra addresses physical hardship — the discomforts of the body. The monk accepts whatever bed or seat is offered, however rough. He does not demand comfort. He bears cold and heat without complaint, and endures biting insects with the same equanimity he brings to doctrinal debate. This is not mere stoicism; it is the recognition that the body is not the self, and therefore its discomforts do not touch what is essential. The refrain *avvaggamaṇe-asaṃpihaṭṭe* — "neither distressed nor elated" — appears again deliberately, linking physical forbearance to the same inner equanimity described in sutra 3.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk is completely okay with a rough bed, extreme weather, and insect bites — he doesn't complain or demand comfort because he knows his body's discomforts are not his ultimate concern.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much of your energy each day goes toward managing your physical comfort — and what might become possible if you loosened your grip on it?

### SUTRA 15.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णो सक्कियमिच्छइ ण पूयं, णो वि य वंदणगं कुओ पसंसं ।
से संजए सुव्वए तवस्सी, सहिए आयगवेसए स भिक्खू ॥५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇo sakkiyamicchhai ṇa pūyaṃ, ṇo vi ya vaṃdaṇagaṃ kuo pasaṃsaṃ |
se saṃjae suvvae tavassī, sahie āyagavesae sa bhikkhū || 5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who does not desire honor, nor worship, nor even veneration — much less praise — he who is restrained, well-vowed, ascetic, endowed with right knowledge, and a seeker of the self — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sakkiyaṃ* = honor, felicitation | *ṇo icchhai* = does not desire | *pūyaṃ* = worship, reverential offering | *vaṃdaṇagaṃ* = veneration, prostration | *kuo* = much less, far from it | *pasaṃsaṃ* = praise | *saṃjae* = restrained, self-controlled | *suvvae* = one with excellent vows | *tavassī* = ascetic, one engaged in tapas | *sahie* = endowed with samyag-jñāna-kriyā (right knowledge-action) | *āyagavesae* = one who guards and seeks the self (ātma-lakṣī, one who acts for the sake of the soul)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra targets the subtlest form of attachment — the desire for respect. The monk does not seek honor (*sakkiya*), worship (*pūyā*), or veneration (*vandanā*). And most crucially: he does not seek praise (*prasaṃsā*). The word *kuo* — "much less, where is the question of" — conveys the monk's utter indifference. Praise is the last thing he would want. This is significant because desire for recognition is often the most persistent ego-driven motivation in spiritual life. The true bhikshu has uprooted it entirely. He is *āyagavesae* — a seeker of the self, the ātman — not a seeker of applause.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk does not want to be praised, honored, or even bowed to — he has no interest in what others think of him; his only interest is in the work of his own soul.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you sometimes do good deeds partly because you hope someone will notice — and what would your spiritual life look like if no one ever did?

## PART II — Restraint from Worldly Entanglements (Sutras 6–10)

---

### SUTRA 15.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जेण पुण जहाइ जीवियं, मोहं वा किसिणं णियच्छइ ।
परणारिं पजहे सया तवस्सी, ण य कोउहलं उवेइ स भिक्खू ॥६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jeṇa puṇa jahāi jīviyaṃ, mohaṃ vā kisiṇaṃ ṇiyacchhai |
paraṇāriṃ pajjahe sayā tavassī, ṇa ya kouhalḥ uveī sa bhikkhū || 6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who stays always far from the company of other men's and women's association — by which saṃyama (the life of restraint) is destroyed and all forms of moha (delusion) arise — who, as an ascetic, always abandons contact with women, and does not engage in any frivolity — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jeṇa* = by whose company/association | *saṃga karavāthī* = through that contact | *jīviyaṃ* = the life of saṃyama (restraint) | *jahāi* = is destroyed, falls away | *kiṣṇaṃ* = completely, in all ways | *mohaṃ* = mohanīya karma, delusion | *ṇiyacchhai* = arises, takes hold | *paraṇāriṃ* = the company of men and women (literally: other's women/men) | *pajjahe* = abandons | *sayā* = always | *tavassī* = ascetic muni | *kauhalḥ* = curiosity, frivolity, idle amusement | *ṇa uveī* = does not approach, does not engage in

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra addresses the monk's relationship with the company of the opposite sex — one of the most powerful forces that can disrupt renunciation. The logic is not moralistic but karmic: association with those who generate attraction-repulsion (moha) has the direct effect of destroying saṃyama. The word *mohaṃ* refers specifically to mohanīya karma — the category of karma that produces delusion, attachment, and passion. The monk who remains vigilant about this association is not driven by misogyny but by a clear-eyed understanding of how delusion operates. He also avoids *kauhal* — frivolity, idle entertainment, unnecessary socializing — all of which are seen as entry points for distraction.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk avoids any company or entertainment that could stir up attachment or delusion, because he knows these are the things most likely to pull him away from his path.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What kinds of company, entertainment, or habits in your life tend to pull you away from your deeper intentions — and how honest are you with yourself about their effect?

### SUTRA 15.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

छिण्णं सरं भोमंतलिक्खं, सुमिणं लक्खण दंड वत्थुविज्जं ।
अंगियारं सरस्स विजयं, जे विज्जाहिं ण जीवइ स भिक्खू ॥७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

chinnaṃ saraṃ bhomantaliakkhaṃ, sumiṇaṃ lakkhaṇa daṃḍa vatthuvijjaṃ |
aṃgiyāraṃ sarassa vijayaṃ, je vijjāhiṃ ṇa jīvai sa bhikkhū || 7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who does not earn his livelihood through any of these arts — the art of cutting cloth and wood, the art of melody and sound, knowledge of omens and ground signs, the knowledge of space and sky, the knowledge of dreams, the knowledge of body marks (physiognomy), the art of sticks and implements, the knowledge of architecture and vastushastra, the art of fire omens, the knowledge of animal and bird language — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*chinnaṃ* = the art of cutting cloth, wood, etc. (śilpa) | *saraṃ* = the art of melody, sound-science (svara-vidyā) | *bhomaṃ* = knowledge relating to the earth, ground omens | *antaliakkhaṃ* = knowledge relating to the sky and space (antarikṣa-vidyā) | *sumiṇaṃ* = dream interpretation (svapna-vidyā) | *lakkhaṇa* = knowledge of body marks, physiognomy | *daṃḍa vatthuvijjaṃ* = knowledge of sticks/implements and architecture (daṇḍa-vidyā and vāstu-vidyā) | *aṃgiyāraṃ* = knowledge of body/limb signs (aṃga-sphuraṇa-vidyā) | *sarassa vijayaṃ* = the knowledge of animal and bird language | *vijjāhiṃ* = through these (corrupt) arts | *ṇa jīvai* = does not earn his livelihood

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra gives a detailed list of fourteen forbidden arts by which a monk must not sustain himself. These range from crafts (cutting cloth, woodwork) to esoteric sciences (dream interpretation, physiognomy, architecture, bird-language). They are forbidden not because they are intrinsically evil, but because using them to sustain oneself creates worldly dependency, ego-investment, and distraction from the inner path. A monk who tells fortunes or practices healing has made his spiritual practice into a livelihood — which corrupts both. This reflects the Jain principle that the monk's only legitimate sustenance is alms received without manipulation or service rendered in return.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk doesn't use special arts, fortune-telling, dream interpretation, or any kind of craft or healing to earn food or income — he receives only what is freely given as alms, with no strings attached.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In your own life, do you ever use your spiritual qualities or knowledge as a tool to get something from others — approval, influence, or advantage?

### SUTRA 15.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मंतं मूलं विविहं वेज्जचिंतं, वमण विरेयण धूमणेत्तसिणाणं ।
आउरे सरणं तिगिच्छियं च, तं परिण्णाय परिव्वए स भिक्खू ॥८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

maṃtaṃ mūlaṃ vivihaṃ vejjacintaṃ, vamaṇa vireyaṇa dhūmaṇetta-siṇāṇaṃ |
āure saraṇaṃ tigicchiyaṃ ca, taṃ pariṇṇāya parivvae sa bhikkhū || 8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Having fully understood and renounced mantra practice, herbal-root remedies, various forms of medical treatment, induced vomiting, purgatives, nasal smoke-inhalation procedures for cleansing, bathing, the impulse to seek shelter in a householder's home when ill, and the practice of medical treatment — he who wanders with this understanding is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*maṃtaṃ* = use of mantras and tantras | *mūlaṃ* = use of herbal roots and medicinal plants | *vivihaṃ vejjacintaṃ* = various forms of medical thinking and treatment | *vamaṇa* = induced vomiting (emesis therapy) | *vireyaṇa* = purgative treatment | *dhūmaṇetta* = nasal smoke-inhalation purification procedure (dhūmana-neti) | *siṇāṇaṃ* = ritual bathing as a remedy | *āure* = when afflicted by illness | *saraṇaṃ* = seeking refuge in a householder's home | *tigicchiyaṃ* = medical treatment (cikitsā) | *taṃ pariṇṇāya* = having understood all of this and renounced it | *parivvae* = wanders in saṃyama

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra extends sutra 7's principle — renunciation of livelihood through arts — to the domain of medicine and healing. A monk must not use mantras, herbal remedies, or medical procedures for himself or for others. He must not seek refuge with a householder when ill. This is one of the most demanding aspects of monastic life: when the body suffers, every instinct pulls toward relief. Yet the Jain monk understands that medical treatment, when used to perpetuate attachment to the body and to worldly comfort, becomes a form of violence against the path. The phrase *taṃ pariṇṇāya* — "having fully understood this" — is crucial: the monk does not blindly follow a rule but has genuinely comprehended why these renunciations serve liberation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk does not use medicine, herbal cures, or healing treatments — even when ill — because his entire orientation is toward the soul, not toward preserving the body's comfort.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How strong is your attachment to your body's health and comfort — and can you distinguish between care that is necessary and care that is driven by fear of discomfort?

### SUTRA 15.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

खत्तियगण उग्गरायपुत्ता, माहणभोइय विविहा य सिप्पिणो ।
णो तेसिं वयइ सिलोगपूयं, तं परिण्णाय परिव्वए स भिक्खू ॥९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

khattiyagaṇa uggarāyaputtā, māhaṇabhoiya vividhā ya sippiṇo |
ṇo tesim vahai silogapūyaṃ, taṃ pariṇṇāya parivvae sa bhikkhū || 9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who does not offer words of praise or eulogy to Kshatriya warrior-clans, mighty kings, royal princes, Brahmins, chieftains, or the various craftsmen and artisans — having understood that such praise is not beneficial to the life of saṃyama — he who wanders with this understanding is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*khattiya* = Kshatriya, warrior caste | *gaṇa* = warrior bands (malla, yodhā) | *ugga* = bodyguards, protectors | *rāyaputtā* = royal princes | *māhaṇa* = Brahmin | *bhoiya* = chief, sāmanta | *vividhā sippiṇo* = various kinds of craftsmen and artisans | *ṇo tesim vahai* = does not say to them | *silogapūyaṃ* = words of praise, eulogy (*śloka-pūjā*) | *taṃ pariṇṇāya* = having known and understood this | *parivvae* = wanders in saṃyama

**5. COMMENTARY**

The monk's restraint extends to speech — specifically, flattery of the powerful. A monk who praises kings, warriors, and wealthy patrons does so to receive favor: better food, shelter, or protection. This transforms the monk into a sycophant, and corrupts the relationship between the monastic community and lay society. The Jain monk holds equal vision (*samadṛṣṭi*) toward all: a Kshatriya king and an unknown farmer are equally worthy of the same non-sycophantic presence. Praise of the powerful, however subtle, is a form of attachment to security and comfort — precisely what the monk has vowed to release.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk does not flatter powerful people — kings, warriors, nobles, or the wealthy — in order to receive better food or protection; he treats everyone with the same quiet equality.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do you ever say things you don't fully believe — praise you don't mean, agreements you don't feel — in order to make powerful or important people comfortable with you?

### SUTRA 15.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गिहिणो जे पव्वइएण दिट्ठा, अप्पव्वइएण व संथुया हविज्जा ।
तेसिं इहलोइयफलट्ठा, जो संथवं ण करेइ स भिक्खू ॥१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

gihiṇo je pavvaieṇa diṭṭhā, appavvaieṇa va saṃthuyā havijjā |
tesim ihaloia-phalattā, jo saṃthavaṃ ṇa karei sa bhikkhū || 10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who, after having taken initiation (pravrajyā), does not maintain special familiarity with any of those householders he knew before initiation or became acquainted with after initiation — seeking worldly gains such as clothing, alms-bowl, food, fame, or praise — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*pavvaieṇa* = after taking dīkṣā (initiation into monkhood) | *diṭṭhā* = seen, having become acquainted | *gihiṇo* = householders | *appavvaieṇa* = while still in the householder state | *saṃthuyā* = having become known, having a prior relationship | *havijjā* = having become so | *tesim* = with those householders | *ihaloia-phalattā* = for worldly purposes — i.e., to obtain cloth, alms-bowl, food, fame, praise | *saṃthavaṃ* = special familiarity, particular relationship | *ṇa karei* = does not maintain

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra addresses a subtle temptation: using old relationships — or newly formed ones — to secure material or social advantages as a monk. After renunciation, the monk encounters two kinds of householders: those he knew before, and those he meets afterward. With neither group does a true monk cultivate *saṃthava* — a special, preferential familiarity — if that familiarity is motivated by the desire for worldly benefit. This does not mean the monk is cold or unfriendly; it means he does not allow any relationship to become a hidden transaction. When he approaches a household, it is as a pure receiver of whatever is freely given — not as someone leveraging a connection.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk doesn't use old friendships or new relationships to get better food, gifts, or praise — every household he visits is approached with the same openness, not as a favor being called in.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there relationships in your life that have become subtly transactional — where connection has become mixed with expectation of return?

## PART III — Equanimity in Receiving Alms (Sutras 11–13)

---

### SUTRA 15.11

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सयणासण पाणभोयणं, विविहं खाइमं साइमं परेसिं ।
अदए पडिसेहिए णियंठे, तेत्थ ण पउस्सइ स भिक्खू ॥११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sayaṇāsaṇa pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ, vivihaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ paresim |
adae paḍisehie ṇiyaṃṭhe, tettha ṇa paussai sa bhikkhū || 11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

When the nirgranth monk goes to householders and they do not give him his bed, seat, water, food, various eatables, or palate-pleasing items — or actively refuse him — he does not feel even the slightest dveṣa (aversion or resentment) toward them — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sayaṇāsaṇa* = bed and seat | *pāṇabhoyaṇaṃ* = water and food | *vivihaṃ khāimaṃ* = various kinds of eatables | *sāimaṃ* = palate-pleasing items, sweet items | *paresim* = at householders' homes | *adae* = not giving | *paḍisehie* = refusing, sending away | *ṇiyaṃṭhe* = the nirgranth monk | *tettha* = toward that householder | *ṇa paussai* = does not feel dveṣa, does not develop resentment

**5. COMMENTARY**

A monk's life of alms-begging creates constant situations of potential insult: a householder may refuse to give anything. This sutra describes the monk's response — zero resentment. The word *paussai* (from *dveṣa*) means to develop aversion, anger, or ill-will. The true monk does not experience this. This is not suppression; it is genuine equanimity. The householder who refuses is not an enemy — they may have valid reasons, they are living their own life. The monk's liberation does not depend on what any householder does; it depends entirely on his own inner state. Refusal, when met with equanimity, is itself a kind of practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: When someone slams the door in a monk's face and refuses to give him anything, the true monk feels absolutely no anger or resentment — it simply doesn't disturb him.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you extend yourself to someone — in generosity, in help, in love — and they reject or ignore you, what arises in you? And what does that response reveal?

### SUTRA 15.12

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जं किंचि आहारपाणगं, विविहं खाइमं साइमं परेसिं लद्धं ।
जो तं तिविहेण णाणुकंपे, मण वय कायसुसंवुडे स भिक्खू ॥१२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jaṃ kiṃci āhārapāṇagaṃ, vivihaṃ khāimaṃ sāimaṃ paresim laddhaṃ |
jo taṃ tivihieṇa ṇāṇukaṃpe, maṇa vaya kāyasusaṃvuḍe sa bhikkhū || 12 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whatever food and water, various eatables or palate-pleasing items are received from householders — he who does not approve of or take delight in them by mind, speech, or body, and who keeps mind, speech, and body fully controlled — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*paresim* = from householders | *jaṃ kiṃci* = whatever | *āhāra pāṇagaṃ* = food and water | *vivihaṃ khāimaṃ* = various kinds of eatables | *sāimaṃ* = palate-pleasing, sweet items | *laddhaṃ* = having received | *jo* = he who | *taṃ* = that food and water | *tivihieṇa* = through the threefold means (mind, speech, body) | *ṇa anukaṃpe* = does not approve of or take delight in (does not practice īhādoṣa — sensory craving) | *maṇa vaya kāya* = mind, speech, and body | *susaṃvuḍe* = keeping fully controlled, restrained

**5. COMMENTARY**

Sutra 11 addressed the monk's equanimity when food is refused; this sutra addresses his equanimity when food is given. The monk receives food without craving it, without mentally relishing it, without vocally expressing delight, and without physically showing eagerness. This is the practice of non-approval (*ananukampā*) — not approving of indulgence even when it is possible. The Jain term *īhādoṣa* refers specifically to sensory craving at the moment of receiving food. The truly restrained monk has triply controlled himself: mentally (no craving thought), verbally (no expression of delight), and bodily (no eager movement toward the food).

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk receives food without craving it — he doesn't think "I hope I get more of this," doesn't express delight, and doesn't eagerly reach for it — he simply receives what comes.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can you eat a meal — or receive anything good — without immediately wanting more, or thinking about when you can have it again?

### SUTRA 15.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आयामगं चेव जवोदणं च, सीयं सोवीर जवोदगं च ।
णो हीलए पिंडं णीरसं तु, पंतकुलाइं परिव्वए स भिक्खू ॥१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āyāmagaṃ ceva javodaṇaṃ ca, sīyaṃ sovīra javodagaṃ ca |
ṇo hīlae piṃḍaṃ ṇīrasaṃ tu, paṃtakulāiṃ parivvae sa bhikkhū || 13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who does not despise or look down upon plain, tasteless alms — whether it be leftover food, barley-rice, cold food, thin gruel made from curds, or barley-water — and who wanders even to ordinary humble households for alms — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*āyāmagaṃ* = remaining food, leftover food (avashiṣṭa) | *javodaṇaṃ* = barley-rice, coarse grain-food | *sīyaṃ* = cold food | *sovīra* = thin gruel or liquid made above curds | *javodagaṃ* = barley-water, coarse liquid | *ṇo hīlae* = does not despise, does not look down upon | *piṃḍaṃ* = the alms-food | *ṇīrasaṃ* = tasteless, without flavor | *paṃtakulāiṃ* = to ordinary, humble households | *parivvae* = wanders for alms

**5. COMMENTARY**

This sutra is a gentle rebuke to the pride of the palate. The items listed — cold food, barley-water, thin sour gruel — were the most ordinary, least valued foods of the time, given to animals or the very poor. The monk goes even to the most ordinary homes and accepts even the most tasteless food without complaint or contempt. This is the direct embodiment of aparigraha (non-possessiveness) in the domain of food. The monk's relationship with food is purely functional — sustenance for the continuation of the body that houses the soul on its path — not pleasure-seeking. Contempt for plain food is a form of ego.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk happily accepts the plainest, most tasteless food from the humblest homes — cold leftovers, watery gruel — without any sense of being above it or deserving better.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In how many areas of your life do you feel you deserve something better than what you have — and what does that sense of deserving cost you?

## PART IV — Fearlessness and Doctrinal Equanimity (Sutras 14–15)

---

### SUTRA 15.14

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सदा विविहा भवंति लोए, दिव्वा माणुस्सगा तहा तिरिच्छा ।
भीमा भयभेरवा उराला, जो सोच्चा ण विहिज्जइ स भिक्खू ॥१४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sadā vividhā bhavaṃti loe, divvā māṇussagā tahā tiricchā |
bhīmā bhayabheravā urāḷā, jo soccā ṇa vihijjai sa bhikkhū || 14 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In this world there always exist various terrifying sounds and extraordinary phenomena — divine, human, and those of animals and birds — extremely frightening, fear-inducing, and overwhelming; he who hears them and is not frightened — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*loe* = in this world | *divvā* = divine, relating to gods | *māṇussagā* = human | *tiricchā* = of animals, birds, tiryañca | *vividhā* = various kinds | *bhīmā* = frightening | *bhayabheravā* = fear-inducing, causing terror | *urāḷā* = overwhelming, great | *sadā bhavaṃti* = always exist | *jo soccā* = he who, upon hearing them | *ṇa vihijjai* = is not frightened

**5. COMMENTARY**

The world is filled with sounds and phenomena that trigger fear — thunder, the cry of wild animals, the roar of crowds, the screech of weapons, the moaning of the dying, supernatural experiences in isolated forest. The monk encounters all of these — often alone, often at night, in cremation grounds, in the wilderness. His quality is not the absence of perception but the absence of fear in response to perception. He hears the terrifying and remains steady. This fearlessness is not bravado but the fruit of a profound inner stability: a being who has settled into the self is no longer threatened by the world's sounds and spectacles, however overwhelming.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk can hear the most terrifying sounds — from gods, humans, or wild animals — and remain completely unafraid, because his inner steadiness is deeper than anything the world can produce.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What sounds, situations, or people trigger the most fear in you — and what does that fear tell you about where your sense of safety actually lives?

### SUTRA 15.15

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वादं विविहं समिच्च लोए, सहिए खेयाणुगए य कोवियप्पा ।
पण्णे अभिभूय सव्वदंसी, उवसंते अविहेडए स भिक्खू ॥१५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vādaṃ vivihaṃ samicc'a loe, sahie kheyāṇugae ya koviyappā |
paṇṇe abhibhūya savvadaṃsī, uvasaṃte avihēḍae sa bhikkhū || 15 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who, having understood the various doctrinal debates prevalent in the world, remains stable in knowledge and vision, who is an adherent of the path of saṃyama, who is the supreme knower of the deeper meaning of the scriptures, who is wise, who has overcome all trials with equanimity, who sees all beings with equal vision, who is calmed in passions, and who causes pain to no living being — he is a bhikshu.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*loe* = in the world | *vādaṃ vivihaṃ* = various philosophical debates, doctrinal arguments | *samicc'a* = having understood | *sahie* = endowed with samyag-darśana, jñāna, cāritra and tapas, or: remaining with other sādhus | *kheyāṇugae* = an adherent of the path of saṃyama | *koviyappā* = learned self, vicakṣaṇa sādhu — one who has understood the supreme meaning of the śāstras | *paṇṇe* = wise, intelligent | *abhibhūya* = having overcome all trials with equanimity | *savvadaṃsī* = seeing all beings equally, keeping saṃyama as the goal | *uvasaṃte* = having calmed the passions (upaśānta-kaṣāya) | *avihēḍae* = not causing pain or harm to any living being

**5. COMMENTARY**

The world brims with competing philosophical claims — each school asserting the truth of its doctrine. The monk engages with all of them, understands them (*samicc'a*), and is moved by none. He remains rooted in his own path of saṃyama (*kheyāṇugae*). He is *koviyappā* — deeply learned, knowing the supreme import of the scriptures — and yet he does not use this knowledge to humiliate others. He is *avihēḍae* — one who harms no being, not even through the violence of doctrinal arrogance. This is anekāntatā in practice: knowing many views, being attached to none, causing pain to none.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk understands all the different philosophies and arguments in the world but is never pulled into debates or ego battles — he is wise, calm, and gentle with everyone, regardless of what they believe.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When someone holds a view very different from yours, do you feel the urge to correct them — and what is that urge really protecting?

## PART V — Complete Renunciation (Sutra 16)

---

### SUTRA 15.16

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

असिप्पजीवी अगिहे अमित्ते, जिइंदिए सव्वओ विप्पमुक्के ।
अणुक्कसाई लहुअप्पभक्खी, चिण्णाए गिहं एगचरे स भिक्खू ॥१६॥
— ति बेमि ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

asippajīvī agihe amitte, jitiṃdie savvao vippamukke |
aṇukkasāī lahuappabhakkhī, ciṇṇāe gihaṃ egacare sa bhikkhū || 16 ||
— ti bemi

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

He who does not earn his livelihood through any craft or art, who is without a home, who is without binding friends or enemies, who has conquered the senses, who is completely free from all external and internal bonds, who has mild passions, who eats nourishing but limited food, who has renounced householder life, and who wanders alone — free from rāga and dveṣa — he is a bhikshu. — Thus I say.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*asippajīvī* = one who does not earn a livelihood through craft or art (śilpa) | *agihe* = without a home, householder-renouncer | *amitte* = without binding friends or enemies (no attachment-generating relationships) | *jitiṃdie* = one who has conquered the senses (jitaindriya) | *savvao vippamukke* = completely free from all external and internal bonds | *aṇukkasāī* = one with mild, reduced passions (alpa-kaṣāya) | *lahuappabhakkhī* = nourishing but limited food, eating lightly (*lahu* = light/nourishing; *appa* = little; *bhakkhi* = eating) | *ciṇṇāe* = having renounced, having abandoned | *gihaṃ* = household life | *egacare* = wandering alone, in solitude, free from rāga-dveṣa

**5. COMMENTARY**

This final sutra gathers all the threads of the chapter into a single portrait of the complete monk. He has no profession, no home, no possessive friendships, no enemies — because he has no attachment generating the distinction between friend and enemy. He has conquered the senses — not suppressed them, but genuinely mastered them so they no longer drive his behavior. He eats what sustains the body without excess. He wanders alone: *egacare* — this aloneness is not loneliness but the clarity of being fully settled in the self, undistracted by company. The chapter ends with *iti bemi* — "Thus I say" — Lord Mahāvīra's own voice, affirming this as the teaching direct from the liberated source. This is not opinion; this is the testimony of one who has walked the path to its end.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Simply put: A true monk has no job, no home, no special friends or enemies, eats lightly, walks alone, and is free from every attachment — inside and out. Lord Mahavira says: this is what I have seen and taught.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of all the qualities described in this chapter — detachment, fearlessness, equanimity, no craving for praise — which one feels most distant from where you are right now, and what would it take to move even one step toward it?
