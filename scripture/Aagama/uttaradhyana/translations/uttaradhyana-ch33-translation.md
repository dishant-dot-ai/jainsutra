# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 33
# Karmaprakṛti (The Nature of Karma)

**Total Sutras:** 25
**Theme:** This chapter provides a systematic exposition of the eight types of karma (jnanavaraniya, darshanavaraniya, vedaniya, mohaniya, ayushya, nama, gotra, antaraya), their sub-types (prakrti-bandha), spatial extent (pradesha-bandha), duration (sthiti-bandha), and intensity (anubhaga-bandha). It describes how karma binds the soul, the varieties within each karmic type, and ultimately how understanding these bonds leads toward liberation.

---

## SUTRA 33.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अट्ठु कम्माइं वोच्छामि, आणुपुव्वि जहक्कमं ।
जेहिं बद्धो अचं जीवो, संसारे परिवट्टइ ॥३३.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aṭṭhu kammāiṃ vocchāmi, āṇupuvvi jahakkamaṃ |
Jehiṃ baddho acaṃ jīvo, saṃsāre parivaṭṭai || 33.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I shall describe the eight karmas in sequence, by which this soul, being bound, wanders in the cycle of worldly existence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṭṭhu** — eight
- **kammāiṃ** — karmas
- **vocchāmi** — I shall describe
- **āṇupuvvi** — in sequence / in order (anupurvi)
- **jahakkamaṃ** — as per their order
- **jehiṃ** — by which
- **baddho** — bound
- **acaṃ** — this
- **jīvo** — soul / living being
- **saṃsāre** — in worldly existence / in samsara
- **parivaṭṭai** — wanders / revolves

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening sutra introduces the subject matter of the entire chapter. The speaker declares the intention to systematically describe the eight types of karma that bind the soul. The word *anupurvi* (in sequence) indicates that the karmas will be explained in a specific traditional order. The soul (*jiva*), when bound by these eight karmas, continues to revolve in the cycle of birth and death (*samsara*). This sutra establishes that understanding the nature of karma is essential for anyone seeking liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The chapter begins by announcing it will explain the eight types of karma that keep the soul trapped in the cycle of birth and death.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I understand what specific forces keep me bound in this world, or do I simply accept suffering without examining its cause?

## SUTRA 33.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पाणस्सावरणिज्जं, दंसणावरणं तहा ।
वेयणिज्जं तहा मोहं, आउकम्मं तहेव य ॥३३.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Pāṇassāvaraṇijjaṃ, daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ tahā |
Veyaṇijjaṃ tahā mohaṃ, āukammaṃ taheva ya || 33.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowledge-obscuring, perception-obscuring, feeling-producing, deluding, and life-determining — these are the karmas described thus.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **pāṇassāvaraṇijjaṃ** — knowledge-obscuring karma (jnanavaraniya)
- **daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ** — perception-obscuring karma (darshanavaraniya)
- **tahā** — likewise / and also
- **veyaṇijjaṃ** — feeling-producing karma (vedaniya)
- **mohaṃ** — deluding karma (mohaniya)
- **āukammaṃ** — life-determining karma (ayushya karma)
- **taheva** — likewise
- **ya** — and

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra begins the enumeration of the eight karmas by naming the first five. Knowledge-obscuring karma (*jnanavaraniya*) obstructs the soul's inherent omniscience. Perception-obscuring karma (*darshanavaraniya*) blocks the soul's capacity for perception. Feeling-producing karma (*vedaniya*) causes experiences of pleasure and pain. Deluding karma (*mohaniya*) creates confusion regarding right faith and right conduct. Life-determining karma (*ayushya*) determines the lifespan of a being in a particular existence. These five, along with the three named in the next sutra, constitute the complete taxonomy of karmic bondage.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The first five karmas are named — they obscure knowledge, obscure perception, produce feelings of pleasure and pain, create delusion, and determine how long you live.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of these five karmic veils do I notice most in my own life — the one that blocks my understanding, distorts my perception, or creates delusion?

## SUTRA 33.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पाणकम्मं च गोयं च, अंतरायं तहेव य ।
एवमेयाइं कम्माइं, अट्ठेव समासओ ॥३३.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Pāṇakammaṃ ca goyaṃ ca, aṃtarāyaṃ taheva ya |
Evameyāiṃ kammāiṃ, aṭṭheva samāsao || 33.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Name-determining karma, lineage-determining karma, and obstructive karma — thus these karmas are eight in all, in brief.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **pāṇakammaṃ** — name-determining karma (nama karma)
- **goyaṃ** — lineage-determining karma (gotra karma)
- **aṃtarāyaṃ** — obstructive karma (antaraya karma)
- **taheva** — likewise
- **ya** — and
- **evam** — thus
- **eyāiṃ** — these
- **kammāiṃ** — karmas
- **aṭṭheva** — eight only
- **samāsao** — in summary / in brief

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra completes the enumeration of the eight karmas by naming the remaining three. Name-determining karma (*nama karma*) determines the body, form, and physical attributes of a being. Lineage-determining karma (*gotra karma*) determines whether one is born in a high or low family. Obstructive karma (*antaraya karma*) obstructs the soul's inherent capacities of giving, gaining, enjoyment, and effort. Together with the five named in the previous sutra, these eight constitute the complete classification of karma in Jain philosophy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The remaining three karmas are named — they determine your body and form, your social standing, and they block your innate abilities. Together, all eight karmas cover every way the soul is bound.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can I see how each of these eight karmas shapes a different aspect of my current life — from what I know, to how I feel, to the body I have?

## SUTRA 33.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पाणावरणं पंचविहं, सुयं आभिणिबोहियं ।
ओहिणाणं च तइयं, मणणाणं च केवलं ॥३३.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Pāṇāvaraṇaṃ paṃcavihaṃ, suyaṃ ābhiṇibohiyaṃ |
Ohiṇāṇaṃ ca taiyaṃ, maṇaṇāṇaṃ ca kevalaṃ || 33.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Knowledge-obscuring karma is of five kinds: it obscures scriptural knowledge, sensory knowledge, clairvoyant knowledge, telepathic knowledge, and omniscient knowledge.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **pāṇāvaraṇaṃ** — knowledge-obscuring (jnanavaraniya)
- **paṃcavihaṃ** — of five kinds
- **suyaṃ** — scriptural knowledge (shruta jnana)
- **ābhiṇibohiyaṃ** — sensory knowledge (abhinibodhika / mati jnana)
- **ohiṇāṇaṃ** — clairvoyant knowledge (avadhi jnana)
- **taiyaṃ** — the third
- **maṇaṇāṇaṃ** — telepathic knowledge (manah-paryaya jnana)
- **kevalaṃ** — omniscient knowledge (kevala jnana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra details the five sub-types of knowledge-obscuring karma. Each sub-type blocks a specific form of knowledge that the soul inherently possesses. The five types of knowledge that are obscured are: (1) *mati jnana* — sensory or perceptual knowledge gained through the senses and mind; (2) *shruta jnana* — scriptural or verbal knowledge gained through study; (3) *avadhi jnana* — clairvoyant knowledge that perceives material things beyond sensory range; (4) *manah-paryaya jnana* — telepathic knowledge that reads the thoughts of others; and (5) *kevala jnana* — absolute omniscient knowledge. The soul's capacity for all five is innate but blocked by these karmic veils.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The karma that blocks knowledge has five sub-types, each blocking a different kind of knowing — from basic learning to complete omniscience.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If my soul already possesses infinite knowledge, what am I doing that keeps reinforcing the veil over it?

## SUTRA 33.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिद्दा तहेव पयला, णिद्दाणिद्दा पयलपयला ।
तत्तो य थीणगिद्धी उ, पंचमा होइ णायव्वा ॥३३.५॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇiddā taheva payalā, ṇiddāṇiddā payalapayalā |
Tatto ya thīṇagiddhī u, paṃcamā hoi ṇāyavvā || 33.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Sleep, drowsiness, sleep-within-sleep, drowsiness-within-drowsiness, and sleepwalking — these are the five types of perception-obscuring karma to be known.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇiddā** — sleep (nidra)
- **payalā** — drowsiness (pracala)
- **ṇiddāṇiddā** — deep sleep / sleep-within-sleep (nidra-nidra)
- **payalapayalā** — heavy drowsiness / drowsiness-within-drowsiness (pracala-pracala)
- **thīṇagiddhī** — sleepwalking (styanagṛddhi)
- **paṃcamā** — the fifth
- **hoi** — is
- **ṇāyavvā** — to be known

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra describes the five sub-types within perception-obscuring karma (*darshanavaraniya*) related to sleep. These are: (1) *nidra* — ordinary sleep; (2) *pracala* — drowsiness or light sleep; (3) *nidra-nidra* — deep sleep from which one is difficult to awaken; (4) *pracala-pracala* — heavy drowsiness or stupor; and (5) *styanagṛddhi* — sleepwalking, where the body acts while consciousness is dormant. In addition to these five sleep-related sub-types, the darshanavaraniya karma also has four sub-types that obstruct the four basic types of perception (eye-perception, non-eye-perception, clairvoyant perception, and omniscient perception), making nine sub-types in total as elaborated in the commentary.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Perception-obscuring karma includes five types of sleep-states — from light drowsiness to sleepwalking — that cloud the soul's natural awareness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do I move through life in a kind of spiritual sleepwalking — physically active but inwardly unconscious?

## SUTRA 33.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चक्खुमचक्खुं ओहिस्स, दंसणे केवले दंसणे ।
एवं तु णवविगप्पं, णायव्वं दंसणावरणं ॥३३.६॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Cakkhum acakkhuṃ ohissa, daṃsaṇe kevale daṃsaṇe |
Evaṃ tu ṇavavigappaṃ, ṇāyavvaṃ daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ || 33.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Eye-perception, non-eye-perception, clairvoyant perception, and omniscient perception — along with the five sleep-types, perception-obscuring karma is thus to be known as ninefold.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **cakkhu** — eye / visual perception (cakshu darshana)
- **acakkhuṃ** — non-eye perception (acakshu darshana)
- **ohissa** — of clairvoyance (avadhi darshana)
- **daṃsaṇe** — perception
- **kevale daṃsaṇe** — omniscient perception (kevala darshana)
- **ṇavavigappaṃ** — nine varieties / ninefold
- **ṇāyavvaṃ** — to be known
- **daṃsaṇāvaraṇaṃ** — perception-obscuring karma

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra completes the enumeration of the sub-types of perception-obscuring karma. Adding the four types of perception-obstruction to the five sleep-types from the previous sutra yields nine sub-types in total. The four perceptions that are obscured are: (1) *cakshu darshana* — visual perception through the eyes; (2) *acakshu darshana* — non-visual perception through the other senses; (3) *avadhi darshana* — clairvoyant perception; and (4) *kevala darshana* — omniscient perception. Each sub-type of darshanavaraniya karma specifically blocks one of these nine capacities.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Adding four types of blocked perception (sight, non-sight senses, clairvoyance, and omniscient perception) to the five sleep-types gives nine total sub-types of perception-obscuring karma.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Beyond physical sight, what deeper forms of perception might I be capable of if these karmic veils were lifted?

## SUTRA 33.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वेयणीयं पि च दुविहं, सायमसायं च आहियं ।
सायस्स उ बहू भेया, एमेव असायस्स वि ॥३३.७॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Veyaṇīyaṃ pi ca duvihaṃ, sāyam asāyaṃ ca āhiyaṃ |
Sāyassa u bahū bheyā, emeva asāyassa vi || 33.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Feeling-producing karma is also of two kinds — pleasant and unpleasant; and the pleasant has many sub-divisions, as does the unpleasant.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **veyaṇīyaṃ** — feeling-producing karma (vedaniya)
- **duvihaṃ** — of two kinds
- **sāyam** — pleasant / agreeable (shata vedaniya)
- **asāyaṃ** — unpleasant / disagreeable (ashata vedaniya)
- **āhiyaṃ** — it is said / it is declared
- **bahū bheyā** — many divisions / many sub-types
- **emeva** — likewise / in the same way

### 5. COMMENTARY

Feeling-producing karma (*vedaniya*) is divided into two fundamental types: (1) *shata vedaniya* — which produces pleasant, agreeable experiences; and (2) *ashata vedaniya* — which produces unpleasant, painful experiences. Every experience of pleasure or pain that a being undergoes is the fruition of this karma. The sutra also indicates that both types have further sub-divisions. When this karma matures, it brings the soul feelings of happiness or suffering regardless of external circumstances, explaining why the same situation may feel different to different beings.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The karma that produces feelings has two types — one causes pleasant experiences and the other causes painful ones.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I experience unexpected joy or sorrow without a clear external cause, could this be the ripening of my own feeling-producing karma?

## SUTRA 33.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मोहणिज्जं पि दुविहं, दंसणे चरणे तहा ।
दंसणे तिविहं वुत्तं, चरणे दुविहं भवे ॥३३.८॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Mohaṇijjaṃ pi duvihaṃ, daṃsaṇe caraṇe tahā |
Daṃsaṇe tivihaṃ vuttaṃ, caraṇe duvihaṃ bhave || 33.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Deluding karma is also of two kinds — perception-deluding and conduct-deluding; the perception-deluding is said to be of three types, and the conduct-deluding is of two types.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **mohaṇijjaṃ** — deluding karma (mohaniya)
- **duvihaṃ** — of two kinds
- **daṃsaṇe** — perception-deluding (darshana mohaniya)
- **caraṇe** — conduct-deluding (charitra mohaniya)
- **tivihaṃ** — of three types
- **vuttaṃ** — it is said
- **bhave** — it is / it becomes

### 5. COMMENTARY

Deluding karma (*mohaniya*) is the most dangerous of all eight karmas, as it directly attacks right faith and right conduct. It has two main divisions: (1) *darshana mohaniya* — which deludes perception/faith, causing wrong belief; and (2) *charitra mohaniya* — which deludes conduct, causing passions and moral weakness. The perception-deluding type is further divided into three: right faith-deluding, mixed faith-deluding, and wrong faith-deluding. The conduct-deluding type has two broad categories: the passions (*kashaya*) and the quasi-passions (*nokashaya*). The commentary elaborates that the total sub-types of mohaniya karma number twenty-eight (3 + 25 = 28).

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Deluding karma has two forms — one distorts your beliefs and the other corrupts your behavior. Together they are the most powerful obstacles to spiritual progress.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can I distinguish in my own life between moments when my beliefs are distorted versus when my conduct is weakened by passions?

## SUTRA 33.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सम्मत्तं चेव मिच्छत्तं, सम्मामिच्छत्तमेव य ।
एयाओ तिण्णि पयडीओ, मोहणिज्जस्स दंसणे ॥३३.९॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sammatttaṃ ceva micchatttaṃ, sammāmicchatttameva ya |
Eyāo tiṇṇi payaḍīo, mohaṇijjassa daṃsaṇe || 33.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Right faith, wrong faith, and mixed faith — these are the three sub-types of the perception-deluding aspect of deluding karma.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sammatttaṃ** — right faith / right belief (samyaktva)
- **micchatttaṃ** — wrong faith / wrong belief (mithyatva)
- **sammāmicchatttam** — mixed faith (samyag-mithyatva)
- **eyāo** — these
- **tiṇṇi** — three
- **payaḍīo** — sub-types / prakritis
- **mohaṇijjassa** — of deluding karma
- **daṃsaṇe** — in the perception-deluding aspect

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra specifies the three sub-types of perception-deluding karma (*darshana mohaniya*). When *samyaktva mohaniya* operates, it partially veils right faith but allows a degree of correct understanding. When *mithyatva mohaniya* operates, it completely distorts one's faith, leading to wrong views about reality. When *samyag-mithyatva mohaniya* operates, it creates a mixed state where the soul oscillates between right and wrong belief. Of these, wrong faith (*mithyatva*) is the most binding, as it keeps the soul firmly attached to false views and prevents the beginning of the spiritual path.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The karma that distorts faith has three types — one that allows right belief, one that causes completely wrong belief, and one that creates a confusing mix of both.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Am I certain that my current beliefs about reality are based on genuine understanding, or could a karmic veil be distorting what I think is true?

## SUTRA 33.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चरित्तमोहणं कम्मं, दुविहं तु विचारियं ।
कसाय मोहणिज्जस्स तहेव य ॥३३.१०॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Carittamohaṇaṃ kammaṃ, duvihaṃ tu vicāriyaṃ |
Kasāya mohaṇijjassa taheva ya || 33.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Conduct-deluding karma, when examined, is of two kinds — the passions (*kashaya*) and the quasi-passions (*nokashaya*).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **carittamohaṇaṃ** — conduct-deluding (charitra mohaniya)
- **kammaṃ** — karma
- **duvihaṃ** — of two kinds
- **vicāriyaṃ** — when examined / when considered
- **kasāya** — passions (kashaya)
- **mohaṇijjassa** — of deluding karma

### 5. COMMENTARY

Conduct-deluding karma is divided into two categories: (1) *kashaya* — the four primary passions of anger (*krodha*), pride (*mana*), deceit (*maya*), and greed (*lobha*); and (2) *nokashaya* — the quasi-passions including laughter, liking, disliking, sorrow, fear, and disgust. The four primary passions each have four degrees of intensity, yielding sixteen kashaya sub-types. The nine nokashaya types bring the total conduct-deluding sub-types to twenty-five. Together with the three perception-deluding sub-types, mohaniya karma has twenty-eight sub-types in all.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Conduct-deluding karma works through two channels — the four major passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed) and the quasi-passions like laughter, fear, and disgust.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Among the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, and greed — which one most frequently controls my behavior without my even noticing?

## SUTRA 33.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सोलसविहं भेण्णं, कम्मं तु कसायज्जं ।
सत्तविहं णवविहं वा, कम्मं च णोकसायज्जं ॥३३.११॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Solasavihaṃ bheṇṇaṃ, kammaṃ tu kasāyajjaṃ |
Sattavihaṃ ṇavavihaṃ vā, kammaṃ ca ṇokasāyajjaṃ || 33.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The passion-produced karma is of sixteen kinds; and the quasi-passion-produced karma is of seven or nine kinds.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **solasavihaṃ** — of sixteen kinds
- **bheṇṇaṃ** — divisions / varieties
- **kammaṃ** — karma
- **kasāyajjaṃ** — passion-produced (kashaya)
- **sattavihaṃ** — of seven kinds
- **ṇavavihaṃ** — of nine kinds
- **ṇokasāyajjaṃ** — quasi-passion-produced (nokashaya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The sixteen sub-types of passion-related conduct-deluding karma arise from four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed), each manifesting at four levels of intensity: (1) *anantanubandhi* — passions of infinite binding that prevent right faith; (2) *apratyakhyanavaraniya* — passions that prevent partial renunciation; (3) *pratyakhyanavaraniya* — passions that prevent complete renunciation; and (4) *sanjvalana* — smoldering passions that prevent perfect conduct. The nokashaya (quasi-passions) are: laughter (*hasya*), liking (*rati*), disliking (*arati*), sorrow (*shoka*), fear (*bhaya*), disgust (*jugupsa*), and the three types of sexual inclination — making nine in total. Some texts count seven by excluding the sexual inclinations.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Each of the four passions has four intensity levels, giving sixteen types. The quasi-passions like laughter, sorrow, and fear add seven to nine more types.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

At what intensity level do my passions usually operate — are they so deep they prevent me from even seeing the truth, or are they mild enough that I can still choose wisely?

## SUTRA 33.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णेरइय तिरिक्खाडं, मणुस्साडं तहेव य ।
देवाउयं चउत्थं तुं, आउकम्मं चउव्विहं ॥३३.१२॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇeraiya tirikkhāḍaṃ, maṇussāḍaṃ taheva ya |
Devāuyaṃ cautthaṃ tuṃ, āukammaṃ cauvvihaṃ || 33.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Hellish, sub-human, human, and celestial — life-determining karma is thus of four kinds.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇeraiya** — hellish (naraka ayushya)
- **tirikkhāḍaṃ** — sub-human / animal (tiryañca ayushya)
- **maṇussāḍaṃ** — human (manushya ayushya)
- **devāuyaṃ** — celestial / divine (deva ayushya)
- **cautthaṃ** — the fourth
- **āukammaṃ** — life-determining karma (ayushya karma)

### 5. COMMENTARY

- **savvesu** — in all
- **payesaggaṃ** — spatial extent (pradesha)
- **savva jīvesu** — in all living beings
- **aicchiyaṃ** — is established / is declared

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra addresses *anubhaga bandha* — the intensity or fruition-strength of karmic bondage. The intensity of karma determines how powerfully it manifests when it matures. Every karma has a range of intensity from minimum to maximum. At each moment of binding, the soul's passions and activities determine not just what type of karma binds, but how intensely it will eventually manifest. The spatial extent (*pradesha bandha*) is established as equal across all beings at the moment of binding. The interplay between quantity, duration, and intensity creates the unique karmic profile of each soul, explaining the infinite diversity of experiences in the universe.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The intensity of karma — how strongly it hits you when it ripens — varies greatly, and this intensity is set at the moment of binding based on the strength of your passions.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Two people may have the same type of karma, yet one suffers more intensely — how does understanding karmic intensity help me accept the diversity of human experience?

## SUTRA 33.25

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तम्हा एप्पसिं कम्माणं, अणुभागा विवाणिया ।
एप्पसिं संवरे चेव, खवणे य जय बुहो ॥ ति बेमि ॥३३.२५॥

⚠️ Prakrit reconstructed from शब्दार्थ and Gujarati commentary.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tamhā eppsiṃ kammāṇaṃ, aṇubhāgā vivāṇiyā |
Eppsiṃ saṃvare ceva, khavaṇe ya jaya buho || ti bemi || 33.25 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Therefore, knowing the intensity of these karmas, one should strive for both stoppage (*samvara*) and shedding (*nirjara*) of karma — this is what I declare, says the wise one.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tamhā** — therefore
- **eppsiṃ** — of these
- **kammāṇaṃ** — karmas
- **aṇubhāgā** — intensities (anubhaga)
- **vivāṇiyā** — having been described / having understood
- **saṃvare** — in stoppage of karma (samvara)
- **khavaṇe** — in shedding / destruction of karma (nirjara / kshaya)
- **jaya** — victory / one should conquer
- **buho** — the wise one
- **ti bemi** — thus I say

### 5. COMMENTARY

This concluding sutra synthesizes the entire chapter's teaching into a practical imperative. Having understood all four aspects of karmic bondage — type (*prakriti*), quantity (*pradesha*), duration (*sthiti*), and intensity (*anubhaga*) — the wise seeker must now act. The two essential actions are *samvara* (stopping the influx of new karma through discipline, restraint, and mindfulness) and *nirjara* (shedding existing karma through austerity and spiritual practice). The phrase *ti bemi* ("thus I say") is a characteristic closing formula in Jain scripture, carrying the authority of the enlightened teacher. This chapter thus moves from theoretical knowledge of karma to the practical call for liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: After understanding all about karma — its types, quantity, duration, and intensity — the wise person works to stop new karma from coming in and burn off the karma already there.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Now that I understand the full structure of karmic bondage, am I ready to move from knowledge to action — to actively stop and shed my karma?
