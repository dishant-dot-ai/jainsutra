# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 21
# Samudrapālīya (The Chapter of Samudrapāla)

**Total Sutras:** 24
**Theme:** Samudrapāla, son of the lay disciple Pālita and born at sea, grows up in Champa city. Upon witnessing a condemned criminal being led to execution, he attains supreme saṃvega, takes dīkṣā, practices the full discipline of monastic life across 13 teaching verses, and ultimately destroys all karmas to attain liberation.

---

## SUTRA 21.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चंपाए पालिए णाम, सावए आसि वाणिए ।
महावीरस्स भगवओ, सीसे सो उ महप्पणो ॥२१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Caṃpāe Pālie ṇāma, sāvae āsi vāṇie |
Mahāvīrassa bhagavao, sīse so u mahappaṇo || 21.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In Champa city there lived a lay follower (shrāvaka) named Pālita, a merchant. He was a disciple — a great-souled one — of the Blessed Lord Mahāvīra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Caṃpāe** — in Champa city
- **Pālie ṇāma** — named Pālita
- **sāvae āsi vāṇie** — was a shrāvaka (lay disciple) and merchant
- **Mahāvīrassa bhagavao** — of the Blessed Lord Mahāvīra
- **sīse** — disciple
- **so u mahappaṇo** — that great-souled one

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens by anchoring the story in two coordinates: the city of Champa (a major Jain narrative hub) and the person of Pālita, who is both a merchant and a shrāvaka. The combination is significant. A shrāvaka is not merely a sympathiser — he is bound by five aṇuvratas (minor vows), three guṇavratas, and four śikṣāvratas. Pālita moves in the world of trade and sea-commerce, yet his inner life is structured by Jain ethical formation. The text calls him mahappaṇo — great-souled — which in Jain usage means one whose inner nobility exceeds what external station reveals. He is described as a disciple of Mahāvīra himself, grounding this chapter in direct lineage from the Tīrthaṃkara.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: In the city of Champa there was a merchant named Pālita, who was a devoted lay disciple of Lord Mahāvīra.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to live in the world of commerce and trade while also being bound by ethical vows? Where in your own daily life do these two worlds meet?

## SUTRA 21.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिग्गंथे पावयणे, सावए से वि कोविए ।
पोएण वहरंते, पिहुंडं णगरमागए ॥२१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Niggaṃthe pāvaṇe, sāvae se vi kovie |
Poeṇa vaharaṃte, pihuṃḍaṃ ṇagaramāgae || 21.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That shrāvaka was well-versed in the Nirgrantha (Jain) teachings. Engaged in sea-trade by ship, he arrived at the city of Pihunda.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Niggaṃthe pāvaṇe** — in the Nirgrantha (Jain) teachings
- **se vi kovie** — he was learned/expert
- **poeṇa** — by ship/boat
- **vaharaṃte** — while conducting trade
- **pihuṃḍaṃ ṇagaraṃ āgae** — arrived at the city of Pihunda

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kovie (Sanskrit: kovida) means one with precise, practised knowledge — not merely familiar but expert. Pālita was not a nominal Jain; he understood the Nirgrantha teachings with depth. Yet he also sailed across the sea for trade. Jain narrative literature has no contradiction here: the shrāvaka lives fully in the world while remaining inwardly anchored. The sea voyage to Pihunda establishes the geographical and narrative setting for the next event — the birth of his son — and carries the symbolic weight that this child born at sea will one day cross the far greater sea of saṃsāra.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Pālita was both a knowledgeable Jain and a sea-merchant. He sailed to the distant city of Pihunda for trade.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Knowledge and action — do you separate them in your own life, or do they inform each other? How does deep understanding of a teaching change the way you live?

## SUTRA 21.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पिहुंडे ववहरंतस्स, वाणिओ देइ धूयरं ।
तं सत्तं पडिगिज्झ, सदेसमह पत्थिओ ॥२१.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Pihuṃḍe vavaharaṃtassa, vāṇio dei dhūyaraṃ |
Taṃ sattaṃ paḍigijjha, sadesāmaha patthio || 21.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

While trading in Pihunda, a local merchant gave Pālita his daughter in marriage. Taking his pregnant wife, Pālita departed toward his homeland.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Pihuṃḍe vavaharaṃtassa** — to Pālita who was trading in Pihunda
- **vāṇio dei dhūyaraṃ** — a merchant gave his daughter (in marriage)
- **taṃ sattaṃ** — that (pregnant) wife
- **paḍigijjha** — taking with him
- **sadesāmaha patthio** — departed toward his own homeland

### 5. COMMENTARY

Pālita's righteousness and mercantile reputation in Pihunda were sufficient for a local merchant to give his daughter to him. The phrase sattaṃ — pregnant wife — is placed deliberately. The child who will become the chapter's hero is already present in the narrative, growing in the womb, even before the sea voyage home. The text is setting up the literal truth of the name Samudrapāla: this child will be born of the sea (samudra), and will eventually overcome the sea of existence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A merchant in Pihunda gave his daughter to Pālita in marriage. Pālita then began the voyage home with his pregnant wife.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Life's most significant events often arrive embedded in the ordinary — a marriage, a journey, a business trip. Where might something transformative already be forming unseen in your life?

## SUTRA 21.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह पालियस्स घरणी, समुद्दम्मि पसवइ ।
अह दारए तहिं जाए, समुदपालि ति णामए ॥२१.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha Pāliyassa gharaṇī, samudḍammi pasavai |
Aha dārae tahiṃ jāe, Samudapāli ti ṇāmae || 21.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

During the sea voyage, Pālita's wife gave birth to a son. Since he was born at sea, he was named Samudrapāla.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Pāliyassa gharaṇī** — Pālita's wife
- **samudḍammi** — in/on the sea
- **pasavai** — gave birth
- **dārae tahiṃ jāe** — a son was born there
- **Samudapāli ti ṇāmae** — he was named Samudrapāla ("protector of the sea" / "born of the sea")

### 5. COMMENTARY

The name Samudrapāla carries a double resonance. Literally it means one who guards or protects the sea (samudra + pāla), but the circumstances of his birth make it mean simply: born of the sea. The Jain tradition of meaningful naming is at work here — the name not only records the circumstance but anticipates the soul's future: just as he entered the world crossing the sea, he will exit saṃsāra by crossing the ocean of existence. Birth at sea — neither fully on land nor fully arrived — is a liminal beginning for a soul whose entire arc is movement from worldly life toward liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: While sailing home, Pālita's wife gave birth to a son at sea. They named him Samudrapāla — born of the ocean.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where were you born, and does that origin carry any meaning or direction for your life? Or does meaning only emerge later, in looking back?

## SUTRA 21.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खेमेण आगए चंपं, सावए वाणिए घरं ।
संवड्डुइ घरे तस्स, दारए से सुहोइए ॥२१.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Khemeṇa āgae caṃpaṃ, sāvae vāṇie gharaṃ |
Saṃvaḍḍui ghare tassa, dārae se suhoie || 21.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The shrāvaka safely returned to his home in Champa city. The boy Samudrapāla grew up happily in his father's house.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Khemeṇa āgae** — safely arrived/returned
- **caṃpaṃ** — to Champa city
- **sāvae vāṇie gharaṃ** — the shrāvaka-merchant's home
- **saṃvaḍḍui** — grew up
- **ghare tassa** — in his (father's) house
- **dārae se suhoie** — that son, happily

### 5. COMMENTARY

Suhoie — happily — is not a trivial word in a Jain sūtra. It signals that Samudrapāla grew up in conditions of genuine well-being: materially secure, spiritually formed by a shrāvaka household, nurtured by a father who carried deep Jain learning. The Jain biographical narrative always carefully establishes the quality of a soul's formation before the turning point. The house of Pālita was a house of both prosperity and principle — an ideal environment for a soul about to make a radical choice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The family safely reached Champa, and Samudrapāla grew up happily in his father's house.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What kind of home did you grow up in, and how has that formation — for good or difficult — shaped your present path?

## SUTRA 21.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बावत्तरि कलाओ य, सिक्किखए णीइकोविए ।
जोवण्णेण य संपण्णे, सुरूवे पियदंसणे ॥२१.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Bāvattari kalāo ya, sikkikhae ṇīikovie |
Jovaṇṇeṇa ya saṃpaṇṇe, surūve piyadaṃsaṇe || 21.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He learned the 72 arts and became expert in ethics and propriety. Endowed with youth, he was handsome and pleasing in appearance.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Bāvattari kalāo** — 72 arts
- **sikkikhae** — learned/mastered
- **ṇīikovie** — expert in ethics/propriety
- **jovaṇṇeṇa ya saṃpaṇṇe** — endowed with/arrived at youth
- **surūve** — handsome, of excellent form
- **piyadaṃsaṇe** — pleasing to behold, agreeable in appearance

### 5. COMMENTARY

The 72 arts (bāvattari kalā) was the standard curriculum of accomplishment for a well-born youth in ancient Indian tradition — encompassing music, sculpture, painting, dance, weapon-craft, mathematics, elephant-lore, horse-riding, and more. Ṇīikovie (nītikovida) adds the crucial element: he was not merely technically accomplished but ethically formed — wise in the principles of righteous conduct. Together with his physical beauty and pleasing presence, Samudrapāla was, by every conventional measure, a complete and fortunate young man. The text builds this portrait carefully — because the greater the worldly endowment, the more striking will be his renunciation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Samudrapāla mastered the 72 arts, was trained in ethics, and grew into a handsome, accomplished young man.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Worldly accomplishments are not opposed to spiritual seeking — but at what point does what you have become a test of what you are willing to release?

## SUTRA 21.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स रूववइं भज्जं, पिया आणेइ रूविणिं ।
पासाए कीलए रम्मे, देवो दोगुंदओ जहा ॥२१.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tassa rūvavaiṃ bhajjaṃ, piyā āṇei rūviṇiṃ |
Pāsāe kīlae ramme, devo doguṃdao jahā || 21.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

His father brought him Rūpiṇī, a beautiful bride. He enjoyed himself pleasantly in the palace — like a Diguṃdaka deva.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tassa rūvavaiṃ bhajjaṃ** — for him, a beautiful wife
- **piyā āṇei** — father brought
- **rūviṇiṃ** — named Rūpiṇī (Rukmini)
- **pāsāe kīlae ramme** — enjoyed pleasantly in the palace
- **devo doguṃdao jahā** — like a Diguṃdaka deva (a class of devas who play freely without obstruction)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Rūpiṇī (also rendered Rukmini) — the name itself means one of beautiful form. The comparison to a Diguṃdaka deva is precise: this class of celestial beings is described in Jain cosmology as those who enjoy sensory pleasure freely, unimpeded. The image deliberately places Samudrapāla at the peak of worldly happiness — beautiful wife, pleasing palace, carefree enjoyment. The Jain narrative structure requires this: the saṃvega (spiritual shock) that follows must come from within a life that lacked nothing. Renunciation born of poverty or failure is comprehensible; renunciation from the midst of everything — that is the harder and more instructive story.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: His father arranged his marriage to the beautiful Rūpiṇī, and Samudrapāla lived in his palace in great pleasure and happiness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When everything you could want is present, what could possibly move you to look beyond it? What kind of vision breaks through contentment?

## SUTRA 21.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह अण्णया कयाइं, पासायालोयणे ठिओ ।
वज्झ-मंडण-सोभागं, वज्झं पासइ वज्झगं ॥२१.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha aṇṇayā kayāiṃ, pāsāyāloyaṇe ṭhio |
Vajjha-maṃḍaṇa-sobhāgaṃ, vajjhaṃ pāsai vajjhagaṃ || 21.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One day, standing at the palace balcony, he saw a criminal being led to the execution stake — adorned with the marks of a death sentence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇṇayā kayāiṃ** — one day, at some time
- **pāsāyāloyaṇe ṭhio** — standing at the palace's observation window/balcony
- **vajjha-maṃḍaṇa-sobhāgaṃ** — adorned with the ornaments/marks of condemnation (death-sentence signs)
- **vajjhaṃ pāsai** — he saw (being led to) the execution stake
- **vajjhagaṃ** — a criminal condemned to death

### 5. COMMENTARY

The pivot of the entire chapter arrives without ceremony: one day, from his palace balcony, Samudrapāla sees a condemned man. The execution procession in ancient times was a public spectacle — the criminal paraded through the city bearing the marks of his sentence before being led to the killing ground. Vajjha-maṃḍaṇa: the word "ornamentation" is used deliberately — the very same word used for festive decoration now describes the signs of death. The juxtaposition is sharp. Samudrapāla stands in his palace of pleasure; below is the naked truth of what inauspicious karma produces. This is the classical Jain moment of pratibodha — the awakening encounter that breaks open complacency.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: One day from his palace balcony, Samudrapāla saw a criminal dressed in the marks of his death sentence being led to execution.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the "condemned man" you have seen — the moment or sight that broke through the surface of comfortable life and made you think differently?

## SUTRA 21.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं पासिऊण संविग्गो, समुद्पालो इणमब्बवी ।
अहो असुभाणं कम्माण, णिज्जाणं पावगं इमं ॥२१.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Taṃ pāsiūṇa saṃviggo, Samudapālo iṇamabbavī |
Aho asubhāṇaṃ kammāṇa, ṇijjāṇaṃ pāvagaṃ imaṃ || 21.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Upon seeing that, Samudrapāla became filled with saṃvega and said: "Alas! This is the sinful and sorrowful final fruit of inauspicious karmas — here directly witnessed."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taṃ pāsiūṇa** — upon seeing that
- **saṃviggo** — filled with saṃvega (deep spiritual agitation/renunciation-impulse)
- **iṇamabbavī** — said this
- **aho** — alas! (exclamation of profound recognition)
- **asubhāṇaṃ kammāṇa** — of inauspicious/unwholesome karmas
- **ṇijjāṇaṃ** — the final result/fruit
- **pāvagaṃ imaṃ** — this sinful/suffering outcome

### 5. COMMENTARY

Saṃvega is one of the most important technical terms in Jain soteriology. It is not mere fear or sadness — it is the specific inner movement that arises when the soul directly perceives the suffering-producing nature of karma and worldly existence. Samudrapāla does not merely feel sorry for the criminal; he sees in that man the universal truth: inauspicious karmas have visible, terrible, inescapable results. The exclamation "Aho!" — Alas! — is the sound of a soul that has understood something it cannot un-understand. The commentary notes that he attained jātismaraṇa (memory of past lives) at this moment, which deepened the recognition into certainty. This is not an intellectual conclusion; it is direct knowing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Seeing the condemned man, Samudrapāla was struck by deep spiritual awakening and exclaimed: "This is the terrible fruit of bad karmas — I can see it directly."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever had a moment of "Aho!" — where something you saw made you understand the nature of cause and consequence in a way that could not be set aside?

## SUTRA 21.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संबुद्धो सो तहिं भगवं, परमसंवेगमागओ ।
आपुच्छ अम्मापियरो, पव्वइए अणगारियं ॥२१.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Saṃbuddho so tahiṃ bhagavaṃ, paramasaṃvegamāgao |
Āpuccha ammāpiyaro, pavvaie aṇagāriyaṃ || 21.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Right there, the fortunate Samudrapāla attained perfect awakening and supreme saṃvega. Taking his parents' permission, he took dīkṣā into the homeless monastic life.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **saṃbuddho** — attained perfect awakening/understanding
- **so bhagavaṃ** — that fortunate/blessed one (Samudrapāla)
- **paramasaṃvegaṃ āgao** — attained supreme saṃvega
- **āpuccha ammāpiyaro** — asking permission of his mother and father
- **pavvaie** — took initiation (dīkṣā)
- **aṇagāriyaṃ** — into the homeless/monastic life

### 5. COMMENTARY

Saṃbuddho — perfectly awakened. Not partially moved, not merely disturbed. The text uses the strongest word: complete awakening in that moment. Bhagavaṃ — blessed, fortunate — is the epithet usually reserved for the Tīrthaṃkara or the liberated; applied to Samudrapāla here, it signals that his inner state in this moment is already aligned with the highest. The act of asking parents' permission before dīkṣā is not weakness — it is the shrāvaka's proper code of conduct, the final act of householder responsibility before the life of the householder ends. Then: pavvaie aṇagāriyaṃ — he took dīkṣā. The life in the palace is over. The chapter now turns to what the monastic life must look like.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Samudrapāla attained complete spiritual awakening then and there. He asked his parents' permission and took dīkṣā, becoming a homeless monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Awakening can arrive in an instant, but acting on it requires stepping out of every structure that held you. What would it take for you to act decisively on a moment of deep understanding?

## SUTRA 21.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहितु सग्गंथ महाकिलेसं, महंतमोहं किसिणं भयावहं ।
परियायधम्मं चाभिरोयएज्जा, वयाणि सीलाणि परीसहे य ॥२१.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jahitu saggaṃtha mahākilesaṃ, mahaṃtamohaṃ kisiṇaṃ bhayāvahaṃ |
Pariyāyadhammaṃ cābhiroyaejjā, vayāṇi sīlāṇi parīsahe ya || 21.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having abandoned complete attachment — the great affliction, total delusion, and all that is fearful — he became devoted to the path of renunciation, the vows, the precepts, and patient endurance of hardships.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **saggaṃtha** — complete/total attachment (parigraha)
- **mahākilesaṃ** — the great affliction/suffering
- **kisiṇaṃ** — complete, entire
- **mahaṃtamohaṃ** — the great delusion
- **bhayāvahaṃ** — that which produces fear
- **Jahitu** — having abandoned
- **pariyāyadhammaṃ** — the path/conduct of renunciation (pravrajyā dharma)
- **vayāṇi** — vows (mahāvratas)
- **sīlāṇi** — precepts/disciplines
- **parīsahe ya** — and hardships/parishads

### 5. COMMENTARY

The teaching section of the chapter begins here (sutras 11–23) — thirteen verses elaborating the content of monastic life as Samudrapāla would have been instructed. Sag-gaṃtha (Sanskrit: sa-grantha) means "with grantha" — attachment in its entirety: possessiveness, emotional binding, and the tying of the self to external objects and persons. The commentary identifies mahākilesa as the great affliction of saṃsāric existence itself; mahaṃtamohaṃ as the great moha (delusion-karma) that keeps souls bound; and bhayāvahaṃ as the fear-inducing nature of attachment — because what you cling to, you fear losing. Having released all of this, the muni enters pariyāyadharma: the ordered life of renunciation structured by the three pillars named — vows, precepts, and equanimous endurance of hardship.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Abandoning all attachment, delusion, and fear, Samudrapāla devoted himself to the path of renunciation — its vows, disciplines, and patient acceptance of hardship.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What attachments in your life function as both a comfort and a source of fear? What would it mean to release them — not by force, but by understanding?

## SUTRA 21.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहिंस सच्चं च अतेणगं च, तत्तो य बंभं अपिरग्गहं च ।
पडिवज्जिया पंच महव्वयाणि, चरिज्ज धम्मं जिणदेसियं विदू ॥२१.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ahiṃsa saccaṃ ca ateṇagaṃ ca, tatto ya baṃbhaṃ apiraggahaṃ ca |
Paḍivajjiyā pañca mahavvayāṇi, carijja dhammaṃ jiṇadesiyaṃ vidū || 21.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The wise muni accepted the five great vows — ahiṃsā (non-violence), truth, non-stealing, brahmacharya, and non-possession — and practiced the Dharma as taught by the Jinas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ahiṃsa** — non-violence (ahiṃsā)
- **saccaṃ** — truth (satya)
- **ateṇagaṃ** — non-stealing (asteya/adattādāna)
- **baṃbhaṃ** — brahmacharya (celibacy)
- **apiraggahaṃ** — non-possession (aparigraha)
- **paḍivajjiyā pañca mahavvayāṇi** — having accepted the five great vows
- **carijja dhammaṃ jiṇadesiyaṃ** — practiced the Dharma taught by the Jinas
- **vidū** — the wise muni

### 5. COMMENTARY

The five mahāvratas are the structural backbone of Jain monastic life — not five separate rules but one integrated orientation of the self toward non-harm, truth, integrity, chastity, and non-acquisition. Each vow has five sub-clauses (bhāvanās) governing its application through thought, speech, and action. The text calls the muni vidū — the knowing one, the wise — because acceptance of these vows is not forced compliance but informed choice arising from correct understanding of karma. Jiṇadesiya: taught by the Jinas. This grounds the vows not in human convention but in the direct insight of the liberated ones who have seen the nature of karma and its cessation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Samudrapāla accepted the five great vows — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, and non-possession — and lived by the Dharma of the Jinas.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the five vows do you find most challenging to even partially live by — and what does that difficulty reveal about your attachments?

## SUTRA 21.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वेहिं भूएहिं दयाणुकंपी, खंतिखमे संजय बंभयारी ।
सावज्ज जोगं परिवज्जयंतो, चरिज्ज भिक्खू सुसमाहिं इंदिए ॥२१.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Savvehiṃ bhūehiṃ dayāṇukaṃpī, khaṃtikhaṃme saṃjaya baṃbhayārī |
Sāvajja jogaṃ parivajjayaṃto, carijja bhikkhu susamāhiṃ iṃdie || 21.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Compassionate toward all beings, forbearing, restrained, and celibate, the bhikshu abandons all sinful activities and wanders with senses well-composed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **savvehiṃ bhūehiṃ** — toward all beings
- **dayāṇukaṃpī** — compassionate, feeling with (anukampā)
- **khaṃtikhaṃme** — forbearing (kṣānti), patient (kṣama)
- **saṃjaya** — restrained, self-controlled
- **baṃbhayārī** — celibate (brahmacarya practitioner)
- **sāvajja jogaṃ parivajjayaṃto** — abandoning all sinful/blameworthy activities
- **carijja bhikkhu** — the bhikshu wanders/lives
- **susamāhiṃ iṃdie** — with senses well-composed and settled

### 5. COMMENTARY

Four qualities are listed in rapid sequence: dayāṇukaṃpī (compassion as active empathy for all beings), khaṃtikhaṃme (the double quality of patience as forbearance toward adversity and kṣamā as forgiveness of offenders), saṃjaya (the restrained one — one who has gathered and disciplined the self), and baṃbhayārī (the celibate — one whose vital energy is redirected entirely from procreative toward liberative ends). These are not external rules; they are the inner texture of the monastic person. Sāvajja-yoga — sinful activities — refers to those engagements of mind, speech, and body that produce karma. To abandon these while wandering is to turn every step of every day into a karma-destroying act.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk lives with compassion for all beings, patience, self-control, and celibacy — abandoning all harmful actions and moving through the world with calm, steady senses.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the four qualities named — compassion, patience, restraint, celibacy — which one, if cultivated more deeply, would most transform your interactions with others?

## SUTRA 21.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कालेण कालं विहरेज्ज रट्ठे, बलाबलं जाणिय अप्पणो य ।
सीहो व सद्देण ण संतसेज्जा, वयजोग सुच्चा ण असभमाहु ॥२१.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Kāleṇa kālaṃ viharejja raṭṭhe, balābalaṃ jāṇiya appaṇo ya |
Sīho va saddeṇa ṇa saṃtasejjā, vayajogaṃ soccā ṇa asabhamāhu || 21.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The muni wanders through the land at the proper time, knowing his own strength and weakness. Like a lion, he is not frightened by harsh sounds; and having heard words of suffering, he does not respond with cruel or improper speech.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kāleṇa kālaṃ** — at the proper time, according to time
- **viharejja raṭṭhe** — wanders through the country/land
- **balābalaṃ jāṇiya** — knowing (his own) strength and weakness
- **appaṇo ya** — and of the self
- **sīho va** — like a lion
- **saddeṇa ṇa saṃtasejjā** — is not frightened by (harsh) sounds
- **vayajogaṃ soccā** — having heard the words that produce suffering
- **ṇa asabhamāhu** — does not speak improper/harsh/cruel words

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three instructions are woven together here. First: movement must be calibrated to time — the monk travels when appropriate, rests when appropriate, accounts for his physical capacity (bala) and its limits (abala). Self-knowledge is a prerequisite for disciplined movement. Second: fearlessness — the lion metaphor is classic in Jain literature. The lion does not run from noise. The monk who has understood the nature of self does not flee from criticism, provocation, or threat. Third: restraint of speech even when provoked. Hearing painful or hostile words, the monk does not retaliate — not because he lacks the ability, but because the vow of satya and ahiṃsā extends to speech: cruel words are a form of violence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk travels wisely, knowing his own capacity. Like a lion unfazed by noise, he is not frightened by harsh words — and he never responds to cruelty with cruelty.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone speaks harshly to you, what is your first impulse? What would it take to respond with silence or gentleness instead of retaliation?

## SUTRA 21.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उवेहमाणो उ परिव्वएज्जा, पियमपियं सव्व तितिक्खएज्जा ।
ण सव्व सव्वत्थाभिरोयएज्जा, ण याविं पूयं गरहं च संजए ॥२१.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Uvehimāṇo u parivvaejjā, piyamapiyaṃ savva titikkhaejjā |
Ṇa savva savvatthābhiroyaejjā, ṇa yāviṃ pūyaṃ garahaṃ ca saṃjae || 21.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Reflecting on higher truths, the saṃyami wanders, enduring equally all things pleasant and unpleasant. He does not crave all things everywhere, and remains indifferent to praise and blame alike.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **uvehimāṇo** — reflecting, keeping higher things in mind (upeha/upekṣā)
- **parivvaejjā** — wanders (as a monk)
- **piyam apiyaṃ** — pleasant and unpleasant
- **savva titikkhaejjā** — endures all equally
- **ṇa savva savvattha** — not all things in all places
- **ābhiroyaejjā** — desires/craves
- **pūyaṃ** — praise, worship, honour
- **garahaṃ** — blame, censure
- **ṇa yāviṃ... saṃjae** — the saṃyami (restrained one) does not (react to) even these

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three distinct inner stances are described here. Uvehimāṇo — the monk moves through the world while keeping awareness turned toward what is ultimate; he walks in the physical but his orientation is toward the transcendent. Titikkha (tितिक्षा) — equanimous endurance of both pleasant and painful — is the practice that eliminates both craving (toward what is pleasant) and aversion (toward what is painful), the twin generators of karma. The final line goes deeper still: not only neutral toward pleasant and unpleasant experiences, but also neutral toward praise and blame — the two most seductive and most destabilising forces in social life. The saṃyami is moved by neither.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk wanders with his mind on higher things, bearing pleasures and pains equally, not craving or avoiding anything, and unmoved by praise or blame.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How do you respond differently to praise versus blame? Which one is harder for you to receive with equanimity — and what does that asymmetry reveal?

## SUTRA 21.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणेगछंदामिह माणवेहिं, जे भावओ संपगरेइ भिक्खू ।
भयभेरवा तत्थ उइंति भीमा, दिव्वा मणुस्सा अदुवा तिरिच्छा ॥२१.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aṇegacchaṃdāmiha māṇavehiṃ, je bhāvao saṃpagarei bhikkhu |
Bhayabheravā tattha uiṃti bhīmā, divvā maṇussā aduvā tiricchā || 21.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In this world, humans hold many varied inclinations. The difficulties that arise against the bhikshu include terrible and fearful ones — from devas, humans, or animals.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇegacchaṃdā** — many/varied inclinations, desires, dispositions
- **māṇavehiṃ** — among humans
- **je bhāvao saṃpagarei bhikkhu** — whatever difficulties/upasargas befall the bhikshu
- **bhayabheravā** — fearful and terror-inducing
- **bhīmā** — terrible, frightening
- **tattha uiṃti** — arise there
- **divvā** — from devas
- **maṇussā** — from humans
- **aduvā tiricchā** — or from animals

### 5. COMMENTARY

The text now catalogues the sources of hardship. Humans carry many kinds of inclinations — some sympathetic to monks, many hostile. The three-fold source of upasargas (adversities): divine, human, and animal — is a classical Jain framework. Devas may test the monk's equanimity through supernatural terror or temptation; humans through physical attack, insult, or challenge; animals through biting, stinging, or obstructing. The bhikshu moves through a world that will not be uniformly welcoming. This is not pessimism — it is preparation. A monk who has anticipated adversity is not surprised by it and therefore not destabilised.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: People in this world have many different attitudes toward monks. Terrible difficulties can come from devas, humans, or animals — and the monk must be ready.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you prepare yourself psychologically for difficulty before entering situations where it is likely — or do you only react when it arrives? What changes if you anticipate hardship in advance?

## SUTRA 21.17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

परीसहा दुव्विसहा अणेगे, सीयंति जत्थ बहु कायरा णरा ।
से तत्थ पत्ते ण विहज्ज भिक्खू, संगामसीसे इव णागरायां ॥२१.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Parīsahā duvvisahā aṇege, sīyaṃti jattha bahu kāyarā ṇarā |
Se tattha patte ṇa vihajja bhikkhu, saṃgāmasīse iva ṇāgarāyāṃ || 21.17 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Many hardships are unbearable, and cowardly men break and wilt under them. But when such hardships befall the bhikshu, he does not waver — like the king of elephants at the forefront of battle.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **parīsahā duvvisahā** — hardships that are very difficult to bear
- **aṇege** — many
- **sīyaṃti** — wilt, collapse, lose spirit
- **jattha bahu kāyarā ṇarā** — where many cowardly people (are broken)
- **se tattha patte** — when these befall him
- **ṇa vihajja bhikkhu** — the bhikshu does not waver/tremble
- **saṃgāmasīse** — at the forefront of battle
- **ṇāgarāyāṃ iva** — like the king of elephants

### 5. COMMENTARY

The parīṣaha list in Jain doctrine contains 22 hardships that a monk must endure: hunger, thirst, cold, heat, insect bites, nakedness (or harsh cloth), restlessness, women (temptation), wandering, the place of rest, abuse, physical punishment, begging, failure to obtain alms, disease, contact sensations, dirt, honour and dishonour, learning, ignorance, lack of faith in others, and failure to attain liberation. Many of these will break an ordinary person — "cowardly men wilt." The bhikshu, however, stands like the elephant king at the vanguard of a battle: the elephant that leads the army faces the most danger and does not retreat. This is courage not as aggression but as unmovable steadiness under fire.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Many hardships break ordinary people. But when these same hardships fall on the bhikshu, he stands firm — like the lead elephant at the front of a battle, immovable.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What hardship in your life has most tested your steadiness? Did you wilt, or hold firm — and what made the difference?

## SUTRA 21.18

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सीओसिणा दंसमसगा य फासा, आयंका विविहा फुसंति देहं ।
अकुक्कुओ तत्थिहासएज्जा, रयाइं खेवेज्ज पुरेकडाइं ॥२१.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sīosiṇā daṃsamasagā ya phāsā, āyaṃkā vivihā phusaṃti dehaṃ |
Akukkuo tatthihāsaejjā, rayāiṃ kheevejja purekadāiṃ || 21.18 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Cold, heat, mosquitoes, flies, and various contacts — diseases and afflictions of many kinds touch the body. Without complaint, he endures them calmly and destroys previously accumulated karmas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sīosiṇā** — cold and heat
- **daṃsamasagā** — mosquitoes and flies
- **phāsā** — touch-sensations (thorn-stab, rough cloth, etc.)
- **āyaṃkā vivihā** — diseases and afflictions of many kinds
- **phusaṃti dehaṃ** — touch/affect the body
- **akukkuo** — without complaint, without lamentation
- **tattha ihāsaejjā** — endures there patiently
- **rayāiṃ... purekadāiṃ** — previously accumulated karmas (karma-rājasas)
- **kheevejja** — destroys, eliminates

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is precise about the mechanics of parīṣaha-endurance: it names specific physical discomforts — cold/heat (the climate-based hardships), insects (the biological constant of outdoor life), disease (the inevitable deterioration of the body). Akukkuo — without complaint. Not merely enduring in silence but without inner protest, without the secondary suffering of resistance and resentment. And the crucial consequence: rayāiṃ kheevejja purekadāiṃ — he destroys previously accumulated karmas. In Jain karma theory, bearing hardship with equanimity (without generating new karma through reactive thought, speech, or action) causes the existing karma-particles to be burned away. The hardship does not cause suffering; it causes liberation — when met with the correct inner stance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Cold, heat, insects, and disease touch the monk's body. He bears them without complaint — and in doing so, destroys the karmas he previously accumulated.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When something uncomfortable happens to your body — illness, pain, discomfort — do you add mental resistance to the physical sensation? What happens when you don't?

## SUTRA 21.19

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पहाय रागं च तहेव दोसं, मोहं च भिक्खू सययं वियक्खणो ।
मेरुव्व वाएण अकंपमाणो, परीसहे आयगुत्ते सहेज्जा ॥२१.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Pahāya rāgaṃ ca taheva dosaṃ, mohaṃ ca bhikkhu sayayaṃ viyakkhaṇo |
Meruvva vāeṇa akaṃpamāṇo, parīsahe āyagutte sahejjā || 21.19 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Always abandoning attachment, aversion, and delusion, the wise bhikshu endures hardships self-protected and unshaken, like Mount Meru unaffected by wind.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **pahāya** — having abandoned/continuously abandoning
- **rāgaṃ** — attachment (rāga)
- **dosaṃ** — aversion (dveṣa)
- **mohaṃ** — delusion (moha)
- **sayayaṃ** — always, continuously
- **viyakkhaṇo** — the wise one, the discerning one
- **meruvva** — like Mount Meru
- **vāeṇa** — by wind
- **akaṃpamāṇo** — unshaken, unmoved
- **āyagutte** — self-protected (ātmagupti)
- **sahejjā parīsahe** — endures hardships

### 5. COMMENTARY

The three roots of karma-production are rāga (attraction), dveṣa (repulsion), and moha (delusion). All karma arises from these three inner movements. Viyakkhaṇo — the discerning one — is one who sees clearly enough to abandon them continuously, not just in formal meditation but in the living texture of every encounter. The Meru simile is among the most powerful in Jain literature: Mount Meru, the cosmic axis, the immovable centre of the universe, is not moved by wind. The monk whose inner axis is established in self-knowledge is similarly immovable — not rigid, but rooted. Āyagutte — self-protected: not protected by fortress or weapon, but by the soul's own clarity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Always releasing attachment, aversion, and delusion, the wise monk endures every hardship unmoved and self-protected — like Mount Meru standing firm against the wind.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the "wind" that most easily moves you — what kind of situation shakes your inner steadiness most readily? What would it mean to cultivate a deeper axis?

## SUTRA 21.20

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणुण्णए णावणए महेसी, ण याविं पूयं गरहं च संजए ।
स उज्जुभावं पडिवज्ज संजए, णिव्वाणमग्गं विरए उवेइ ॥२१.२०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aṇuṇṇae ṇāvaṇae mahesī, ṇa yāviṃ pūyaṃ garahaṃ ca saṃjae |
Sa ujjubhāvaṃ paḍivajja saṃjae, ṇivvāṇamaggaṃ virae uvei || 21.20 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The great sage is neither elated nor depressed; the saṃyami is indifferent to both praise and blame. Accepting straightforwardness, the saṃyami — detached from sensual pleasures — attains the path of nirvāṇa.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇuṇṇae** — not elated/uplifted (by favourable things)
- **ṇāvaṇae** — not depressed/downcast (by unfavourable things)
- **mahesī** — the great sage
- **pūyaṃ garahaṃ** — praise and blame
- **ṇa yāviṃ saṃjae** — the saṃyami does not (react to) even these
- **ujjubhāvaṃ** — straightforwardness, simplicity, directness
- **paḍivajja** — accepting, adopting
- **virae** — detached (from sensual pleasures)
- **ṇivvāṇamaggaṃ uvei** — attains the path of nirvāṇa

### 5. COMMENTARY

Ujjubhāva — straightforwardness — is the quality of one whose inner life and outer expression are the same: no performance, no concealment, no curvature for social advantage. The saṃyami who has mastered indifference to both praise and blame, who is neither elevated by fortune nor depressed by misfortune, who accepts simplicity as his mode of being — this person is not seeking nirvāṇa as a future goal but is already walking on its path. Virae — detached: this is not the detachment of apathy or coldness but the freedom of one who has seen through the mechanism of craving and aversion and is no longer driven by them. Such a person naturally moves toward liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The great sage is unmoved by praise or blame, unshaken by good or bad fortune. Accepting simplicity and releasing desire, the monk walks naturally on the path to nirvāṇa.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to live "straightforwardly" — without curvature, without performance? Where in your life are you most likely to bend yourself for social approval?

## SUTRA 21.21

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अरइरइसहे पहीणसंथवे, विरए आयहिए पहाणवं ।
परमट्टपएिहं चिट्ठु, छिण्णसोए अममे अकिंचणे ॥२१.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Araiṛaisahe pahīṇasaṃthave, virae āyahie pahāṇavaṃ |
Paramaṭṭhapaeihi ciṭṭhu, chiṇṇasoe amame akiṃcaṇe || 21.21 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Bearing both arati and rati equally, having abandoned worldly associations, detached, contemplating the self — he stands established in the supreme goal, with grief severed, free of possessiveness and all attachment.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **arai** — arati: discomfort/restlessness in renunciation
- **rai** — rati: pleasure/attraction in worldly life
- **sahe** — bearing/enduring both equally
- **pahīṇasaṃthave** — having abandoned worldly associations (saṃstava)
- **virae** — detached
- **āyahie** — contemplating the self, engaged with the self
- **pahāṇavaṃ** — the primary/chief (i.e., saṃyama/restraint)
- **paramaṭṭhapaeihi ciṭṭhu** — standing established in the supreme goal (mokṣa)
- **chiṇṇasoe** — with grief/sorrow severed
- **amame** — free of "mine-ness," no sense of possession
- **akiṃcaṇe** — without attachment to any thing

### 5. COMMENTARY

Arati and rati are the two poles of inner life: arati is the restlessness, discomfort, or boredom that can arise in the renunciant life (wishing for worldly comfort); rati is the residual pull of worldly pleasures. The muni who can bear both — who neither collapses into arati nor returns toward rati — has achieved a genuine inner equilibrium that cannot be disrupted from either direction. Pahīṇasaṃthava — having abandoned worldly associations — refers to two types of saṃstava (familiar intimacy): past acquaintances remembered, and new ones formed; both are released. Chiṇṇasoe (grief-severed), amame (no mine-ness), akiṃcaṇe (nothing as mine) — three aspects of freedom that together constitute the life of the truly homeless monk.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk bears equally the discomfort of renunciation and the pull of pleasure, has released all worldly ties, and stands established in liberation — with no grief, no possessiveness, no attachment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever felt the "arati" of your own spiritual path — restlessness, doubt, the wish to return to familiar comfort? How did you meet it?

## SUTRA 21.22

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विवित्त लयणाइं भएज्ज ताई, णिरुवलेवाइं असंथडाइं ।
इसिहिं चिण्णाइं महायसेहिं, काएण फासेज्ज परीसहाइं ॥२१.२२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Vivitta layaṇāiṃ bhaejja tāī, ṇiruvalevāiṃ asaṃthaḍāiṃ |
Isihiṃ ciṇṇāiṃ mahāyasehiṃ, kāeṇa phāsejja parīsahāiṃ || 21.22 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should resort to secluded dwellings — free from attachment, without seeds or household companions — as practiced by the great-famed sages, and with his body endure the hardships that come.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vivitta layaṇāiṃ** — secluded dwellings/resting places
- **bhaejja** — should resort to/use
- **ṇiruvalevāiṃ** — without attachment (upalep), not clung to
- **asaṃthaḍāiṃ** — without seeds (bīja) and without household companions (jana-kulata-rahita)
- **isihiṃ... mahāyasehiṃ** — by the great-famed sages/rishis
- **ciṇṇāiṃ** — practiced, trodden
- **kāeṇa** — with the body (through bodily endurance)
- **phāsejja parīsahāiṃ** — endure the hardships that arise

### 5. COMMENTARY

Vivikta-śayana (secluded dwelling) is one of the bhāvanās (sub-vows) of brahmacharya — the monk sleeps alone, in a place free of women, eunuchs, and animals, away from seeds (which represent fertility and biological life), and without the companionship of householders. The three conditions are: seclusion from people, freedom from biological fertility associations, and freedom from the social warmth of household intimacy. These conditions are not punitive — they serve the specific purpose of sustaining the monk's inner orientation. Isihiṃ ciṇṇāiṃ — practiced by the sages — grounds this discipline in the lineage of the liberated. And still the hardships come: parīsahāiṃ — they are met with the body itself, not avoided.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk should seek secluded, simple resting places — as the great sages have always done — and use his body to endure whatever hardships arise there.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What kind of environment supports your own inner clarity — and are you willing to seek it out, even when it is simpler or more solitary than what is comfortable?

- **cariuṃ** — practicing, following
- **dhammasaṃcayaṃ** — the collection/accumulation of virtues/dharmas
- **aṇuttare ṇāṇadhare** — the bearer of unsurpassed knowledge
- **jasaṃsī** — glorious, of great fame
- **obhāsai** — shines, illuminates
- **sūrie iva** — like the sun
- **vaṃtalikkhe** — in the expanse of sky

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The great sage attains the highest knowledge, practices the supreme virtues, and shines in the assembly of liberated ones like the sun blazing in the open sky.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The 13-verse teaching section (sutras 11–23) culminates here with the image of the fully formed monk: equipped with all five knowledges (śruta-jñāna as the base, ascending through avadhi, manaḥparyāya, and kevalajñāna in the highest cases), practicing the supreme dharma-collection (the full range of virtues from ahiṃsā to equanimity), and radiating that inner reality outward into the saṃgha like the sun radiating light in open sky. The sun metaphor is precise: the sun does not illuminate selectively, does not withhold light, does not tire. The liberated sage's presence has this quality: it illuminates by its nature, not by effort.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What kind of inner work, if practiced consistently, would allow your presence itself to become a form of illumination for those around you?

## SUTRA 21.24

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दुविहं खवेऊण य पुण्णपावं, णिरंगणे सव्वओ विप्पमुक्के ।
तरित्ता समुद्दं च महाभवोघं, समुदपाले अपुणागमगं गए ॥ ति बेमि ॥२१.२४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Duvihaṃ khaveūṇa ya puṇṇapāvaṃ, ṇiraṃgaṇe savvao vippamukke |
Tarittā samudḍaṃ ca mahābhavoghāṃ, Samudapāle apuṇāgamagaṃ gae || iti bemi || 21.24 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having destroyed both types of karmas — merit and sin — completely free from all karmas, Samudrapāla crossed the great ocean of saṃsāra and attained the non-returning destination: liberation. Thus I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **duvihaṃ** — both types (of karma)
- **khaveūṇa** — having destroyed, eliminated
- **puṇṇapāvaṃ** — merit (puṇya) and sin (pāpa) — i.e., both ghāti and aghāti karmas
- **ṇiraṃgaṇe** — free from karmas (nirañjana — unstained)
- **savvao vippamukke** — completely liberated from all
- **tarittā** — having crossed
- **samudḍaṃ mahābhavoghāṃ** — the great ocean of saṃsāra (mahābhava-ogha — great flood of existence)
- **Samudapāle** — Samudrapāla
- **apuṇāgamagaṃ gae** — attained the non-returning destination (mokṣa)
- **iti bemi** — thus I say (the standard closing formula)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The final verse completes the story with full doctrinal precision. Duvihaṃ karma — both types — refers in Jain karma theory to the complete range: ghāti-karmas (knowledge-obscuring, vision-obscuring, feeling-producing, and power-obstructing) and aghāti-karmas (life-determining, body-forming, status-determining, and conduct-deluding). All must be destroyed for liberation; partial destruction is not liberation. Ṇiraṃgaṇe — unstained — the soul in its pure state, freed from the clinging of karma-particles. Mahābhava-ogha — the great flood of existence: the accumulated momentum of countless lives of birth, death, and rebirth — Samudrapāla crossed it. His name comes full circle: born crossing the sea, he ends by crossing the ultimate sea. Iti bemi — thus I say — is the authenticating signature of Sudharmasvāmi transmitting what he received from Mahāvīra through Jambūsvāmi.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Samudrapāla destroyed all karmas — merit and sin alike — and crossed the great sea of existence. He attained liberation, the non-returning state. Thus it is said.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The name Samudrapāla — born of the sea, liberated by crossing the sea. What is the great ocean in your own life that you sense you are here to cross?
