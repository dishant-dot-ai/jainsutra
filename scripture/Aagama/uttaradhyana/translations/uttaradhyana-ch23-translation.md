# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 23
## Keśī-Gautamīya (The Dialogue of Keśī and Gautama)
### Trevisaṃ Adhyayana — Twenty-Third Chapter

**Total Sutras:** 89
**Theme:** A historic dialogue between Keśī Kumāra Śramaṇa (chief disciple of Pārśvanātha) and Gautama Svāmī (chief disciple of Mahāvīra), resolving doctrinal differences between the Cāturyāma (4 vows) and Pañca-mahāvrata (5 vows) traditions through twelve cycles of question and answer. The dialogue culminates in Keśī accepting the five great vows and joining Mahāvīra's order.

---

## SUTRA 23.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
जिणे पासे ति णामेणं, अरहा लोगपूइओ ।
संबुद्धप्पा य सव्वण्णू, धम्म तित्थयरे जिणे ॥२३.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
jiṇe pāse ti ṇāmeṇaṃ, arahā logapūio |
saṃbuddhappā ya savvaṇṇū, dhamma titthayare jiṇe || 23.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The Jina named Pārśva, the worthy one honored by the world, was self-enlightened, omniscient, and the ford-maker of the Dharma.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
jiṇe = Jina (conqueror); pāse = Pārśva (name); arahā = Arhat, worthy one; logapūio = honored by the world; saṃbuddhappā = self-enlightened; savvaṇṇū = omniscient; dhamma titthayare = ford-maker/establisher of Dharma; jiṇe = Jina.

### 5. COMMENTARY
The chapter opens by establishing the lineage of Keśī through his master Pārśvanātha, the twenty-third Tīrthaṅkara. Pārśvanātha is described with four epithets: Jina (conqueror of passions), Arhant (worthy of veneration), Saṃbuddha (self-awakened), and Sarvajña (omniscient). The word "Tīrthaṅkara" means one who creates the ford — a crossing point over the ocean of saṃsāra — making the path of liberation accessible to all souls.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Simply put: This verse introduces Lord Pārśva, a great spiritual teacher who had conquered all inner enemies and could see all of reality, and who showed others the path to freedom.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Do I seek teachers and traditions because of their titles and lineage, or because their teachings actually help me cross the ocean of suffering within?

## SUTRA 23.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
तस्स लोगपईवस्स, आसी सीसे महायसे ।
केसीकुमार समणे, विज्जाचरण पारगे ॥२३.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
tassa logapaīvassa, āsī sīse mahāyase |
kesīkumāra samaṇe, vijjācaraṇa pārage || 23.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
That world-illuminating lord had a great and renowned disciple — Keśī Kumāra the ascetic, who had reached the far shore of knowledge and conduct.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
tassa = his; logapaīvassa = world-illuminating lord; sīse = disciple; mahāyase = greatly renowned; kesīkumāra samaṇe = Keśī Kumāra the ascetic; vijjācaraṇa = knowledge and conduct; pārage = one who has reached the far shore (master).

### 5. COMMENTARY
Keśī Kumāra is introduced as the foremost disciple of Pārśvanātha. The compound vijjācaraṇa-pārage means he had mastered both theoretical knowledge (vijjā) and practical conduct (caraṇa) — he was not merely learned but fully lived what he knew. This dual mastery is the hallmark of true spiritual attainment in Jain tradition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Simply put: Lord Pārśva's greatest student was Keśī, a monk who had completely mastered both spiritual knowledge and the disciplined life that goes with it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Is my spiritual learning matched by my actual way of living, or is there a gap between what I know and how I act?

## SUTRA 23.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
ओहिणाणसुए बुद्धे, सीससंघसमाउले ।
गामाणुगाम रीयते, सावत्थिं पुरिमागए ॥२३.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
ohiṇāṇasue buddhe, sīsasaṃghasamāule |
gāmāṇugāma rīyate, sāvatthiṃ purimāgae || 23.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
Possessed of clairvoyant knowledge and scriptural learning, wise, surrounded by his assembly of disciples, wandering village to village, he arrived at the city of Śrāvastī.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
ohiṇāṇa = clairvoyant knowledge (avadhi-jñāna); sue = scriptural knowledge; buddhe = wise, awakened; sīsasaṃgha = assembly of disciples; samāule = surrounded by; gāmāṇugāma = village to village; rīyate = wandering; sāvatthiṃ = Śrāvastī; purimāgae = arrived.

### 5. COMMENTARY
Keśī is described as possessing avadhi-jñāna (clairvoyant perception) in addition to scriptural learning — a supernatural faculty that allowed him to perceive beyond ordinary sensory range. The wandering ascetic life (caryā) from village to village was the prescribed mode of existence for Jain monks, preventing attachment to any fixed place. Śrāvastī was one of the great cities of ancient India, a major center of religious life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT
Simply put: Keśī, gifted with special inner sight and surrounded by his monk-students, traveled from town to town and eventually arrived at a great city called Śrāvastī.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
What would it mean for me to truly "wander" — to hold nothing and no place as mine — while remaining fully engaged with the world?

## SUTRA 23.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
तिंदुयं णाम उज्जाणं, तम्मि णगरमंडले ।
फासुए सिज्जसंथारे, तत्थ वासमुवागए ॥२३.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
tiṃduyaṃ ṇāma ujjāṇaṃ, tammi ṇagaramaṃḍale |
phāsue sijjasaṃthāre, tattha vāsamuvāgae || 23.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
In the district of that city there was a garden called Tinduka, and there, on a pure and permissible bed and mat, he took up residence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
tiṃduyaṃ = Tinduka (name); ujjāṇaṃ = garden; ṇagaramaṃḍale = in the city's district; phāsue = permissible, faultless (not harming living beings); sijjasaṃthāre = bed and mat; tattha = there; vāsam = residence; uvāgae = took up.

### 5. COMMENTARY
The Tinduka garden (named after the tinduka/tendu tree) was in the outskirts of Śrāvastī. The term phāsua (prāsuka) indicates that the resting place was checked to ensure no living beings were harmed — a fundamental Jain principle of ahiṃsā even in the most mundane acts. Jain monks cannot stay in any place without first ensuring it is free of harm to living beings.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Keśī settled in a garden outside the city, making sure his resting place didn't cause harm to any living creature — as Jain monks always do.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
How careful am I about the unseen harm my daily choices — where I rest, eat, walk — might cause to living beings?

## SUTRA 23.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
अह तेणेव कालेणं, धम्मतित्थयरे जिणे ।
भगवं वद्धमाणित्ति, सव्वलोगम्मि विस्सुए ॥२३.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
aha teṇeva kāleṇaṃ, dhammattithayare jiṇe |
bhagavaṃ vaddhamāṇitti, savvaloggammi vissue || 23.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
And at that very same time, the Jina who was the ford-maker of the Dharma — the Blessed One named Vardhamāna — was renowned throughout the entire world.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
aha = and then; teṇeva kāleṇaṃ = at that very time; dhammattithayare = ford-maker of Dharma; jiṇe = Jina; bhagavaṃ = Blessed One; vaddhamāṇi = Vardhamāna (Mahāvīra); savvaloggammi = throughout all the world; vissue = renowned.

### 5. COMMENTARY
The sutra introduces Mahāvīra (Vardhamāna) as a parallel figure at the very same moment in history — a masterly narrative technique establishing the two traditions as contemporaneous. Mahāvīra is called the twenty-fourth Tīrthaṅkara, the ford-maker of Dharma, paralleling Pārśvanātha in sutra 1. This symmetry prepares for the profound dialogue to follow.

### 6. SIMPLY Put
Simply put: At the same time Keśī arrived in Śrāvastī, the great teacher Mahāvīra — also known as Vardhamāna — was famous throughout the world.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When two great teachers emerge at the same time, what does that tell us about the nature of truth — is it owned by one tradition or accessible through many?

## SUTRA 23.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
तस्स लोगपईवस्स, आसी सीसे महायसे ।
भगवं गोयमे णामं, विज्जाचरणपारगे ॥२३.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
tassa logapaīvassa, āsī sīse mahāyase |
bhagavaṃ goyame ṇāmaṃ, vijjācaraṇapārage || 23.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
That world-illuminating lord had a great and renowned disciple — the Blessed Gautama by name — who had reached the far shore of knowledge and conduct.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
tassa = his; logapaīvassa = world-illuminating lord; sīse = disciple; mahāyase = greatly renowned; bhagavaṃ = Blessed One; goyame = Gautama; vijjācaraṇapārage = master of knowledge and conduct.

### 5. COMMENTARY
This sutra is deliberately constructed as a mirror of sutra 2, using identical language to describe Gautama that was used for Keśī. This parallel structure is a literary device showing that both disciples are equals in attainment — only the masters differ. Gautama (Indrabhūti Gautama) was Mahāvīra's foremost disciple and chief questioner in many dialogues of the Āgamas.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Just as Keśī was Pārśva's greatest student, Gautama was Mahāvīra's greatest student — equally accomplished in both learning and practice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Do I measure people by whose student they are, or by the depth of what they have realized within themselves?

## SUTRA 23.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
बारसंगविऊ बुद्धे, सीससंघसमाउले ।
गामाणुगाम रीयते, से वि सावत्थिमागए ॥२३.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
bārasaṃgaviū buddhe, sīsasaṃghasamāule |
gāmāṇugāma rīyate, se vi sāvatthimāgae || 23.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
A knower of the twelve Aṅgas, wise, surrounded by his assembly of disciples, wandering village to village, he too arrived at Śrāvastī.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
bārasaṃgaviū = knower of the twelve Aṅgas (canonical scriptures); buddhe = wise; sīsasaṃgha = assembly of disciples; gāmāṇugāma = village to village; rīyate = wandering; se vi = he also; sāvatthim = Śrāvastī; āgae = arrived.

### 5. COMMENTARY
Gautama is described as bārasa-aṅga-viu — master of all twelve Aṅgas of the Jain canon. This mirrors Keśī's description as possessing avadhi-jñāna in sutra 3, but with a different emphasis: Keśī has supernatural perception while Gautama has comprehensive scriptural mastery. Together, the two disciples represent the fullness of spiritual achievement in their respective traditions.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Gautama, who knew all twelve sacred Jain scriptures by heart, was also wandering with his students and arrived at the same city of Śrāvastī.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
What is the difference between knowing sacred texts perfectly and knowing reality directly — and which matters more for liberation?

## SUTRA 23.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
कोट्टगं णाम उज्जाणं, तम्मि णगरमंडले ।
फासुए सिज्जसंथारे, तत्थ वासमुवागए ॥२३.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
koṭṭagaṃ ṇāma ujjāṇaṃ, tammi ṇagaramaṃḍale |
phāsue sijjasaṃthāre, tattha vāsamuvāgae || 23.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
In the district of that city there was a garden called Koṭṭaka, and there, on a pure and permissible bed and mat, he took up residence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
koṭṭagaṃ = Koṭṭaka (name); ujjāṇaṃ = garden; ṇagaramaṃḍale = city district; phāsue = permissible; sijjasaṃthāre = bed and mat; tattha = there; vāsam = residence; uvāgae = took up.

### 5. COMMENTARY
Just as Keśī stayed in the Tinduka garden (sutra 4), Gautama stayed in the nearby Koṭṭaka garden. The two great teachers were now residing in the same city but in different gardens — a divinely arranged proximity that would enable the historic dialogue. The identical phrasing once again underscores the structural parallelism of the two traditions.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Gautama settled in a different garden in the same city as Keśī — the two greatest disciples of two great teachers were now living very close to each other.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When great souls are brought near each other by circumstance, do I recognize the sacred opportunity, or does the difference in their traditions blind me to it?

## SUTRA 23.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
केसीकुमार समणे, गोयमे य महायसे ।
उभओ वि तत्थ विहरिंसु, अल्लीणा सुसमाहिया ॥२३.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
kesīkumāra samaṇe, goyame ya mahāyase |
ubhao vi tattha vihariṃsu, allīṇā susamāhiyā || 23.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
Both Keśī Kumāra the ascetic and the great-souled Gautama dwelled there, absorbed and well-composed in deep equanimity.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
kesīkumāra samaṇe = Keśī Kumāra the ascetic; goyame = Gautama; mahāyase = great-souled; ubhao vi = both; tattha = there; vihariṃsu = dwelled; allīṇā = absorbed; susamāhiyā = well-composed, in deep equanimity.

### 5. COMMENTARY
The word allīṇā (absorbed, inward) and susamāhiyā (well-established in samādhi) describe both monks as deeply settled in their practice — not restless or competitive, but inwardly peaceful. Great souls, even when representing different traditions, share this quality of inner stability. The stage is set: two masters of equanimity are about to meet.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Both Keśī and Gautama were living near each other in a state of deep inner peace and spiritual focus — two great meditating monks in the same city.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Can I remain inwardly composed and equanimous when I encounter someone whose beliefs differ significantly from my own?

## SUTRA 23.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
उभओ सीससंचाणं, संजयाणं तवस्सिणं ।
तत्थ चिंता समुप्पण्णा, गुणवंताण ताइणं ॥२३.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
ubhao sīsasaṃcāṇaṃ, saṃjayāṇaṃ tavassīṇaṃ |
tattha ciṃtā samuppaṇṇā, guṇavaṃtāṇa tāiṇaṃ || 23.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
Among the disciples of both — the self-restrained and ascetic ones, virtuous and protecting — a concern arose.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
ubhao = both (sides); sīsasaṃcāṇaṃ = among the disciples; saṃjayāṇaṃ = self-restrained; tavassīṇaṃ = ascetics; ciṃtā = concern, inquiry; samuppaṇṇā = arose; guṇavaṃtāṇa = virtuous ones; tāiṇaṃ = protectors (of living beings).

### 5. COMMENTARY
The impetus for the great dialogue did not come from the masters themselves, but from their disciples — a detail of great narrative significance. The disciples' sincere concern about doctrinal differences reflects genuine spiritual inquiry, not rivalry or hostility. The text carefully describes these disciples as self-restrained (saṃjaya), ascetic (tapassī), virtuous (guṇavanta), and compassionate protectors of life (tāin) — establishing their motivation as pure.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: The students of both Keśī and Gautama began to wonder and worry about the differences between their teachers' traditions — not out of rivalry, but out of genuine curiosity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When I encounter someone from a different religious tradition, do I approach the differences with genuine curiosity, or with defensiveness?

## SUTRA 23.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
केरिसो वा इमो धम्मो, इमो धम्मो वा केरिसो ।
आयार-धम्मपणिही, इमा वा सा व केरिसी ॥२३.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
keriso vā imo dhammo, imo dhammo vā keriso |
āyāra-dhammapaṇihī, imā vā sā va keriso || 23.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
What is the nature of this Dharma? And what is the nature of that Dharma? What is the conduct-discipline of this one, and what is the conduct-discipline of that one?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
keriso = of what kind; imo dhammo = this Dharma (Mahāvīra's); imā dhamma = that Dharma (Pārśva's); āyāra = conduct; dhammapaṇihī = religious discipline/disposition; keriso = what kind.

### 5. COMMENTARY
The disciples' inquiry is expressed through this beautifully balanced verse — alternating "this" and "that" without declaring either superior. Their question is not polemical but philosophical: what are the essential natures of these two paths? The word āyāra-dhammapaṇihī is significant — it asks not just about doctrine but about the actual lived conduct-discipline (āyāra) that each tradition prescribes.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: The students were asking: "What exactly is our teacher's path and what is the other teacher's path? What rules of conduct do they each follow, and why are they different?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When I look at a different spiritual path, am I asking "what is the nature of this?" with openness — or already assuming I know the answer?

## SUTRA 23.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
चाउज्जामो य जो धम्मो, जो इमो पंचसिक्खओ ।
देसिओ वद्धमाणेण, पासेण य महामुणी ॥२३.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
cāujjāmo ya jo dhammo, jo imo paṃcasikkhao |
desio vaddhamāṇeṇa, pāseṇa ya mahāmuṇī || 23.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The four-fold restraint that is one Dharma, and the five-fold discipline that is this Dharma — both were taught by great sages: Vardhamāna and Pārśva.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
cāujjāmo = four-fold restraint (cāturyāma); jo dhammo = that Dharma; paṃcasikkhao = five-fold discipline (pañca-mahāvrata); desio = taught; vaddhamāṇeṇa = by Vardhamāna (Mahāvīra); pāseṇa = by Pārśva; mahāmuṇī = great sages.

### 5. COMMENTARY
This sutra names the core doctrinal difference precisely. Pārśvanātha taught cāturyāma — four great restraints (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, non-possession). Mahāvīra taught pañca-mahāvrata — five great vows, adding brahmacarya (celibacy) as the fifth. Both are described equally as "taught by great sages," neither being dismissed. The disciples' concern was genuine: if both teachers are great sages, why do the vow-counts differ?

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: One teacher (Pārśva) taught four main vows for monks, and the other teacher (Mahāvīra) taught five — and the students wanted to understand why two great teachers gave different rules.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
When two teachers I respect seem to teach different things, do I choose one and dismiss the other, or do I go deeper to understand why?

## SUTRA 23.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
अचेलगो य जो धम्मो, जो इमो संतरुत्तरो ।
एगकज्ज-पवण्णाणं, विसेसे किण्णु कारणं ॥२३.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
acelagao ya jo dhammo, jo imo saṃtaruttaro |
egakajja-pavaṇṇāṇaṃ, visese kiṇṇu kāraṇaṃ || 23.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
The Dharma of nakedness and the Dharma with upper and lower garments — for those who have entered the same goal, what is the reason for this difference?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
acelagao = naked (without clothes); jo dhammo = that Dharma; saṃtaruttaro = with inner and outer garment (clothed); egakajja = same goal; pavaṇṇāṇaṃ = those who have entered; visese = in difference; kiṇṇu kāraṇaṃ = what is the reason?

### 5. COMMENTARY
A second difference is identified: Mahāvīra's monks went completely naked (digambara practice in its root form), while Pārśvanātha's monks wore two pieces of cloth. The disciples' question is incisive — if both paths lead to the same liberation, why the difference in dress? This question cuts to the heart of the relationship between external form and inner realization.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: One tradition's monks wore no clothes at all, and the other wore simple robes — students were asking why monks going for the same goal should look different.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
How much weight do I give to external religious forms — dress, ritual, symbol — versus the inner transformation they are meant to support?

## SUTRA 23.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
अह ते तत्थ सीसाणं, विण्णाय पवित्तिकयं ।
समागमे कयमई, उभओ केसि-गोयमा ॥२३.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
aha te tattha sīsāṇaṃ, viṇṇāya pavittikayaṃ |
samāgame kayamaī, ubhao kesi-goyamā || 23.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
Then, having perceived the conduct and activity of their disciples there, both Keśī and Gautama resolved to meet.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
aha = then; sīsāṇaṃ = of the disciples; viṇṇāya = having perceived; pavittikayaṃ = conduct, stirring; samāgame = to meet; kayamaī = resolved, made up mind; ubhao = both; kesī-goyamā = Keśī and Gautama.

### 5. COMMENTARY
This is a pivotal moment: both masters, being clairvoyant and deeply perceptive, independently became aware of their disciples' concerns without being told directly. Rather than ignoring the situation, both resolved to address it through dialogue. This demonstrates the ideal of a true teacher — responsive to the needs of students even before being asked. Both masters initiated the meeting, showing no hierarchy between them.

### 6. SIMPLY put
Simply put: Both Keśī and Gautama somehow sensed what their students were wondering about, and both decided on their own to meet each other and talk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE
Am I the kind of person who notices the unspoken questions of others and takes initiative to address them — or do I wait to be asked?

## SUTRA 23.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT
गोयमो पडिरूवण्णू, सीससंघसमाउले ।
जेट्ठुं कुलमवेक्खंतो, तिंदुयं वणमागओ ॥२३.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION
goyamo paḍirūvaṇṇū, sīsasaṃghasamāule |
jeṭṭhuṃ kulamavekhaṃto, tiṃduyaṃ vaṇamāgao || 23.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION
Gautama, knowledgeable about proper conduct, surrounded by his community of disciples, honoring the elder lineage, came to the Tinduka garden.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA
goyamo = Gautama; paḍirūvaṇṇū = knowledgeable of proper propriety; sīsasaṃgha = community of disciples; jeṭṭhuṃ kulam = elder lineage; avekhaṃto = honoring/respecting; tiṃduyaṃ vaṇam = Tinduka garden; āgao = came.

### 5. COMMENTARY
Gautama came to Keśī's garden — not Keśī to Gautama's. This is a significant detail: Gautama, despite being the disciple of the later and current Tīrthaṅkara Mahāvīra, showed deference to Keśī's seniority as a disciple of the earlier Tīrthaṅkara Pārśvanātha.
