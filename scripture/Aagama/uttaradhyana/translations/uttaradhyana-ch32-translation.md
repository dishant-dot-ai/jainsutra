# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 32
## Pramādasthāna — The Abodes of Negligence

**Total Sutras:** 111
**Source:** Gujarati commentary edition, Volume 2, internal pages 275–311

---

## Chapter Overview

Chapter 32 is one of the most architecturally structured chapters in the entire Uttaradhyayana. Its Prakrit title, *Pramādasthāna*, means "the stations (or abodes) of negligence." The chapter opens with a solemn declaration of purpose — to show the path out of all suffering — and then systematically traces suffering to its root: attachment through the six sense channels (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind). For each sense, the same 13-verse sequence is used, varying only the sense-object word. This repetition is deliberate — it is a meditative drumbeat intended to drive the teaching into consciousness. The chapter closes with 12 concluding sutras describing liberation from desire and the final attainment of eternal peace.

## SECTION I: INTRODUCTION (Sutras 1–5)

---

### SUTRA 32.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अच्छंतकालस्स समूलगस्स, सव्वस्स दुक्खस्स उ जो पमोक्खो ।
तं भासओ मे पडिपुण्णचित्ता, सुणेह एगंतहियं हियत्थं ॥ ३२.१ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

acchantakālassa samūlagassa, savvassa dukkhassa u jo pamokko |
taṃ bhāsao me paḍipuṇṇacittā, suṇeha egaṃtahiyaṃ hiyatthaṃ || 32.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Listen with focused minds to what I declare — the uniquely beneficial, welfare-bringing means for liberation from all suffering, including its very roots, which has persisted since beginningless time.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*acchantakālassa* = from beginningless time; *samūlagassa* = together with its roots, including the root causes (false belief, etc.); *savvassa dukkhassa* = from all suffering; *pamokko* = liberation, the means of liberation; *taṃ* = that; *me* = by me; *bhāsao* = being declared; *paḍipuṇṇacittā* = with fully concentrated mind, one-pointedly; *suṇeha* = listen; *egaṃtahiyaṃ* = exclusively beneficial, singularly welfare-giving; *hiyatthaṃ* = that which is for your welfare.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening verse is the *upthānikā* — the invocation of the entire chapter. The speaker announces that what follows is not mere philosophical speculation but a practical path that cuts through suffering at its very root. The phrase *acchantakāla* — beginningless time — is significant. Jain philosophy holds that the soul's entanglement in karma and suffering has no traceable beginning; it is a condition so ancient that no first cause can be identified. Yet it has an *end*, and this chapter will describe that end and how to reach it.

The phrase *samūlagassa* — "together with its roots" — is equally important. Merely suppressing suffering is insufficient. The great teacher is promising to address not just the symptoms but the deep causal roots of all pain: false perception, attachment, aversion, and karma. This is a complete cure, not a temporary relief.

The call to listen *paḍipuṇṇacittā* — with fully gathered, one-pointed mind — reflects the Jain understanding that spiritual teaching can only be absorbed when the mind is still and undistracted. Spiritual knowledge does not penetrate a scattered mind any more than water fills a cracked pot. The teacher's love for the listener is expressed in *egaṃtahiyaṃ* — "exclusively for your welfare" — signaling that these teachings are pure gift, offered with no motive but the liberation of the listener.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Listen carefully — I am about to share the one teaching that can free you from every pain you have ever felt, down to its deepest root.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I truly ready to listen — with my full heart and an unscattered mind — or do I only half-receive the teachings that could free me?

### SUTRA 32.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणस्स सव्वस्स पगासणाए, अण्णाण मोहस्स विवज्जणाए ।
रागस्स दोसस्स य संखएणं, एगंत सोक्खं समुवेइ मोक्खं ॥ ३२.२ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇassa savvassa pagāsaṇāe, aṇṇāṇa mohassa vivajjaṇāe |
rāgassa dosassa ya saṃkhaēṇaṃ, egaṃta sokkhaṃ samuvei mokkhaṃ || 32.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Through the illumination of complete knowledge, through the abandonment of ignorance and delusion, and through the complete destruction of attachment and aversion — one attains liberation, which is singular, unalloyed bliss.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ṇāṇassa savvassa* = of all knowledge; *pagāsaṇāe* = through illumination, through full manifestation; *aṇṇāṇa* = ignorance; *mohassa* = and delusion; *vivajjaṇāe* = through abandonment, removal; *rāgassa* = of attachment; *dosassa* = of aversion; *saṃkhaēṇaṃ* = through complete destruction, full extirpation; *egaṃta sokkhaṃ* = singular, absolute happiness; *mokkhaṃ* = liberation; *samuvei* = one attains.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse defines liberation (*moksha*) with precision. Three conditions are enumerated: (1) the full illumination of all knowledge, (2) the complete abandonment of ignorance and delusion, and (3) the total destruction of attachment and aversion. These are not three separate paths but three aspects of one integrated transformation.

In Jain philosophy, the soul's nature is pure, omniscient consciousness. Suffering arises because this natural light is obscured by karma — specifically by knowledge-obscuring karma and conduct-deluding karma. When ignorance (*aṇṇāṇa*) and delusion (*moha*) are removed, knowledge blazes forth in its natural fullness. When attachment and aversion cease, no new karma binds the soul. This is the double movement of liberation: the removal of obscurations and the cessation of new binding.

The description of liberation as *egaṃta sokkhaṃ* — "singularly blissful" — is a philosophical claim. The happiness of liberation is not merely greater than worldly happiness in degree; it is different in kind. It is not the pleasure of sense-gratification (which is always mixed with anxiety, satiation, and loss) but the uncaused, unobstructed bliss of a soul at rest in its own nature. There is nothing to lose, nothing to fear, nothing to want. This is the destination toward which the chapter's teachings point.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When you truly know everything, drop all delusion, and let go of all likes and dislikes completely — that is liberation, and it is pure, endless joy.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What small piece of ignorance or clinging to "likes" might I be protecting right now, not yet willing to release?

### SUTRA 32.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तस्सेसमग्गो गुरुविद्ध-सेवा, विवज्जणा बालजणस्स दूरा ।
सज्झाय-एगंत-णिसेवणा य, सुत्तत्थ-संचिंतणया धिई य ॥ ३२.३ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tassesa-maggo guru-viddha-sevā, vivajjaṇā bālajañassa dūrā |
sajjhāya-egaṃta-ṇisevāṇā ya, suttatta-saṃciṃtaṇayā dhiī ya || 32.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The path to that liberation is: serving learned elders and teachers; keeping complete distance from the company of the ignorant; practicing solitary scriptural study; deep contemplation of the meaning of the texts; and maintaining resolute patience.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tassesa maggo* = the path to that (liberation); *guru-viddha-sevā* = serving teachers and elder monks; *bālajañassa* = of the ignorant; *vivajjaṇā dūrā* = keeping far away; *sajjhāya* = scriptural study; *egaṃta ṇisevāṇā* = solitary practice; *suttatta saṃciṃtaṇayā* = careful contemplation of the meaning of scripture; *dhiī* = patience, steadfastness; *maggo* = path.

**5. COMMENTARY**

Having stated the destination (Sutra 2), the teacher now outlines the practical path. Five elements are enumerated:

First, *guru-viddha-sevā* — serving learned teachers and elder monks. In Jain tradition, the word "guru" denotes the enlightened guide who has direct experience of the path, while "viddha" (aged) refers to those who have walked this path long and have wisdom born of years of practice. The disciple who serves such a teacher gains insight that cannot be obtained from books alone. Service here is not mere physical attendance but an orientation of the whole being toward learning.

Second, *vivajjaṇā bālajañassa dūrā* — keeping far away from the ignorant. "The ignorant" (*bāla*) here does not mean the uneducated; it means those who do not distinguish between what is to be accepted and what is to be abandoned, those who act contrary to the path of right conduct. The sage-seeker must guard his field of influence carefully. As the proverb goes, "By association one takes on the color" (*saṃg tevo raṃg*). Just as a white cloth takes on the color of whatever dye it touches, the practitioner's mind takes on the quality of those he keeps company with.

Third and fourth, solitary scriptural study (*sajjhāya-egaṃta-ṇisevāṇā*) and deep contemplation of meaning (*suttatta-saṃciṃtaṇayā*). Study without contemplation is mechanical repetition. Contemplation without study is imagination without anchor. Together they form a loop that deepens understanding. The practitioner reads carefully, then sits with the meaning until it becomes insight.

Fifth, *dhiī* — resolute patience and steadfastness. The path is long. Obstacles arise. Progress is not always visible. The practitioner who expects quick results will be disappointed and may abandon the practice. Patience is not passive waiting; it is the active maintenance of direction and commitment across all the storms that the path inevitably brings.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The road to freedom requires: a good teacher, avoiding bad company, quiet study, deep thinking, and patient perseverance.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which of these five elements is missing or weakest in my own practice right now?

### SUTRA 32.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आहारिमच्छे मियमेसणिज्जं, सहायिमच्छे णिठुणत्थबुद्धिं ।
णिकेयिमच्छेज्ज विवेगजोग्गं, समाहिकामे समणे तवस्सी ॥ ३२.४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āhārimicchaṃ miyameṇaṇijjaṃ, sahāyimicchaṃ ṇiṭhuṇatthabuddhiṃ |
ṇikeyimicchaṃ vivegajogaṃ, samāhikāme samaṇe tavassī || 32.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ascetic-practitioner who desires inner equilibrium should desire food that is measured and properly obtained; a companion who is skillful and of sharp, wise understanding; and a dwelling-place fit for solitary practice — free from women, animals, and hermaphrodites.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*samāhikāme* = one who desires inner equilibrium (the purpose); *samaṇe tavassī* = the ascetic practitioner; *āhāra imicchaṃ* = with respect to food, desires; *miya* = measured, limited; *meṇaṇijjaṃ* = properly sought, fault-free; *sahāya imicchaṃ* = with respect to companion, desires; *ṇiṭhuṇa* = skillful, sharp; *atthabuddhiṃ* = wise in meaning and purpose; *ṇikeyi imicchaṃ* = with respect to dwelling; *vivega jogaṃ* = fit for solitude and renunciation; *ṇikeyi* = dwelling free from women, animals, hermaphrodites.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse offers pragmatic guidance on three foundational conditions for the practitioner who seeks inner equilibrium (*samādhi*): food, companionship, and dwelling. These are not trivial logistics; they are structural pillars of the contemplative life.

Regarding food: excess in eating strengthens the body's sensory impulses and makes the mind dull and sluggish. The practitioner who overeats cannot maintain the kind of wakeful, alert awareness that deep contemplation requires. "Measured food" means not just quantity but quality — food obtained without causing harm, free from defects of time, preparation, and source.

Regarding the companion: in the early stages of practice, the right companion is invaluable. A companion who is sharp, wise, and genuinely committed to the path provides accountability, insight, and support. A companion who is undisciplined, self-willed, or attached to comfort breaks the practitioner's concentration at every turn. The text calls for a companion with *ṇiṭhuṇa-attha-buddhi* — piercing wisdom about what truly matters.

Regarding dwelling: the ideal practice-space is secluded, quiet, and free from the kinds of sensory disturbance that pull the mind outward. The specific exclusions mentioned here (women, animals, hermaphrodites) reflect the monastic context — a male monk's residence must be free from sources of potential distraction to celibacy and concentration.

Taken together, these three conditions create the outer architecture of an inner life. When the outer environment is aligned with the inner aspiration, the work of liberation becomes possible.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A monk who wants true inner peace should eat simply, choose wise companions, and live in a quiet, undisturbing place.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In my own life, what aspects of my environment, diet, or company are quietly working against my deeper intentions?

### SUTRA 32.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण वा लभेज्ज णिठुणं सहायं, गुणाहियं वा गुणओ समं वा ।
एक्को वि पावाइं विवज्जयंतो, विहरेज्ज कामेसु असज्जमाणो ॥ ३२.५ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa vā labheijja ṇiṭhuṇaṃ sahāyaṃ, guṇāhiyaṃ vā guṇao samaṃ vā |
ekko vi pāvāiṃ vivajjayaṃto, viharjja kāmesu asajjamāṇo || 32.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

If one cannot find a skillful companion — one superior in virtue or equal in virtue — then one should wander alone, abandoning sinful acts and remaining unattached to sense-pleasures.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*vā* = or; *guṇāhiyaṃ* = superior in virtue; *vā* = or; *guṇao samaṃ* = equal in virtue; *ṇiṭhuṇaṃ sahāyaṃ* = skillful companion; *ṇa labheijja* = if one cannot find; *ekko vi* = even alone; *pāvāiṃ* = sinful acts; *vivajjayaṃto* = abandoning; *kāmesu* = in sense-pleasures; *asajjamāṇo* = not becoming attached; *viharjja* = one should wander, practice.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse is the practical completion of Sutra 4. The ideal of a wise companion is acknowledged, but what if no such companion is available? The answer is unambiguous: one must walk alone rather than compromise. The wrong companion can undo years of practice in days. A self-willed, undisciplined associate creates disturbances at every moment of silence, pulls the practitioner back toward sense-attachments, and substitutes entertainment for contemplation.

The verse establishes a hierarchy: first seek a companion superior in virtue (from whom you will be lifted), then one equal in virtue (with whom you will be strengthened), and if neither is available, walk alone. The Jain tradition records several examples — including the great Gautama Swami's own periods of solitary practice — where solitude, when chosen rightly and with full self-reliance, becomes a powerful vehicle of progress.

The conditions for successful solitary practice are clearly stated: abandon all sinful actions (*pāvāiṃ vivajjayaṃto*) and remain free from attachment to sense-pleasures (*kāmesu asajjamāṇo*). These are the two legs on which solitary practice stands. Without them, solitude becomes loneliness or self-indulgence. With them, it becomes the fertile ground in which the deepest realizations take root.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

If you can't find a good spiritual companion, walk alone — just make sure you stay away from wrongdoing and don't get pulled in by pleasures.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I am spiritually alone — without external support — do I rise to my own highest standard, or do I quietly relax it?

## SECTION II: THE CAUSAL CHAIN OF SUFFERING (Sutras 6–8)

---

### SUTRA 32.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जहा य अंडप्पभवा बलागा, अंडं बलागप्पभवं जहा य ।
एमेव मोहाययणं खु तण्हा, मोहं च तण्हाययणं वयंति ॥ ३२.६ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jahā ya aṃḍappabhavā balāgā, aṃḍaṃ balāgappabhavaṃ jahā ya |
emeva mohāyayaṇaṃ khu taṇhā, mohaṃ ca taṇhāyayaṇaṃ vayaṃti || 32.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Just as the heron is born from the egg and the egg is born from the heron — in exactly the same way, craving is the birthplace of delusion, and delusion is the birthplace of craving, the wise ones declare.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā* = just as; *balāgā* = the heron (a large wading bird); *aṃḍappabhavā* = born from the egg; *aṃḍaṃ* = the egg; *balāgappabhavaṃ* = born from the heron; *emeva* = exactly in the same way; *taṇhā* = craving, thirst; *mohāyayaṇaṃ* = the birthplace, origin of delusion; *mohaṃ* = delusion; *taṇhāyayaṇaṃ* = the birthplace of craving; *vayaṃti* = the wise declare.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The egg-and-heron image is one of the most elegant illustrations of a circular causal chain in all of Jain literature. Which came first? Neither. They produce each other in an endless loop — and this is exactly the point. The soul has been rotating in this loop since beginningless time.

Delusion (*moha*) here refers primarily to false belief and the deluding passions — the fundamental misidentification of the self with the body and world. This delusion generates *craving* (*taṇhā*): the desperate thirst to acquire, possess, and hold what we believe will satisfy us. But craving, when fulfilled, only deepens the delusion — the acquisition feels confirming, the attachment grows, the belief that "this is mine" and "I need more" intensifies. Craving thus regenerates delusion at a deeper level. And deeper delusion generates more intense craving.

This mutual causation is why breaking the cycle is not a matter of simply "trying harder" to resist cravings while leaving the underlying delusion untouched. That is like treating the egg while leaving the heron alive. The root must be addressed — and that root, as Sutra 8 will show, is the very first link of the chain. The chapter is building methodically toward that diagnosis and the prescription that follows.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Confusion feeds craving, and craving feeds confusion — they go around and around in a loop that traps you.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I trace, in some area of my life, how a moment of confusion led to a wanting, and how satisfying that wanting only deepened my confusion?

### SUTRA 32.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रागो य दोसो वि य कम्मबीयं, कम्मं च मोहप्पभवं वयंति ।
कम्मं च जाईमरणस्स मूलं, दुक्खं च जाईमरणं वयंति ॥ ३२.७ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāgo ya doso vi ya kammabīyaṃ, kammaṃ ca mohappabhavaṃ vayaṃti |
kammaṃ ca jāīmaraṇassa mūlaṃ, dukkhaṃ ca jāīmaraṇaṃ vayaṃti || 32.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Attachment and aversion are the seeds of karma; karma, the wise say, arises from delusion; karma is the root of birth and death; and birth and death, they say, are suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rāgo* = attachment; *doso* = aversion; *kammabīyaṃ* = seeds of karma, the root cause of karma-binding; *kammaṃ* = karma; *mohappabhavaṃ* = born from delusion; *vayaṃti* = the wise say; *kammaṃ* = karma; *jāīmaraṇassa* = of birth and death; *mūlaṃ* = root; *jāīmaraṇaṃ* = birth and death; *dukkhaṃ* = suffering; *vayaṃti* = they say.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse extends the causal chain outward into full display. The structure is a logical sequence: delusion → attachment/aversion → karma → birth-death → suffering. Each link is shown to lead to the next.

Attachment and aversion (*rāga* and *dosa*) are here called "seeds of karma." This is precise language. It is not the physical act alone that binds karma; it is the charged emotional state — the grasping desire or the pushing-away aversion — that creates the karma-binding condition in the soul. A person who performs the same action with complete equanimity creates no karma; the same action performed with burning attachment or fierce aversion binds powerful karma.

Karma, in turn, is said to arise from delusion (*moha*). This connects back to Sutra 6: it is because the soul operates under the delusion of self-misidentification (thinking it is the body, its relations, its possessions) that attachment and aversion arise in the first place.

Karma then drives birth and death — the very mechanism of continued existence in cyclic form. And birth-death is identified as suffering, not because particular lives are always miserable, but because the condition of being subject to birth, aging, disease, and death is itself a form of suffering — an inherent instability and vulnerability that no accumulation of pleasure can permanently resolve.

The verse is thus a complete map of the problem. The next verse will describe the solution.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Clinging and pushing away create karma, karma causes you to be born and die again and again, and that cycle of birth and death is what suffering actually is.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In what ways am I currently seeding karma through my attachments or aversions — not through grand actions, but through subtle emotional reactions?

### SUTRA 32.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दुक्खं हयं जस्स ण होइ मोहो, मोहो हओ जस्स ण होइ तण्हा ।
तण्हा हया जस्स ण होइ लोहो, लोहो हओ जस्स ण किंचणाइं ॥ ३२.८ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dukkhaṃ hayaṃ jassa ṇa hoi moho, moho hao jassa ṇa hoi taṇhā |
taṇhā hayā jassa ṇa hoi loho, loho hao jassa ṇa kiṃcaṇāiṃ || 32.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Suffering is destroyed in one who has no delusion; delusion is destroyed in one who has no craving; craving is destroyed in one who has no greed; greed is destroyed in one who has no possessiveness at all.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jassa* = in whom, for whom; *moho* = delusion; *ṇa hoi* = does not exist; *dukkhaṃ* = suffering; *hayaṃ* = destroyed; *moho* = delusion; *hao* = destroyed; *jassa taṇhā ṇa hoi* = in whom craving does not exist; *taṇhā* = craving; *hayā* = destroyed; *jassa loho ṇa hoi* = in whom greed does not exist; *loho* = greed; *hao* = destroyed; *jassa kiṃcaṇāiṃ ṇa* = in whom there is no possessiveness, no clinging to anything.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the most structurally elegant verse in the chapter — a reverse unwinding of the causal chain presented in Sutras 6 and 7. Where those sutras traced the descent from delusion into suffering, this sutra traces the ascent from non-possessiveness to liberation from suffering. It is a four-step ladder constructed in reverse order: (4) non-possessiveness → (3) no greed → (2) no craving → (1) no delusion → liberation from all suffering.

The logic is precise. Possessiveness (*kiṃcaṇā* — clinging to anything as "mine") is the root of greed. When the "mine-feeling" disappears entirely — when there is nothing one clings to — greed has nothing to feed on and dissolves. With greed gone, craving (the active *thirst* to acquire more) has no engine and ceases. With craving gone, the deluding passions that constitute *moha* find no fuel and extinguish. And with delusion gone, suffering — which was always a product of the deluded state — simply has no cause and ends.

This verse reveals the prescription hidden in the diagnosis of Sutra 6. The cycle can be broken — not by directly attacking delusion (which is subtle and hard to see) but by working on possessiveness, which is visible in daily life. Every act of releasing "mine-ness," every moment of practicing true non-possessiveness, cuts a link in the chain that leads all the way up to delusion and all the way down to the end of suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Let go of the idea that anything is truly "mine," and greed fades; greed gone, craving fades; craving gone, confusion fades; confusion gone, suffering ends.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are the things I cling to most fiercely as "mine"? What would it feel like — truly feel like — to hold them lightly?

## SECTION III: METHODS TO UPROOT ATTACHMENT (Sutras 9–20)

---

### SUTRA 32.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रागं च दोसं च तहेव मोहं, उद्धतु कामेण समूल जालं ।
जे जे उवाया पडिवज्जियव्वा, ते कित्तइस्सामि अहाणुपुठ्विं ॥ ३२.९ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāgaṃ ca dosaṃ ca taheva mohaṃ, uddhatu kāmeṇa samūla jālaṃ |
je je uvāyā paḍivajjiyavvā, te kittaissāmi ahāṇupuṭṭhiṃ || 32.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

I will enumerate in proper sequence all the means that must be adopted by one who desires to uproot, together with its very roots, the entire net of attachment, aversion, and delusion.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rāgaṃ* = attachment; *dosaṃ* = aversion; *mohaṃ* = delusion; *samūla jālaṃ* = entire net together with its roots; *kāmeṇa* = by one who desires; *uddhatu* = to uproot, to tear up; *je je uvāyā* = whatever means; *paḍivajjiyavvā* = are to be adopted; *te* = those; *ahāṇupuṭṭhiṃ* = in proper order, step by step; *kittaissāmi* = I will enumerate, describe.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse functions as a second invocation — or more precisely, as the transition from diagnosis to treatment. Having mapped the causal chain (Sutras 6-8) and shown how it can be unwound in reverse, the teacher now announces that he will systematically present the practical methods of that unwinding.

The image of a "net" (*jāla*) is deliberate. Attachment, aversion, and delusion do not operate as isolated forces; they are woven together into a net from which the soul cannot simply step out. The net must be *uprooted* — the Prakrit word *uddhatu* conveys pulling up forcefully, as one pulls out a deep-rooted plant. The phrase *samūla* — "together with the roots" — echoes the opening verse's promise to address root causes, not just symptoms.

The promise to enumerate in *proper sequence* (*ahāṇupuṭṭhiṃ*) signals the organized, systematic teaching that follows through the rest of the chapter. What follows is not a random list of virtues but a precise, ordered program: first, control of food (Sutra 10), then metaphors of the danger of sensory excess (Sutra 11), then conditions for protecting restraint (Sutra 12), then warnings about specific threats to celibacy (Sutras 13-16), and finally the detailed, structured treatment of each sense organ (Sutras 21-99).

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

I will now teach you, step by step, every method you need to pull out attachment, hatred, and delusion by their roots.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I willing to follow a structured path of practice, or do I want liberation without the discipline the path requires?

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Rich and stimulating foods should not be consumed in excess; for generally, such foods inflame the senses; and when the senses are inflamed, sensual desires attack the person, just as birds swarm a fruit-laden tree.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rasā* = rich foods — milk, ghee, and the like; *pagāmaṃ* = in excess; *ṇa ṇiseviyavvā* = should not be consumed; *pāyaṃ* = generally, as a rule; *rasā* = such rich foods; *dittikarā* = inflaming, stimulating; *ṇarāṇaṃ* = for people; *dittaṃ* = aroused, inflamed; *kāmā* = sensual desires; *samibhaddavaṃti* = attack, assail; *dumaṃ* = tree; *sāuphalaṃ* = bearing sweet fruit; *pakkhī* = birds; *va* = like.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse opens the practical program with the most fundamental physical factor: diet. In the Jain contemplative tradition, the connection between food and mental state is understood with remarkable precision. Rich, stimulating foods — dairy products, sweets, highly seasoned preparations — provide excess building material for the body's energy systems, particularly the vital essence (*dhātu*) that fuels sensory arousal. When the body is overfed with stimulating foods, the sensory energies flare up, and desires become more insistent and harder to resist.

The bird-and-fruit-tree analogy is vivid and exact. A bare tree draws no birds; a tree laden with sweet fruit is immediately mobbed. Similarly, the practitioner whose body is agitated with excess rich food becomes an irresistible target for desire. This teaching does not advocate starvation or extreme asceticism. It advocates *miya* — measured, appropriate, fault-free food (as stated in Sutra 4). The practitioner eats to sustain the body for practice, not to gratify the tongue. This is a foundational discipline: when the dietary root is controlled, the sensory system becomes calmer, the mind becomes clearer, and the inner work becomes possible.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Eating too much rich food stirs up desires, just like how sweet fruit draws a flock of birds — keep your eating simple to keep your mind calm.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How honestly do I relate to food — as fuel for practice, or as one of my quiet pleasures that I'm not yet willing to examine?

The teaching here is not puritanical but practical. It is like a fire scientist explaining that you cannot extinguish a fire while continuously adding fuel to it. The celibate practitioner who overeats is working against himself at the most basic physical level. The path of restraint begins in the kitchen.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Overeating is like throwing more wood on a forest fire — it makes the fire of your desires impossible to put out.

What habits of consumption in my life — not just food, but anything I consume — am I using to feed a fire I claim I want to extinguish?

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

This verse summarizes the protective conditions against passion. Three disciplines are named: solitude, moderate diet, and sense-restraint. Together, they form a shield against the assault of passion.

This verse thus functions as a summary teaching and also as a promise: the path is not endless struggle. When the right conditions are maintained consistently, there is a tipping point at which passion loses its grip. **6. SIMPLY PUT**

Live in a quiet place, eat little, control your senses — and desire loses its power over you, just like a cured disease doesn't come back.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have I created the outer conditions for inner freedom, or am I trying to be free while living in conditions that make freedom harder?

### SUTRA 32.13

Just as it is not commendable for mice to reside near the dwelling of a cat — in exactly the same way, it is not proper for a celibate practitioner to reside in the midst of women's dwelling-places.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā* = just as; *birālavasahassa* = of the dwelling-place of a cat; *mūle* = near, close to; *mūsagāṇaṃ* = for mice; *vasahī* = residence; *pasatthā* = commendable, fitting; *ṇa* = not; *emeva* = in exactly the same way; *itthī ṇilayassa* = of women's dwelling; *majjhe* = in the midst; *baṃbhayārissa* = for the celibate practitioner; *ṇivāso* = residing; *khamo* = fitting, appropriate; *ṇa* = not.

This verse begins a series of teachings (Sutras 13-20) specifically addressing the challenge of celibacy and the proximity of women for the male monastic practitioner. The verse employs a dry, practical analogy: mice should not live near a cat's den. Not because the cat is evil, but because the structural arrangement creates conditions for harm. The mouse near the cat's den is perpetually at risk; its very survival is compromised by the proximity.

In the monastic context, the celibate male practitioner is vulnerable to the arousal of sexual desire when in proximity to women's dwelling spaces. This is not a judgment on women; it is an honest assessment of the practitioner's own vulnerability in his early stages of training. The tradition is consistent: the burden of managing this vulnerability lies with the practitioner, not with those around him. He is to arrange his outer environment to reduce unnecessary exposure, just as a person in recovery arranges their environment to reduce exposure to what triggers relapse.

This teaching is to be understood in its historical and social context. It addresses a specific monastic situation and should not be generalized into social attitudes toward women. The underlying principle — that practitioners should honestly assess what conditions make their inner work easier or harder, and structure their environment accordingly — is universal and timeless.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A monk committed to celibacy should not live among women's quarters — just as it makes no sense for a mouse to live right next to a cat.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I honest with myself about which environments and situations weaken my spiritual commitments, and do I take responsibility for avoiding them?

### SUTRA 32.14

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

इत्थीण चित्तंसि णिवेसइत्ता, दटुं ववस्से समणे तवस्सी ॥ ३२.१४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa rūva lāvaṇṇavilāsa hāsaṃ, ṇa jaṃpiyaṃ iṃgiyaṃ pehiyaṃ vā |
itthīṇa cittaṃsi ṇiveseiittā, daṭuṃ vavasse samaṇe tavassī || 32.14 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ascetic practitioner should not fix in his mind — and then seek to gaze upon — the beauty, grace, playfulness, and laughter of women, nor their speech, gestures, and glances.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*samaṇe tavassī* = the ascetic, the austere practitioner; *itthīṇa* = of women; *rūva-lāvaṇṇa-vilāsa-hāsaṃ* = beauty, grace, playfulness, and laughter; *jaṃpiyaṃ* = sweet speech; *iṃgiyaṃ* = gestures, physical movements; *pehiyaṃ* = glances, looks; *vā* = or; *cittaṃsi ṇiveseiittā* = establishing in the mind with passion; *daṭuṃ* = to see; *ṇa vavasse* = should not attempt.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse addresses the inner side of what Sutra 13 addressed externally. Sutra 13 taught about physical proximity; this sutra teaches about attention. Even if the practitioner is not in physical proximity, the mind can become agitated through intentional, fascinated looking. The verse identifies a specific danger: when the practitioner first notices, then holds the attention on, then becomes absorbed in the aesthetic appeal of women — their beauty, grace, playful manner, sweet words, gestures, glances — this sequence progressively intensifies sensory desire and weakens the inner equilibrium.

The psychological insight here is sharp. Looking without attachment is simply the eye performing its natural function. But *dwelling* on what is seen — deliberately holding the attention on a pleasant form and savoring it — is a choice that generates and strengthens craving. This is precisely what the practitioner must learn not to do. The senses must be used; they cannot be disabled. But they need not be *indulged*. There is a difference between seeing and savoring.

The teaching applies mutatis mutandis to all sense channels: the ear that keeps listening to music that arouses desire, the nose that keeps inhaling a pleasing fragrance with deliberate pleasure — in each case, the act of deliberately returning attention to the pleasant sense-object is what the practitioner must learn to refrain from.

Don't stare at what stirs up desire in you — don't dwell on it in your mind or keep looking for it. That is how the fire gets started.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

adaṃsaṇaṃ ceva apatthṇaṃ ca, aciṃtaṇaṃ ceva akittaṇaṃ ca |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Not seeing, not desiring, not thinking about, and not praising the forms of women — and remaining engaged in noble, meditation-worthy contemplation — this is always the welfare of those devoted to the celibate vow.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*adaṃsaṇaṃ* = not seeing with passion; *apatthṇaṃ* = not desiring, not wishing for; *aciṃtaṇaṃ* = not thinking about, not mentally dwelling on; *akittaṇaṃ* = not praising, not glorifying the form; *itthijañasāriya* = pertaining to women; *jhāṇajuggaṃ* = fit for meditation, worthy of contemplation — i.e., noble spiritual objects; *hiyaṃ* = welfare-giving; *sayā* = always; *baṃbhavae rayāṇaṃ* = for those devoted to the celibate vow.

This verse provides a positive reformulation of the previous verse's warnings. The practitioner who admires out loud what he is trying not to crave internally is working against his own practice. This is why the tradition requires not just behavioral restraint but a comprehensive reorientation of attention, speech, and thought.

The verse then gives the positive alternative: remain immersed in *jhāṇajuggaṃ* — contemplation worthy of meditation, meaning the noble objects of reflection befitting an aspirant of truth: the nature of the soul, the qualities of the liberated ones, the transience of the body, the suffering of cyclic existence, and similar themes. The practitioner does not merely empty the mind; he redirects it toward what uplifts and clarifies.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

For the celibate practitioner, the four-fold protection is: don't look at, don't desire, don't think about, don't praise — and keep your mind on what truly matters.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In my daily life, what am I unconsciously praising — speaking admiringly of — that I know quietly strengthens an attachment I am trying to release?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Even though those monks who have achieved the three-fold restraint cannot be disturbed by divine women adorned with ornaments — even so, knowing that solitary dwelling is exclusively beneficial, it is commendable for such monks.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

By applying the standard universally — even to those who genuinely meet it — the tradition creates a clear, consistent discipline that is not subject to individual assessment and rationalization. This is the wisdom of rules: they protect not just the weak but also the strong from the subtle convincing that their own strength is greater than it is.

Even the strongest, most disciplined monk should live in solitude — because the standard protects everyone, and solitude is simply the wisest environment.

### SUTRA 32.19

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कामाणुगिद्धिप्पभवं खु दुक्खं, सव्वस्स लोगस्स सदेवगस्स ।
जं काइयं माणिसयं च किंचि, तस्संतगं गच्छइ वीयरागो ॥ ३२.१९ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kāmāṇugiddhippabhavaṃ khu dukkhaṃ, savvassa logassa sadevagassa |
jaṃ kāiyaṃ māṇisayaṃ ca kiṃci, tassaṃtaṃ gacchai vīyarāgo || 32.19 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

All the suffering of the entire world, including the gods — whatever is physical, mental, or any kind at all — is verily born from attachment to sense-pleasures; the dispassionate one brings it to an end.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sadevagassa* = including the gods; *savvassa logassa* = of the entire world; *kāmāṇugiddhippabhavaṃ* = born from, arising from attachment to sense-pleasures; *dukkhaṃ* = suffering; *khu* = verily, indeed; *jaṃ kiṃci* = whatever; *kāiyaṃ* = physical, bodily; *māṇisayaṃ* = mental; *vīyarāgo* = the dispassionate one; *tassa* = that suffering's; *aṃtaṃ* = end; *gacchai* = arrives at, brings about.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse makes a sweeping claim: *all* suffering in the entire universe, including the suffering of the gods, originates in attachment to sensory pleasure. This is a cornerstone of Jain philosophy. The claim is not that sense-objects are inherently evil; it is that *clinging* to them — the grasping desire that cannot let go — is the root of every form of suffering that exists.

Even the gods suffer. Despite their magnificent sensory environments, their immense lifespans, their beautiful companions and celestial pleasures, the gods are not free. They are deeply attached to their pleasures, and this attachment — precisely because the pleasures are so refined and so available — makes them more deeply trapped, not less. When those pleasures eventually end (and they do end, as every divine life has its expiration), the suffering of the gods is immense.

The verse's climax is the dispassionate one (*vīyarāgo*): he who has released attachment to all sense-pleasures brings all suffering to its end. Not just his own suffering — the verse implies that through liberation, the dispassionate one steps out of the entire economy of suffering that addiction to pleasure perpetuates. Liberation is not just personal escape; it is the complete dissolution of the mechanism that generates suffering.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Every single suffering — yours, mine, even the gods' — comes from wanting and clinging to pleasures. The person who lets go of that wanting ends suffering completely.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I trace a current suffering in my life back to an attachment to some pleasure I am not willing to fully release?

### SUTRA 32.20

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जहा य किंपागफला मणोरमा, रसेण वण्णेण य भुज्जमाणा ।
ते खुइए जीवियं पच्चमाणा, एओवमा कामगुणा विवागे ॥ ३२.२० ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jahā ya kiṃpāgaphalā maṇoramā, raseṇa vaṇṇeṇa ya bhujjamāṇā |
te khuḍue jīviyaṃ paccamāṇā, eovamā kāmaguṇā vivāge || 32.20 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Just as the fruits of the Kimpaka tree are beautiful to look at and sweet to taste when consumed — yet in the end, when digested, they destroy life itself — in exactly the same way is the consequence of sense-pleasures.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jahā* = just as; *kiṃpāgaphalā* = fruits of the Kimpaka tree (a poisonous tree); *maṇoramā* = beautiful, pleasing to the mind; *raseṇa* = in taste; *vaṇṇeṇa* = in appearance; *bhujjamāṇā* = when consumed; *te* = they; *khuḍue* = after some time, after being consumed; *jīviyaṃ* = life; *paccamāṇā* = killing, destroying; *eovamā* = this is the analogy; *kāmaguṇā* = sense-pleasures; *vivāge* = in their consequence, in their ripening.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The Kimpaka fruit analogy is one of the most memorable images in the Uttaradhyayana. The Kimpaka (also known as Kinipaka or Tumba) is a tree whose fruits are beautiful in color and sweet in taste but lethally poisonous. The person who eats one experiences pleasure — and then, as digestion proceeds, death.

Sense-pleasures work the same way. In the moment of experience, they are genuinely pleasant — the sweetness is real, the beauty is real. The Jain tradition does not deny the immediate reality of sensory pleasure. What it reveals is the *consequence* — the *vipāka* — that plays out afterward: the karma bound in the pleasure-seeking, the deepened attachment, the strengthened craving, the continued birth-death cycle, and ultimately more suffering across many lifetimes.

The gods, as Sutra 19 noted, have access to the most refined sensory pleasures. And yet even they are ultimately destroyed by them — their divine lives end, they fall back into lower births, and they suffer. The Kimpaka fruit is an image of exactly this: exquisite pleasure, lethal consequence.

This verse closes the introductory section before the systematic treatment of the individual sense channels begins. The message: know what you are dealing with before you assume you are safe.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Pleasures are like a beautiful poison fruit — lovely to eat but deadly in the end. This is the truth about sensory indulgence.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What pleasure in my life has a "Kimpaka quality" — beautiful on the surface, but with consequences I prefer not to think about?

## SECTION IV: CONQUEST OF THE EYE (Sutras 21–34)

*The fourteen sutras of this section trace the complete cycle of sight-attachment: from the initial declaration of the master principle (Sutra 21), through the two-way causal relation of eye and form (Sutras 22–23), the death-by-moth analogy (Sutra 24), the counter-movement of aversion (Sutra 25), through the cascade of theft, lying, craving, and misery (Sutras 26–33), and finally to the lotus-in-water image of the detached practitioner (Sutra 34). The same 13-sutra arc repeats for each subsequent sense channel.*

---

### SUTRA 32.21

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे इंदियाणं विसया मणुण्णा, ण तेसु भावं णिसिरे कयाइ ।
ण यामणुण्णेसु मणं पि कुज्जा, समाहिकामे समणे तवस्सी ॥ ३२.२१ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je iṃdiyāṇaṃ visayā maṇuṇṇā, ṇa tesu bhāvaṃ ṇisirae kayāi |
ṇa yāmaṇuṇṇesu maṇaṃ pi kujjā, samāhikāme samaṇe tavassī || 32.21 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ascetic practitioner who desires inner equilibrium should never allow attachment to arise toward pleasing sense-objects, nor should he allow even the mind to generate aversion toward displeasing ones.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*samāhikāme* = desiring inner equilibrium; *samaṇe tavassī* = the austere monk; *iṃdiyāṇaṃ visayā* = objects of the senses; *maṇuṇṇā* = pleasing, agreeable; *tesu bhāvaṃ* = attachment-state toward them; *kayāi ṇa ṇisirae* = should never cause to arise; *amaṇuṇṇesu* = toward displeasing objects; *maṇaṃ pi* = even the mind; *ṇa kujjā* = should not generate aversion.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse is the master-principle for the entire six-section program that follows. The practitioner seeking inner equilibrium must train to neither generate attachment toward pleasing sense-objects nor aversion toward displeasing ones. This double restraint — refusing both grasping and pushing-away — is equanimity in action.

The senses will naturally encounter both pleasing and displeasing objects; this is unavoidable. The objects are not the problem. The problem is the habitual lurch toward pleasure and recoil from pain that the untrained mind automatically performs. Each such lurch or recoil is a micro-binding of karma, a micro-reinforcement of the deluded self-identification.

The path is not to destroy the senses or make them numb. It is to use the senses without being enslaved by them — to let the eye see without the heart lurching toward what it likes or pulling away from what it dislikes. The equanimous practitioner actually sees more clearly than the desire-driven person, because he is not overlaying experience with the noise of craving and aversion. Genuine perception becomes possible when reaction ceases.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Don't let pleasant things make you cling, and don't let unpleasant things make you react with hatred — that middle path is where true inner peace lives.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In the last 24 hours, which sense-encounter caused me to cling, and which caused me to push away? Can I notice how automatic that was?

### SUTRA 32.22

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

चक्खुस्स रूवं गहणं वयंति, तं रागहेउं तु मणुण्णमाहु ।
तं दोसहेउं अमणुण्णमाहु, समो य जो तेसु स वीयरागो ॥ ३२.२२ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

cakkhusssa rūvaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, taṃ rāgaheūṃ tu maṇuṇṇamāhu |
taṃ dosaheuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu, samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo || 32.22 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The eye's grasping-object is said to be form; a pleasing form is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing form is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*cakkhusssa* = of the eye; *rūvaṃ* = form; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *vayaṃti* = they say; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing form; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *āhu* = is called; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing form; *dosaheuṃ* = cause of aversion; *jo tesu samo* = who is equanimous in both; *sa vīyarāgo* = that one is the truly dispassionate one.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse opens the systematic analysis with the eye-form relationship. Three points establish the structure: (1) form is what the eye grasps; (2) pleasing form causes attachment; (3) displeasing form causes aversion; and (4) the equanimous one who is undisturbed by both is truly free.

The word *gahaṇa* — grasping — is significant. The eye does not merely perceive; it grasps. The same word is used for the hand taking hold of an object. This reveals the Jain understanding that ordinary perception is already an act of reaching-and-holding. The trained practitioner learns to let the eye see without this grasping quality — to witness without clutching.

The definition of the dispassionate one is precise: *samo* — equal, undisturbed — toward both pleasing and displeasing forms. This is not the anesthesia of someone who cannot see; it is the active equanimity of someone who sees clearly but is not controlled by what he sees. The eye continues to function; the soul remains free.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The eye sees forms — pretty ones pull you toward them, ugly ones push you away. The truly free person doesn't get pulled or pushed by either.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Right now, is there something I am seeing that is generating either attraction or aversion in me? Can I simply let it be seen without the reactive charge?

### SUTRA 32.23

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रूवस्स चक्खुं गहणं वयंति, चक्खुस्स रूवं गहणं वयंति ।
रागस्स हेउं समणुण्णमाहु, दोसस्स हेउं अमणुण्णमाहु ॥ ३२.२३ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūvassa cakkhuṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, cakkhusssa rūvaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti |
rāgassa heuṃ samaṇuṇṇamāhu, dosassa heuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu || 32.23 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Form's grasping-organ is said to be the eye; the eye's grasping-object is said to be form. The pleasing form is the cause of attachment; the displeasing form is the cause of aversion.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūvassa* = of form; *cakkhuṃ* = the eye; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-organ; *cakkhusssa* = of the eye; *rūvaṃ* = form; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *samaṇuṇṇaṃ* = the pleasing; *rāgassa heuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = the displeasing; *dosassa heuṃ* = cause of aversion.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse deepens the analysis by showing the mutual dependency between the perceiving organ and the perceived object. The eye is the grasping-organ for form; form is the grasping-object of the eye. This bidirectional relationship reflects the Jain understanding that perception is not a one-way transmission from object to subject. The subject and object are intertwined in the act of perception itself.

This has a practical implication: one cannot isolate the problem as being either in the object or in the eye alone. Beautiful form is genuinely beautiful — the eye does not manufacture its beauty. But the eye is also genuinely inclined to grasp — form does not force the eye to clutch. The karma-creating event happens in the *meeting* of the two: when consciousness meets form and responds with attachment or aversion rather than clear seeing.

The training must therefore address both the inner response (the tendency to cling or recoil) and the outer conditions (proximity to sense-objects that habitually trigger these responses). Sutra 12 addressed outer conditions; these sutras analyze the inner mechanism.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The eye and form are bound together — each grasps the other. The pleasant form creates liking, the unpleasant creates disliking. This is simply how it works — until you train yourself out of it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I observe, in a quiet moment, the act of seeing itself — before the label "pleasant" or "unpleasant" is applied? What is pure perception, before the reaction?

### SUTRA 32.24

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रूवेसु जो गिद्धिमुवेइ तिव्वं, अकालियं पावइ से विणासं ।
रागाउरे से जह वा पयंगे, आलोयलोले समुवेइ मच्चुं ॥ ३२.२४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūvesu jo giddhimuvei tivvaṃ, akāliyaṃ pāvai se viṇāsaṃ |
rāgāure se jaha vā payaṃge, āloyalole samuvei maccuṃ || 32.24 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to forms meets untimely destruction — just as the moth, inflamed with passion and infatuated with the light, flies into the flame and meets death.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūvesu* = in forms; *tivvaṃ giddhiṃ* = intense addiction; *uvei* = develops; *se* = that one; *akāliyaṃ* = untimely; *viṇāsaṃ* = destruction; *pāvai* = obtains; *rāgāure* = inflamed with passion; *payaṃge* = the moth; *āloyalole* = infatuated with light, flame-obsessed; *maccuṃ* = death; *samuvei* = arrives at.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The moth-and-flame analogy is one of the most ancient and universal images for the destruction caused by sensory infatuation. In the Uttaradhyayana it serves as the central analogy for vision-based attachment. The moth is not forced into the flame; it flies in willingly, driven by the very object that will destroy it. Its attraction to the light is so overwhelming that it cannot process the danger inherent in what it desires.

The word *akāliyaṃ* — untimely — is important. The practitioner whose soul has obtained a human birth, with all its potential for liberation, destroys that potential prematurely by following intense form-attachment to its consequences. The human birth is the rare, precious opportunity to practice and advance. Form-addiction consumes it.

The teaching is not that forms are evil or that the eye should be closed. It is that *intense addiction* (*tivva giddhi*) — the state in which the mind is so absorbed in the pursuit of a pleasing form that it can no longer choose wisely — is literally lethal to spiritual progress. The flame does not come to the moth; the moth comes to the flame. The form does not compel the mind; the untrained mind compels itself.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A person wildly addicted to beautiful things gets destroyed by them — just like the moth that can't resist flying into the flame.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What beautiful thing am I currently circling like a moth — drawn toward it, knowing somewhere that it has the quality of a flame?

### SUTRA 32.25

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जे याविं दोसं समुवेइ तिव्वं, तंसिकखणे से उ उवेइ दुक्खं ।
दुदंत दोसेण सएण जंतु, ण किंचि रूवं अवरज्झइ से ॥ ३२.२५ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je yāviṃ dosaṃ samuvei tivvaṃ, taṃsikhaṇe se u uvei dukkhaṃ |
dudaṃta doseṇa saeṇa jaṃtu, ṇa kiṃci rūvaṃ avarajjhai se || 32.25 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whoever develops intense aversion toward a displeasing form immediately experiences suffering — from their own fierce aversion. In this, no form is at fault.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*yāviṃ* = whoever; *tivvaṃ dosaṃ* = intense aversion; *samuvei* = develops; *taṃsikhaṇe* = at that very moment; *se* = that one; *dukkhaṃ uvei* = obtains suffering; *dudaṃta doseṇa saeṇa* = by one's own fierce aversion; *rūvaṃ* = form; *kiṃci avarajjhai* = commits no fault; *ṇa se* = not in this.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse is the mirror of Sutra 24. Where Sutra 24 addressed intense attachment to pleasing forms, this sutra addresses intense aversion to displeasing ones. Both are equally binding and equally suffering-producing — and in both cases, the fault lies not in the object but in the reaction.

The verse makes a statement that is profoundly liberating when genuinely understood: when a person suffers intense aversion toward a displeasing form, the suffering comes from the aversion itself, not from the form. The ugly thing, the offensive sight, the disturbing image — none of these are the actual source of suffering. The suffering is generated entirely by the mind's fierce recoiling. The form is innocent.

This is radical because the ordinary mind instinctively blames external objects for its distress: "That sight upset me." But in each case, the mechanism of suffering is internal — it is the aversion-reaction that burns, not the external object. This understanding is enormously practical: the practitioner has full authority over his own suffering, because he has authority — through training — over his own reactions.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When something ugly makes you furious, the pain comes from your own anger — not from the ugly thing itself. The thing isn't at fault.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What displeasing sight or situation am I currently allowing to "make me" upset? Can I locate the suffering in my own reaction rather than in the external thing?

### SUTRA 32.26

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगंतरते रूइरंसि रूवे, अतालिसे से कुणइ पओसं ।
दुक्खस्स संपीलमुवेइ बाले, ण लिप्पइ तेण मुणी विरागो ॥ ३२.२६ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

egaṃtaratte ruiraṃsi rūve, atālise se kuṇai pāosaṃ |
dukkhaassa saṃpīlamuvei bāle, ṇa lippai teṇa muṇī virāgo || 32.26 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ignorant one who is deeply attached to beautiful forms and harbors aversion toward dissimilar (ugly) forms accumulates a mass of suffering — but the detached monk is not tainted by this.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*egaṃtaratte* = deeply, single-mindedly attached; *ruiraṃsi rūve* = in beautiful forms; *atālise* = toward unlike, ugly forms; *pāosaṃ kuṇai* = generates aversion; *bāle* = the ignorant one; *dukkhaassa saṃpīlaṃ* = a mass of suffering, crushed by suffering from all sides; *uvei* = obtains; *muṇī virāgo* = the detached monk; *teṇa ṇa lippai* = is not tainted by this.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse synthesizes Sutras 24 and 25. The ignorant person does both simultaneously: he is intensely attached to the beautiful (pulling toward it) and intensely averse to the ugly (pushing away from it). Together these two reactive poles generate a *mass* of suffering — the word *saṃpīla* suggests something compressed from all sides. The person trapped between craving-for-beauty and aversion-to-ugliness is perpetually squeezed between two forces he cannot control.

The detached monk, by contrast, is not tainted. The image of "not being smeared" (*ṇa lippai*) implies that suffering is like mud: it sticks to what it touches. The equanimous monk moves through the same world, sees the same beautiful and ugly forms, but because he neither grasps nor recoils, nothing adheres. He remains clean.

This contrast — the ignorant person drowning in the accumulated suffering of reactive vision, and the detached monk moving unstained — is the central image of the entire sense-conquest program. It will recur in parallel for each subsequent sense.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The person who chases beautiful things and hates ugly ones gets crushed between both — but the wise monk attached to neither stays clean and free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in my life am I being squeezed between something I desperately want and something I desperately want to avoid?

### SUTRA 32.27

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रूवाणुगासाणुगए य जीवे, चराचरे हिंसइ णेगरूवे ।
चित्तेहिं ते परितावेइ बाले, पीलेइ अत्तदुगुरू किलिट्टे ॥ ३२.२७ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūvāṇugāsāṇugae ya jīve, carācre hiṃsai ṇegarūve |
cittehiṃ te paritāvei bāle, pīlei attadugurū kiliṭṭe || 32.27 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Ensnared in the hope of form and following after it, the ignorant one harms many kinds of moving and stationary living beings; through his inner states he torments them, and being enslaved to self-interest with wicked results, he oppresses many creatures.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūvāṇugāsā āṇugae* = following after form, ensnared in the hope of obtaining beautiful forms; *jīve* = living beings; *ṇegarūve* = of many kinds; *carācre* = moving and stationary; *hiṃsai* = harms; *bāle* = the ignorant one; *cittehiṃ* = through inner states; *paritāvei* = causes distress; *attadugurū* = enslaved to self-interest; *kiliṭṭe* = wicked, defiled one; *pīlei* = oppresses, hurts.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse reveals that form-attachment does not only damage the practitioner internally — it radiates outward as harm to other living beings. In the pursuit of beautiful objects — fine goods, ornaments, possessions — the attached person participates in the harm of countless creatures: in their production, transport, and maintenance, living beings at multiple levels are damaged.

More subtly, the verse points to *inner states* (*cittehiṃ*) as the medium of harm. The person absorbed in form-lust generates mental states of envy, aggression, comparison, and possessiveness that, even without physical action, torment those around him. The obsessed person makes others miserable through his constant comparisons, demands, and dissatisfaction.

The phrase *attadugurū* — "enslaved to self-interest" — names the root. The form-attached person has made self-gratification supreme, and everything else — welfare of other beings, ethical constraints, the deeper needs of his own soul — is sacrificed to it. This is precisely the opposite of the spiritual path.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Someone obsessed with having beautiful things ends up hurting many other living beings in the process — and causes harm through their very mental state of grasping and jealousy.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What suffering — in people, animals, or the natural world — is silently downstream from my current patterns of consuming and visual desire?

### SUTRA 32.28

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रूवाणुवाएण परिग्गहेण, उप्पायणे रक्खण सण्णिओगे ।
वए विओगे य कहं सुहं से, संभोगकाले य अतित्तलाभे ॥ ३२.२८ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūvāṇuvāeṇa pariggaheṇa, uppāyaṇe rakkhaṇa saṇṇioge |
vae vióge ya kahaṃ suhaṃ se, saṃbhogakāle ya atittalābhe || 32.28 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Through form-driven possessiveness — in acquiring beautiful things, in protecting them, in using them, in their loss and separation — where is happiness for such a person? Even at the time of enjoyment there is the suffering of insatiability.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūvāṇuvāeṇa* = driven by form, following after form; *pariggaheṇa* = through possessiveness; *uppāyaṇe* = in acquiring; *rakkhaṇa* = in protecting; *saṇṇioge* = in using; *vae* = in their loss; *vióge* = in separation from them; *kahaṃ suhaṃ se* = how can there be happiness for that one?; *saṃbhogakāle* = at the time of enjoyment; *atittalābhe* = the suffering of insatiability.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse provides a complete economic analysis of form-attachment through its full lifecycle. At every stage — acquiring, protecting, using, losing, being separated from — the form-attached person suffers. The verse asks: "Where is happiness for such a person?" The implied answer is: nowhere, at any stage.

Acquiring involves anxiety, competition, and the energy of obsessive pursuit. Protecting involves constant vigilance and fear of loss. Using involves the suffering of insatiability — the dissatisfied quality of pleasure that never quite delivers what was promised. Loss involves grief. Separation involves longing. Not one phase of this cycle delivers the stable, uncaused happiness the soul seeks.

This verse methodically dismantles the illusion that form-attachment can bring lasting satisfaction. The nature of forms is transient; the nature of craving is insatiable. The meeting of a transient object with an insatiable desire can only generate the cycle of brief gratification followed by renewed hunger — never the resolution the soul is actually seeking.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Chasing beautiful things brings stress when you want them, anxiety when you keep them, suffering when you lose them, and even dissatisfaction while you have them — there's no peace in it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Pick one possession or experience you're working hard to obtain or keep — trace its full lifecycle honestly. Where exactly is the happiness?

### SUTRA 32.29

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अतुट्टि दोसेण दुही परस्स, लोभाविले आययइ अदत्तं ॥ ३२.२९ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The one deeply attached to forms and their possession never attains contentment. Made miserable by the fault of discontentment, with mind clouded by greed, he seizes what belongs to others.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūve pariggahimmi sattovastto* = deeply attached to forms and their possession; *tuṭṭiṃ ṇa uvei* = does not attain contentment; *atuṭṭi doseṇa* = by the fault of discontentment; *duhī* = made miserable; *lobhāvile* = with mind clouded by greed; *parassa adattaṃ* = what is not given, others' property; *āyayai* = seizes, takes.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse traces the inevitable path from form-attachment to theft. The chain is clear and psychologically precise: deep attachment to beautiful forms → insatiability → discontentment → misery → greed-clouded mind → taking what belongs to others.

The word *atuṭṭi* — discontentment, insatiability — is the pivot. The form-attached person who cannot find contentment becomes *duhī* — miserable — not because he lacks what he desires but because the desire-structure itself generates misery. From that misery, greed (*lobha*) arises as a desperate attempt to find relief through more acquisition. And greed, when intense enough, dissolves the inhibitions against taking what is not given.

This is not an extreme or unusual case; it is the natural consequence of the attachment-structure when followed to its logical conclusion. Every form of theft, fraud, and exploitation can be traced to this sequence. The teaching reveals the root and asks the practitioner to address it there, before it manifests in gross violation of others.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Wanting beautiful things and never being satisfied with them leads to misery and eventually to theft — because greed has no natural stopping point.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Where in my life does insatiability operate quietly — not in gross theft, but in the subtle taking of time, attention, or resources from others without honest reciprocity?

### SUTRA 32.30

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मायामुसं वड्डुइ लोभदोसा, तत्था वि दुक्खा ण विमुच्चइ से ॥ ३२.३० ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

taṇhābhibhūyassa adattahāriṇo, rūve atittassa pariggahe ya |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The one overwhelmed by craving, who steals what is not given, who is insatiable in forms and their possession — through the fault of greed, his deception and falsehood increase; and even then he is not freed from suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*taṇhābhibhūyassa* = overwhelmed by craving; *adattahāriṇo* = who takes what is not given; *rūve atittassa* = insatiable in forms; *pariggahe* = in possession; *lobhadosā* = by the fault of greed; *māyāmusaṃ* = deception and falsehood; *vaḍḍui* = increases; *tatthā vi* = even then; *dukkhā ṇa vimucci* = is not freed from suffering.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The cascade continues. Where Sutra 29 showed insatiable form-attachment leading to theft, this sutra shows what happens afterward: deception and lying grow, and yet the suffering does not end. The form-attached thief must now lie to cover his theft, deceive to maintain appearances. The deception and falsehood that arise as protective shields around the original greed now become habitual — they grow, deepen, and become the person's characteristic mode of operation.

And yet even after all this — the craving, the insatiability, the theft, the deception, the lying — the suffering continues. The person is still not satisfied. This is the cruel irony of the entire attachment-structure: every escalation of the desperate attempt to find satisfaction through grasping generates more entanglement and more suffering. The solution is never "more" — it is the release of the craving itself.

The teaching warns that the path of attachment does not plateau; it escalates. Each stage of compromise makes the next stage easier. The practitioner who understands this is motivated to address attachment in its earliest stages, before the cascade has run far.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The person who steals to satisfy their craving then has to lie to cover it up — and still they're not happy. The trap only deepens.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have I ever noticed a small dishonesty in myself that was quietly protecting a desire I wasn't willing to examine?

### SUTRA 32.31

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Miserable before the lie, miserable after it, and miserable at the time of uttering it — in this way, the one who seizes what is not given, insatiable in forms, is miserable and helpless.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*mosassa* = of the lie, the falsehood; *pacchā* = afterward; *puratthao* = before; *pāogakāle* = at the time of its utterance; *duhī* = miserable; *duraṃte* = the hard-hearted, the desperate person; *evaṃ adattāṇi samāyayaṃto* = thus seizing what is not given; *rūve atitto* = insatiable in forms; *duhio* = miserable; *aṇisso* = helpless, friendless.

This verse completes the picture of the liar's inner state. The lie is miserable before it is spoken — in the anxiety of planning the deception, in the fear of being found out. And it is miserable afterward — in the ongoing maintenance of the fabrication, in the fear of exposure, in the corrosion of self-respect.

Genuine friendship requires honesty; the deceiver cannot have real friends because he cannot be genuinely known. The very mechanisms he used to try to gain beauty and pleasure have left him alone with his suffering.

The teaching is complete: form-craving, taken to its natural consequences, leads to the polar opposite of what it promised. It promised beauty, pleasure, admiration, and companionship. It delivers theft, lying, misery, and isolation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Before you lie, while you're lying, and after you lie — you suffer. The person who lives by deception ends up miserable and alone.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a deception I am currently maintaining — however small — that is quietly costing me inner peace and authentic connection?

### SUTRA 32.32

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तत्थोवभोगे वि किलेस दुक्खं, णिच्चत्तइ जस्स कएण दुक्खं ॥ ३२.३२ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūvāṇurattassa ṇarassa evaṃ, katto suhaṃ hojja kayāi kiṃci |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For the person infatuated with forms in this way, where can there ever be even the slightest happiness? Even in enjoying forms there is intense suffering — and through its cause (the form-attachment), suffering repeatedly arises.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūvāṇurattassa ṇarassa* = for the person infatuated with forms; *evaṃ* = in this way; *katto* = from where; *suhaṃ* = happiness; *hojja* = can be; *kayāi kiṃci* = ever, even a little; *tatthova bhoge vi* = even in the very enjoyment of forms; *kilesa dukkhaṃ* = intense suffering; *ṇiccattai* = arises repeatedly; *jassa kaeṇa* = through whose cause (the form-attachment); *dukkhaṃ* = suffering.

This verse is a summary challenge: for the person caught in the full cycle just described (Sutras 24-31), where can there ever be genuine happiness? The answer is: nowhere. Even at the peak of enjoyment — while experiencing the very beautiful form he craved — the form-attached person suffers: from insatiability (it is never quite enough), from the anxiety of loss (it will end), and from the spiritual consequences of the attachment (karma being bound).

The phrase *ṇiccattai* — "repeatedly arises" — captures the cyclic nature of the suffering. The cause (form-attachment) keeps producing the effect (suffering) in an unbroken loop. There is no natural end point at which the attachment exhausts itself and the suffering stops. The loop continues until the cause is addressed — until the attachment itself is released.

This verse drives home the essential diagnostic conclusion of the entire eye-section: form-attachment is not a harmless indulgence that occasionally creates problems. It is a systematic generator of misery with no off-switch, producing suffering at every stage of its cycle, without exception.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

For someone completely caught in form-obsession, there is no happiness anywhere — not even while enjoying beautiful things. Suffering comes with the territory.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I willing to accept that certain pleasures I currently pursue come packaged with a form of suffering I am not yet acknowledging?

### SUTRA 32.33

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एमेव रूविम्मि गओ पओसं, उवेइ दुक्खोह परंपराओ ।
पदुट्टचित्तो य चिणाइ कम्मं, जं से पुणो होइ दुहं विवागे ॥ ३२.३३ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

emeva rūvimmi gao pāosaṃ, uvei dukkhoha paraṃparāo |
paduttacitto ya ciṇāi kammaṃ, jaṃ se puṇo hoi duhaṃ vivāge || 32.33 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In exactly this way, the one who has generated aversion toward a displeasing form obtains a succession of sufferings; with defiled mind, he accumulates karma, which will again become suffering at the time of its fruition.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*emeva* = in exactly this way; *rūvimmi* = in form; *pāosaṃ gao* = having generated aversion; *dukkhoha paraṃparāo* = a succession of sufferings; *uvei* = obtains; *paduttacitto* = with a defiled, agitated mind; *kammaṃ ciṇāi* = accumulates karma; *jaṃ* = which; *se* = for that one; *puṇo* = again; *vivāge* = at the time of fruition; *duhaṃ hoi* = becomes suffering.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse mirrors the attachment-cascade with the aversion-cascade. Just as intense attachment to beautiful forms leads to a chain of sufferings (Sutras 24-32), intense aversion toward displeasing forms leads to the same outcome. The mechanism is identical: agitation, karma accumulation, future suffering at fruition.

The word *paduttacitto* — "defiled mind" — is key. A mind agitated by strong aversion is not neutral. It generates defiled mental states that bind karma just as surely as attachment does. Hatred, disgust, fury, revulsion — these are not cleaner than craving; they are simply the opposite pole of the same binding mechanism.

The verse thus completes the symmetry of the eye-section. Both extremes — intense attraction to beautiful forms and intense aversion to ugly ones — lead to the same destination: a cycle of karma, suffering, and continued bondage. The only way out is through the middle: equanimity toward both.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Hating ugly things traps you just as much as loving beautiful things — your hatred creates karma that comes back as suffering later.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What am I currently pushing away with the same intensity that I am pulling other things toward? Can I see both movements as part of the same problem?

### SUTRA 32.34

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण लिप्पइ भवमज्झे वि संतो, जलेण वा पोक्खरिणी पलासं ॥ ३२.३४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rūve viratto maṇuo visogo, eeṇa dukkhoha paraṃpareṇa |
ṇa lippai bhavamajjhe vi saṃto, jaleṇa vā pokkhariṇī palāsaṃ || 32.34 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The person detached from forms is free from grief; dwelling in existence, he is not tainted by this succession of sufferings — just as the lotus leaf dwelling in a pond is not wetted by the water.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rūve viratto* = detached from forms; *maṇuo* = person; *visogo* = free from grief, sorrowless; *eeṇa dukkhoha paraṃpareṇa* = by this succession of sufferings; *bhavamajjhe vi saṃto* = even while dwelling in existence, in the world; *ṇa lippai* = not tainted, not smeared; *jaleṇa vā* = just as by water; *pokkhariṇī palāsaṃ* = the lotus leaf of a pond.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The lotus-leaf image is the perfect closing image for the eye-section — and it will close each of the five remaining sections as well. The lotus leaf lives in water, surrounded by water, but the water does not wet it. The leaf's nature is such that water simply runs off without adhering. This is the condition of the detached practitioner living in the world.

He does not leave the world — he lives in it (*bhavamajjhe vi saṃto*). He sees all the same beautiful and ugly forms. He is present to the same sensory reality. But because he has released attachment and aversion toward forms, nothing sticks. The entire cascade of suffering traced through Sutras 24-33 simply does not manifest for him, because its first cause — form-attachment — is absent.

The word *visogo* — free from grief — is the culminating description. Not free from perception, not free from seeing, not free from existence — but free from *grief*. The grief of craving, the grief of loss, the grief of aversion, the grief of karma accumulation — all of this simply has no ground to stand on in the detached mind.

This is the promise of the entire section, and of the chapter as a whole: the path is not the destruction of the senses but the liberation of the practitioner from the reactive bondage that ordinarily accompanies sense-perception. The lotus leaf remains in water and remains dry. The liberated practitioner remains in the world and remains free.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The person free from form-attachment lives in the world but isn't touched by its suffering — like a lotus leaf that lives in water but never gets wet.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

In what moments have I already experienced this lotus quality — being present to experience without being controlled by it? Can I cultivate more of that?

## SECTION V: CONQUEST OF THE EAR (Sutras 35–47)

*The thirteen sutras of this section follow the identical structure as the eye section, with "sound" (sadda) replacing "form" (rūva) as the sense-object, and the deer lured by music (Sutra 37) replacing the moth-and-flame as the central death analogy.*

### SUTRA 32.35

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सोयस्स सदुं गहणं वयंति, तं रागहेउं तु मणुण्णमाहु ।
तं दोस हेउं अमणुण्णमाहु, समो य जो तेसु स वीयरागो ॥ ३२.३५ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

soyassa saduṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, taṃ rāgaheuṃ tu maṇuṇṇamāhu |
taṃ dosa heuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu, samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo || 32.35 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ear's grasping-object is said to be sound; a pleasing sound is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing sound is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*soyassa* = of the ear; *saduṃ* = sound; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing sound; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing sound; *dosa heuṃ* = cause of aversion; *jo tesu samo* = who is equanimous in both; *sa vīyarāgo* = the truly dispassionate one.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The ear-section begins with the same foundational framework as the eye-section. The ear grasps sound as its object; pleasing sounds generate attachment, displeasing sounds generate aversion; the one equanimous in both is liberated. This parallel structure is intentional. The same mechanism of bondage operates through each sense channel, varying only in the specific sense-object. By applying the identical analysis to each sense in turn, the teaching drives home that the problem is not in any particular sense but in the structure of reactive consciousness itself.

Sound presents its own particular challenges. Music, in particular, has a direct pathway to the emotions that bypasses rational processing. A beautiful melody can pierce the heart, arouse longing, and create attachment before the mind has registered what is happening. Harsh noise can create immediate aversion and agitation. The practitioner who understands this will be especially watchful at the level of the ear.

The detached monk neither pursues pleasing sounds nor avoids displeasing ones with emotional reactivity. He hears both with equal steadiness, allowing neither to disturb the inner equilibrium that is his most precious possession.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Beautiful sounds make you cling to them; harsh ones push you away. The free person is moved by neither.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What sounds am I currently attached to — music, voices, praise — and what sounds do I strongly resist? Can I notice the pull in both directions?

### SUTRA 32.36

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सदस्स सोयं गहणं वयंति, सोयस्स सदुं गहणं वयंति ।
रागस्स हेउं समणुण्णमाहु, दोसस्स हेउं अमणुण्णमाहु ॥ ३२.३६ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sadassa soyaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, soyassa saduṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti |
rāgassa heuṃ samaṇuṇṇamāhu, dosassa heuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu || 32.36 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Sound's grasping-organ is said to be the ear; the ear's grasping-object is said to be sound. The pleasing sound is the cause of attachment; the displeasing sound is the cause of aversion.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sadassa* = of sound; *soyaṃ* = the ear; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-organ; *soyassa* = of the ear; *saduṃ* = sound; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *samaṇuṇṇaṃ* = the pleasing; *rāgassa heuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = the displeasing; *dosassa heuṃ* = cause of aversion.

As with the eye-form relationship (Sutra 23), this verse establishes the mutual dependency between ear and sound. The ear is the grasping-organ for sound; sound is the grasping-object of the ear. This mutuality matters philosophically. The subjective and objective poles of perception are not independent. A sound that no ear will ever hear is, in experiential terms, not yet a sound in the full sense. The ear that has never heard sound has no concept of sound. The relationship creates the perceived reality of both.

For the practitioner, this insight leads to a watchfulness about the ear's own initiative in seeking sound — the way the mind-with-ear tends to reach toward pleasing sounds, to strain to hear, to extend the experience. The grasping quality is in the *listening* itself, not just in the reaction after the fact.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The ear grasps sound and sound is grasped by the ear — they belong to each other. What's pleasant creates liking; what's unpleasant creates dislike.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I notice, in a quiet moment, my ear "reaching" toward sounds — the subtle seeking quality in hearing itself?

### SUTRA 32.37

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sadesu jo giddhimuvei tivvaṃ, akāliyaṃ pāvai se viṇāsaṃ |
rāgāure hariṇa mige va muddhe, sade atitte samuvei maccuṃ || 32.37 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to sounds meets untimely destruction — just as the deer, inflamed with passion and stupefied by music, exhausted by insatiable craving for sound, meets death.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sadesu* = in sounds; *tivvaṃ giddhiṃ* = intense addiction; *uvei* = develops; *akāliyaṃ viṇāsaṃ* = untimely destruction; *pāvai* = obtains; *rāgāure* = inflamed with passion; *hariṇa mige va* = like the deer; *muddhe* = stupefied, mesmerized; *sade atitte* = insatiably immersed in sound; *maccuṃ samuvei* = meets death.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The deer-and-music analogy is the ear-section's counterpart to the eye-section's moth-and-flame. The deer in classical Indian poetry is the archetype of the creature led to its death by music. Hunters used flutes and musical instruments to draw deer out of protective cover; the deer, entranced by the music, forgot its natural vigilance and walked toward the sound — and toward the waiting hunter.

The parallel is exact: the person intensely addicted to beautiful sounds — exquisite music, flattering speech, the voice of a beloved, harmonious chanting — can be drawn into situations of danger or spiritual compromise by the lure of sound. The ear, like the deer, follows the pleasure without processing the consequences.

The word *muddhe* — stupefied, mesmerized — captures the quality of strong sound-attachment. The mind is not thinking clearly; it is under a kind of spell. Reason has been bypassed. This is why the practitioner must develop watchfulness at the level of the ear, training to hear without becoming mesmerized.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A person obsessed with beautiful sounds is like a deer hypnotized by music — it follows the melody straight to its death.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What kinds of sound do I use to escape discomfort or boredom? Am I using sound as a drug, to avoid being present with silence?

### SUTRA 32.38

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दुदंतदोसेण सएण जंतु, ण किंचि सदुं अवरज्झइ से ॥ ३२.३८ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

je yāviṃ dosaṃ samuvei tivvaṃ, taṃsikhaṇe se u uvei dukkhaṃ |
dudaṃtadoseṇa saeṇa jaṃtu, ṇa kiṃci saduṃ avarajjhai se || 32.38 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whoever develops intense aversion toward a displeasing sound immediately experiences suffering — from their own fierce aversion. In this, no sound is at fault.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tivvaṃ dosaṃ* = intense aversion; *taṃsikhaṇe* = at that very moment; *dukkhaṃ uvei* = obtains suffering; *dudaṃtadoseṇa saeṇa* = by one's own fierce aversion; *saduṃ* = sound; *avarajjhai ṇa se* = commits no fault.

**5. COMMENTARY**

Parallel to Sutra 25 for the eye: intense aversion toward displeasing sound causes immediate suffering, and the sound itself is innocent. Harsh noise, jarring music, the voice of someone one dislikes — none of these cause suffering directly. The suffering is caused entirely by the intensity of the aversion-reaction in the listener's own mind.

The particular challenge of sound-aversion is its immediacy. When a displeasing sound reaches the ear, the aversion-reaction can be instantaneous and total. The person who has not trained becomes immediately agitated; this agitation further amplifies the perceived unpleasantness of the sound, and the amplified perception generates more aversion — a rapid escalating loop. The trained practitioner, by contrast, allows sound to be heard without triggering this loop.

This is not indifference to quality; it is freedom from reactive bondage. The practitioner can discern that a sound is harsh or unharmonious without suffering over it. The distinction between the displeasing quality of the sound and the unnecessary suffering of aversion-reaction is the entire point.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When an unpleasant sound makes you miserable, your own reaction is causing the suffering — not the sound itself.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What sounds trigger an immediate, automatic, agitated reaction in me? What would it be like to hear them without the reaction?

### SUTRA 32.39

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगंतरत्ते रुइरंसि सद्दे, अतालिसे से कुणइ पओसं ।

dukkhaassa saṃpīlamuvei bāle, ṇa lippai teṇa muṇī virāgo || 32.39 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ignorant one who is deeply attached to beautiful sounds and harbors aversion toward displeasing ones accumulates a mass of suffering — but the detached monk is not tainted by this.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*egaṃtaratte* = deeply attached; *ruiraṃsi sadde* = in beautiful, pleasing sounds; *atālise* = toward unlike, displeasing sounds; *pāosaṃ kuṇai* = generates aversion; *bāle* = the ignorant; *dukkhaassa saṃpīlaṃ uvei* = obtains a mass of suffering; *muṇī virāgo teṇa ṇa lippai* = the detached monk is not tainted.

**5. COMMENTARY**

Parallel to Sutra 26, this verse presents the contrast between the ignorant person crushed between sound-craving and sound-aversion, and the detached monk who remains untainted. The structure and teaching are identical to the eye-section equivalent; only the sense-object word changes.

The ear presents a particular vulnerability here: sound is ubiquitous and penetrating in a way that other sense-stimuli are not. One can look away from a visual stimulus; one cannot so easily close the ears. Sound enters whether we invite it or not. This is why sound-equanimity — the ability to hear without being controlled by what one hears — is such a valuable and demanding discipline. The practitioner who achieves it has genuinely mastered one of the most pervasive channels of sensory bondage.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The person obsessed with beautiful sounds and repelled by ugly ones gets crushed between both. The detached monk hears all sounds and is untouched.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I encounter sound I don't like, do I notice a physical as well as mental aversion — a bracing, a tightening? What would it be like to let the sound pass through without that reaction?

Parallel to Sutra 27, this verse shows the outward ethical consequences of sound-attachment. More subtly, the sound-craving person torments others through his constant pursuit and his inner states of agitation, demand, and dissatisfaction. He creates noise, demands entertainment, is unable to be still in silence. Those around him are drawn into his restlessness and cannot find peace.

The commentary on the ear section in the source notes the specific analogy of hunters who use musical instruments to lure deer — the sound-attachment of the deer becomes the instrument of harm for many beings in the hunter's trap. The oblivious deer, following the music, leads others to harm as well.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Someone obsessed with beautiful sounds ends up causing harm to other creatures in their pursuit — and torments those around them with their constant need for more.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

How much of my daily noise consumption — music, media, conversation — is genuinely nourishing versus simply filling silence I'm afraid to be in?

### SUTRA 32.41

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

Through sound-driven possessiveness — in acquiring, protecting, and using sounds; in their loss and separation — where is happiness for such a person? Even at the time of listening there is the suffering of insatiability.

*saddhāṇuvāeṇa* = driven by sound-attachment; *pariggaheṇa* = through possessiveness; *uppāyaṇe rakkhaṇa saṇṇioge* = in acquiring, protecting, using; *vae vióge* = in loss and separation; *kahaṃ suhaṃ se* = where is happiness?; *saṃbhogakāle ya atittalābhe* = even at the time of enjoyment, the suffering of insatiability.

Parallel to Sutra 28, this verse applies the lifecycle-of-possession analysis to sound-attachment. The cycle of acquiring → protecting → enjoying → losing → being separated from pleasant sounds brings suffering at every stage. Even while listening to the most beautiful music, the sound-attached person suffers insatiability: the desire for the sound to be even more beautiful, to last longer, to continue after it ends.

The possessiveness around sound takes modern forms that are entirely recognizable: the obsessive collector of music, the person who cannot work without background sound, the one who falls into despair when their carefully curated audio environment is disrupted. In each case, the dependence on external sound as a source of happiness has created vulnerability to suffering the moment that sound is unavailable or imperfect.

True happiness does not depend on what one hears. It arises from within, independent of sense-stimulation. This is the happiness the teaching is pointing toward.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Chasing and collecting beautiful sounds brings the same misery as chasing and collecting anything else — there's no lasting satisfaction in it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I spend a period of genuine silence today — not as deprivation but as an exploration of what is present when sound is absent?

Insatiable craving for sounds leads to lying and deception — and still no relief comes.

### SUTRA 32.47

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

He hears with full clarity and without distortion, because neither craving nor aversion distorts his perception.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

## SECTION VI: CONQUEST OF THE NOSE (Sutras 48–60)

*Thirteen sutras. The sense-object is fragrance (gandha). The central death analogy (Sutra 50): the snake lured out of its hole by medicinal fragrance, caught and killed by hunters.*

### SUTRA 32.48

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

घाणस्स गंधं गहणं वयंति, ते रागहेउं तु मणुण्णमाहु ।
तं दोसहेउं अमणुण्णमाहु, समो य जो तेसु स वीयरागो ॥ ३२.४८ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ghāṇassa gaṃdhaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, te rāgaheuṃ tu maṇuṇṇamāhu |
taṃ dosaheuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu, samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo || 32.48 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The nose's grasping-object is said to be fragrance; a pleasing fragrance is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing fragrance is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*ghāṇassa* = of the nose; *gaṃdhaṃ* = fragrance; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing fragrance; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing fragrance; *dosaheuṃ* = cause of aversion; *jo tesu samo* = equanimous in both; *sa vīyarāgo* = the truly dispassionate one.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The nose-section opens with the same foundational verse as the eye and ear sections. Fragrance is the object of the nose's grasping. Pleasing fragrances — incense, flowers, perfumes, the fragrance of a beloved person — create attachment. Displeasing odors create aversion. Equanimity in both is liberation.

Of the five physical senses, smell perhaps has the most direct and unmediated pathway to emotional response. Fragrances bypass the rational mind almost entirely and connect directly to memory, desire, and emotional states. The smell of sandalwood or a familiar perfume can instantaneously evoke powerful longing. The smell of decay can create visceral revulsion. This directness makes olfactory attachment particularly subtle and difficult to notice — it often operates before the practitioner has registered that a reaction has occurred.

The nose-discipline therefore requires a particular kind of awareness: the ability to notice the fragrance-reaction at its very inception, before it has already generated craving or aversion.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Pleasant smells make you want more; bad smells make you pull away. The free person is undisturbed by either.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What fragrance, when encountered, instantly creates a powerful emotional state in me — longing, pleasure, aversion? Can I trace what that reaction is made of?

### SUTRA 32.49

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रागस्स हेउं समणुण्णमाहु, दोसस्स हेउं अमणुण्णमाहु ॥ ३२.४९ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

gaṃdhassa ghāṇaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, ghāṇassa gaṃdhaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Fragrance's grasping-organ is said to be the nose; the nose's grasping-object is said to be fragrance. The pleasing fragrance is the cause of attachment; the displeasing fragrance is the cause of aversion.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*gaṃdhassa* = of fragrance; *ghāṇaṃ* = the nose; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-organ; *ghāṇassa* = of the nose; *gaṃdhaṃ* = fragrance; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *samaṇuṇṇaṃ rāgassa heuṃ* = pleasing is cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ dosassa heuṃ* = displeasing is cause of aversion.

**5. COMMENTARY**

Parallel to Sutras 23 and 36: the mutual dependency between nose and fragrance. The nose is the grasping-organ of fragrance; fragrance is what the nose reaches toward. This bidirectional relationship means that the nose actively seeks fragrance — it does not merely receive passively. The person who has developed attachment to fragrances will literally seek them out, sniffing, inhaling, lingering in scented spaces — the nose is reaching, not merely receiving.

This active reaching quality of olfactory grasping is important to notice. It reveals that sense-attachment involves not just reaction but intention — a habitual turning of the sense-organ toward its preferred objects. The training of non-attachment therefore includes the gradual cessation of this habitual seeking.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The nose reaches toward fragrance and fragrance is what the nose grasps — they belong together. Pleasant smells create liking; unpleasant ones create dislike.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I notice, at some point today, the active reaching quality of my nose — the way it seeks certain fragrances, not just passively receives them?

### SUTRA 32.50

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गंधेसु जो गिद्धिमुवेइ तिव्वं, अकालियं पावइ से विणासं ।
रागाउरे ओसहिगंधगिद्धे, सप्पे बिलाओ विव णिक्खमंते ॥ ३२.५० ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

gaṃdhesu jo giddhimuvei tivvaṃ, akāliyaṃ pāvai se viṇāsaṃ |
rāgāure osahigaṃdhagiddhe, sappe bilāo viva ṇikkhmaṃte || 32.50 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to fragrances meets untimely destruction — just as the snake, inflamed with passion and deeply attached to the fragrance of medicinal herbs, emerges from its hole and is killed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*gaṃdhesu tivvaṃ giddhiṃ uvei* = develops intense addiction to fragrances; *akāliyaṃ viṇāsaṃ pāvai* = meets untimely destruction; *rāgāure* = inflamed with passion; *osahi-gaṃdha-giddhe* = deeply attached to the fragrance of medicinal herbs; *sappe* = the snake; *bilāo* = from its hole; *viva ṇikkhmaṃte* = like one emerging.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The snake-and-fragrance analogy is this section's central death image. Certain medicinal herbs (*osadhi*), particularly "nagadamani" (a plant used to charm and capture snakes), have a fragrance that proves irresistible to snakes. When hunters wanted to catch or kill a snake, they would bring such herbs near the snake's burrow. The snake, drawn by the irresistible fragrance, would emerge from the safety of its hole — and the waiting hunter would kill it. The fragrance itself caused no harm; the snake's intense attachment to that fragrance led it directly to its death.

The human parallel is precise. The person intensely attached to fragrances — perfumes, incense, scented places, the fragrance of a particular person or environment — can be drawn out of the "safety" of inner restraint and spiritual discipline by the lure of a pleasing scent. One whiff of a beloved fragrance, and years of practice can be disrupted. The attachment, not the fragrance itself, is the weapon in the hand of karma.

This analogy also captures something subtle about the quality of olfactory attachment: it has an "emerging from hiding" quality. The snake was safe in its burrow, protected from the hunter, until the fragrance drew it out into the open. In the same way, a practitioner who has achieved a degree of inner stability may find that a specific fragrance — tied to old memories or desires — can draw the hidden desire out of its quiet state into active craving.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Intense attachment to beautiful fragrances destroys you — like the snake that comes out of its safe hole because of a scent and gets killed.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is there a fragrance — physical or metaphorical — that has the power to draw me out of my inner steadiness, to make me emerge from protective quiet into reactive craving?

## SECTION VII: CONQUEST OF THE TONGUE (Sutras 61–73)

*Thirteen sutras. The sense-object is taste (rasa). The central death analogy (Sutra 63): the fish caught on a hook baited with meat, killed by the iron hook in its insatiable craving for the taste of flesh.*

### SUTRA 32.61

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The tongue's grasping-object is said to be taste; a pleasing taste is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing taste is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*jibbhāe* = of the tongue; *rasaṃ* = taste; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing taste; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing taste; *dosaheuṃ* = cause of aversion; *samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo* = equanimous in both is the truly dispassionate one.

The tongue-section opens with the foundational verse, now applied to taste. Of all the sense channels addressed in this chapter, the tongue holds a particularly central place — the chapter's earlier instruction on diet (Sutras 10-11) already established that sensory restraint begins with food. The Kimpaka fruit analogy of Sutra 20 prepared the mind for the taste-section: what is sweet in the mouth is lethal in its consequences.

The tongue is the sense-organ through which we most directly and repeatedly engage with the world — multiple times every day, from earliest childhood. The habitual pleasures of taste are among the deepest and most ancient of all sensory attachments. This is why Jain tradition places food-discipline at the very center of monastic practice: it is not possible to achieve true sense-restraint while remaining unexamined in one's relationship to taste.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 32.63

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāgāure baḍisa vibhiṇṇakāe macche jahā āmisobhogigiddhe || 32.63 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to tastes meets untimely destruction — just as the fish, inflamed with passion and intensely craving the taste of meat, is pierced through the body by the iron hook and killed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*rasesu tivvaṃ giddhiṃ uvei* = develops intense addiction to tastes; *akāliyaṃ viṇāsaṃ pāvai* = meets untimely destruction; *rāgāure* = inflamed with passion; *āmisabhogi-giddhe* = deeply attached to the taste of meat; *macche* = the fish; *baḍisa* = iron hook; *vibhiṇṇakāe* = pierced through the body.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The fish-and-hook analogy is this section's central death image. The fisherman places meat on an iron hook and lowers it into the water. The fish, intensely craving the taste of meat, rushes toward the bait without caution, bites down — and the hook drives through its body. The very thing it craved — the taste — was the instrument of its death.

The human parallel is unmistakable. The person intensely addicted to certain tastes — rich foods, meat, sweet indulgences — pursues those tastes with the same headlong recklessness as the fish. The "bait" is real: the food does taste as it appears to taste. But the "hook" — the karma, the deepened craving, the spiritual damage, the bodily harm — is hidden within the bait, inseparable from it. The fish does not see the hook; the taste-addicted person does not see the hook hidden in their pleasure.

The word *vibhiṇṇakāe* — "pierced through the body" — suggests something violent and total. The fish is not just caught; it is penetrated, destroyed from within by what it grasped. This is the quality of taste-addiction taken to its extreme: it destroys from within.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A person obsessed with delicious food is like a fish that bites a hook baited with meat — the very pleasure destroys it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The person who eats a meal they find unpleasant and suffers greatly from it — while someone next to them eats exactly the same meal with equanimity — demonstrates exactly what this verse teaches. The same food; two entirely different inner states. The difference is not in the food.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When a bad taste makes you miserable, your own violent reaction is causing the pain — not the taste itself.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

## SECTION VIII: CONQUEST OF THE BODY (Sutras 74–86)

*Thirteen sutras. The sense-object is touch (phāsa). The central death analogy (Sutra 76): the buffalo, craving the cool touch of forest pool water, seized by a crocodile in the depths.*

### SUTRA 32.74

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कायस्स फासं गहणं वयंति, तं रागहेउं तु मणुण्णमाहु ।
तं दोसहेउं अमणुण्णमाहु, समो य जो तेसु स वीयरागो ॥ ३२.७४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

taṃ dosaheuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu, samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo || 32.74 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The body's grasping-object is said to be touch; a pleasing touch is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing touch is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kāyassa* = of the body; *phāsaṃ* = touch; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing touch; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing touch; *dosaheuṃ* = cause of aversion; *samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo* = equanimous in both is the truly dispassionate one.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The touch-section opens with the foundational verse applied to tactile sensation. Touch is the most immediate and enveloping of the physical senses — the entire skin surface is a sense-organ, and touch encompasses temperature, pressure, texture, and pain. It is also the sense most directly linked to the physical pleasures and discomforts of existence: warmth and cold, softness and roughness, pleasure and pain.

Touch-attachment includes the craving for physical comfort: soft beds, pleasant temperatures, gentle breezes, the warmth of fire. These are not grand indulgences; they are the ordinary, unexamined pleasures of physical existence. The Jain tradition's attention to them reflects the understanding that it is precisely these small, habitual comfort-cravings that keep the practitioner's consciousness anchored to the body rather than established in the soul's nature.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Pleasant sensations make you cling; painful ones make you recoil. The free person is equanimous toward both.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What physical sensation — temperature, texture, a particular kind of comfort — am I most unwilling to be without? How does that dependence shape my choices?

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*phāsassa* = of touch; *kāyaṃ* = the body; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-organ; *kāyassa* = of the body; *phāsaṃ* = touch; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *samaṇuṇṇaṃ rāgassa heuṃ*; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ dosassa heuṃ*.

### SUTRA 32.76

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

रागाउरे सीयजलावसण्णे, गाहग्गहीए महिसे व रण्णे ॥ ३२.७६ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

rāgāure sīyajālāvasaṇṇe, gāhaggahīe mahise va raṇṇe || 32.76 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to touch meets untimely destruction — just as the buffalo, craving the cool touch of forest pool water, is seized by a crocodile and destroyed.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*phāsesu tivvaṃ giddhiṃ uvei* = develops intense addiction to touch; *akāliyaṃ viṇāsaṃ pāvai* = meets untimely destruction; *rāgāure* = inflamed with passion; *sīyajāla-vasaṇṇe* = craving the cool touch of forest pool water, deeply attached to the coolness of cool water; *mahise* = the buffalo; *raṇṇe* = in the forest; *gāhag-gahīe* = seized by a crocodile.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The buffalo-and-crocodile analogy is vivid and memorable. The large forest buffalo, in the heat of the day, craves the cool touch of water. Driven by this craving, it enters the pool without caution. The crocodile waiting in the depths seizes it — and the buffalo, which has no crocodile in the city's nearby water sources, is powerless. Its craving for cool touch has led it to its death.

The human parallel: the craving for pleasant physical sensations — warmth in winter, coolness in summer, soft beds, pleasant temperatures — leads the practitioner into environments and situations that compromise his practice. The heat of the day is real; the coolness of the water is real; but the crocodile hidden in the depths is also real. The pleasant sensation was genuine, but the danger associated with seeking it was not visible.

The specific commentary notes that the forest pond is dangerous precisely because it lacks the protective structures of a city pond. The jungle pool is unguarded, unknown, potentially harboring what cannot be seen from the surface. This matches the spiritual situation: the pleasant sensation is on the surface; what lies beneath — the karma, the deepened craving, the spiritual compromise — is hidden.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Intense addiction to physical comfort is like a buffalo that enters a dangerous pool for the coolness of the water and gets grabbed by a crocodile.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What physical comfort am I entering "dangerous pools" for — places, situations, or relationships where I go seeking a pleasant sensation but where hidden dangers await?

## SECTION IX: CONQUEST OF THE MIND (Sutras 87–99)

*Thirteen sutras. The sense-object is mental states/objects (bhāva). The central death analogy (Sutra 89): the elephant maddened by desire for female elephants, seized and destroyed by hunters' traps. This section is the culminating conquest — the mind governs all five physical senses, and its conquest completes the liberation.*

---

### SUTRA 32.87

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मणस्स भावं गहणं वयंति, तं रागहेउं तु मणुण्णमाहु ।
तं दोसहेउं अमणुण्णमाहु, समो य जो तेसु स वीयरागो ॥ ३२.८७ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

maṇassa bhāvaṃ gahaṇaṃ vayaṃti, taṃ rāgaheuṃ tu maṇuṇṇamāhu |
taṃ dosaheuṃ amaṇuṇṇamāhu, samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo || 32.87 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The mind's grasping-object is said to be mental states; a pleasing mental state is called the cause of attachment; a displeasing mental state is called the cause of aversion; and one who maintains equanimity toward both is the truly dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*maṇassa* = of the mind; *bhāvaṃ* = mental states, objects of the mind's grasping; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *maṇuṇṇaṃ* = pleasing mental states; *rāgaheuṃ* = cause of attachment; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ* = displeasing mental states; *dosaheuṃ* = cause of aversion; *samo ya jo tesu sa vīyarāgo* = equanimous in both is the truly dispassionate one.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The mind-section is the culminating section of the entire sense-conquest program. In Jain philosophy, the mind is the sixth sense — it grasps mental objects (thoughts, imaginations, memories, fantasies) just as the physical senses grasp their respective objects. And it is also the governing instrument through which all five physical senses operate: without the mind's engagement, no physical sensation becomes a moment of craving or aversion. The mind is therefore both the last sense to be addressed and the most fundamental.

The "mental states" (*bhāva*) that the mind grasps include sense-fantasies, memories of pleasure, anticipations of future enjoyment, imagined scenarios of desire fulfillment, and their opposites — feared scenarios, memories of pain, anticipations of loss. The mind that is not trained grasps all of these with the same reactive quality as the physical senses grasp their objects: pulling toward the pleasant, pushing away from the unpleasant.

The person who has conquered all five physical sense channels but has not addressed the mind finds that the mind recreates the same sensory desires internally — replaying the most beautiful sounds, imagining the most pleasing tastes, constructing fantasies of pleasant touch. The conquest of the mind is therefore the final and definitive conquest.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The mind grasps mental states just like the eye grasps forms — pleasant thoughts create wanting, unpleasant ones create resistance. Freedom from both is liberation.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Right now, what mental state is the mind grasping — a pleasant fantasy, a worried anticipation, a cherished memory? Can I notice the grasping quality itself?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*bhāvassa* = of mental states; *maṇaṃ* = the mind; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-organ; *maṇassa* = of the mind; *bhāvaṃ* = mental states; *gahaṇaṃ* = grasping-object; *samaṇuṇṇaṃ rāgassa heuṃ*; *amaṇuṇṇaṃ dosassa heuṃ*.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The mind and mental states are bound in mutual dependency just as each physical sense is bound to its object. The mind grasps mental states as its objects; mental states exist as experienced only in relation to the mind that grasps them. This bidirectional relationship is particularly intimate in the mind-section: the subject and object here are not as clearly separated as eye and form or nose and fragrance. The mind grasping a mental object is, in a sense, the mind grasping itself — creating, holding, and responding to its own constructions.

This is why mind-training is the most subtle and most comprehensive of the sense-disciplines. The practitioner who has learned to see without grasping, to hear without grasping, can apply the same principle to the mind: to think without grasping, to allow thoughts to arise and pass without the added quality of emotional clutching or rejection.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The mind reaches toward mental states and mental states are what the mind grasps. Pleasant thoughts create wanting; unpleasant ones create resistance.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I observe a thought arising in this moment without immediately reacting to it as "good" or "bad," "pleasant" or "unpleasant"? Can I see it simply as a thought?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One who becomes intensely addicted to mental states (desire-fantasies) meets untimely destruction — just as the elephant, inflamed with passion and obsessed with desire for female elephants, is overwhelmed and destroyed by hunters on the path.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

**5. COMMENTARY**

The elephant analogy is the culminating death image of the entire sense-section program. The elephant in must — maddened by desire for female elephants — loses all judgment and reason. It abandons its natural caution, charges blindly, and in its frenzy falls into hunters' pits or is seized by ropes and destroyed. The very faculty of reasoning that makes the elephant a powerful and intelligent creature is temporarily obliterated by the force of desire.

This is the condition of the person intensely addicted to desire-fantasies — to the mental replaying and projecting of sensory pleasures. The mind, which is capable of profound wisdom and clear discernment, becomes completely occupied with its own fantasies of pleasure. Reason is suspended. Caution is abandoned. The person walks into situations, relationships, and compromises that their own clearer intelligence would warn them away from — because the mind is not functioning as the clarity-instrument but as the desire-generator.

The elephant analogy is particularly apt because the elephant is the most powerful and majestic land animal in the Indian tradition. Even it — with all its strength and intelligence — is destroyed by the force of sexual desire. The lesson is clear: no amount of ordinary intelligence, social standing, or physical power protects against the destruction that desire-fantasies can cause. Only the training of the mind itself provides protection.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

### SUTRA 32.99

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

भावे विरत्तो मणुओ विसोगो, एएण दुक्खोह परंपरेण ।

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The person detached from mental states is free from grief; dwelling in existence, he is not tainted by this succession of sufferings — just as the lotus leaf dwelling in a pond is not wetted by the water.

Parallel to Sutras 34, 47, 60, 73, 86, with *bhāve* replacing earlier sense-object words.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The lotus-leaf closes the mind-section and, with it, the entire six-section sense-conquest program. ### SUTRA 32.100

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एविंदियत्था य मणस्स अत्था, दुक्खस्स हेउं मणुयस्स रागिणो ।
ते चेव थोवं पि कयाइ दुक्खं, ण वीयरागस्स करेंति किंचि ॥ ३२.१०० ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eviṃdiyatthā ya maṇassa atthā, dukkhaassa heuṃ maṇuyassa rāgiṇo |
te ceva thovaṃ pi kayāi dukkhaṃ, ṇa vīyarāgassa karaṃti kiṃci || 32.100 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

These — the objects of the five senses and the objects of the mind — are the causes of suffering for the passionate person; yet those very same objects never cause even the slightest suffering for the dispassionate one.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*eviṃdiyatthā* = the objects of the five senses (form, sound, fragrance, taste, touch); *maṇassa atthā* = and the objects of the mind (mental states); *rāgiṇo maṇuyassa* = for the passionate person; *dukkhaassa heuṃ* = causes of suffering; *te ceva* = those very same things; *vīyarāgassa* = for the dispassionate one; *thovaṃ pi* = even a little; *kayāi kiṃci dukkhaṃ* = suffering at any time; *ṇa karaṃti* = do not cause.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse is the definitive summary of the entire six-section sense-conquest program. The objects — forms, sounds, fragrances, tastes, touches, and mental states — are neutral in themselves. They are not the problem. The same form that creates agony for a passionate person creates nothing for the dispassionate one. The same sound, the same fragrance, the same taste, the same touch, the same mental state — different responses, entirely different consequences.

This conclusion is both logically precise and profoundly liberating. It means that liberation does not require the abolition of sensory experience. The world of forms, sounds, and fragrances remains fully present to the liberated one. He sees, he hears, he smells, he tastes, he touches — but without the reactive charge that generates karma and suffering. His experience is richer, not poorer: perception without distortion, sensation without craving or aversion.

The passionate person suffers from the same world that the dispassionate one moves through freely. They inhabit the same universe; only their inner orientation differs. And that inner orientation is what the entire program of practice is designed to transform.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The same world that causes endless suffering for someone full of desire causes absolutely no suffering for someone who is truly free of desire.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If the world is not the problem, and I am suffering — what does that tell me about where the work needs to happen?

### SUTRA 32.101

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण कामभोगा समयं उर्वेंति, ण याविं भोगा विगइं उर्वेंति ।
जे तप्पओसी य परिग्गही य, सो तेसु मोहा विगइं उवेइ ॥ ३२.१०१ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇa kāmabhogā samaṃ ur̃veṃti, ṇa yāviṃ bhogā vigaiṃ ur̃veṃti |
je tappaoosī ya pariggahī ya, so tesu mohā vigaiṃ uvei || 32.101 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Sensory pleasures do not produce equanimity of themselves, nor do they produce detachment of themselves; it is the person who is averse to them and possessive of them who, through his delusion toward them, arrives at their distorted effect.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kāmabhogā* = sensory pleasures — the five sense objects; *samaṃ* = equanimity; *ṇa ur̃veṃti* = do not produce by themselves; *bhogā* = sense-pleasures; *vigaiṃ* = distorted state, derangement; *ṇa ur̃veṃti* = do not produce by themselves; *tappaoosī* = averse to them, hating them; *pariggahī* = possessive of them; *mohaṃ* = through delusion; *tesu vigaiṃ uvei* = arrives at the distorted state toward them.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse makes a philosophically precise distinction. Sense-pleasures do not by themselves produce equanimity (which would make them spiritually useful), nor do they by themselves produce the "distorted state" (*vigai*) — the corruption of the passions. What produces the distorted state is the person's own deluded relationship to them: simultaneously desiring them (*tappaoosī* is slightly ambiguous but the commentary reads it as "one who harbors passion toward them") and possessing them (*pariggahī*). It is the combination of craving and possessiveness, both rooted in delusion, that generates the corruption.

The teaching implies: if the deluded attachment and possessiveness are removed, the same sense-objects that formerly generated corruption become, in effect, neutral. The dispassionate person who encounters them simply perceives and passes on — no distortion arises.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Pleasures don't automatically cause problems — it's the deluded clinging and possessiveness that creates corruption. The same things are harmless for a free person.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I bring to mind a sense-pleasure I currently pursue — and honestly examine whether it is the pleasure itself I seek, or the sense of possession and control that comes with it?

### SUTRA 32.102

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कोहं च माणं च तहेव मायं, लोभं दुगुंछं अरइं रइं च ।
हासं भयं सोग पुमित्थिवेयं, णपुंसवेयं विविहे य भावे ॥ ३२.१०२ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kohaṃ ca māṇaṃ ca taheva māyaṃ, lobhaṃ diguṃcaṃ araiṃ raiṃ ca |
hāsaṃ bhayaṃ soga pumitthiveyaṃ, ṇapuṃsaveyaṃ vividhe ya bhāve || 32.102 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Anger, pride, deception, greed, disgust, displeasure, pleasure, laughter, fear, grief, the male-feeling-body, the female-feeling-body, the hermaphrodite-feeling-body — and many other such states...

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kohaṃ* = anger; *māṇaṃ* = pride; *māyaṃ* = deception; *lobhaṃ* = greed; *diguṃcaṃ* = disgust, loathing; *araiṃ* = displeasure, boredom; *raiṃ* = pleasure, delight; *hāsaṃ* = laughter; *bhayaṃ* = fear; *sogaṃ* = grief; *pumitthiveyaṃ* = male-gender-feeling; *ṇapuṃsaveyaṃ* = hermaphrodite-gender-feeling; *vividhe bhāve* = various states.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse begins a two-sutra listing (completed in Sutra 103) of the fourteen passions and pseudo-passions (*kashāya* and *no-kashāya*) that are stirred up by sense-attachment. This is the specific mechanism by which sense-attachment causes "distortion" (as described in Sutra 101): the passions that are activated.

The first four — anger, pride, deception, greed — are the four primary passions (*kashāya*) in Jain psychology. These are the main binding agents of karma. The subsequent states — disgust, displeasure, pleasure, laughter, fear, grief, and the gender-feeling-bodies — are the nine subsidiary passions (*no-kashāya* or *upakashāya*). Together, these fourteen constitute the full range of the soul's deluded inner life.

What is remarkable about this list is its comprehensiveness: it includes states we might think of as "positive" (pleasure, laughter) alongside clearly negative states (anger, fear, grief). The teaching is that all of these, when they arise from sense-attachment and delusion, are forms of bondage. Even laughter arising from the grasping quality is a form of passion-activation. Even pleasure, when it arises from craving, binds karma.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When you are caught in sense-attachment, these fourteen states arise in you — anger, pride, deception, greed, disgust, boredom, delight, laughter, fear, grief, and the various gender-feelings...

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Looking at this list honestly — which of these fourteen states am I most frequently in? Which ones do I not even recognize as forms of bondage?

### SUTRA 32.103

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

आवज्जइ एवमणेगरूवे, एवं विहे कामगुणेसु सत्तो ।
अण्णे य एयप्पभवे विससे, काउण्णदीणे हिरिमे वइस्से ॥ ३२.१०३ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

āvajjai evamṇegarūve, evaṃ vihe kāmaguṇesu satto |
aṇṇe ya eyappabhave visasse, kāruṇṇadīṇe hirime vaisse || 32.103 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

...the being attached to sensory pleasures in this way arrives at these many forms of corruption; and along with other sufferings born from the same source, becomes an object of compassion — pitiable, shameful, and unlovable.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kāmaguṇesu satto* = the being attached to sensory pleasures; *evaṃ vihe* = in this way; *aṇegarūve* = in many forms; *āvajjai* = arrives at; *aṇṇe ya eyappabhave visasse* = and other sufferings born from these same (passions); *kāruṇṇadīṇe* = an object of compassion, pitiable, extremely helpless; *hirime* = shameful, one who brings about shame; *vaisse* = unlovable, one who is disliked.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse completes the enumeration begun in Sutra 102. The person attached to sense-pleasures does not merely suffer internally — they become the very embodiment of the confused, deluded, passion-driven life. The word *kāruṇṇadīṇe* — "object of compassion" — is striking. From the perspective of one who has understood the path, the passion-driven person is not hateful or contemptible; he is pitiable. He is suffering, confused, trapped in mechanisms he cannot see clearly. He deserves compassion, not condemnation.

Yet from the world's perspective, such a person is also *hirime* — shameful — because his behavior arising from passion causes harm and generates disgust in others. And *vaisse* — unlovable — because the agitation, manipulation, and unpredictability of the passion-driven person makes genuine loving connection difficult.

Together, these three descriptions — pitiable, shameful, unlovable — paint the complete portrait of the destination that unaddressed sense-attachment leads to. The teaching is compassionate but clear: this is what awaits if the path is not taken.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The person caught in sense-attachment ends up confused, pitiful, ashamed, and cut off from real love — these passions are the mechanism that does it.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Have I ever seen someone whose life had been consumed by a particular passion — and felt both compassion for their suffering and sadness at what it had made of them? Can I see traces of that same process in myself?

### SUTRA 32.104

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कप्पं ण इच्छिज्ज सहायिलिच्छु, पच्छाणुतावे ण तवप्पभावं ।
एवं वियारे अमियप्पयारे, आवज्जइ इंदिय चोरवस्से ॥ ३२.१०४ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

kappaṃ ṇa icchijja sahāyalichhu, pacchāṇutāve ṇa tavappabhāvaṃ |
evaṃ viyāre amiyappayāre, āvajjai iṃdiya choravasse || 32.104 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The disciplined monk should not desire a serving companion, should not fall into remorse after disciplinary undertakings, and should not desire to manifest the powers of austerity — for in this way, through unlimited thoughts and desires, one falls under the enslavement of the senses as thieves.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*kappaṃ* = the disciplined monk, equivalent to a shishya; *sahāyalichhu* = desiring a servant-companion; *ṇa icchijja* = should not desire; *pacchāṇutāve* = in remorse afterward; *tavappabhāvaṃ* = the powers arising from austerity; *ṇa* = should not desire; *evaṃ viyāre amiyappayāre* = through limitless, countless desires and thoughts; *iṃdiya choravasse* = under the enslavement of the senses as thieves; *āvajjai* = one falls.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse turns from diagnosis to a specific practical instruction for the practitioner who has accepted the ascetic life. Three particular cravings are warned against: (1) the desire for a personal serving companion, (2) the self-indulgent remorse after accepting difficult vows, and (3) the desire to display or use the powers gained through austerity.

The first — desiring a personal companion — reflects the importance of self-reliance in spiritual practice. Dependence on others for even practical needs creates emotional attachments and vulnerabilities. The practitioner who needs a companion is not yet fully self-reliant.

The second — remorse after vow-acceptance — reveals a subtle spiritual problem: accepting a vow and then spending energy lamenting its difficulty is a way of maintaining attachment to the comfortable life one has formally renounced. The remorse-prone practitioner has not truly let go.

The third — desire for austerity-powers — is perhaps the most subtle. Some practitioners develop psychic or miraculous powers through deep austerity and begin to desire these powers, to show them off, or to use them for recognition. This desire is itself a form of craving — now craving for spiritual status rather than sensory pleasure, but craving nonetheless, and just as binding.

The verse's closing image is striking: *iṃdiya chora* — "senses as thieves." The senses are thieves because they steal the practitioner's inner steadiness and clarity, capturing the mind's attention and leading it away from its true purpose, leaving the practitioner spiritually impoverished even while the outer life appears to continue.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

A monk should not crave a helper, should not regret their vows, and should not desire to display spiritual powers — because through all these desires, the senses steal away inner freedom.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What subtle craving — not for obvious sensory pleasure but for something "spiritual" like recognition, power, or admiration — might currently be acting as a thief of my inner peace?

### SUTRA 32.105

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तओ से जायंति पओयणाइं, णिमज्जिउं मोहमहण्णवम्मि ।
सुहेसिणो दुक्खविणोयणट्टा, तप्पच्चयं उज्जमए य रागी ॥ ३२.१०५ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tao se jāyaṃti paoyaṇāiṃ, ṇimajjiuṃ mohamahaṇṇavimmi |
suheṇo dukkhaviṇoyaṇaṭṭā, tappaccayaṃ ujjamae ya rāgī || 32.105 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

From these desires and thoughts, many purposes arise — for drowning in the great ocean of delusion; and the passionate person who seeks happiness and the elimination of suffering strives earnestly through that very attachment toward sense-objects.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tao* = from these (desires and thoughts); *se paoyaṇāiṃ jāyaṃti* = many purposes, reasons arise; *ṇimajjiuṃ* = for drowning; *mohamahaṇṇavimmi* = in the great ocean of delusion; *suheṇo* = for one seeking happiness; *dukkhaviṇoyaṇaṭṭā* = for the purpose of eliminating suffering; *rāgī* = the passionate person; *tappaccayaṃ* = through that very attachment; *ujjamae* = strives earnestly.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse reveals the tragic irony at the heart of desire-driven existence: the passionate person is genuinely trying to find happiness and eliminate suffering. He is not evil; he is simply wrong about the method. He strives earnestly — using all his energy and intelligence — but in exactly the wrong direction. His earnest effort through sense-attachment drowns him in the very ocean of delusion he was hoping to escape.

This is deeply compassionate teaching. The passionate person is not condemned; he is understood. He wants happiness — the same happiness that the liberated practitioner enjoys. He wants freedom from suffering — the same freedom that liberation brings. His error is entirely in the means: he believes that sense-gratification can provide what only the release of craving can provide.

The image of "drowning in the great ocean of delusion" captures the quality of this mistaken effort perfectly. The harder the drowning person struggles, the more exhausted they become and the deeper they sink. The correct response to drowning is not more frantic effort; it is stillness and trust in the water's capacity to support. In the same way, the correct response to suffering is not more earnest striving in the direction of desire-gratification; it is the cessation of grasping itself.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The passionate person is genuinely trying to be happy and escape suffering — they're just trying in exactly the wrong way, which drowns them deeper in confusion.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there ways I am working very hard to be happy that are actually making me less happy? What would it look like to stop striving and simply release?

### SUTRA 32.106

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

विरज्जमाणस्स य इंदियत्था, सद्दाइया तावइयप्पगारा ।
ण तस्स सव्वे वि मणुण्णयं वा, णिच्चत्तयंति अमणुण्णयं वा ॥ ३२.१०६ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

virajjamāṇassa ya iṃdiyatthā, saddāiyā tāvaiyappagārā |
ṇa tassa savve vi maṇuṇṇayaṃ vā, ṇiccattayaṃti amaṇuṇṇayaṃ vā || 32.106 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For the one who is becoming free of attachment, the sense-objects — sounds and the others of that many-formed kind — cannot produce either pleasantness or unpleasantness in him.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*virajjamāṇassa* = for the one becoming detached, progressively free of passion; *iṃdiyatthā* = sense-objects; *saddāiyā* = sounds and the others; *tāvaiyappagārā* = of that many-formed kind (all the various types); *tassa* = toward him; *savve vi* = all; *maṇuṇṇayaṃ* = pleasantness; *amaṇuṇṇayaṃ* = unpleasantness; *ṇiccattayaṃti* = cannot produce; *ṇa* = not.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse describes the phenomenological reality of progressive liberation. The practitioner who is genuinely advancing in detachment — not as a theory but as a living inner reality — begins to experience the sense-objects differently. They no longer automatically generate the reactive charge of "pleasant" or "unpleasant." The sounds remain sounds, but they do not trigger craving or aversion. The forms remain forms, but they do not pull the practitioner toward them or push him away.

This is not numbness or suppression; it is the natural condition of a mind that has begun to release the reactive conditioning. Just as a person who has recovered from a particular addiction no longer feels the compulsive pull toward the addictive substance, the practitioner who has genuinely progressed in detachment no longer experiences the objects of the senses as inherently "pleasant" or "unpleasant" in the charged, reactive sense.

The word *virajjamāṇassa* — "the one who is becoming detached" — suggests an ongoing process, not a sudden switch. This is the path as it is actually lived: a gradual, progressive loosening of the reactive charge around sense-objects, from the inside, through steady practice.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

For someone genuinely progressing in detachment, sense-objects stop generating the automatic "like" and "dislike" reactions — the world simply is what it is.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Can I recall a moment — even a brief one — when a sense-object that normally triggers craving simply registered neutrally? What was the quality of that moment?

### SUTRA 32.107

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एवं स संकप्प विकप्पणासु, संजायइ समयमुवट्टियस्स ।
अत्थे य संकप्पयओ तओ से, पहीयए कामगुणेसु तण्हा ॥ ३२.१०७ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

evaṃ sa saṃkappa vikappāṇāsu, saṃjāyai samayamuvattiyassa |
atthe ya saṃkappayao tao se, pahīyae kāmaguṇesu taṇhā || 32.107 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In this way, in the dissolution of his constructive and deconstructive thoughts, equanimity arises for the one established in right perception; and then, in his clear reflection on things, his craving for sensory pleasures is extinguished.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sa* = this one; *saṃkappa vikappāṇāsu* = in the dissolution of positive-desire thoughts and negative-aversion thoughts; *samayamuvattiyassa* = for the one established in equanimity; *saṃjāyai* = arises; *atthe saṃkappayao* = in clear thinking about the nature of things; *tao se* = from that; *kāmaguṇesu taṇhā* = craving for sensory pleasures; *pahīyae* = is extinguished.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse describes the mechanics of liberation's final approach. When both constructive (desire-based) thinking and deconstructive (aversion-based) thinking dissolve, equanimity (*samaya*) arises naturally. This equanimity is not created; it is revealed — it was always present beneath the noise of constructive and deconstructive mental activity. When that noise ceases, equanimity spontaneously manifests.

From this equanimity, clear thinking about the nature of things (*atthe saṃkappayao*) becomes possible — not the distorted thinking of desire and aversion, but the clear, direct perception of what actually is. And from that clear perception, the craving for sensory pleasures simply extinguishes. Not suppressed, not fought against, but naturally extinguished — like a fire that has burned through all its fuel and simply goes out.

This is the description of the path's culminating movement: equanimity → clear seeing → natural extinguishing of craving. Each stage follows from the previous with the inevitability of a natural process. No heroic effort is required at this stage; only the continued maintenance of equanimity and honest seeing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

When both craving and aversion thoughts dissolve, true equanimity appears; and from that equanimity, clear seeing naturally extinguishes the craving for pleasures — like a fire burning out.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it be like to be in perfect equanimity right now — neither reaching for anything pleasant nor pushing away anything unpleasant? Can I rest there, even for one breath?

### SUTRA 32.108

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

स वीयरागो कयसव्विकिच्चो, खवेइ णाणावरणं खणेणं ।
तहेव जं दंसणमावरेइ, जं चंतरायं पकरेइ कम्मं ॥ ३२.१०८ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sa vīyarāgo kayasavvikicco, khavei ṇāṇāvaraṇaṃ khaṇeṇaṃ |
taheva jaṃ daṃsaṇamāvare, jaṃ caṃtarāyaṃ pakarei kammaṃ || 32.108 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That dispassionate one who has completed all duties destroys knowledge-obscuring karma in a single moment, and in the same way destroys the karma that obscures right perception, and the karma that creates obstacles.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*sa vīyarāgo* = that dispassionate one; *kayasavvikicco* = who has completed all duties (the kṛtakṛtya — the one for whom there is nothing further to do); *khaṇeṇaṃ* = in a single moment; *ṇāṇāvaraṇaṃ* = knowledge-obscuring karma; *khavei* = destroys; *taheva* = in the same way; *jaṃ daṃsaṇamāvare* = that which obscures right perception (perception-obscuring karma); *jaṃ caṃtarāyaṃ pakarei kammaṃ* = the karma that creates obstacles (obstruction karma); these are also destroyed.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse describes the moment of omniscience — the instant when the last three "knowledge-obscuring" karmas are simultaneously destroyed. In Jain philosophy, there are four "destructive" karmas that directly impede the soul's natural capacities: knowledge-obscuring karma, perception-obscuring karma, deluding karma, and obstructing karma. When deluding karma (mohanīya) has been exhausted through the path of equanimity and detachment, the remaining three are destroyed "in a single moment" (*khaṇeṇaṃ*) — instantaneously.

The phrase *kayasavvikicco* — "one for whom all duties are completed" — is the Jain term for the soul at the threshold of omniscience. All that needed to be done has been done; the path has been walked to its end. What remains is not further effort but the final dissolution of the last obscuring karmas.

In a single moment, the accumulated obscurations of beginningless time are removed — and the soul's natural omniscience blazes forth. This is the event the entire Jain path of practice is oriented toward. It is not earned by any single heroic act; it is the fruit of the patient, consistent, thorough work of equanimity, right conduct, and detachment that the entire chapter has been describing.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The truly dispassionate person, having done everything that needed to be done, destroys all remaining knowledge-blocking karma in a single instant — and infinite knowledge appears.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean if, beneath all the obscurations of conditioning and desire, my natural state is already omniscient and free? Can I sense that possibility right now?

### SUTRA 32.109

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सव्वं तओ जाणइ पासइ य, अमोहणे होइ णिरंतराए ।
अणासवे झाण समाहिजुत्ते, आउकखए मोक्खमुवेइ सुद्धे ॥ ३२.१०९ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

savvaṃ tao jāṇai pāsai ya, amohaṇe hoi ṇiraṃtarāe |
aṇāsave jhāṇa samāhijutte, āukkhe mokkhamuvei suddhe || 32.109 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

After that he knows and sees all things; he becomes free from delusion, free from all obstacles. Without any influx of karma, engaged in pure deep meditation, when the life-karma is exhausted, he attains liberation — purified.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*tao* = after that (after the destruction of the three karmas); *savvaṃ jāṇai pāsai* = knows and sees all things; *amohaṇe hoi* = becomes free from delusion; *ṇiraṃtarāe* = free from all obstacles without interruption; *aṇāsave* = without influx of karma, free from all āsravas; *jhāṇa samāhijutte* = engaged in pure deep meditation; *āukkhe* = when life-karma is exhausted, at the end of the lifespan; *suddhe mokkhaṃ uvei* = attains liberation, purified.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse describes the state of the kevalī — the omniscient being who, having destroyed the three knowledge-obscuring karmas in the previous sutra, now abides in the fullness of omniscience until the final karma — the life-determining karma (*āyu-karma*) — is also exhausted at the natural end of that lifetime.

The kevalī "knows and sees all things" — past, present, and future, throughout all of existence. He is "free from delusion" — the deluding karma has been fully exhausted. He is "free from all obstacles" — no force in the universe can interrupt his knowing. He accumulates no new karma ("without any influx") because the very mechanism of karma-binding — passion, delusion, reactive consciousness — no longer exists in him. He abides in *pure deep meditation* (*jhāṇa samāhijutte*) — not the effortful meditation of the practitioner still working on the path, but the natural, effortless resting of omniscient consciousness in its own nature.

When the life-karma runs its course, liberation (*moksha*) follows as naturally as a fruit ripening and falling from the tree. The soul, freed from all karma, rises to the summit of the universe — the *siddha-loka* — and abides there in eternal, uncaused, infinite bliss, knowledge, perception, and energy. This is the destination toward which the first sutra of this chapter pointed.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

After that moment, the liberated soul knows everything, sees everything, is troubled by nothing, and when the last karma expires, attains complete liberation — pure and perfect.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If liberation is the soul's natural state — if it is what remains when all obscurations are removed — what am I adding to my experience right now that is preventing that naturalness from shining through?

### SUTRA 32.110

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सो तस्स सव्वस्स दुहस्स मुक्को, जं बाहइ सययं जंतुमेयं ।
दीहामयं विप्पमुक्को पसत्थो, तो होइ अच्चंतसुही कयत्थो ॥ ३२.११० ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

so tassa savvassa duhassa mukko, jaṃ bāhai sayayaṃ jaṃtumeyaṃ |
dīhāmayaṃ vippamukko pasatho, to hoi accaṃtasuhi kayattho || 32.110 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That soul is freed from all the suffering which has continuously afflicted this living being; freed from the long-lasting karmic disease and become praiseworthy, it then becomes eternally blissful and fully accomplished.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*so* = that soul; *savvassa duhassa mukko* = freed from all suffering; *jaṃ sayayaṃ jaṃtumeyaṃ bāhai* = which has continuously afflicted this living being; *dīhāmayaṃ* = from the long-enduring illness — the karma-disease; *vippamukko* = completely freed; *pasatho* = praiseworthy; *accaṃtasuhi* = eternally blissful; *kayattho* = fully accomplished, one whose purpose is fulfilled.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse speaks with extraordinary tenderness and warmth. The metaphor of karma as a *long-enduring illness* (*dīhāmaya*) is deeply compassionate. Karma has been described throughout this chapter in many ways: as a net, as seeds, as chains. Here it is described as a disease — a chronic condition that has afflicted the living being "continuously" (*sayayaṃ*) — without intermission, for beginningless time.

The liberated soul is not just "escaping" something; it is being *healed* of something. The liberation is medical: a complete cure of a condition that has caused suffering without end. And the cured soul is *praiseworthy* (*pasatho*) — it has achieved what is worthy of the highest admiration, not merely technically or philosophically but in the fullest human sense. It has done what is to be done. It has become what it always had the potential to be.

*Kayattho* — "fully accomplished" — is the perfect closing word. Not merely liberated in the abstract sense, but accomplished: the soul has reached its fullest actualization, the complete expression of its own nature, which is infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite energy, and infinite bliss.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

That soul is finally free — from every pain, from the ancient disease of karma — and becomes eternally, completely, perfectly happy. Its purpose is fulfilled.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it feel like to be fully accomplished — to have nothing left to do, nothing left to be, nothing left to become? Is there a whisper of that feeling anywhere in your experience right now?

### SUTRA 32.111

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अणाइ कालप्पभवस्स एसो, सव्वस्स दुक्खस्स पमोक्खमग्गो ।
वियाहिओ जं समुविच्च सत्ता, कमेण अच्चंतसुही भवंति ॥ ति बेमि ॥ ३२.१११ ॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

aṇāi kālappabhavassa eso, savvassa dukkhaassa pamokkhmaggo |
viyāhio jaṃ samuvicca sattā, kameṇa accaṃtasuhi bhaṃti || ti bemi || 32.111 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This is the path of liberation from all suffering that has arisen since beginningless time — as declared by the knowers. By properly adopting this path, living beings become eternally blissful, step by step. Thus I declare.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

*aṇāi kālappabhavassa* = that which has arisen since beginningless time; *savvassa dukkhaassa* = from all suffering; *pamokkhmaggo* = path of liberation, way of release; *eso* = this; *viyāhio* = declared, described; *jaṃ* = which; *sattā* = living beings, souls; *samuvicca* = having properly understood and adopted; *kameṇa* = step by step, gradually; *accaṃtasuhi bhaṃti* = become eternally blissful; *ti bemi* = thus I declare.

**5. COMMENTARY**

The closing verse mirrors the opening verse (Sutra 1) with deliberate symmetry. Sutra 1 promised to describe the path of liberation from all beginningless suffering. Sutra 111 declares that this path has been fully described. The circle is complete.

The phrase *kameṇa* — "step by step," "gradually" — is a final, important teaching. Liberation is not a sudden leap; it is a progression. The living being who genuinely adopts this path — who practices equanimity toward the senses, who controls diet and dwelling and companionship, who releases possessiveness and craving step by step — becomes "eternally blissful" gradually. Each step of genuine practice changes the inner landscape. Each moment of true equanimity dissolves some quantum of the karmic obscuration. The path is walked one step at a time, and each step brings the practitioner measurably closer to the destination.

The closing formula *ti bemi* — "thus I declare" — is the traditional Jain certification of authentic teaching. It affirms that what has been said is not the teacher's personal opinion or speculation but the transmission of truth that has been realized and verified through direct experience. The chapter closes not with theory but with testimony.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

This is the path out of all the suffering that has existed since beginningless time. By following it step by step, every living being can become eternally happy. This is the truth.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of everything in this chapter, what one teaching lands most deeply for me right now — and what one step could I take today toward actually living it?
