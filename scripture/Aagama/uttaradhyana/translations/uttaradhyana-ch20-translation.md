## SUTRA 20.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सिद्धाणं णमो किच्चा, संजयाणं च भावओ ।
अत्थधम्मगइं तच्चं, अणुसिट्टुं सुणेह मे ॥२०.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

siddhāṇaṃ ṇamo kiccā, saṃjayāṇaṃ ca bhāvao |
atthadhammagaiṃ taccaṃ, aṇusiṭṭuṃ suṇeha me || 20.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having bowed in devotion to the Siddhas and to the restrained ones, listen to me as I teach truly the path of dharma leading to liberation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **siddhāṇaṃ** = to the Siddhas (liberated souls)
- **ṇamo** = salutation, obeisance
- **kiccā** = having done, having offered
- **saṃjayāṇaṃ** = to the restrained ones (monks, ācāryas, upādhyāyas)
- **bhāvao** = with devotion, with heartfelt feeling
- **atthadhammagaiṃ** = the path leading to liberation through dharma; the truth of soul, conduct, and the beautiful goal
- **taccaṃ** = truly, in truth, the real nature
- **aṇusiṭṭuṃ** = to teach, to instruct, to give upadeśa
- **suṇeha** = listen, hear
- **me** = from me, through me

### 5. COMMENTARY

This mangala verse opens the chapter with a dual salutation — to the Siddhas (those who have already reached the summit of liberation) and to the saṃyata (restrained monastics still on the path). The word **bhāvao** signals that this is not a ritual bow but a bow of genuine inner devotion. The sutra then announces its purpose: to teach the true path (attha-dhamma-gati) — the way of the soul, the way of dharma, and the way to liberation. This chapter is named Mahānirgranthīya because it is the great teaching of the Nirgrantha — the one who has cut all knots of attachment. What follows is not abstract philosophy but a living story that illuminates the deepest meaning of being a true protector of oneself and others.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Having bowed to the liberated and the disciplined with full heart, I will teach you the true path of dharma — listen.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I offer respect to a teacher or a teaching, is it a habit of the body or a movement of the heart?

## SUTRA 20.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पभूयरयणो राया, सेणिओ मगहाहिवो ।
विहारजत्तं णिज्जाओ, मंडिकुच्छिस चेइए ॥२०.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

pabhūyarayaṇo rāyā, seṇio magahāhivo |
vihārajattaṃ ṇijjāo, maṃḍikucchisa ceie || 20.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

King Śreṇika, the lord of Magadha, wealthy in jewels and riches, set out from the city for a stroll to the Maṇḍikukṣi garden.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **pabhūyarayaṇo** = one who is wealthy in many jewels, richly endowed
- **rāyā** = king
- **seṇio** = Śreṇika (the famous king of Magadha, also known as Bimbisāra)
- **magahāhivo** = lord/master of Magadha
- **vihārajattaṃ** = for the purpose of a pleasure walk, an outing
- **ṇijjāo** = went out, departed from the city
- **maṃḍikucchisa** = of Maṇḍikukṣi (name of the garden)
- **ceie** = garden, park (cetya — a sacred or beautiful grove)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The scene is set in the great kingdom of Magadha, whose capital was Rājagṛha. King Śreṇika (Bimbisāra in Buddhist sources) is described as **pabhūyarayaṇo** — overflowing with every form of worldly wealth: precious gems, armies, palaces, fame. He is the epitome of the "sanātha" — one who appears to have everything. The Maṇḍikukṣi garden is a royal pleasure garden. This setting — a king of supreme wealth walking into a garden — is the perfect foil for what is about to happen: an encounter with a monk who calls himself "without any master."

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

King Śreṇika, the powerful and wealthy lord of Magadha, went out for a garden walk to the Maṇḍikukṣi grove.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to be truly wealthy? Can external riches ever guarantee inner protection?

## SUTRA 20.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णाणादुम लयाइण्णं, णाणापक्खि णिसेवियं ।
णाणाकुसुम संछण्णं, उज्जाणं णंदणोवमं ॥२०.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ṇāṇāduma layāiṇṇaṃ, ṇāṇāpakkhi ṇiseviyaṃ |
ṇāṇākusuma saṃchaṇṇaṃ, ujjāṇaṃ naṃdaṇovamaṃ || 20.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The garden — filled with many kinds of trees and creepers, frequented by many kinds of birds, covered with many kinds of flowers — was like the Nandana grove of heaven.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇāṇāduma** = many kinds of trees
- **layāiṇṇaṃ** = filled with creepers and vines
- **ṇāṇāpakkhi** = many kinds of birds
- **ṇiseviyaṃ** = frequented, inhabited
- **ṇāṇākusuma** = many kinds of flowers
- **saṃchaṇṇaṃ** = covered, spread with
- **ujjāṇaṃ** = garden, park (udyāna)
- **naṃdaṇovamaṃ** = like Nandanavana — the celestial pleasure garden of Indra

### 5. COMMENTARY

The text dwells on the beauty of the garden to establish the worldly splendour surrounding King Śreṇika. Nandanavana is the paradise garden in Indra's heaven — comparing Maṇḍikukṣi to it signals the very peak of earthly beauty. Everything around the king is abundant, beautiful, pleasant. The contrast that follows — a solitary monk under a tree, without possessions — will be all the more striking. The garden's description is not decoration; it frames the question: in all this abundance and beauty, who is truly "with a master"?

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The garden was filled with countless trees, flowering vines, singing birds, and blooming flowers — it was like a paradise on earth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Beauty surrounds us. Do we use it to awaken, or do we sink into it and forget ourselves?

## SUTRA 20.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थ सो पासइ साहुं, संजयं सुसमाहियं ।
णिसण्णं रुक्खमूलम्मि, सुकुमालं सुहोइयं ॥२०.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tattha so pāsai sāhuṃ, saṃjayaṃ susamāhiyaṃ |
ṇisaṇṇaṃ rukkhamūlammi, sukumālaṃ suhoiyaṃ || 20.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There he saw a monk — restrained, perfectly composed in meditation — seated at the foot of a tree, delicate and radiant with well-being.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tattha** = there, in that garden
- **so** = he (the king)
- **pāsai** = saw, beheld
- **sāhuṃ** = a monk, a sādhu
- **saṃjayaṃ** = restrained, a saṃyatī
- **susamāhiyaṃ** = perfectly composed, well-absorbed in meditation
- **ṇisaṇṇaṃ** = seated
- **rukkhamūlammi** = at the foot of a tree
- **sukumālaṃ** = tender, delicate (of complexion and form)
- **suhoiyaṃ** = radiant, worthy of enjoyment, glowing with health and peace

### 5. COMMENTARY

The first glimpse of Anāthī Muni. He is seated under a tree — no throne, no roof, no furniture. Yet he is **susamāhiya**: perfectly composed, deep in equanimity. And he is **sukumāla**: his body radiates a delicacy and glow that immediately arrests the king's gaze. There is a paradox here that the story will unpack: the monk has nothing — no wealth, no servants, no medical care — yet he sits in radiating composure. The king has everything, yet he is about to be shown that he "has" nothing in the deepest sense. The tree is both a literal shelter and a symbol: under its shade, this monk has found what palaces cannot provide.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

There in the garden the king saw a monk — calm in meditation, glowing with inner peace — seated quietly under a tree.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does the face of someone truly at peace look like? Have I ever seen it — and recognised what I was seeing?

## SUTRA 20.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स रूवं तु पासित्ता, राइणो तम्मि संजए ।
अच्चंतपरमो आसी, अउलो रूविविम्हओ ॥२०.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tassa rūvaṃ tu pāsittā, rāiṇo tammi saṃjae |
accaṃtaparamo āsī, aualo rūvivimhao || 20.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Upon beholding the form of that monk, the king was filled with the most extreme and unparalleled amazement at his beauty.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tassa** = of that monk
- **rūvaṃ** = form, appearance, beauty
- **pāsittā** = having seen, upon beholding
- **rāiṇo** = of the king
- **tammi saṃjae** = in regard to that restrained one
- **accaṃtaparamo** = of the highest and most extreme degree
- **āsī** = was, arose
- **aualo** = unparalleled, matchless
- **rūvivimhao** = amazement at the form/beauty (rūpa-vismaya)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king's reaction is **rūvivimhao** — not just admiration but profound astonishment. The form of a true monk is not merely aesthetically beautiful; it carries the unmistakable signature of inner transformation. A composed, disciplined, desire-free person radiates something that wealth and physical health alone cannot produce. The king — surrounded by the most beautiful people in his court — is struck by this monk's appearance as extraordinary. This is the power of inner stillness: it becomes visible on the outside. The word **aualo** (unparalleled) emphasises that this beauty cannot be replicated by worldly means.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the king looked at the monk's form, he was filled with extraordinary, unmatched amazement — he had never seen anything quite like it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the source of the beauty that stops people in their tracks? Is it the face, or something behind the face?

## SUTRA 20.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहो वण्णो अहो रूवं, अहो अज्जस्स सोमया ।
अहो खंती अहो मुत्ती, अहो भोगे असंगया ॥२०.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

aho vaṇṇo aho rūvaṃ, aho ajjassa somayā |
aho khaṃtī aho muttī, aho bhoge asaṃgayā || 20.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Wonderful is his complexion! Wonderful is his form! Wonderful is this noble one's serenity! Wonderful his patience! Wonderful his freedom from desire! Wonderful his non-attachment to pleasures!"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aho** = wonderful! what a marvel! (exclamation of deep wonder)
- **vaṇṇo** = complexion, colour, radiance
- **rūvaṃ** = form, physical appearance
- **ajjassa** = of this noble one, of this ārya (the monk)
- **somayā** = serenity, gentleness, pleasant composure
- **khaṃtī** = patience, forbearance (kṣānti)
- **muttī** = freedom from desire, non-possessiveness, liberation from craving
- **bhoge** = in pleasures, regarding sensual enjoyments
- **asaṃgayā** = non-attachment, non-clinging

### 5. COMMENTARY

Six exclamations — each beginning with **aho** — pour from the king spontaneously. He is not reciting a list; he is seeing. What he sees in order: (1) radiance of complexion, (2) beauty of form, (3) inner serenity, (4) patience/forbearance, (5) freedom from craving, (6) non-attachment to pleasures. The progression moves from outer to inner: from what the eye first catches to what the heart recognises. Significantly, the king recognises **khaṃtī** (patience) and **asaṃgayā** (non-attachment) — qualities that go beyond physical beauty. This is the beginning of his own awakening. He can see what he has been missing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king marvelled aloud: "What complexion! What form! What serenity, what patience, what freedom from craving, what non-attachment to pleasure!"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which quality in a person most awes me — their looks, their peace, their patience, or their freedom from wanting?

## SUTRA 20.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स पाए उ वंदित्ता, काऊण य पयाहिणं ।
णाइदूरमणासण्णे, पंजली पडिपुच्छइ ॥२०.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tassa pāe u vaṃdittā, kāūṇa ya payāhiṇaṃ |
ṇāidūramaṇāsaṇṇe, paṃjalī paḍipucchhai || 20.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having bowed at his feet and circumambulated him, the king stood neither too far nor too close, and with joined palms began to question him.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tassa pāe** = at his (the monk's) feet
- **vaṃdittā** = having bowed, having offered salutation
- **kāūṇa** = having done, having performed
- **payāhiṇaṃ** = pradakṣiṇā — circumambulation (walking around in respect)
- **ṇāidūram** = not too far
- **aṇāsaṇṇe** = not too close
- **paṃjalī** = with joined palms (añjali)
- **paḍipucchhai** = began to ask, began to question

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king's approach is described with care. He bows, circumambulates (pradakṣiṇā — the traditional mark of highest respect, walking clockwise around the honoured one), and then positions himself at the correct distance — not too close (which would be presumptuous) and not too far (which would be disrespectful). He joins his palms. This is the etiquette of genuine respect — body, movement, and gesture aligned. Pradakṣiṇā in ancient Indian tradition is the physical expression of "may your virtue surround me on all sides." King Śreṇika — the most powerful man in Magadha — is now a student.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king bowed at the monk's feet, walked around him in reverence, then stood respectfully nearby with joined palms and began to ask him questions.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I approach a teacher, does my body carry respect or does only my mouth say respectful things?

## SUTRA 20.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तरुणोसि अज्जो पव्वइओ, भोगकालिम्मि संजया ।
उवट्टिओऽसि सामण्णे, एयमट्टुं सुणेमि ता ॥२०.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

taruṇosi ajjo pavvaio, bhogakālimmi saṃjayā |
uvaṭṭio'si sāmaṇṇe, eyamaṭṭuṃ suṇemi tā || 20.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O noble one, you are young and have taken initiation in the time of pleasures. O restrained one, you have come forth into the śramaṇa life — I wish to hear the reason for this."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taruṇosi** = you are young
- **ajjo** = O noble one, O ārya (respectful address)
- **pavvaio** = you have taken initiation, you have renounced (pravrajita)
- **bhogakālimmi** = in the time of pleasures, at the age meant for enjoyment
- **saṃjayā** = O restrained one, O saṃyata (monk)
- **uvaṭṭio'si** = you have come forth, you have taken up (upasthita)
- **sāmaṇṇe** = in the śramaṇa dharma, in monastic life
- **eyamaṭṭuṃ** = this reason, this purpose
- **suṇemi tā** = I wish to hear, I desire to know

### 5. COMMENTARY

Śreṇika's first question is natural, even universal: you are young — why have you given up pleasure at the very age meant for it? The word **bhogakālimmi** is significant: the king assumes there is a "right time" for everything — a time for pleasure, perhaps later a time for renunciation. This is the worldly view of time as a sequence of stages (āśramas). The monk's answer will shatter this assumption entirely. The king is respectful but genuinely puzzled. He is asking from within the framework of the world — a framework the monk has already stepped outside of.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king asked: "You are young — yet you have chosen the monastic life in the very season of pleasure. O restrained one, I want to hear your reason."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there ever a "wrong time" to turn towards the truth? What am I waiting for?

## SUTRA 20.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणाहो मि महाराय, णाहो मज्झ ण विज्जइ ।
अणुकंपगं सुहिं वावि, कंचि णाभिसमेमहं ॥२०.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

aṇāho mi mahārāya, ṇāho majjha ṇa vijjai |
aṇukaṃpagaṃ suhiṃ vāvi, kaṃci ṇābhisamemahaṃ || 20.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O Mahārāja, I am without a master (anātha). No master exists for me. I could not find a single compassionate friend to protect me — and so I took initiation."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇāho mi** = I am anātha (without a protector/master)
- **mahārāya** = O great king
- **ṇāho** = a nātha — a master, a protector, a refuge
- **majjha** = mine, for me
- **ṇa vijjai** = does not exist, is not found
- **aṇukaṃpagaṃ** = compassionate, one who shows compassion and care
- **suhiṃ** = a friend, a companion
- **vāvi** = also, even
- **kaṃci** = any, anyone
- **ṇābhisamemahaṃ** = I was not able to find, I could not encounter

### 5. COMMENTARY

The answer is the philosophical heart of the entire chapter, delivered in a single breath: "I am anātha — without a master." The monk does not mean he has no family, no friends, no wealth. He means something far deeper: in the hour of my real suffering, no one could protect me. No person, however loving or powerful, could take my pain. This is the first statement of the Jain understanding of karma: suffering is personal. No external being — not parents, not spouse, not siblings, not doctors — can experience your karma for you. The recognition of this fact is not pessimism; it is the beginning of genuine self-responsibility and the path to liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk answered: "O great king, I am without a master. I found no one — no friend, no companion — who could truly protect me. That is why I took initiation."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I was in real pain — physical, emotional, existential — who could truly protect me? What does that tell me?

## SUTRA 20.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तओ सो पहसिओ राया, सेणिओ मगहाहिवो ।
एवं ते इंदिमंतस्स, कहं णाहो ण विज्जइ ॥२०.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tao so pahasio rāyā, seṇio magahāhivo |
evaṃ te iṃdimantassa, kahaṃ ṇāho ṇa vijjai || 20.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then King Śreṇika, lord of Magadha, laughed and said: "How can one with such beauty and power as yours have no master?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tao** = then, upon hearing this
- **so** = he (the king)
- **pahasio** = laughed, smiled, began to speak with amusement
- **rāyā seṇio** = King Śreṇika
- **magahāhivo** = lord of Magadha
- **evaṃ** = in this way, thus
- **te** = yours
- **iṃdimantassa** = of one who is endowed with the beauty and aura of Indra (the king of gods)
- **kahaṃ** = how
- **ṇāho ṇa vijjai** = can a master not exist, can there be no protector

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king laughs. This laugh is not mockery — it is the laugh of someone who cannot yet understand. From within the logic of the world, the monk's claim makes no sense at all: he is young, radiantly beautiful, clearly extraordinary — how can such a person be without protection? The king's logic: beauty + power = sanātha (one who has a master/refuge). He is seeing only the surface. The monk has already moved to a different level of understanding where the criteria are completely different. The stage is set for the king to be turned inside out.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

King Śreṇika laughed and said: "With your beauty and bearing like Indra himself — how can you possibly be without a master?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have I ever laughed at a truth I didn't understand yet — only to realise later how right it was?

## SUTRA 20.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

होमि णाहो भयंताणं, भोगे भुंजाहि संजया ।
मित्त-णाईपरिवुडो, माणुस्सं खु सुदुल्लहं ॥२०.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

homi ṇāho bhayaṃtāṇaṃ, bhoge bhuṃjāhi saṃjayā |
mitta-ṇāīparivuḍo, māṇussaṃ khu sudullhaṃ || 20.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I will be your master, O Venerable One. O restrained one, enjoy pleasures surrounded by friends and relatives — for human birth is indeed very rare."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **homi** = I will be, I shall become
- **ṇāho** = master, protector, refuge
- **bhayaṃtāṇaṃ** = of the Venerable One, of the Bhagavanta (respectful address)
- **bhoge** = pleasures, sense enjoyments
- **bhuṃjāhi** = enjoy, partake of
- **saṃjayā** = O restrained one
- **mitta-ṇāī** = friends and relatives
- **parivuḍo** = surrounded, encompassed
- **māṇussaṃ** = human birth, human life
- **khu** = indeed, certainly
- **sudullhaṃ** = very difficult to obtain, extremely rare

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king offers himself as the monk's nātha — his protector, patron, master. He sweetens the offer with the classic worldly argument: human birth is rare (māṇussaṃ sudullhaṃ) — therefore use it for pleasure while you can. Ironically, the Jain tradition also teaches that human birth is rare — but for the opposite reason: it is rare precisely because it is the only birth in which liberation is possible. Pleasure-seeking wastes the rare opportunity. The king unknowingly repeats a truth whose conclusion is the exact opposite of what he intends. He offers friendship and family — the very things the monk will explain were powerless to help him.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The king offered: "I will be your master. Enjoy pleasures surrounded by friends and family — for human birth is very rare and precious."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone uses "life is short" as a reason to indulge — are they using the truth or wasting it?

## SUTRA 20.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्पणा वि अणाहो सि, सेणिया मगहाहिवा ।
अप्पणा अणाहो संतो, कहं णाहो भविस्ससि ॥२०.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

appaṇā vi aṇāho si, seṇiyā magahāhivā |
appaṇā aṇāho saṃto, kahaṃ ṇāho bhavissasi || 20.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O Śreṇika, lord of Magadha — you yourself are anātha. Being yourself without a master, how can you become a master for another?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **appaṇā vi** = you yourself, you too
- **aṇāho si** = are anātha (without a true master/protector)
- **seṇiyā** = O Śreṇika
- **magahāhivā** = O lord of Magadha
- **appaṇā** = yourself
- **aṇāho saṃto** = being (yourself) without a master
- **kahaṃ** = how
- **ṇāho bhavissasi** = will you become a master/protector (for another)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the turning point of the entire chapter. The monk's counter-question hits like a thunderclap: *you* are anātha, Śreṇika. The most powerful king in the land is being told — gently, directly — that he too is without ultimate protection. The logic is airtight: if you cannot protect yourself from the karma that will ripen in your own experience, you cannot protect me. Protection in the deepest sense is not about armies or wealth — it is about the soul's relationship to its own suffering. A king who is himself anātha in this fundamental sense cannot be a nātha for anyone else. This overturns every worldly hierarchy in a single sentence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

- **gachai** = goes
- **kaṭṭu** = having accumulated
- **pāvaṃ** = sin, sinful karma

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra addresses the specific monastic discipline around food. ### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं थुणित्ता स रायसीहो, अणगारसीहं परमाइ भत्तिए ।
सओरोहो सपरियणो सबंधवो, धम्माणुरत्तो विमलेण चेयसा ॥२०.५८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

evaṃ thuṇittā sa rāyasīho, aṇagārasīhaṃ paramāi bhattie |
saorohho sapariyaṇo sabaṃdhavo, dhammāṇuratto vimaleṇa ceyasā || 20.58 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Having thus praised the lion among monks with utmost devotion — that lion among kings, together with his queens, retinue, and relatives, became devoted to dharma with a pure mind."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evaṃ** = thus, in this way
- **thuṇittā** = having praised, having offered eulogy
- **sa rāyasīho** = that lion among kings (Śreṇika — the king compared to a lion)
- **aṇagārasīhaṃ** = the lion among monks (Anāthī Muni — described as a lion among the homeless)
- **paramāi bhattie** = with utmost devotion, with supreme bhakti
- **saorohho** = together with his inner palace/queens (antaḥpura)
- **sapariyaṇo** = together with his retinue
- **sabaṃdhavo** = together with his relatives
- **dhammāṇuratto** = became attached to, devoted to, absorbed in dharma
- **vimaleṇa ceyasā** = with a pure mind, with an immaculate consciousness

### 5. COMMENTARY

A beautiful conclusion to the encounter. Two lions face each other: the rāyasīha (lion among kings — Śreṇika) and the aṇagārasīha (lion among monks — Anāthī Muni). The lion metaphor honours both: Śreṇika's worldly power and Anāthī Muni's spiritual power. After the encounter, the entire royal circle — queens, retinue, relatives — all together undergo the inner transformation. **Dhammāṇuratto vimaleṇa ceyasā**: devoted to dharma with a pure mind. The purity of the mind is the key: not just outward observance, but inner clarity, free from delusion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"The lion among kings praised the lion among monks with supreme devotion — and together with his queens, retinue, and relatives, he became devoted to dharma with a pure mind."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When a teaching truly lands — does it transform only the individual, or does it ripple outward to those around them?

## SUTRA 20.59

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ऊससिय-रोमकूवो, काऊण य पयाहिणं ।
अभिवंदिऊण सिरसा, अइयाओ णराहिवो ॥२०.५९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ūsasiya-romakūvo, kāūṇa ya payāhiṇaṃ |
abhivaṃdiūṇa sirasā, aiyāo ṇarāhivo || 20.59 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"His body's hair standing upright with joy, having circumambulated and bowed his head in salutation — the king departed to his own place."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ūsasiya** = risen, standing upright (ūrdhva — upward)
- **romakūvo** = the hair-pores, the hairs of the body (roma-kūpa)
- **kāūṇa** = having done
- **ya** = and
- **payāhiṇaṃ** = circumambulation (pradakṣiṇā)
- **abhivaṃdiūṇa** = having bowed, having offered salutation
- **sirasā** = with the head, bowing the head
- **aiyāo** = departed, went back
- **ṇarāhivo** = the king, the lord of men

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king departs — but the detail that closes this scene is extraordinary: **ūsasiya-romakūvo** — the hairs of his body were standing on end. This is the classical Sanskrit-Prakrit description of the body's response to genuine spiritual emotion: goosebumps of joy, of awe, of devotion. The king's transformation is not merely intellectual — it has reached his body. He circumambulates the monk one final time (pradakṣiṇā), bows his head to the ground, and departs. Everything about this departure is the opposite of his arrival — he came full of laughter and possession; he leaves full of awe and emptying.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"His body hair standing on end with spiritual joy, the king circumambulated the monk, bowed his head, and departed to his own place."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever been so moved by truth or beauty that your body responded — and what was that experience teaching you?

## SUTRA 20.60

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इयरो वि गुणसमिद्धो, तिगुत्तिगुत्तो तिदंडविरओ य ।
विहग इव विप्पमुक्को, विहरइ वसुहं विगयमोहो ॥६०॥ – ति बेमि

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

iyaro vi guṇasamiddho, tigutti-gutto tidaṃḍaviraho ya |
vihaga iva vippamukko, viharai vasuhaṃ vigayamoho || 60 || — ti bemi

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"And the other — Anāthī Muni — rich in all virtues, guarded by the triple guard (of mind, speech, body), free from the three sticks (of violence), and free from delusion — wandered the earth like a bird set free." — Thus I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **iyaro vi** = and the other one (Anāthī Muni, contrasted with the departing king)
- **guṇasamiddho** = rich in virtues, endowed with 27 qualities of a sādhu
- **tigutti-gutto** = protected by the three guptis (guarded in mind, speech, and body)
- **tidaṃḍaviraho** = free from the three daṇḍas (the three rods — violent action of mind, speech, body)
- **ya** = and
- **vihaga iva** = like a bird
- **vippamukko** = completely free, completely released (vi-pra-mukta)
- **viharai** = wanders, moves about
- **vasuhaṃ** = on the earth
- **vigayamoho** = free from delusion (vi-gata-moha)
- **ti bemi** = thus I say (the concluding formula of the Uttaradhyayana)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The final verse closes with two figures: the king has gone back to his palace; the monk remains on the earth. And the monk is described in his full glory: **guṇasamiddho** — rich in the 27 qualities of a sādhu; **tigutti-gutto** — protected by the three internal guards (mind, speech, body); **tidaṃḍaviraho** — free from all three rods of violence; **vigayamoho** — completely free from delusion. The crowning image: **vihaga iva vippamukko** — like a bird set free. This is the ultimate image of liberation in the Jain tradition: the soul, freed from the cage of karma, wandering the earth without bondage, going wherever the path of dharma leads. **Ti bemi** — the formula that closes canonical sutras: "Thus I say." The words of Mahāvīra, transmitted through generations, close here.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

"And Anāthī Muni — rich in virtue, guarded within, free from violence, free from delusion — wandered the earth like a bird set free. Thus I say."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it feel like to be a bird set free — not running from anything, not running toward anything, simply wandering the earth in unobstructed peace?
