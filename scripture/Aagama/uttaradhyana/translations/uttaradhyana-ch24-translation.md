# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 24
# Pravachana Mata (The Mothers of the Teaching)

**Chapter 24 of the Uttaradhyayana Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Volume 2, printed pages 64–77 (PDF pages 118–131)*
*Total Sutras: 27*
*Theme:* The eight foundations that "give birth to" the spiritual path — five forms of carefulness (samiti) and three forms of self-control (gupti). These are called the "mothers" of the teaching because just as a mother nurtures and protects her child, these eight practices nurture and protect the monk's spiritual life.

---

## SUTRA 24.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अट्ठु पवयणमायाओ, समिई गुत्ती तहेव य ।
पंचेव य समिईओ, तओ गुत्तीउ आहिया ॥२४.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

aṭṭhu pavayaṇamāyāo, samiī guttī taheva ya |
paṃceva ya samiīo, tao guttīu āhiyā || 24.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The mothers of the teaching are eight — five forms of carefulness and three forms of self-control.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṭṭhu** = eight
- **pavayaṇamāyāo** = pravachana-mata — mothers of the teaching/scripture
- **samiī** = samiti — forms of carefulness
- **guttī** = gupti — forms of self-control
- **taheva ya** = and also, likewise
- **paṃceva ya** = five indeed
- **samiīo** = samitis (carefulness practices)
- **tao** = three
- **guttīu** = guptis (self-control practices)
- **āhiyā** = are declared, are said to be

### 5. COMMENTARY

The opening verse announces the subject of the entire chapter: the eight mothers of the teaching. These eight practices are called "mothers" because they give birth to the spiritual path and protect it from destruction — just as a mother gives birth to a child and protects it. Five of these are forms of active carefulness — meaning how a monk should conduct himself while engaging with the world. Three are forms of self-control — meaning how a monk should restrain himself from harmful activities. Together, these eight practices form the complete foundation of monastic discipline. Without them, no amount of knowledge or faith can bear fruit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The spiritual path has eight foundational practices — five about being careful in your actions and three about controlling yourself.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What are the foundations that protect my own growth? Am I nurturing them, or am I neglecting the very ground I stand on?

## SUTRA 24.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ईरिया भासेसणादाणे, उच्चारे समिई इव ।
दुवालसंगं जिणक्खायं, मायं जत्थ उ पवयणं ॥२४.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

īriyā bhāsesaṇādāṇe, uccāre samiī iva |
duvālasaṃgaṃ jiṇakkhāyaṃ, māyaṃ jatthu u pavayaṇaṃ || 24.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Walking, speech, food-seeking, handling objects, and disposal — these are the five forms of carefulness found in the twelve-limbed scripture proclaimed by the Conqueror.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **īriyā** = walking, movement (irya samiti)
- **bhāse** = speech (bhasha samiti)
- **saṇā** = food-seeking (eshana samiti)
- **dāṇe** = handling, picking up and putting down (adana-nikshepana samiti)
- **uccāre** = disposal, excretion (utsarga samiti)
- **samiī iva** = these are the samitis
- **duvālasaṃgaṃ** = in the twelve-limbed (canonical scripture)
- **jiṇakkhāyaṃ** = proclaimed by the Jina (the Conqueror)
- **māyaṃ** = mothers
- **jatthu** = where, in which
- **pavayaṇaṃ** = the teaching, the scripture

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names the five forms of carefulness one by one. The first is carefulness in walking — being aware of every step so as not to harm any living being. The second is carefulness in speech — speaking only what is necessary, true, and non-harmful. The third is carefulness in seeking food — accepting only proper food that has been obtained without causing harm. The fourth is carefulness in handling objects — picking up and putting down belongings with full awareness. The fifth is carefulness in disposal — eliminating bodily waste only in places free from living beings. These five are not invented rules; they come directly from the canonical scripture taught by the liberated teachers. They are the practical application of non-violence in daily monastic life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The five forms of carefulness are: how you walk, how you speak, how you eat, how you handle things, and how you dispose of waste — all taught by the enlightened ones.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In how many of my daily actions — walking, speaking, eating, handling, disposing — do I act with full awareness?

## SUTRA 24.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयाओ पंच समिईओ, समासेण वियाहिया ।
एत्तो य तओ गुत्तीओ, वुच्छामि अणुपुव्वसो ॥२४.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

eyāo paṃca samiīo, samāseṇa viyāhiyā |
etto ya tao guttīo, vucchāmi aṇupuvvaso || 24.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

These five forms of carefulness have been briefly described. Now I shall describe the three forms of self-control in proper sequence.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **eyāo** = these
- **paṃca** = five
- **samiīo** = samitis (forms of carefulness)
- **samāseṇa** = in brief, in summary
- **viyāhiyā** = have been described, explained
- **etto** = from here, now
- **ya** = and
- **tao** = three
- **guttīo** = guptis (forms of self-control)
- **vucchāmi** = I shall describe
- **aṇupuvvaso** = in proper sequence, one after another

### 5. COMMENTARY

This transitional verse marks the structure of the chapter. The teacher has just named the five forms of carefulness and now announces that the three forms of self-control will follow. The word "in proper sequence" signals that the chapter will treat each practice systematically — first explaining each of the five forms of carefulness in detail, and then each of the three forms of self-control. This methodical approach is characteristic of the scripture: nothing is left vague or general. Every practice is defined, explained, and illustrated so the practitioner knows exactly what to do.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The five forms of carefulness have been named — now the three forms of self-control will be explained one by one.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I approach my own growth systematically, or do I leave important practices vague and undefined?

## SUTRA 24.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आलंबणेण कालेण, मग्गेण उप्पहवज्जिए ।
चउकारणपरिसुद्धं, ईरियं पल्लए मुणी ॥२४.४॥

⚠️ Some characters in the second line may differ slightly from the PDF edition.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

ālambaṇeṇa kāleṇa, maggeṇa uppahavajjie |
caukāraṇaparisuddhaṃ, īriyaṃ pallae muṇī || 24.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

With proper support, at the right time, on the right path, and avoiding harm — the sage walks with purity in all four aspects.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ālambaṇeṇa** = with proper support/foundation (knowledge, faith, conduct)
- **kāleṇa** = at the proper time (during daylight)
- **maggeṇa** = on the proper path (inspected and clear)
- **uppahavajjie** = avoiding harm, free from injury
- **caukāraṇaparisuddhaṃ** = purified in four causes/aspects
- **īriyaṃ** = walking, movement
- **pallae** = observes, practises
- **muṇī** = the sage, the monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse begins the detailed treatment of the first form of carefulness — walking. A monk does not simply walk from place to place; every step is a spiritual practice. Four aspects must be pure: the support (meaning the monk walks grounded in knowledge, faith, and conduct), the time (walking only during daylight hours when living beings on the path can be seen), the path (choosing routes that have been inspected and are free from small creatures), and the intention (walking only when necessary, not out of restlessness or desire). When all four are pure, the act of walking itself becomes an expression of non-violence. This is what distinguishes a mindful sage from an ordinary person who walks without any awareness of the life beneath their feet.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A sage walks carefully — with the right knowledge, at the right time, on the right path, and with the right intention — so that no living being is harmed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I move through the world, am I aware of the impact of my steps — both literal and metaphorical?

## SUTRA 24.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तत्थ आलंबणं णाणं, दंसणं चरणं तहा ।
तवो य चउव्विहालंबणं, समासेण वियाहियं ॥२४.५॥

⚠️ Second line partially reconstructed from commentary context.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

tattha ālambaṇaṃ ṇāṇaṃ, daṃsaṇaṃ caraṇaṃ tahā |
tavo ya cauvvihālambaṇaṃ, samāseṇa viyāhiyaṃ || 24.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The four supports for walking are: knowledge, faith, conduct, and austerity — this is the fourfold foundation briefly described.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tattha** = therein, in that (walking carefulness)
- **ālambaṇaṃ** = support, foundation
- **ṇāṇaṃ** = knowledge (jnana)
- **daṃsaṇaṃ** = faith, right vision (darshana)
- **caraṇaṃ** = conduct (charitra)
- **tahā** = and also
- **tavo** = austerity (tapa)
- **ya** = and
- **cauvvihālambaṇaṃ** = the fourfold support
- **samāseṇa** = in brief
- **viyāhiyaṃ** = is described

### 5. COMMENTARY

Before a monk takes a single step, he must be grounded in four supports. The first is knowledge — he must know which beings inhabit the path, what times are safe for walking, and what routes are appropriate. The second is faith — he must have unwavering conviction that protecting all life is the highest duty. The third is conduct — he must have the discipline to actually follow through on what he knows and believes. The fourth is austerity — he must have the inner strength that comes from spiritual practice, which keeps him alert and undistracted. Without knowledge, he walks blindly. Without faith, he walks carelessly. Without conduct, he walks hypocritically. Without austerity, he walks weakly. All four together create the complete foundation for walking without harm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

To walk without causing harm, you need four things: the knowledge of what to avoid, the belief that it matters, the discipline to follow through, and the inner strength to stay alert.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of these four foundations — knowledge, faith, discipline, inner strength — is weakest in my own practice?

## SUTRA 24.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दव्वओ खेत्तओ चेव, कालओ भावओ तहा ।
जयणा चउव्विहा वुत्ता, तं मे कित्तयओ सुण ॥२४.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

davvao khettao ceva, kālao bhāvao tahā |
jayaṇā cauvvihā vuttā, taṃ me kittayao suṇa || 24.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Carefulness is fourfold — by substance, by place, by time, and by disposition. Listen as I describe them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **davvao** = by substance (dravya) — what is being walked upon
- **khettao** = by place (kshetra) — the area/region
- **ceva** = and, also
- **kālao** = by time (kala) — the appropriate time
- **bhāvao** = by disposition (bhava) — the inner mental state
- **tahā** = likewise
- **jayaṇā** = carefulness, vigilance
- **cauvvihā** = fourfold, of four kinds
- **vuttā** = stated, declared
- **taṃ** = that
- **me** = from me
- **kittayao** = I describe, I explain
- **suṇa** = listen

### 5. COMMENTARY

Every form of carefulness is examined through four lenses. By substance means: what is the thing being walked upon? Is there a living being there? By place means: what kind of ground is this — is it a field, a forest floor, a path known to have insects? By time means: is this the right time for walking — during daylight when one can see, or at night when creatures are invisible? By disposition means: what is the monk's inner state — is he alert and focused, or distracted and careless? These four dimensions ensure that carefulness is not a superficial rule but a thorough practice. It is not enough to simply "be careful" — one must be careful in every dimension simultaneously. This fourfold analysis is applied to all five forms of carefulness throughout the chapter.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Being careful means checking four things at once: what you're dealing with, where you are, when you're doing it, and what state of mind you're in.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I try to be careful, do I only check one dimension, or do I examine the full picture?

## SUTRA 24.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दव्वओ चक्खुसा पेहे, जुगमित्तं च खेत्तओ ।
कालओ जाव रीएज्जा, उवउत्ते य भावओ ॥२४.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

davvao cakkhusā pehe, jugamittaṃ ca khettao |
kālao jāva rīejjā, uvaütte ya bhāvao || 24.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

By substance — look with your eyes. By place — two arm-lengths ahead. By time — as long as one is walking. By disposition — with full awareness.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **davvao** = by substance
- **cakkhusā** = with the eyes
- **pehe** = look, observe, inspect
- **jugamittaṃ** = two arm-lengths ahead (yuga-matra)
- **ca** = and
- **khettao** = by place
- **kālao** = by time
- **jāva** = as long as, until
- **rīejjā** = one walks, one moves
- **uvaütte** = with proper awareness, with attentiveness
- **ya** = and
- **bhāvao** = by disposition, by mental state

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse gives the practical application of the four dimensions of carefulness in walking. By substance: use your own eyes to look at the ground before stepping — do not walk blindly or while looking elsewhere. By place: always look at least two arm-lengths ahead so there is time to avoid any creature on the path. By time: maintain this vigilance for the entire duration of walking, not just at the beginning — carelessness can arise at any moment. By disposition: walk with a mind that is fully present and aware, not lost in thought or conversation. These are not abstract principles; they are exact instructions. A monk who follows them transforms every step into an act of compassion. The measurement of "two arm-lengths" shows how precise this practice is — it is not a vague suggestion but a concrete standard.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When walking: look at the ground with your own eyes, scan two arm-lengths ahead, stay alert the entire time, and keep your mind fully present.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How often do I walk on autopilot — and what might I be stepping on, literally or figuratively, without noticing?

## SUTRA 24.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ईदियत्थे विवज्जित्ता, सज्झायं जयं जई ।
तम्मुत्ती तप्पुरक्कारे, उवउत्ते रियं रिए ॥२४.८॥

⚠️ Some characters may differ slightly from the PDF edition.

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

īdiyatthe vivajjittā, sajjhāyaṃ jayaṃ jaī |
tammuttī tappurakkāre, uvaütte riyaṃ rie || 24.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Avoiding the objects of the senses, devoted to study, and with full awareness — the restrained one walks with proper carefulness.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **īdiyatthe** = the objects of the senses (sense pleasures)
- **vivajjittā** = having avoided, having renounced
- **sajjhāyaṃ** = in study, in scriptural recitation (svadhyaya)
- **jayaṃ** = vigilant, careful
- **jaī** = the restrained one, the disciplined monk
- **tammuttī** = devoted to that (the teaching)
- **tappurakkāre** = giving priority to that (carefulness)
- **uvaütte** = with proper awareness
- **riyaṃ** = walking, movement
- **rie** = practises, observes

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the section on walking carefulness by addressing the monk's inner state during movement. The five sense objects — beautiful sights, pleasing sounds, fragrant smells, delicious tastes, and pleasant touches — are the great distractors. A monk who is attracted to these while walking loses his awareness and may step on a living being without noticing. Therefore, the first instruction is to turn away from sense attractions. The second instruction is to keep the mind engaged in study or recitation of scripture — an occupied mind does not wander toward distractions. The third is to give priority to carefulness itself, making it the primary activity rather than an afterthought. When all these come together, walking becomes a meditative practice rather than a mere physical activity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

While walking, ignore distractions, keep your mind on higher things, and make carefulness your top priority — then every step becomes a spiritual act.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What distracts me most when I am trying to be mindful? Can I make awareness my priority rather than an afterthought?

## SUTRA 24.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कोहे माणे य मायाए, लोभे य उवउत्तया ।
हासे भए मोहरिए, विगहासु तहेव य ॥२४.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

kohe māṇe ya māyāe, lobhe ya uvaüttayā |
hāse bhae moharie, vigahāsu taheva ya || 24.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Anger, pride, deceit, greed, laughter, fear, delusion, and quarrelling — these are the occasions that corrupt speech.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kohe** = in anger (krodha)
- **māṇe** = in pride (mana)
- **ya** = and
- **māyāe** = in deceit (maya)
- **lobhe** = in greed (lobha)
- **ya** = and
- **uvaüttayā** = the occasions, the causes
- **hāse** = in laughter, humour
- **bhae** = in fear
- **moharie** = in delusion, infatuation
- **vigahāsu** = in quarrels, disputes
- **taheva ya** = and likewise

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse begins the second form of carefulness — carefulness in speech. Before describing how to speak correctly, the text first identifies the eight root causes of harmful speech. When a person speaks from anger, the words become weapons. When speaking from pride, the words become dismissive or condescending. When speaking from deceit, the words become lies. When speaking from greed, the words become manipulative. Laughter leads to mockery and hurtful jokes. Fear leads to false promises or cowardly silence. Delusion leads to confused and misleading statements. Quarrelling leads to aggressive, divisive language. A monk must recognize these eight emotional states as the enemies of proper speech. The moment any of these arises in the mind, the mouth should remain closed. This is the negative instruction — what to avoid. The positive instruction follows in the next verse.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Eight emotions ruin your speech: anger, pride, deceit, greed, mockery, fear, confusion, and the urge to argue. Recognize them before you open your mouth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of these eight states most often drives my speech? What would change if I paused before speaking from that state?

## SUTRA 24.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवाइं अट्ठु ठाणाइं, परिवज्जिहु संजए ।
असावज्जं मियं काले, भासं भासिज्ज पण्णवं ॥२४.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

evāiṃ aṭṭhu ṭhāṇāiṃ, parivajjihu saṃjae |
asāvajjaṃ miyaṃ kāle, bhāsaṃ bhāsijja paṇṇavaṃ || 24.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Avoiding these eight causes, the restrained one should speak words that are blameless, measured, timely, and wise.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evāiṃ** = these, such
- **aṭṭhu** = eight
- **ṭhāṇāiṃ** = causes, occasions, grounds
- **parivajjihu** = should avoid, should abandon
- **saṃjae** = the restrained one
- **asāvajjaṃ** = blameless, faultless, non-sinful
- **miyaṃ** = measured, limited, moderate
- **kāle** = at the right time, timely
- **bhāsaṃ** = speech, words
- **bhāsijja** = should speak
- **paṇṇavaṃ** = wise, informed, knowledgeable

### 5. COMMENTARY

After identifying the eight enemies of proper speech, this verse gives the positive standard. The restrained person's speech must meet four criteria simultaneously. First, it must be blameless — meaning it should not lead to harm, sin, or suffering for anyone. Second, it must be measured — not excessive, not too little, just enough to convey what is needed. Third, it must be timely — spoken at the appropriate moment, not when it would cause disruption or when the listener cannot receive it. Fourth, it must be wise — informed by knowledge and understanding, not by ignorance or impulse. These four qualities transform speech from a potential source of harm into a tool for truth and compassion. Notice the order: first remove the faults (the eight causes), then cultivate the virtues (the four qualities). This is the complete practice of carefulness in speech.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

After avoiding the eight harmful emotions, speak words that are harmless, measured, well-timed, and wise — nothing more, nothing less.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Does my speech consistently meet all four standards — harmless, measured, timely, and wise? Which one do I most often fail at?

## SUTRA 24.11

आहारं उदगं गेण्हे, समणो संजए सया ।

The restrained monk should always accept food and water only after thoroughly inspecting and verifying them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **āhāraṃ** = food
- **udagaṃ** = water
- **geṇhe** = accepts, takes
- **samaṇo** = the monk, the ascetic
- **saṃjae** = restrained, disciplined

This verse introduces the third form of carefulness — seeking food and water. A monk does not earn money or cook for himself; he depends on the householder community for sustenance. But this dependence does not mean blind acceptance. Every morsel of food and every drop of water must be inspected before consumption. The inspection has multiple dimensions: Was the food specifically prepared for the monk? (If so, it cannot be accepted — that would mean someone laboured and potentially harmed living beings specifically for him.) Was it obtained honestly? Is it free from living organisms? Is the quantity appropriate? This careful inspection is not paranoia — it is the practical expression of the principle that a monk must never be the cause of harm, even indirectly. The food a monk eats must be "clean" in every sense — physically, ethically, and karmically.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk must carefully inspect every bit of food and water before accepting it, making sure no harm was caused in getting it to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How aware am I of the chain of actions that brings my food to my plate? Does that awareness change how I eat?

## SUTRA 24.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उग्गमुप्पायणं पढमे, बीए सोहेज्ज एसणं ।
परिभोयम्मि चउक्कं, विसोहेज्ज जयं जई ॥२४.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

uggamuppāyaṇaṃ paḍhame, bīe sohejja esaṇaṃ |
paribhoyammi caükkaṃ, visohejja jayaṃ jaī || 24.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

First, one should inspect the origin and preparation. Second, the seeking itself. In consumption, one should verify the fourfold purity — the vigilant, restrained one.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **uggamuppāyaṇaṃ** = origin (uggama/udgama) and preparation (utpādana)
- **paḍhame** = first, primarily
- **bīe** = secondly
- **sohejja** = should inspect, should purify
- **esaṇaṃ** = the seeking, the food-quest itself
- **paribhoyammi** = in consumption, while eating
- **caükkaṃ** = the fourfold (inspection)
- **visohejja** = should verify, should thoroughly inspect
- **jayaṃ** = vigilant
- **jaī** = the restrained one

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse lays out the systematic procedure for food inspection. The first step is to check the origin and preparation: where did this food come from, and how was it made? If it was cooked specifically for the monk, it is unacceptable. If living beings were harmed in its preparation, it is unacceptable. saṃraṃbha samāraṃbhe, āraṃbhe ya taheva ya |
kāyaṃ pavattamāṇaṃ tu, ṇiyattejja jayaṃ jaī || 24.25 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In preparation, commencement, and execution — the vigilant, restrained one should restrain the body from unnecessary action.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **saṃraṃbha** = preparation
- **samāraṃbhe** = commencement
- **āraṃbhe** = execution
- **kāyaṃ** = the body
- **pavattamāṇaṃ** = being set in motion
- **ṇiyattejja** = should restrain
- **jayaṃ** = vigilant
- **jaī** = the restrained one

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse reinforces the three-stage restraint of bodily activity. The repetition is intentional — it drives home the point that body-control requires constant vigilance at every stage of action. In the context of the chapter's structure, this verse may apply specifically to the combination of carefulness and self-control in daily monastic life: even while practising the five forms of carefulness (which require some physical activity), the three forms of self-control must operate simultaneously. The monk must be active enough to walk, speak, eat, handle, and dispose — but restrained enough to do only what is strictly necessary and nothing more. This balance between active carefulness and passive restraint is the essence of the eight mothers of the teaching.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Keep repeating the lesson: catch every unnecessary action at its root — in the thought, in the first movement, and in the act itself.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Am I willing to repeat a difficult lesson until it becomes second nature, or do I assume one hearing is enough?

## SUTRA 24.26

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयाओ पंच समिईओ, चरणस्स य पवत्तणे ।
गुत्ती णियत्तणे वुत्ता, असुभत्थेसु सव्वसो ॥२४.२६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

eyāo paṃca samiīo, caraṇassa ya pavattaṇe |
guttī ṇiyattaṇe vuttā, asubhatthesu savvaso || 24.26 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The five forms of carefulness are for proper conduct in action. The three forms of self-control are for complete withdrawal from all unwholesome things.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **eyāo** = these
- **paṃca** = five
- **samiīo** = samitis (forms of carefulness)
- **caraṇassa** = of conduct, of practice
- **ya** = and
- **pavattaṇe** = in engagement, in active practice
- **guttī** = guptis (forms of self-control)
- **ṇiyattaṇe** = in withdrawal, in restraint, in cessation
- **vuttā** = are stated, are declared
- **asubhatthesu** = from unwholesome things, from inauspicious objects
- **savvaso** = in all ways, completely

### 5. COMMENTARY

This crucial verse draws the fundamental distinction between the two categories of practice. The five forms of carefulness govern how the monk acts when action is necessary — they are about doing things right. The three forms of self-control govern restraint from all that is unwholesome — they are about not doing what is harmful. This distinction is vital: a spiritual path cannot be only about restraint (that would lead to paralysis), nor only about careful action (that would leave harmful impulses unchecked). Both are needed. The five forms of carefulness ensure that the monk's necessary activities — walking, speaking, eating, handling, disposing — are done without causing harm. The three forms of self-control ensure that the monk's instruments of engagement — mind, speech, body — are not deployed for unnecessary or harmful purposes. Together, they form the complete discipline: act when needed, but only with full awareness; restrain yourself from everything else.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Carefulness is about doing necessary things the right way. Self-control is about not doing unnecessary things at all. You need both.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In my own life, am I stronger at doing things carefully or at restraining myself from doing things unnecessarily? Which needs more attention?

## SUTRA 24.27

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एसा पवयणमाया, जे सम्मं आवरे मुणी ।
सो खिप्पं सव्वसंसारा, विप्पमुच्चइ पंडिए ॥ ति बेमि ॥२४.२७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

esā pavayaṇamāyā, je sammaṃ āvare muṇī |
so khippaṃ savvasaṃsārā, vippamuccai paṃḍie || ti bemi || 24.27 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

This is the mother of the teaching. The sage who properly observes it is quickly liberated from the entire cycle of rebirth — thus I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **esā** = this
- **pavayaṇamāyā** = pravachana-mata — the mother of the teaching
- **je** = whoever, the one who
- **sammaṃ** = properly, rightly, correctly (samyak)
- **āvare** = observes, guards, practises
- **muṇī** = the sage, the monk
- **so** = he
- **khippaṃ** = quickly, swiftly
- **savvasaṃsārā** = from the entire cycle of rebirth
- **vippamuccai** = is liberated, is freed
- **paṃḍie** = the wise one
- **ti bemi** = thus I say (the traditional closing formula of Mahavira's teachings)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The final verse brings the entire chapter to its conclusion with a powerful promise. The eight practices described in this chapter — five forms of carefulness and three forms of self-control — are not merely rules of conduct. They are the very foundation that gives birth to liberation. The word "quickly" is significant: it suggests that these practices, when followed properly and completely, are the most direct path to freedom. The closing formula "thus I say" marks these as the words of the enlightened teacher himself, lending them the highest authority. The chapter began by calling these eight practices the "mothers" of the teaching, and it ends by declaring their ultimate fruit: complete liberation from the endless cycle of birth and death. A mother gives life; these eight practices give spiritual life — the life that never ends.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Follow these eight foundational practices properly, and you will be freed from the cycle of suffering — this is the direct promise of the enlightened teacher.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If liberation is the promise, what is my excuse for not practising these foundations with my full heart?
