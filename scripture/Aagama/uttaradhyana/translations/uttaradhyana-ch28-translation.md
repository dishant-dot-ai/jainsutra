# Uttaradhyana Sutra — Chapter 28
# Mokshamarg Gati (Movement on the Path of Liberation)

Aḍhāvīsamuṃ Adhyayana

---

## Introduction

This chapter is named "Mokshamarg Gati" — the movement on, and journey along, the path of liberation. It provides a comprehensive and systematic exposition of the four pillars of liberation as taught by the Jinas: Right Knowledge, Right Perception, Right Conduct, and Right Austerity. The chapter unfolds these four tools in sequence — defining knowledge and its five types, then the six substances and nine realities, then the ten dispositions of Right Perception, then the five types of Right Conduct, and finally the two categories of Right Austerity. The final verses show how these four work in concert to burn away karmas and bring the soul to complete liberation. This is one of the most philosophically dense and complete chapters of the Uttaradhyana Sutra.

---

## SUTRA 28.1

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मोक्खमग्ग-गाई तच्चं, सुणेह जिणभासियं ।
चाउकारण-संजुत्तं, णाण दंसण लक्खणं ॥२८.१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

mokkhamagg-gāī taccaṃ, suṇeha jiṇabhāsiyaṃ |
cāukāraṇa-saṃjuttaṃ, ṇāṇa daṃsaṇa lakkhaṇaṃ || 28.1 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Listen to the truth of movement on the path of liberation — spoken by the Jina, combined with four causes, and characterized by knowledge and perception.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Jiṇabhāsiyaṃ = spoken by the Jina Lord
- Cāukāraṇasaṃjuttaṃ = combined with four causes (right knowledge, right perception, right conduct, right austerity)
- Ṇāṇadaṃsaṇalakkhaṇaṃ = characterized by knowledge and perception
- Taccaṃ = the truth, the reality
- Mokkhamagg-gāī = movement on the path of liberation
- Suṇeha = listen

**5. COMMENTARY**

This opening verse sets the stage for the entire chapter. The word "suṇeha" — "listen" — is an invocation of attention: what follows is not speculation or philosophy for its own sake, but the direct teaching of the Jina, the liberated omniscient one. The seeker is asked to hear this with full awareness.

The path of liberation is defined immediately by its four causes: right knowledge, right perception, right conduct, and right austerity. These are not four separate paths but four interlocking dimensions of a single transformative journey. The Jain tradition has often summarized the path of liberation in this fourfold formula, known as the "Moksha-marg Chatushṭaya" — and this verse opens the chapter by anchoring that formula in its source: the teaching of the Jinas.

The phrase "ṇāṇa daṃsaṇa lakkhaṇaṃ" — "characterized by knowledge and perception" — is significant. Of the four causes, knowledge and perception are called the characteristic markers. This is because they are the two spiritual qualities that are intrinsic to the soul itself; they are the soul's own nature. Conduct and austerity are their expression and implementation in action and discipline. The soul, by its very essence, is a being of knowing and seeing — and the path of liberation is simply the restoration and purification of those essential capacities.

The Gujarati commentary notes that various philosophical traditions have described the path to liberation in different ways. The sutra emphasizes "jiṇabhāsiyaṃ" — spoken by the Jina — to establish that this fourfold path is complete, verified, and supremely authentic: it comes from the omniscient ones who have themselves walked it to its end.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The Jina taught us that the road to freedom has four essential parts: knowing rightly, seeing rightly, living rightly, and practicing discipline.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I walking all four dimensions of the path — or am I strong in knowledge while neglecting to live by it?

## SUTRA 28.2

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणं च दंसणं चेव, चरित्तं च तवो तहा ।
एस मग्गो ति पण्णत्तो, जिणेहिं वरदंसीहिं ॥२८.२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇaṃ ca daṃsaṇaṃ ceva, carittaṃ ca tavō tahā |
esa maggō ti paṇṇattō, jiṇehiṃ varadaṃsīhiṃ || 28.2 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Knowledge, perception, conduct, and austerity — this has been proclaimed as the path by the Jinas, the supreme seers.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Varadaṃsīhiṃ = supremely perceptive, omniscient
- Jiṇehiṃ = by the Jineśvaras (the Jina Lords)
- Ṇāṇaṃ = knowledge
- Daṃsaṇaṃ = perception
- Carittaṃ = conduct
- Tavō = austerity
- Esa = this
- Maggō = path
- Paṇṇattō = proclaimed, declared

**5. COMMENTARY**

Where the previous verse introduced the formula, this verse confirms it with full authority: the omniscient Jinas — called "varadaṃsīhiṃ," the supreme seers, those who see everything — have proclaimed this fourfold formula as the path itself.

The word "varadaṃsī" is rich. "Vara" means supreme or excellent; "daṃsī" means one who sees. The Jinas see reality as it is, completely and without distortion. Their proclamation therefore carries absolute epistemic weight — it is not an opinion or a tradition but a report from those who have seen the truth directly.

The verse uses the word "maggō" — path. A path is not the destination; it is the way to the destination. The teaching here is that liberation is reached by walking — by actively engaging with knowledge, perception, conduct, and austerity. These four are not goals in themselves but instruments. A seeker who studies them without walking them has learned the map but not traveled the journey.

Jain commentators note that this verse pairs beautifully with verse 3: together they form a complete teaching — this is the path (verse 2), and souls who walk it reach liberation (verse 3). The structure is: definition, confirmation of source, and result.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The all-knowing Jinas declared that the four-part path of knowledge, right belief, good conduct, and discipline is the only real road to freedom.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I trust a teaching, am I trusting it because of who taught it — or because I have tested it in my own experience?

## SUTRA 28.3

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणं च दंसणं चेव, चरित्तं च तवो तहा ।
एयं मग्गमणुपत्ता, जीवा गच्छंति सुग्गई ॥२८.३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇaṃ ca daṃsaṇaṃ ceva, carittaṃ ca tavō tahā |
eyaṃ maggamaṇupattā, jīvā gacchanti suggaī || 28.3 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Souls who follow the path of knowledge, perception, conduct, and austerity attain liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Eyaṃ = this
- Maggaṃ = path
- Aṇupattā = having followed, having practiced, having walked
- Jīvā = souls
- Suggaī = liberation, the auspicious state, the liberated realm
- Gacchanti = attain, reach, go toward

**5. COMMENTARY**

This third verse completes the opening triad of the chapter by stating the result of walking the path: souls attain liberation. The word "suggaī" — literally "sugati," the auspicious destination — refers here to the state of final liberation, the liberated realm of Siddha souls.

The construction "maggamaṇupattā" — "having followed the path" — is deliberate. The root "anu + pattā" means to walk behind, to follow, to practice in order. The path must be followed step by step, in its proper sequence and fullness. A partial or fragmented engagement with only one of the four limbs is not sufficient.

The commentary notes that the complete destruction of all eight types of karma — which is liberation — happens through the combined power of these four. Knowledge illuminates the nature of reality; perception establishes right belief in it; conduct stops new karma from entering; and austerity burns away the karmas already accumulated. Each has its specific function, and none can be replaced by another.

These first three verses are sometimes recited together as a summary declaration of the entire Jain path. Their structure — proclamation, confirmation, result — mirrors the teaching process itself: here is the truth, here is who teaches it, here is what it accomplishes. The rest of the chapter unfolds each limb in careful philosophical detail.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Souls who genuinely follow all four parts of the path — knowledge, right belief, good conduct, and discipline — actually reach freedom.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I following the path as a seeker, or merely learning about it as a student?

## SUTRA 28.4

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तत्थ पंचविहं णाणं, सुयं आभिणिबोहियं ।
ओहिणाणं तु तइयं, मणणाणं च केवलं ॥२८.४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tattha paṃcavihaṃ ṇāṇaṃ, suyaṃ āphiṇibōhiyaṃ |
ohiṇāṇaṃ tu taiyaṃ, maṇaṇāṇaṃ ca kevalaṃ || 28.4 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Among these, knowledge has five types: scriptural knowledge, sensory-cognitive knowledge, clairvoyance as the third, mind-reading knowledge, and omniscience.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Tattha = among these (the four causes)
- Paṃcavihaṃ = five types
- Ṇāṇaṃ = knowledge
- Āphiṇibōhiyaṃ = Abhinibodhika (Mati) — sensory-cognitive knowledge
- Suyaṃ = Shruta — scriptural knowledge
- Taiyaṃ = third
- Ohiṇāṇaṃ = Avadhi — clairvoyance
- Maṇaṇāṇaṃ = Manahparyava — mind-reading knowledge
- Kevalaṃ = Kevala — omniscience

**5. COMMENTARY**

Having established the fourfold path, the chapter now unpacks each limb in detail, beginning with knowledge. Knowledge — the soul's primary capacity — is classified into five types based on how the soul accesses reality: the medium through which knowing occurs, the range of what can be known, and the degree of directness of the knowing.

The first type, Abhinibodhika (also called Mati), is the knowledge that arises through the five senses and the mind. It includes four sub-stages: initial contact (avagaha), speculation (īhā), perceptual judgment (avāya), and retention (dhāraṇā). This is the most common form of knowing — how we experience and understand the world around us through our sensory apparatus.

The second type, Shruta, is scriptural knowledge — knowledge gained through language, words, texts, and the transmission of teachings. Mati and Shruta are both "mediated" — they depend on external means (senses, language) and are therefore subject to the limitations of those media.

The third type, Avadhi, is clairvoyance — direct knowledge of physical forms and matters beyond the normal range of the senses. It is a more refined, partially direct form of knowing that some souls develop through the partial removal of the knowledge-obscuring karma.

The fourth type, Manahparyava, is the ability to directly read the mental states and thoughts of other beings within a limited range. It is more refined than Avadhi and available only to highly disciplined ascetics.

The fifth type, Kevala, is omniscience — the direct, complete, unmediated knowledge of all substances, all qualities, and all modifications, across all three times. It is the natural state of the liberated soul, the full flowering of the soul's knowledge-nature. The first four types are found in worldly souls in varying degrees; Kevala is the perfection toward which all the others point.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are five ways a soul can know: through the senses, through scriptures, through a supernatural inner vision, through reading minds, and finally through total all-at-once knowing of everything.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of the five types of knowing, which am I relying on most — and which am I neglecting?

## SUTRA 28.5

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एयं पंचविहं णाणं, दव्वाण य गुणाण य ।
पज्जवाणं च सव्वेसिं, णाणं णाणीहिं देसियं ॥२८.५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

eyaṃ paṃcavihaṃ ṇāṇaṃ, davvāṇa ya guṇāṇa ya |
pajjavāṇaṃ ca savvesiṃ, ṇāṇaṃ ṇāṇīhiṃ desiyaṃ || 28.5 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This five-fold knowledge — the knowledge of all substances, qualities, and their modifications — has been described by the wise.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Eyaṃ = this
- Paṃcavihaṃ = five types
- Ṇāṇaṃ = knowledge
- Davvāṇa = of substances
- Guṇāṇa = of qualities
- Savvesiṃ = of all
- Pajjavāṇaṃ = of modifications, modes
- Ṇāṇaṃ = knowledge
- Ṇāṇīhiṃ = by the wise, by knowers
- Desiyaṃ = taught, described

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse explains what knowledge is of — its object. The five types of knowledge described in the previous verse all point toward the same reality: substances (dravya), their qualities (guṇa), and their modifications (paryāya). These three categories — substance, quality, mode — form the basic ontological framework of Jain metaphysics.

A substance is the fundamental unit of reality — that which has qualities and undergoes modifications but persists through them. A quality is a permanent, essential attribute that stays with the substance always. A modification (or mode) is the changing, momentary expression of the substance as it transforms through time.

The purpose of this verse is to ground knowledge in reality. Knowledge is not a self-referential loop, not mere mental activity for its own sake. It is the soul's capacity to know what is real — and what is real is organized into substances, qualities, and modes. The wise (ṇāṇīhiṃ) who have described this system are the Jinas and the great scholarly teachers who followed them.

For the spiritual seeker, the implication is practical: the purpose of cultivating right knowledge is to see reality clearly — to understand what the soul is, what the non-soul is, what karma is and how it works, and what liberation looks like. All of this is organized within the structure of substances, qualities, and modes. The next several sutras will detail the six substances and nine realities, giving that structure its content.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The knowledge taught by the wise covers everything that exists: what things are made of, what qualities they have, and how they change.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When I learn about the world, am I learning about the surface — or am I inquiring into the deeper structure of what things truly are?

## SUTRA 28.6

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गुणाणमासओ दव्वं, एगदव्विस्सिया गुणा ।
लक्खणं पज्जवाणं तु, उभओ अस्सिया भवे ॥२८.६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

guṇāṇamāsao davvaṃ, egadavvissiyā guṇā |
lakkhaṇaṃ pajjavāṇaṃ tu, ubhao assiyā bhave || 28.6 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Substance is the foundation of qualities; qualities reside in only one substance; the characteristic of modes is that they depend on both substance and quality.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Davvaṃ = substance
- Guṇāṇaṃ = of qualities
- Āsao = basis, foundation, support
- Egadavvissiyā = residing in one substance only
- Guṇā = qualities
- Pajjavāṇaṃ = of modes, modifications
- Lakkhaṇaṃ = characteristic, mark
- Ubhao = both (substance and quality)
- Assiyā = dependent on, residing in
- Bhave = are

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse defines the exact relationship between the three basic categories of Jain ontology: substance (dravya), quality (guṇa), and mode (paryāya).

Substance is the primary reality — it is what exists. Qualities are not independent entities; they always belong to a specific substance and cannot float free of it. A quality of knowledge, for instance, belongs to the soul — it cannot detach from the soul and exist independently. This is the meaning of "egadavvissiyā" — qualities reside in one substance only. A quality of one substance cannot simultaneously be a quality of a different substance.

Modes, however, have a more complex relationship: they depend on both the substance and the quality. A modification of the soul's knowledge quality (say, gaining new understanding) involves both the substance (the soul itself) and its quality (knowledge). The mode is the changing expression; the quality and substance together are what undergoes that change.

This ontological framework is essential for understanding karma: karma is a type of substance (pudgala) that binds to the soul (another substance), modifying the soul's qualities. Liberation is the process of freeing the soul from those alien substances so its own qualities — knowledge, perception, bliss, energy — can shine in their full and natural state.

The Gujarati commentary explains that qualities are of two kinds: permanent (sahabhāvī) and changing (kramabhāvī). Permanent qualities always accompany the substance; changing qualities are its modifications over time. This distinction clarifies why the soul's knowledge and bliss, though currently obscured, are never destroyed — they are permanent qualities of the substance that is the soul.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Things (substances) have permanent qualities that belong to them, and those things also change over time — those changes are called modes.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What are the unchanging qualities of my own soul — and how much of what I experience is only a temporary modification, not my true nature?

## SUTRA 28.7

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मो अहम्मो आगासं, कालो पुग्गल जंतवो ।
एस लोगो ति पण्णत्तो, जिणेहिं वरदंसीहिं ॥२८.७॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammō ahammō āgāsaṃ, kālō puggala jaṃtavō |
esa lōgō ti paṇṇattō, jiṇehiṃ varadaṃsīhiṃ || 28.7 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The medium of motion, the medium of rest, space, time, matter, and souls — these six substances constitute the universe, as proclaimed by the omniscient Jinas.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Dhammō = Dharmāstikāya (medium of motion)
- Ahammō = Adharmāstikāya (medium of rest)
- Āgāsaṃ = Ākāśāstikāya (space)
- Kālō = Kāla (time)
- Puggala = Pudgalāstikāya (matter)
- Jaṃtavō = Jīvāstikāya (souls)
- Esa = this
- Lōgō = the universe
- Paṇṇattō = proclaimed
- Jiṇehiṃ varadaṃsīhiṃ = by the omniscient Jinas

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse presents the Jain model of the universe in its most compact form: six substances. Everything that exists — all of reality — is composed of, or belongs to, one of these six categories.

Dharmāstikāya is not "dharma" in the ethical sense but the medium that assists the motion of souls and matter. Like water assists the movement of fish without pushing them, Dharmāstikāya passively enables motion without causing it. Adharmāstikāya is its counterpart — the medium that assists rest and stability. Ākāśāstikāya is infinite space, which provides room for all other substances to exist. Kāla is time, the substance that governs change and transformation. Pudgala is matter — everything physical, including the karmic particles that bind to souls. And Jīvāstikāya is the totality of souls.

What is most striking about this model is its precision and exhaustiveness. There is no category for God as a separate creator or ruler; the universe operates through these six substances according to their own natures and interactions. The soul's bondage and liberation are entirely explicable through these six without invoking any external agency.

For the spiritual seeker, understanding the six substances is not merely philosophical — it reveals exactly what the soul is among all existing things, what karma is, and how the soul's relationship with karma works. This knowledge is the foundation of the entire path. The omniscient Jinas who proclaimed this model had seen it directly and completely; their formulation is therefore offered here as a reliable map of the real.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Everything in the universe — all that exists — is made up of six things: the medium of movement, the medium of rest, space, time, matter, and souls.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If I understand that I am a soul among six substances — not the center of the universe, but one among many souls — how does that change the way I relate to the world around me?

## SUTRA 28.8

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

धम्मो अहम्मो आगासं, दव्वं इक्किक्कमाहियं ।
अणंताणि य दव्वाणि, कालो पुग्गल जंतवो ॥२८.८॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

dhammō ahammō āgāsaṃ, davvaṃ ikkikkamāhiyaṃ |
aṇaṃtāṇi ya davvāṇi, kālō puggala jaṃtavō || 28.8 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The medium of motion, the medium of rest, and space — each of these is one; time, matter, and souls are infinite in number.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Davvaṃ = substance
- Ikkikkamāhiyaṃ = each is one (singular)
- Aṇaṃtāṇi = infinite
- Davvāṇi = substances

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse adds a crucial numerical clarification to the six substances introduced in the previous verse. Three of the six — the medium of motion, the medium of rest, and space — are singular: there is exactly one Dharmāstikāya, one Adharmāstikāya, and one Ākāśāstikāya. They are each vast, pervasive, and undivided.

The other three — time, matter, and souls — are infinite in number. There are innumerable time-units (each moment of time can be considered a separate Kāla particle in some formulations). Matter (Pudgala) consists of infinite atoms and their combinations. And souls — Jīvas — are infinite in number, each one a distinct and eternal entity, none ever truly merged with or destroyed by another.

This distinction has profound spiritual implications. The soul is not alone in the universe, but it is definitively singular and unique. Each of the infinite souls is its own complete entity with its own karma, its own path, its own trajectory toward liberation or continued bondage. No soul can liberate another; no soul carries the burden of another. This is the basis for the Jain understanding that liberation is an individual attainment — each soul must walk its own path.

The singularity of space also implies that all substances — souls and matter alike — coexist within one infinite space. Bound souls and liberated souls, gross matter and subtle karmic matter, all exist within this one infinite space. Liberation is not going to a "place" outside the universe; it is the purified soul rising to the topmost part of that one infinite space.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The medium of movement, the medium of rest, and space each exist as single, all-pervading realities; but time, matter, and souls exist in infinite numbers.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If every soul is a completely separate, unique entity — responsible for its own liberation — what does that mean for how I live today?

## SUTRA 28.9

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गई लक्खणो उ धम्मो, अहम्मो ठाण लक्खणो ।
भायणं सव्वदव्वाणं, णहं ओगाह लक्खणं ॥२८.९॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

gaī lakkhaṇo u dhammō, ahammō ṭhāṇa lakkhaṇō |
bhāyaṇaṃ savvadavvāṇaṃ, nahaṃ ōgāha lakkhaṇaṃ || 28.9 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The medium of motion is characterized by its assistance to movement; the medium of rest is characterized by its assistance to stability; space is the container of all substances and is characterized by providing accommodation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Gaī-lakkhaṇō = characterized by motion/movement assistance
- Dhammō = Dharmāstikāya
- Ṭhāṇa-lakkhaṇō = characterized by rest/stability assistance
- Savvadavvāṇaṃ = of all substances
- Bhāyaṇaṃ = container, vessel
- Nahaṃ = sky, Ākāśāstikāya (space)
- Ōgāha-lakkhaṇaṃ = characterized by providing space, accommodation

**5. COMMENTARY**

Having listed the six substances, the chapter now describes the specific characteristics of each, beginning with three of the non-soul substances.

The Dharmāstikāya's characteristic is its assistance to motion. Like water that allows fish to swim — it does not push the fish, but without it the fish could not move at all — Dharmāstikāya provides the passive medium for souls and matter to move. It is not a cause of motion; it is the enabling condition. An important clarification: souls and matter move by their own nature and energy; Dharmāstikāya simply does not obstruct them.

The Adharmāstikāya's characteristic is its assistance to rest. Like a shaded tree that allows a weary traveler to rest — it does not compel rest but enables it — Adharmāstikāya is the passive medium for stability. Without it, souls and matter could not maintain any stable state.

Space (Ākāśāstikāya) provides accommodation — it is the infinite container within which all the other substances exist and move. Like a vessel that holds its contents without being identical to any of them, space pervades all and provides room for all. Space itself is divided into the "inhabited" region (where souls and matter and the other substances exist) and the "uninhabited" region beyond it (pure empty space).

These three substances are described through their function rather than their physical appearance, because they are formless. They cannot be seen or touched; they are known through reasoning and the testimony of the omniscient. The Jain teaching here is that the universe has an infrastructure — invisible supporting conditions — without which the movement and rest of souls and matter would be impossible.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The medium of motion helps things move, the medium of rest helps things stay still, and space gives room for everything to exist.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What in my life provides me the conditions I need to grow and move forward — and am I grateful for it?

## SUTRA 28.10

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

वत्तणा लक्खणो कालो, जीवो उवओग-लक्खणो ।
णाणेणं दंसणेणं च, सुहेण य दुहेण य ॥२८.१०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

vattaṇā lakkhaṇō kālō, jīvō uvōga-lakkhaṇō |
ṇāṇeṇaṃ daṃsaṇeṇaṃ ca, suheṇa ya duheṇa ya || 28.10 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Time is characterized by transformation; the soul is characterized by consciousness — and the soul is known through knowledge, perception, happiness, and suffering.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Vattaṇā-lakkhaṇō = characterized by change/transformation
- Kālō = time
- Uvōga-lakkhaṇō = characterized by consciousness/awareness
- Jīvō = soul
- Ṇāṇeṇaṃ = by knowledge
- Daṃsaṇeṇaṃ = by perception
- Suheṇa = by happiness
- Duheṇa = by suffering

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse introduces the characteristics of the remaining two substances: time and the soul.

Time (Kāla) is characterized by transformation — its function is to facilitate change in all substances. The Gujarati commentary explains that time is the substance that makes the new seem new, the old seem aged, the small grow large, the warm become cool. It enables all change in the other substances. Interestingly, time itself is formless and reckoned from the Adīpa (the middle world) outward; beyond the Adīpa's cosmic boundaries, the conventional reckoning of night and day does not apply.

The soul (Jīva) is characterized by consciousness (upayoga). This is the single most distinctive feature of the soul — the one quality that no other substance possesses. Consciousness means the capacity to know and to see: knowledge-consciousness (jñānopayoga) and perception-consciousness (darśanopayoga). These two are the soul's intrinsic activities.

Practically, the soul is known — recognized and understood — through four expressions: knowledge (what it knows), perception (how it sees), happiness (what it experiences as pleasant), and suffering (what it experiences as painful). These four are the empirical windows through which the soul's existence can be confirmed even by beings who cannot perceive it directly.

The connection between time and the soul is subtle: it is time that governs the soul's stages — its current state of bondage, its progress through the stages of spiritual development, and ultimately its liberation. A soul liberated from karma no longer has the "changing" aspect that time modulates; it exists in the unchanging fullness of its nature.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Time is what makes everything change; the soul is recognized by the fact that it is conscious — it knows, perceives, feels joy, and feels pain.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

If consciousness is the one thing that distinguishes my soul from everything else in the universe, how am I honoring and developing that capacity?

## SUTRA 28.11

वीरियं उवओगो य, एयं जीवस्स लक्खणं ॥२८.११॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇaṃ ca daṃsaṇaṃ ceva, carittaṃ ca tavō tahā |
vīriyaṃ uvōgō ya, eyaṃ jīvassa lakkhaṇaṃ || 28.11 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity, energy, and consciousness — these are the specific characteristics of the soul.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Vīriyaṃ = energy, spiritual power, inner strength
- Uvōgō = consciousness, awareness
- Jīvassa = of the soul
- Lakkhaṇaṃ = characteristic, defining quality

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse extends the description of the soul's characteristics. While the previous verse identified consciousness as the soul's primary mark, this verse lists a fuller set of six qualities that together constitute the soul's nature: knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity, energy, and consciousness.

This sixfold list is remarkable for what it includes. The first four — knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity — are precisely the four pillars of the path of liberation introduced at the start of this chapter. This is not coincidental. The teaching here is that the path of liberation is not something imposed on the soul from outside; it is a restoration of the soul's own nature. The soul is intrinsically a being of knowledge, perception, right orientation, and inner discipline. Liberation is simply the full expression of what the soul already is.

Energy (vīrya) is the soul's inherent power and vitality — its capacity to act, to strive, to resist the pull of karma. In the bound state, this energy is partly suppressed by energy-obscuring karma; in liberation, it is fully manifest.

Consciousness (upayoga) is the soul's most foundational quality — the pure capacity to know and see.

None of these qualities belong to any other substance. Matter does not know; space does not feel; time does not perceive. Only the soul is characterized by knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity, energy, and consciousness. This is what makes the soul unique among the six substances — and it is this uniqueness that makes liberation possible.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul is recognized by six things: it can know, perceive, conduct itself rightly, practice discipline, exert spiritual energy, and is fundamentally conscious.

Which of these six qualities of my soul do I feel most connected to right now — and which feels most covered over or suppressed?

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

saīṃdhayār ujjōy, phabhā chāyātave i vā |

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Sound, darkness, light, luster, shadow, heat/sunlight, and color, smell, taste, touch — these are the characteristics of matter.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Sad = sound
- Aṃdhayār = darkness
- Ujjōy = light
- Phabhā = luster, brilliance
- Chāyā = shadow
- Ātave = heat, sunlight, warmth
- Vaṇṇa = color

This verse lists the observable characteristics of matter (Pudgala). In Jain thought, matter is not limited to what we ordinarily call "physical stuff" — it includes everything that has color, smell, taste, and touch, including things we might not normally think of as material, such as sound, darkness, and shadow.

Sound arises when pudgala particles are set in vibration by contact; it is therefore material. Darkness is not merely the absence of light but itself a particular form of pudgala — the Gujarati commentary explains that just as light has a warm, bright quality, darkness has its own cool, dark-colored quality as a modification of matter. Luster, shadow, and heat are similarly all modifications or expressions of pudgala.

The four primary qualities of matter — color, smell, taste, and touch — are present in every material particle. These are the qualities by which matter is known through the senses. They are the objects of sensory knowledge (mati-jñāna) and the raw material of our experiential world.

The significance of listing matter's characteristics at this point in the chapter is that it helps complete the map of the six substances. The soul is defined by consciousness; matter is defined by color, smell, taste, touch, and their derivatives. Karma — which is a form of matter — binds to the soul not because the soul wills it, but because the soul's passionate states attract karmic matter to it. Understanding the nature of matter helps the seeker understand what karma actually is and how it works.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Matter is everything that has color, smell, taste, or touch — including sound, darkness, light, shadow, and heat.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

## SUTRA 28.13

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एगत्तं च पुहत्तं य, संखा संठाणमेव य ।
संजोगा य विभागा य, पज्जवाणं तु लक्खणं ॥२८.१३॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

egattaṃ ca puhatttaṃ ya, saṃkhā saṃṭhāṇameva ya |
saṃjōgā ya vibhāgā ya, pajjavāṇaṃ tu lakkhaṇaṃ || 28.13 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Unity, separateness, number, shape, combination, and division — these are the characteristics of modes.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Egattaṃ = oneness, unity, coming together
- Puhatttaṃ = separateness, diversity, splitting apart
- Saṃkhā = number, count
- Saṃṭhāṇa = shape, form, configuration
- Saṃjōgā = combination, union, joining
- Vibhāgā = separation, division, parting
- Pajjavāṇaṃ = of modes, modifications
- Lakkhaṇaṃ = characteristic

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse completes the description of the three ontological categories — substance, quality, mode — by defining the characteristics of modes (paryāya). Modes are the changing, momentary expressions of substances as they transform through time.

The six characteristics listed here are all forms of change: things come together (union), they separate (division); they take on a particular count (number), a particular shape (form); and these processes are further described as movements toward oneness (egattaṃ — the coming together of particles to form a compound) or toward multiplicity (puhatttaṃ — the breaking apart into constituents).

The Gujarati commentary gives the example of clay: many particles of clay come together to form a pot (union, shape, number). Over time the pot breaks (division); the pieces scatter (multiplicity). Yet through all these modifications, the underlying substance (clay/matter) remains the same. The clay atoms did not cease to exist; they changed their mode of expression.

For the spiritual path, this understanding of modes is essential. The soul in its current state of bondage is in a particular mode — a mode characterized by ignorance, passion, and karma-bondage. But modes change. The soul is capable of moving through the stages of spiritual development to a purified mode. Liberation is not the creation of something new in the soul; it is the soul arriving at its own natural mode — its modification in its purest, unobscured expression.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Modes are how things change — things come together, split apart, take on different shapes and numbers — but the underlying substance doesn't disappear.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

## SUTRA 28.14

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवाजीवा य बंधो य, पुण्णं पावासवो तहा ।
संवरो णिज्जरा मोक्खो, संतेए तहिया णव ॥२८.१४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

jīvājīvā ya baṃdhō ya, puṇṇaṃ pāvāsavō tahā |
saṃvarō ṇijjarā mōkkhō, saṃtēe tahiyā ṇava || 28.14 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Soul, non-soul, merit, sin, influx, stopping, shedding, bondage, and liberation — these are the nine realities.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Jīvā = soul (Jīva)
- Ajīvā = non-soul (Ajīva)
- Baṃdhō = bondage (Bandha)
- Puṇṇaṃ = merit (Puṇya)
- Pāva = sin (Pāpa)
- Āsavō = influx of karma (Āśrava)
- Saṃvarō = stopping of karma influx (Saṃvara)
- Ṇijjarā = shedding/burning of karma (Nirjarā)
- Mōkkhō = liberation (Mokṣa)
- Ṇava = nine
- Tahiyā = realities/truths (tattvas)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse presents the nine realities (nava tattvas) — the nine fundamental categories through which Jain philosophy understands the entire spiritual situation of the soul. These nine are not arbitrary; together they constitute a complete description of what the soul is, what has happened to it, and how it can be freed.

Soul (Jīva) is the conscious, knowing entity — the subject of the entire spiritual journey. Non-soul (Ajīva) is everything that is not the soul: matter, space, time, the medium of motion and rest. The soul is enmeshed with non-soul — particularly with karmic matter — and the entire spiritual project is the disentanglement of these two.

Merit (Puṇya) and sin (Pāpa) are the two types of karma — those that produce pleasant results and those that produce unpleasant results. Both are forms of bondage; both must ultimately be shed.

Influx (Āśrava) is the process by which karmic matter flows into and attaches to the soul. This happens through the soul's activities — mental, verbal, and physical — when driven by passion. Stopping (Saṃvara) is the blocking of new karmic influx through the great vows, discipline, and restraint. Shedding (Nirjarā) is the burning away of already-accumulated karmas through austerity and other practices.

Bondage (Bandha) is the actual attachment of karma to the soul — the state of being bound. Liberation (Mokṣa) is the complete removal of all karma, leaving the soul in its natural, pure state.

These nine realities form the philosophical scaffold of the entire path: understand the soul; understand what is not the soul; understand how they got entangled (influx, bondage); understand what created the entanglement (merit and sin); understand how to stop the entanglement (stopping); understand how to remove what has already accumulated (shedding); and understand what complete freedom looks like (liberation).

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Jain teaching describes nine key realities: the soul, everything that is not the soul, good actions, bad actions, how karma enters the soul, how to stop karma entering, how to remove karma already there, the state of being bound, and the state of being completely free.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of these nine realities, which do I understand most clearly — and which do I still experience as distant or abstract?

## SUTRA 28.15

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तहियाणं तु भावाणं, सभावे उवएसणं ।
भावेण सद्दहंतस्स, सम्मत्तं तं वियाहियं ॥२८.१५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tahiyāṇaṃ tu bhāvāṇaṃ, sabhāve uvaeṣaṇaṃ |
bhāveṇa saddhaṃtassa, sammattaṃ taṃ viyāhiyaṃ || 28.15 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

To believe naturally or through instruction in the true existence of these nine realities — that has been declared by the Jineśvaras as right perception.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Tahiyāṇaṃ = of these realities (the nine)
- Bhāvāṇaṃ = of the entities, substances
- Sabhāve = in their true mode of existence
- Uvaeṣaṇaṃ = through instruction by another
- Bhāveṇa = naturally, by one's own inner disposition
- Saddhaṃtassa = of one who believes/has faith
- Sammattaṃ = right perception, right faith (samyaktva)
- Viyāhiyaṃ = declared, spoken of

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse defines right perception (samyaktva) — the second of the four pillars of the path. And it defines it in a beautifully simple way: right perception is genuine, heartfelt belief in the nine realities as they truly are.

Two modes of arriving at this belief are acknowledged: through one's own natural inclination (perhaps from spiritual merit of past lives, or through inner awakening), or through instruction from another — a teacher, a text, a guru. Both are valid. The verse emphasizes "bhāveṇa" — naturally, from within — because even when instruction is received from outside, it must ultimately land in the heart for it to become genuine right perception.

The Gujarati commentary distinguishes right perception from mere intellectual knowledge: knowing the nine realities is not the same as believing in them with full inner conviction. Right perception involves a quality of faith that goes beyond cerebral understanding — it is an inner orientation of the soul toward the truth. The soul, having recognized the truth about its own nature and situation, aligns itself with that truth.

This is why right perception is described as the most important of the four pillars. Without it, knowledge remains academic, conduct remains mechanical, and austerity remains physical. Right perception is the inner transformation that gives all the other limbs their true force. The commentary from later verses (28.29–28.30) will make explicit that without right perception, none of the other three can bear fruit.

The significance of the phrase "as they truly are" (sabhāve) should not be overlooked. Right perception is not blind faith; it is accurate faith — belief in things as they actually exist. Believing in false things with great conviction is not right perception; it is error. The entire intellectual content of this chapter — the six substances, the nine realities — is the content of right perception.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Right belief means genuinely trusting in your heart that the nine spiritual realities — the soul, karma, liberation, and the rest — are truly real.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Do I believe in the soul's liberation as a living possibility for myself — or is it something I accept intellectually but haven't yet felt as real?

This verse names the ten types of "ruchi" — inclinations or dispositions — through which right perception can arise and manifest. The subsequent sutras (17–27) define each of the ten in detail. The verse also reiterates "sayameva" — by oneself, on one's own — to distinguish this from the next type (Upadesha-ruchi), which involves receiving instruction from another. In Nisarga-ruchi, there is no external instruction; the alignment with truth is entirely self-arising.

One who, upon receiving instruction from a Jineśvara or another non-omniscient teacher, believes in these same realities — should be known as having instructional right faith.

- Daṃsaṇe = in right perception
- Bhaiyavvaṃ = conduct is found, is included
- Sammatta-carittāīṃ = right perception and right conduct
- Jugavaṃ = simultaneously, together
- Puvvaṃ = first, earlier
- Sammattaṃ = right perception

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse establishes a crucial relationship between the second and third pillars of the path: right perception and right conduct. The verse makes three related points.

First: without right perception, right conduct does not exist. This does not mean that disciplined behavior is impossible without right perception — obviously, people can behave well without spiritual insight. But "right conduct" in the Jain sense is a specific, spiritually charged practice that stops the influx of new karma and purifies the soul. That kind of conduct can only function when it is grounded in genuine right perception. Without the inner orientation of right perception, external discipline remains at the level of societal virtue or personal cultivation, not liberation-oriented practice.

Second: conduct is "found within" right perception. This is a subtle point. Right perception is not merely a belief held quietly in the mind; when it is genuinely present, it naturally expresses itself in how one lives. Genuine right perception carries within it the orientation and motivation for right conduct. The Gujarati commentary explains that right perception already contains the seed of right conduct as its natural expression.

Third: the temporal relationship between them. In most cases, right perception comes first and right conduct follows — one must first have the inner orientation before the outer discipline takes root. However, there are exceptional cases where a soul can attain both simultaneously (as when a soul moves very rapidly through the stages of spiritual development). The verse acknowledges both possibilities without making one absolute.

This teaching has important practical implications: spending all one's effort on external conduct without cultivating right perception is a spiritual error. The inner work of right perception is the indispensable foundation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

You can't have truly transformative conduct without genuine belief — right belief naturally leads to right conduct, and usually comes first.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Is my spiritual practice grounded in genuine inner conviction — or am I going through motions without the inner alignment that gives them real power?

## SUTRA 28.30

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णादंसिणस्स णाणं, णाणेण विणा ण हुंति चरणगुणा ।
अगुणिस्स णत्थि मोक्खो, णत्थि अमोक्खस्स णिव्वाणं ॥२८.३०॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇādaṃsiṇissa ṇāṇaṃ, ṇāṇeṇa viṇā ṇa huṃti caraṇaguṇā |
aguṇissa ṇatthi mōkkhō, ṇatthi amōkkhassa ṇibbāṇaṃ || 28.30 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

One without right perception cannot have right knowledge; without right knowledge, the qualities of right conduct do not arise; without the qualities of right conduct, there is no liberation; without liberation, there is no complete liberation.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Adaṃsiṇissa = of one without right perception
- Ṇāṇaṃ = right knowledge
- Ṇa = not
- Ṇāṇeṇa = by right knowledge
- Viṇā = without
- Na huṃti = do not arise
- Caraṇaguṇā = qualities of right conduct
- Aguṇissa = of one without conduct qualities
- Natthī = there is no
- Mōkkhō = liberation (freedom from karma)
- Amōkkhassa = of one without liberation from karma
- Nibbāṇaṃ = complete liberation, nirvāṇa, the siddha state

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is one of the most logically precise verses in the Uttaradhyana Sutra. It presents a fourfold causal chain: right perception → right knowledge → right conduct → liberation → complete liberation. Each step depends on the previous one; break any link and the chain fails.

Without right perception, even a learned person's knowledge does not become "right knowledge" in the liberating sense. Their information about the nine realities remains information — intellectual content — rather than living knowledge aligned with and serving the soul's liberation. The Gujarati commentary explains: a person with false belief may know many things, but their knowing lacks the inner alignment that makes knowledge a tool of liberation.

Without right knowledge, the qualities of right conduct cannot genuinely arise. Right conduct is not merely good behavior; it is a specific set of qualities — restraint, non-violence, non-attachment — that emerge when knowledge has illuminated what they are for and why they matter. Without that illumination, what looks like right conduct is only conventional virtue.

Without the qualities of right conduct, liberation from karma does not occur. Karma-bondage is broken precisely by the conduct that stops new karma from entering and austerity that burns old karma away. Without conduct, this does not happen.

And without liberation from karma (moksha), complete liberation (nirvāṇa — the siddha state of perfect, permanent freedom) cannot be attained. Nirvāṇa is the state in which all karma is gone, permanently. It presupposes moksha — the progressive liberation — as its precondition.

This verse is the philosophical core of the chapter: a rigorous, sequential logic showing why all four pillars of the path are necessary, and why right perception is foundational to all of them.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Without right belief, knowledge goes wrong; without right knowledge, good conduct doesn't form; without good conduct, there is no freedom from karma; without that freedom, complete liberation cannot happen.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which link in this chain is weakest for me right now — and what would it take to strengthen it?

## SUTRA 28.31

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णिस्संकिय णिक्कंखिय, णिव्वितिगिच्छा अमूढदिट्ठी य ।
उवबूह थिरीकरणे, वच्छल्ल पभावणे अट्ठु ॥२८.३१॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇissaṃkiya ṇikkaṃkhiya, ṇibbvitigicchā amūḍhadiṭṭhī ya |
uvabūha thirīkaraṇe, vacchall pabhāvaṇe aṭṭhu || 28.31 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Freedom from doubt, freedom from desire for other faiths, freedom from disgust in dharma's fruits, non-deluded perception, praising the virtuous, stabilizing wavering souls, affectionate care for co-practitioners, and propagation of the faith — these eight are the qualities of right perception.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Ṇissaṃkiya = Niḥśaṃkita — freedom from doubt in the nine realities and in the Jina's teaching
- Ṇikkaṃkhiya = Niḥkāṃkṣita — freedom from desire/expectation of rewards from other faiths
- Ṇibbvitigicchā = Nirvicikitsā — freedom from disgust or doubt regarding the fruits of dharma
- Amūḍhadiṭṭhī = Amūḍhadṛṣṭi — non-deluded perception; not being deluded by false teachers or false dharmas
- Uvabūha = Upabṛṃhaṇa — praising, fostering the qualities of virtuous souls
- Thirīkaraṇe = Sthirikāraṇa — stabilizing those who are wavering in their dharma practice
- Vacchall = Vātsalya — warm, affectionate love for fellow practitioners
- Pabhāvaṇe = Prabhāvanā — propagation of the Jina's teaching, spreading the light of dharma
- Aṭṭhu = eight

**5. COMMENTARY**

This verse lists the eight qualities (aṅgas) of right perception — the practical, behavioral, and attitudinal dimensions of what authentic right perception looks like in a seeker's life. These eight qualities are not prerequisites for right perception; they are its expressions and accompaniments. When right perception is genuine, these eight arise naturally.

Freedom from doubt (Niḥśaṃkita) is the absence of uncertainty about the nine realities and the Jina's teaching. The seeker with genuine right perception does not waver between belief and skepticism about the fundamental truths. Doubt about individual philosophical questions is natural and healthy; the doubt that right perception eliminates is doubt about the foundation itself.

Freedom from desire for other faiths (Niḥkāṃkṣita) means the seeker is not drawn to other religious systems in search of better results. This is not contempt for other traditions — it is contentment and confidence in one's own path. The seeker does not practice dharma while secretly hoping that some other system might offer a better route to liberation.

Freedom from disgust about dharma's fruits (Nirvicikitsā) addresses a subtle temptation: seeing the hardships of a disciplined spiritual life and doubting that it is worth it. The seeker with this quality trusts that the results of dharma — stopping and shedding karma — are real, even when not immediately visible.

Non-deluded perception (Amūḍhadṛṣṭi) is the quality of not being fooled by false teachers, false miracles, or corrupted forms of dharma. The Gujarati commentary identifies several types of delusion to avoid: being attracted to powerful deities (devamūḍhatā), following ascetics who violate their vows (gurumūḍhatā), following practices that involve violence or falsehood in the name of dharma (dharmamūḍhatā), and following popular but philosophically incorrect practices (lokmūḍhatā).

Praising the virtuous (Upabṛṃhaṇa) means actively celebrating and honoring the qualities of those who are practicing dharma sincerely — rather than diminishing, criticizing, or envying them.

Stabilizing the wavering (Sthirikāraṇa) means supporting those who are losing their grip on the path — through encouragement, teaching, and compassionate engagement — to help them find their footing again.

Affectionate care (Vātsalya) is the warm, selfless love that a practitioner feels for fellow seekers on the path — the sense of being part of a family of souls walking together toward liberation.

Propagation (Prabhāvanā) is spreading the Jina's teaching — through speaking, writing, modeling, and any other means — so that more souls have access to the light of the path.

These eight qualities together describe a seeker who is not only personally convinced but actively nurturing — of their own faith, of others' faith, and of the broader culture of dharma in the world.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Right belief shows itself in eight ways: no inner doubt, no attraction to other paths, trust in dharma's results, clear perception not fooled by fakes, celebrating good people, supporting those who are struggling, loving your fellow practitioners, and spreading the light of the teaching.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Of these eight qualities, which am I living most fully — and which reveals where I still have growing to do?

## SUTRA 28.32

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सामाइयत्थ पढमं, छेदोवट्ठावणं भवे बीयं ।
परिहारविसुद्धीयं, सुहुमं तह संपरायं च ॥२८.३२॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

sāmāiyattha padhamaṃ, chedovaṭṭhāvaṇaṃ bhave bīyaṃ |
parihāravisuddhīyaṃ, suhumaṃ taha saṃparāyaṃ ca || 28.32 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The first is equanimity conduct, the second is restoration conduct, the third is purification-through-avoidance conduct, and the fourth is subtle-passions conduct.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Padhamaṃ = first
- Sāmāiyaṃ = Sāmāyika — equanimity conduct
- Bīyaṃ = second
- Chedovaṭṭhāvaṇaṃ = Cchedopasthāpanīya — restoration conduct
- Bhave = is
- Parihāravisuddhīyaṃ = Parihāra Viśuddhi — purification-through-avoidance conduct (third)
- Suhumaṃ = subtle
- Saṃparāyaṃ = Saṃparāya — subtle passions conduct (fourth)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The chapter now moves to the third pillar of the path: right conduct. This verse names four of the five types of right conduct practiced by advanced ascetics. The fifth — the highest — is named in the next verse.

The first type, Sāmāyika conduct, is the conduct of equanimity — the abandonment of all harmful activities and the maintenance of a state of equal, undisturbed awareness. It is the broadest form of right conduct, available in principle to all practitioners. The Gujarati commentary distinguishes two forms: temporary Sāmāyika (for set periods) and lifelong Sāmāyika (maintained from initiation to death).

The second type, Cchedopasthāpanīya (restoration conduct), is the conduct in which, after a breach of the great vows, the practitioner undergoes a formal restoration — their previous vow-period is "cut" (ccheda) and renewed through re-initiation. This ensures that the integrity of the vow-life is maintained even after failures.

The third type, Parihāra Viśuddhi (purification-through-avoidance conduct), is a highly specialized form practiced by groups of nine ascetics. They engage in extremely rigorous austerity over an 18-month cycle, carefully rotating roles to ensure proper practice and support. It results in the purification of many karmas.

The fourth type, Sūkṣma Saṃparāya (subtle-passions conduct), occurs when three of the four major passions have been fully subdued and only the subtlest form of greed remains. This is the conduct of the 10th stage of spiritual development.

These four types represent a progression from broad to increasingly refined: from the general equanimity of all ascetics to the rarefied purity of the advanced spiritual stages. Each type represents a more complete stopping of karma and a more transparent expression of the soul's natural conduct-quality.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

There are four main stages of ascetic conduct: basic equanimity, formal restoration of vows after a lapse, a special group-austerity practice, and a very refined state where almost all passions have been subdued.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

What is my current "conduct stage" — and what would it mean for me to advance to the next level of inner purity?

The fifth and highest type of right conduct, Yathākhyāta (conduct as it truly should be — fully expressed, fully natural), is described here. The Gujarati commentary uses a vivid metaphor: just as a drain empties a reservoir, right conduct drains the reservoir of karmas stored in the soul. The five types correspond to the five stages in which this draining progressively intensifies.

The final phrase — "āhiyaṃ" — "thus Jineśvara has declared" — returns us to the authority of the teaching. This is not a personal philosophical opinion; this is the testimony of those who walked this path fully and saw it from the other side: the omniscient beings for whom all five stages of conduct have been lived and transcended.

## SUTRA 28.34

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तवो य दुविहो वुत्तो, बाहिरभंतरो तहा ।
बाहिरो छव्विहो वुत्तो, एवमभंतरो तवो ॥२८.३४॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

tavō ya duviho vuttō, bāhirabhāṃtarō tahā |
bāhirō chabbivihō vuttō, evaṃ abhaṃtarō tavō || 28.34 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Austerity is of two types — external and internal — and external austerity has six types; similarly, internal austerity also has six types.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Tavō = austerity
- Duviho = two types
- Vuttō = stated, said
- Bāhira = external
- Bhaṃtarō = internal
- Chabbivihō = six types
- Evaṃ-abhaṃtarō = similarly, internal austerity also

**5. COMMENTARY**

The fourth pillar — right austerity — is introduced here. The chapter's Gujarati commentary notes that austerity is a distinctive instrument of liberation that supplements the other three: it actively burns away karmas already accumulated in past lives. While right conduct stops new karma from entering, austerity burns old karma that has already attached.

External austerity (bāhya tapa) refers to practices that can be observed from outside — primarily involving the body and its needs. The six types of external austerity are: (1) Fasting (anaśana) — complete abstention from food; (2) Eating less than one's fill (avamodaryā) — taking less than the body desires; (3) Restrictive begging (bhikṣācaryā) — accepting food only under specific conditions; (4) Abandoning taste (rasaparityāga) — giving up tasty, rich foods; (5) Bodily hardship (kāyakleśa) — sitting in fixed postures, tolerating heat and cold; (6) Withdrawal from external objects (pratisamlinayā) — pulling the senses inward, away from worldly stimuli.

Internal austerity (ādhyātmika tapa) refers to practices that work primarily at the inner level: (1) Atonement (prāyaścitta) — purifying transgressions; (2) Respect and humility (vinaya) — showing reverence to those worthy of it, which removes karmas through right conduct; (3) Service to the worthy (vaiyāvṛtya) — serving advanced practitioners; (4) Study (svādhyāya) — disciplined study and recitation of scriptures; (5) Meditation (dhyāna) — stabilizing the mind on a single pure object; (6) Abandonment of the body (vyutsarga) — progressive release of attachment to the physical body.

These twelve forms of austerity together provide a complete physical and inner technology for karma-removal. The external forms primarily address bodily attachment and sense-indulgence; the internal forms address the mental and emotional dimensions of karma-generation. Together they are comprehensive.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Spiritual discipline has two sides: outer practices like fasting and limiting food, and inner practices like atonement, study, meditation, and serving others — each has six types, twelve in all.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Which of the twelve forms of austerity — outer or inner — am I most neglecting in my life right now?

## SUTRA 28.35

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणेण जाणइ भावे, दंसणेण य सद्दहे ।
चरित्तेण णिगिण्हाइ, तवेण परिसुज्झइ ॥२८.३५॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

ṇāṇeṇa jāṇaī bhāve, daṃsaṇeṇa ya saddahe |
carittteṇa ṇigiṇhāī, taveṇa parisujjhāi || 28.35 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Through knowledge the soul knows the realities; through perception it has faith in them; through conduct it stops the influx of karma; through austerity it becomes purified.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Ṇāṇeṇa = by knowledge
- Bhāve = the realities, the substances
- Jāṇaī = knows
- Daṃsaṇeṇa = by right perception
- Saddahe = has faith
- Charittteṇa = by right conduct
- Nigiṇhāī = stops, restrains (the influx of karma)
- Taveṇa = by austerity
- Parisujjhāi = becomes purified, is cleansed (of accumulated karma)

**5. COMMENTARY**

This is the synthesis verse of the entire chapter — perhaps of the entire Jain path in its most concentrated form. It assigns a specific function to each of the four pillars, showing precisely how each one operates in the soul's liberation.

Knowledge knows. Perception believes. Conduct stops. Austerity purifies.

Each verb is carefully chosen. Knowledge (jñāna) operates through "jāṇaī" — knowing, understanding, seeing clearly. It is the cognitive function: the soul apprehends reality as it is through the light of knowledge. Perception (darśana) operates through "saddahe" — has faith, holds with conviction. It is the confirmatory function: the soul does not merely know intellectually but commits internally to the truth of what it knows.

Conduct (cāritra) operates through "nigiṇhāī" — stops, holds back, restrains. Its specific function is Saṃvara — the stopping of new karma from entering the soul. This is the defensive function of the path: closing the gates through which karma has been flowing. Austerity (tapa) operates through "parisujjhāi" — becomes purified. Its specific function is Nirjarā — the burning away and shedding of karma already accumulated. This is the cleansing function.

The Gujarati commentary offers a beautiful analogy: consider a person who wants to recover from a serious illness. They need to (1) understand the nature of the illness (knowledge), (2) genuinely believe in the diagnosis and the cure (perception), (3) stop doing what causes the illness to worsen (conduct as Saṃvara), and (4) take the medicine that burns away the illness already present (austerity as Nirjarā). All four are necessary; any one alone is insufficient.

This verse reveals something profound: the path of liberation is not arbitrary. Each pillar has a precise, non-redundant function. Knowledge illuminates; perception commits; conduct prevents; austerity purifies. These four together constitute a complete system for the soul's transformation.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

Knowledge tells you what is true; belief makes you commit to it; right conduct stops new karma coming in; and discipline removes karma already there — these four work together like a complete treatment.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

Am I using all four tools — or am I relying on one or two while neglecting the others?

## SUTRA 28.36

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

खवित्ता पुव्वकम्माइं, संजमेण तवेण य ।
सव्वदुक्ख पहीण्टा, पक्कमंति महेसिणो ॥ ति बेमि ॥२८.३६॥

**2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION**

khavittā puvvakammāīṃ, saṃjameṇa taveṇa ya |
savvadukkha pahīṇṭā, pakkamanti mahesiṇō || ti beṃi || 28.36 ||

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The great sages, through restraint and austerity, destroy all previously accumulated karmas, become free from all suffering, and attain the liberated state — thus I declare.

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

- Mahesiṇō = the great sages (mahāṛṣis)
- Saṃjameṇa = through restraint, right conduct
- Taveṇa = through austerity
- Puvvakammāīṃ = previously accumulated karmas
- Khavittā = having destroyed, having eliminated
- Savvadukkha-pahīṇṭā = free from all suffering, having shed all suffering
- Pakkamanti = move forward, attain, proceed to the liberated state
- Ti beṃi = thus I say (the traditional closing declaration of the Uttaradhyana Sutra)

**5. COMMENTARY**

The final verse brings the entire chapter — and the entire teaching of the path of liberation — to its culminating point: the great sages, who have walked this path fully, stand as the proof and the promise of everything that has been taught.

"Mahesiṇō" — the great sages — are those who have completed the journey. Through restraint (the comprehensive term for right conduct) and austerity (the burning away of accumulated karma), they have destroyed every karma — the vast accumulation from countless past lives — and arrived at complete freedom.

"Savvadukkha-pahīṇṭā" — free from all suffering — is the description of the liberated state. All suffering, without exception, arises from the relationship between the soul and karma. When karma is completely gone, suffering is completely gone. What remains is the soul in its natural state: infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, infinite energy. The liberation that the entire path has been building toward is simply the full expression of what the soul has always been, at its core.

"Pakkamanti" — they move forward, they proceed — captures the momentum of the liberated soul. Liberation is not a passive state of exhaustion after a long struggle; it is the soul's natural movement into its own pure being. The image is one of joyful, unobstructed forward motion.

"Ti beṃi" — "thus I say" — is the traditional closing declaration of a verse or section of the Uttaradhyana Sutra. It marks the completion of the teaching and carries the implication: I have seen this, I have walked this path, I am reporting what is true. The authority behind these words is the teaching lineage traced back to the liberated Jinas themselves.

The Gujarati commentary closes with a medical analogy that mirrors the four pillars: just as a sick person must (1) understand their illness through the doctor's knowledge, (2) trust the doctor's diagnosis, (3) take the prescribed medicine, and (4) follow dietary and lifestyle regulations — so the soul must engage all four pillars of the path. To seek liberation without any one of them is as futile as seeking recovery while ignoring part of the treatment. The soul that engages all four, with sincerity and sustained effort, will reach the freedom that the great sages have demonstrated is real.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

The great sages who followed this path completely — through discipline and inner purification — destroyed all their accumulated karma, left all suffering behind, and attained perfect freedom. This is what the path leads to.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

The great sages showed that this path actually leads to freedom. Do I believe this deeply enough to live as though it is true — starting today?
