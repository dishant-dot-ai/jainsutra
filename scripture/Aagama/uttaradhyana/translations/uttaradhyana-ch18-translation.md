## SUTRA 18.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कंपिल्ले णयरे राया, उदिण्ण बलवाहणे ।
णामेणं संजए णामं, मिगव्वं उवणिग्गए ॥१८.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Kaṃpille ṇayare rāyā, udiṇṇa balavāhaṇe |
Ṇāmeṇaṃ sañjae ṇāmaṃ, migavvaṃ uvaṇiggae || 18.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the city of Kāmpilya, the renowned King Sañjaya, endowed with a vast army and many vehicles, went out one day for a hunt.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**udiṇṇa balavāhaṇe** = endowed with an extensive army, elephants, horses and vehicles; **ṇāmeṇaṃ sañjae ṇāmaṃ** = named Sañjaya; **migavvaṃ** = for hunting; **uvaṇiggae** = went out (from the city)

### 5. COMMENTARY

King Sañjaya was the famous ruler of Kāmpilyanagara in the Pañcāla country, rich in military might and royal pomp. Ancient kings and even ordinary people in those times were fond of hunting. This final-bodied Sañjaya Rajārṣi was himself a hunting enthusiast. One day he set out from his city for a hunt, surrounded by the full splendour of his four-fold army.

📷 Visual Aid: A powerful king on horseback departing through the gates of a walled city, surrounded by the banners and dust of a vast army.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: A powerful king named Sañjaya rode out from his grand city one day to go hunting in the forest with his enormous army surrounding him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your life do you ride out surrounded by outer pomp — unaware that a life-changing encounter may be waiting just ahead?

## SUTRA 18.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

हयाणीए गयाणीए, रहाणीए तहेव य ।
पायत्ताणीए महया, सव्वओ परिवारिए ॥१८.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Hayāṇīe gayāṇīe, rahāṇīe taheva ya |
Pāyattāṇīe mahayā, savvao parivārie || 18.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He was surrounded on all sides by a great cavalry, elephant corps, chariot division, and infantry.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**hayāṇīe** = cavalry, the regiment of horses; **gayāṇīe** = elephant corps; **rahāṇīe** = the great chariot convoy; **pāyattāṇīe** = infantry, the regiment of foot soldiers; **mahayā** = in great numbers; **savvao parivārie** = surrounded on all sides

### 5. COMMENTARY

The four-fold army (caturańgī senā) consisting of horses, elephants, chariots, and infantry completely encircled the king as he moved toward the hunting ground. This royal grandeur sets the stage — a man at the peak of worldly power, about to encounter something that will shatter his attachment to all of it.

📷 Visual Aid: A bird's-eye view of a king at the center of concentric rings of cavalry, elephant corps, chariots, and infantry all moving through a landscape.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: King Sañjaya went hunting accompanied by thousands of soldiers on horseback, on elephants, in chariots, and on foot — the full might of his kingdom around him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of the "army" that surrounds you in life — your status, resources, followers — do you genuinely need, versus what merely feeds your sense of importance?

## SUTRA 18.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मिए छुहित्ता हयगओ, कंपिल्लुज्जाण केसरे ।
भीए संते मिए तत्थ, वहेइ रसमुच्छिए ॥१८.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Mie chuhittā hayagao, kaṃpillujjāṇa kesare |
Bhīe saṃte mie tattha, vaheī rasamucchie || 18.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Riding on horseback, addicted to the taste of flesh, he disturbed and pursued deer in the Keśara garden of Kāmpilya, shooting the frightened and exhausted animals with arrows.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**kaṃpillujjāṇa kesare** = in the Keśara garden of Kāmpilyanagara; **rasamucchie** = intoxicated with the taste of meat; **mie** = deer; **chuhittā** = disturbing them, causing pain; **bhīe** = frightened; **saṃte** = exhausted; **vaheī** = began to kill

### 5. COMMENTARY

Mounted on horseback and intoxicated by craving for the taste of animal flesh, the king drove terrified deer through the royal garden of Keśara and slaughtered them. The Gujarati commentary notes that in ancient times both kings and common people indulged in hunting. Yet the text's imagery is stark — a man of power hunting the helpless, pleasure-drunk, oblivious to the suffering he causes. This is the state of the soul lost in sense-craving.

📷 Visual Aid: A king on horseback charging through a royal garden, bow drawn, as terrified deer scatter in all directions.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: King Sañjaya, drunk on the pleasure of the hunt, was chasing and killing frightened deer in the royal garden — completely absorbed in craving and violence.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In what ways does your craving for sensory pleasure — food, entertainment, stimulation — make you numb to the harm it causes to others?

## SUTRA 18.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह केसरम्मि उज्जाणे, अणगारे तवोधणे ।
सज्झायज्झाण संजुत्ते, धम्मज्झाणं झियायइ ॥१८.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha kesarammi ujjāṇe, aṇagāre tavodhane |
Sajjhāyajjhāṇa saṃjutte, dhammajjhāṇaṃ jhiyāyai || 18.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that very time in the Keśara garden, a houseless ascetic, rich in austerity, absorbed in scriptural study and meditation, was engaged in religious contemplation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**aha** = at that very time; **tavodhane** = rich in austerity, a tapodhanī; **aṇagāre** = a houseless monk (Gardabhāli by name, per commentary); **sajjhāyajjhāṇa saṃjutte** = absorbed in svādhyāya (scriptural study) and dhyāna (meditation); **dhammajjhāṇaṃ** = dharmadhyāna (righteous meditation); **jhiyāyai** = was engaged in

### 5. COMMENTARY

In the same garden where violence raged, a tapodhanī ascetic — identified in the commentary as Gardabhāli — sat in perfect stillness, absorbed in dharmadhyāna. The contrast is complete: the king in full sensory frenzy; the monk in perfect inner stillness. These two human possibilities — outer conquest and inner conquest — meet in one space. The text invites us to see which is the nobler pursuit.

📷 Visual Aid: Split scene in a garden — on one side a frenzied king on horseback chasing animals, on the other a monk sitting motionless in meditation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Right there in the same garden where the king was hunting and killing, a holy monk named Gardabhāli sat completely still and peaceful, deep in meditation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you stood in that garden — driven by craving on one side, perfected stillness on the other — which would you be drawn toward?

## SUTRA 18.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्फोवमंडवम्मि, झायइ खवियासवे ।
तस्सागए मिए पासं, वहेइ से णराहिवे ॥१८.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Apphovamandavammi, jhāyai khaviyāsave |
Tassāgae mie pāsaṃ, vaheī se ṇarāhive || 18.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who destroys karmic influx was meditating in a bower of vines and creepers, and beside him the king's arrows drove wounded deer that fled to his side.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**khaviyāsave** = one who destroys āsravas (karmic influxes); **apphovamandavammi** = in a natural bower made of creepers and vines; **jhāyai** = was meditating; **tassa pāsaṃ** = beside him; **āgae** = came; **ṇarāhive** = by the king; **vaheī** = shot, wounded

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk sat in a simple bower of wild vines, engaged in the supreme work of destroying all karmic influx. The wounded and terrified deer, shot by the king's arrows, fled to the monk's very side — as if seeking refuge beside the one being in that space who had renounced all harm. The image is powerful: the one who harms drives the victims toward the one who protects.

📷 Visual Aid: A monk seated in a leafy vine-bower meditating, with wounded deer collapsed or huddled at his side.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk was meditating quietly in a leafy shelter in the garden, and the deer that the king shot and wounded ran and collapsed right next to him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do the wounded in your world gravitate toward you — or away from you?

## SUTRA 18.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह आसगओ राया, खिप्पमागम्म सो तिहिं ।
हए मिए उ पासित्ता, अणगारं तत्थ पासइ ॥१८.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha āsagao rāyā, khippamāgamma so tihiṃ |
Hae mie u pāsittā, aṇagāraṃ tattha pāsai || 18.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, still mounted on horseback, the king quickly came to that place, saw the deer he had struck, and also saw the monk sitting there in meditation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**āsagao** = mounted on horseback; **khippaṃ** = quickly; **āgamma** = having come; **hae mie** = the wounded deer; **pāsittā** = having seen; **u** = at the same moment; **aṇagāraṃ tattha** = also the monk sitting right there; **pāsai** = saw

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king, still full of the energy of the hunt, rode swiftly to where his arrows had driven the deer — and was suddenly confronted with two images at once: the wounded animals he had caused to suffer, and the silent, unmoving monk in meditation beside them. This double vision — harm done and holiness present — was the first crack in his heart.

📷 Visual Aid: A king on horseback pulling up short, staring at a stillness he did not expect — a monk unmoved in meditation with wounded deer lying nearby.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The king rode quickly to see the deer he had shot, but when he arrived he also saw a monk sitting completely still and peaceful right beside the wounded animals.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever arrived at the scene of your own harm and seen its full picture clearly — including what your actions look like from the outside?

## SUTRA 18.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह राया तत्थ संभंतो, अणगारो मणाऽऽहओ ।
मए उ मंदपुण्णेणं, रसगिद्धेण चंतुणा ॥१८.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha rāyā tattha saṃbhaṃto, aṇagāro maṇā'hao |
Mae u maṃdapuṇṇeṇaṃ, rasagiddhena caṃtuṇā || 18.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Seeing this, the king fell into confusion; he thought — "I, the lowly in merit, blinded by craving for taste, a violent one, have needlessly pained the heart of this monk."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**saṃbhaṃto** = fell into bewilderment, confusion; **maṇā'hao** = the monk's heart was pained, hurt; **mae u** = by me; **maṃdapuṇṇeṇaṃ** = the merit-poor one (self-reproach); **rasagiddhena** = intoxicated by craving for taste; **caṃtuṇā** = one who has become violent

### 5. COMMENTARY

The moment of moral awakening begins with confusion. The king does not immediately become enlightened — he becomes shaken. He sees his own portrait in stark terms: merit-poor, taste-addicted, violent. He feels remorse not as weakness but as the first light of self-knowledge. This is how transformation begins — not with great speeches but with honest self-reflection in a moment of confrontation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Seeing the monk, the king felt confused and ashamed — he thought to himself, "What kind of person am I? I have disturbed this holy man's peace just to satisfy my craving for meat."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When was the last time you saw yourself clearly — not as you'd like to be seen, but as your actions reveal you to be?

## SUTRA 18.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आसं विसज्जित्ताणं, अणगारस्स सो णिवो ।
विणएण वंदए पाए, भगवं एत्थ मे खमे ॥१८.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Āsaṃ visajjittāṇaṃ, aṇagārassa so ṇivo |
Viṇaeṇa vaṃdae pāe, bhagavaṃ ettha me khame || 18.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That king immediately dismounted from his horse and bowed with reverence at the monk's feet, saying: "Blessed One, please forgive me for this transgression."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**visajjittāṇaṃ** = having dismissed, descended from the horse; **ṇivo** = the king; **viṇaeṇa** = with reverence and humility; **pāe vaṃdae** = bowed at the feet; **bhagavaṃ** = O Blessed One; **ettha** = in this matter; **me** = to me; **khame** = please forgive

### 5. COMMENTARY

The king's response to remorse is immediate and humble: he dismounts (symbolically stepping down from his height of power), approaches the monk on foot, and bows at his feet asking forgiveness. A king asking a homeless monk for pardon — this reversal of worldly hierarchy reveals the king's genuine spiritual sensitivity beneath the surface of royal arrogance. The seed of dharma was always within him.

📷 Visual Aid: A king kneeling with folded hands at the feet of a seated monk, the king's horse standing riderless behind him.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The king immediately got off his horse, walked up to the monk, bowed at his feet and said, "Holy man, please forgive me for disturbing you."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there someone whose peace you have disturbed — by your craving, your carelessness, your power — from whom you owe an apology you have not yet offered?

## SUTRA 18.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह मोणेण सो भगवं, अणगारे झाणमिस्सिए ।
रायाणं ण पडिमंतेइ, तओ राया भयद्दुओ ॥१८.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha moṇeṇa so bhagavaṃ, aṇagāre jhāṇamissie |
Rāyāṇaṃ ṇa paḍimaṃteī, tao rāyā bhayadduо || 18.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

But that Blessed monk, absorbed in meditation and observing silence, gave no response to the king; at this the king grew even more fearful.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**moṇeṇa** = observing mauna (vow of silence); **jhāṇamissie** = absorbed in meditation; **ṇa paḍimaṃteī** = gave no answer, no response; **tao** = then; **bhayadduо** = became greatly frightened

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk's silence is not coldness — it is the fruit of total inner absorption. Yet the king, accustomed to being acknowledged and obeyed, interprets this silence as danger. The commentary explains that the king thought: "A tapasvin monk in anger can reduce thousands to ash with his spiritual power — and this monk has not replied, which means he must be furious with me." His fear reveals how deeply worldly power depends on recognition from others.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk did not reply at all — he stayed completely still and silent in his meditation. This made the king even more frightened, thinking the monk might be angry with him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you feel threatened or unsettled when you are met with silence? What does that reveal about your need for validation?

## SUTRA 18.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

संजओ अहमम्मीति, भगवं वाहराहि मे ।
कुद्धे तेएण अणगारे, दहेज्ज णरकोडिओ ॥१८.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sañjao ahamammīti, bhagavaṃ vāharāhi me |
Kuddhe teeṇa aṇagāre, dhahejja ṇarakoḍio || 18.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The king said: "I am Sañjaya! Blessed One, please speak with me and look at me — for a wrathful ascetic can with his own power reduce millions of people to ashes."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**sañjao ahammi** = I am Sañjaya the king; **iti** = therefore; **me vāharāhi** = please speak with me, look at me; **kuddhe** = if angered; **teeṇa** = by his own radiant power; **aṇagāre** = a monk; **ṇarakoḍio** = millions of people; **dhahejja** = can burn, can destroy

### 5. COMMENTARY

The frightened king introduces himself by name — a humble act for one of his status, but driven by self-protective fear. His words reveal both his respect for the monk's power and his worldly logic: "I am not a common man — surely you will speak with me." He genuinely believes the monk's silence is anger, and fears that the tapasvin's accumulated spiritual energy could be unleashed in destruction. This fear is ironically the teacher: it drives him to seek the monk's grace.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The scared king said to the monk, "I am King Sañjaya! Please talk to me! I know that a holy person who gets angry can destroy millions of people with their spiritual power."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your approach to spiritual life is motivated by genuine love of virtue, and how much by fear of consequences?

## SUTRA 18.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अभओ पित्थवा तुभं, अभयदाया भवाहि य ।
अिणच्चे जीवलोगम्मि, किं हिंसाए पसंजिसि ॥१८.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Abhao pitthavā tubhaṃ, abhayadāyā bhavāhi ya |
Aṇicce jīvalogammi, kiṃ hiṃsāe pasañjisi || 18.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk said: "O king, you have nothing to fear from me — now become a giver of fearlessness to others; why do you remain attached to violence in this impermanent world?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**pitthavā** = O king!; **tubhaṃ abhao** = you have nothing to fear (from me); **abhayadāyā bhavāhi** = become a giver of fearlessness to others; **aṇicce** = impermanent; **jīvalogammi** = in this world of living beings; **hiṃsāe** = in violence; **kiṃ pasañjisi** = why do you remain attached?

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk's first words dissolve the king's fear and immediately transform it into teaching. "You are safe — now make others safe." This is the essence of abhaya (fearlessness): one who has truly stopped fearing for himself naturally extends safety to all beings. The monk then poses the fundamental question: In a world where everything is impermanent (anicca), what is the point of violence? This question is the hinge on which the entire chapter turns.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The monk finally spoke and said, "You have nothing to fear from me — now go and stop scaring others too. In this temporary world where nothing lasts, why are you addicted to hurting living beings?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you knew you were completely safe — that no harm could come to you — would you still find yourself hurting others? What would that reveal?

## SUTRA 18.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया सव्वं परिच्चज्ज, गंतव्वमवसस्स ते ।
अिणच्चे जीवलोगम्मि, किं रज्जम्मि पसंजिसि ॥१८.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jayā savvaṃ paricchajja, gaṃtavvamavassassa te |
Aṇicce jīvalogammi, kiṃ rajjammi pasañjisi || 18.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"When, leaving everything behind involuntarily, you must one day depart — why do you remain attached to your kingdom in this impermanent world?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**jayā** = when; **savvaṃ** = everything; **paricchajja** = leaving right here; **avassassa** = being helpless, involuntarily; **te gaṃtavvaṃ** = you must go; **aṇicce** = impermanent; **jīvalogammi** = in this world; **rajjammi** = in the kingdom; **kiṃ pasañjisi** = why are you attached?

### 5. COMMENTARY

The monk presses deeper: not only is violence pointless, but so is all attachment to royalty. The word "avassa" (helpless/involuntary) is striking — death is not a choice; you will be compelled to leave everything. Knowing this, clinging to the kingdom is not ambition but delusion. This teaching echoes across all traditions: the wise person prepares for the inevitable departure rather than pretending it won't come.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "One day you will be forced to leave everything behind — your kingdom, your wealth, everything — so why are you so attached to your kingdom in a world where nothing lasts?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you knew you had to leave everything behind in one hour, what would you most regret having spent your life clinging to?

## SUTRA 18.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जीविंयं चेव रूवं च, विज्जुसंपाय चंचलं ।
जत्थ तं मुज्झिसि रायं, पेच्चत्थं णावबुज्झसे ॥१८.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jīviṃyaṃ ceva rūvaṃ ca, vijjusaṃpāya caṃcalaṃ |
Jattha taṃ mujjhisi rāyaṃ, peccatthaṃ ṇāvabujjhase || 18.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O king, the life and the form of the body you are attached to flash as briefly as lightning — why do you not contemplate what benefits the next world?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**rāyaṃ** = O king!; **jattha** = that which you are attached to; **jīviṃyaṃ** = this human life; **rūvaṃ** = the beauty of the body; **vijjusaṃpāya caṃcalaṃ** = as flickering as a lightning flash; **peccatthaṃ** = regarding the benefit of the next life; **ṇāvabujjhase** = you do not wake up, do not contemplate

### 5. COMMENTARY

Life flashing like lightning — this image in Prakrit carries immense force. The very thing you have staked your entire existence upon — this body, this beauty, this vitality — is as brief as a bolt of lightning in the night sky. The monk does not say this to induce despair but to awaken urgency: if the time is so short, should it not be spent on what is permanent? The question "why don't you think about the next world?" is not a threat but an invitation to wisdom.

📷 Visual Aid: A single bolt of lightning splitting a dark night sky — vivid, instant, and already gone.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "The life you love and the body you're proud of will vanish as quickly as a flash of lightning — so why aren't you thinking about what will actually last beyond your death?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you look at a flash of lightning and see it gone in an instant — can you sit with the fact that your life is equally brief?

## SUTRA 18.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दाराणि य सुया चेव, मित्ता य तह बंधवा ।
जीवंतमणुजीवंति, मयं णाणुव्वयंति य ॥१८.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Dārāṇi ya suyā ceva, mittā ya taha baṃdhavā |
Jīvaṃtamaṇujīvaṃti, mayaṃ ṇāṇuvvayaṃti ya || 18.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Wives, sons, friends, and kinsmen follow only the living — none of them accompanies the dead."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**dārāṇi** = wives; **suyā** = sons; **mittā** = friends; **baṃdhavā** = relatives; **jīvaṃtaṃ** = the living one; **aṇujīvaṃti** = follow, accompany; **mayaṃ** = the dead one; **ṇa aṇuvvayaṃti** = no one follows, no one accompanies

### 5. COMMENTARY

This truth cuts through the most powerful illusion that keeps souls bound to the world: the belief that family loves us absolutely. In this self-centered world, says the monk, wives, sons, friends, and kinsmen accompany only the living — they enjoy a share of the living person's wealth and pleasure. When death comes, not one of them can or will follow. The soul departs alone with only its karma for company. This is not cynicism; it is compassionate clarity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "Your wife, your children, your friends — they are with you while you are alive and useful to them. The moment you die, not one of them can come with you."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the deepest quality of love you have offered in your relationships — love that serves the other's soul, or love that serves your own comfort?

## SUTRA 18.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णीहरंति मयं पुत्ता, पियरं परमदुक्खिया ।

—ति बेमि।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Kahaṃ dhīre ahehiṃ, attāṇaṃ pariyāvase |
Savvasaṃga viṇimmukke, siddhe havai ṇīrae || 18.54 ||

—iti bemi.

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"How could a wise person dwell with their soul in the company of false arguments? The one who is freed from all attachments, having destroyed all karma, becomes a Siddha — liberated and karma-free."

—Thus I declare.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**kahaṃ** = how?; **dhīre** = a wise person; **ahehiṃ** = in false arguments; **attāṇaṃ** = their own soul; **pariyāvase** = allow to dwell?; **savvasaṃga viṇimmukke** = freed from all attachments and karma-bondage; **ṇīrae** = having destroyed all karma; **siddhe havai** = becomes a Siddha (liberated soul)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing sutra mirrors sutra 52 — returning to the same rhetorical question — but now the positive truth is stated fully: the wise one, freed from all attachments and all karma, becomes a Siddha. The journey from hunter-king Sañjaya in sutra 1 to this declaration of the Siddha-state is the journey of the entire chapter: from violence and craving, through teaching, renunciation, historical exemplars, and philosophical clarity, to the final image of the liberated soul. "Iti bemi" — thus I declare — is the seal of the Tīrthaṅkara's voice speaking through the tradition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "Why would a truly wise person allow their soul to be trapped among false arguments? The one who frees themselves from all attachments and destroys all karma becomes fully liberated — a Siddha."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If becoming a Siddha — a perfectly free, perfectly pure soul — is the destination, what is the very next step on that journey that only you can take?
