# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 19
# Mṛgaputrīya (The Son of the Deer)

**Total Sutras:** 99
**Theme:** Prince Mṛgaputra (Balashrī), son of King Balabhadra of Sugrīva city, gains past-life knowledge upon seeing a Jain monk, recalls naraka suffering, and despite his parents' warnings takes dīkṣā and attains liberation.

---

## SUTRA 19.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुग्गीवे णयरे रम्मे, काणणुज्जाण सोहिए ।
राया बलभद्दित्ति, मिया तस्सऽग्गमाहिसी ॥१९.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Suggīve ṇayare ramme, kāṇaṇujjāṇa sohie |
Rāyā Balabhaddi tti, miyā tassaggamāhisī || 19.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the pleasant city of Sugrīva, adorned with forests and gardens, there ruled a king named Balabhadra, whose foremost queen was named Mṛgāvatī.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Suggīve ṇayare** — in the city of Sugrīva
- **ramme** — pleasant, beautiful
- **kāṇaṇujjāṇa sohie** — adorned with forests and pleasure-gardens
- **rāyā Balabhaddi tti** — a king named Balabhadra
- **miyā** — (named) Mṛgāvatī
- **tassaggamāhisī** — his foremost/principal queen

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with precise geographical and dynastic anchoring: the city of Sugrīva, abundant with forests and gardens, ruled by the righteous king Balabhadra with his chief queen Mṛgāvatī. The name Mṛgāvatī ("she who has deer-like qualities") foreshadows the chapter's title Mṛgaputrīya — the son of the deer-queen. Jain narrative literature always places the soul's birth within a specific, identifiable context, affirming that liberation is achieved not in abstract space but within the concrete reality of a particular life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: This chapter begins in the beautiful city of Sugrīva, where King Balabhadra ruled with his queen Mṛgāvatī.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Every life begins with a particular birthplace, family, and name — what does your particular birth context offer you for your spiritual journey?

## SUTRA 19.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तेसिं पुत्ते बलसिरी, मियापुत्ते ति विस्सुए ।
अम्मापिऊण दइए, जुवराया दमीसरे ॥१९.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tesiṃ putte Balasirī, Miyāputte tti vissue |
Ammāpiūṇa daie, juvārāyā damīsare || 19.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Their son was Balashrī, famous as Mṛgaputra — deeply loved by his mother and father, and a crown prince who subdued enemies.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tesiṃ putte** — their son
- **Balasirī** — named Balashrī
- **Miyāputte tti vissue** — famous by the name Mṛgaputra
- **ammāpiūṇa daie** — very dear to his mother and father
- **juvārāyā** — crown prince
- **damīsare** — lord who subdues enemies / lord who subdues the senses

### 5. COMMENTARY

The epithet Mṛgaputra — "son of the deer" — carries layered meaning: born of Mṛgāvatī, and possessing the gentle yet fleet nature of the deer. The word damīsara typically means "subduer of enemies" in the royal context, but in the Jain reading carries the secondary sense of one who will subdue the inner enemies — the passions. Beloved by his parents and invested with the crown-prince's role, he has every worldly advantage — which makes his renunciation all the more significant.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: King Balabhadra and Queen Mṛgāvatī had a son named Balashrī, also known as Mṛgaputra — their beloved crown prince.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What roles and names have been given to you by others — and which ones truly belong to you?

## SUTRA 19.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णंदणे सो उ पासाए, कीलए सह इत्थिहिं ।
देवो दोगुंदगो चेव, णिच्चं मुइयमाणसो ॥१९.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇaṃdaṇe so u pāsāe, kīlae saha itthihiṃ |
Devo doguṃdago ceva, ṇiccaṃ muiyamāṇaso || 19.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He played in the pleasure-garden palace with women — like a dohundaga deity, ever joyful of mind.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇaṃdaṇe** — in the pleasure-garden / in joy
- **so u pāsāe** — he in the palace
- **kīlae** — played, sported
- **saha itthihiṃ** — with women
- **devo doguṃdago ceva** — like a dohundaga deity (the Trāyastriṃśa class of devas, known for constant pleasure-seeking)
- **ṇiccaṃ muiyamāṇaso** — ever cheerful/joyful of mind

### 5. COMMENTARY

The comparison to the dohundaga (Trāyastriṃśa) devas places Mṛgaputra at the peak of worldly enjoyment — these devas are known in the Jain cosmology for living in perpetual sensory delight. The palace pleasure-garden becomes the world in miniature: beautiful, insulated, and apparently inexhaustible in its pleasures. This is the life the chapter will subvert entirely when one unexpected sight shatters the illusion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra lived like a pleasure-deity — always joyful, playing in the palace garden with women, with every desire satisfied.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is your happiness of the kind that one unexpected sight could completely dissolve?

## SUTRA 19.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मणिरयण कोट्टिमतले, पासायालोयणट्ठिओ ।
आलोएइ णगरस्स, चउक्क-तिय-चच्चरे ॥१९.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Maṇirayana koṭṭimatale, pāsāyāloyaṇaṭṭhio |
Āloei ṇagarassa, caukka-tiya-caccaire || 19.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Seated in the palace lattice-window, on a floor inlaid with precious gems, he was looking out at the crossroads, triple-roads, and many thoroughfares of the city.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **maṇirayana koṭṭimatale** — on a floor inlaid with jewels and precious gems
- **pāsāyāloyaṇaṭṭhio** — seated/positioned at the palace lattice-window (āloyana)
- **āloei** — was looking out at
- **ṇagarassa** — of the city
- **caukka-tiya-caccaire** — crossroads (four-way), triple-roads, and multiple thoroughfares

### 5. COMMENTARY

The setting of the palace lattice-window is a recurring motif in Jain narrative: it represents the liminal space between the inner world of royal comfort and the outer world where renunciants walk. Mṛgaputra looks out — not as a casual observer but with the alert curiosity of a soul on the edge of transformation. The gem-inlaid floor beneath him and the busy streets below him represent the two worlds between which he is about to choose.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: One day Mṛgaputra sat at his palace window — on a beautiful jeweled floor — and looked out at the busy city streets below.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What are you looking out at from your comfortable position — and what do you see that you have not truly let yourself see before?

## SUTRA 19.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह तत्थ अइच्छंतं, पासइ समणसंजयं ।
तव–णियम–संजमधरं, सीलडुं गुण आगरं ॥१९.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha tattha aicchantaṃ, pāsai samaṇasaṃjayaṃ |
Tava-ṇiyama-saṃjamadhāraṃ, sīlaḍuṃ guṇa āgaṃ || 19.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then, unexpectedly passing there, he saw a restrained śramaṇa — a bearer of austerity, rules, and saṃyama, holding thousands of precepts, a treasury of virtues.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aha tattha** — then there
- **aicchantaṃ** — passing unexpectedly, without (his) intention
- **pāsai** — he saw, beheld
- **samaṇasaṃjayaṃ** — a restrained śramaṇa (Jain monk)
- **tava-ṇiyama-saṃjamadhāraṃ** — bearing austerity, rules, and restraint
- **sīlaḍuṃ** — holding thousands of precepts/virtues
- **guṇa āgaṃ** — a treasury/mine of virtues

### 5. COMMENTARY

The word aicchantaṃ — "passing unexpectedly" — captures the unplanned nature of the encounter. Neither Mṛgaputra sought out the monk, nor did the monk seek him. This is the Jain understanding of nimittakāraṇa — the instrumental cause of awakening arrives uninvited. The monk is described with three qualities: tapa (austerity), niyama (rules of conduct), and saṃyama (restraint) — the complete external expression of the liberated path — plus thousands of śīlāṅgas (virtue-disciplines) that constitute the internal structure of the monastic life.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Unexpectedly, Mṛgaputra saw a Jain monk passing below — a man who practiced austerity, followed all rules, and was filled with countless virtues.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Has a chance encounter with someone's way of living ever changed the direction of your life?

## SUTRA 19.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं देहइ मियापुत्ते, दिट्टीए अणिमिसाए उ ।
काहिं मण्णेरिसं रूवं, दिट्टपुव्वं मए पुरा ॥१९.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Taṃ dehai miyāputte, diṭṭīe aṇimisāe u |
Kāhiṃ maṇṇerisiṃ rūvaṃ, diṭṭhapubbaṃ mae purā || 19.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Mṛgaputra gazed at him with unblinking eyes, thinking: "Where have I seen such a form as this before — at some time in the past?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taṃ dehai miyāputte** — Mṛgaputra looked at him
- **diṭṭīe aṇimisāe** — with unblinking eyes (animiṣa = without blinking)
- **kāhiṃ** — where
- **maṇṇerisiṃ rūvaṃ** — such a form as this
- **diṭṭhapubbaṃ mae** — seen by me before
- **purā** — previously, in the past

### 5. COMMENTARY

The unblinking gaze — animiṣa — is significant: it mirrors the monk's own quality of sustained, unwavering focus. Mṛgaputra cannot look away. Something in the sight of the monk triggers a recognition that goes deeper than ordinary memory — he feels he has seen this before, but he cannot locate it in his current life's experience. The soul's deeper memory is beginning to stir. This is the first sign that jātismaraṇa is approaching.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra stared at the monk with wide, unblinking eyes, thinking: "I've seen someone like this before — but where?"

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you encounter something truly pure or spiritual, can you hold your gaze on it without looking away?

## SUTRA 19.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

साहुस्स दरिसणे तस्स, अञ्झवसाणम्मि सोहणे ।
मोह गयस्स संतस्स, जाईसरणं समुप्पणं ॥१९.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sāhussa darisaṇe tassa, ajjhavasāṇammi sohaṇe |
Moha gayassa saṃtassa, jāīsaraṇaṃ samuppaṇṇaṃ || 19.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Upon seeing that monk, with moha gone and inner resolution purified and tranquil, jātismaraṇa knowledge arose in him.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sāhussa darisaṇe** — upon seeing the monk
- **tassa** — of him (Mṛgaputra)
- **ajjhavasāṇammi sohaṇe** — with inner resolve becoming pure/shining
- **moha gayassa** — with moha (delusion/mohanīya karma) having gone
- **saṃtassa** — tranquil, pacified
- **jāīsaraṇaṃ** — jātismaraṇa knowledge (memory of past births)
- **samuppaṇṇaṃ** — arose, was born

### 5. COMMENTARY

The technical Jain term here is significant: moha-gaya — "with moha having departed." The obscuring force of the mohanīya karma momentarily lifts, the inner resolve (adhyavasāya) is purified, and jātismaraṇa jñāna — the extraordinary knowledge of past births — arises spontaneously. This is not visualization or imagination but actual knowledge. The sight of the monk, himself free of moha, resonates with Mṛgaputra's own deeper nature and catalyzes the lifting of karmic fog.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The moment Mṛgaputra saw the monk, the veil of delusion momentarily lifted from his mind and he suddenly gained the ability to remember his past lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What conditions in your own life — what sights, people, or moments of stillness — have lifted the fog of your ordinary mind?

## SUTRA 19.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

देवलोग–चुओ संतो, माणुसं भवमागओ ।
सिणिणाणे समुप्पण्णे, जाई सरइ पुराणयं ॥१९.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Devaloga-cuo saṃto, māṇusaṃ bhavamāgao |
Siṇiṇāṇe samuppaṇṇe, jāī sarai purāṇayaṃ || 19.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having descended from the deva-world and come into the human existence — when saṃjñā-jñāna (jātismaraṇa) arose — he remembered his ancient births.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **devaloga-cuo saṃto** — having descended from the deva-world
- **māṇusaṃ bhavam āgao** — having come into the human existence
- **siṇiṇāṇe** — saṃjñā-jñāna / jātismaraṇa knowledge
- **samuppaṇṇe** — when it arose
- **jāī sarai purāṇayaṃ** — he remembered his ancient births/lives

### 5. COMMENTARY

The narrative reveals that Mṛgaputra's immediately previous birth was as a deva — a celestial being in one of the heavenly realms. This is the classic Jain trajectory of a soul approaching liberation: descending from a deva birth into a human birth, where the conditions for final liberation are available. The recall is complete — purāṇayaṃ means "ancient, long-past" — his memory reaches not just to the previous life but to many previous existences.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra remembered that he had been born in a heavenly realm before this human life, and his past-life memory was now revealing all his previous existences.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If your soul has lived countless lives before this one, what might the patterns of your deepest tendencies be telling you about where your soul has been?

## SUTRA 19.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जाईसरणे समुप्पणे, मियापुत्ते महिड्डिए ।
सरइ पोराणियं जाइं, सामण्णं च पुराकयं ॥१९.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jāīsaraṇe samuppaṇṇe, miyāputte mahiḍḍie |
Sarai porāṇiyaṃ jāiṃ, sāmaṇṇaṃ ca purākayaṃ || 19.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When jātismaraṇa arose in the great-powered Mṛgaputra, he remembered his ancient births and also the śramaṇa-dharma he had practiced in previous lives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jāīsaraṇe samuppaṇṇe** — when jātismaraṇa arose
- **miyāputte mahiḍḍie** — the great-powered (maharddhika) Mṛgaputra
- **sarai** — remembered
- **porāṇiyaṃ jāiṃ** — ancient births
- **sāmaṇṇaṃ ca** — and also the śramaṇa-dharma
- **purākayaṃ** — practiced in the past

### 5. COMMENTARY

The remembrance includes not only the births and their suffering but specifically the śramaṇa-dharma practiced previously. This is a soul with prior karmic credit in the renunciant path — the practice of saṃyama from a previous birth has left impressions (saṃskāras) that resurface now. The epithet mahiḍḍia — "great-powered" — acknowledges the soul's accumulated spiritual energy, which enables this extraordinary recall.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: When past-life memory arose in the great Mṛgaputra, he remembered not only his old births but also the spiritual discipline he had practiced in those past lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a spiritual practice that calls to you with a familiar pull — as if you have done it before?

## SUTRA 19.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विसएसु अरज्जंतो, रज्जंतो संजमम्मि य ।
अम्मा–पियरमुवागम्म, इमं वयणमब्बवी ॥१९.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Visaesu arajjaṃto, rajjaṃto saṃjamammi ya |
Ammā-piyaramuvāgamma, imaṃ vayaṇamabbavī || 19.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Becoming dispassionate toward sense-objects and developing love for saṃyama, he approached his mother and father and spoke these words.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **visaesu arajjaṃto** — becoming dispassionate (not-attached) toward sense-objects
- **rajjaṃto saṃjamammi** — developing love/attachment for saṃyama (monastic restraint)
- **ammā-piyaram uvāgamma** — approaching his mother and father
- **imaṃ vayaṇam abbavī** — spoke these words

### 5. COMMENTARY

The transition from rāga-in-the-world to rāga-in-saṃyama is the inner pivot of the chapter. Mṛgaputra does not become cold or empty — he becomes redirected. The same capacity for love that once moved toward sense-objects now moves toward liberation. He goes first to his parents: not to escape them but to engage them, to ask for their blessing. This act of filial reverence is the foundation of the renunciation that follows.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Having lost his taste for sensory pleasures and feeling drawn toward the monk's life of discipline, Mṛgaputra respectfully approached his parents to speak.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When dispassion arises in you toward something you once craved, does it feel like loss — or like freedom?

## SUTRA 19.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुयाणि मे पंच महव्वयाणि, णरएसु दुक्खं च तिरिक्खजोणिसु ।
णिच्छिवणकामो मि महण्णवाओ, अणुजाणह पव्वइस्सामि अम्मो ॥१९.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Suyāṇi me paṃca mahavvayāṇi, ṇaraessu dukkhaṃ ca tirikkhajōṇisu |
Ṇicchīvaṇakāmo mi mahaṇṇavāo, aṇujāṇaha pavvaissāmi ammo || 19.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I know the five great vows; I know the suffering in the hells and in animal wombs — I am desirous of crossing this great ocean (of saṃsāra); give me permission, O Mother: I will take dīkṣā."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **suyāṇi me** — I have known / I know
- **paṃca mahavvayāṇi** — the five great vows
- **ṇaraessu dukkhaṃ ca** — the suffering in the hells too
- **tirikkhajōṇisu** — in animal wombs/existences
- **ṇicchīvaṇakāmo mi** — I am desirous of crossing over, of liberation
- **mahaṇṇavāo** — this great ocean (of saṃsāra)
- **aṇujāṇaha** — give me permission
- **pavvaissāmi ammo** — O Mother, I will take dīkṣā

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is Mṛgaputra's single, comprehensive opening statement to his parents: three pillars of his readiness — (1) knowledge of the vows, (2) knowledge of the suffering that awaits without them, and (3) the desire to cross saṃsāra. The word aṇujāṇaha — "give permission" — reflects the Jain value of seeking blessing before renunciation. He does not flee; he asks. And his knowledge is not secondhand theology but the direct recall of jātismaraṇa.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra told his parents: "I know the five great vows, I know the suffering of hell and animal life — please give me permission to become a monk and cross this ocean of rebirth."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are you asking for what you truly need from those who love you — or avoiding the conversation that could free you?

## SUTRA 19.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अम्मताय मए भोगा, भुत्ता विसफलोवमा ।
पच्छा कडुयविवागा, अणुबंध दुहावहा ॥१९.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ammatāya mae bhogā, bhuttā visaphalovamā |
Pacchā kaḍuyavivāgā, aṇubaṃdha duhāvahā || 19.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"O Mother and Father! The pleasures I have enjoyed are comparable to poisonous fruit — after-ripening bitterly, they bring continuous suffering."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ammatāya** — O Mother and Father
- **mae bhogā bhuttā** — pleasures enjoyed by me
- **visaphalovamā** — comparable to poisonous fruit (the kimpāka fruit)
- **pacchā** — afterward, later
- **kaḍuyavivāgā** — with a bitter/harsh result/outcome
- **aṇubaṃdha** — continuous bondage, perpetual
- **duhāvahā** — bringing suffering

### 5. COMMENTARY

Mṛgaputra's vairāgya speech to his parents occupies sutras 12–24. He begins with the poisonous fruit analogy — the kimpāka fruit of the forest looks beautiful and tastes sweet but causes death. Pleasures appear attractive in anticipation, are pleasant momentarily, and then leave behind suffering, bondage, and karmic accumulation. This is the empirical observation of a soul who has lived many lives and has felt the full cycle from pleasure to suffering.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra told his parents that the pleasures he had enjoyed were like poisonous fruit — sweet at first, but bringing bitter pain and continuing suffering in the end.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you identify a pleasure in your life that looked beautiful from the outside but left you worse off after you pursued it?

## SUTRA 19.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इमं सरीरं अणिच्चं, असुइं असुइसंभवं ।
असासया वासिमणं, दुक्ख केसाण भायणं ॥१९.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Imaṃ sarīraṃ aṇiccaṃ, asuiṃ asuisaṃbhavaṃ |
Asāsayā vāsimaṇaṃ, dukka kesāṇa bhāyaṇaṃ || 19.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"This body is impermanent, impure, born of impurity; its residence is temporary — it is a vessel of sufferings and afflictions."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **imaṃ sarīraṃ aṇiccaṃ** — this body is impermanent
- **asuiṃ** — impure
- **asuisaṃbhavaṃ** — born of impurity (from impure matter)
- **asāsayā** — temporary, not permanent
- **vāsimaṇaṃ** — as a residence
- **dukka kesāṇa bhāyaṇaṃ** — a vessel/container of sufferings and afflictions

### 5. COMMENTARY

The body is analyzed through four lenses: (1) anicca — impermanent; (2) aśuci — impure; (3) born of impurity — arising from blood, semen, and the processes of digestion; (4) a temporary residence — we stay in it for a while and must leave it. Against these four realities, the practice of body-beautification and body-worship appears misguided. The body is not the enemy but it is not the self — and building one's happiness on it is structurally flawed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra told his parents that the body is impermanent, impure, born from impure substances, only a temporary home — and ultimately a container of suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What changes when you see the body as a temporary vessel rather than as your permanent identity?

## SUTRA 19.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

असासए सरीरम्मि, रइं णोवलभामहं ।
पच्छा पुरा व चइयव्वे, फेणबुब्बुय–सणिभे ॥१९.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Asāsae sarīrammi, raiṃ ṇovalabhāmahaṃ |
Pacchā purā va caiyavve, pheṇabubbuya-saṇibhe || 19.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"In this impermanent body — comparable to a water-bubble of foam — I find no joy; whether sooner or later, this body must certainly be abandoned."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **asāsae sarīrammi** — in this impermanent body
- **raiṃ ṇovalabhāmahaṃ** — I find no joy, no delight comes
- **pacchā purā va** — whether later or sooner
- **caiyavve** — must be abandoned
- **pheṇabubbuya-saṇibhe** — comparable to a water-bubble of foam (fleeting, insubstantial)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The foam-bubble image (pheṇabubbuya) is one of the most ancient and universal metaphors for bodily impermanence — a bubble appears and dissolves within moments, leaving no trace. Mṛgaputra extends the logic: if the body is bubble-like in its permanence, and if genuine joy requires a stable foundation, then the body cannot be that foundation. Whether the bubble lasts a moment or a century, it will burst. The rational response is to invest in what endures.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The body is like a bubble of foam — it must be left behind sooner or later — so Mṛgaputra found no real joy in it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are you building your sense of joy on something that, like a bubble, cannot last?

The monk's life practiced with complete inner stillness (nimbuta) and complete fearlessness (niśśaṅka) represents a similar enormity. ### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Just as no scale can weigh a cosmic mountain, the level of inner calm and fearlessness required for true monkhood is beyond ordinary human capacity.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much of your spiritual life is still marked by anxiety, doubt, or inner restlessness — and what would complete stillness require of you?

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया मिगस्स आयंको, महारण्णम्मि जायइ ।
अच्छंतं रुक्खमूलम्मि, को णं ताहे तिगिच्छइ ॥१९.७९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jayā migassa āyaṃko, mahāraṇṇammi jāyai |
Achaṃtaṃ rukkhhamūlammi, ko ṇaṃ tāhe tigichai || 19.79 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"When illness comes to a deer in the great forest, seated under a tree — who then treats that deer?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jayā** — when
- **migassa** — of the deer, to the deer
- **āyaṃko** — illness, disease
- **mahāraṇṇammi** — in the great forest
- **jāyai** — arises, comes
- **achaṃtaṃ** — seated, resting
- **rukkhhamūlammi** — under the root/base of a tree
- **ko ṇaṃ** — who then, who indeed
- **tāhe** — at that time
- **tigichai** — treats, heals, gives medical attention?

### 5. COMMENTARY

Mṛgaputra develops the analogy with surgical precision. When the forest deer falls ill and rests under a tree, there is no healer — no physician, no medicine, no one even to ask after its welfare. And yet the deer recovers (when it recovers) through its own nature, without anxiety about its lack of care. The question "who treats it?" is rhetorical: the answer is, no one. This is not tragedy; it is the natural order of self-reliant life. The monk's situation is identical: when illness comes during the wandering life, there is no treatment. But the illness is not the problem — it is the attachment to wellness, the anxiety about its absence, that constitutes the real suffering.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra asks: when a deer in the deep forest gets sick and sits under a tree — who takes care of it? No one. And yet the deer lives its life fully.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Who takes care of you when you are ill — and how much of your sense of security depends on having that care available? What would it mean to be at peace even without that guarantee?

## SUTRA 19.80

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

को वा से ओसहं देइ, को वा से पुच्छइ सुहं ।
को से भत्तं च पाणं च, आहिरत्तु पणामए ॥१९.८०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ko vā se osahaṃ dei, ko vā se pucchai suhaṃ |
Ko se bhattaṃ ca pāṇaṃ ca, āhirattu paṇāmae || 19.80 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Who gives it medicine? Who asks after its welfare? Who brings it food and water and offers it?"

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ko vā se** — who to it, who for it
- **osahaṃ dei** — gives medicine/remedy
- **ko vā se** — who to it
- **pucchai suhaṃ** — asks about its welfare/comfort
- **ko se** — who for it
- **bhattaṃ ca** — food and
- **pāṇaṃ ca** — water and
- **āhirattu** — having brought/fetched
- **paṇāmae** — offers, presents

### 5. COMMENTARY

Three rhetorical questions complete the deer analogy: Who brings medicine? Who inquires about its health? Who brings it food and water? The answer to all three is: no one. No one does these things for the forest deer. And this, Mṛgaputra argues, is not a deficiency but a condition of freedom. The monk who undertakes mṛgacārī — deer-conduct — does not require these things either, because the monk's inner wellbeing is not contingent on external care. This is a radical restatement of what it means to be "cared for": not by another's service, but by one's own undisturbed equanimity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra asks three simple questions about the sick deer in the forest: Who gives it medicine? Who asks how it is? Who brings it food and water? No one. And yet it lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If no one brought you food, asked after you, or offered medicine — what resources, within yourself, would you discover? What does the answer reveal about where your real strength lies?

## SUTRA 19.81

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जया य से सुही होइ, तया गच्छइ गोयरं ।
भत्तपाणस्स अट्टाए, वल्लराणि सराणि य ॥१९.८१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jayā ya se suhī hoi, tayā gacchai goyaraṃ |
Bhattapāṇassa aṭṭāe, vallarāṇi sarāṇi ya || 19.81 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"And when the deer recovers its health, it goes to its pasture — seeking food and water among the forest thickets and ponds."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jayā ya** — and when
- **se suhī hoi** — it becomes well/healthy again
- **tayā** — then
- **gacchai goyaraṃ** — it goes to its grazing ground, its pasture
- **bhattapāṇassa** — for food and water
- **aṭṭāe** — for the sake of, in order to get
- **vallarāṇi** — forest thickets, places of creepers and vines
- **sarāṇi ya** — and ponds, lakes, water-bodies

### 5. COMMENTARY

When the deer recovers — without having had any medicine or care — it simply returns to its natural activity: grazing. It goes to the forest thickets (vallarā) and the ponds (sara), seeking what it needs without urgency, without hoarding, without anxiety about tomorrow. "Suhī hoi" — it becomes well — not because of treatment but because of its own constitution and resilience. The monk similarly: when the illness passes (as illness does, even untreated, unless karma makes it terminal), the monk resumes the wandering life without drama, without complaint about what was not provided.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: When the deer gets better on its own, it simply goes back to grazing in the forest and drinking from ponds — naturally, without making a fuss about having been sick.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you recover from a difficulty, do you return to living fully — or do you linger in recovery mode, guarding against the next illness? What would it mean to simply return to the forest?

## SUTRA 19.82

खाइत्ता पाणियं पाउं, वल्लरेहिं सरेहि य ।

Khāittā pāṇiyaṃ pāuṃ, vallarehiṃ sarehiṃ ya |
Migacāriyaṃ carittāṇaṃ, gacchai migacāriyaṃ || 19.82 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Having eaten grass and drunk water at the forest thickets and ponds — practicing the deer-conduct — it proceeds on its deer-way."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **khāittā** — having eaten (grass and plants)
- **pāṇiyaṃ pāuṃ** — having drunk water
- **vallarehiṃ** — at the forest thicket places
- **sarehiṃ ya** — and at the ponds
- **migacāriyaṃ** — the deer-conduct (mṛgacārī — the deer's characteristic way of living and moving)

- **gacchai** — it goes, it proceeds
- **migacāriyaṃ** — in the deer-way, living the deer-life

The deer's daily pattern is simple: eat what the forest offers, drink from the ponds, and move. It does not aspire to be something else, does not compare itself to others, does not accumulate beyond what the day requires. The monk who follows mṛgacārī embodies this: accepting what comes in terms of food and water (alms received), not seeking better or lamenting less, and continuing the spiritual movement — the wandering forward.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The deer eats what the forest offers, drinks from ponds, and moves on — living simply and naturally. Mṛgaputra says: this is how I will live as a monk.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 19.83

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं समुट्टिओ भिक्खू, एवमेव अणेगओ ।
मिगचारियं चरित्ताणं, उड्डुं पक्कमइ दिसं ॥१९.८३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ samuṭṭhio bhikkhū, evameva aṇegao |
Migacāriyaṃ carittāṇaṃ, uḍḍuṃ pakkamāi disaṃ || 19.83 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Likewise the monk who arises with this resolve — roaming many places just like the deer — practicing deer-conduct — proceeds toward the upward direction: liberation."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evaṃ** — in this same way
- **samuṭṭhio** — arising with resolve, committed, firmly established
- **bhikkhū** — the monk (bhikṣu)
- **evameva** — in exactly this same manner (as the deer)
- **aṇegao** — one who roams many places
- **migacāriyaṃ** — deer-conduct
- **carittāṇaṃ** — having practiced
- **uḍḍuṃ** — upward (ūrdhva — the upward direction, toward mokṣa)
- **pakkamāi** — proceeds, goes
- **disaṃ** — direction, realm

The deer analogy now comes full circle: the monk who practices mṛgacārī — living simply without medical treatment, without fixed residence, without accumulation — moves "upward" (uḍḍuṃ). In Jain cosmology, the upward direction (ūrdhvagati) is the direction of liberation: the liberated soul rises to Siddha-śilā at the apex of the universe. The deer's wandering in the forest, though horizontal in the physical world, is for the monk a vertical movement toward freedom. The mṛgacārī, practiced with equanimity and restraint, leads not in circles but upward.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: In the same way the deer lives freely in the forest, the monk who practices that same simple, unattached way of living rises upward — toward liberation.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is your spiritual life moving in circles — revisiting the same struggles — or are you genuinely moving in the "upward direction"? What one thing might help you move more truly upward?

## SUTRA 19.84

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जहा मिगे एग अणेगचारी, अणेगवासे धुवगोयरे य ।
एवं मुणी गोयरियं पविट्टे, णो हीलए णो वि य खिंसएज्जा ॥१९.८४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jahā mige ega aṇegacārī, aṇegavāse dhuvagoyare ya |
Evaṃ muṇī goyariyaṃ paviṭṭhe, ṇo hīlae ṇo vi ya khiṃsaejjā || 19.84 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Just as the deer wanders many places alone, dwells in many locations, and always grazes wherever it is — so too the monk who has entered on the path of alms-wandering should neither feel contempt for it, nor be angry about it."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jahā** — just as
- **mige** — the deer
- **ega** — alone, singular
- **aṇegacārī** — one who moves in many places
- **aṇegavāse** — dwelling in many locations
- **dhuvagoyare ya** — and always grazing wherever it is, perpetually finding its pasture
- **evaṃ muṇī** — so too the monk
- **goyariyaṃ paviṭṭhe** — having entered on the alms-wandering life
- **ṇo hīlae** — should not feel contempt for it (the life or the food received)
- **ṇo vi ya khiṃsaejjā** — and should not become angry/disparaging either

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse expands the mṛgacārī teaching into its full ethical and psychological dimension. The deer, wherever it roams, does not feel contempt for the grass available, nor does it grow angry when only poor grass is found. The monk, similarly, should not feel contempt for the alms received (whether abundant or meager) and should not grow angry or resentful when the wandering life offers less than expected. "Dhuvagoyare" — always finding its pasture — implies that wherever the monk goes, nourishment for the soul is available: in every situation, the opportunity for equanimity and practice exists.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Just as the deer roams freely and eats whatever grass it finds — never looking down on what's available or getting angry about it — the monk should practice the same contentment on the alms-wandering path.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you feel contempt for the "small" or "ordinary" provisions life offers you — the simple meal, the quiet day, the ordinary kindness? What would it mean to see all of it as pasture?

## SUTRA 19.85

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मिगचारियं चरिस्सामि, एवं पुत्त जहासुहं ।
अम्मापिउहिं अणुण्णाओ, जहाइ उवहिं तओ ॥१९.८५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Migacāriyaṃ carissāmi, evaṃ putta jahāsuhaṃ |
Ammāpiuhiṃ aṇuṇṇāo, jahāi uvahaṃ tao || 19.85 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"'I will practice the deer-conduct' — thus Mṛgaputra spoke; and the parents said: 'Do as it brings you well-being, son.' Having received his parents' permission, he then abandoned all household possessions."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **migacāriyaṃ carissāmi** — I will practice the deer-conduct (Mṛgaputra's words)
- **evaṃ putta** — thus, O son (parents' response)
- **jahāsuhaṃ** — as it brings you well-being, do as you find beneficial
- **ammāpiuhiṃ** — from his parents (mother and father)

- **jahāi** — he abandoned, gave up
- **uvahaṃ** — all possessions, paraphernalia of household life
- **tao** — then, thereupon

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse marks the formal exchange of permission. Mṛgaputra declares his intent in the language of the analogy he has built: "I will practice mṛgacārī." The parents, hearing the full deer-conduct argument, respond with "jahāsuhaṃ" — do as brings you happiness, do as your welfare dictates. The permission is unconditional and loving. Then, having received it, Mṛgaputra immediately abandons all household possessions ("uvahaṃ tao" — then the possessions). There is no delay, no lingering. The permission received is the trigger for action.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra said "I will live like the deer," and his parents replied "Do what brings you well-being, son." With their blessing, he immediately gave up all his worldly belongings.

## SUTRA 19.86

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मिगचारियं चरिस्सामि, सव्वदुक्खविमोक्खिणिं ।
तुभेहिं अऽभणुण्णाओ, गच्छ पुत्त जहासुहं ॥१९.८६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Migacāriyaṃ carissāmi, savvadukkha-vimokkhiṇiṃ |
Tubhehiṃ a'bhaṇuṇṇāo, gaccha putta jahāsuhaṃ || 19.86 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Mṛgaputra: "I will practice the deer-conduct — which liberates from all suffering." Parents: "Having received your permission, go, son — as it brings you well-being."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **migacāriyaṃ carissāmi** — I will practice the deer-conduct
- **savvadukkha-vimokkhiṇiṃ** — which is the liberator from all suffering
- **tubhehiṃ** — by you (both parents)
- **a'bhaṇuṇṇāo** — having been given permission/blessing
- **gaccha** — go! (parents' command/blessing)
- **putta** — son
- **jahāsuhaṃ** — as it brings you well-being

This sutra is a couplet of departing words. Mṛgaputra adds the crucial qualifier to his mṛgacārī declaration: "savvadukkha-vimokkhiṇiṃ" — which liberates from all suffering. He is not merely saying he will live like a deer; he is saying this conduct, followed with complete commitment, leads to total liberation. The parents respond with "gaccha putta" — "go, son" — the formal blessing of departure. The Gujarati commentary notes that this is the moment of complete renunciation of household life: not just of possessions but of the parental claim itself. The parents release him.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra said "I will follow the path that frees from all suffering" — and his parents said "Go, son, as it brings you well-being." With those words, he was truly released.

## SUTRA 19.87

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं सो अम्मापियरो, अणुमाणित्ताण बहुविहं ।
ममत्तं छिंदइ ताहे, महाणागो व्व कंचुयं ॥१९.८७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ so ammāpiyaro, aṇumāṇittāṇa bahuvihaṃ |
Mamattaṃ chiṃdai tāhe, mahāṇāgo vva kaṃcuyaṃ || 19.87 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"In this way, having convinced his parents in many ways, he then cuts off all attachment — as a great serpent sheds its skin."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evaṃ so** — in this way, he (Mṛgaputra)
- **ammāpiyaro** — his parents
- **aṇumāṇittāṇa** — having convinced, having persuaded
- **bahuvihaṃ** — in many ways, through many arguments
- **mamattaṃ** — attachment, sense of "mine-ness" toward family, wealth, everything
- **chiṃdai** — cuts off, severs
- **tāhe** — then
- **mahāṇāgo vva** — like a great serpent
- **kaṃcuyaṃ** — its old skin (the shed skin of the snake)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The snake-shedding-its-skin simile is one of the most celebrated images for renunciation in the Indic traditions. The great serpent (mahānāga) sheds its old skin not with struggle or regret but with ease and completeness — it moves forward, and the old skin is simply left behind. Mṛgaputra, having done the work of persuasion through the long naraka account and the mṛgacārī argument, now cuts his mamatta — his sense of "mine" toward family, possessions, status, and even his parents themselves — cleanly and completely. The mamatta is the skin. The self that emerges is new.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: After convincing his parents with all his arguments, Mṛgaputra cut away every trace of attachment — like a great snake that simply sheds its old skin and moves forward.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 19.89

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पंचमहव्वयजुत्तो, पंचसिमिओ तिगुत्तिगुत्तो य ।
सऽभिंतर बाहिरओ, तवोकम्मंसि उज्जुओ ॥१९.८९॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Endowed with the five great vows, with the five careful observances, protected by the three guards — engaged in inner and outer austerity, he was diligent in the practice of tapa."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

Tapa — austerity — encompasses both the body's discipline and the mind's refinement.

## SUTRA 19.90

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिम्ममो णिरहंकारो, णिस्संगो चत्तगारवो ।
समो य सव्वभूएसु, तसेसु थावरेसु य ॥१९.९०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Nimmamo nirahṃkāro, nissaṃgo cattagāravo |
Samo ya savvabhūesu, tasesu thāvaresu ya || 19.90 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Free of attachment, free of ego, free of all bonds, having abandoned the three types of pride — equanimous toward all beings, mobile and immobile."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **nimmamo** — free of mamata (the sense of "mine," of possessiveness)
- **nirahṃkāro** — free of ahaṃkāra (ego, pride of self)
- **nissaṃgo** — free of all saṃga (bonds, attachments of any kind)
- **cattagāravo** — having abandoned the three gāravas (pride of body, pride of learning, pride of status)
- **samo ya** — and equanimous
- **savvabhūesu** — toward all beings
- **tasesu** — mobile beings (animals, humans)
- **thāvaresu ya** — and immobile beings (plants, earth, water, fire, air bodies)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the inner qualities of Mṛgaputra's renunciation: no "mine-ness," no ego, no attachments, no pride. The three gāravas abandoned — pride of body, pride of knowledge, pride of gain/status — are the three subtle forms of ego that persist in even advanced practitioners. "Samo ya savvabhūesu" — equanimous toward all beings — extends this inner freedom outward: the monk who has no ego has no preference, no favoritism, no prejudice toward any of the six types of beings, from the subtlest immobile organism to the human being.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: As a monk, Mṛgaputra had no sense of "mine," no ego, no attachments, no pride — and he treated every single living being, plant or animal, with complete equality.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the three forms of pride — pride of body, pride of knowledge, or pride of status — is most active in you? What would your interactions look like if that pride were genuinely absent?

## SUTRA 19.91

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लाभालाभे सुहे दुक्खे, जीविए मरणे तहा ।
समो णिंदा पसंसासु, तहा माणावमाणओ ॥१९.९१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Lābhālābhe suhe dukke, jīvie maraṇe tahā |
Samo niṃdā pasaṃsāsu, tahā māṇāvamāṇao || 19.91 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Equanimous in gain and loss, in pleasure and pain, in life and death — equally disposed toward blame and praise, toward honor and dishonor."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **lābhālābhe** — in gain and in loss
- **suhe** — in pleasure, happiness
- **dukke** — in pain, suffering
- **jīvie maraṇe tahā** — and so in life and in death
- **samo** — equanimous, of equal disposition
- **niṃdā** — blame, censure
- **pasaṃsāsu** — in praise, in commendation
- **tahā** — and so too
- **māṇāvamāṇao** — in honor and in dishonor

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse catalogues six pairs of opposites across which Mṛgaputra maintains perfect equanimity: gain/loss, pleasure/pain, life/death, blame/praise, honor/dishonor. The spiritual ideal is not indifference to experience but freedom from being moved by the poles — neither pulled toward one nor repelled by the other. "Samo" — equal-natured — is the technical Jain term for this state: samabhāva. The soul that rests in samabhāva is not tossed between the opposites because it has found the ground that holds regardless of what the surface brings.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra was the same person whether he received gain or loss, pleasure or pain, praise or blame, honor or insult. None of these moved him. That is what equanimity means.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of the six pairs hits you hardest — moves you most easily off your center? Gain/loss? Praise/blame? Life/death? What would it take to find the ground that holds in that pair?

## SUTRA 19.92

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गारवेसु कसाएसु, दंड सल्ल भएसु य ।
णियत्तो हास सोगाओ, अणियाणो अबंधणो ॥१९.९२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Gāravesu kasāesu, daṃḍa salla bhaesu ya |
Niyatto hāsa sogāo, aṇiyāṇo abaṃdhaṇo || 19.92 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Turned away from pride, from the passions, from the three rods of punishment and the seven fears — withdrawn from laughter and sorrow — without nidāna (worldly seeking) and without bondage."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **gāravesu** — from the gāravas (forms of pride — body, learning, status)
- **kasāesu** — from the kaṣāyas (passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed)
- **daṃḍa** — the three daṇḍas (punishments/sticks — of mind, speech, body)
- **salla** — the seven śalyas (thorns — deceit, wrong belief, etc.)
- **bhaesu ya** — and from the three/seven fears (bhaya)
- **niyatto** — turned away from, withdrawn from
- **hāsa** — laughter (frivolity, careless joy)
- **sogāo** — from sorrow/grief
- **aṇiyāṇo** — without nidāna (no craving for worldly or heavenly rewards from spiritual practice)
- **abaṃdhaṇo** — without bondage (free of all karmic bonds)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse maps the complete interior freedom Mṛgaputra attained: turned away from the gāravas, kaṣāyas, daṇḍas, śalyas, and bhaya — these are the entire catalogue of the soul's inner enemies in Jain soteriology. "Aṇiyāṇo" — without nidāna — is critical: the monk does not practice tapa or dharma with any ulterior motive (whether wealth, rebirth in heaven, or even liberation sought as a reward). The practice is pure. "Abaṃdhaṇo" — without bondage — is the result: no new karma is being generated because there is no ego, no passion, no motive.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra was free of pride, passion, the punishments of uncontrolled mind/speech/body, and all the inner "thorns" of wrong seeking — neither frivolously joyful nor sorrowful — practicing with no ulterior motive.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are any of your spiritual practices done with an ulterior motive — seeking a reward, avoiding a punishment, impressing someone? What would pure practice, with no hidden agenda, feel like?

## SUTRA 19.93

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अणिस्सिओ इहं लोए, परलोए अणिस्सिओ ।
वासीचंदणकप्पो य, असणे अणसणे तहा ॥१९.९३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aṇissio ihaṃ loe, paraloe aṇissio |
Vāsīcaṃḍaṇakappo ya, asaṇe aṇasaṇe tahā || 19.93 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Without dependence on this world, without dependence on the next world — equanimous like the chisel and the sandalwood — the same whether receiving food or going without."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇissio** — without dependence, without attachment
- **ihaṃ loe** — in this world (present life's pleasures and goods)
- **paraloe aṇissio** — without dependence on the next world (not practicing tapa for heavenly reward)
- **vāsīcaṃḍaṇakappo ya** — equanimous like the adze/chisel (vāsī) and the sandalwood (candana) — the famous simile: sandalwood yields its fragrance even to the adze that cuts it; be equal toward the cutter and the anointer alike
- **asaṇe** — when food (āśana) is received
- **aṇasaṇe tahā** — and equally when no food comes (anāśana — fasting)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The vāsī-candana simile — the adze and sandalwood — is one of the most celebrated in Jain ethics. The sandalwood does not distinguish between the adze that cuts it and the person who applies it as perfume: it gives its fragrance equally to both. The monk who has reached this state does not prefer food over fasting, praise over blame, this world over the next. "Aṇissio ihaṃ loe, paraloe aṇissio" — not dependent on this world, not dependent on the next — captures the totality of the monk's freedom: nothing in either world exerts a pull.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Mṛgaputra was equally at peace whether he ate a full meal or fasted completely — like sandalwood that releases its fragrance equally to the adze that cuts it and the hand that applies it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you think of one relationship or situation in your life where you respond as differently as possible to two outcomes that are actually equally neutral? What would the sandalwood perspective look like there?

## SUTRA 19.94

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अप्पसत्थेहिं दारेहिं, सव्वओ पिहियासवे ।
अञ्झप्पझाणजोगेहिं, पसत्थ दमसासणे ॥१९.९४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Appasattehiṃ dārehiṃ, savvao pihiyāsave |
Añjhappajhāṇajogheḥiṃ, pasattha damasāsane || 19.94 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"With all inauspicious gates closed, all āsravas blocked from all directions — through the yoga of inner, auspicious meditation — he was steady in the praised discipline of self-restraint."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **appasattehiṃ dārehiṃ** — with inauspicious/unworthy gates/channels (aśubha āśrava-dvāras — the sources of inauspicious karma inflow)
- **savvao** — from all directions, completely
- **pihiyāsave** — with āsravas blocked (karma-inflow stopped)
- **añjhappajhāṇajogheḥiṃ** — through the yoga of inner, spiritual, auspicious meditation (adhyātma-dhyāna)
- **pasattha damasāsane** — in the praised, noble discipline of self-restraint/saṃyama

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the technical achievement of Mṛgaputra's practice: the blocking of all āsravas (sources of karma inflow) through the power of inner meditation. "Savvao pihiyāsave" — blocked from all directions — implies completeness: no single gate remains through which karma can enter. This is the state of saṃvara (stoppage of karma inflow), the penultimate stage before nirjarā (shedding of existing karma) and ultimately mokṣa. The instrument of this achievement is adhyātma-dhyāna — deep inner meditation that is not merely focused thought but a qualitative transformation of the soul's orientation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Through deep inner meditation, Mṛgaputra blocked every pathway through which new karma could enter his soul — settling firmly into the highest discipline of self-restraint.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What are the "open gates" in your life through which karma freely flows in — unguarded moments of reaction, habitual patterns of craving or aversion? What would it mean to begin closing them, one by one?

## SUTRA 19.95

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं णाणेण चरणेण, दंसणेण तवेण य ।
भावणाहिं य सुद्धाहिं, सम्मं भावेत्तु अप्पयं ॥१९.९५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ ṇāṇeṇa caraṇeṇa, daṃsaṇeṇa taveṇa ya |
Bhāvaṇāhiṃ ya suddhāhiṃ, sammaṃ bhāvettu appayaṃ || 19.95 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"In this way — through knowledge, through conduct, through insight, through austerity, and through pure contemplations — he thoroughly cultivated his own soul."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evaṃ** — in this way, thus
- **ṇāṇeṇa** — through jñāna (right knowledge)
- **caraṇeṇa** — through cāritra (right conduct)
- **daṃsaṇeṇa** — through darśana (right insight/faith)
- **taveṇa ya** — and through tapa (austerity)
- **bhāvaṇāhiṃ** — through bhāvanās (contemplations, meditative reflections)
- **suddhāhiṃ** — pure, uncontaminated
- **sammaṃ** — rightly, thoroughly, in the proper way
- **bhāvettu** — having cultivated, having deeply imbued
- **appayaṃ** — his own soul (ātman)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse is the structural summary of Mṛgaputra's spiritual path, naming its five pillars: jñāna (knowledge), cāritra (conduct), darśana (insight), tapa (austerity), and śuddha-bhāvanā (pure contemplation). The combination of these five, applied "sammam" — rightly, in the proper way — constitutes the complete Jain path of liberation. "Bhāvettu appayaṃ" — cultivated his own soul — is the goal: the soul that cultivates itself through these instruments transforms from within. No external force accomplishes this. The soul transforms itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Through right knowledge, right conduct, right insight, austerity, and pure meditation — Mṛgaputra cultivated and transformed his own soul from within.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of the five — knowledge, conduct, insight, austerity, and contemplation — which is most alive in your spiritual life right now? Which is most dormant? What would happen if you gave just a little more to the dormant one?

## SUTRA 19.96

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

बहुयाणि उ वासाणि, सामण्णमणुपालिया ।
मासिएण उ भत्तेण, सिद्धिं पत्तो अणुत्तरं ॥१९.९६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Bahuyāṇi u vāsāṇi, sāmaṇṇamaṇupāliyā |
Māsieṇa u bhattena, siddhiṃ patto aṇuttaraṃ || 19.96 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Having observed the monastic life for many years — and at the end, through a one-month santhārā (fast unto death) — he attained the supreme, unsurpassed Siddhi."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **bahuyāṇi u vāsāṇi** — for many years
- **sāmaṇṇam** — the śramaṇa (monastic) life, the Jain monastic discipline
- **aṇupāliyā** — having observed, having followed/protected
- **māsieṇa u bhattena** — through a month-long fast (māsika bhakta-pratyākhyāna — one-month total fasting)
- **siddhiṃ patto** — he attained Siddhi (the liberated state)
- **aṇuttaraṃ** — the supreme/unsurpassed (Siddhi-gati, the highest state)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Gujarati commentary notes that the exact number of years is not specified in the text, and that since Mṛgaputra was a soul from the time of Ajitanātha Bhagavān, his practice may have extended across crores or arba (billions) of years. The concluding act is the santhārā — a voluntary, conscious, one-month fast unto physical death, fully accepted with equanimity. This is not suicide but the Jain ideal of death in meditation: the final expression of complete non-attachment to the body. Through this, Mṛgaputra attains "aṇuttaraṃ siddhi" — the unsurpassed liberation, the Siddha state.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: After many years of perfect monastic practice, Mṛgaputra fasted completely for a full month — and at the end of that fast, reached liberation: the supreme, undying state of the Siddha.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What is the "final fast" — the final letting go — that your spiritual life is moving toward? Not necessarily physical, but the deepest attachment you sense that will one day need to be completely released?

## SUTRA 19.97

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं करंति संबुद्धा, पंडिया पविवक्खणा ।
विणियट्टंति भोगेसु, मियपुत्ते जहा रिसी ॥१९.९७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ karaṃti saṃbuddhā, paṃḍiyā pavivakkhaṇā |
Viṇiyaṭṭaṃti bhogesu, miyaputte jahā risī || 19.97 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Thus do the awakened, the wise, the discerning ones act — they turn away from pleasures — just as Mṛgaputra, the sage, did."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **evaṃ karaṃti** — thus they act, in this way they do
- **saṃbuddhā** — the fully awakened (those who have attained right understanding)
- **paṃḍiyā** — the wise ones
- **pavivakkhaṇā** — the discerning, the clear-sighted
- **viṇiyaṭṭaṃti** — they turn away from, they withdraw from
- **bhogesu** — from the pleasures and enjoyments
- **miyaputte** — Mṛgaputra
- **jahā risī** — just as the sage (ṛṣi — the royal sage who became a monk)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse universalizes the lesson: what Mṛgaputra did is what all the awakened do. The chapter is not the story of one exceptional young prince — it is the pattern of how wisdom operates in any soul. "Saṃbuddhā, paṃḍiyā, pavivakkhaṇā" — the fully awakened, the wise, the discerning — these three terms together form a comprehensive portrait of the spiritually mature soul: one who has woken up, one who has understood, one who sees clearly. All three "viṇiyaṭṭaṃti bhogesu" — turn away from pleasures. Not by force, not by fear, but by clarity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: This is what truly wise and awake people do — they turn away from sensory pleasures, just as Mṛgaputra the sage did. His path is the path of all genuine seekers.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to turn away from a pleasure not because it is forbidden but because you clearly see that it no longer leads where you want to go?

तवप्पहाणं चरियं च उत्तमं, गइप्पहाणं च तिलोगविस्सुयं ॥१९.९८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Mahāpabhāvassa mahājasassa, miyāi puttassa ṇisammaṃ bhāsiyaṃ |

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Having heard the words of Mṛgaputra — who is of great radiance and great fame — his supreme conduct that is foremost in austerity, his ultimate destination that is renowned throughout the three worlds — practice the supreme path."

Simply put: Having heard the story of Mṛgaputra — his great radiance, his supreme austerity, his liberation famous throughout all three worlds — now go and practice that same supreme path.

## SUTRA 19.99

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वियाणिया दुक्खविवद्धणं धणं, ममत्तबंधं च महाभयावहं ।
सुहावहं धम्मधुर अणुत्तर, धारेज्ज णिव्वाण गुणावहं महं ॥१९.९९॥

ति बेमि ।

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Viyāṇiyā dukkha-vivaddhaṇaṃ dhanaṃ, mamattabandhaṃ ca mahābhayāvahaṃ |
Suhāvahaṃ dhammadhura aṇuttara, dhārejja nivvāṇa guṇāvahaṃ mahaṃ ||19.99||

Iti bemi.

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"Knowing that wealth increases suffering, that the bond of attachment brings supreme fear — bear the supreme dharma-burden, which brings happiness and confers the qualities of Nirvāṇa." Thus I speak.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **viyāṇiyā** — knowing, having understood (vijñāyā)
- **dukkha-vivaddhaṇaṃ** — increasing/multiplying suffering
- **dhanaṃ** — wealth, property
- **mamattabandhaṃ ca** — and the bond of attachment (mamatta = sense of "mine")
- **mahābhayāvahaṃ** — bringing supreme fear, causing great terror
- **suhāvahaṃ** — that which brings happiness
- **dhammadhura** — the dharma-burden, the yoke of dharma (the weight of spiritual practice borne willingly)
- **aṇuttara** — supreme, unsurpassed
- **dhārejja** — one should bear, one should uphold
- **nivvāṇa guṇāvahaṃ** — which confers/brings the qualities of Nirvāṇa
- **mahaṃ** — great, the great one (referring to the supreme dharma-yoke)
- **Iti bemi** — Thus I speak (Mahāvīra's traditional closing formula: "This is what I, the Tīrthaṃkara, declare")

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the final sutra and iti bemi — the seal of Mahāvīra. The closing teaching synthesizes the entire chapter: wealth, understood clearly, increases suffering; attachment, understood clearly, is the source of the greatest fear. The counter to both is the "dharma-dhura" — the dharma-burden — which sounds like a weight but is described as "suhāvahaṃ" — happiness-bringing — and as "nirvāṇa-guṇāvahaṃ" — conferring the qualities of liberation. The iti bemi — "Thus I speak" — is Mahāvīra's voice directly: this is not anonymous scripture but his personal declaration. The chapter opened with a story. It closes with a command.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Knowing that wealth multiplies suffering and that attachment is the root of the greatest fear — pick up the yoke of spiritual practice willingly. It is the one thing that brings real happiness and leads to liberation. Thus speaks Mahāvīra.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What burden are you carrying right now that is making you miserable — and what burden, if you picked it up willingly, might make you free?
