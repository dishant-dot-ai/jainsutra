# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 22
# Rathanemīya (The Chapter of Rathanemi)

**Total Sutras:** 51
**Theme:** The chapter centers on Ariṣṭanemi (the 22nd Tīrthaṃkara), his renunciation upon seeing animals caged for his wedding feast, and his fiancée Rājematī's subsequent dīkṣā. The second half narrates Rathanemi's (Ariṣṭanemi's younger kinsman) fall from saṃyama and his restoration by Rājematī's courageous and precise teaching — making this chapter the Uttaradhyayana's foremost text on temptation, spiritual firmness, and the power of right speech.

---

## SUTRA 22.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सोरियपुरम्मि णयरे, आसी राया महिड्ढिए ।
वसुदेव ति णामेणं, रायलक्खण संजुए ॥२२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Soriyapurammi ṇayare, āsī rāyā mahiḍḍhie |
Vasudeva ti ṇāmeṇaṃ, rāyalakkhaṇa saṃjue || 22.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the city of Śoryapura there lived a king of great splendor named Vasudeva, endowed with royal marks.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Soriyapurammi ṇayare** — in the city of Śoryapura
- **mahiḍḍhie** — of great prosperity/splendor
- **Vasudeva ti ṇāmeṇaṃ** — named Vasudeva
- **rāyalakkhaṇa saṃjue** — endowed with royal marks (cakra, svastika, etc.)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens in Śoryapura — the Jain name for the city associated with the Yādava clan (corresponding roughly to Śaurīpura/Sauripura near modern Agra/Mathura). Vasudeva is the Jain counterpart of the Vasudeva of the Mahābhārata tradition. In Jain cosmology, each half-time cycle (avasarpiṇī) produces 63 great souls — 24 Tīrthaṃkaras, 12 Cakravartins, 9 Baladevas, 9 Vāsudevas, and 9 Prativāsudevas. Vasudeva here is one of the nine Vāsudevas; his son Kṛṣṇa (Keśava) is another. The dynasty of Śoryapura is thus the precise historical-cosmological setting into which Ariṣṭanemi, the 22nd Tīrthaṃkara, is born.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: In the city of Śoryapura there ruled the splendid king Vasudeva — the patriarch of the Yādava line in Jain narrative.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Every great spiritual event arrives within a specific family, city, and time. What does it mean that even a Tīrthaṃkara is born into a particular historical context?

## SUTRA 22.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स भज्जा दुवे आसी, रोहिणी देवई तहा ।
तासिं दोणहं दुवे पुत्ता, इट्ठा रामकेसवा ॥२२.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tassa bhajjā duve āsī, Rohinī Devaī tahā |
Tāsiṃ doṇhaṃ duve puttā, iṭṭhā Rāmakesavā || 22.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

He had two wives — Rohinī and Devakī. To both of them were two beloved sons: Rāma (Baladeva) and Keśava (Kṛṣṇa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tassa bhajjā duve** — his two wives
- **Rohinī Devaī tahā** — Rohinī and Devakī
- **tāsiṃ doṇhaṃ** — of those two
- **duve puttā iṭṭhā** — two beloved sons
- **Rāmakesavā** — Rāma (Baladeva, son of Rohinī) and Keśava (Kṛṣṇa, son of Devakī)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Jain account of the Kṛṣṇa story — narrated across multiple Āgamas — aligns with but differs from the Purāṇic version. Here Baladeva (Rāma) and Kṛṣṇa (Keśava) are half-brothers, each the beloved son of one of Vasudeva's queens. Their presence in this chapter is significant: Kṛṣṇa is the one who will negotiate Rājematī's engagement to Ariṣṭanemi, accompany the wedding procession, and ultimately bless both Ariṣṭanemi and Rājematī upon their renunciation. He functions not as an obstacle to liberation but as its witness and facilitator.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Vasudeva had two queens — Rohinī and Devakī. Each gave him a son: Rāma and Kṛṣṇa — the beloved pair.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Even within a single family, two different births produce two different paths. What determines why siblings raised in the same home make different choices?

## SUTRA 22.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सोरियपुरम्मि णयरे, आसी राया महिड्ढिए ।
समुद्विजए णामं, रायलक्खण संजुए ॥२२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Soriyapurammi ṇayare, āsī rāyā mahiḍḍhie |
Samudavijae ṇāmaṃ, rāyalakkhaṇa saṃjue || 22.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Also in the city of Śoryapura there lived a king of great splendor named Samudravijaya, endowed with royal marks.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Samudavijae ṇāmaṃ** — named Samudravijaya
- All other terms same as Sutra 1

### 5. COMMENTARY

Samudravijaya was the elder brother of Vasudeva — making Ariṣṭanemi (his son) a cousin of Kṛṣṇa and Baladeva. The Jain genealogy is precise: two brothers, two kingdoms (or two royal households in the same city), and from one lineage the Vāsudeva (Kṛṣṇa), from the other the Tīrthaṃkara (Ariṣṭanemi). This cosmic symmetry — two supreme souls (mahāpuruṣas) born in the same generation, same city, related family — is one of the most theologically loaded facts in Jain narrative. Their paths diverge completely: one attains liberation, one rules the world.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: In the same city of Śoryapura, Vasudeva's elder brother Samudravijaya also ruled — and from his family would come the Tīrthaṃkara Ariṣṭanemi.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Two cousins born in the same dynasty — one becomes a world-ruler, one becomes a Tīrthaṃkara. What internal difference creates such divergent destinies?

## SUTRA 22.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्स भज्जा सिवा णाम, तीसे पुत्तो महायसो ।
भगवं अरिट्टणेमि ति, लोगणाहे दमीसरे ॥२२.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tassa bhajjā Sivā ṇāma, tīse putto mahāyaso |
Bhagavaṃ Ariṭṭaṇemi ti, logaṇāhe damīsare || 22.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

His wife was named Śivā. Their son was the greatly glorious Bhagavān Ariṣṭanemi — lord of the three worlds and supreme master of the senses.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Sivā ṇāma** — named Śivā
- **mahāyaso** — of great glory/fame
- **Bhagavaṃ Ariṭṭaṇemi** — Bhagavān Ariṣṭanemi
- **logaṇāhe** — lord of the (three) worlds
- **damīsare** — supreme master/subduer of the senses

### 5. COMMENTARY

Ariṣṭanemi's name carries meaning: ariṣṭa = harmless, nemi = wheel-rim. The "indestructible wheel-rim" — the soul that cannot be broken, that moves without harming. Damīsara (Sanskrit: damīśvara) — lord who subdues — is applied here to his mastery of the senses from birth. Unlike ordinary souls who must work to subdue the senses, the Tīrthaṃkara arrives with this mastery already established. Bhagavān — the Blessed One — is the standard Jain honorific for a Tīrthaṃkara, carrying the full weight of one who possesses all six characteristics of bhaga: power, glory, renown, prosperity, wisdom, and dispassion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Samudravijaya's wife Śivā bore him a son of great glory — Bhagavān Ariṣṭanemi, lord of the three worlds and master of the senses from birth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

"Lord of the three worlds and master of the senses" — which of these two, in your experience, is the greater achievement?

## SUTRA 22.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सो अरिट्टुणेमि णामो य, लक्खणस्सरसंजुओ ।
अट्टुसहस्स-लक्खणधरो, गोयमो कालगच्छवी ॥२२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

So Ariṭṭuṇemi ṇāmo ya, lakkhaṇassarasaṃjuo |
Aṭṭusahassa-lakkhaṇadharo, Goyamo kālagacchavī || 22.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That Ariṣṭanemi was endowed with auspicious marks and beautiful voice, bearing 1008 auspicious marks, of Gautama gotra and dark complexion.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **lakkhaṇassarasaṃjuo** — endowed with auspicious marks and sweet voice
- **aṭṭusahassa-lakkhaṇadharo** — bearer of 1008 auspicious marks
- **Goyamo** — of Gautama gotra
- **kālagacchavī** — of dark/blue-black (Kṛṣṇa) complexion

### 5. COMMENTARY

The 1008 marks (aṭṭasahassa-lakṣaṇa) is the standard Jain enumeration of the auspicious physical characteristics of a Tīrthaṃkara — analogous to the 32 mahāpuruṣa-lakṣaṇas of Buddhist tradition, but vastly expanded. Gautama gotra — the same lineage as Indrabhūti Gautama, Mahāvīra's chief disciple — establishes the clan. Kālagacchavī (dark complexion, Kṛṣṇa-varṇa) is consistent with the Jain iconographic tradition of Ariṣṭanemi being represented in blue-black color, in contrast to the golden-colored Mahāvīra. This sutra grounds the abstract (lord of worlds) in the physical: a specific body, gotra, and appearance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Ariṣṭanemi bore 1008 auspicious marks, had a beautiful voice, was of Gautama lineage, and dark in complexion.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean for a spiritual being to be described so precisely in physical terms — skin color, posture, voice? What is the relationship between the outer form and the inner realization?

## SUTRA 22.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वज्जिरसहसंघयणो, समचउरंसो झसोयरो ।
तस्स राईमई कण्णं, भज्जं जायइ केसवो ॥२२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Vajjirasahasaṃghayaṇo, samacauraṃso jhasoyaro |
Tassa Rāīmaī kaṇṇaṃ, bhajjaṃ jāyai Kesavo || 22.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Of vajra-ṛṣabha-nārāca constitution, samacaturasra form, and fish-belly shaped abdomen — Keśava (Kṛṣṇa) sought for him Rāīmatī (Rājematī) as wife.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **vajjirasahasaṃghayaṇo** — of vajra-ṛṣabha-nārāca skeletal constitution (the highest Jain bodily structure)
- **samacauraṃso** — of samacaturasra bodily form (perfectly proportioned)
- **jhasoyaro** — fish-belly shaped abdomen (beautiful, tapered)
- **Rāīmaī kaṇṇaṃ** — the daughter Rājematī
- **Kesavo** — Keśava (Kṛṣṇa) sought on his behalf

### 5. COMMENTARY

Vajra-ṛṣabha-nārāca saṃhanana is the highest of the six types of bodily constitution in Jain physiology — the bones interlocked like vajra (diamond), ṛṣabha (bull), and nārāca (spike). This is the body that can endure the most intense tapas without breaking. Samacaturasra — perfectly proportioned in all directions: sitting and standing, the body's span equals its height, the extremities are equal. These are the marks of the Tīrthaṃkara's body. That Kṛṣṇa arranged this marriage is narratively important — he functions as the matchmaker for his own cousin, the Tīrthaṃkara, which makes his grief and respect at the subsequent renunciation all the more significant.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Ariṣṭanemi had the highest bodily constitution and perfect proportions. Kṛṣṇa arranged for Rājematī, daughter of King Ugrasena, to become his bride.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Kṛṣṇa arranges the wedding of a Tīrthaṃkara — and yet that Tīrthaṃkara renounces. What does it feel like to arrange something for someone, and then watch them choose a completely different path?

## SUTRA 22.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह सा रायवरकण्णा, सुसीला चारुपेहिणी ।
सव्वलक्खणसंपण्णा, विज्जुसोयामिणप्पभा ॥२२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha sā rāyavarakaṇṇā, susīlā cārupehinī |
Savvalakkhaṇasaṃpaṇṇā, vijjusoyāmiṇapphabhā || 22.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

She was the foremost daughter of the king — virtuous, beautiful in gait, endowed with all auspicious marks, with the splendor of a streak of flashing lightning.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **rāyavarakaṇṇā** — foremost daughter of the king
- **susīlā** — virtuous, of good character
- **cārupehinī** — of beautiful gait/bearing
- **savvalakkhaṇasaṃpaṇṇā** — endowed with all auspicious marks
- **vijjusoyāmiṇapphabhā** — with the splendor of a lightning-streak

### 5. COMMENTARY

The description of Rājematī is careful: she is not merely physically beautiful (though the lightning-splendor image is striking) but susīlā — of good character, virtuous. Her entire profile as it will unfold — the dignified grief, the courageous dīkṣā, the fierce teaching of Rathanemi — is consistent with this inner quality established from the start. The lightning metaphor is precise: lightning is radiant, powerful, momentary, and purifying. These qualities will all manifest in Rājematī's pivotal moment in the cave.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Rājematī was the king's finest daughter — virtuous, graceful, bearing all auspicious marks, and radiant as a bolt of lightning.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The text describes Rājematī's inner quality (susīlā) before her outer beauty. What does it reveal about a tradition's values — which quality it names first?

## SUTRA 22.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहाह जणओ तीसे, वासुदेवं महिड्ढियं ।
इहागच्छउ कुमारो, जा से कण्णं ददामिहं ॥२२.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ahāha jaṇao tīse, Vāsudevaṃ mahiḍḍhiyaṃ |
Ihāgacchau kumāro, jā se kaṇṇaṃ dadāmihaṃ || 22.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then her father said to the great Vāsudeva: "Let the prince come here — I will give him my daughter."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jaṇao** — father (of Rājematī — King Ugrasena)
- **Vāsudevaṃ mahiḍḍhiyaṃ** — to the great Vāsudeva (Kṛṣṇa)
- **Ihāgacchau kumāro** — let the prince come here
- **kaṇṇaṃ dadāmihaṃ** — I will give (my) daughter (to him)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The formal consent of the bride's father is the social anchor of the marriage arrangement. Ugrasena's willingness is unqualified — he gives his word directly to Kṛṣṇa. The irony that will unfold is radical: every human institution — betrothal, consent, wedding procession, divine ceremony — moves forward perfectly, and yet the wedding will never happen. The Tīrthaṃkara's path cannot be intercepted by social arrangement, however legitimate. The text does not present Ariṣṭanemi's renunciation as a violation of dharma — it presents it as the higher dharma superseding the conventional one.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Rājematī's father, King Ugrasena, gave Kṛṣṇa his word: "Let the prince come — I will give him my daughter."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Every plan, every promise, every arrangement can be superseded by a higher calling. When has a "higher dharma" interrupted your own carefully made plans?

## SUTRA 22.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वोसहीहिं णहवियो, कयकोउयमंगलो ।
दिव्वजुयल-परिहिओ, आभरणेहिं विभूसिओ ॥२२.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Savvosahīhiṃ ṇahavio, kayakouyamaṃgalo |
Divvajuyala-parihio, ābharaṇehiṃ vibhūsio || 22.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Ariṣṭanemi was bathed in all medicinal herbs, auspicious rites performed, clothed in a divine garment-pair, and adorned with ornaments.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **savvosahīhiṃ ṇahavio** — bathed in water mixed with all medicinal substances
- **kayakouyamaṃgalo** — auspicious (pre-wedding) rites performed
- **divvajuyala-parihio** — clothed in a divine pair of garments
- **ābharaṇehiṃ vibhūsio** — adorned with ornaments

### 5. COMMENTARY

The pre-wedding preparation described here follows classical Indian ritual: the medicinal bath (combining herbs associated with victory, health, and auspiciousness), the kautuka-maṃgala (the preliminary rites of the groom including forehead-marking and sacred thread rituals), and the donning of the wedding garments. Every action here is a mark of transition — from the ordinary to the extraordinary, from boyhood to marriage. The text dwells on these preparations precisely because they are about to be rendered irrelevant. The garments that Ariṣṭanemi wears for his wedding will become the ones he removes in renunciation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Ariṣṭanemi was prepared for his wedding — bathed in herbal water, auspicious rites performed, dressed in divine garments and ornaments.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The most elaborate preparations for one life-event become the occasion for an entirely different event. Has preparation for one thing ever unexpectedly opened the door to something else?

## SUTRA 22.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मत्तं च गंधहत्थिं च, वासुदेवस्स जेट्टुगं ।
आरूढो सोहए अहियं, सिरे चूडामणी जहा ॥२२.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Mattaṃ ca gaṃdhahatthiṃ ca, Vāsudevassa jeṭṭugaṃ |
Ārūḍho sohae ahiyaṃ, sire cūḍāmaṇī jahā || 22.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Mounted on Vāsudeva's foremost intoxicated elephant (Gandhahastin), he shone exceedingly — like a crest-jewel upon the head.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **mattaṃ gaṃdhahatthiṃ** — the intoxicated (mada-flowing) elephant Gandhahastin
- **Vāsudevassa jeṭṭugaṃ** — Vāsudeva's foremost/principal elephant
- **ārūḍho** — mounted upon
- **sohae ahiyaṃ** — shone exceedingly
- **sire cūḍāmaṇī jahā** — like a crest-jewel on the head

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gandhahastin — the "fragrant elephant" — was Kṛṣṇa's principal war elephant, the most powerful in the Yādava army. That Ariṣṭanemi rides this elephant to his wedding is a sign of maximum honor. The crest-jewel simile (cūḍāmaṇī) is one of the most common metaphors in Sanskrit literature for something that crowns, completes, or surpasses all else. Ariṣṭanemi mounted on the war elephant moving toward his wedding is, in this moment, the most honored figure in the world — and within a few verses, he will be the most renounced.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Ariṣṭanemi rode Kṛṣṇa's great war elephant Gandhahastin and shone like a jewel set at the crown of the world.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The highest worldly honor — riding the king's greatest elephant — precedes the highest act of renunciation. What must one have fully in order to genuinely release it?

## SUTRA 22.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह ऊसिएण छत्तेण, चामराहि य सोहिए ।
दसारचक्केण य सो, सव्वओ परिवारिओ ॥२२.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha ūsieṇa chattteṇa, cāmarāhi ya sohie |
Dasāracakkeṇa ya so, savvao parivārio || 22.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then adorned with a raised royal umbrella and fly-whisks, and surrounded on all sides by the ten-fold Daśāra (Yādava) clan — he shone greatly.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ūsieṇa chatteṇa** — with a raised royal umbrella
- **cāmarāhi** — with fly-whisks (cāmaras)
- **Dasāracakkeṇa** — by the Daśāra circle (the ten Yādava clans headed by Samudravijaya, Akṣobha, etc.)
- **savvao parivārio** — surrounded on all sides

### 5. COMMENTARY

The three royal insignia — umbrella, fly-whisks, and clan-entourage — constitute the fullest possible display of worldly sovereignty and honor. The Daśāra (ten Yādava clans) are named elsewhere as: Samudravijaya, Akṣobha, Stimita, Sāgara, Himavān, Acala, Dharaṇa, Pūrṇa, Abhicandra, and Vasudeva. This entire apparatus of worldly greatness accompanies Ariṣṭanemi. The procession is at its most magnificent — and its most brief.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: With royal umbrella, fly-whisks, and the full Yādava clan surrounding him on all sides — Ariṣṭanemi moved in the grandeur of a cosmic king.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Surrounded by the most powerful family on earth, at the peak of worldly ceremony — what must it feel like to choose, in that moment, to see through it all?

## SUTRA 22.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चउरंगिणीए सेणाए, रइयाए जहक्कमं ।
तुरियाण सणिणाएण, दिव्वेण गगणं फुसे ॥२२.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Cauraṃgiṇīe seṇāe, raiyāe jahakkhamaṃ |
Turiyāṇa saṇiṇāeṇa, divveṇa gagaṇaṃ phuse || 22.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

With the fourfold army arrayed in proper formation, the sky resounded with the divine sound of instruments — drums, conches, and music.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **cauraṃgiṇīe seṇāe** — fourfold army (elephants, horses, chariots, infantry)
- **raiyāe jahakkhamaṃ** — arrayed according to proper order
- **turiyāṇa saṇiṇāeṇa divveṇa** — with the divine sound of musical instruments
- **gagaṇaṃ phuse** — the sky was filled/touched

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fourfold army (caturaṃginī senā) is the classical Sanskrit designation of a complete military force — elephants, horses, chariots, and foot soldiers. The sky filled with divine sound is a standard narrative marker of a mahāpuruṣa's procession. The entire world — military, musical, cosmic — participates in this moment of apparent triumph. Yet the text is building toward a pivot so profound that all of this will dissolve within a few more gathas.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The complete fourfold army moved in perfect formation, and divine music filled the sky — the grandest procession imaginable for a Tīrthaṃkara's wedding.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When everything aligns perfectly around an event — what makes it the occasion of an awakening rather than a further deepening of attachment?

## SUTRA 22.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयारिसाए इड्ढीए, जुईए उत्तमाइ य ।
णियगाओ भवणाओ, णिज्जाओ वणिहपुंगवो ॥२२.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Eyārisāe iḍḍhīe, juīe uttamāi ya |
Ṇiyagāo bhavaṇāo, ṇijjāo vaṇihapuṃgavo || 22.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

With such excellent prosperity and supreme splendor, the foremost of the Vṛṣṇi clan departed from his home.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **eyārisāe iḍḍhīe** — with such prosperity/splendor
- **juīe uttamāi** — and supreme brilliance/radiance
- **ṇiyagāo bhavaṇāo** — from his own home
- **ṇijjāo** — departed

- **carittteṇa** — in conduct (cāritra)
- **taveṇa** — in austerity (tapas)
- **khaṃtīe** — in forbearance/patience (kṣānti)
- **muttīe** — in liberation (mukti)
- **vaḍḍumāṇo bhavāhi** — may you grow/increase

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kṛṣṇa's blessing enumerates the complete path of liberation: jñāna-darśana-cāritra (the Jain triple-gem of right knowledge, right vision, right conduct) plus tapas (austerity), kṣānti (forbearance as the crown of all virtues), and mukti (liberation itself). This is not just a blessing — it is a catechism. Kṛṣṇa, who will not achieve liberation in this birth, articulates the complete path for the one who will. He knows the path even if he cannot walk it yet. This verse is among the most cited in Jain ritual — used as a benediction at all saṃyama-related ceremonies.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Kṛṣṇa blessed Ariṣṭanemi: "May you grow in knowledge, vision, conduct, austerity, forbearance, and liberation."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Someone who cannot yet walk a path can still bless those who can. What spiritual qualities do you most wish to grow in — and can you identify at least one person who has walked further on that path than you?

- **evaṃ kareṃti** — act in this way
- **bhogesu viṇiyaṭṭuṃti** — turn away from sensual pleasures
- **purisuttamo** — the foremost of men (Rathanemi)
- **iti bemi** — thus I say

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing formula is theologically precise about what Rathanemi represents. Purisuttama — the foremost of men. Not despite his fall, but including it. The text calls him the foremost of men precisely because of the complete arc of his story: the fall, the restoration, the firm vows, and the liberation. The teaching of this verse is not about perfect conduct — it is about bhuttabhogī tao paccā jiṇamaggaṃ carisssāmo: the one who truly turned away from sensuality after seeing it clearly and choosing against it, not just by initial vow but through the test of temptation. The truly awakened one is not the one who was never tempted, but the one who, having been tempted and having chosen rightly, does not return. Thus I say.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: This is what the awakened, wise, and discerning do — they turn away from pleasures. The foremost of men (Rathanemi) showed how. Thus it is said.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The "foremost of men" is someone who fell, was restored by a woman's courageous speech, and then practiced with unwavering firmness until liberation. What kind of greatness is this — and how does it differ from a greatness that never faltered?
