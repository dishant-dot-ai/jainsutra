# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 35
# Aṇagāra Mārga Gati (The Path of the Homeless Ascetic)

**Total Sutras:** 21
**Theme:** This chapter provides a complete set of practical instructions for the homeless ascetic. It covers the five great vows in their active form, the monk's relationship to dwelling, the prohibition of house-building and cooking, the rejection of fire, commerce, and precious metals, the correct method of seeking alms, the inner attitude toward food and honor, the practice of deep meditation, and the final act of relinquishing the body at death — culminating in the attainment of omniscience and eternal liberation.

---

## SUTRA 35.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुणेह मे एगग्गमणा, मग्गं बुद्धेहिं देसियं ।
जमायरंतो भिक्खू, दुक्खाणंतकरे भवे ॥३५.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Suṇeha me egaggamaṇā, maggaṃ buddhehi desiyaṃ |
Jamāyaraṃto bhikkhu, dukkhaṇaṃtakare bhave || 35.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

With concentrated mind, listen from me to the path shown by the Omniscient; the monk who practices it becomes the ender of suffering.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **buddhehi** — by the Omniscient Lord (the all-knowing one)
- **desiyaṃ** — shown, taught, indicated
- **maggaṃ** — the path
- **me** — from me
- **egaggamaṇā** — with concentrated, one-pointed mind
- **suṇeha** — listen
- **jaṃ** — which
- **āyaraṃto** — practicing, following
- **bhikkhu** — monk, ascetic
- **dukkhaṇaṃ** — of suffering
- **aṃtakare** — the ender, the one who brings to an end
- **bhave** — becomes

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening verse establishes the purpose and tone of the entire chapter. The speaker invites monks to listen with undivided, single-pointed attention to the path of the homeless ascetic as taught by the Omniscient One. The emphasis on concentrated listening signals that this path requires more than intellectual understanding — it demands deep, undistracted receptivity. The teaching originates from the all-knowing one, which establishes its absolute reliability. The promise is direct and unambiguous: the monk who actually practices this path becomes the one who brings all suffering to its end. The phrase "from me" suggests a living transmission — a teacher passing the teaching personally to students ready to receive it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Listen carefully with your full attention — here is the path the all-knowing one showed, and a monk who truly follows it will end all his suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Am I truly listening to spiritual guidance with my full, undivided attention — or am I hearing it while distracted by other thoughts?

## SUTRA 35.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गिहवासं परिच्छज्ज, पव्वज्जामस्सिए मुणी ।
इमे संगे वियाणिज्जा, जेहिं सज्जंति माणवा ॥३५.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Gihavāsaṃ pariccajja, pavvajjāmassie muṇī |
Ime saṃge viyāṇijjā, jehiṃ sajjaṃti māṇavā || 35.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who has renounced household life and taken refuge in renunciation should recognize these attachments by which human beings become entangled.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **gihavāsaṃ** — household life
- **pariccajja** — having renounced, abandoned
- **pavvajjaṃ** — renunciation, monastic initiation
- **assie** — having taken refuge in, having embraced
- **muṇī** — monk, sage
- **ime** — these
- **saṃge** — attachments, companions (mother, father, son, wife, etc.)
- **viyāṇijjā** — should recognize, should know clearly
- **jehiṃ** — by which
- **sajjaṃti** — become entangled, become attached
- **māṇavā** — humans, people

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having introduced the path, this verse identifies the first fundamental challenge for the renunciant: the pull of domestic relationships. The monk has outwardly left household life, yet the emotional bonds with parents, spouse, and children continue to exert a powerful pull from within. The instruction is not to hate these people but to "recognize" — to clearly see the nature of these connections as bonds that generate fresh karmic entanglement. This clear seeing is described as wisdom, not coldness or rejection. The scripture specifically names this as the first teaching for the renunciant because even after initiation, many monks drift back into worldly thinking through these very connections. Awareness of the trap is the first step toward freedom from it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Once someone becomes a monk, they should clearly understand that family relationships — parents, spouse, children — are the very things that keep humans tied to the world.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which relationships in my life am I truly free from — and which do I merely think I've let go of?

## SUTRA 35.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव हिंसं अलियं, चोज्जं अबंभसेवणं ।
इच्छाकामं च लोभं च, संजओ परिवज्जए ॥३५.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Taheva hiṃsaṃ aliyaṃ, cojjaṃ abaṃbhasevanaṃ |
Icchakamaṃ ca lobhaṃ ca, saṃjao parivajjae || 35.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, the disciplined monk should completely abandon violence, falsehood, stealing, the practice of non-celibacy, sensual desires, and greed.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taheva** — likewise, similarly
- **hiṃsaṃ** — violence, harm
- **aliyaṃ** — falsehood, untruth
- **cojjaṃ** — theft, stealing
- **abaṃbhasevanaṃ** — practice of non-celibacy, sexual indulgence
- **icchakamaṃ** — desires, longing for unobtained things
- **lobhaṃ** — greed, deep attachment to obtained things
- **saṃjao** — the disciplined, restrained monk
- **parivajjae** — should completely abandon, should thoroughly give up

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse presents the core ethical foundation of the homeless ascetic's life — the five great vows expressed in their practical, active form. Violence, falsehood, theft, non-celibacy, and possessiveness (expressed here as desire and greed) are recognized as the five root causes of continued bondage. The word "likewise" connects this to the previous verse — just as one must recognize the trap of personal relationships, one must also abandon these five harmful behaviors completely. The verse goes deeper than the surface acts by naming their psychological roots: desire covers unfulfilled craving, and greed covers deep attachment to what one already has. True discipline means uprooting these at the level of intention, not merely restraining overt behavior.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A disciplined monk must completely give up all forms of harm, lying, stealing, sexual activity, craving for things he doesn't have, and greed for things he does.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Of these six — violence, falsehood, theft, non-celibacy, unfulfilled desires, and greed — which one do I find easiest to justify in my own life?

## SUTRA 35.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

मणोहरं चित्तघरं, मल्लधूवेण वासियं ।
सकवाडं पंडुरुल्लोयं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ॥३५.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Maṇoharaṃ cittagharaṃ, mallhadhūveṇa vāsiyaṃ |
Sakavāḍaṃ paṃḍurulloyaṃ, maṇasā vi ṇa patthae || 35.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should not even mentally desire a beautiful, painting-adorned dwelling perfumed with garlands and incense, furnished with fine doors, and most attractively decorated.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **maṇoharaṃ** — mind-captivating, charming
- **cittagharaṃ** — dwelling adorned with paintings
- **mallhadhūveṇa** — with garlands and fragrant incense (agaru, sandalwood, etc.)
- **vāsiyaṃ** — perfumed, scented
- **sakavāḍaṃ** — with fine doors, equipped with always-closing doors
- **paṃḍurulloyaṃ** — extremely beautiful, well-decorated, attractively adorned
- **maṇasā vi** — even mentally, even in the mind
- **ṇa patthae** — should not desire, should not wish for

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having established the prohibitions on harmful behavior, the scripture now turns to the monk's relationship with his physical environment. The verse describes a house of extraordinary beauty — decorated with paintings, fragrant with incense and garlands, equipped with fine doors. The instruction is radical: the monk should not even mentally wish for such a place. This goes beyond merely refusing luxury — it targets the subtlest level of craving, which is the inner wish itself. The commentary reveals the reasoning: beautiful, sensory-rich environments feed and amplify passion-energies. What the eye finds pleasing, the mind begins to crave; what the mind craves, the will eventually pursues. The monk who desires beautiful surroundings has already begun to undermine his restraint, long before any physical indulgence occurs.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should not even silently wish for a beautiful, fragrant, decorated house — not even in his own mind.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are there beautiful or comfortable things I tell myself I don't want — but secretly do?

## SUTRA 35.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इंदियाणि उ भिक्खुस्स, तारिसम्मि उवस्सए ।
दुक्कराइं णिवारेउं, कामराग विवड्डणे ॥३५.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Iṃdiyāṇi u bhikkhussa, tārisammi upassae |
Dukkarāiṃ ṇivāreūṃ, kāmarāga vivaḍḍaṇe || 35.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For a monk staying in such a desire-inflaming dwelling, it becomes extremely difficult to restrain the senses.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tārisammi** — in such a place (as described in the previous verse)
- **kāmarāga vivaḍḍaṇe** — which increases, inflames sensual desire
- **upassae** — in a dwelling, in a lodging
- **bhikkhussa** — for the monk
- **iṃdiyāṇi** — the senses
- **ṇivāreūṃ** — to restrain, to check, to keep controlled
- **dukkarāiṃ** — very difficult, hard to accomplish

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse provides the reasoning behind the previous prohibition — the scripture does not merely issue a rule but explains exactly why. When a monk lives in a beautiful, sensory-stimulating environment, the very atmosphere amplifies the pull of desire. In such conditions, the effort required to hold the senses back from their natural inclinations multiplies enormously. This is a remarkably perceptive psychological teaching: it acknowledges that the human senses are profoundly shaped by environment. A monk is not a machine who can ignore his surroundings — his inner practice is directly affected by outer conditions. The wise monk therefore chooses his living space with great care, preferring places that support restraint rather than work against it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Staying in a beautiful, pleasant house makes it very hard for a monk to keep his senses under control — which is exactly why such places should be avoided.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in my current environment is secretly feeding desires I am trying to reduce?

## SUTRA 35.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुसाणे सुण्णगारे वा, रुक्खमूले व इक्कओ ।
पडिरिक्के परकडे वा, वास तत्थाभिरोयए ॥३५.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Susāṇe suṇṇagāre vā, rukkhamūle va ikkao |
Paḍirikke parakaḍe vā, vāsa tatthābhiroyae || 35.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should prefer to live — alone, free from attraction and aversion — in a cremation ground, an empty house, at the root of a tree, in a secluded spot, or in a place built by others.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **susāṇe** — in a cremation ground
- **suṇṇagāre** — in an empty or abandoned house
- **vā** — or
- **rukkhamūle** — at the base of a tree
- **ikkao** — alone, solitary
- **paḍirikke** — in a secluded place free from women, animals, and neuters
- **parakaḍe** — in a place built by others (by householders for their own use)
- **tattha** — there
- **abhiroyae** — should prefer to stay, should wish to dwell (free from attachment and aversion)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having described what the monk should avoid, this verse now prescribes what is appropriate. The options — cremation ground, abandoned building, tree root, secluded spot — are deliberately austere. These environments do not invite sensory proliferation or social engagement. The word "alone" is important: solitude protects the monk from the disturbances of social life, noise, and the pull of others' activities. "Built by others" means the monk does not commission or construct his own dwelling — he accepts what is already available, without generating any new harm. The phrase "free from attraction and aversion" reminds us that the austerity is not self-punishment — the goal is the equanimity of one who is genuinely at peace in simple circumstances, not the suffering of one who is forcing himself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should prefer to live alone in simple, plain places — like a cremation ground, an empty house, or under a tree — rather than seeking comfortable or attractive spots.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can I stay quietly content in a plain, ordinary setting — or do I always feel the urge to improve or beautify my surroundings?

## SUTRA 35.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

फासुयम्मि अणाबाहे, इत्थीहिं अणभिदुए ।
तत्थ संकप्पए वासं, भिक्खू परमसंजए ॥३५.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Phāsuyammi aṇābāhe, itthīhiṃ aṇabhidue |
Tattha saṃkappae vāsaṃ, bhikkhu paramasaṃjae || 35.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The supremely disciplined monk should resolve to stay in a place that is free from living organisms, free from disturbances, and free from the trouble caused by women and others.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **phāsuyammi** — free from living organisms, suitable from a non-violence standpoint (prasuka)
- **aṇābāhe** — free from disturbances, causing no obstruction to oneself or others
- **itthīhiṃ** — from women (and others whose proximity could disturb)
- **aṇabhidue** — free from trouble and disturbance caused by them
- **tattha** — in such a place
- **paramasaṃjae** — the supremely disciplined, supremely restrained
- **bhikkhu** — monk
- **vāsaṃ** — residence, stay
- **saṃkappae** — should resolve, should intentionally determine

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse adds three positive qualities that the monk's ideal dwelling should possess: freedom from organisms that could be harmed by his living there, freedom from conditions that create disturbance to his practice or to others, and freedom from persons whose proximity could unsettle his celibate discipline. The phrase "supremely disciplined" describes an aspirational standard. The teaching acknowledges that environment shapes spiritual practice, and that a monk who genuinely seeks the highest progress makes deliberate, conscious choices about where to stay. Expressing the dwelling choice as a "resolve" rather than a rule is significant — it places the responsibility inside the monk, as a matter of wisdom and intention, not merely external compliance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The best kind of monk actively chooses to stay only in places that are safe, quiet, and free from anything that could disturb his spiritual practice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What conditions in my life am I accepting as inevitable that are actually undermining my inner practice?

## SUTRA 35.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण सयं गिहाइं कुच्छिज्जा, णेव अण्णेहिं कारए ।
गिहकम्मसमारंभे, भूयाण दिस्सए वहो ॥३५.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇa sayaṃ gihāiṃ kuccijjā, ṇeva aṇṇehiṃ kārae |
Gihakammasamāraṃbhe, bhūyāṇa dissae vaho || 35.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should neither build a house himself nor have it built by others, because in the undertaking of house-building, the killing of living beings is clearly evident.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sayaṃ** — himself, on his own
- **gihāiṃ** — house
- **ṇa kuccijjā** — should not build
- **ṇeva** — nor
- **aṇṇehiṃ** — through others
- **kārae** — have it built, cause it to be made
- **gihakammasamāraṃbhe** — in the undertaking/commencement of house-building
- **bhūyāṇa** — of living beings
- **vaho** — killing, slaughter
- **dissae** — is seen, is clearly evident

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse establishes a clear prohibition with a clear reason: house construction is incompatible with the monk's commitment to non-violence because building necessarily kills living beings. Digging earth, cutting wood, mixing materials, laying foundations — each of these destroys countless life forms, many invisible to the naked eye. The prohibition covers both direct action and indirect commissioning — causing another to build on one's behalf is equally culpable, because the intention and the benefit are the same. This reflects a deep principle in Jain ethics: one who causes harm to be done, even through another's hands, shares in the karmic consequence. The monk may accept an already-existing dwelling offered freely by a householder, but he may not initiate or participate in its construction in any way.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should never build a house himself or ask someone else to build one for him — because construction always kills many living beings.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How much harm do I cause indirectly — through what I purchase, commission, or request of others — that I never think to count as my own?

## SUTRA 35.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तसाणं थावराणं च, सुहुमाणं बायराण य ।
तम्हा गिहसमारंभं, संजओ परिवज्जए ॥३५.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tasāṇaṃ thāvarāṇaṃ ca, suhūmāṇaṃ bāyarāṇa ya |
Tammhā gihasamāraṃbhaṃ, saṃjao parivajjae || 35.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Because house-building causes harm to mobile beings and stationary beings, subtle beings and gross beings — therefore the disciplined monk should completely refrain from the undertaking of house construction.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tasāṇaṃ** — of mobile beings (those that can move)
- **thāvarāṇaṃ** — of stationary beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants)
- **ca** — and
- **suhūmāṇaṃ** — of subtle beings (imperceptible to the senses)
- **bāyarāṇa** — of gross or coarse beings (perceptible)
- **ya** — and
- **tammhā** — therefore, because of this
- **gihasamāraṃbhaṃ** — the commencement/undertaking of house-building
- **saṃjao** — the disciplined monk
- **parivajjae** — should completely abandon, should thoroughly refrain from

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the explanation of the prohibition on house-building by cataloguing precisely which categories of life are harmed. Jain cosmology recognizes an enormous diversity of life — mobile beings and stationary beings, and within each, both the subtle forms invisible to human senses and the gross forms that are perceptible. Construction harms all of these simultaneously. Digging earth injures earth-bodied beings; mixing water harms water-bodied beings; using fire destroys fire-bodied and air-bodied beings; cutting trees destroys plant-life; the disturbance kills mobile creatures both large and microscopic. The monk who genuinely feels compassion for all life — not just animals visible to the eye — has an inescapable reason to abstain from any involvement in construction.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Building a house harms tiny beings and large beings, creatures that move and those that stay still — so a self-controlled monk must completely avoid it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How aware am I of the harm to invisible and tiny life forms that happens in ordinary activities I never think twice about?

## SUTRA 35.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तहेव भत्तपाणेसु, पयणे पयावणेसु य ।
पाण भूयदयट्टाए, ण पए ण पयावए ॥३५.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Taheva bhattapāṇesu, payaṇe payāvaṇesu ya |
Pāṇa bhūyadayaṭṭhāe, ṇa pae ṇa payāvae || 35.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Likewise, out of compassion for living beings and elementary organisms, the monk should neither cook food and water himself nor have it cooked by others.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **taheva** — likewise, in the same way
- **bhattapāṇesu** — in (the preparation of) food and water
- **payaṇe** — in cooking (by oneself)
- **payāvaṇesu** — in having it cooked (by others)
- **ya** — and
- **pāṇa** — living beings (from two-sensed beings upward)
- **bhūya** — elementary organisms (earth-bodied, etc.)
- **dayaṭṭhāe** — out of compassion, for the sake of protecting
- **ṇa pae** — should not cook himself
- **ṇa payāvae** — should not cause others to cook

### 5. COMMENTARY

The prohibition on cooking follows from exactly the same principle that prohibits house-building — the act of preparing food inevitably destroys life. Cooking requires fire, water, grain, and various materials; each of these harbors living beings that are killed in the process. Microscopic organisms living in water are destroyed when water is boiled; organisms inhabiting grain and vegetables die in the heat of cooking; beings living in firewood are killed the moment it is burned. The monk's practical response is to accept only food that has already been prepared by householders for their own families — he receives what already exists without generating any new harm on his behalf. The compassion motivating this practice is directed at the smallest, most overlooked creatures — beings so tiny that ordinary human life never thinks of them, yet fully possessing the desire to live.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just like building a house, cooking food — or asking others to cook for you — harms many tiny living beings, so a monk neither cooks himself nor asks others to cook for him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I think about the living beings affected by the food I eat and by the way it was prepared?

## SUTRA 35.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जलधण्णणिस्सिया जीवा, पुढवी कट्टुणिस्सिया ।
हम्मंति भत्तपाणेसु, तम्हा भिक्खू ण पयावए ॥३५.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jaladhaṇṇaṇissiyā jīvā, puḍhavī kaṭṭuṇissiyā |
Hammaṃti bhattapāṇesu, tammhā bhikkhu ṇa payāvae || 35.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Living beings that depend on water and grain, and those that depend on earth and firewood, are killed in the preparation of food and water; therefore the monk should not have food cooked.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jaladhaṇṇa-ṇissiyā** — those dependent on water and grain
- **puḍhavī-kaṭṭu-ṇissiyā** — those dependent on earth and wood/fuel
- **jīvā** — living beings
- **bhattapāṇesu** — in (the preparation of) food and water
- **hammaṃti** — are killed, are struck
- **tammhā** — therefore
- **bhikkhu** — monk
- **ṇa payāvae** — should not have it cooked (even by others)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse names specific categories of life destroyed in cooking. Beings living in or depending on water are killed when water is boiled. Beings inhabiting grain — insects, mites, microscopic organisms — die when grain is processed and cooked. Earth-bodied beings are harmed whenever earth is disturbed. Fire-bodied and air-bodied beings are destroyed in the very act of using firewood as fuel. What appears to be a simple act of boiling water or cooking rice is, at a more perceptive level, an act of mass destruction affecting multiple layers of life simultaneously. This understanding motivates the monk's practice of accepting only food that has been prepared — without any request or instigation on his part — by householders going about their own lives.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Cooking destroys many tiny beings that live in water, grain, earth, and wood — which is exactly why a monk refuses to ask others to cook for him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How differently would I relate to food if I could actually see all the life that exists within it?

## SUTRA 35.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

विसप्पे सव्वओ धारे, बहुपाणी विणासणे ।
णत्थि जोइसमे सत्थे, तम्हा जोई ण दीवए ॥३५.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Visappe savvao dhāre, bahupāṇī viṇāsaṇe |
Ṇatthi joisame satthe, tammhā joī ṇa dīvae || 35.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

There is no weapon comparable to fire — spreading in all directions like the edge of a blade, destroying multitudes of living beings; therefore the monk should not light a fire.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **savvao** — in all directions
- **dhāre** — like the edge of a weapon/blade
- **visappe** — spreading, extending
- **bahupāṇī** — of many living beings
- **viṇāsaṇe** — destroyer
- **joisame** — comparable to fire, equal to fire
- **satthe** — weapon
- **ṇatthi** — there is not, there is no
- **tammhā** — therefore
- **joī** — fire
- **ṇa dīvae** — should not light, should not kindle

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse extends the principle of non-violence to fire. The comparison of fire to a weapon is vivid and exact: just as a sword blade cuts in every direction it sweeps, fire spreads through air, consuming all in its path. But fire is even more destructive than any physical weapon — it kills beings in all directions simultaneously, without any limit on its range. Fire destroys beings living in the air it ignites, beings crawling on the ground below it, beings living in the fuel it consumes, and beings in the surrounding environment affected by its heat and smoke. This is why the monk's rule is absolute: he does not light fires for warmth, cooking, light, or any other purpose — not because warmth or light are bad, but because the price in living beings is too high.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Fire is like a weapon that spreads in every direction and destroys countless living beings — so a monk never lights a fire.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in my life am I using casually that may be causing far more harm than I realize?

## SUTRA 35.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

हिरण्णं जायरूवं च, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ।
समलेट्टु कंचणे भिक्खू, विरए कयविक्कए ॥३५.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Hiraṇṇaṃ jāyarūvaṃ ca, maṇasā vi ṇa patthae |
Samalettu kaṃcaṇe bhikkhu, virae kayavikkae || 35.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who sees no difference between a clod of mud and gold, who is completely detached from all buying and selling, should not desire silver or gold even mentally.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **hiraṇṇaṃ** — silver
- **jāyarūvaṃ** — gold
- **ca** — and
- **maṇasā vi** — even mentally, even in the mind
- **ṇa patthae** — should not desire, should not wish for
- **samalettu-kaṃcaṇe** — one who regards a clod of earth and gold as equal
- **bhikkhu** — monk
- **kayavikkae** — from buying and selling transactions
- **virae** — detached, withdrawn

### 5. COMMENTARY

The previous verses addressed non-violence. This verse turns to non-possessiveness. Gold and silver represent wealth, security, and social standing — the most condensed form of worldly value. The teaching is absolute: the monk should not merely refrain from handling money, but should reach the inner state where gold and a lump of dirt feel genuinely equivalent. This is a transformation of perception, not merely a discipline of behavior. The phrase "even mentally" rules out the situation where a monk outwardly abstains but inwardly covets — that inner covetousness is itself the bondage the teaching addresses. The additional quality of being "detached from buying and selling" confirms that this monk has stepped entirely outside the economy of want and acquisition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should feel no more interest in gold than in a lump of mud, and should have nothing whatsoever to do with buying or selling anything.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there anything I secretly value more than I think I should — something I would feel genuinely reluctant to give up?

## SUTRA 35.14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

किणंतो कइओ होइ, विक्किणंतो य वाणिओ ।
कयविक्कयम्मि वट्टंतो, भिक्खू ण भवइ तारिसो ॥३५.१४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Kiṇaṃto kaio hoi, vikkinaṃto ya vāṇio |
Kayavikkayammi vaṭṭaṃto, bhikkhu ṇa bhavai tāriso || 35.14 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One who buys becomes a customer; one who sells becomes a merchant; one engaged in buying and selling is not the kind of monk described in the scriptures.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **kiṇaṃto** — one who buys
- **kaio** — buyer, customer
- **hoi** — becomes
- **vikkinaṃto** — one who sells
- **vāṇio** — merchant, trader
- **ya** — and
- **kayavikkayammi** — in the activity of buying and selling
- **vaṭṭaṃto** — engaged in, active in
- **bhikkhu** — monk
- **ṇa bhavai** — does not become, is not
- **tāriso** — such (as described in the scriptures)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse states the incompatibility of commerce and monkhood in categorical terms. When a person engages in commercial activity — whether buying or selling — they function as an economic actor: calculating value, competing for advantage, pursuing personal gain. Both roles are fundamentally shaped by self-interest and attachment to outcomes. These mental attitudes are directly opposed to the detachment the monk is cultivating. The verse does not condemn commerce for householders — it simply observes that a person cannot simultaneously be a monk as the tradition defines it, and a market participant. The identity is mutually exclusive. The person who lives by commercial activity has already, by that choice, stepped outside the monastic role.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Someone who buys is a shopper, and someone who sells is a merchant — a monk is neither one.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In what ways do I still approach the world as someone who is trying to "get something" from it?

## SUTRA 35.15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

भिक्खियव्वं ण केयव्वं, भिक्खुणा भिक्खवत्तिणा ।
कयिवक्कओ महादोसो, भिक्खिवत्ती सुहावहा ॥३५.१५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Bhikkhiyavvaṃ ṇa keyavvaṃ, bhikkhūṇā bhikkhavattīṇā |
Kayivakko mahādoso, bhikkhivattī suhāvahā || 35.15 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk who lives by alms should sustain himself by begging alone and not purchase anything; engaging in buying and selling is a great fault, while the practice of begging is beneficial in this life and the next.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **bhikkhavattīṇā** — by the one whose livelihood is alms-seeking
- **bhikkhūṇā** — by the monk
- **bhikkhiyavvaṃ** — begging should be practiced, one should subsist by alms
- **ṇa keyavvaṃ** — should not purchase anything
- **kayivakko** — buying and selling
- **mahādoso** — a great fault
- **bhikkhivattī** — the practice of alms-seeking
- **suhāvahā** — bringing benefit and welfare, conducive to happiness in this world and the next

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse makes the positive case for the monk's practice of alms-seeking — not as a hardship to endure but as a genuinely beneficial way of life. When a monk accepts food offered freely by householders from what they have already prepared for their own families, no new harm is generated on the monk's account. The householder earns merit through generosity; the monk receives sustenance without violence. Commerce, by contrast, entangles the monk in desire, calculation, and self-interest — and these are the very mental states that generate continued bondage. The text is explicit: commerce is not merely inconvenient for a monk, it is a "great fault" — actively harmful to spiritual progress. The alms-based life, by contrast, supports both worlds: the monk advances toward liberation, and those who give advance in merit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk must live by asking for food, never by buying it — because trading things damages spiritual life, while living on offered food brings real benefit in this world and the next.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How do I meet my needs — through calculation and acquisition, or through openness and acceptance of what comes?

## SUTRA 35.16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समुयाणं उंछमेसिज्जा, जहासुत्तमणिंदियं ।
लाभालाभम्मि संतुट्टे, पिंडवायं चरे मुणी ॥३५.१६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Samuyāṇaṃ uṃchamessijjā, jahāsuttamaṇiṃdiyaṃ |
Lābhālābhammi saṃtuṭṭe, piṃḍavāyaṃ care muṇī || 35.16 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The monk should seek communal alms, collecting a little from many non-censured households as prescribed in scripture, remaining content whether he receives food or not.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jahāsuttaṃ** — according to scriptural prescription
- **aṇiṃdiyaṃ** — from non-censured, non-prohibited households
- **uṃchaṃ** — a little food collected bit by bit from multiple homes
- **samuyāṇaṃ** — communal, collective alms-seeking
- **essijjā** — should seek, should search
- **lābhālābhammi** — whether gain or no-gain (whether food is received or not)
- **saṃtuṭṭe** — content, satisfied
- **piṃḍavāyaṃ** — on the alms round, for the purpose of food
- **care** — should wander, should go about
- **muṇī** — the monk

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse offers a detailed positive description of how the monk should seek food. Collecting small portions from many households — rather than depending on one or two generous patrons — prevents the formation of special attachments to particular families and ensures no single household is burdened by the monk's presence. The monk visits only households that are not in prohibited categories, maintaining the careful boundaries prescribed by tradition. But the most important quality described is the inner one: the monk goes out each day equally prepared to receive food or to receive nothing. This equanimity toward material outcomes is not resignation or suppression — it is a genuine inner freedom. The alms round is practiced daily as a spiritual exercise, not merely a practical necessity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should wander from house to house collecting a little food from each, following the proper rules — and be genuinely content whether he gets food or doesn't.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How would I truly feel if I worked hard for something and received nothing — can I be genuinely at peace with that?

## SUTRA 35.17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अलोले ण रसे गिद्धे, जिभ्भादंते अमुच्छिए ।
ण रसट्टाए भुंजिज्जा, जवणट्टाए महामुणी ॥३५.१७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Alole ṇa rase giddhe, jibhbhādaṃte amucchie |
Ṇa rasaṭṭhāe bhuṃjijjā, javaṇaṭṭhāe mahāmuṇī || 35.17 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Not gluttonous, not greedy for tastes, with the tongue-faculty under control, free from infatuation — the great sage should eat not for savoring but for the sustenance of his journey of restraint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **alole** — not gluttonous, without craving for rich food
- **ṇa rase giddhe** — not greedy for tastes or flavors
- **jibhbhādaṃte** — having the tongue-faculty under control
- **amucchie** — free from infatuation, free from craving-attachment
- **mahāmuṇī** — the great sage
- **rasaṭṭhāe** — for the sake of taste, for savoring
- **ṇa bhuṃjijjā** — should not eat
- **javaṇaṭṭhāe** — for the sake of sustenance, to maintain the journey (of restraint)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having described how the monk seeks food, this verse addresses the inner attitude while eating. The monk eats not for pleasure but for maintenance — fuel for the body that supports the spiritual journey. The tongue is specifically named as the instrument of taste-craving, and mastering it is presented as a central discipline. Being "free from infatuation" means the monk does not build an emotional relationship with food — he does not anticipate meals with eagerness, does not feel disappointment when food is plain, and does not allow the pleasure of eating to accumulate into a force in his inner life. The great sage eats as a traveler refuels for a journey — efficiently, without savoring, without lingering, without making a ceremony of it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A great monk eats not because food tastes good but only to keep his body capable of continuing his spiritual journey.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I eat, am I doing it for nourishment and clarity — or for comfort, pleasure, or escape from something?

## SUTRA 35.18

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अच्चणं रयणं चेव, वंदणं पूयणं तहा ।
इडुईसक्कारसम्माणं, मणसा वि ण पत्थए ॥३५.१८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Accaṇaṃ rayaṇaṃ ceva, vaṃdaṇaṃ pūyaṇaṃ tahā |
Iḍuī sakkārasammāṇaṃ, maṇasā vi ṇa patthae || 35.18 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Ceremonial reception with forehead markings, auspicious welcome arrangements, reverential salutation, worship with fine offerings, and all forms of honor, reception, and respect — the monk should not desire even these in his mind.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **accaṇaṃ** — ceremonial welcome with sandalwood marking on the forehead
- **rayaṇaṃ** — welcome arrangement in one's honor (e.g., auspicious rice or pearl patterns placed before the honored person)
- **ceva** — indeed, also
- **vaṃdaṇaṃ** — reverential salutation, bow of respect
- **pūyaṇaṃ** — worship with offerings of fine cloth, objects, etc.
- **tahā** — likewise
- **iḍuī** — honor based on spiritual attainment or prestige
- **sakkāra** — reception, welcome
- **sammāṇaṃ** — respect, honor
- **maṇasā vi** — even mentally
- **ṇa patthae** — should not desire

This verse extends the monk's detachment from physical comfort and material wealth to detachment from social esteem. The forms of honor listed — forehead markings, elaborate welcome arrangements, bowing, offerings of valuable items, public recognition — are precisely the honors that respected monks often receive from devoted followers. The instruction is not merely to refuse such honor when offered, but to have no inner desire for it whatsoever. This is a demanding teaching because the craving for respect and recognition is among the subtlest and most persistent of human desires. Even spiritual persons who have genuinely released desire for physical things often still harbor a hunger for acknowledgment and appreciation. The truly free monk, this verse insists, is inwardly indifferent to whether he is honored or ignored.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A monk should not even want to be welcomed, respected, or honored by anyone — not even in the privacy of his own heart.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 35.19

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुक्कज्झाणं झियाएज्जा, अणियाणे अकिंचणे ।
वोसटुकाए विहरेज्जा, जाव कालस्स पज्जओ ॥३५.१९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sukkajjhāṇaṃ jhiyāejjā, aṇiyāṇe akiṃcaṇe |
Vosaṭukāe viharejjā, jāva kālassa pajjao || 35.19 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Free from ulterior motive, free from all possessions, with the body inwardly relinquished, the monk should meditate on pure, flawless contemplation and wander thus until the time of death arrives.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇiyāṇe** — free from ulterior motive, without any desire-based aim
- **akiṃcaṇe** — possessionless, without any belongings
- **vosaṭukāe** — having inwardly relinquished the body, free from bodily self-concept
- **sukkajjhāṇaṃ** — pure, flawless meditation (the highest form, free from all passion)
- **jhiyāejjā** — should meditate, should contemplate
- **jāva** — until
- **kālassa pajjao** — the time of death arrives, one's final moment approaches
- **viharejjā** — should wander, should live

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the culminating daily practice of the homeless ascetic. Having abandoned comfort, commerce, cooking, fire, gold, and the desire for honor, the monk now dedicates himself entirely to the purest form of meditation — the highest of the four types recognized in Jain contemplative tradition, characterized by perfect stillness, complete absence of passionate content, and progressively deepening clarity of consciousness. Three qualities define the meditator: no ulterior motive (the practice is for liberation alone, not for any worldly benefit including spiritual prestige), no possessions (complete material freedom), and no identification with the body (the self is not experienced as the physical form). The monk lives in this state continuously until the very moment of death — which, approached in this condition, becomes the final act of liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Free from wanting, free from owning, and not even attached to his own body, the monk meditates deeply every single day until it is time to die.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there any hidden motive — any underlying benefit I am seeking — in my spiritual practice?

## SUTRA 35.20

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिज्जूहिऊण आहारं, कालधम्मे उवट्टिए ।

When the time of natural death arrives, having relinquished all food and abandoned the human body, the capable monk is freed from all suffering.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **uvaṭṭie** — when it arrives, when it approaches
- **āhāraṃ** — food (all four types)
- **ṇijjūhiūṇa** — having completely relinquished, having abandoned
- **māṇusaṃ** — relating to human beings

- **jahiūṇa** — having abandoned, having left behind
- **pahū** — the capable one, the powerful monk
- **dukkhā** — from suffering
- **vimuccai** — is freed, becomes liberated

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse describes the final act of the monk's physical life: the conscious, willing relinquishment of food when death approaches. When the monk perceives that his life is drawing to its close, he progressively gives up all nourishment and enters deep meditation, allowing the body to reach its natural end in a state of inner peace. The body, which has served as the vehicle of spiritual practice throughout the monk's entire ascetic career, is now released — not with fear, not with clinging, but with the calm mastery of one who has been preparing for this moment throughout his life. The word "capable" is key: this is not suffering through starvation but the decisive act of a spiritually powerful being who has already freed himself inwardly from fear of death. ## SUTRA 35.21

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णिम्ममो णिरहंकारो, वीयरागो अणासवो ।
संपत्तो केवलं णाणं, सासयं परिणव्वुए ॥ ति बेमि ॥३५.२१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇimmamo ṇirahakāro, vīyarāgo aṇāsavo |
Saṃpatto kevalaṃ ṇāṇaṃ, sāsayaṃ pariṇavvue || ti bemi || 35.21 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Free from possessiveness, free from ego, free from attachment and aversion, free from all inflow of karma — having attained complete, perfect knowledge, such a being attains eternal liberation. Thus I say.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇimmamo** — free from the sense of "mine," without possessiveness
- **ṇirahakāro** — free from ego, without pride or self-assertion
- **vīyarāgo** — free from attachment and aversion, passionless
- **aṇāsavo** — without any inflow of karma, free from all causes of new bondage
- **kevalaṃ ṇāṇaṃ** — complete, perfect, omniscient knowledge
- **saṃpatto** — having attained, having achieved
- **sāsayaṃ** — eternal, permanent, forever
- **pariṇavvue** — attains final liberation, achieves complete emancipation
- **ti bemi** — "thus I say" (the teacher's closing formula)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This final verse describes the ultimate fruit of the homeless ascetic's path — everything the chapter has been leading toward. The monk who has lived by all these principles arrives at a state defined by four absences: no sense of ownership, no ego, no attraction or aversion, and no new karmic inflow. From this state of perfect inner freedom, the final remaining layers of karma are exhausted one by one. The soul attains omniscience — complete, unbounded knowledge of all that exists, arising naturally when every veil of karmic obscuration has been removed. This is followed immediately, and permanently, by eternal liberation. The traditional closing words "thus I say" are the teacher's seal — this is exactly what the path leads to, verified by direct experience, and nothing in this account has been embellished or understated.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When a monk becomes completely free of selfishness, ego, attraction, and the causes of new karma, he attains perfect all-knowing awareness and is forever liberated from all suffering.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it truly mean to live without any sense of "mine" — not just without physical possessions, but without the inner feeling that anything, anyone, or even my own body belongs to me?
