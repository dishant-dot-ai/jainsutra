# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 25
# Yajñiya (The Chapter of the Sacrifice)

**Total Sutras:** 45
**Theme:** Two Brahmin brothers — Jayaghosha, a Jain monk, and Vijayaghosha, a yajñika (sacrificer) — meet in Varanasi. Vijayaghosha refuses the monk alms, insisting only learned Brahmin yajñikas deserve food. Jayaghosha responds with a profound inquiry: what is truly the "chief" (mukha) of the Vedas, of yajna, of the nakshatras, of dharma? He then delivers the Uttaradhyayana's definitive teaching on the true Brahmin — not one born of the right family, but one of right action, inner purity, and liberation. The chapter closes with Vijayaghosha's conversion and both brothers attaining ultimate liberation.

## SUTRA 25.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

माहण कुल संभूओ, आसी विप्पो महाजसो ।
जायाई जम्मजण्णिमि, जयघोसिति णामओ ॥२५.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Māhaṇa kula saṃbhūo, āsī vippo mahājaso |
Jāyāī jammajāṇṇimi, Jayaghositi Ṇāmao || 25.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Born of Brahmin lineage, there was a greatly renowned Brahmin named Jayaghosha, who performed yama-niyama as his yajna (sacrifice).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **māhaṇa kula saṃbhūo** — born of Brahmin lineage
- **mahājaso** — of great fame/splendor
- **jammajāṇṇimi** — one whose yajna is yama-niyama (ethical vows as inner sacrifice); bhāva-yajña
- **jāyāī** — such (a person)
- **vippo** — Brahmin (vipra)
- **Jayaghositi Ṇāmao** — named Jayaghosha

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens by introducing Jayaghosha: a Brahmin by birth but a Jain monk by inner realization. The key phrase is *jammajāṇṇimi* — one for whom the ethical restraints (yama-niyama: ahiṃsā, satya, asteya, brahmacarya, aparigraha) constitute the real yajna. This immediately sets the philosophical axis of the chapter: the Śramaṇa tradition's inner sacrifice vs. the Brahmanical tradition's outer fire-ritual. Jayaghosha embodies the Jain answer to the question "what is the true yajna?" before a single argument is made.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Jayaghosha was a famous Brahmin who had become a Jain monk — and his "sacrifice" was not a fire ritual but the daily practice of non-violence and self-control.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The word "yajna" (sacrifice) appears in religious life everywhere — but what am I truly sacrificing? What am I offering into the inner fire?

## SUTRA 25.2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इंदियग्गाम णिग्गाही, मग्गगामी महामुणी ।
गामाणुगामं रीयंतो, पत्तो वाणारसिं पुरिं ॥२५.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Iṃdiyaggāma ṇiggāhī, maggagāmī mahāmuṇī |
Gāmāṇugāmaṃ rīyaṃto, patto Vāṇārasiṃ puriṃ || 25.2 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That great muni, who had conquered the group of senses and was walking the path of liberation, traveling from village to village, arrived one day at the city of Varanasi.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **iṃdiyaggāma ṇiggāhī** — one who restrains/conquers the group (grāma) of senses (indriya)
- **maggagāmī** — one who walks the path (mokṣa-mārga)
- **mahāmuṇī** — great muni (Jayaghosha)
- **gāmāṇugāmaṃ rīyaṃto** — traveling from village to village (vihāra)
- **patto Vāṇārasiṃ puriṃ** — arrived at the city of Varanasi

### 5. COMMENTARY

Jayaghosha is described with two of the most concentrated epithets in the Jain vocabulary of spiritual achievement: *indriya-grāma-niggāhī* (conqueror of the entire cluster of senses) and *mārga-gāmī* (one traveling the path of liberation). This is not incidental — the very descriptions that define him as a monk are precisely what the chapter will argue defines a true Brahmin. Varanasi (Banaras) is chosen with intent: it was the supreme center of Vedic Brahmanical learning and sacrifice, the city of ritual fire.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Jayaghosha, the disciplined Jain monk who had mastered his senses, was traveling on foot from village to village when he arrived at the great city of Varanasi.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

A monk walks into the heartland of a tradition that will challenge everything he stands for. What gives a person the inner steadiness to walk into intellectual confrontation without fear?

## SUTRA 25.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वाणारसीए बहिया, उज्जाणम्मि मणोरमे ।
फासुए सेज्जसंथारे, तत्थ वासमुवागए ॥२५.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Vāṇārasīe bahiyā, ujjāṇammi maṇorame |
Phāsue sejjasaṃthāre, tattha vāsamuvāgae || 25.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Outside the city of Varanasi, in a delightful garden where there was a prāsuka (pure, fit) bed and seating, he took up his residence there.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Vāṇārasīe bahiyā** — outside (bahiḥ) the city of Varanasi
- **ujjāṇammi maṇorame** — in a charming garden (ujjāna = udyāna)
- **phāsue** — prāsuka, pure/free of living organisms (fit for a monk)
- **sejjasaṃthāre** — bed and sitting-mat/bedding
- **tattha vāsamuvāgae** — took up residence there

### 5. COMMENTARY

A Jain monk's residence must be *prāsuka* — free of living organisms, acceptable under the vows of non-violence. Jayaghosha does not enter the busy city to rest; he stays on its outskirts in a garden. This detail is historically accurate: Jain monks commonly stayed in gardens outside cities during their wandering. The contrast with Vijayaghosha's yajna-śālā (sacrifice hall) inside the city will be sharp: the monk, unattached, outside; the householder-priest, attached, inside.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Jayaghosha set up his simple resting place in a garden on the outskirts of Varanasi — the kind of quiet, clean spot a monk needs.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where do you find your place of rest in the world — in the thick of activity or on the edge of it? What does your choice of resting place say about your inner life?

## SUTRA 25.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह तेणेव कालेणं, पुरीए तत्थ माहणे ।
विजयघोसिति णामेणं, जण्णं जयइ वेयवी ॥२५.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha teṇeva kāleṇaṃ, purīe tattha māhaṇe |
Vijayaghositi Nāmeṇaṃ, jaṇṇaṃ jayai veyavī || 25.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

At that very time, in that city, a Brahmin named Vijayaghosha — learned in the Vedas — was performing a yajna (sacrifice).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aha teṇeva kāleṇaṃ** — at that very time (atha, tenaiva kālena)
- **purīe tattha** — in that city (Varanasi)
- **māhaṇe** — a Brahmin
- **Vijayaghositi Nāmeṇaṃ** — by the name Vijayaghosha
- **jaṇṇaṃ jayai** — was performing yajna (sacrifice)
- **veyavī** — learned in the Vedas (vedavidyā-jñātā)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The narrative brings the two brothers into the same city at the same moment. Vijayaghosha is Jayaghosha's younger brother — the two are flesh and blood, same family, same Kashyapa gotra — and yet they represent two entirely different orientations of life. Vijayaghosha represents orthodox Brahmanical life at its most learned and earnest: he performs yajna, he knows the Vedas. He is not a bad man; he is a man who has not yet looked deeply.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: At that same time in Varanasi, Jayaghosha's own younger brother Vijayaghosha — a learned Vedic priest — was conducting a fire sacrifice.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever encountered someone from your own world — same background, same roots — who had found a completely different path? How did that meeting change you?

## SUTRA 25.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अह से तत्थ अणगारे, मासक्खमण पारणे ।
विजयघोसस्स जण्णम्मि, भिक्खमट्टा उवट्टिए ॥२५.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Aha se tattha aṇagāre, māsakkhamaṇa pāraṇe |
Vijayaghosassa jaṇṇammi, bhikkhāmaṭṭā uvaṭṭie || 25.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Then that homeless monk (Jayaghosha), on the day of breaking his month-long fast (māsākṣamaṇa pāraṇā), arrived at Vijayaghosha's sacrifice hall to seek alms.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **aṇagāre** — the homeless one, the monk (anagāra)
- **māsakkhamaṇa pāraṇe** — on the day of breaking the month-long fast (māsā-kṣamaṇa = one-month fast; pāraṇā = the breaking of the fast)
- **Vijayaghosassa jaṇṇammi** — at the yajna-śālā of Vijayaghosha
- **bhikkhāmaṭṭā** — for the purpose of seeking alms (bhikṣā)
- **uvaṭṭie** — arrived, came

### 5. COMMENTARY

The detail of *māsākṣamaṇa* (a one-month continuous fast) is significant: this is one of the highest forms of Jain austerity. Jayaghosha is not casually wandering; he has completed thirty days without food. He comes to end this fast at his own brother's home. The scene carries enormous emotional weight — a man who has fasted for a month approaches his brother for a simple meal, and will be turned away. It also establishes the complete reversal of roles: the monk, by the rules of Jain practice, may only eat if freely offered; the sacrificer, by the rules of Brahmanical hospitality, is expected to feed the worthy.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Jayaghosha had been fasting for an entire month and went to his brother's sacrifice hall to receive a simple meal — as a monk, he could only eat what was freely given.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean to depend entirely on others' goodwill for your survival? What does such total vulnerability do to the ego?

## SUTRA 25.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

समुवट्टियं तहिं संतं, जायगो पडिसेहए ।
ण हु दाहामि ते भिक्खं, भिक्खु जायाहि अण्णओ ॥२५.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Samuvaṭṭiyaṃ tahiṃ saṃtaṃ, jāyago paḍisehae |
Ṇa hu dāhāmi te bhikkhaṃ, bhikkhu jāyāhi aṇṇao || 25.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The yajñika (Vijayaghosha), seeing him arrived there, refused him, saying: "O monk! I will not give you alms. Go and seek alms elsewhere."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **samuvaṭṭiyaṃ tahiṃ saṃtaṃ** — seeing him arrived there
- **jāyago** — the yajñika (Vijayaghosha; jāyaga = one who performs yajna)
- **paḍisehae** — refused, objected
- **ṇa hu dāhāmi te bhikkhaṃ** — I will not give you alms
- **bhikkhu jāyāhi aṇṇao** — O monk, go seek alms elsewhere

### 5. COMMENTARY

Vijayaghosha's refusal is not personal cruelty — it is ideological. He will articulate in the next sutras exactly why he refuses: only Brahmin scholars of the Vedas, yajna, and jyotiṣa who can liberate themselves and others are worthy of the finest food. From his worldview, a wandering Jain monk — even his own elder brother — does not qualify. The chapter now becomes a philosophical debate encoded in a family drama.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Vijayaghosha turned his fasting monk-brother away: "I won't give you food. Go ask someone else."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Has someone ever refused you help based on a category they put you in, rather than seeing who you actually are? How does it feel to be reduced to a label?

## SUTRA 25.7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे य वेयविउ विप्पा, जण्णट्टा य जे दिया ।
जोइसंगविउ जे य, जे य धम्माण पारगा ॥२५.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Je ya veyaviu vippā, jaṇṇaṭṭā ya je diyā |
Joiosaṃgaviu je ya, je ya dhammāṇa pāragā || 25.7 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those Brahmins who are knowers of the Vedas, those twice-born who are accomplished in yajna, those who know jyotiṣa (astrology/astronomy) and its limbs, and those who have crossed to the other shore of dharma —

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **veyaviu vippā** — Brahmins who know the Vedas (veda-vid)
- **jaṇṇaṭṭā** — accomplished in yajna, yajñārthī
- **diyā** — twice-born (dvija)
- **joiosaṃgaviu** — knowers of jyotiṣa-śāstra and its ancillary subjects (śikṣā, kalpa, vyākaraṇa, nirukta, chandas, jyotiṣa)
- **dhammāṇa pāragā** — those who have reached the other shore of dharma, those fully versed in religious law

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra begins Vijayaghosha's criterion for receiving food at a yajna: the food is for the best — those who know Vedas, know sacrifice, know the astronomical sciences, and are masters of dharmaśāstra. This is the Brahmanical hierarchy of worthiness. Vijayaghosha is not being arbitrary; he is following his tradition's rules of dāna (giving) faithfully. Jayaghosha listens without anger. Sutras 7–8 together form a single condition.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Vijayaghosha explains who deserves food at his sacrifice: Brahmin scholars of the Vedas, experts in sacrificial ritual, astronomers, and masters of religious law.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What criteria do you use to decide who is "worthy" of your care, generosity, or respect? Are those criteria truly your own, or inherited?

## SUTRA 25.8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे समत्था समुद्धतुं, परमप्पाणमेव य ।
तेसिं अण्णिमण देयं, भो भिक्खू सव्वकामियं ॥२५.८॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Je samattā samuddhatuṃ, paramappāṇameva ya |
Tesiṃ aṇṇimaṇa deyaṃ, bho bhikkhū savvakāmiyaṃ || 25.8 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Those who are capable of lifting up their own self and others to the highest — to them, O monk, this complete and all-satisfying food is to be given.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **samattā samuddhatuṃ** — capable of lifting up, rescuing (samarthaḥ samuddhartum)
- **paramappāṇameva** — one's own self and the supreme (others') self
- **tesiṃ** — to those (alone)
- **aṇṇimaṇa** — this food, the finest food
- **deyaṃ** — is to be given
- **savvakāmiyaṃ** — complete, all-satisfying, of all flavors; the six-flavored food (ṣaḍ-rasa)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Vijayaghosha's condition is philosophically rich: the food at a yajna is for those who can liberate themselves *and others*. He is not merely saying "give food to the learned" — he is saying the food belongs to those who are both self-realizing and redemptive. This is precisely the criterion that Jayaghosha will turn back on him: *you* cannot answer who fits this description, and *I* can. The six-flavored food (*savvakāmiyaṃ*) is the highest ritual offering — full of all pleasures — and the irony is that Jayaghosha, the fasting monk, wants none of it except to end his austerity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Vijayaghosha says: only those who can save themselves and others deserve this best-quality food — not wandering monks.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you look for teachers or guides who can "lift others up"? What does it truly mean to elevate another soul?

## SUTRA 25.9

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सो तत्थ एवं पडिसिद्धो, जायगेण महामुणी ।
ण वि रुट्टो ण वि तुट्टो, उत्तमट्टु गवेसओ ॥२५.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

So tattha evaṃ paḍisiddho, jāyageṇa mahāmuṇī |
Ṇa vi ruṭṭo ṇa vi tuṭṭo, uttamaṭṭhu gaveṃsao || 25.9 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The great muni, having been thus refused by the yajñika, was neither angered nor pleased — he remained the seeker of the highest purpose (liberation).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **paḍisiddho** — refused, turned away
- **jāyageṇa** — by the yajñika (Vijayaghosha)
- **ṇa vi ruṭṭo** — not angered
- **ṇa vi tuṭṭo** — not pleased (not disturbed in either direction)
- **uttamaṭṭhu gaveṃsao** — seeker of the highest purpose (uttama-artha = liberation/ātman)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is a model of equanimity (samatā). A monk who has fasted for a month is turned away from his own brother's door — yet he is neither angry nor dejected. The phrase *uttamaṭṭhu gaveṃsao* ("seeker of the highest purpose") explains why: Jayaghosha's purpose is ātman, not alms. He was never really there for the food. He came because this was an opportunity — the meeting with his misguided brother was itself the purpose of arriving.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Being turned away didn't make Jayaghosha angry or sad — his goal was liberation, not a meal.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When someone dismisses or rejects you, what rises first — hurt, anger, or steadiness? What would it take to remain truly unmoved?

## SUTRA 25.10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जण्णटुं पाणहेउं वा, ण वि णिव्वाहणाय वा ।
तेसिं विमोक्खणट्टाए, इमं वयणमब्बवी ॥२५.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Jaṇṇaṭuṃ pāṇaheūṃ vā, ṇa vi ṇivvāhaṇāya vā |
Tesiṃ vimokkhanaṭṭāe, imaṃ vaṇamabbavī || 25.10 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Not for the sake of yajna, not for water, not even for sustaining life — but to liberate them from ignorance and false knowledge, he spoke these words:

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **jaṇṇaṭuṃ** — not for the sake of yajna or food from it
- **pāṇaheūṃ** — not for the sake of water/drink
- **ṇa vi ṇivvāhaṇāya** — not even for life-sustenance
- **tesiṃ vimokkhanaṭṭāe** — for the purpose of liberating them (freeing them from karma-bondage and false knowledge)
- **imaṃ vaṇamabbavī** — he spoke these words

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the philosophical pivot of the chapter. Jayaghosha clarifies his own motivation for speaking: it is not the food, not even life — it is *liberation* of the other. This is the Jain definition of the highest compassion: dharmopadēśa (teaching of truth) for the liberation of another soul. He is not arguing to win. He is speaking to free his brother from the chains of misconception — the very "vimokkhaṇa" (liberation from bondage) that Vijayaghosha claimed only the best Brahmins can achieve for others.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Jayaghosha wasn't speaking to get food or make a point — he spoke only to free his brother from false beliefs.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you speak to someone who has wronged or dismissed you, what is your true intention? To prove yourself, or to genuinely help them see?

## SUTRA 25.11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण वि जाणासि वेयमुहं, ण वि जण्णाण जं मुहं ।
णक्खत्ताण मुहं जं च, जं च धम्माण वा मुहं ॥२५.११॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇa vi jāṇāsi veyamuhaṃ, ṇa vi jaṇṇāṇa jaṃ muhaṃ |
Ṇakkhattāṇa muhaṃ jaṃ ca, jaṃ ca dhammāṇa vā muhaṃ || 25.11 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

You do not know the chief (mukha) of the Vedas, nor do you know what is the chief of the yajnas; you do not know the chief of the nakshatras, nor the chief of the dharmas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **veyamuhaṃ** — the chief/principal (mukha) of the Vedas
- **jaṇṇāṇa muhaṃ** — the chief/principal of the yajnas
- **ṇakkhattāṇa muhaṃ** — the chief/principal of the nakshatras (stars/lunar mansions)
- **dhammāṇa muhaṃ** — the chief/principal of the dharmas

### 5. COMMENTARY

Jayaghosha now goes to the root. He does not argue against Vijayaghosha's criteria for worthiness — he accepts them and raises the bar. "You said the food is for one who knows Vedas, yajna, jyotiṣa, and dharma — tell me then: what is the *principal* of each of these?" This is Socratic questioning at its finest: making the opponent realize he does not know the deepest truth of the very tradition he claims to uphold. The fourfold question — Vedas, yajna, nakshatras, dharma — is carefully chosen. Each has an inner truth that the yajñika has been performing outwardly without grasping inwardly.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "You don't actually know the most important thing about Vedas, sacrifice, astronomy, or dharma," Jayaghosha tells him.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there something you practice or believe in deeply — but have never asked yourself: what is the *core* of this? What is its most essential purpose?

## SUTRA 25.12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जे समत्था समुद्धतुं, परमप्पाणमेव य ।
ण ते तुमं वियाणासि, अह जाणासि तो भण ॥२५.१२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Je samattā samuddhatuṃ, paramappāṇameva ya |
Ṇa te tumaṃ viyāṇāsi, aha jāṇāsi to bhaṇa || 25.12 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

You do not know those who are capable of lifting up their own self and others to the highest. If you do know, then tell me.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **samattā samuddhatuṃ** — capable of lifting up, liberating (self and others)
- **paramappāṇameva** — one's own self and the highest (others')
- **ṇa te tumaṃ viyāṇāsi** — you do not know them (those who are truly capable)
- **aha jāṇāsi to bhaṇa** — but if you do know, then tell me

### 5. COMMENTARY

Jayaghosha picks up Vijayaghosha's own criterion — "the food is for those who can liberate self and others" (sutra 8) — and turns it into a question. "You used this criterion. Can you tell me who actually qualifies?" The challenge is elegant: Vijayaghosha, expert in Vedic learning, cannot name who genuinely has the power to liberate. The question exposes the gap between ritual competence and spiritual liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "You said the best food goes to those who can liberate themselves and others — but do you actually know who that is? Tell me if you do."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a gap between what you claim to value and what you actually know deeply? Are the standards you apply to others ones you could meet yourself?

## SUTRA 25.13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तस्सक्खेव पमोक्खं च, अचयंतो तहिं दिओ ।
सपरिसो पंजली होउं, पुच्छइ तं महामुणिं ॥२५.१३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tassakkheṃva pamokkhāṃ ca, acayaṃto tahiṃ dio |
Sapariso paṃjalī houṃ, pucchhai taṃ mahāmuṇiṃ || 25.13 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Unable to give answers to the monk's sharp questions, the Brahmin (Vijayaghosha), together with his entire assembly, joined his palms and asked the great muni:

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sapariso** — together with his assembly/parishad

A transformation has quietly occurred. The man who just refused alms to his monk-brother with dismissal is now standing with folded hands before him, asking him to teach. The *paṃjalī* (folded hands) is the posture of a student before a teacher — the Brahmin hierarchy has just been inverted.

Vijayaghosha now applies Jayaghosha's own criteria back to him — and affirms that Jayaghosha meets every one of them. In sutra 7–8, these were the conditions Vijayaghosha set. In sutra 38, he now declares that his monk-brother is the one who actually fulfills them. The irony and grace of the moment is complete: the man who was refused alms is now recognized as the most worthy recipient of the finest food at the entire yajna.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Vijayaghosha now recognizes: "You are the true Vedic scholar, the true sacrificer, the true sage — you are everything I said I was looking for."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever dismissed someone, only to discover later they were exactly what you were searching for? What did that recognition feel like?

## SUTRA 25.39

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तुभे समत्था समुद्धतुं, परमप्पाणमेव य ।
तमणुग्गहं करेहम्ह, भिक्खेण भिक्खु उत्तमा ॥२५.३९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Tubhe samattā samuddhatuṃ, paramappāṇameva ya |
Tamaṇuggahaṃ karehamma, bhikkheṇa bhikkhu uttamā || 25.39 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"You are truly capable of lifting up your own self and others to the highest. Therefore, O best of monks! please do us the grace of accepting alms from us."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **tubhe samattā samuddhatuṃ** — you are capable of lifting up (self and others)
- **paramappāṇameva** — to the highest (liberation)
- **tamaṇuggahaṃ karehamma** — do us this grace/favor (anugraha)
- **bhikkheṇa** — by accepting alms
- **bhikkhu uttamā** — O best of monks

### 5. COMMENTARY

The narrative has come full circle with great elegance. Vijayaghosha had refused alms in sutra 6 with the criterion that only those who can liberate self and others deserve the yajna's food. Now, in sutra 39, he applies that same criterion and asks Jayaghosha to *please* accept the food — as a grace to the giver, not merely a need of the receiver. The giver now recognizes that *receiving* the monk's presence is itself the gift. Alms-giving to a fully qualified monk generates immense spiritual merit for the giver.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "You are truly the one who can lift us to liberation — please honor us by accepting our alms, O greatest of monks."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there a grace in allowing others to give to you? Can receiving be an act of generosity?

## SUTRA 25.40

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण कज्जं मज्झ भिक्खेण, खिप्पं णिक्खमसू दिया ।
मा भमिहिसि भयावट्टे, घोरे संसारसागरे ॥२५.४०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ṇa kajjaṃ majjha bhikkheṇa, khippaṃ ṇikkhāmasū diyā |
Mā bhamihi bhayāvaṭṭe, ghore saṃsārasāgare || 25.40 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I have no need for your alms. O Brahmin! Quickly take the initiation of renunciation. Do not wander in the terrifying, whirlpool-filled, dreadful ocean of saṃsāra."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ṇa kajjaṃ majjha bhikkheṇa** — I have no need for alms (from you now)
- **khippaṃ ṇikkhāmasū** — quickly take the abhiniṣkramaṇa (monastic initiation); renounce
- **diyā** — O dvija (O Brahmin)
- **mā bhamihi** — do not wander (bhramati)
- **bhayāvaṭṭe** — in the terrifying whirlpools (bhaya-āvarta — fearful vortexes: the seven fears of saṃsāra)
- **ghore saṃsārasāgare** — in the dreadful ocean of saṃsāra

### 5. COMMENTARY

Jayaghosha's response is the greatest possible act of compassion: he declines the alms — which he came for, which ended his month-long fast — and instead urges immediate renunciation. The word *khippaṃ* (quickly) is urgent. The monk has seen the ripeness of his brother's soul; this is the moment. The image of *saṃsārasāgara* — the ocean of worldly existence — with its terrifying whirlpools (*bhayāvaṭṭe*: birth and death, the seven fears) is one of the Jain tradition's most powerful metaphors for urgency of liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: "I don't need your food — what I need is for you to renounce right now. Don't keep spinning in the terrifying ocean of rebirth."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If someone who loved you deeply said "stop everything and transform your life right now" — would you hear it? What would make you actually listen?

## SUTRA 25.41

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उवलेवो होइ भोगेसु, अभोगी णोविलिप्पइ ।
भोगी भमइ संसारे, अभोगी विप्पमुच्चइ ॥२५.४१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Uvalevo hoi bhogesu, abhogī ṇovilippai |
Bhogī bhamai saṃsāre, abhogī vippamucci || 25.41 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Karmic bondage arises from sense-enjoyments; one who does not enjoy (the abhogī) is not smeared (by karma). The enjoyer wanders in saṃsāra; the non-enjoyer is swiftly liberated.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **uvalevo hoi bhogesu** — karmic lepa (smearing, bondage) occurs through bhoga (enjoyment of sense objects)
- **abhogī ṇovilippai** — one who does not enjoy (the abstainer) is not smeared (karma-lepa does not stick)
- **bhogī bhamai saṃsāre** — the enjoyer wanders in saṃsāra (birth-death cycle)
- **abhogī vippamucci** — the non-enjoyer is quickly liberated (vipramucyate)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The word *lepa* (smearing/stain) is a key technical term in Jain karma theory: karmic matter sticks to the soul like paint sticks to a wet surface. Bhoga (sense-enjoyment) keeps the soul's surface "wet" with passion, making karma adhesive. The abhogī — one who has dried the surface through saṃyama and vairāgya — is simply not able to be stained. This is not joylessness; the abhogī experiences the deepest joy, which is the ātman's own bliss — not the borrowed pleasure of sense objects.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Sense-pleasures stick to the soul like paint — the one who indulges keeps reincarnating, the one who doesn't is quickly freed.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Which of your pleasures feel "sticky" — hard to let go, always wanting more? Which ones leave you feeling clean and free?

## SUTRA 25.42

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उल्लो सुक्को य दो छूढा, गोलया मट्टियामया ।
दो वि आविडिया कुड्डे, जो उल्लो सोत्थ लग्गइ ॥२५.४२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Ullo sukko ya do chūḍhā, golayā maṭṭiyāmayā |
Do vi āviḍiyā kuḍḍe, jo ullo sottha lagai || 25.42 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A wet ball and a dry ball — both made of clay — are thrown at a wall. Both hit the wall, but only the wet ball sticks to it.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **ullo** — wet (clay ball)
- **sukko** — dry (clay ball)
- **golayā maṭṭiyāmayā** — clay balls (made of earth/clay)
- **do chūḍhā** — both thrown
- **kuḍḍe** — at a wall
- **do vi āviḍiyā** — both hit/strike the wall
- **jo ullo sottha lagai** — the wet one sticks there

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the most vivid metaphors in the Uttaradhyayana. The soul is the clay ball; the world is the wall; karma is the moisture. Both the renunciant and the householder encounter the world — both "hit the wall" of worldly experience. But only the moisture-laden soul (passionate, desirous) sticks. The dry ball (the soul cleansed by saṃyama) strikes the same wall and bounces free. The metaphor makes the abstract principle of karma-bandha viscerally comprehensible.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Throw a wet mud ball and a dry mud ball at a wall — only the wet one sticks. The soul wet with desire gets stuck in the world; the dry, detached soul bounces free.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Imagine your soul as a clay ball. How wet or dry is it right now — how much of your desire, craving, and attachment makes you "sticky"?

## SUTRA 25.43

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं लग्गंति दुम्मेहा, जे णरा कामलालसा ।
विरत्ता उ ण लग्गंति, जहा से सुक्कगोलए ॥२५.४३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ laggaṃti dummedhā, je ṇarā kāmalālasā |
Virattā u ṇa laggaṃti, jahā se sukkagolae || 25.43 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In this same way, the foolish and dull-minded who are enamored of sense-pleasures get stuck (in saṃsāra). But the detached do not stick — just like the dry clay ball.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **dummedhā** — dull-minded, foolish (durmedha)
- **kāmalālasā** — enamored of kāma (sense-desires), languorous with desire
- **laggaṃti** — get stuck (in saṃsāra — karma-bondage)
- **virattā** — the detached, the dispassionate (virāgī)
- **ṇa laggaṃti** — do not stick
- **sukkagolae** — like the dry clay ball

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra applies the simile of sutra 42 directly to the human situation. *Virattā* (the detached) is the key word: it is not merely those who have intellectually understood detachment, but those who have actually, emotionally, volitionally released the grip of desire. The commentary makes clear that simply performing yajna or ritual does not dry the ball; only the genuine inner withdrawal from kāma does. This is the final answer to Vijayaghosha's yajna-practice: ritual activity, if performed with desire and passion, keeps the ball wet.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Foolish people who crave pleasures get stuck in rebirth — but the truly detached, like the dry ball, don't cling to anything and go free.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Right now — how much of what you call your "spiritual practice" is still moist with desire for results, recognition, or comfort? What would fully drying out actually look like?

## SUTRA 25.44

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं से विजयघोसे, जयघोसस्स अंतिए ।
अणगारस्स णिक्खंतो, धम्मं सोच्चा अणुत्तरं ॥२५.४४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Evaṃ se Vijayaghose, Jayaghosassa aṃtie |
Aṇagārassa ṇikkhāṃto, dhammaṃ soccā aṇuttaraṃ || 25.44 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In this way, Vijayaghosha, having heard the unsurpassed dharma from Jayaghosha — the homeless monk — took initiation (dīkṣā) into homelessness (renunciation).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **Vijayaghose** — Vijayaghosha
- **Jayaghosassa aṃtie** — in the presence of Jayaghosha
- **aṇagārassa** — the homeless monk (anagāra)
- **dhammaṃ soccā** — having heard the dharma
- **aṇuttaraṃ** — the unsurpassed/highest
- **ṇikkhāṃto** — took initiation, renounced (abhiniṣkrānta — the great departure)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The word *soccā* — "having heard" — is the operative word of Jain conversion narratives. It is not intellectual argument that converts; it is *śravaṇa* — genuine listening with an open heart. Vijayaghosha heard the unsurpassed dharma and took the great departure. The *anuttara* dharma (the highest, the unsurpassed) is specifically used — this is not just "a good teaching"; it is the teaching that stands beyond all others, the one that leads directly to liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Having listened deeply to the highest teaching from his monk-brother, Vijayaghosha renounced everything and took monastic initiation on the spot.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 25.45

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खवित्ता पुव्वकम्माइं, संजमेण तवेण य ।
जयघोस-विजयघोसा, सिद्धिं पत्ता अणुत्तरं ॥ ति बेमि ॥२५.४५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Khavittā puvvakammāiṃ, saṃjameṇa taveṇa ya |
Jayaghosa-Vijayaghosā, siddhi pattā aṇuttaraṃ || ti bemi || 25.45 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having destroyed all past karmas through saṃyama (restraint) and tapas (austerity), both Jayaghosha and Vijayaghosha attained the unsurpassed siddhi (liberation). — Thus I speak.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **khavittā puvvakammāiṃ** — having exhausted/destroyed (kṣapayitvā) all past karmas
- **saṃjameṇa** — through saṃyama (the five great vows, the restraint of the three yogas)
- **taveṇa** — through tapas (the twelve forms of austerity)
- **Jayaghosa-Vijayaghosā** — both Jayaghosha and Vijayaghosha
- **siddhi pattā** — attained siddhi (the state of liberation; the Siddha state)
- **aṇuttaraṃ** — the unsurpassed, the highest
- **ti bemi** — thus I speak (the traditional closing of a chapter or teaching)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter closes with the most complete possible resolution: both brothers — the teacher and the newly initiated student — exhaust all accumulated karma through restraint and austerity and attain the Siddha state. The phrase *puvvakammāiṃ khavittā* — "destroying past karmas" — is the Jain technical description of nirjarā (the shedding of karma through austerity). The two instruments — saṃyama (restraint) and tapas (austerity) — are the twin pillars of Jain liberation. *Ti bemi* ("Thus I speak") is the Mahavira's closing seal, signifying that this teaching is offered with full authority of the liberated one.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Through restraint and deep austerity, both brothers burned away all their past karma and reached the ultimate liberation — the state of the Siddhas. Thus it is taught.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Two brothers, same family, same blood — one a little earlier, one a little later — both arrive at the same destination. What does this say about the potential that lives in every soul, regardless of where they start?
