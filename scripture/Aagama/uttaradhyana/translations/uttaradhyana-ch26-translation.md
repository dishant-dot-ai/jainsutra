# Uttaradhyayana Sutra — Chapter 26
# Samācārī (The Chapter on Monastic Conduct)

**Total Sutras:** 52
**Theme:** This chapter is a comprehensive manual for monastic life — a practical guidebook for the daily conduct of Jain monks. It begins by naming the ten samācārī (proper rules of conduct): āvassiyā (essential duties), naivedikī (daily schedule), āpucchhaṇā (seeking permission), paḍipucchhaṇā (inquiry), chhandaṇā (seeking consent), icchākāra (willing acceptance), micchākāra (seeking pardon), tahakkāra (acknowledgment), gurupūjā (worship of the guru), and uvasampayā (proper approach/sitting). The chapter then details the daily and nightly routine of monks — the pauruṣhī system of dividing day and night into watches, rules for inspection of belongings, food gathering, study, meditation, confession, and the overall framework through which monastic discipline leads to liberation.

## SUTRA 26.1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सामायारिं पवक्खामि, सव्वदुक्खविमोक्खणे ।
जं चरित्ताण णिगंथा, तिण्णा संसार सागरं ॥२६.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Sāmāyāriṃ pavakkhāmi, savvadukkhavimokkhane |
Jaṃ carittāṇa ṇigaṃthā, tiṇṇā saṃsāra sāgaraṃ || 26.1 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I shall describe the samācārī (monastic code of conduct) that leads to liberation from all suffering — by practicing which, the Nirgrantha monks have crossed the ocean of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **sāmāyāriṃ** — samācārī; the code of proper monastic conduct
- **pavakkhāmi** — I shall describe / I shall expound
- **savvadukkhavimokkhane** — for liberation from all suffering (sarva-duḥkha-vimokṣaṇa)
- **jaṃ** — which (conduct)
- **carittāṇa** — having practiced / having followed
- **ṇigaṃthā** — Nirgrantha monks (those free from all bonds)
- **tiṇṇā** — crossed over / transcended
- **saṃsāra sāgaraṃ** — the ocean of worldly existence (cycle of birth and death)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This opening verse establishes both the subject and the stakes of the chapter. The term *samācārī* derives from *sam* (proper, complete) + *ācāra* (conduct) — it is the complete framework of right practice for monastic life. The verse declares that this is not mere convention or arbitrary rule-making: it is the very path by which Nirgrantha monks — those who have freed themselves from all internal and external bonds — have crossed the ocean of saṃsāra. The word *savvadukkhavimokkhane* (liberation from *all* suffering) signals that these practices address not just physical discipline but the deepest roots of karmic bondage.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: This chapter will describe the rules of monastic conduct — the very practices by following which Jain monks have freed themselves from all suffering and escaped the cycle of rebirth.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What daily practices in my own life form the foundation of my inner discipline — and are they leading me toward freedom, or merely toward comfort?

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The first samācārī is called āvassiyā (essential duties), the second is naivedikī (daily reporting), the third is āpucchhaṇā (seeking permission), and the fourth is paḍipucchhaṇā (making inquiry).

- **ṇeveiyā** — naivedikī; daily schedule reporting / informing of daily routine
- **taiyā** — the third
- **āpucchhaṇā** — seeking permission (before undertaking one's own tasks)
- **caüṭṭhī** — the fourth
- **paḍipucchhaṇā** — making inquiry / asking about others' tasks

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse begins the enumeration of the ten samācārī by naming the first four. *Āvassiyā* refers to the six essential duties (*āvaśyaka*) that every monk must perform daily — including sāmāyika, confession, and study. *Naivedikī* is the practice of reporting one's daily schedule and activities. *Āpucchhaṇā* is the discipline of seeking permission before doing anything on one's own. *Paḍipucchhaṇā* is the complementary practice of inquiring about what needs to be done for others. Together, these four establish the monk's relationship to duty, transparency, initiative, and service.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The first four rules of monastic conduct are: (1) performing essential daily duties, (2) reporting your daily schedule, (3) asking permission before acting on your own, and (4) asking how you can help others.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

## SUTRA 26.3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पंचमी छंदणा णामो, इच्छाकारो य छट्ठओ ।
सत्तमो मिच्छाकारो य, तहक्कारो य अट्ठमो ॥२६.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Paṃcamī chhaṃdaṇā ṇāmo, icchākāro ya chhaṭṭhao |
Sattamo micchākāro ya, tahakkāro ya aṭṭhamo || 26.3 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The fifth is called chhandaṇā (seeking consent), the sixth is icchākāra (willing acceptance), the seventh is micchākāra (seeking pardon), and the eighth is tahakkāra (acknowledgment).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **paṃcamī** — the fifth
- **chhaṃdaṇā** — seeking consent / approval for material needs
- **ṇāmo** — by name / called
- **icchākāro** — icchākāra; willing acceptance / expressing willingness
- **chhaṭṭhao** — the sixth
- **sattamo** — the seventh
- **micchākāro** — micchākāra; seeking pardon / acknowledging error (literally: "may my wrong be pardoned")
- **tahakkāro** — tahakkāra; acknowledgment / saying "so be it"
- **aṭṭhamo** — the eighth

### 5. COMMENTARY

The verse continues the enumeration with samācārī five through eight. *Chhandaṇā* is seeking the guru's consent before using any material resource — food, clothing, or equipment. *Icchākāra* is the monk's response of willing acceptance when given instruction or correction: "I accept this willingly." *Micchākāra* is the practice of seeking pardon when one has erred — the ritualized acknowledgment of wrongdoing that prevents pride from taking root. *Tahakkāra* is the response of acknowledgment — "so be it" — when hearing a teaching or instruction. These four practices regulate the monk's relationship to material needs, instruction, error, and acceptance.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The next four rules are: (5) getting consent before using anything, (6) accepting instruction willingly, (7) asking forgiveness when you make a mistake, and (8) acknowledging what you're told with humility.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When I make a mistake, do I seek genuine forgiveness — or do I just explain it away?

## SUTRA 26.4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अक्खपुट्टाणं गुरुपूया, दसमी उवसंपया ।
एवं दुपंचसंजुत्ता, सामायारी पवेइया ॥२६.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Akkhapuṭṭāṇaṃ gurupūyā, dasamī uvasaṃpayā |
Evaṃ dupaṃcasaṃjuttā, sāmāyārī paveiyā || 26.4 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The ninth is gurupūjā through the akkhapuṭṭa (worship of the guru through sacred offerings), and the tenth is uvasampayā (proper sitting/approach). Thus, endowed with these twice-five (ten), the samācārī has been described.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **akkhapuṭṭāṇaṃ** — through akkhapuṭṭa (a form of sacred offering / eye-salve offering to the guru)
- **gurupūyā** — worship of the guru; the ninth samācārī
- **dasamī** — the tenth
- **uvasaṃpayā** — uvasampayā; proper approach / sitting near the guru with due respect
- **evaṃ** — thus
- **dupaṃcasaṃjuttā** — endowed with twice-five (i.e., ten)
- **sāmāyārī** — samācārī; the code of monastic conduct
- **paveiyā** — has been described / proclaimed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse completes the enumeration of the ten samācārī. The ninth, *gurupūjā* through *akkhapuṭṭa*, refers to the practice of honoring and worshipping the guru — the spiritual teacher who guides one's monastic path. The tenth, *uvasampayā*, is the discipline of properly approaching and sitting near the guru with reverence and correct posture. The compound *dupaṃcasaṃjuttā* (twice-five = ten) elegantly summarizes: these ten practices together constitute the complete framework of samācārī. They cover every dimension of monastic life — duty, communication, material use, humility, and devotion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: The last two rules are: (9) worshipping and honoring your teacher, and (10) sitting near the teacher with proper respect. These ten rules together make up the complete code of monastic conduct.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do I honor my teachers only in word, or do I express reverence through how I approach them, sit with them, and carry their teachings in my daily life?

## SUTRA 26.5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

गमणे आवस्सियं कुज्जा, ठाणे कुज्जा पिणसीहियं ।
आपुच्छणा सयंकरणे, परकरणे पडिपुच्छणा ॥२६.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Gamaṇe āvassiyaṃ kujjā, ṭhāṇe kujjā piṇasīhiyaṃ |
Āpucchhaṇā sayaṃkaraṇe, parakaraṇe paḍipucchhaṇā || 26.5 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

While going (traveling), one should perform āvassiya (essential duties); while staying (stationary), one should perform naivedikī (daily reporting). For one's own tasks, one should seek āpucchhaṇā (permission); for others' tasks, one should make paḍipucchhaṇā (inquiry).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **gamaṇe** — while going / during travel (vihāra)
- **āvassiyaṃ kujjā** — one should perform the essential duties (āvaśyaka)
- **ṭhāṇe** — while staying / while stationary
- **piṇasīhiyaṃ kujjā** — one should perform naivedikī (daily reporting)
- **āpucchhaṇā** — seeking permission
- **sayaṃkaraṇe** — for one's own work / tasks
- **parakaraṇe** — for others' work / tasks
- **paḍipucchhaṇā** — inquiry / asking about what needs to be done

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having listed the ten samācārī by name, the text now specifies when each is to be applied. The first four are mapped to specific contexts: *āvassiya* is performed during travel — the monk must never skip essential duties even while moving from place to place. *Naivedikī* is performed while stationary — when settled in one place, the monk reports the daily schedule to superiors. *Āpucchhaṇā* applies to personal initiative — before doing anything for oneself, one must ask permission. *Paḍipucchhaṇā* applies to service — before doing something for another, one must inquire what is needed. This creates a system of accountability that leaves no action unsupervised.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Essential duties must be done while traveling; daily reporting when you're settled. Ask permission before doing your own work, and ask others what they need before serving them.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In my daily actions, do I act from impulse or from inquiry? How often do I truly ask before I assume?

## SUTRA 26.6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

छंदणा दव्वजाएणं, इच्छाकारो य सारणे ।
मिच्छाकारो य पिंदाए, तहक्कारो पडिस्सुए ॥२६.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

Chhaṃdaṇā davvajāeṇaṃ, icchākāro ya sāraṇe |
Micchākāro ya piṃdāe, tahakkāro paḍissue || 26.6 ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Chhandaṇā (seeking consent) is for material needs; icchākāra (willing acceptance) is for receiving instruction. Micchākāra (seeking pardon) is for food acceptance; tahakkāra (acknowledgment) is for listening to teachings.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **chhaṃdaṇā** — seeking consent / approval
- **davvajāeṇaṃ** — for material needs / for things involving material resources (dravya-jāta)
- **icchākāro** — willing acceptance
- **sāraṇe** — for instruction / correction / being reminded of duties
- **micchākāro** — seeking pardon
- **piṃdāe** — for food (piṇḍa) / for accepting alms
- **tahakkāro** — acknowledgment / "so be it"
- **paḍissue** — for listening / for receiving teachings (pratiśruti)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse maps samācārī five through eight to their specific contexts. *Chhandaṇā* (seeking consent) applies whenever material resources are involved — before using food, clothing, or any equipment, the monk must seek the guru's consent. *Icchākāra* (willing acceptance) is the proper response when corrected or instructed — the monk says "I willingly accept this." *Micchākāra* (seeking pardon) is specifically required in the context of food acceptance — acknowledging any possible error in the process of gathering alms. *Tahakkāra* (acknowledgment) is the proper response when hearing a teaching or instruction — saying "so be it" to affirm understanding.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: Get consent before using material things. Accept correction willingly. Seek pardon when receiving food (in case of any error). And when taught something, respond with humble acknowledgment.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

How do I receive correction — with resistance and defensiveness, or with the willingness of one who genuinely wants to grow?

This is a direct expression of ahiṃsā at the micro level. Only after this inspection does the monk proceed to *vandanā* — saluting the guru. The order is significant: protect life first, then honor the teacher.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: At sunrise, the monk first checks all belongings to make sure no small creatures are harmed, and then bows to the teacher. This is how every day begins.

With folded hands, one should ask: "What should I do here?" Having received permission — saying "O Lord" (bhaṃte) — one then inspects the garments and other belongings.

### 5. COMMENTARY

After the initial morning salutation, the monk's next act is to approach the guru with folded hands and ask: "What is my duty today?" The guru assigns tasks, and the monk responds with *icchākāra* — willing acceptance, signified by the respectful address *bhaṃte* (O venerable one). Only then does the monk proceed to inspect garments and equipment. Every step is a choreography of humility, service, and mindfulness.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: With folded hands, the monk asks the teacher: "What should I do today?" After receiving instructions, the monk then inspects clothing and belongings before starting the day.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having completed the service (vaiyāvṛttya), having received the response, one departs. One practices kāyotsarga (standing meditation) to cross the ocean of saṃsāra.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **veyāvacce** — in service / vaiyāvṛttya (attending to the guru and fellow monks)
- **giṇautteṇaṃ** — having completed / having finished the task

- **kāussaggaṃ** — kāyotsarga (standing meditation / abandonment of the body)
- **sāhae** — performs / practices

### 5. COMMENTARY

After completing morning duties — inspection, salutation, and inquiry — the monk engages in *vaiyāvṛttya* (service to elders and fellow monks). This service is not a menial task but a spiritual practice: attending to the needs of others cultivates humility and dissolves ego. Once service is complete and the monk has received acknowledgment, the next practice is *kāyotsarga* — literally "abandonment of the body." This is the practice of standing perfectly still in meditation, withdrawing awareness from the body. It is one of the most powerful Jain contemplative practices and a direct method for shedding karma.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Simply put: After serving fellow monks and receiving acknowledgment, the monk practices standing meditation (kāyotsarga) — a form of deep stillness that helps shed karma.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

A discerning monk should divide the day into four parts, and in each of these four parts, he should practice the superior virtues (uttaraguṇa).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **divasassa** — of the day

This verse introduces the *pauruṣhī* system — the division of daylight into four watches (*pauruṣhī*). Each watch is a distinct period with specific duties. The word *viyakkhaṇo* (discerning) is key: the monk must not blindly follow routine but practice with awareness and intelligence. In each of the four watches, the monk cultivates *uttaraguṇa* — the higher virtues of study, meditation, austerity, and service. The day is not left to chance or mood; every hour is purposefully assigned to spiritual progress.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

### 5. COMMENTARY

The chapter closes by circling back to its opening (sutra 1), creating a perfect ring-structure. Where sutra 1 promised to describe the samācārī for liberation from all suffering, this final verse confirms: "It has been described." The samācārī is presented not as a narrow monastic code but as a universal path that countless souls have walked to freedom. The closing phrase *tti bemi* (thus do I say) is a declaration of authority and authenticity — this is the living voice of the teaching tradition affirming its own truth. The chapter that began with a promise ends with a fulfilled testament.

Simply put: This concludes the description of the monastic code of conduct. Many beings have practiced these rules and crossed the ocean of rebirth. These practices work — they have been proven across countless lives.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

The teaching ends where it began: a promise of liberation through practice. The question is not whether the path works — but whether I will walk it.
