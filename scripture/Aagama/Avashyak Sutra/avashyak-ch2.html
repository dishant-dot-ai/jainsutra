The Second Essential — the Logassa Sūtra. Seven verses of praise to the twenty-four Tirthankaras, from Rishabh to Mahavir, recited standing in the upright Jin-mudrā.

Avashyak 2: Chaturviṃśatistava

About This Essential

The Praise of the Twenty-Four

After establishing equanimity, the practitioner needs an object on which to fix the mind. The Tirthankaras are those who have already completed what the seeker has only begun. To praise them — to recall their names, contemplate their qualities, and invite their grace — is to align oneself with the direction in which one wishes to travel. As the Sanskrit verse reminds us: śraddhāmayo'yaṃ puruṣaḥ — 'A person becomes whatever they hold faith in.'

The Logassa was asked of Lord Mahavira directly. In the Uttaradhyayana Sutra, Gautam Swami asks what the soul gains by praising the twenty-four. Mahavira's answer: 'By such praise, the karma of false belief is reduced; the practitioner attains the purity of right perception; through love of their qualities those qualities arise within the practitioner's own being.' The sutra has a precise five-part structure: the nature of the Tirthankaras, their names, their distinguishing marks, the devotee's request, and the closing similes.

2.1

The Resolve to Praise

लोगस्स उज्जोयगरे, धम्मतित्थयरे जिणे । अरिहंते कित्तइस्सं, चउवीसं पि केवली

I will praise the twenty-four Arihants — illuminators of the world by the light of knowledge, founders of the four-fold ford of dharma, conquerors of inner enemies, omniscient ones — and all the other Kevalis as well.

The opening verse is a declaration of intent. Before reciting any praise, the practitioner first announces who is being praised and why they deserve the praise. Four qualities are named, and each one corresponds to one of the four "great superlatives" (mula-atishaya) of the Arihants. Light-makers of the world (ujjoyagare): The Tirthankaras' kevalajñāna is an unbroken, all-pervading, eternal light that illumines every substance and every state of every being everywhere — not metaphorically, but literally. Ordinary light reaches only what it falls upon and lasts only as long as its source burns. The light of omniscience reaches all and never sets. This is the jñāna-atishaya. Founders of the dharma-ford (dhammatitthayare): Souls have been trapped in the ocean of saṃsāra since beginningless time. The Tirthankara, by establishing the four-fold sangha and giving the teaching that anchors it, builds a tirth — a crossing-place — by which any sincere being can step out of the flood. This is the vacana-atishaya — the gift of teaching that can be understood by every kind of being in their own language. Jinas — conquerors (jiṇe): The Tirthankaras have won the only war that matters — the inner war against attachment and aversion. They are total victors over the passions. This is the apāya-apagama-atishaya — the complete absence of all defects, both inner and outer. Arihants — worthy of worship (arihaṃte): Devas, demons, humans, kings, chakravartis — all bow before them. This is the pūjā-atishaya. The verse closes with cauvīsaṃ pi kevalī — "the twenty-four, and also the Kevalis." The little word pi ("also") is significant: the practitioner here praises not only the twenty-four named Tirthankaras but also includes within the bow all general Kevalis (those who attained omniscience without becoming Tirthankaras), the twenty Tirthankaras presently in the Mahavideha realm, and the twenty-four of every other region and cycle. The praise opens outward to embrace every awakened being everywhere.

Simply Put: The seeker begins by saying: "I am about to praise the twenty-four perfect teachers — those who lit up the world with knowledge, who built the bridge of dharma, who conquered themselves completely, and who are worthy of all reverence — and not only them, but every fully awakened being."

2.2

The First Eight Names

उसभमजियं च वंदे, संभवमभिनंदणं च सुमइं च । पउमप्पहं सुपासं, जिणं च चंदप्पहं वंदे

I bow to Rishabh and Ajit; I bow to Sambhav, Abhinandan, and Sumati; I bow to Padmaprabhu and Suparshva; and I bow to the Jina Chandraprabhu.

In Jain tradition, the Tirthankara's name is never arbitrary. It emerges from a sign — a dream, a wish, a transformation in the mother — that occurs while the great soul rests in her womb. The presence of a Tirthankara-to-be is so powerful that it changes the surroundings, the family, and the mother herself, and the name records what was changed. These first eight Tirthankaras span an immense stretch of cosmic time. Rishabhdev, the first, lived at the dawn of the present avasarpini, when humanity was just transitioning from the kalpa-vriksha (wish-fulfilling tree) age to the age of agriculture and craft. He is credited in Jain tradition with teaching seventy-two arts and sixty-four crafts to humankind. Ajitnath came after an inconceivable interval. By Chandraprabhu the eighth, the conditions of human life were already becoming more like those we recognise today. The names themselves are gentle teachings. Each one points to a quality made visible: invincibility (Ajit), abundance (Sambhav), praiseworthiness (Abhinandan), wise discernment (Sumati), purity like a lotus rising from mud (Padmaprabhu), beauty of form (Suparshva), the cooling clarity of moonlight (Chandraprabhu). To recite them slowly is to invite each of those qualities into one's own field of mind.

Simply Put: The seeker bows by name to the first eight perfect teachers, each of whom received their name from a sign that appeared while their mother was carrying them: from Rishabh through Chandraprabhu.

2.3

The Middle Eight Names

सुविहिं च पुप्फदंतं, सीयल-सिज्जंस-वासुपुज्जं च । विमल-मणंतं च जिणं, धम्मं संतिं च वंदामि

I bow to Suvidhi (also called Pushpadanta); to Sheetal, Shreyans, and Vasupujya; to Vimal, Anant the Jina, Dharma, and Shanti.

These eight Tirthankaras occupy the middle of the cosmic line. Each of their names is a gift recorded — a debt the world acknowledged owing to them at the moment of their entry into a body. Sheetal cooled fever; Shreyans dissolved a curse; Shanti ended a plague. Even before their birth, their presence resolved suffering. A particularly beautiful pattern emerges: the Tirthankara's effect on the world begins not when they preach, not when they renounce, not even when they are born — but at the moment of conception. The womb itself becomes a transformer. This expresses a profound Jain teaching about karma and pure consciousness: a soul carrying the Tirthankara-nāmakarma — the karma that destines it to be a world-teacher — radiates such auspiciousness that its mere presence reshapes circumstances around it. Suvidhinath uniquely has two names — Suvidhi (skilled in rites) and Pushpadanta (flower-toothed). The Logassa retains both, an act of textual generosity: where tradition has preserved more than one name for the same teacher, the praise honours all of them.

Simply Put: The seeker bows to the middle eight Tirthankaras — from Suvidhi (also called Pushpadanta) through Shanti — each named for a real change that came over the world or the family the moment the great soul entered the womb.

2.4

The Last Eight Names

कुंथुं अरं च मल्लिं, वंदे मुणिसुव्वयं नमिजिणं च । वंदामि रिट्ठनेमिं, पासं तह वद्धमाणं च

I bow to Kunthu, Ar, and Malli; I bow to Munisuvrat and to Nami the Jina; I bow to Arishtanemi, to Parshva, and to Vardhaman.

The final eight Tirthankaras bring the praise into the recent past. Among them is Mallinath, who in the Shvetambara tradition is recorded as having taken birth in a female body — a detail of profound theological significance, since it teaches that the path to becoming a Tirthankara is open regardless of bodily form. Among them too are Neminath, the cousin of Krishna; Parshvanath, who lived 250 years before Mahavir and whose order Mahavir's parents belonged to; and finally Vardhaman, the personal name of Lord Mahavir himself — the twenty-fourth and last Tirthankara of this cosmic half-cycle, whose teaching this very Avashyak Sutra preserves. The verse arc that began with Rishabh — born when humans first learned to till and weave — closes here with Vardhaman, born around 599 BCE, whose words are still being spoken aloud each day in Jain homes twenty-five centuries later. By naming all twenty-four in unbroken succession, the practitioner connects to a lineage that spans the entire moral history of the present cosmic cycle.

Simply Put: The seeker bows to the last eight perfect teachers — from Kunthu through Vardhaman, who is Lord Mahavir himself — completing the line of all twenty-four.

2.5

Praise Complete; Invocation of Grace

एवं मए अभिथुआ, विहुयरयमला पहीणजरमरणा । चउवीसं पि जिणवरा, तित्थयरा मे पसीयंतु

Thus praised by me — these who have shaken off the dust of karma and the stain of bondage, who have passed beyond old age and death — may all twenty-four supreme Jinas, the founders of the ford of dharma, be gracious unto me!

The fifth verse is the moment of completion. The seeker has named all twenty-four; now the seeker names what those twenty-four are. Three qualities are crystallised: Free of karmic dust and stain (vihuyarayamalā): The Tirthankaras have not merely reduced their karma — they have shaken it off entirely. The verse uses two words for karma to make a precise philosophical distinction: raj (the new karma being absorbed at every moment by ordinary souls) and mala (the densely-bound karma accumulated over countless past lives). The Tirthankaras have closed the door to the first and dissolved the deposit of the second. Both vihuta — shaken off, like dust from a cloth. Beyond birth, old age, and death (pahīṇajaramaraṇā): These three are the master-griefs of saṃsāra. They are not external fates but the natural consequence of having a body. The Tirthankaras, having severed the karmic root that compels rebirth, will never again wear a body — and so the trio of birth, ageing, dying can never again touch them. Supreme Jinas, founders of the ford (jiṇavarā titthayarā): Among all Jinas (and there are infinite Jinas across infinite time), the Tirthankaras are the supreme ones — for not only have they crossed, they have built the bridge by which others can cross. The verse closes with the request: me pasīyaṃtu — "may they be gracious to me." But the Jain understanding of grace is unique. The Tirthankaras, being totally dispassionate, do not actively favour or disfavour anyone; they cannot, for that would compromise their freedom from attachment. Their "grace" is not given by them but received by the practitioner. By orienting the heart toward them in love and reverence, the practitioner opens themselves to receive what the Tirthankara's example always silently transmits: the inspiration to walk the same path. The grace flows because the practitioner has prepared the channel.

Simply Put: Having now named all twenty-four perfect teachers — those who have shaken off all karma and gone beyond birth, old age, and death — the seeker asks: "May you all be gracious to me."

2.6

The Devotee's Request

कित्तिय-वंदिय-महिया, जे ए लोगस्स उत्तमा सिद्धा । आरुग्ग-बोहिलाभं, समाहिवरमुत्तमं दिंतु

Praised, saluted, and worshipped — those who are the foremost in the world, the perfected Siddhas — may they grant me the gain of (true) health, the gain of right vision, and the highest, most excellent samadhi.

This verse contains the only "request" in the entire Logassa, and it is worth noticing what is not asked for. The seeker does not ask for wealth, success, long life, family welfare, or any worldly benefit. The seeker asks for three things only — and even these three are described in their highest spiritual sense: Ārugga (health): Not the body's freedom from illness, which is fleeting and karma-bound. The text is explicit: "the body's health or sickness is impermanent and of no special importance to the practitioner's life." What is requested is the soul's true health — the unbreaking wholeness of pure consciousness, freed at last from the master-disease of being born and dying again. Bohilābh (gain of bodhi): The right perception of reality — the moment when the seeker first sees clearly that the soul is not the body, that karma is the bondage, and that the path of right perception, right knowledge, and right conduct leads to release. Without bodhi, no spiritual progress is possible. With it, the seed of liberation is planted. Samāhi-vara-uttamaṃ (the supreme samadhi): The deepest stillness, the highest grade of samadhi. There is bodily samadhi (the body's wellness from following the three jewels), and there is bhāva-samadhi — the inner stillness of the soul. The verse uses both vara and uttama to indicate that what is sought is the highest tier of bhāva-samadhi — the samadhi by which liberation itself becomes possible. The verse begins by recalling the three stages of devotion: kīrtana (praising aloud), vandana (bowing in reverence), and mahima/pūjana (worship through mind, speech, and body). These are sequential — praise leads to reverence, reverence leads to worship, worship leads to receiving. The seeker who has now completed all three is positioned to receive what is being asked for. The verse implicitly teaches: ask greatly, or do not ask. Of beings who have transcended all desire, ask only for that which transcendence itself can provide. To ask the Tirthankaras for wealth would be to misunderstand entirely whom one is addressing.

Simply Put: The seeker, having praised and bowed and honoured these supreme Siddhas, asks for only three things: the soul's true wholeness, the gain of right understanding, and the deepest possible inner stillness.

2.7

The Three Similes; The Final Request

चंदेसु निम्मलयरा, आइच्चेसु अहियं पयासयरा । सागरवरगंभीरा, सिद्धा सिद्धिं मम दिसंतु

Purer than the moons; brighter even than the suns; deep as the supreme self-bound ocean — may these Siddhas grant me siddhi (liberation).

The closing verse turns from request to celebration — and uses three of the most beautiful similes in all Jain literature to do so. The world contains pure things: the moon, the sun, the great oceans. The Tirthankaras transcend even these. Purer than the moon: The moon is the world's symbol of purity, yet even the moon has a stain (the dark spot). The Siddhas have no stain at all, and their purity is not seasonal or phase-dependent — it is permanent across all three times. Brighter than the sun: The sun is the world's source of light. Yet the sun rises and sets; it illumines what its rays reach and only as long as it shines. The Siddhas' light — kevalajñāna — never sets, illumines everything (loka and aloka) at once, and never weakens. Deeper than the deepest ocean: The svayambhuramana is the outermost ocean in Jain cosmography — vast and unfathomable. Yet even it has currents, storms, and tides. The Tirthankaras' inner depth is gambhīra in a way no ocean is: no favourable or adverse circumstance ever produces a ripple in their equanimity. Their stillness is shastashvat — eternal. Three similes, each one giving the worldly equivalent and then transcending it. This is the practice of contemplation by graded comparison — the seeker first calls to mind the highest worldly purity, brightness, and depth they can imagine, then is invited to recognise that the Siddhas exceed even those. The mind is thus stretched, by stages, toward the inconceivable. The final request — siddhā siddhiṃ mama disaṃtu — uses a beautiful play of words: "May the Siddhas show me siddhi." The verb disaṃtu literally means "may they point out the direction." The Tirthankaras, being totally dispassionate, do not give liberation to anyone — but they show the way. Their teaching, their example, their qualities mapped in the seeker's own mind: these are how the way is shown. The seeker walks the way themselves; the Tirthankaras have shown them where it lies. The verse closes the entire Logassa not with petition but with a clear-eyed recognition: the Siddhas point; the seeker walks; the goal is the same goal the Siddhas themselves attained.

Simply Put: The seeker closes by recalling that these perfected ones are purer than the moon, brighter than the sun, and deeper than the deepest ocean — and asks them, the Siddhas, to point the way to the same liberation they themselves have reached.
