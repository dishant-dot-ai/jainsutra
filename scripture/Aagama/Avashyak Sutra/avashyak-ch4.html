Pratikramaṇa પ્રતિક્રમણ

The Fourth and Longest Essential — the daily turning back from every transgression. Ten paths of confession ending in universal forgiveness offered to every living being.

Avashyak 4: Pratikramaṇa

About This Essential

The Heart of Jain Practice

The fourth and longest essential is Pratikramaṇa — literally, 'going back' — the daily ritual of confessing, withdrawing from, and atoning for every transgression of the day, fortnight, four months, or year. To do pratikraman is not merely to say 'sorry'; it is to actively return — by mind, speech, and body — to the place of right conduct from which one had drifted. Even the smallest unwitting harm — a step on an insect, a sharp word, a wandering thought — must be acknowledged daily, before sleep, lest it harden into karmic deposit.

Avashyak 4 contains ten paths in a precise order: the renewed Karemi Bhante vow; the Chattari Mangalam (four refuges); the synoptic Ichchhami Padikkamiu confession; the Iriyavahiyam (confession for the violence of motion); five Shraman Sutras covering sleep, alms-tour, time-discipline, the great 33-fold enumeration of monastic conduct, and the affirmation of faith with salutation to all contemporary sadhus; and finally the Kshamapana — closing forgiveness offered to every living being in the universe.

The Sutras of Avashyak 4

Prakrit original with English translation, commentary, and the simple explanation.

4.1

Karemi Bhante (Renewed)

करेमि भंते ! सामाइयं, सावज्जं जोगं पच्चक्खामि, जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं, न करेमि, न कारवेमि, करंतंपि अन्नं न समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि, निंदामि, गरिहामि, अप्पाणं वोसिरामि

I undertake, O Lord, the vow of Samayika. (See the full translation under Sutra 1.2 — the same vow-formula opens both essentials.)

The Karemi Bhante is recited a second time at the opening of Pratikramana, not as repetition but as renewal. Before any confession can be made, the practitioner must first re-establish themselves in the state of equanimity (Samayika) — for confession made from a disturbed or distracted mind is no confession at all. Without first stepping back into the renunciation of savajja jog, the practitioner cannot truly examine what they did wrong; the disturbed mind will rationalise, justify, or minimise. Hence the vow is taken again: now I sit in equanimity; now I am ready to look honestly at the day.

Simply Put: Before confessing the day's mistakes, the practitioner first re-takes the vow of equanimity — because honest self-examination is only possible from a calm and balanced mind.

4.2

Chattari Mangalam (The Four Refuges)

(i) चत्तारि मंगलं — अरिहंता मंगलं, सिद्धा मंगलं, साहू मंगलं, केवलि-पण्णत्तो धम्मो मंगलं । (ii) चत्तारि लोगुत्तमा — अरिहंता लोगुत्तमा, सिद्धा लोगुत्तमा, साहू लोगुत्तमा, केवलि-पण्णत्तो धम्मो लोगुत्तमो । (iii) चत्तारि सरणं पवज्जामि — अरिहंते सरणं पवज्जामि, सिद्धे सरणं पवज्जामि, साहू सरणं पवज्जामि, केवलि-पण्णत्तं धम्मं सरणं पवज्जामि

(i) Four are auspicious — the Arihants are auspicious, the Siddhas are auspicious, the Sadhus are auspicious, the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones is auspicious. (ii) Four are supreme in the world — the Arihants are supreme, the Siddhas are supreme, the Sadhus are supreme, the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones is supreme. (iii) Four refuges I take — I take refuge in the Arihants, I take refuge in the Siddhas, I take refuge in the Sadhus, I take refuge in the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones.

Before confession can begin, the practitioner needs to know what holds them up while the act of self-exposure is happening. Confession is dangerous: it requires looking at one's own faults without flinching. To do so safely, the practitioner first declares four refuges — four supports that will not fail. The world is full of "auspicious" things — saffron, kumkum, unbroken rice — but every such object is itself perishable; they cannot dissolve karma or grant lasting welfare. The four true mangalas — Arihants, Siddhas, Sadhus, and the Kevali-proclaimed dharma — are auspicious in a different sense: they actually destroy the karmic root of suffering, and so are auspicious in fact, not in symbol. The Arihants and Siddhas are the goal — already attained, embodied (Arihant) and disembodied (Siddha). The Sadhus are the present companions on the path. The dharma is the path itself. Together they form a complete shelter: a goal to aim at, companions to walk with, and a road to walk on. The Uttaradhyayana Sutra puts it directly: dhammo dīvo paiṭṭhā ṇaṃ — "Dharma is the lamp, the resting-place." The drowning soul cannot save itself; only by taking refuge in what stands above the flood can the practitioner cross. The threefold declaration — they are auspicious / they are supreme / I take refuge in them — moves through three psychological registers: recognition, valuation, surrender. Without all three, refuge is incomplete. Many recognise the Arihants are auspicious; fewer hold them as supreme above all other supports; fewer still actually surrender into their refuge. The sutra walks the practitioner through all three movements before pratikraman begins.

Simply Put: Before confessing the day's wrongs, the practitioner declares the four supports they will lean on while doing so: the embodied awakened ones, the perfected liberated ones, the living spiritual community, and the teaching itself — recognising that these alone are truly safe to lean on.

4.3

Ichchhami Padikkamiu (The Synoptic Confession)

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं (ठामि काउस्सग्गं) जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ, काइओ, वाइओ, माणसिओ, उस्सुत्तो, उम्मग्गो, अकप्पो, अकरणिज्जो, दुज्झाओ, दुव्विचिंतिओ, अणायारो, अणिच्छियव्वो, असमण-पाउग्गो, णाणे तह दंसणे चरित्ते सुए सामाइए; तिण्हं गुत्तीणं, चउण्हं कसायाणं, पंचण्हं महव्वयाणं, छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं, सत्तण्हं पिंडेसणाणं, अट्ठण्हं पवयणमाऊणं, णवण्हं बंभचेरगुत्तीणं, दसविहे समणधम्मे समणाणं जोगाणं, जं खंडियं जं विराहियं तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं !

I wish to perform pratikraman (I stand in kāyotsarga). Whatever transgression of this day I have committed — by body, by speech, by mind — contrary to scripture, contrary to the path, contrary to the rules of conduct, of things-not-to-be-done, of bad meditation, of bad thought, of misconduct, of things-not-to-be-wished-for, unworthy of an ascetic — in knowledge, perception, conduct, scripture-study, in the practice of equanimity; in the three guptis, the four kashayas (passions), the five great vows, the six classes of life, the seven kinds of food-procurement, the eight maternal virtues of the doctrine, the nine guardrails of celibacy, in the ten-fold ascetic dharma, in the activities of monks — whatever I have broken, whatever I have damaged — for that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

This sutra is the synoptic confession — a compressed verbal map of every category of monastic transgression, made before the practitioner enters the longer sutras that examine each category in detail. Its purpose is psychological: to acknowledge that all possible categories of fault are now being placed on the table, before any single one is examined. The structure moves through three layers. First, the practitioner names the means by which transgression occurs: body, speech, mind. Second, the kinds of transgression: contrary to scripture, contrary to the path, against rules of conduct, things not to be done, bad meditation, bad thought, misconduct, unworthy actions. Third, the areas of transgression: knowledge, perception, conduct, scripture-study, equanimity — the five jewels — followed by the eight numbered categories of conduct (3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10) which together exhaust the entire range of monastic discipline. The most commented-upon phrase is micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ — three short words that close every Jain confession. The literal meaning is "may my wrong-doing be undone (be reduced to falsehood)." The Avashyak Niryukti says these words destroy karma if spoken with a heart genuinely melted by remorse — and only if so. Spoken mechanically, they have no effect; spoken with the bhava (inner state) of true contrition, they immediately weaken the karmic bond. The pratikraman commentators carefully distinguish four grades of transgression: atikrama (the mere intention to break a vow), vyatikrama (taking steps toward breaking it), aticāra (partial breach, the vow still standing), and anācāra (full breach, the vow destroyed). The first three can be cleansed by pratikraman alone; the fourth requires additional Guru-ordained atonement (prāyaścitta). This sutra's jaṃ khaṃḍiyaṃ jaṃ virāhiyaṃ covers both the third and fourth grades — preparing the practitioner for both ordinary and extraordinary repair.

Simply Put: Before going through each category of conduct in detail, the practitioner makes a single all-covering confession: "Whatever I have broken or damaged today — by body, speech, or mind, in any of the categories of right conduct — may my wrong-doing be undone."

4.4

Iriyavahiyam (Confession for the Violence of Motion)

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं इरियावहियाए, विराहणाए, गमणागमणे, पाणक्कमणे, बीयक्कमणे, हरियक्कमणे, ओसा-उत्तिंग-पणग-दग-मट्टी-मक्कडा-संताणा-संकमणे, जे मे जीवा विराहिया, एगिंदिया, बेइंदिया, तेइंदिया, चउरिंदिया, पंचिंदिया, अभिहया, वत्तिया, लेसिया, संघाइया, संघट्टिया, परियाविया, किलामिया, उद्दविया, ठाणाओ ठाणं संकामिया, जीवियाओ ववरोविया, तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं

I wish to perform pratikraman for the violence of motion (irya-pathik) — for harm caused while coming and going: by stepping on living creatures, on seeds, on green plants; by stepping on dew-drops, anthills, mildew, water, earth, spider-webs; whatever beings I have harmed — one-sensed, two-sensed, three-sensed, four-sensed, five-sensed; (whatever I have done) by striking, by covering with dust, by pressing into the earth, by colliding-together, by touching, by inflicting suffering, by causing pain, by terrorising, by displacing from one place to another, by depriving of life — for all that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

The Iriyāvahiyam is the daily confession for the violence of motion — and it teaches the most counterintuitive idea in Jain ethics: that motion itself is a form of harm. To walk anywhere is to step on something. To breathe is to disturb air-bodied beings. To speak is to dislodge subtle life. The world is so densely populated with life — visible and invisible — that no movement is karmically neutral. But Jain ethics is not paralysed by this recognition. The same texts that name the harm also name a precise, ten-fold typology of how harm occurs: striking, covering, pressing, colliding, touching, afflicting, fatiguing, terrorising, displacing, killing. The Tattvārtha Sūtra defines violence as pramatta-yogāt prāṇa-vyaparopaṇaṃ hiṃsā — "the destruction of any life-force, performed under the influence of negligence." The crucial qualifier is pramatta (negligent). A surgeon cuts a body; the cut is not violence because the surgeon's intent is healing. A practitioner who walks with awareness causes less karma than one who walks without — even if the same insect is stepped on. The sutra enumerates five categories of beings (one-sensed through five-sensed) because the karmic weight of harming each is different. Crushing a one-sensed earth-body is karmically lighter than killing a five-sensed deer; but neither is dismissed. Both must be confessed. The Iriyāvahiyam is recited before any major activity — before kāyotsarga, before alms-tour, before scripture-study, before pratilekhana. The reasoning: whatever the next activity will be, it will involve motion, and the practitioner must first cleanse the field before entering it. The Avashyak Niryukti calculates that the total possible distinctions of pratikraman across all life-forms, all kinds of harm, all means (mind/speech/body), all times, and all witnesses come to 18,24,120 distinct types — a number meant not as taxonomy but as a vivid reminder that no harm, however subtle, escapes the awareness of the careful practitioner.

Simply Put: Before doing anything else, the practitioner confesses that simply by walking and moving today, they have likely stepped on, brushed against, or displaced countless tiny living beings — and asks that the harm of such unintentional acts be reduced to nothing.

4.5

Pahelu Shraman Sutra (The Sleep Confession)

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं पगामसिज्जाए, निगामसिज्जाए, संथारा उव्वट्टणाए, परियट्टणाए, आउंटण-पसारणाए, छप्पइ संघट्टणाए, कूइए, कक्कराइए, छीए, जंभाइए, आमोसे, ससरक्खामोसे, आउलमाउलाए, सोवणवत्तियाए, इत्थीविप्परियासियाए, दिट्ठीविप्परियासियाए, मणविप्परियासियाए, पाणभोयणविप्परियासियाए, जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं

I wish to perform pratikraman for transgressions of sleep — for sleeping too long, for sleeping repeatedly long, for turning on the bedding, for turning over again and again, for contracting and stretching the limbs, for crushing tiny lice or bedbugs, for indistinct utterances, for grinding the teeth, for sneezing un-awarely, for yawning openly, for unmindful touching, for touching things covered in dust, for being agitated by dreams, for visions of women in sleep, for distorted visions, for distorted thoughts, for distorted (cravings) for food and drink — whatever transgression of this day I have committed, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

The Jain teaching is that karma binds even during sleep. The waking mind is responsible for waking acts; the sleeping mind, while dormant, still carries forward the impressions of the day, and the body still moves — turning, crushing, brushing — and binds karma through that motion. There is no time-out from accountability. Hence the practitioner makes pratikraman not only for the day's deeds but also for the night's. The sutra distinguishes two phases: the conscious moments around sleep (the way one lies down, turns, touches the bed) and the unconscious dream-life (lustful visions, agitated dreams, dreams of breaking the rules). Both are confessed. Why confess what was unconscious? Because the dream is a leakage from the day's unprocessed psychic material. If the day was lived in equanimity, the dreams are usually clear. If the day was lived in agitation, the dreams reveal it. So the dream-pratikraman serves a double purpose: it cleanses the karma of the night, and it diagnoses what the day failed to settle. The Dashavaikalika Sutra contains the rule jayaṃ saye — "sleep with care." Even the way one lies down is part of the practice. A Jain monk traditionally sleeps on a thin straw mat (santhara), turns minimally, and inspects the area before each motion to avoid harming small creatures. The "excessive softness" mentioned in the sutra is rejected not for ascetic harshness but because soft, comfortable bedding nourishes the very pramada (negligence) that pratikraman is trying to dissolve.

Simply Put: The practitioner confesses for sleep itself — for sleeping too much, turning carelessly, crushing tiny creatures in the bedding, and even for the wandering dreams of the night that revealed what the day had not yet settled.

4.6

Bijju Shraman Sutra (The Alms-Tour Confession)

पडिक्कमामि गोयरचरियाए, भिक्खायरियाए, उग्घाड-कवाड उग्घाडणाए, साणा-वच्छा-दारा-संघट्टणाए, मंडी-पाहुडियाए, बलि-पाहुडियाए, ठवणा-पाहुडियाए, संकिए, सहसागारे, अणेसणाए, पाणभोयणाए, बीयभोयणाए, हरियभोयणाए, पच्छाकम्मियाए, पुरेकम्मियाए, अदिट्ठहडाए, दगसंसट्ठहडाए, रयसंसट्ठहडाए, पारिसाडणियाए, परिट्ठावणियाए, ओहासणभिक्खाए, जं उग्गमेणं उप्पायणेसणाए, अपरिसुद्धं परिग्गहियं वा जं न परिट्ठवियं तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं

I perform pratikraman for the conduct of the alms-tour and the conduct of begging — for opening half-open or fully closed doors, for brushing past dogs, calves, or children, for accepting the assembled-and-stored offering, for accepting offering meant for ritual sacrifice, for accepting offering specially set aside, for accepting suspect food, for taking food in sudden haste, for accepting food without proper investigation, for taking food containing living beings, food with seeds, food with green plants, food with pre-touch (action done after for the sake of the monk), food with post-touch, food brought from un-seen places, food touched by living water, food touched by dust-bodied life, for spilling food on the way, for discarding food carelessly, for begging in the manner of a beggar (with attachment), and for any food taken with defects of udgama (donor-side faults), utpādana (monk-side faults), eshana (acceptance faults); for any unclean food received, or for food that should have been discarded but was not — for all that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

A Jain monk lives entirely on alms (gocharī). The food is gathered each day from many households, a small portion from each, in imitation of a cow grazing the tops of grass without uprooting it: the metaphor is exact — no household should feel the burden, no plant should be uprooted. The monk eats only what is already prepared, never anything cooked specially for them, and accepts only what is offered freely. The practice rests on the navakoṭi (nine-fold) commitment — neither cooking nor causing cooking nor approving cooking, by mind, speech, or body. This sutra confesses the forty-two well-known doṣas (faults) of alms-procurement. The classical enumeration is sixteen udgama (faults caused by the donor — e.g. preparing food specially for monks); sixteen utpādana (faults caused by the monk — e.g. praising the householder to influence them); ten eshana (faults at the moment of acceptance — e.g. taking suspect food). The sutra walks through this list compactly, naming each by its key term. The deepest fault is the last: ohāsaṇabhikkhāe — begging in the manner of a beggar. A monk is not a mendicant in the worldly sense. The monk does not beg; the monk receives. Begging carries attachment ("I want this"; "give it to me"); receiving is dispassionate ("if it comes, it comes"). The moment the monk's bowl is approached with desire — for a particular taste, a particular household, a particular outcome — the monk has fallen into the most subtle and dangerous fault, because it is invisible. The food may be perfectly pure but the inner state corrupt. Hence the confession enumerates this last. The sutra closes with two clauses for completeness: any food received with unidentified faults (aparishuddhaṃ pariggahiyaṃ); and food that should have been thrown away (because contaminated) but was kept and consumed (jaṃ na pariṭṭhaviyaṃ). Together they cover both the failure to refuse and the failure to discard.

Simply Put: The monk confesses for the day's alms-tour: for any food taken without proper care for who prepared it, for whom it was prepared, and how it was offered — and for any subtle attachment in the very act of receiving it.

4.7

Trijju Shraman Sutra (The Time-Inspection Confession)

पडिक्कमामि चाउक्कालं सज्झायस्स अकरणयाए, उभओकालं भंडोवगरणस्स अप्पडिलेहणाए, दुप्पडिलेहणाए, अप्पमज्जणाए, दुप्पमज्जणाए, अइक्कमे, वइक्कमे, अइयारे, अणायारे, जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं

I perform pratikraman for the failure to perform scripture-study at the four time-periods (of day and night), for the failure (or improper performance) of the inspection of robes and equipment at the two time-periods (dawn and dusk), for not inspecting at all, for inspecting badly, for not cleaning at all, for cleaning badly — for any atikrama (intent to break a vow), vyatikrama (motion toward breaking it), aticāra (partial breach), or anācāra (full breach) — whatever transgression of this day I have committed, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

This sutra is the time-discipline confession. The monastic life is governed by precise time-windows for each duty: when to study, when to inspect equipment, when to meditate, when to take alms, when to sleep. Slippage in these windows is itself a transgression — not because the schedule is sacred, but because losing the discipline of time loosens the discipline of mind. The Uttaradhyayana states: kālapaḍilehaṇāe ṇaṃ ñāṇāvaraṇijjaṃ kammaṃ khavei — "by inspecting time properly, the karma that obscures knowledge is destroyed." Two specific duties are named: svādhyāya (scripture-study) at four periods of the day-and-night cycle, and pratilekhana–pramārjana (inspection-and-cleaning) at the two transitions of the day. The pratilekhana is the daily ritual of carefully unfolding each robe, alms-bowl, and piece of equipment, scanning for any tiny life that may have settled, and gently removing it to safety. Done with attention, it can take an hour. Done carelessly — duppaḍilehaṇāe — it nominally fulfils the rule but actually causes harm, because the inspector is no longer seeing. The sutra closes by confessing the four grades of breach. Atikrama is the first step into transgression: even the thought "I might break this vow" begins the breach. Vyatikrama is taking practical steps in that direction. Aticāra is partial breach — the vow is wounded but not killed. Anācāra is the full and final breach. The first three can be cleansed by pratikraman alone; the fourth requires the Guru's prescribed prāyaścitta (additional atonement). By naming all four grades together, the sutra ensures that no level of negligence escapes acknowledgement.

Simply Put: The practitioner confesses for any failure to do scripture-study or the careful inspection-and-cleaning of belongings at the right times of day, and for any thought, motion, partial slip, or full breach of the vows that this carelessness might have caused.

4.8

Chothu Shraman Sutra (The Great Enumeration of Conduct)

I perform pratikraman for: (1) one-fold non-restraint; (2) the two bondages — bondage of attachment, bondage of aversion; (3) the three rods — rod of mind, rod of speech, rod of body; (4) the three guards — guarding the mind, speech, body; (5) the three darts — the dart of deceit, the dart of attached aspiration, the dart of false belief; (6) the three prides — pride of wealth, pride of taste, pride of comfort; (7) the three violations — violation of knowledge, of perception, of conduct; (8) the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed; (9) the four impulses — impulse for food, fear, sex, possession; (10) the four false-stories — about women, food, country, kings; (11) the four meditations — sorrowful, violent, dharmic, pure; (12) the five activities — bodily, instrumental, jealous, afflictive, life-destroying; (13) the five sense-objects — sound, form, smell, taste, touch; (14) the five great vows — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession; (15) the five carefulnesses — in walking, speaking, eating, handling, disposing; ... and so through (33) the thirty-three forms of disrespect to the Guru — for any of these in which I have transgressed today, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing. ### 4. ANVAYARTHA (key terms) - असंजम (asaṃjam) = non-restraint — the failure to govern desires; the root of all karmic bondage - बंधण (baṃdhaṇa) = bondage; karmic binding through attachment (rāga) or aversion (dveṣa) - दंड (daṃḍa) = "rod" — that by which the soul is punished; the harmful activity of mind, speech, body - गुत्ति (gutti) = guard, restraint — the protective harnessing of mind, speech, body - सल्ल (salla) = "dart" — the inner thorn that obstructs spiritual progress; three: māyā (deceit hidden in self), nidāna (attached aspiration to a future worldly fruit), mithyā-darśana (false belief, the master-thorn) - गारव (gārava) = pride; three kinds: ṛddhi (pride in wealth/honour), rasa (pride in taste/food), sātā (pride in comfort/health) - विराहणा (virāhaṇā) = violation, breach — of the three jewels (jñāna, darśana, cāritra) - कसाय (kasāya) = passion — that which causes the soul to suffer rebirth (kaṣ = suffering; āya = gain — that which gains by causing suffering): krodha (anger), māna (pride), māyā (deceit), lobha (greed) - सण्णा (saṃṇā) = innate impulse — four primal urges shared with all life: hunger, fear, sex, possession - विकहा (vikahā) = "perverted speech" — talk that destroys the spiritual life: gossip about women, food, countries, kings (i.e., sense-bait, gluttony-bait, geographic curiosity, political fascination) - झाण (jhāṇa) = meditation; four kinds: ārta (sorrowful), raudra (cruel-violent), dharma (right), śukla (pure). The first two are aśubha (unwholesome); the last two are śubha - किरिया (kiriyā) = action (karma-binding); five named here from the larger 25: kāyikī (bodily), adhikaraṇikī (instrumental — using weapons), prādveṣikī (jealous), paritāpanikī (afflictive), prāṇātipātikī (life-destroying) - कामगुण (kāmaguṇa) = the five sense-objects, called "qualities of desire" because they bind the soul through attraction - महव्वय (mahavvaya) = the five great vows — total non-violence, total truth, total non-stealing, total celibacy, total non-possession - समिति (samiti) = the five carefulnesses — in walking, speaking, eating, handling/placing of objects, disposing of bodily wastes - जीवणिकाय (jīvaṇikāya) = the six classes of living beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants, mobile creatures) - पिंडेसणा (piṇḍesaṇā) = the seven kinds of investigation in alms-procurement - पवयणमाऊ (pavayaṇamāū) = the eight maternal disciplines of the Pravacan: 5 samitis + 3 guptis (the pravacan-mātā or "mothers of the doctrine," because they nurture the practitioner) - बंभचेरगुत्ति (baṃbhaceragutti) = the nine guardrails of celibacy (Uttaradhyayana 16): no shared lodging with women/animals/eunuchs, no women's stories, no shared seat, no gazing at limbs, no listening to women's voices/sounds, no recalling past pleasures, no rich food, no excess food, no bodily ornamentation - समणधम्म (samaṇadhamma) = the ten ascetic dharmas: kṣamā (forbearance), mārdava (humility), ārjava (straightforwardness), mukti (non-greed), tapa (austerity), saṃyam (restraint), satya (truth), śauca (purity), akiñcanya (non-possessiveness), brahmacarya (celibacy) - आसायणा (āsāyaṇā) = disrespect — to the Guru, the elders, or the three jewels (the thirty-three categories enumerated in the Daśāśrutaskandha) ### 5. COMMENTARY This is the longest single sutra of the entire Avashyak — a complete inventory of monastic conduct expressed as a series of thirty-three numbered enumerations, ascending from one (one-fold non-restraint) through thirty-three (the thirty-three āśātanās of the Guru). Together these enumerations exhaust the categories of right conduct named in the Jain tradition; whatever can go wrong in monastic life falls somewhere within them. The structural genius of this sutra is its completeness through enumeration. Rather than asking the practitioner to remember every possible fault, the sutra walks them through the entire field of conduct one numerical category at a time. The practitioner cannot complete the recitation without passing through every domain of their own life: passions, impulses, speech, action, sense-engagement, vows, carefulnesses, the protection of life-classes, the dignity of the doctrine, the nine walls of celibacy, the ten qualities of the ascetic, and so on through to the thirty-three subtle disrespects to the teacher. By the end, no domain is unexamined. Several philosophical landmarks deserve mention. The three darts (shalya) name the three deepest inner obstacles: māyā (the deceit one practises on oneself — covering one's faults from one's own awareness); nidāna (attaching one's spiritual practice to a worldly fruit, e.g. "I will fast in order to be reborn rich"); and mithyā-darśana (false belief — the master-dart, which makes all the others invisible). The Tattvārtha Sūtra states niḥśalyo vratī — "the vow-keeper must be without these darts." A practitioner with even one of them cannot truly hold a vow. The three prides (gārava) are the inner movements that pull the soul into lower destinies. Ṛddhi-gārava (pride in attainment) corrupts the seasoned practitioner; rasa-gārava (pride in taste) corrupts the alms-tour; sātā-gārava (pride in comfort) corrupts the entire monastic life by making the body's ease the unspoken priority. The four meditations divide all mental concentration into wholesome and unwholesome. Ārta-dhyāna (sorrowful concentration) is the mind's circling around lost or threatened pleasure. Raudra-dhyāna (violent concentration) is the mind absorbed in violence, lying, theft, or pleasure-protection. Dharma-dhyāna is concentration on the teaching, the path, the welfare of beings. Śukla-dhyāna is the pure stillness that culminates in omniscience. The pratikraman confesses both the failure to do dharma and śukla and the lapse into ārta and raudra. The five great vows (mahāvrats) are the structural skeleton of the entire monastic life: total non-harming, total truth, total non-taking, total celibacy, total non-possession. Each is taken with all three karaṇas (doing, causing, approving) and all three yogas (mind, speech, body) — the navakoṭi commitment. This sutra confesses any breach of any vow at any of those nine doors. The closing enumeration — thirty-three āśātanās — covers the most subtle breach of all: disrespect to the Guru in any of thirty-three categories enumerated in the Daśāśrutaskandha (e.g. walking in front of the Guru, speaking before the Guru is finished, sitting on a higher seat than the Guru, etc.). That this is the thirty-third and final category is significant: the entire sutra began with disrespect to one's own restraint and ends with disrespect to the one who teaches it. The full circle is closed. ### 6. SIMPLY PUT This is the master enumeration: the practitioner walks methodically from "one" through "thirty-three," confessing in each numerical category every possible failure of monastic conduct — from the single failure of restraint to the thirty-three subtle ways of disrespecting the teacher — leaving no domain of practice unexamined. ### 7. CONTEMPLATE If you walked through your day numerically — one category, two, three, all the way — would there be a single category that you could honestly skip? ---

4.9

Panchmu Shraman Sutra (Affirmation of Faith and Salutation)

(1) Salutations to the twenty-four Tirthankaras, beginning with Rishabh and ending with Mahavir. (2) This very Nirgranth doctrine is true, supreme, proclaimed by the omniscient ones, complete, leading to liberation, perfectly pure, dart-extracting, the path to perfection, the path to release, the path out of saṃsāra, the path to nirvāṇa, free of falsehood, unbroken, the path that ends all suffering. (3) Beings established in this doctrine attain perfection, awaken to omniscience, are released, attain final release, end all suffering. (4) That dharma I trust, I have faith in, I take pleasure in, I touch (in practice), I observe, I observe continuously. (5) Trusting that dharma, having faith in it, taking pleasure in it, touching it, observing it, observing it continuously — for the practice of that dharma I now stand up, I withdraw from its violation. Recognising non-restraint, I take up restraint; recognising un-celibacy, I take up celibacy; recognising the un-allowable, I take up the allowable; recognising ignorance, I take up knowledge; recognising no-action, I take up (right) action; recognising false belief, I take up right belief; recognising un-awakening, I take up awakening; recognising the wrong path, I take up the right path. Whatever (faults) I remember, whatever I do not remember; whatever I have already pratikraman'd, whatever I have not yet pratikraman'd — for all those transgressions of this day I now perform pratikraman. (6) I am a śramaṇa — restrained, withdrawn, having destroyed and renounced sinful action, free of attached aspiration, possessed of right belief, free of māyā-mṛṣā (the deceit-and-falsehood combine). (7) In the two-and-a-half continents and the surrounding oceans, in the fifteen lands-of-action, whatever sādhus exist — bearers of the broom, the brush, and the alms-bowl; bearers of the five great vows; bearers of the eighteen-thousand-fold conduct of śīla; observers of the unbreakable conduct — I salute them all with my head, with my mind, with my forehead. ### 4. ANVAYARTHA (selected) - णमो चउवीसाए तित्थयराणं = salutations to the twenty-four Tirthankaras - णिग्गंथं पावयणं (ṇiggaṃthaṃ pāvayaṇaṃ) = the Nirgranth doctrine — nirgrantha = "without knot," free of all inner and outer attachment; pravacana = the spoken teaching - सच्चं अणुत्तरं केवलियं = true, supreme, proclaimed by the omniscient - पडिपुण्णं (paḍipuṇṇaṃ) = complete (un-broken in any part) - णेआउयं (ṇeāuyaṃ) = leading to liberation (nyāyya — just; nayanaśīla — leading) - संसुद्धं = perfectly pure - सल्लकत्तणं = dart-extracting (cutting out the three shalyas) - सिद्धि / मुक्ति / णिज्जाण / णिव्वाण मग्गं = path to perfection / release / exit (from saṃsāra) / nirvāṇa - अवितह (avitaha) = without falsehood (vitatha = false) - अविसंधि (avisaṃdhi) = unbroken in continuity (eternal teaching) - सव्वदुक्खप्पहीणमग्गं = the path that ends all suffering - सद्दहामि, पत्तिआमि, रोएमि, फासेमि, पालेमि, अणुपालेमि = I trust (śraddhā), I have faith (pratīti), I take pleasure (ruci), I touch (sparśa — practice), I observe (pālan), I observe continuously (anupālan) — the six progressive movements of religious commitment - अब्भुट्ठिओमि (abbhuṭṭhioṃi) = "I have stood up" — I am present, I am ready, I am committed (Sanskrit abhyutthitaḥ-asmi; profoundly active) - आराहणाए / विराहणाए = for the practice / from the violation - The eight pairs (asaṃyam/saṃyam, abrahma/brahma, akalpa/kalpa, ajñāna/jñāna, akriyā/kriyā, mithyā/samyak, abodhi/bodhi, amārga/mārga) = the eight specific renunciations and acceptances that make up the practitioner's vow-of-practice - अड्ढाइज्जेसु दीव समुद्देसु = in the two-and-a-half continents (Jambudweep + Dhātakikhaṇḍa + half of Puṣkarvar) and the encircling oceans — the entire human-region of Jain cosmography - पण्णरससु कम्मभूमिसु = in the fifteen lands-of-action (where the Tirthankara's teaching is currently active) - रयहरण-गुच्छग-पडिग्गह = the broom (rajoharaṇ), the brush (gucchā), the alms-bowl (pātra) — the three identifying marks of the Jain monk - अट्ठारस-सहस्स-सीलंग = the eighteen-thousand divisions of conduct (śīlāṅga) — derived as 10 dharmas × 10 life-types × 5 senses × 4 saṃjñās × 3 yogas × 3 karaṇas = 18,000 ### 5. COMMENTARY This is the affirmation sutra — a seven-movement crescendo that takes the practitioner from salutation through reasoned recognition of the doctrine to renewed personal commitment, and finally to a sweeping salute to every other practitioner currently walking the path anywhere in the universe. Movement 1 (salutation): Before declaring faith in the doctrine, the practitioner first salutes the twenty-four who proclaimed it. The doctrine is not a free-standing abstraction; it has a lineage of awakened proclaimers, and faith in the doctrine cannot be separated from reverence for those who gave it. Movement 2 (the doctrine described): In a remarkable string of fifteen attributes, the Nirgranth doctrine is named. Each attribute corresponds to a specific philosophical claim: true (it is not false); supreme (none higher); proclaimed by omniscient ones (so cannot be partially mistaken); complete (nothing missing); leading-to-liberation (its end is mokṣa, not worldly fruit); perfectly pure (no defects); dart-extracting (it removes the three shalyas); path to siddhi / mukti / nirvāṇa / nirvāṇa-mārga (four near-synonyms emphasising the unity of the goal); without falsehood (no portion can be set aside as later proven wrong); unbroken (the same doctrine taught by all twenty-four); path that ends all suffering (the final criterion). Movement 3 (the doctrine's fruit): Beings who establish themselves in this doctrine siddhanti (become Siddhas), bujjhanti (become Buddhas — awakened), muccanti (are released), parinivvāyanti (attain final nirvāṇa), and end all suffering. The five verbs trace the practitioner's career through completion. Movement 4 (the practitioner's faith — six grades): The sutra now turns to the personal commitment, expressed in six progressive grades of religious feeling. Sadda-hāmi (I trust — bare confidence). Patti-āmi (I have inner faith — confidence has become conviction). Roe-mi (I take pleasure in — conviction has warmed into love). Phāse-mi (I touch — love has begun to act). Pāle-mi (I observe — action has become discipline). Anu-pāle-mi (I observe continuously — discipline has become a way of life). Each verb is a higher tier of the same orientation; together they describe how mere intellectual assent becomes lived practice. Movement 5 (the eight renunciations): This is the heart of the practitioner's pratigya. Eight pairs are named. In each pair, the practitioner recognises (parijānāmi) the negative side and takes up (upasampadyāmi) the positive side. Recognising means seeing the negative for what it is — its causes, its consequences, its taste in the mouth of one who carries it. Without this recognition, renunciation is only a wish. Once recognised, the negative can be set down; once the negative is set down, the positive can be picked up. The eight pairs cover the entire range of practice: from gross conduct (non-restraint vs. restraint, un-celibacy vs. celibacy, the un-allowable vs. the allowable) through cognitive orientation (ignorance vs. knowledge, no-action vs. action, false vs. right belief) to the deepest level (un-awakening vs. awakening, wrong path vs. right path). The movement closes with a moving acknowledgement: jaṃ saṃbharāmi, jaṃ ca na saṃbharāmi — "what I remember, what I do not remember"; jaṃ paḍikkamāmi, jaṃ ca na paḍikkamāmi — "what I have already pratikraman'd, what I have not yet pratikraman'd." The practitioner confesses even the faults they cannot recall, and even the faults that have not yet been formally addressed in the day's pratikraman. This is the final completeness. Movement 6 (the practitioner's character): Now the practitioner declares what they are: I am a śramaṇa — restrained, withdrawn, having renounced sinful action, free of nidāna, possessed of right belief, free of māyā-mṛṣā. This is not boast; it is self-naming — a deliberate articulation of the identity the practitioner intends to live up to. The Jain tradition holds that to name oneself accurately is to summon oneself toward what is named. Movement 7 (salute to all contemporary sādhus): The sutra closes by reaching outward. In the two-and-a-half continents — the entire human world of Jain cosmography — wherever any sādhu exists, bearing the three marks (broom, brush, bowl), holding the five great vows, observing the eighteen-thousand divisions of conduct, the practitioner salutes them all with head, mind, and forehead. The practice is not solitary; the practitioner is part of a worldwide invisible community of those undertaking the same work, and the daily pratikraman closes by recognising and saluting them. The number 18,000 deserves a note: it is calculated as 10 ascetic dharmas × 10 categories of life × 5 senses × 4 sañjñās × 3 yogas × 3 karaṇas. The number is meant not as count but as expression of completeness — every possible combination of right action across every dimension is included. ### 6. SIMPLY PUT The practitioner declares faith in the doctrine, names its qualities, accepts the eight specific renunciations of the practice, identifies themselves as a śramaṇa, and salutes every other monk and nun currently walking this same path anywhere in the world. ### 7. CONTEMPLATE When you commit to a path, do you commit only with your understanding — or do you also let yourself feel pleasure in it, touch it, observe it, observe it continuously? ---

4.10

Kshamapana Sutra (The Forgiveness Closing)

खामेमि सव्वे जीवा, सव्वे जीवा वि खमंतु मे । मित्तीमे सव्व भूएसु, वेरं मज्झं ण केणइ ॥१॥ एवमहं आलोइय, णिंदिय गरिहिय दुगंछियं सम्मं । तिविहेण पडिक्कंतो, वंदामि जिणे चउव्वीसं ॥२॥

I forgive all living beings; may all living beings forgive me. My friendship is with all beings; I have enmity with none. Thus, having confessed (āloiya), having censured before my own conscience (ṇiṃdiya), having confessed before my Guru (garihiya), having looked upon (my own faults) with disgust (dugaṃchiyaṃ) — properly — having performed pratikraman by all three (mind, speech, body) — I bow to the twenty-four Jinas.

The Kshamapana is the closing of the entire pratikraman — and arguably the most beloved verse in lay Jain practice. After all the categories have been examined, all the faults confessed, all the pratigyās renewed, the practitioner now turns outward one last time and offers two simple commitments to every other living being in the universe: Khāmemi savve jīvā — "I forgive all living beings." If, anywhere in any past or present life, any being has caused me suffering — by harm, by deceit, by indifference, by injustice — I now forgive them. I lay down whatever resentment I have carried. I do not carry the karmic seed of vengeance any further. Savve jīvā vi khamaṃtu me — "May all living beings forgive me." If, anywhere in any past or present life, I have caused suffering to any being — knowingly or unknowingly, by my hand, by my speech, by my passive approval — I ask their forgiveness. I do not minimise; I do not justify; I simply ask. Mittīme savva bhūesu — "My friendship is with all beings." Forgiveness is the negative half; friendship is the positive half. To merely cancel old debts is not enough; the practitioner must also actively extend friendship toward every form of life. Veraṃ majjhaṃ ṇa keṇai — "I have enmity with none." This is the result that was being aimed at all along. The pratikraman ritual was not an end in itself; its end was this — to dissolve enmity completely, so that the practitioner enters sleep tonight without a single inner thread of vengeance, resentment, or grudge tying them to any other being in the universe. The second verse summarises the whole work: thus, having confessed openly, censured inwardly, confessed before the Guru, looked upon my faults with proper disgust, having performed pratikraman by all three of mind-speech-body — I bow to the twenty-four Jinas. The bow at the end is significant: the entire ritual closes where it began, with reverence to those who showed the path. The Jain teaching is that one who can extend pure friendship (maitrī) to all beings and hold enmity with none arrives at vītarāgatā — total dispassion. And from total dispassion, omniscience follows. So this small two-verse closing is not minor; it is the door through which every other essential delivers its fruit.

Simply Put: The practitioner closes the entire pratikraman with the simplest and deepest commitment of all: "I forgive every living being who has wronged me. May every living being I have wronged forgive me. My friendship is with all beings. I have enmity with no one." Then, having confessed by mind, speech, and body, the practitioner bows to the twenty-four Jinas — and the ritual is complete.
