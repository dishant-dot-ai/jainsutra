Avashyak Sutra · Avashyak 5

Kāyotsarga કાયોત્સર્ગ

The Fifth Essential — release of the body. The standing or seated meditation in which the practitioner sets down even the body, so that the mind can rest in pure consciousness.

Avashyak 5: Kāyotsarga

About This Essential

The Healing of the Wound

The body cannot literally be discarded while life remains; what is released is attachment to the body — the constant micro-pulse of identification that says 'this is me,' 'I must move,' 'I must protect this.' For the duration of the practice, the practitioner stands or sits absolutely still, suspends bodily activity, restrains speech, harnesses the mind into pure meditation, and rests in the consciousness that is the soul — separated from the bodily sheath that ordinarily disguises it.

The Anuyogadwar Sutra calls Kāyotsarga the vraṇa-cikitsā — the 'dressing of the wound.' Pratikramana opens the wound; Kāyotsarga heals it. The Avashyak Niryukti gives five direct fruits: the inertness of the body is destroyed; the intelligence is purified; the capacity to bear both pleasure and pain develops; subtle contemplation increases; one-pointed concentration in wholesome meditation is gained.

5.3

Kayotsarga Pratigya Sutra (Tassa Uttarīkaranenaṃ)

(i) तस्स उत्तरीकरणेणं पायच्छित्तकरणेणं विसोहीकरणेणं विसल्लीकरणेणं पावाणं कम्माणं निग्घायणट्ठाए, ठामि काउस्सग्गं । (ii) अन्नत्थ ऊससिएणं, नीससिएणं, खासिएणं, छीएणं, जंभाइएणं, उड्डुएणं, वायनिसग्गेणं, भमलीए, पित्तमुच्छाए, सुहुमेहिं अंगसंचालेहिं, सुहुमेहिं खेल संचालेहिं, सुहुमेहिं दिट्ठि संचालेहिं, एवमाइएहिं आगारेहिं, अभग्गो, अविराहिओ, हुज्ज मे काउस्सग्गो । (iii) जाव अरिहंताणं भगवंताणं नमुक्कारेणं ण पारेमि ताव कायं ठाणेणं, मोणेणं, झाणेणं, अप्पाणं वोसिरामि

(i) For the further treatment, for atonement, for purification, for the extraction of the inner darts, for the destruction of sinful karmas — I stand in Kāyotsarga. (ii) Apart from inhaling, exhaling, coughing, sneezing, yawning, belching, breaking wind, dizziness, fainting from bile, the subtle movement of limbs, the subtle movement of phlegm, the subtle movement of vision — apart from these and similar exemptions — may my Kāyotsarga be unbroken and undamaged. (iii) Until I conclude with the salutation to the Arihant Bhagavans (i.e., until I say "Namo Arihantāṇaṃ"), so long do I let go of (claim upon) my body — by remaining in one place, by silence, by meditation. I let go of (the kashaya- and yoga-bound part of) my self.

This formula, called Tassa Uttarīkaraneṇaṃ from its opening words, is the master vow of every Kāyotsarga in Jain practice — recited before each separate session, whether daily, festival, or special. It has three movements, each one teaching a different aspect of how to enter and hold this practice. Movement 1: Five Reasons for Standing. The practitioner first names why. The five reasons trace a precise sequence: (a) uttarīkaraṇa — further-treatment, the recognition that the soul, even after pratikraman, still needs deeper work; (b) prāyaścitta — formal atonement; (c) visodhī — inner purification of unwholesome dispositions; (d) visalli — extraction of the three deepest darts (deceit-of-self, attached aspiration, false belief); (e) pāvāṇaṃ kammāṇaṃ ṇigghāyaṇa — total destruction of karmas at their seed. The Avashyak Niryukti compares the soul's purification to washing a stained cloth in three stages: first doṣa-mārjana (removing visible stains, like soaking in soap-water — accomplished by ālocanā and pratikraman); then hīnāṅga-pūrti (mending what is torn, accomplished by Kāyotsarga); then atiśayadhāyaka (final polishing for special brightness, accomplished by pratyākhyāna). Each stage is essential. Pratikraman alone leaves the soul mended but unpolished; Kāyotsarga alone, without pratikraman first, has no torn place to mend. The five reasons named in this movement encompass the second stage in its full depth. Movement 2: The Twelve (or Sixteen) Exemptions. The practitioner cannot literally stop the body. Breath continues; phlegm rises; the eye blinks. To make the vow honestly, the practitioner first lists what the vow does not attempt to control. Twelve are named explicitly: breathing in, breathing out, coughing, sneezing, yawning, belching, breaking wind, dizziness, fainting from bile, subtle limb-movement, subtle phlegm-movement, subtle eye-movement. The Niryukti adds four more covered by evamāiehiṃ ("and so on"): sudden contact with fire, sudden bodily wound, abduction by a king or thief, attack by a poisonous creature. In any of these cases, the Kāyotsarga is not broken. This is a masterstroke of practical wisdom. Any vow that pretends to control what cannot be controlled produces guilt and distraction. By specifying exactly what is exempt, the sutra liberates the practitioner to focus on what can be controlled — voluntary motion, voluntary speech, voluntary thought. Movement 3: Time-Limit and Posture. The Kāyotsarga is bounded in time: from now until the practitioner formally ends it by reciting "Namo Arihantāṇaṃ." Within that window, three commitments are made: kāyaṃ ṭhāṇeṇaṃ (the body in one place — kāya-gupti), moṇeṇaṃ (in silence — vacana-gupti), jhāṇeṇaṃ (in meditation — mano-gupti). These are the three guptis (restraints) being practised simultaneously — exactly the structural definition of Kāyotsarga. The closing words are the most profound: appāṇaṃ vosirāmi — "I let go of (that which I had been calling) myself." This appāṇa is not the pure soul (which cannot be let go of, since the soul is the very subject doing the letting-go); it is the kashaya-bound, yoga-bound self — the small "I" that ordinarily identifies with the body's positions, the speech's habits, and the mind's wanderings. That self is set down, so that what remains is the soul as pure upayoga — pure awareness, alone with itself. The commentators give four grades of Kāyotsarga: (1) Utthita-utthita — best: the body stands and the soul stands too (alert in dharma-dhyāna or shukla-dhyāna); (2) Utthita-niviṣṭa — the body stands but the soul has gone to sleep (negligence); (3) Upaviṣṭa-utthita — the body sits (due to old age or illness) but the soul stands (acceptable); (4) Upaviṣṭa-niviṣṭa — neither body nor soul stands (this is not Kāyotsarga at all, only its outward form). Of these, only the first and third are true Kāyotsarga. The Niryukti also enumerates nineteen postural defects (dosha) to be avoided — the horse-defect (standing on one foot), the creeper-defect (swaying with the wind), the pillar-defect (leaning on a wall), the necked-defect (head bent), the silent-defect (dumb-like inarticulate sounds), and so on — each named after the way it visibly resembles. Avoiding them is part of the practice.

Simply Put: The practitioner stands (or sits) absolutely still and says: "I am standing in Kāyotsarga to atone, to purify, to remove the inner darts, and to destroy karma. Apart from natural acts I can't control — breathing, coughing, blinking — may my standing be unbroken. Until I formally end with 'Namo Arihantāṇaṃ,' I keep the body in one place, the speech in silence, the mind in meditation, and I let go of my identification with this body."
