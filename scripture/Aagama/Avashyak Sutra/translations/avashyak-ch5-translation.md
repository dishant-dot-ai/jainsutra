# Avashyak Sutra — Avashyak 5
# Kayotsarga (કાયોત્સર્ગ) — Release of the Body

**Avashyak 5 of the Avashyak Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Avashyak Sutra, printed pages 128–136 (PDF pages 192–200)*
*Total Paths in this Avashyak: 4 (the central new sutra is the Kayotsarga Pratigya Sutra)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fifth essential is **Kāyotsarga** — literally, "release of the body" (kāya + utsarga). The body cannot literally be discarded while life remains; what is released is *attachment to the body* — the constant micro-pulse of identification that says "this is me," "I must move," "I must protect this." For the duration of the practice, the practitioner stands or sits absolutely still, suspends bodily activity, restrains speech, harnesses the mind into pure meditation, and rests in the consciousness that *is the soul* — separated from the bodily sheath that ordinarily disguises it.

Why does Kāyotsarga come fifth? The earlier essentials cleansed the field: Samayika established equanimity; Chaturvinshatistava established the ideal; Vandana repaired the relationship with the Guru; Pratikramana confessed and atoned for the day's transgressions. But cleansing is not the same as healing. After the wound has been disclosed and apologised for, the wound itself must be allowed to close. The Anuyogadwar Sutra calls Kāyotsarga the *vraṇa-cikitsā* — the "dressing of the wound." Pratikramana opens the wound; Kāyotsarga heals it.

The Avashyak Niryukti gives five direct fruits of Kāyotsarga:
> *dehamai jaḍḍa-suḍḍhī sukha-duḥkhati-tikkhayā aṇuppehā |*
> *jhāyai ya suha jhāṇaṃ eyaggo kāusaggami ||1462||*

(1) the inertness of the body is destroyed; (2) the intelligence is purified; (3) the capacity to bear both pleasure and pain develops; (4) subtle contemplation increases; (5) one-pointed concentration in wholesome meditation is gained.

Avashyak 5 contains four paths. Three of them — Karemi Bhante (Samayika), the synoptic Ichchhami Padikkamiu, and the Logassa — have already been treated in earlier Avashyaks; they are recited again here as the framework within which the central new practice happens. The fourth and central path is the **Kāyotsarga Pratigya Sutra** (also called *Tassa Uttarīkaraneṇaṃ*) — the formal vow-formula that opens every Kāyotsarga in Jain practice. It is given here with its three movements: the statement of purpose, the acknowledged exemptions, and the time-limit and posture vow.

---

## SUTRA 5.1 — Karemi Bhante (Recited Again)

The Samayika vow opens Kāyotsarga as it opens every essential. (Full translation in Sutra 1.2.) The reasoning is the same: meditation cannot occur from a mind still entangled in *savajja jog*; the practitioner first re-enters equanimity before standing in stillness.

---

## SUTRA 5.2 — Ichchhami Padikkamiu (Synoptic Pratikraman, Recited Again)

The brief synoptic pratikraman is recited again. (Full translation in Sutra 4.3.) The Kāyotsarga is performed in order to atone for whatever residual transgressions remain after Pratikramana — those that pratikraman addressed inadequately, those one did not remember in time, and those that lie below conscious recall. The synoptic confession is recited here to acknowledge that Kāyotsarga is the *uttarīkaraṇa* — the "further treatment" — for what pratikraman alone could not finish.

## SUTRA 5.3 — Kayotsarga Pratigya Sutra (Tassa Uttarīkaranenaṃ)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

(i) तस्स उत्तरीकरणेणं पायच्छित्तकरणेणं विसोहीकरणेणं विसल्लीकरणेणं पावाणं कम्माणं निग्घायणट्ठाए, ठामि काउस्सग्गं ।

(ii) अन्नत्थ ऊससिएणं, नीससिएणं, खासिएणं, छीएणं, जंभाइएणं, उड्डुएणं, वायनिसग्गेणं, भमलीए, पित्तमुच्छाए, सुहुमेहिं अंगसंचालेहिं, सुहुमेहिं खेल संचालेहिं, सुहुमेहिं दिट्ठि संचालेहिं, एवमाइएहिं आगारेहिं, अभग्गो, अविराहिओ, हुज्ज मे काउस्सग्गो ।

(iii) जाव अरिहंताणं भगवंताणं नमुक्कारेणं ण पारेमि ताव कायं ठाणेणं, मोणेणं, झाणेणं, अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥५.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 5.3 ||
(i) tassa uttarīkaraṇeṇaṃ pāyacchittakaraṇeṇaṃ visohīkaraṇeṇaṃ visallīkaraṇeṇaṃ pāvāṇaṃ kammāṇaṃ ṇigghāyaṇaṭṭhāe, ṭhāmi kāussaggaṃ |

(ii) annattha ūsasieṇaṃ, ṇīsasieṇaṃ, khāsieṇaṃ, chīeṇaṃ, jaṃbhāieṇaṃ, uḍḍueṇaṃ, vāyaṇisaggeṇaṃ, bhamalīe, pittamucchāe, suhumehiṃ aṃgasaṃcālehiṃ, suhumehiṃ khela saṃcālehiṃ, suhumehiṃ diṭṭhi saṃcālehiṃ, evamāiehiṃ āgārehiṃ, abhaggo, avirāhio, hujja me kāussaggo |

(iii) jāva arihaṃtāṇaṃ bhagavaṃtāṇaṃ namukkāreṇaṃ ṇa pāremi tāva kāyaṃ ṭhāṇeṇaṃ, moṇeṇaṃ, jhāṇeṇaṃ, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(i) For the further treatment, for atonement, for purification, for the extraction of the inner darts, for the destruction of sinful karmas — I stand in Kāyotsarga.

(ii) Apart from inhaling, exhaling, coughing, sneezing, yawning, belching, breaking wind, dizziness, fainting from bile, the subtle movement of limbs, the subtle movement of phlegm, the subtle movement of vision — apart from these and similar exemptions — may my Kāyotsarga be unbroken and undamaged.

(iii) Until I conclude with the salutation to the Arihant Bhagavans (i.e., until I say "Namo Arihantāṇaṃ"), so long do I let go of (claim upon) my body — by remaining in one place, by silence, by meditation. I let go of (the kashaya- and yoga-bound part of) my self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**Movement (i) — Five Purposes:**
- **तस्स** (tassa) = "of that" — referring to the (fault-stained) self that has been confessed in pratikraman
- **उत्तरीकरणेणं** (uttarīkaraṇeṇaṃ) = "for further-treatment" — *uttarī* = subsequent/improving; *karaṇa* = action. Just as a torn garment requires not only washing but mending, the soul needs not only confession but further refinement
- **पायच्छित्तकरणेणं** (pāyacchittakaraṇeṇaṃ) = for atonement — *prāyaścitta* = "that by which the heart (citta) is pacified"; the formal correction of past wrong
- **विसोहीकरणेणं** (visohīkaraṇeṇaṃ) = for purification — by purifying the inner state, all unwholesome dispositions are dissolved
- **विसल्लीकरणेणं** (visallīkaraṇeṇaṃ) = for extraction of the inner darts — the three *shalyas* (māyā, nidāna, mithyā-darśana) which obstruct the soul's path; Kāyotsarga, being itself an atonement, removes these inner thorns
- **पावाणं कम्माणं निग्घायणट्ठाए** (pāvāṇaṃ kammāṇaṃ ṇigghāyaṇaṭṭhāe) = for the destruction of sinful karmas. *Nighātaṇ* = total annihilation; *for the seedless destruction*, so that the karma cannot return
- **ठामि काउस्सग्गं** (ṭhāmi kāussaggaṃ) = I stand in Kāyotsarga

**Movement (ii) — Twelve Natural Exemptions (and four additional):**
- **अन्नत्थ** (annattha) = "apart from / except for" — establishes the list of involuntary acts that do not break the Kāyotsarga
- **ऊससिएणं, नीससिएणं** = inhaling, exhaling (breath cannot be stopped)
- **खासिएणं** = coughing
- **छीएणं** = sneezing
- **जंभाइएणं** = yawning
- **उड्डुएणं** = belching
- **वायनिसग्गेणं** = breaking wind / passing of *apāna-vāyu*
- **भमलीए** = dizziness
- **पित्तमुच्छाए** = fainting from bile-disorder (the four bodily humours acting up)
- **सुहुमेहिं अंगसंचालेहिं** = subtle movements of the limbs (involuntary muscle twitches)
- **सुहुमेहिं खेल संचालेहिं** = subtle movements of phlegm (rising in throat, etc.)
- **सुहुमेहिं दिट्ठि संचालेहिं** = subtle movements of vision (involuntary blinking)
- **एवमाइएहिं आगारेहिं** = and other such exemptions (covering four more named in the Niryukti: sudden touch of fire, attack on the body, abduction by a thief or king, attack by a venomous creature)
- **अभग्गो, अविराहिओ** = unbroken (abhagna), undamaged (avirādhita) — even with these natural exemptions
- **हुज्ज मे काउस्सग्गो** = may my Kāyotsarga be (so)

**Movement (iii) — The Time-Limit and Posture Vow:**
- **जाव** (jāva) = until / as long as
- **अरिहंताणं भगवंताणं नमुक्कारेणं ण पारेमि** = until I conclude with the salutation to the Arihant Bhagavans (i.e., until I formally end the Kāyotsarga by saying "Namo Arihantāṇaṃ")
- **ताव कायं** = until then, the body
- **ठाणेणं** (ṭhāṇeṇaṃ) = by remaining in one place (kāya-gupti)
- **मोणेणं** (moṇeṇaṃ) = by silence (vacana-gupti)
- **झाणेणं** (jhāṇeṇaṃ) = by meditation (mano-gupti)
- **अप्पाणं वोसिरामि** (appāṇaṃ vosirāmi) = I let go of the (kashaya-laden, yoga-laden) self — I release the body from being "mine," and rest in the consciousness that is *upayoga* (pure awareness)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This formula, called *Tassa Uttarīkaraneṇaṃ* from its opening words, is the master vow of every Kāyotsarga in Jain practice — recited before each separate session, whether daily, festival, or special. It has three movements, each one teaching a different aspect of how to enter and hold this practice.

**Movement 1: Five Reasons for Standing.** The practitioner first names *why*. The five reasons trace a precise sequence: (a) *uttarīkaraṇa* — further-treatment, the recognition that the soul, even after pratikraman, still needs deeper work; (b) *prāyaścitta* — formal atonement; (c) *visodhī* — inner purification of unwholesome dispositions; (d) *visalli* — extraction of the three deepest darts (deceit-of-self, attached aspiration, false belief); (e) *pāvāṇaṃ kammāṇaṃ ṇigghāyaṇa* — total destruction of karmas at their seed.

The Avashyak Niryukti compares the soul's purification to washing a stained cloth in three stages: first *doṣa-mārjana* (removing visible stains, like soaking in soap-water — accomplished by ālocanā and pratikraman); then *hīnāṅga-pūrti* (mending what is torn, accomplished by Kāyotsarga); then *atiśayadhāyaka* (final polishing for special brightness, accomplished by pratyākhyāna). Each stage is essential. Pratikraman alone leaves the soul mended but unpolished; Kāyotsarga alone, without pratikraman first, has no torn place to mend. The five reasons named in this movement encompass the second stage in its full depth.

**Movement 2: The Twelve (or Sixteen) Exemptions.** The practitioner cannot literally stop the body. Breath continues; phlegm rises; the eye blinks. To make the vow honestly, the practitioner first lists what the vow does *not* attempt to control. Twelve are named explicitly: breathing in, breathing out, coughing, sneezing, yawning, belching, breaking wind, dizziness, fainting from bile, subtle limb-movement, subtle phlegm-movement, subtle eye-movement. The Niryukti adds four more covered by *evamāiehiṃ* ("and so on"): sudden contact with fire, sudden bodily wound, abduction by a king or thief, attack by a poisonous creature. In any of these cases, the Kāyotsarga is not broken.

This is a masterstroke of practical wisdom. Any vow that pretends to control what cannot be controlled produces guilt and distraction. By specifying exactly what is exempt, the sutra liberates the practitioner to focus on what *can* be controlled — voluntary motion, voluntary speech, voluntary thought.

**Movement 3: Time-Limit and Posture.** The Kāyotsarga is bounded in time: from now until the practitioner formally ends it by reciting "Namo Arihantāṇaṃ." Within that window, three commitments are made: *kāyaṃ ṭhāṇeṇaṃ* (the body in one place — kāya-gupti), *moṇeṇaṃ* (in silence — vacana-gupti), *jhāṇeṇaṃ* (in meditation — mano-gupti). These are the three guptis (restraints) being practised simultaneously — exactly the structural definition of Kāyotsarga.

The closing words are the most profound: *appāṇaṃ vosirāmi* — "I let go of (that which I had been calling) myself." This *appāṇa* is not the pure soul (which cannot be let go of, since the soul is the very subject doing the letting-go); it is the *kashaya-bound, yoga-bound self* — the small "I" that ordinarily identifies with the body's positions, the speech's habits, and the mind's wanderings. *That* self is set down, so that what remains is the soul as pure *upayoga* — pure awareness, alone with itself.

The commentators give four grades of Kāyotsarga: (1) *Utthita-utthita* — best: the body stands and the soul stands too (alert in dharma-dhyāna or shukla-dhyāna); (2) *Utthita-niviṣṭa* — the body stands but the soul has gone to sleep (negligence); (3) *Upaviṣṭa-utthita* — the body sits (due to old age or illness) but the soul stands (acceptable); (4) *Upaviṣṭa-niviṣṭa* — neither body nor soul stands (this is not Kāyotsarga at all, only its outward form). Of these, only the first and third are true Kāyotsarga.

The Niryukti also enumerates nineteen postural defects (*dosha*) to be avoided — the horse-defect (standing on one foot), the creeper-defect (swaying with the wind), the pillar-defect (leaning on a wall), the necked-defect (head bent), the silent-defect (dumb-like inarticulate sounds), and so on — each named after the way it visibly resembles. Avoiding them is part of the practice.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner stands (or sits) absolutely still and says: "I am standing in Kāyotsarga to atone, to purify, to remove the inner darts, and to destroy karma. Apart from natural acts I can't control — breathing, coughing, blinking — may my standing be unbroken. Until I formally end with 'Namo Arihantāṇaṃ,' I keep the body in one place, the speech in silence, the mind in meditation, and I let go of my identification with this body."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you set down — even for ten minutes — the constant low pulse of "this is me, I must move, I must speak" — what remains as the one who notices the setting-down?

## SUTRA 5.4 — Logassa (Recited Again)

After the Kāyotsarga is held for the duration of one Logassa (or four Logassas, depending on the occasion), the practitioner concludes by reciting the Logassa Sutra aloud. (Full translation in Sutra 2.1 through 2.7.) The Logassa is recited as the natural seal of every Kāyotsarga: the practitioner has stood in stillness; now the body returns to motion, and the first words are those of praise to the twenty-four — the same Tirthankaras whose example made the stillness possible.

## END OF AVASHYAK 5

॥ આવશ્યક-૫ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Avashyak-5 sampurna — "Avashyak 5 is complete.")*

The fifth essential closes here. Of its four paths, three are recitations carried over from earlier essentials (Samayika, synoptic pratikraman, Logassa); the central new offering is the Kāyotsarga Pratigya Sutra — *tassa uttarīkaraneṇaṃ* — which establishes the practitioner in the body-released stillness in which the deepest karmic wound is at last allowed to close.

What follows in the sixth and final essential — **Pratyākhyāna** — is the formal renunciation of food, comfort, and sense-pleasure for a definite period: the polishing-stroke that completes the work all five preceding essentials have built toward.
