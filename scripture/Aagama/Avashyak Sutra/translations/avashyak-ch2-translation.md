# Avashyak Sutra — Avashyak 2
# Chaturvinshatistava (ચતુર્વિંશતિસ્તવ) — The Praise of the Twenty-Four

**Avashyak 2 of the Avashyak Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Avashyak Sutra, printed pages 22–31 (PDF pages 86–95)*
*Total Sutras (gathas) in this Avashyak: 7*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The second essential is **Chaturvinshatistava** — the praise of the twenty-four Tirthankaras of this descending half-cycle (avasarpini) in Bharat-kshetra: from Rishabhdev, the first, to Vardhaman Mahavir, the last. Together these seven verses form a single sutra known to every Jain by its opening word: the **Logassa**.

Why does the praise of the Tirthankaras come second, immediately after Samayika? Because the practitioner of equanimity needs an object on which to fix the mind. Just as a painter first sketches the form before applying colours, the seeker first establishes the image of perfected souls upon the inner canvas before attempting the work of self-purification. The Tirthankaras are those who have already completed what the seeker has only begun. To praise them — to recall their names, contemplate their qualities, and invite their grace — is to align oneself with the direction in which one wishes to travel. As the Sanskrit verse reminds us: *śraddhāmayo'yaṃ puruṣaḥ, yo yacchraddhaḥ sa eva saḥ* — "A person becomes whatever they hold faith in."

The Logassa was asked of Lord Mahavira directly. In the Uttaradhyayana Sutra, Gautam Swami asks: "O Lord, what does the soul gain by praising the twenty-four Tirthankaras?" Mahavir's answer: "By such praise, the karma of false belief is reduced; the practitioner attains the purity of right perception; through love of their qualities those qualities arise within the practitioner's own being; and in a short time the soul becomes liberated."

The sutra has a precise five-part structure: (1) the nature of the Tirthankaras [gatha 1]; (2) their names [gathas 2–4]; (3) their distinguishing marks [gatha 5]; (4) the devotee's request [gatha 6]; (5) the similes by which they are honoured [gatha 7]. The recitation is performed standing, in the upright posture called Jin-mudra — feet together, arms hanging straight at the sides, in imitation of the Tirthankara's own meditative form.

## SUTRA 2.1 — The Resolve to Praise

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

लोगस्स उज्जोयगरे, धम्मतित्थयरे जिणे ।
अरिहंते कित्तइस्सं, चउवीसं पि केवली ॥२.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.1 ||
logassa ujjoyagare, dhammatitthayare jiṇe |
arihaṃte kittaissaṃ, cauvīsaṃ pi kevalī ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I will praise the twenty-four Arihants — illuminators of the world by the light of knowledge, founders of the four-fold ford of dharma, conquerors of inner enemies, omniscient ones — and all the other Kevalis as well.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **लोगस्स** (logassa) = of the world (loka — the entire cosmos in its four aspects: substance-loka, field-loka, time-loka, and inner-state-loka)
- **उज्जोयगरे** (ujjoyagare) = makers of light, illuminators (uddyota-kara) — here meaning the inner light of perfect knowledge, not the outer light of fire or sun
- **धम्मतित्थयरे** (dhammatitthayare) = founders of the dharma-tirth, the four-fold ford of dharma (the four-fold sangha of monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen) by which souls cross the ocean of existence
- **जिणे** (jiṇe) = victors (Jinas) — those who have conquered all sinful tendencies of attachment and aversion
- **अरिहंते** (arihaṃte) = the Arihants — the worthy of worship, possessed of the eight great pratiharyas and four mula-atishayas
- **कित्तइस्सं** (kittaissaṃ) = I will praise, I will sing the names of (kīrtana)
- **चउवीसं पि** (cauvīsaṃ pi) = the twenty-four also (the twenty-four of this avasarpini)
- **केवली** (kevalī) = the omniscient ones — the **pi** ("also") here implies inclusion of other general Kevalis (omniscient liberated ones who were not Tirthankaras), and the Tirthankaras of other regions and cycles

### 5. COMMENTARY

The opening verse is a declaration of intent. Before reciting any praise, the practitioner first announces *who* is being praised and *why* they deserve the praise. Four qualities are named, and each one corresponds to one of the four "great superlatives" (mula-atishaya) of the Arihants.

**Light-makers of the world** (ujjoyagare): The Tirthankaras' kevalajñāna is an unbroken, all-pervading, eternal light that illumines every substance and every state of every being everywhere — not metaphorically, but literally. Ordinary light reaches only what it falls upon and lasts only as long as its source burns. The light of omniscience reaches all and never sets. This is the *jñāna-atishaya*.

**Founders of the dharma-ford** (dhammatitthayare): Souls have been trapped in the ocean of saṃsāra since beginningless time. The Tirthankara, by establishing the four-fold sangha and giving the teaching that anchors it, builds a tirth — a crossing-place — by which any sincere being can step out of the flood. This is the *vacana-atishaya* — the gift of teaching that can be understood by every kind of being in their own language.

**Jinas — conquerors** (jiṇe): The Tirthankaras have won the only war that matters — the inner war against attachment and aversion. They are total victors over the passions. This is the *apāya-apagama-atishaya* — the complete absence of all defects, both inner and outer.

**Arihants — worthy of worship** (arihaṃte): Devas, demons, humans, kings, chakravartis — all bow before them. This is the *pūjā-atishaya*.

The verse closes with **cauvīsaṃ pi kevalī** — "the twenty-four, and also the Kevalis." The little word *pi* ("also") is significant: the practitioner here praises not only the twenty-four named Tirthankaras but also includes within the bow all general Kevalis (those who attained omniscience without becoming Tirthankaras), the twenty Tirthankaras presently in the Mahavideha realm, and the twenty-four of every other region and cycle. The praise opens outward to embrace every awakened being everywhere.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker begins by saying: "I am about to praise the twenty-four perfect teachers — those who lit up the world with knowledge, who built the bridge of dharma, who conquered themselves completely, and who are worthy of all reverence — and not only them, but every fully awakened being."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you set out to honour what is highest, what does the simple act of *announcing the intention* do to your mind?

## SUTRA 2.2 — The First Eight Names

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

उसभमजियं च वंदे, संभवमभिनंदणं च सुमइं च ।
पउमप्पहं सुपासं, जिणं च चंदप्पहं वंदे ॥२.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.2 ||
usabhamajiyaṃ ca vaṃde, saṃbhavamabhinaṃdaṇaṃ ca sumaiṃ ca |
paumappahaṃ supāsaṃ, jiṇaṃ ca caṃdappahaṃ vaṃde ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I bow to Rishabh and Ajit; I bow to Sambhav, Abhinandan, and Sumati; I bow to Padmaprabhu and Suparshva; and I bow to the Jina Chandraprabhu.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **उसभं** (usabhaṃ) = Rishabhdev (1st) — Mother Marudeva saw a bull (vrishabh) first among her fourteen great dreams, and the prince bore a bull-mark on his thigh; hence "Rishabh"
- **अजियं** (ajiyaṃ) = Ajitnath (2nd) — while in the womb, his mother always defeated her husband at the dice-game; hence "Ajit" (the Unconquered)
- **वंदे** (vaṃde) = I bow / I salute
- **संभवं** (saṃbhavaṃ) = Sambhavnath (3rd) — when he entered the womb, the kingdom experienced abundant production (sambhav) of grain; hence "Sambhav"
- **अभिनंदणं** (abhinaṃdaṇaṃ) = Abhinandan Swami (4th) — when in the womb, Indra repeatedly came to praise (abhinandan) the unborn one; hence "Abhinandan"
- **सुमइं** (sumaiṃ) = Sumatinath (5th) — while he was in the womb, his mother's intelligence (sumati) became so refined that she peacefully resolved a sharp quarrel between two co-wives; hence "Sumati"
- **पउमप्पहं** (paumappahaṃ) = Padmaprabhu (6th) — while he was in the womb, his mother developed a craving (dohad) to lie on a bed of lotuses (padma); hence "Padmaprabhu"
- **सुपासं** (supāsaṃ) = Suparshvanath (7th) — while in the womb, his mother's flanks (parshva) became exceptionally beautiful; hence "Suparshva"
- **जिणं च** (jiṇaṃ ca) = and to the Jina
- **चंदप्पहं** (caṃdappahaṃ) = Chandraprabhu (8th) — while in the womb, his mother developed a craving to drink the moon's nectar (chandra-rasa); hence "Chandraprabhu"

### 5. COMMENTARY

In Jain tradition, the Tirthankara's name is never arbitrary. It emerges from a *sign* — a dream, a wish, a transformation in the mother — that occurs while the great soul rests in her womb. The presence of a Tirthankara-to-be is so powerful that it changes the surroundings, the family, and the mother herself, and the name records what was changed.

These first eight Tirthankaras span an immense stretch of cosmic time. Rishabhdev, the first, lived at the dawn of the present avasarpini, when humanity was just transitioning from the kalpa-vriksha (wish-fulfilling tree) age to the age of agriculture and craft. He is credited in Jain tradition with teaching seventy-two arts and sixty-four crafts to humankind. Ajitnath came after an inconceivable interval. By Chandraprabhu the eighth, the conditions of human life were already becoming more like those we recognise today.

The names themselves are gentle teachings. Each one points to a quality made visible: invincibility (Ajit), abundance (Sambhav), praiseworthiness (Abhinandan), wise discernment (Sumati), purity like a lotus rising from mud (Padmaprabhu), beauty of form (Suparshva), the cooling clarity of moonlight (Chandraprabhu). To recite them slowly is to invite each of those qualities into one's own field of mind.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker bows by name to the first eight perfect teachers, each of whom received their name from a sign that appeared while their mother was carrying them: from Rishabh through Chandraprabhu.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If a name could record the quality you most want to bring into your life, what would that name be?

## SUTRA 2.3 — The Middle Eight Names

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुविहिं च पुप्फदंतं, सीयल-सिज्जंस-वासुपुज्जं च ।
विमल-मणंतं च जिणं, धम्मं संतिं च वंदामि ॥२.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.3 ||
suvihiṃ ca pupphadaṃtaṃ, sīyala-sijjaṃsa-vāsupujjaṃ ca |
vimala-maṇaṃtaṃ ca jiṇaṃ, dhammaṃ saṃtiṃ ca vaṃdāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I bow to Suvidhi (also called Pushpadanta); to Sheetal, Shreyans, and Vasupujya; to Vimal, Anant the Jina, Dharma, and Shanti.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **सुविहिं** (suvihiṃ) = Suvidhinath (9th) — while he was in the womb, his mother became skilled at all the proper rites and duties (suvidhi); hence "Suvidhi"
- **च पुप्फदंतं** (ca pupphadaṃtaṃ) = also called "Pushpadanta" — because his rows of teeth were as white and clean as flowers (pushpa); his second name
- **सीयल** (sīyala) = Sheetalnath (10th) — when his mother's hand touched her ailing husband, his incurable burning fever was instantly cooled (shitala); hence "Sheetal"
- **सिज्जंस** (sijjaṃsa) = Shreyansnath (11th) — there was a divinely-cursed bed in his father's house that no one could sit on; the moment his pregnant mother sat on it, the curse vanished and welfare (shreya) came to all; hence "Shreyans"
- **वासुपुज्जं च** (vāsupujjaṃ ca) = and Vasupujya Swami (12th) — while he was in the womb, his mother was repeatedly worshipped by Vasava (Indra); hence "Vasupujya"
- **विमल** (vimala) = Vimalnath (13th) — while he was in the womb, his mother's intellect became spotlessly clear (vimal); hence "Vimal"
- **मणंतं च जिणं** (maṇaṃtaṃ ca jiṇaṃ) = and Anantnath the Jina (14th) — while he was in the womb, his mother saw a beautiful garland studded with countless (anant) jewels; hence "Anant"
- **धम्मं** (dhammaṃ) = Dharmanath (15th) — while he was in the womb, his mother's commitment to charity and other dharmic duties became firmly established; hence "Dharma"
- **संतिं च वंदामि** (saṃtiṃ ca vaṃdāmi) = and to Shantinath I bow (16th) — when he entered the womb, an epidemic in the city was instantly pacified (shanti); hence "Shanti"

### 5. COMMENTARY

These eight Tirthankaras occupy the middle of the cosmic line. Each of their names is a *gift recorded* — a debt the world acknowledged owing to them at the moment of their entry into a body. Sheetal cooled fever; Shreyans dissolved a curse; Shanti ended a plague. Even before their birth, their presence resolved suffering.

A particularly beautiful pattern emerges: the Tirthankara's effect on the world begins not when they preach, not when they renounce, not even when they are born — but at the moment of conception. The womb itself becomes a transformer. This expresses a profound Jain teaching about karma and pure consciousness: a soul carrying the *Tirthankara-nāmakarma* — the karma that destines it to be a world-teacher — radiates such auspiciousness that its mere presence reshapes circumstances around it.

Suvidhinath uniquely has two names — **Suvidhi** (skilled in rites) and **Pushpadanta** (flower-toothed). The Logassa retains both, an act of textual generosity: where tradition has preserved more than one name for the same teacher, the praise honours all of them.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker bows to the middle eight Tirthankaras — from Suvidhi (also called Pushpadanta) through Shanti — each named for a real change that came over the world or the family the moment the great soul entered the womb.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What in your presence — even before you say a word — does your environment feel?

## SUTRA 2.4 — The Last Eight Names

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कुंथुं अरं च मल्लिं, वंदे मुणिसुव्वयं नमिजिणं च ।
वंदामि रिट्ठनेमिं, पासं तह वद्धमाणं च ॥२.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.4 ||
kuṃthuṃ araṃ ca malliṃ, vaṃde muṇisuvvayaṃ namijiṇaṃ ca |
vaṃdāmi riṭṭhanemiṃ, pāsaṃ taha vaddhamāṇaṃ ca ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I bow to Kunthu, Ar, and Malli; I bow to Munisuvrat and to Nami the Jina; I bow to Arishtanemi, to Parshva, and to Vardhaman.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कुंथुं** (kuṃthuṃ) = Kunthunath (17th) — while he was in the womb, his mother dreamed of a circle of munis (sages) protecting tiny living beings called *kunthu*; hence "Kunthu"
- **अरं** (araṃ) = Aranath (18th) — while he was in the womb, his mother saw a jewelled wheel-spoke (ara) in her dream; hence "Ar"
- **च मल्लिं** (ca malliṃ) = and Mallinath (19th) — while she was in the womb (the only female-bodied Tirthankara of this cycle, in the Shvetambara tradition), her mother developed a craving to sleep on a fragrant bed of malli flowers, which the devas fulfilled; hence "Malli"
- **वंदे** (vaṃde) = I bow
- **मुणिसुव्वयं** (muṇisuvvayaṃ) = Munisuvrat Swami (20th) — while he was in the womb, his mother became *suvrata* — well-vowed, took up the vows of a sage; hence "Muni-suvrata"
- **नमिजिणं च** (namijiṇaṃ ca) = and Nami the Jina (21st) — while he was in the womb, even hostile, unbending kings bowed (nami) and submitted; hence "Nami"
- **वंदामि** (vaṃdāmi) = I bow
- **रिट्ठनेमिं** (riṭṭhanemiṃ) = Arishtanemi / Neminath (22nd) — while he was in the womb, his mother saw in her dream the inner rim (nemi) of a wheel made of arishta jewels; hence "Arishta-nemi" (the cousin of Krishna)
- **पासं** (pāsaṃ) = Parshvanath (23rd) — while in the womb, his mother saw a serpent (parshva) approaching her bedside; hence "Parshva"
- **तह वद्धमाणं च** (taha vaddhamāṇaṃ ca) = and Vardhaman (24th) — when he entered the womb, the wealth and grain (dhana-dhanya) of the Jnata-clan family began to *vardhamana* — to increase greatly; hence "Vardhaman" (this is the personal name of Lord Mahavir)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The final eight Tirthankaras bring the praise into the recent past. Among them is Mallinath, who in the Shvetambara tradition is recorded as having taken birth in a female body — a detail of profound theological significance, since it teaches that the path to becoming a Tirthankara is open regardless of bodily form. Among them too are Neminath, the cousin of Krishna; Parshvanath, who lived 250 years before Mahavir and whose order Mahavir's parents belonged to; and finally **Vardhaman**, the personal name of Lord Mahavir himself — the twenty-fourth and last Tirthankara of this cosmic half-cycle, whose teaching this very Avashyak Sutra preserves.

The verse arc that began with Rishabh — born when humans first learned to till and weave — closes here with Vardhaman, born around 599 BCE, whose words are still being spoken aloud each day in Jain homes twenty-five centuries later. By naming all twenty-four in unbroken succession, the practitioner connects to a lineage that spans the entire moral history of the present cosmic cycle.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker bows to the last eight perfect teachers — from Kunthu through Vardhaman, who is Lord Mahavir himself — completing the line of all twenty-four.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does it mean for the same words you are reciting today to have been recited, in unbroken succession, for twenty-five centuries?

## SUTRA 2.5 — Praise Complete; Invocation of Grace

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं मए अभिथुआ, विहुयरयमला पहीणजरमरणा ।
चउवीसं पि जिणवरा, तित्थयरा मे पसीयंतु ॥२.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.5 ||
evaṃ mae abhithuā, vihuyarayamalā pahīṇajaramaraṇā |
cauvīsaṃ pi jiṇavarā, titthayarā me pasīyaṃtu ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Thus praised by me — these who have shaken off the dust of karma and the stain of bondage, who have passed beyond old age and death — may all twenty-four supreme Jinas, the founders of the ford of dharma, be gracious unto me!

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **एवं मए अभिथुआ** (evaṃ mae abhithuā) = thus praised by me (through the recitation of names just completed)
- **विहुयरयमला** (vihuyarayamalā) = those who have shaken off (vidhuta) the dust (raj — karmas presently being bound) and the stain (mala — karmas previously bound) — they are free of both new and accumulated karma. The commentary distinguishes: *raj* = karmas in the act of binding; *mala* = karmas already deeply bound (nikachit)
- **पहीणजरमरणा** (pahīṇajaramaraṇā) = those who have utterly destroyed (prahīna) old age (jara) and death (marana) — and by extension, birth (janma) as well. Since the root cause of birth-and-death (attachment-aversion) is fully gone, the cycle cannot resume
- **चउवीसं पि** (cauvīsaṃ pi) = the twenty-four also (and other Jinas as well)
- **जिणवरा** (jiṇavarā) = the supreme Jinas (the foremost of all victors)
- **तित्थयरा** (titthayarā) = the Tirthankaras (founders of the dharma-ford)
- **मे** (me) = upon me
- **पसीयंतु** (pasīyaṃtu) = may they be gracious / may they look upon me with favour

### 5. COMMENTARY

The fifth verse is the moment of completion. The seeker has named all twenty-four; now the seeker names what those twenty-four are. Three qualities are crystallised:

**Free of karmic dust and stain** (vihuyarayamalā): The Tirthankaras have not merely reduced their karma — they have shaken it off entirely. The verse uses two words for karma to make a precise philosophical distinction: *raj* (the new karma being absorbed at every moment by ordinary souls) and *mala* (the densely-bound karma accumulated over countless past lives). The Tirthankaras have closed the door to the first and dissolved the deposit of the second. Both *vihuta* — shaken off, like dust from a cloth.

**Beyond birth, old age, and death** (pahīṇajaramaraṇā): These three are the master-griefs of saṃsāra. They are not external fates but the natural consequence of having a body. The Tirthankaras, having severed the karmic root that compels rebirth, will never again wear a body — and so the trio of birth, ageing, dying can never again touch them.

**Supreme Jinas, founders of the ford** (jiṇavarā titthayarā): Among all Jinas (and there are infinite Jinas across infinite time), the Tirthankaras are the supreme ones — for not only have they crossed, they have built the bridge by which others can cross.

The verse closes with the request: **me pasīyaṃtu** — "may they be gracious to me." But the Jain understanding of grace is unique. The Tirthankaras, being totally dispassionate, do not actively favour or disfavour anyone; they cannot, for that would compromise their freedom from attachment. Their "grace" is not given by them but received by the practitioner. By orienting the heart toward them in love and reverence, the practitioner *opens themselves* to receive what the Tirthankara's example always silently transmits: the inspiration to walk the same path. The grace flows because the practitioner has prepared the channel.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Having now named all twenty-four perfect teachers — those who have shaken off all karma and gone beyond birth, old age, and death — the seeker asks: "May you all be gracious to me."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If grace is not given but received, what in you would have to soften before you could receive it?

## SUTRA 2.6 — The Devotee's Request

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कित्तिय-वंदिय-महिया, जे ए लोगस्स उत्तमा सिद्धा ।
आरुग्ग-बोहिलाभं, समाहिवरमुत्तमं दिंतु ॥२.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.6 ||
kittiya-vaṃdiya-mahiyā, je e logassa uttamā siddhā |
ārugga-bohilābhaṃ, samāhivaramuttamaṃ diṃtu ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Praised, saluted, and worshipped — those who are the foremost in the world, the perfected Siddhas — may they grant me the gain of (true) health, the gain of right vision, and the highest, most excellent samadhi.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **कित्तिय** (kittiya) = praised — recited by name (kīrtana, the first stage of bhakti)
- **वंदिय** (vaṃdiya) = saluted — bowed to (vandana, the second stage; bowing the five limbs in reverence)
- **महिया** (mahiyā) = worshipped — honoured (mahima/pūjana, the third stage; honour by mind, speech, and body — true bhāva-pūjā)
- **जे ए** (je e) = those who
- **लोगस्स उत्तमा** (logassa uttamā) = the supreme of the world / the highest in the world (because they have destroyed the root of bondage — attachment and aversion)
- **सिद्धा** (siddhā) = the Siddhas / the accomplished ones (those whose every undertaking has been completed)
- **आरुग्ग** (ārugga) = (true) health — *not* the body's wellness but the soul's wholeness; the unbreakability of the soul's natural state, freedom from the great disease of birth-and-death
- **बोहिलाभं** (bohilābhaṃ) = the gain of bodhi — the gain of right understanding of reality (samyak-darshan, samyak-jñāna), which is the seed of the three jewels
- **समाहिवरमुत्तमं** (samāhivaramuttamaṃ) = the highest, most excellent samadhi — the supreme stillness; the unshakeable concentration of the purified mind. *Vara* = excellent; *uttama* = highest. The repetition emphasises that what is sought is not ordinary calm but the loftiest grade of samadhi
- **दिंतु** (diṃtu) = may they grant / may they bestow

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse contains the only "request" in the entire Logassa, and it is worth noticing what is *not* asked for. The seeker does not ask for wealth, success, long life, family welfare, or any worldly benefit. The seeker asks for three things only — and even these three are described in their highest spiritual sense:

**Ārugga** (health): Not the body's freedom from illness, which is fleeting and karma-bound. The text is explicit: "the body's health or sickness is impermanent and of no special importance to the practitioner's life." What is requested is the soul's *true* health — the unbreaking wholeness of pure consciousness, freed at last from the master-disease of being born and dying again.

**Bohilābh** (gain of bodhi): The right perception of reality — the moment when the seeker first sees clearly that the soul is not the body, that karma is the bondage, and that the path of right perception, right knowledge, and right conduct leads to release. Without bodhi, no spiritual progress is possible. With it, the seed of liberation is planted.

**Samāhi-vara-uttamaṃ** (the supreme samadhi): The deepest stillness, the highest grade of samadhi. There is bodily samadhi (the body's wellness from following the three jewels), and there is *bhāva*-samadhi — the inner stillness of the soul. The verse uses both *vara* and *uttama* to indicate that what is sought is the highest tier of bhāva-samadhi — the samadhi by which liberation itself becomes possible.

The verse begins by recalling the three stages of devotion: *kīrtana* (praising aloud), *vandana* (bowing in reverence), and *mahima/pūjana* (worship through mind, speech, and body). These are sequential — praise leads to reverence, reverence leads to worship, worship leads to receiving. The seeker who has now completed all three is positioned to receive what is being asked for.

The verse implicitly teaches: ask greatly, or do not ask. Of beings who have transcended all desire, ask only for that which transcendence itself can provide. To ask the Tirthankaras for wealth would be to misunderstand entirely whom one is addressing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker, having praised and bowed and honoured these supreme Siddhas, asks for only three things: the soul's true wholeness, the gain of right understanding, and the deepest possible inner stillness.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you stand before what is highest, what would you ask for — if you knew that what you ask for is exactly what you will train yourself to want?

## SUTRA 2.7 — The Three Similes; The Final Request

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

चंदेसु निम्मलयरा, आइच्चेसु अहियं पयासयरा ।
सागरवरगंभीरा, सिद्धा सिद्धिं मम दिसंतु ॥२.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 2.7 ||
caṃdesu ṇimmalayarā, āiccesu ahiyaṃ payāsayarā |
sāgaravaragaṃbhīrā, siddhā siddhiṃ mama disaṃtu ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Purer than the moons; brighter even than the suns; deep as the supreme self-bound ocean — may these Siddhas grant me siddhi (liberation).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चंदेसु निम्मलयरा** (caṃdesu ṇimmalayarā) = purer than the moons (chandra) — though the moon is renowned for purity, it has the dark spot (kalank); the perfected ones, having shaken off all karmic stain, are purer still and their purity is *traikalik* — beyond all three times (past, present, future)
- **आइच्चेसु अहियं पयासयरा** (āiccesu ahiyaṃ payāsayarā) = brighter than the suns (āditya) — the sun rises and sets and illumines only what is within its reach for a limited time; the Siddhas' kevalajñāna is unbroken, never sets, and illumines all of loka and aloka simultaneously
- **सागरवरगंभीरा** (sāgaravaragaṃbhīrā) = deep as the supreme svayambhuramana ocean — the outermost, self-existing ocean of cosmography, deepest of all oceans; even amid favourable and adverse circumstances, the Tirthankaras' inner state never ripples, never rises or falls; their equanimity is permanent
- **सिद्धा** (siddhā) = the Siddhas (the perfected, liberated ones)
- **सिद्धिं** (siddhiṃ) = siddhi — the liberated state, *moksha* itself
- **मम** (mama) = to me / for me
- **दिसंतु** (disaṃtu) = may they show / may they reveal / may they grant (literally: may they point out the way)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The closing verse turns from request to celebration — and uses three of the most beautiful similes in all Jain literature to do so. The world contains pure things: the moon, the sun, the great oceans. The Tirthankaras transcend even these.

**Purer than the moon**: The moon is the world's symbol of purity, yet even the moon has a stain (the dark spot). The Siddhas have no stain at all, and their purity is not seasonal or phase-dependent — it is permanent across all three times.

**Brighter than the sun**: The sun is the world's source of light. Yet the sun rises and sets; it illumines what its rays reach and only as long as it shines. The Siddhas' light — kevalajñāna — never sets, illumines everything (loka and aloka) at once, and never weakens.

**Deeper than the deepest ocean**: The svayambhuramana is the outermost ocean in Jain cosmography — vast and unfathomable. Yet even it has currents, storms, and tides. The Tirthankaras' inner depth is *gambhīra* in a way no ocean is: no favourable or adverse circumstance ever produces a ripple in their equanimity. Their stillness is shastashvat — eternal.

Three similes, each one giving the worldly equivalent and then transcending it. This is the practice of contemplation by graded comparison — the seeker first calls to mind the highest worldly purity, brightness, and depth they can imagine, then is invited to recognise that the Siddhas exceed even those. The mind is thus stretched, by stages, toward the inconceivable.

The final request — **siddhā siddhiṃ mama disaṃtu** — uses a beautiful play of words: "May the Siddhas show me siddhi." The verb *disaṃtu* literally means "may they point out the direction." The Tirthankaras, being totally dispassionate, do not give liberation to anyone — but they *show the way*. Their teaching, their example, their qualities mapped in the seeker's own mind: these are how the way is shown. The seeker walks the way themselves; the Tirthankaras have shown them where it lies.

The verse closes the entire Logassa not with petition but with a clear-eyed recognition: the Siddhas point; the seeker walks; the goal is the same goal the Siddhas themselves attained.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The seeker closes by recalling that these perfected ones are purer than the moon, brighter than the sun, and deeper than the deepest ocean — and asks them, the Siddhas, to point the way to the same liberation they themselves have reached.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you imagine the highest purity, the brightest light, and the deepest stillness you can conceive — and then realise that what you seek is even beyond that — what does that recognition do to your sense of direction?

## END OF AVASHYAK 2

॥ આવશ્યક-૨ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Avashyak-2 sampurna — "Avashyak 2 is complete.")*

The Logassa Sutra closes here. The Avashyak Niryukti summarises the fruit of this entire praise in a single verse: *bhattīi jiṇavarāṇaṃ, paramāe khīṇapijjadosāṇaṃ | ārugga bohilābhaṃ samāhi maraṇaṃ pāvaṃti ||* — "By supreme devotion to the Jinas, whose attachment and aversion have been entirely destroyed, beings attain (true) health, the gain of right vision, and a death in samadhi."

Having praised the perfected ones in the second essential, the practitioner now turns toward the living guides who keep the path visible: in the third essential, **Vandana**, the practitioner offers reverence to the Guru.
