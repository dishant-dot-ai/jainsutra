# Avashyak Sutra — Avashyak 3
# Vandana (વંદના) — Reverence to the Guru

**Avashyak 3 of the Avashyak Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Avashyak Sutra, printed pages 32–41 (PDF pages 96–105)*
*Total Sutras in this Avashyak: 1 (the Ichchhami Khamasamano — Guru-Vandana Sutra)*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The third essential is **Vandana** — formal reverence to the Guru. After the practitioner has steadied the mind in equanimity (Samayika) and praised the perfected ones who have already reached the goal (Chaturvinshatistava), the current of devotion naturally flows toward the *living* guide who keeps the path visible. Without a Guru — a master who has walked the path and can correct the seeker's missteps — the texts are silent letters and the seeker walks blind.

There are three grades of vandana. **Jaghanya** (the simplest) — when one happens to encounter the Guru on the road, joining the palms and saying "matthen vandāmi" while bowing the head. **Madhyam** (the middle grade) — a fuller bow with three āvartana (circular gestures of the joined hands) and the recitation of the *Tikkhutto* formula, performed when meeting the Guru during the day. **Utkrishta** (the highest) — the full ritual with twelve āvartanas performed during pratikraman, seated on a low stool, before the Guru. The sutra translated here is the formula of this highest grade.

Acharya Umaswati gives the chain of cause and effect that begins with vandana: *the fruit of vinaya (humility) is the wish to serve; the fruit of serving the Guru is the gain of scriptural knowledge; the fruit of knowledge is detachment; the fruit of detachment is the stopping of inflow; the fruit of stopping inflow is the gathering of austerity; the fruit of austerity is the shedding of karma; the shedding of karma stops activity; stopping activity ends the cycle of birth-and-death; ending the cycle is liberation.* The whole spiritual ladder rests on its first rung: humility expressed through reverence to the teacher.

The sutra has a precise four-part inner structure: (1) **Ichchhanivedan** — the disciple's expression of the wish to bow; (2) **Agya yachana** — request for permission to enter the Guru's three-and-a-half-cubit personal space (the avagrah); (3) **Sukhshata pruchchha** — enquiry about the Guru's bodily, sensory, and disciplinary well-being; (4) **Kshama yachana** — request for forgiveness for any of the thirty-three forms of disrespect (āshātanā) that may have occurred. Together these form a complete teaching on the right relationship between disciple and teacher.

## SUTRA 3.1 — The Guru-Vandana Sutra (Ichchhami Khamasamano)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्छामि खमासमणो ! वंदिउं जावणिज्जाए निसीहियाए, अणुजाणह मे मि उग्गहं निसीहि, अहोकायं कायसंफासं खमणिज्जो भे ! किलामो अप्पकिलंताणं बहुसुभेण भे ! दिवसो वइक्कंतो जत्ता भे जवणिज्जं च भे ? खामेमि खमासमणो देवसियं वइक्कमं आवस्सियाए पडिक्कमामि खमासमणाणं देवसियाए आसायणाए तित्तीसण्णयराए जं किंचि मिच्छाए मणदुक्कडाए वयदुक्कडाए कायदुक्कडाए कोहाए माणाए मायाए लोहाए सव्व कालियाए सव्वमिच्छोवयाराए सव्व धम्माइक्कमणाए आसायणाए जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स खमासमणो ! पडिक्कमामि निंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥३.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 3.1 ||
icchāmi khamāsamaṇo ! vaṃdiuṃ jāvaṇijjāe ṇisīhiyāe, aṇujāṇaha me mi uggahaṃ ṇisīhi, ahokāyaṃ kāyasaṃphāsaṃ khamaṇijjo bhe ! kilāmo appakilaṃtāṇaṃ bahusubheṇa bhe ! divaso vaikkaṃto jattā bhe javaṇijjaṃ ca bhe ? khāmemi khamāsamaṇo devasiyaṃ vaikkamaṃ āvassiyāe paḍikkamāmi khamāsamaṇāṇaṃ devasiyāe āsāyaṇāe tittīsaṇṇayarāe jaṃ kiṃci micchāe maṇadukkaḍāe vayadukkaḍāe kāyadukkaḍāe kohāe māṇāe māyāe lohāe savva kāliyāe savvamicchovayārāe savva dhammāikkamaṇāe āsāyaṇāe jo me devasio aiyāro kao tassa khamāsamaṇo ! paḍikkamāmi niṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I wish, O forgiving ascetic (Khamasamano), to bow — having stopped my sinful activities, with whatever bodily strength I possess. Grant me permission to enter your limited personal space; with the word "nisīhi" (renouncing all other activity) I bow, touching your feet (the lower part of your body) with my body. Forgive me, O Lord! — for any pain my touch may have caused you, who are yourself untired.

Has your day passed in great wholesomeness? Is your spiritual journey (of restraint) proceeding without obstacle, and are your senses and mind in good order?

I beg forgiveness, O forgiving ascetic, for my transgressions of this day. With "āvassiyāe" (returning from the avagrah), I perform pratikraman of any of the thirty-three forms of disrespect (āshātanā) of this day toward the forgiving ascetics — whatever was done from false belief, by misdeed of mind, by misdeed of speech, by misdeed of body, from anger, from pride, from deceit, from greed; whatever transgression of all time, whatever wrong conduct toward all, whatever violation of all dharmas — for any disrespect, for whatever transgression of this day I have committed, O forgiving ascetic, I turn back from it; I censure it; I confess it before you; I let go of (that) sinful self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**Part 1 — Ichchhanivedan (Expression of Wish):**
- **इच्छामि** (icchāmi) = I wish — the practice of dharma must be voluntary (svecchā); a forced practice has no fruit. Hence every formula in the Avashyak begins with *ichchhāmi*
- **खमासमणो** (khamāsamaṇo) = O Khamasamano (kṣamā-śramaṇa) — "O forgiving ascetic." The compound *kṣamā-śramaṇa* combines *kṣamā* (forgiveness, the Guru's chief quality at this moment) with *śramaṇa* (one who labours in austerity). Three Sanskrit equivalents are recognised: *śramaṇa* (one who exerts), *śamana* (one who pacifies passions), *samana* (one who is equal toward friend and foe)
- **वंदिउं** (vaṃdiuṃ) = to bow / to do vandana
- **जावणिज्जाए** (jāvaṇijjāe) = with bodily strength as it permits — the disciple offers what they can; the elderly or ill are not held to the same physical posture as the strong
- **निसीहियाए** (ṇisīhiyāe) = having ceased / having renounced — having stopped all sinful activity to enter this sacred act

**Part 2 — Agya Yachana (Request for Permission):**
- **अणुजाणह** (aṇujāṇaha) = grant permission
- **मे** (me) = to me
- **मि उग्गहं** (mi uggahaṃ) = into the limited space (mita-avagraha) — the Guru's personal space, defined as a circle of three-and-a-half cubits (about 5 feet) in all four directions; entering requires explicit permission
- **निसीहि** (ṇisīhi) = "ceasing" — uttered as the disciple steps in, signifying renunciation of all other activity at the moment of entry
- **अहोकायं** (ahokāyaṃ) = the lower body / the feet (adhaḥ-kāya)
- **कायसंफासं** (kāyasaṃphāsaṃ) = touching with the body — the disciple lays their head on the Guru's feet
- **खमणिज्जो भे** (khamaṇijjo bhe) = please forgive — bhe = O Lord
- **किलामो** (kilāmo) = pain, fatigue (klāma)
- **अप्पकिलंताणं** (appakilaṃtāṇaṃ) = of you, who are unfatigued — recognising that the Guru, having transcended bodily labour, would not feel fatigue, but the disciple still asks forgiveness in case the touch caused even the slightest discomfort

**Part 3 — Sukhshata Pruchchha (Enquiry about Welfare):**
- **बहुसुभेण भे** (bahusubheṇa bhe) = with great wholesomeness, O Lord — the disciple asks if the Guru's day passed peacefully
- **दिवसो वइक्कंतो** (divaso vaikkaṃto) = has the day passed? (this phrase varies by occasion: *rāi vaikkaṃto* in morning pratikraman; *pakkhī vaikkaṃto* in fortnightly; *cāumāsī vaikkaṃto* in four-monthly; *saṃvaccharo vaikkaṃto* in annual)
- **जत्ता भे** (jattā bhe) = is your spiritual journey, O Lord — *jātrā* here means the journey of restraint (saṃyam-yātrā): is the Guru's path of austerity, scripture-study, meditation proceeding without obstacle?
- **जवणिज्जं च भे** (javaṇijjaṃ ca bhe) = and is your *yāpanīya* (sustenance) well, O Lord — *yāpanīya* means "what sustains the journey"; here, are the senses and mind in good order, free from disturbance by their objects?

**Part 4 — Kshama Yachana (Request for Forgiveness):**
- **खामेमि** (khāmemi) = I beg forgiveness
- **देवसियं वइक्कमं** (devasiyaṃ vaikkamaṃ) = the transgressions of this day (*deva-sika* = pertaining to the day; varies by occasion as above)
- **आवस्सियाए पडिक्कमामि** (āvassiyāe paḍikkamāmi) = with "āvassiyā" (the announcement of stepping back out of the avagrah), I perform pratikraman
- **खमासमणाणं देवसियाए आसायणाए** (khamāsamaṇāṇaṃ devasiyāe āsāyaṇāe) = of disrespect to the forgiving ascetics during the day. **āshātanā** = "that by which the soul's qualities are destroyed" (*ā samantāt śātyante jñāna-ādayo guṇāḥ yathā sā āśātanā*); also "that which obstructs the path of liberation"
- **तित्तीसण्णयराए** (tittīsaṇṇayarāe) = of any of the thirty-three (kinds of disrespect enumerated in the Dasha-shrutaskandha Sutra)
- **जं किंचि मिच्छाए** (jaṃ kiṃci micchāe) = whatever was done from false belief
- **मणदुक्कडाए वयदुक्कडाए कायदुक्कडाए** (maṇa-dukkaḍāe vaya-dukkaḍāe kāya-dukkaḍāe) = by misdeed of mind, of speech, of body
- **कोहाए माणाए मायाए लोहाए** (kohāe māṇāe māyāe lohāe) = from anger, pride, deceit, greed (the four kashayas)
- **सव्व कालियाए** (savva kāliyāe) = pertaining to all time — covering the past, present, and future of disrespect
- **सव्वमिच्छोवयाराए** (savva-micchovayārāe) = from any false dealings — any conduct toward the Guru that springs from one's own self-will or wrong understanding rather than from the Guru's teaching
- **सव्व धम्माइक्कमणाए** (savva-dhammāikkamaṇāe) = from violation of all dharma — any breach of the ten dharmas of an ascetic (kṣama, mardava, ārjava, etc.)
- **जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ** (jo me devasio aiyāro kao) = whatever transgression of this day I have committed
- **तस्स खमासमणो ! पडिक्कमामि निंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि** (tassa khamāsamaṇo ! paḍikkamāmi niṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi) = of that, O forgiving ascetic, I turn back, I censure, I confess, I let go of (that) sinful self — the same closing formula as the Karemi Bhante vow

### 5. COMMENTARY

This single sutra is the verbal half of the highest-grade vandana ritual (utkrishta vandana). The other half is physical — twenty-five precise actions called the *vandana panchavishika* (the twenty-five components of vandana), enumerated by the Samavayanga Sutra: two bows (avanata), one yathāj̄āta posture, twelve āvartana (circular hand-gestures), four shira-namana (head-bowings), three guptis (restraints), two pravesha (entries), and one nishkraman (exit). The sutra and the gestures together make a single ritual; neither one is complete without the other.

The four-part inner structure of the sutra teaches a complete philosophy of the disciple-Guru relationship.

**1. Ichchhanivedan — "I wish."** Every spiritual practice begins with the disciple's own wish, freely offered. A vandana performed because someone said "you must" is empty. The very first word — *ichchhāmi*, "I wish" — establishes that the act about to be performed is not coerced. This is why every Avashyak formula begins with the same word. The sutras of dharma know that the only acts that purify are those born of the seeker's own free choice.

**2. Agya Yachana — "Grant me permission to enter."** The Guru, like every monk and nun, is held to occupy a personal space of three-and-a-half cubits (about five feet) in every direction — the *avagraha*. Within this circle the Guru sits, studies, meditates, sleeps. No one — disciple or stranger — may enter without explicit permission. Why? Because the Guru's sphere is sacred to the work of liberation, and unannounced intrusion is itself a disrespect. The disciple stands at the boundary, asks *aṇujāṇaha me mi uggahaṃ*, waits for the Guru to grant *aṇujāṇāmi*, and only then steps in with the word *nisīhi* — "I cease all other activity at this moment." This single ritual teaches an entire ethic of presence: do not assume access, ask for it; when granted, enter wholly.

**3. Sukhshata Pruchchha — "How is your day, your journey, your sustenance?"** Three questions, each more interior than the last. *Has the day passed wholesomely?* — outward enquiry about the Guru's day. *Is the journey of restraint proceeding without obstacle?* — interior enquiry about the Guru's spiritual practice (saṃyam-yātrā). *Are the senses and mind well?* — innermost enquiry about whether the Guru is being disturbed by the pull of sense-objects or the wandering of mind. The disciple is being trained, by these three questions, to attend to the Guru's whole condition: body, practice, and inner state. Not as flattery, not as small-talk, but as the real care of one who depends on the Guru's continued well-being for their own spiritual life.

**4. Kshama Yachana — "Forgive my transgressions."** The fourth and longest movement of the sutra is the request for forgiveness. The Dasha-shrutaskandha Sutra enumerates thirty-three specific āshātanās (forms of disrespect to the Guru); the commentators cluster them into four broad categories: *dravya-āshātanā* (refusing to share food, drink, robes, or vessels with the elders), *kshetra-āshātanā* (taking the choicer seat or place and leaving the inferior for the elder), *kāla-āshātanā* (failing to answer when the Guru calls at night, or remaining silent when one is awake and able to respond), *bhāva-āshātanā* (lacking inward respect; speaking to the Guru with familiar pronouns like "you" instead of honorifics).

The sutra then enumerates by what *means* these disrespects can have arisen: by misdeed of mind, speech, or body; from anger, pride, deceit, or greed; from any false dealing or violation of dharma. The phrase **savva-kāliyāe** is read in two ways by the commentators — either *all kinds of time-bound transgressions* (covering the day's daily / fortnightly / four-monthly / yearly āshātanās), or *transgressions of all time* (covering past, present, and future — confessing the past, withdrawing from the present, vowing against the future). The wider reading is preferred: the disciple who confesses has just stepped, in this ritual moment, outside the timeline of their own karmic accumulation.

The sutra closes with the same formula as the Karemi Bhante vow: *paḍikkamāmi, niṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi* — I turn back, I censure inwardly, I confess outwardly, I let go of the part of me that did the deed. The ritual is not complete with mere acknowledgement; the disciple must release identification with the part of the self that committed the disrespect.

What this single sutra teaches, taken whole, is that reverence is a precise discipline — not a feeling, not a posture, but a sequence of actions and inner movements that together remake the relationship between disciple and Guru. The disciple's pride is dissolved through asking permission. The disciple's self-absorption is broken through enquiry about the Guru's condition. The disciple's accumulated faults are released through confession. And the disciple's identification with the small, faulty self is set aside through *vosirāmi*. In the space cleared by this fourfold movement, the Guru's teaching can land without obstruction. This is why vandana is the third essential, and why no further practice — neither pratikraman, nor kāyotsarga, nor pratyākhyāna — is possible until this one is firm.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The disciple says to the teacher: "I want to bow. May I come into your space? I have come; please forgive any discomfort I cause. Has your day been peaceful? Is your spiritual practice going well? Are your senses calm? And please forgive any disrespect I may have shown you today — by mind, speech, or body, from anger, pride, deceit, or greed — I am sorry, I confess it, and I let go of the part of me that did it."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you go to learn from someone wiser than yourself, do you ask permission to take their time — or do you assume access?

## END OF AVASHYAK 3

॥ આવશ્યક-૩ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Avashyak-3 sampurna — "Avashyak 3 is complete.")*

The third essential closes here. By means of the six "stations" (sthānak) — three offered by the disciple (the wish to bow, the request for entry, the enquiry about welfare) and three answered by the Guru (permission, response, blessing) — the relationship between disciple and teacher is renewed each day.

Now, with the mind steadied (Samayika), the ideal honoured (Chaturvinshatistava), and the relationship with the living guide repaired (Vandana), the practitioner is ready for the inward work of the fourth essential — **Pratikramana**, the formal turning-back from all transgressions of the day.
