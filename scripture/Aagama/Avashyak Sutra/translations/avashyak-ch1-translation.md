## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The Avashyak Sutra ("The Essentials") sets out six obligatory practices that every Jain practitioner — monk, nun, layman, laywoman — must perform daily. The Nandi Sutra defines them in order: (1) Samayika — equanimity; (2) Chaturvinshatistava — praise of the twenty-four Tirthankaras; (3) Vandana — reverence to the spiritual teacher; (4) Pratikramana — confession and turning back from transgressions; (5) Kayotsarga — release of bodily attachment through meditation; (6) Pratyakhyana — the formal renunciation of food, comfort, and other indulgences.

The first essential — **Samayika** — is the foundation. Its very name carries the meaning of *sama* (equanimity, balance, sameness toward all beings) and *aya* (gain, attainment). To practise Samayika is to step out, even briefly, from the world of like and dislike, profit and loss, friend and enemy — and to rest in that balanced state where the soul recognises every other living being as identical in essence to itself. The Anuyogadwar Sutra calls this practice *savajja-jog-virai* — withdrawal from sinful activity. It is the first essential because no other practice can succeed until the mind has been first stilled into equanimity.

This chapter contains two foundational texts. The first is the **Namaskar Mahamantra** — the great salutation to the five supreme beings (Arihant, Siddha, Acharya, Upadhyaya, and all Sadhus), which serves as the auspicious opening (mangalachran) of the entire Avashyak Sutra and indeed of all Jain practice. The second is the **Karemi Bhante** — the formal pratigya-sutra (vow-formula) of Samayika, in which the practitioner takes the vow before the spiritual teacher to renounce all sinful activity by the three karanas (doing, causing to be done, approving) and the three yogas (mind, speech, body) for the duration of the practice.

Together these two sutras form the doorway into the entire path: a salutation that establishes the ideal, and a vow that begins the journey.

## SUTRA 1.1 — Namaskar Mahamantra

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णमो अरिहंताणं ।
णमो सिद्धाणं ।
णमो आयरियाणं ।
णमो उवज्झायाणं ।
णमो लोए सव्वसाहूणं ॥१.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.1 ||
ṇamo arihaṃtāṇaṃ |
ṇamo siddhāṇaṃ |
ṇamo āyariyāṇaṃ |
ṇamo uvajjhāyāṇaṃ |
ṇamo loe savvasāhūṇaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Salutations to the Arihants (the worthy ones who have conquered inner enemies). Salutations to the Siddhas (the liberated, fully perfected souls). Salutations to the Acharyas (the heads of the spiritual order). Salutations to the Upadhyayas (the teachers of scripture). Salutations to all the Sadhus (monks and nuns) in the world.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **णमो** (ṇamo) = salutations be / I bow
- **अरिहंताणं** (arihaṃtāṇaṃ) = to the Arihants — those who have destroyed inner enemies (ari = enemy, hanta = destroyer); they have annihilated the four destructive karmas (jñānāvaraṇīya, darśanāvaraṇīya, mohanīya, antarāya), attained omniscience (kevalajñāna), perfect perception (kevaladarśana), perfect dispassion (vītarāgatā), and infinite spiritual power, while still in embodied form
- **सिद्धाणं** (siddhāṇaṃ) = to the Siddhas — those who have destroyed all eight karmas, become bodiless, and reside eternally at the summit of the universe (siddhakshetra) in pure consciousness
- **आयरियाणं** (āyariyāṇaṃ) = to the Acharyas — heads of the four-fold sangha; those who themselves observe and cause others to observe the five-fold conduct (knowledge, perception, character, austerity, vigour)
- **उवज्झायाणं** (uvajjhāyāṇaṃ) = to the Upadhyayas — teachers near whom disciples study scripture; masters of the twelve Angas and twelve Upangas
- **लोए** (loe) = in the world (loka)
- **सव्व** (savva) = all
- **साहूणं** (sāhūṇaṃ) = to the Sadhus — those who help themselves and others to attain liberation; those who treat all souls as their own; observers of the five great vows

### 5. COMMENTARY

This mantra is the auspicious opening of the entire Avashyak Sutra and, by long tradition, of every undertaking in Jain practice. It is not addressed to a god, nor to any single founder, nor to a sect or school. It bows instead to *qualities* — to the five categories of awakened beings, named not by their personal identities but by what they have achieved. Anyone who attains those qualities becomes worthy of this salutation; the mantra recognises no caste, lineage, gender, or denomination. This is what makes it universal among Jain texts.

The order of the five salutations is deliberate but, as the commentators note, deeply paradoxical. The Siddhas — the fully liberated, bodiless, perfected souls — are spiritually superior to the Arihants, who still wear a body. Yet the Arihants are saluted first. The reason: the Siddhas are silent. It is the embodied, teaching Arihant who first reveals the path of liberation; it is from the Arihant's instruction that we even know the Siddhas exist. So practical primacy is given to the one who teaches, even though ultimate primacy belongs to the one who has finished.

The descending order through Acharya, Upadhyaya, and Sadhu mirrors the practitioner's own journey. Every spiritual life begins with becoming a Sadhu — withdrawing from harmful activity and accepting the discipline of the great vows. With time, the practitioner becomes an Upadhyaya, mastering the scriptures and teaching them to others. Through deepening conduct, the practitioner becomes an Acharya, the leader of the community. Continuing further, the four destructive karmas fall away and the practitioner becomes an Arihant. Finally, when even the four non-destructive karmas exhaust at the moment of bodily death, the soul becomes a Siddha. The mantra is therefore not merely a salutation; it is a map of the path itself — read from top to bottom, the goal; read from bottom to top, the journey.

The text explains that this mantra is called by many names — Namaskar Mahamantra, Navkar Mantra, Parameshthi Mantra, Mahamantra — and is regarded by Jain tradition as the essence of the entire fourteen Purvas (the lost foundational scriptures). The act of bowing, the commentators say, is itself the discipline: it is a confession of one's own smallness ("I am insignificant from myself; you are great") and a release of pride. By doing so, the inner stains of arrogance, ill-will, and unwholesome tendencies begin to dissolve, and the practitioner becomes receptive to the path.

The traditional closing two lines — "एसो पंच नमुक्कारो सव्वपावप्पणासणो । मंगलाणं च सव्वेसिं पढमं हवइ मंगलं ॥" ("This five-fold salutation destroys all sins; it is the foremost of all auspicious things") — are recognised by the commentators (Niryukti, Churni, Vritti) as a chulika (later appended verse) that explains the *fruit* of the mantra rather than being part of the mantra proper. The fruit is two-fold: the worldly removal of obstacles (artha, kama, arogya, abhirati, nishpatti) and the transcendental attainment of liberation (siddhi, swarga, sukulotpatti). But the deeper teaching is that the mantra works not by magic but by transformation — it remakes the one who recites it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This is a salute to the five kinds of spiritually awakened beings — those still teaching with a body, those completely free, the leaders of the order, the teachers of scripture, and all the monks and nuns. It bows not to people but to what they have become, and that is why anyone, anywhere, can recite it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you bow, even silently, to someone greater than yourself, what falls away inside you?

## SUTRA 1.2 — Karemi Bhante (The Vow of Samayika)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

करेमि भंते ! सामाइयं, सावज्जं जोगं पच्चक्खामि, जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं, न करेमि, न कारवेमि, करंतंपि अन्नं न समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि, निंदामि, गरिहामि, अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥१.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 1.2 ||
karemi bhaṃte ! sāmāiyaṃ, sāvajjaṃ jogaṃ paccakkhāmi, jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tivihaeṇaṃ, maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ, ṇa karemi, ṇa kāravemi, karaṃtaṃpi annaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi, niṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I undertake, O Lord, the vow of Samayika. I renounce all sinful activity. For the duration of my life. By all three modes and all three agents — by mind, by speech, by body — I will not do it myself, I will not cause others to do it, I will not approve of others doing it. From that (sinful past), O Lord, I turn back; I censure it before my own conscience; I condemn it before my Guru; I let go of (that) sinful self.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **करेमि** (karemi) = I do / I undertake
- **भंते** (bhaṃte) = O Lord / O Bhagavan — addressed to the Guru, or in absence to the Devadhideva (the Lord of lords), or even to one's own true Self as witness
- **सामाइयं** (sāmāiyaṃ) = the Samayika — the practice of equanimity
- **सावज्जं** (sāvajjaṃ) = sinful, sin-bearing; activity stained by passion
- **जोगं** (jogaṃ) = activity / yoga (of mind, speech, body)
- **पच्चक्खामि** (paccakkhāmi) = I renounce, I formally give up
- **जावज्जीवाए** (jāvajjīvāe) = for as long as I live (yāvajjīvam) — the monk's lifelong vow; for the layperson, the duration is mutually shortened to the prescribed period (typically 48 minutes / one muhurta) and the phrase **जाव नियमं** (jāva niyamaṃ — "as long as the rule lasts") is substituted
- **तिविहं तिविहेणं** (tivihaṃ tivihaeṇaṃ) = by three karanas (doing, causing, approving) and by three yogas (mind, speech, body) — making nine modes (navakoti). Laypeople take a six-koti vow (excluding "approval" since household life makes total non-approval impossible)
- **मणेणं वायाए काएणं** (maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ) = by mind, by speech, by body
- **न करेमि** (ṇa karemi) = I will not do it myself
- **न कारवेमि** (ṇa kāravemi) = I will not cause it to be done by others
- **करंतंपि अन्नं न समणुजाणामि** (karaṃtaṃpi annaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi) = nor will I approve of another who is doing it
- **तस्स** (tassa) = of that (past sinful activity)
- **पडिक्कमामि** (paḍikkamāmi) = I turn back, I retreat, I withdraw (pratikraman)
- **निंदामि** (niṃdāmi) = I censure (privately, before my own soul's witness)
- **गरिहामि** (garihāmi) = I confess and condemn (publicly, before the Guru)
- **अप्पाणं वोसिरामि** (appāṇaṃ vosirāmi) = I let go of that (sin-stained) self — I renounce the part of me that did the deed

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the vow-formula of Samayika and arguably the most important sentence in lay Jain practice. Where the Namaskar Mahamantra was a salutation to ideals, this sutra is the *contract* — the moment the practitioner stops being a witness and becomes a participant. Every word of it is structurally precise.

The vow opens with **karemi bhante** — "I undertake, O Lord." The choice of words is significant: not "I will try" or "I intend," but "I do" — present tense, definite, performative. To say "I do" is, in that very moment, to have already begun. And it is said before a *bhante* — a witness greater than oneself — because a vow taken privately can be quietly revised, while a vow taken before a witness binds. Where no Guru is physically present, the practitioner takes the inner Self as witness, for the pure soul is itself the supreme Lord.

What is renounced is **savajja jog** — sinful activity. The text is careful here: not all activity is renounced (the practitioner still breathes, sits, thinks), only that activity which is stained by passion and which therefore binds karma. The eighteen pap-sthanas (sites of sin) — killing, lying, stealing, sexual misconduct, possessiveness, anger, pride, deceit, greed, attachment, hatred, quarreling, slander, blame, rejoicing in others' faults, deceitful speech, false belief — these are what is set aside.

The renunciation is **tivihaṃ tivihaeṇaṃ** — three by three, the famous "navakoti" (nine-fold) vow. Sin can enter through any of three doors (doing, causing, approving) using any of three instruments (mind, speech, body). To plug all nine doors at once is the monk's vow. The layperson, who must still run a household, traditionally takes only six of the nine — excluding the "approval" component, since household life inevitably involves silent acceptance of activities one does not personally perform. This is why lay Samayika is six-koti, not nine.

The temporal scope is **jāvajjīvāe** — "as long as I live." For the monk, this is taken absolutely; the vow lasts a lifetime. For the layperson, the word is substituted with **jāva niyamaṃ** ("as long as the rule lasts") — usually two ghadis (forty-eight minutes), the duration of one muhurta during which the layperson sits in formal Samayika. This is why a Jain layperson's daily Samayika is typically forty-eight minutes long.

The second half of the sutra — **tassa bhante paḍikkamāmi niṃdāmi garihāmi appāṇaṃ vosirāmi** — turns to the past. It is not enough to vow against future sin; the vow must also include a release of past sin. The four steps form a precise psychological sequence. *Pratikraman* — turning back — is the act of pulling the awareness back from where it had drifted into transgression. *Ninda* — self-censure — is the inner moment of seeing the wrong as wrong, before any other person knows. *Garha* — confession before the Guru — is the courage of speaking the wrong aloud to a higher witness, which destroys pride and makes the atonement complete. *Vosirami* — "I let go of that self" — is the deepest step: the practitioner does not just regret the deed but releases identification with the part of the self that was capable of the deed. The "I" who sinned is set down; the pure "I" alone remains.

The commentary observes that without this fourth step, no purification is final. To say "I did wrong" while still considering "I did wrong" as part of one's identity keeps the karma bound. To say "the part of me that did this is not the true me" releases the binding and allows the soul's natural luminosity to reappear. This is why the formula ends not with regret but with renunciation — *appāṇaṃ vosirāmi*, "I let go of that self."

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner formally promises before a teacher (or before their own truest self): "From now until my time is up, I will do no harmful act — by my own hands, by asking another, or by silently approving when someone else does it. And whatever harmful things I have done before, I turn from them, I am sorry for them, I confess them, and I let go of the part of me that did them."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have done something you know was wrong, can you let go of the part of yourself that did it — not just feel sorry, but actually set that self down?

The first essential closes here. With the Namaskar Mahamantra the practitioner has bowed to the ideal; with the Karemi Bhante vow the practitioner has stepped onto the path. What follows in the next essential — the Chaturvinshatistava — is the praise of the twenty-four Tirthankaras whose example makes the path walkable.
