# Avashyak Sutra — Avashyak 4
# Pratikramana (પ્રતિક્રમણ) — Turning Back from Transgression

**Avashyak 4 of the Avashyak Sutra**
*Gujarati Source: Shri Avashyak Sutra, printed pages 42–127 (PDF pages 106–191)*
*Total Paths in this Avashyak: 10*

---

## CHAPTER INTRODUCTION

The fourth and longest essential is **Pratikramana** — literally, "going back" — the daily ritual of confessing, withdrawing from, and atoning for every transgression of the day, fortnight, four months, or year. It is the heart of Jain practice. Where Samayika established equanimity, Chaturvinshatistava established the ideal, and Vandana established the relationship with the Guru, Pratikramana is where the ledger of the day's actual conduct is finally laid open and reconciled.

The Sanskrit root is precise: *prati* (back) + *kraman* (going). To do pratikraman is not merely to say "sorry"; it is to actively return — by mind, speech, and body — to the place of right conduct from which one had drifted. The Jain understanding insists that even the smallest unwitting harm — a step on an insect, a sharp word, a wandering thought — must be acknowledged daily, before sleep, lest it harden into karmic deposit.

Avashyak 4 contains ten paths (paaths) in a precise order: (1) the Karemi Bhante vow renewed; (2) the auspicious Chattari Mangalam — declaring the four refuges; (3) the brief Ichchhami Padikkamiu — the synoptic confession; (4) the Iriyavahiyam — confession for the violence of motion; (5) Pahelu Shraman Sutra — confession for the night's sleep; (6) Bijju Shraman Sutra — confession for the day's alms-tour; (7) Trijju Shraman Sutra — confession for the day's study and inspection; (8) Chothu Shraman Sutra — the great enumeration, covering thirty-three categories of monastic conduct; (9) Panchmu Shraman Sutra — the affirmation of faith in the Nirgranth Pravachan and salutation to all contemporary sadhus; (10) Kshamapana — the closing request for forgiveness from all beings.

Together these ten form the complete daily pratikraman of a monk or nun. The layperson's pratikraman (the *shravak pratikraman*) borrows much of this structure but substitutes the twelve lay vows (anuvrats, gunavrats, shikshavrats) for the five great vows.

## SUTRA 4.1 — Karemi Bhante (Renewed)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

करेमि भंते ! सामाइयं, सावज्जं जोगं पच्चक्खामि, जावज्जीवाए, तिविहं तिविहेणं, मणेणं वायाए काएणं, न करेमि, न कारवेमि, करंतंपि अन्नं न समणुजाणामि । तस्स भंते ! पडिक्कमामि, निंदामि, गरिहामि, अप्पाणं वोसिरामि ॥४.१॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.1 ||
karemi bhaṃte ! sāmāiyaṃ, sāvajjaṃ jogaṃ paccakkhāmi, jāvajjīvāe, tivihaṃ tivihaeṇaṃ, maṇeṇaṃ vāyāe kāeṇaṃ, ṇa karemi, ṇa kāravemi, karaṃtaṃpi annaṃ ṇa samaṇujāṇāmi | tassa bhaṃte ! paḍikkamāmi, niṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I undertake, O Lord, the vow of Samayika. (See the full translation under Sutra 1.2 — the same vow-formula opens both essentials.)

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

Identical to Sutra 1.2. (See above for full word-by-word breakdown.)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Karemi Bhante is recited a second time at the opening of Pratikramana, not as repetition but as renewal. Before any confession can be made, the practitioner must first re-establish themselves in the state of equanimity (Samayika) — for confession made from a disturbed or distracted mind is no confession at all. Without first stepping back into the renunciation of *savajja jog*, the practitioner cannot truly examine what they did wrong; the disturbed mind will rationalise, justify, or minimise. Hence the vow is taken again: now I sit in equanimity; now I am ready to look honestly at the day.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before confessing the day's mistakes, the practitioner first re-takes the vow of equanimity — because honest self-examination is only possible from a calm and balanced mind.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you confess a mistake from a place where you are still defending it?

## SUTRA 4.2 — Chattari Mangalam (The Four Refuges)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

(i) चत्तारि मंगलं — अरिहंता मंगलं, सिद्धा मंगलं, साहू मंगलं, केवलि-पण्णत्तो धम्मो मंगलं ।
(ii) चत्तारि लोगुत्तमा — अरिहंता लोगुत्तमा, सिद्धा लोगुत्तमा, साहू लोगुत्तमा, केवलि-पण्णत्तो धम्मो लोगुत्तमो ।
(iii) चत्तारि सरणं पवज्जामि — अरिहंते सरणं पवज्जामि, सिद्धे सरणं पवज्जामि, साहू सरणं पवज्जामि, केवलि-पण्णत्तं धम्मं सरणं पवज्जामि ॥४.२॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.2 ||
(i) cattāri maṃgalaṃ — arihaṃtā maṃgalaṃ, siddhā maṃgalaṃ, sāhū maṃgalaṃ, kevali-paṇṇatto dhammo maṃgalaṃ |
(ii) cattāri loguttamā — arihaṃtā loguttamā, siddhā loguttamā, sāhū loguttamā, kevali-paṇṇatto dhammo loguttamo |
(iii) cattāri saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi — arihaṃte saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi, siddhe saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi, sāhū saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi, kevali-paṇṇattaṃ dhammaṃ saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(i) Four are auspicious — the Arihants are auspicious, the Siddhas are auspicious, the Sadhus are auspicious, the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones is auspicious.
(ii) Four are supreme in the world — the Arihants are supreme, the Siddhas are supreme, the Sadhus are supreme, the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones is supreme.
(iii) Four refuges I take — I take refuge in the Arihants, I take refuge in the Siddhas, I take refuge in the Sadhus, I take refuge in the dharma proclaimed by the omniscient ones.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चत्तारि** (cattāri) = four
- **मंगलं** (maṃgalaṃ) = auspicious; that which dissolves the sense of "mine" and the karmic stain of worldly clinging (*mām galayati iti maṃgalam*)
- **लोगुत्तमा** (loguttamā) = supreme in the world (*loka + uttama*); not surpassed by anything
- **सरणं पवज्जामि** (saraṇaṃ pavajjāmi) = I go to refuge / I take shelter
- **अरिहंता / सिद्धा / साहू** = the Arihants, Siddhas, Sadhus (already explained in Sutra 1.1)
- **केवलि-पण्णत्तो धम्मो** (kevali-paṇṇatto dhammo) = the dharma declared by the omniscient ones — *dharma* = the soul's own nature, the path that prevents falling into the lower destinies (*durgati-prapannam ātmānaṃ dhārayati iti dharmaḥ*)

### 5. COMMENTARY

Before confession can begin, the practitioner needs to know what holds them up while the act of self-exposure is happening. Confession is dangerous: it requires looking at one's own faults without flinching. To do so safely, the practitioner first declares four refuges — four supports that will not fail.

The world is full of "auspicious" things — saffron, kumkum, unbroken rice — but every such object is itself perishable; they cannot dissolve karma or grant lasting welfare. The four true *mangalas* — Arihants, Siddhas, Sadhus, and the Kevali-proclaimed dharma — are auspicious in a different sense: they actually destroy the karmic root of suffering, and so are auspicious in fact, not in symbol.

The Arihants and Siddhas are the goal — already attained, embodied (Arihant) and disembodied (Siddha). The Sadhus are the present companions on the path. The dharma is the path itself. Together they form a complete shelter: a goal to aim at, companions to walk with, and a road to walk on. The Uttaradhyayana Sutra puts it directly: *dhammo dīvo paiṭṭhā ṇaṃ* — "Dharma is the lamp, the resting-place." The drowning soul cannot save itself; only by taking refuge in what stands above the flood can the practitioner cross.

The threefold declaration — *they are auspicious / they are supreme / I take refuge in them* — moves through three psychological registers: recognition, valuation, surrender. Without all three, refuge is incomplete. Many recognise the Arihants are auspicious; fewer hold them as supreme above all other supports; fewer still actually surrender into their refuge. The sutra walks the practitioner through all three movements before pratikraman begins.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before confessing the day's wrongs, the practitioner declares the four supports they will lean on while doing so: the embodied awakened ones, the perfected liberated ones, the living spiritual community, and the teaching itself — recognising that these alone are truly safe to lean on.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have to look honestly at your own faults, what do you actually lean on for support?

## SUTRA 4.3 — Ichchhami Padikkamiu (The Synoptic Confession)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं (ठामि काउस्सग्गं) जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ, काइओ, वाइओ, माणसिओ, उस्सुत्तो, उम्मग्गो, अकप्पो, अकरणिज्जो, दुज्झाओ, दुव्विचिंतिओ, अणायारो, अणिच्छियव्वो, असमण-पाउग्गो, णाणे तह दंसणे चरित्ते सुए सामाइए; तिण्हं गुत्तीणं, चउण्हं कसायाणं, पंचण्हं महव्वयाणं, छण्हं जीवणिकायाणं, सत्तण्हं पिंडेसणाणं, अट्ठण्हं पवयणमाऊणं, णवण्हं बंभचेरगुत्तीणं, दसविहे समणधम्मे समणाणं जोगाणं, जं खंडियं जं विराहियं तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं ! ॥४.३॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.3 ||
icchāmi paḍikkamiuṃ (ṭhāmi kāussaggaṃ) jo me devasio aiyāro kao, kāio, vāio, māṇasio, ussutto, ummaggo, akappo, akaraṇijjo, dujjhāo, duvviciṃtio, aṇāyāro, aṇicchiyavvo, asamaṇa-pāuggo, ṇāṇe taha daṃsaṇe caritte sue sāmāie; tiṇhaṃ guttīṇaṃ, cauṇhaṃ kasāyāṇaṃ, paṃcaṇhaṃ mahavvayāṇaṃ, chaṇhaṃ jīvaṇikāyāṇaṃ, sattaṇhaṃ piṃḍesaṇāṇaṃ, aṭṭhaṇhaṃ pavayaṇamāūṇaṃ, ṇavaṇhaṃ baṃbhaceragguttīṇaṃ, dasavihe samaṇadhamme samaṇāṇaṃ jogāṇaṃ, jaṃ khaṃḍiyaṃ jaṃ virāhiyaṃ tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I wish to perform pratikraman (I stand in kāyotsarga). Whatever transgression of this day I have committed — by body, by speech, by mind — contrary to scripture, contrary to the path, contrary to the rules of conduct, of things-not-to-be-done, of bad meditation, of bad thought, of misconduct, of things-not-to-be-wished-for, unworthy of an ascetic — in knowledge, perception, conduct, scripture-study, in the practice of equanimity; in the three guptis, the four kashayas (passions), the five great vows, the six classes of life, the seven kinds of food-procurement, the eight maternal virtues of the doctrine, the nine guardrails of celibacy, in the ten-fold ascetic dharma, in the activities of monks — whatever I have broken, whatever I have damaged — for that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं** (icchāmi paḍikkamiuṃ) = I wish to perform pratikraman
- **(ठामि काउस्सग्गं)** = (I stand in kāyotsarga — bodily release through meditation)
- **देवसिओ अइयारो** (devasio aiyāro) = transgression of the day (varies by occasion: rāi = night; pakkhī = fortnight; cāumāsī = four months; saṃvaccharī = annual)
- **काइओ, वाइओ, माणसिओ** (kāio, vāio, māṇasio) = bodily, verbal, mental
- **उस्सुत्तो** (ussutto) = contrary to scripture (proclaiming or doing what differs from Agama)
- **उम्मग्गो** (ummaggo) = contrary to the path (acting against the conduct established by the elders)
- **अकप्पो** (akappo) = contrary to the rules of conduct (kalpa)
- **अकरणिज्जो** (akaraṇijjo) = of things that ought not to be done
- **दुज्झाओ** (dujjhāo) = of bad meditation (ārta-dhyāna or raudra-dhyāna — anguished or violent meditation)
- **दुव्विचिंतिओ** (duvviciṃtio) = of bad thought (kashaya-laden contemplation)
- **अणायारो** (aṇāyāro) = of misconduct
- **अणिच्छियव्वो** (aṇicchiyavvo) = of things not to be wished for
- **असमण-पाउग्गो** (asamaṇa-pāuggo) = unworthy of an ascetic
- The numbered enumerations (3 guptis, 4 kashayas, 5 mahavrats, 6 jivanikayas, 7 pindeshanas, 8 pravachan-matas, 9 brahmacharya-guptis, 10 shraman-dharmas) are the standard categories of monastic conduct, each treated in detail in subsequent sutras
- **जं खंडियं जं विराहियं** (jaṃ khaṃḍiyaṃ jaṃ virāhiyaṃ) = whatever has been broken, whatever has been damaged. *Khanditam* = totally broken (a fundamental breach); *Virāhitam* = damaged or violated in part (an aticār). The two cover the full range of severity
- **तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं** (tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ) = "may my misdeed be reduced to nothing" — the standard Jain formula of renunciation. The Niryukti decomposes it: *mi* = humility; *cchā* = covering/abandoning the faults; *mi* = firmness in restraint; *du* = self-censure for the bad-doer; *ka* = acknowledgement of the wrong done; *ḍa* = pacifying/destroying the karma

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the *synoptic confession* — a compressed verbal map of every category of monastic transgression, made before the practitioner enters the longer sutras that examine each category in detail. Its purpose is psychological: to acknowledge that *all* possible categories of fault are now being placed on the table, before any single one is examined.

The structure moves through three layers. First, the practitioner names the *means* by which transgression occurs: body, speech, mind. Second, the *kinds* of transgression: contrary to scripture, contrary to the path, against rules of conduct, things not to be done, bad meditation, bad thought, misconduct, unworthy actions. Third, the *areas* of transgression: knowledge, perception, conduct, scripture-study, equanimity — the five jewels — followed by the eight numbered categories of conduct (3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10) which together exhaust the entire range of monastic discipline.

The most commented-upon phrase is **micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ** — three short words that close every Jain confession. The literal meaning is "may my wrong-doing be undone (be reduced to falsehood)." The Avashyak Niryukti says these words destroy karma if spoken with a heart genuinely melted by remorse — and *only* if so. Spoken mechanically, they have no effect; spoken with the *bhava* (inner state) of true contrition, they immediately weaken the karmic bond.

The pratikraman commentators carefully distinguish four grades of transgression: *atikrama* (the mere intention to break a vow), *vyatikrama* (taking steps toward breaking it), *aticāra* (partial breach, the vow still standing), and *anācāra* (full breach, the vow destroyed). The first three can be cleansed by pratikraman alone; the fourth requires additional Guru-ordained atonement (prāyaścitta). This sutra's *jaṃ khaṃḍiyaṃ jaṃ virāhiyaṃ* covers both the third and fourth grades — preparing the practitioner for both ordinary and extraordinary repair.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before going through each category of conduct in detail, the practitioner makes a single all-covering confession: "Whatever I have broken or damaged today — by body, speech, or mind, in any of the categories of right conduct — may my wrong-doing be undone."

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you set out to examine your day, do you start by confessing everything in summary, or do you only acknowledge the wrongs you can already name?

## SUTRA 4.4 — Iriyavahiyam (Confession for the Violence of Motion)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं इरियावहियाए, विराहणाए, गमणागमणे, पाणक्कमणे, बीयक्कमणे, हरियक्कमणे, ओसा-उत्तिंग-पणग-दग-मट्टी-मक्कडा-संताणा-संकमणे, जे मे जीवा विराहिया, एगिंदिया, बेइंदिया, तेइंदिया, चउरिंदिया, पंचिंदिया, अभिहया, वत्तिया, लेसिया, संघाइया, संघट्टिया, परियाविया, किलामिया, उद्दविया, ठाणाओ ठाणं संकामिया, जीवियाओ ववरोविया, तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं ॥४.४॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.4 ||
icchāmi paḍikkamiuṃ iriyāvahiyāe, virāhaṇāe, gamaṇāgamaṇe, pāṇakkamaṇe, bīyakkamaṇe, hariyakkamaṇe, osā-uttiṃga-paṇaga-daga-maṭṭī-makkaḍā-saṃtāṇā-saṃkamaṇe, je me jīvā virāhiyā, egiṃdiyā, beiṃdiyā, teiṃdiyā, cauriṃdiyā, paṃciṃdiyā, abhihayā, vattiyā, lesiyā, saṃghāiyā, saṃghaṭṭiyā, pariyāviyā, kilāmiyā, uddaviyā, ṭhāṇāo ṭhāṇaṃ saṃkāmiyā, jīviyāo vavarovīyā, tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I wish to perform pratikraman for the violence of motion (irya-pathik) — for harm caused while coming and going: by stepping on living creatures, on seeds, on green plants; by stepping on dew-drops, anthills, mildew, water, earth, spider-webs; whatever beings I have harmed — one-sensed, two-sensed, three-sensed, four-sensed, five-sensed; (whatever I have done) by striking, by covering with dust, by pressing into the earth, by colliding-together, by touching, by inflicting suffering, by causing pain, by terrorising, by displacing from one place to another, by depriving of life — for all that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **इरियावहियाए** (iriyāvahiyāe) = pertaining to *iryāpathik* — the karma bound through the path of motion. *iryā* = motion; *patha* = path
- **विराहणाए** (virāhaṇāe) = of harm, violation
- **गमणागमणे** (gamaṇāgamaṇe) = in coming and going (the practitioner's daily movements)
- **पाणक्कमणे** = stepping on living creatures (two-sensed and above)
- **बीयक्कमणे** = stepping on seeds
- **हरियक्कमणे** = stepping on green plants
- **ओसा** (osā) = dew, frost
- **उत्तिंग** (uttiṃga) = anthills (or burrows of small creatures)
- **पणग** (paṇaga) = mildew, mould (five-coloured)
- **दग** (daga) = water (with one-sensed water-bodies)
- **मट्टी** (maṭṭī) = earth (with one-sensed earth-bodies)
- **मक्कडा-संताणा** (makkaḍā-saṃtāṇā) = spiders' webs
- **संकमणे** = stepping on
- **एगिंदिया** through **पंचिंदिया** = beings with one to five senses (one-sensed: earth, water, fire, air, plants; two-sensed: shells, worms; three-sensed: ants, lice; four-sensed: flies, bees; five-sensed: animals, humans, devas, hell-beings)
- **अभिहया** (abhihayā) = struck head-on
- **वत्तिया** (vattiyā) = covered (with dust); piled up; trapped under heaped material
- **लेसिया** (lesiyā) = pressed into the ground; stuck flat against earth
- **संघाइया** (saṃghāiyā) = collided together (one with another)
- **संघट्टिया** (saṃghaṭṭiyā) = touched (so as to terrorise)
- **परियाविया** (pariyāviyā) = afflicted with all-round suffering
- **किलामिया** (kilāmiyā) = caused fatigue / pain
- **उद्दविया** (uddaviyā) = caused calamity, terror, suffocation
- **ठाणाओ ठाणं संकामिया** (ṭhāṇāo ṭhāṇaṃ saṃkāmiyā) = displaced from one place to another (against the creature's will)
- **जीवियाओ ववरोविया** (jīviyāo vavarovīyā) = deprived of life

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Iriyāvahiyam is the daily confession for the violence of motion — and it teaches the most counterintuitive idea in Jain ethics: that *motion itself* is a form of harm. To walk anywhere is to step on something. To breathe is to disturb air-bodied beings. To speak is to dislodge subtle life. The world is so densely populated with life — visible and invisible — that no movement is karmically neutral.

But Jain ethics is not paralysed by this recognition. The same texts that name the harm also name a precise, ten-fold typology of how harm occurs: striking, covering, pressing, colliding, touching, afflicting, fatiguing, terrorising, displacing, killing. The Tattvārtha Sūtra defines violence as *pramatta-yogāt prāṇa-vyaparopaṇaṃ hiṃsā* — "the destruction of any life-force, performed under the influence of negligence." The crucial qualifier is *pramatta* (negligent). A surgeon cuts a body; the cut is not violence because the surgeon's intent is healing. A practitioner who walks with awareness causes less karma than one who walks without — even if the same insect is stepped on.

The sutra enumerates five categories of beings (one-sensed through five-sensed) because the karmic weight of harming each is different. Crushing a one-sensed earth-body is karmically lighter than killing a five-sensed deer; but neither is dismissed. Both must be confessed.

The Iriyāvahiyam is recited before any major activity — before kāyotsarga, before alms-tour, before scripture-study, before pratilekhana. The reasoning: whatever the next activity will be, it will involve motion, and the practitioner must first cleanse the field before entering it.

The Avashyak Niryukti calculates that the total possible distinctions of pratikraman across all life-forms, all kinds of harm, all means (mind/speech/body), all times, and all witnesses come to **18,24,120** distinct types — a number meant not as taxonomy but as a vivid reminder that no harm, however subtle, escapes the awareness of the careful practitioner.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Before doing anything else, the practitioner confesses that simply by walking and moving today, they have likely stepped on, brushed against, or displaced countless tiny living beings — and asks that the harm of such unintentional acts be reduced to nothing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you watched your own movements through the world today with the same care a Jain monk does, what would you see that you usually miss?

## SUTRA 4.5 — Pahelu Shraman Sutra (The Sleep Confession)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इच्छामि पडिक्कमिउं पगामसिज्जाए, निगामसिज्जाए, संथारा उव्वट्टणाए, परियट्टणाए, आउंटण-पसारणाए, छप्पइ संघट्टणाए, कूइए, कक्कराइए, छीए, जंभाइए, आमोसे, ससरक्खामोसे, आउलमाउलाए, सोवणवत्तियाए, इत्थीविप्परियासियाए, दिट्ठीविप्परियासियाए, मणविप्परियासियाए, पाणभोयणविप्परियासियाए, जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं ॥४.५॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.5 ||
icchāmi paḍikkamiuṃ pagāma-sijjāe, nigāma-sijjāe, saṃthārā uvvaṭṭaṇāe, pariyaṭṭaṇāe, āuṃṭaṇa-pasāraṇāe, chappai saṃghaṭṭaṇāe, kūie, kakkarāie, chīe, jaṃbhāie, āmose, sasarakkhāmose, āulamāulāe, sovaṇavattiyāe, itthī-vippariyāsiyāe, diṭṭhī-vippariyāsiyāe, maṇa-vippariyāsiyāe, pāṇa-bhoyaṇa-vippariyāsiyāe, jo me devasio aiyāro kao tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I wish to perform pratikraman for transgressions of sleep — for sleeping too long, for sleeping repeatedly long, for turning on the bedding, for turning over again and again, for contracting and stretching the limbs, for crushing tiny lice or bedbugs, for indistinct utterances, for grinding the teeth, for sneezing un-awarely, for yawning openly, for unmindful touching, for touching things covered in dust, for being agitated by dreams, for visions of women in sleep, for distorted visions, for distorted thoughts, for distorted (cravings) for food and drink — whatever transgression of this day I have committed, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **पगामसिज्जाए** (pagāma-sijjāe) = sleeping excessively (*prakāma* = beyond limit; *śayyā* = bed) — excessive softness or excessive duration on bedding
- **निगामसिज्जाए** (nigāma-sijjāe) = repeatedly sleeping excessively
- **उव्वट्टणाए, परियट्टणाए** = turning over once / turning over repeatedly (which can crush small insects in the bedding)
- **आउंटण-पसारणाए** = contracting and stretching the limbs (without prior pratilekhana)
- **छप्पइ संघट्टणाए** = touching/crushing six-footed creatures (bedbugs, lice)
- **कूइए, कक्कराइए** = inarticulate utterances; teeth-grinding (signs of disturbed mind)
- **छीए, जंभाइए** = sneezing un-awarely; yawning openly (without covering the mouth, exposing air-bodied beings)
- **आमोसे, ससरक्खामोसे** = touching things without prior inspection; touching dust-covered objects
- **आउलमाउलाए** = mental agitation (from disturbing dreams)
- **सोवणवत्तियाए** = pertaining to dreams in sleep
- **इत्थी-विप्परियासियाए** = distorted (sensual) visions of women in dreams
- **दिट्ठी-विप्परियासियाए** = distorted vision (in dream)
- **मण-विप्परियासियाए** = distorted thought (in dream)
- **पाण-भोयण-विप्परियासियाए** = distorted craving for food and drink (in dream — including dreaming of taking the prohibited night meal)

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Jain teaching is that karma binds even during sleep. The waking mind is responsible for waking acts; the sleeping mind, while dormant, still carries forward the impressions of the day, and the body still moves — turning, crushing, brushing — and binds karma through that motion. There is no time-out from accountability. Hence the practitioner makes pratikraman not only for the day's deeds but also for the night's.

The sutra distinguishes two phases: the conscious moments around sleep (the way one lies down, turns, touches the bed) and the unconscious dream-life (lustful visions, agitated dreams, dreams of breaking the rules). Both are confessed. Why confess what was unconscious? Because the dream is a leakage from the day's unprocessed psychic material. If the day was lived in equanimity, the dreams are usually clear. If the day was lived in agitation, the dreams reveal it. So the dream-pratikraman serves a double purpose: it cleanses the karma of the night, and it diagnoses what the day failed to settle.

The Dashavaikalika Sutra contains the rule *jayaṃ saye* — "sleep with care." Even the way one lies down is part of the practice. A Jain monk traditionally sleeps on a thin straw mat (santhara), turns minimally, and inspects the area before each motion to avoid harming small creatures. The "excessive softness" mentioned in the sutra is rejected not for ascetic harshness but because soft, comfortable bedding nourishes the very *pramada* (negligence) that pratikraman is trying to dissolve.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner confesses for sleep itself — for sleeping too much, turning carelessly, crushing tiny creatures in the bedding, and even for the wandering dreams of the night that revealed what the day had not yet settled.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What does the quality of your dreams tell you about what your waking day has failed to resolve?

## SUTRA 4.6 — Bijju Shraman Sutra (The Alms-Tour Confession)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिक्कमामि गोयरचरियाए, भिक्खायरियाए, उग्घाड-कवाड उग्घाडणाए, साणा-वच्छा-दारा-संघट्टणाए, मंडी-पाहुडियाए, बलि-पाहुडियाए, ठवणा-पाहुडियाए, संकिए, सहसागारे, अणेसणाए, पाणभोयणाए, बीयभोयणाए, हरियभोयणाए, पच्छाकम्मियाए, पुरेकम्मियाए, अदिट्ठहडाए, दगसंसट्ठहडाए, रयसंसट्ठहडाए, पारिसाडणियाए, परिट्ठावणियाए, ओहासणभिक्खाए, जं उग्गमेणं उप्पायणेसणाए, अपरिसुद्धं परिग्गहियं वा जं न परिट्ठवियं तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं ॥४.६॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.6 ||
paḍikkamāmi goyaracariyāe, bhikkhāyariyāe, ugghāḍa-kavāḍa ugghāḍaṇāe, sāṇā-vacchā-dārā-saṃghaṭṭaṇāe, maṇḍī-pāhuḍiyāe, bali-pāhuḍiyāe, ṭhavaṇā-pāhuḍiyāe, saṃkie, sahasāgāre, aṇesaṇāe, pāṇabhoyaṇāe, bīyabhoyaṇāe, hariyabhoyaṇāe, pacchākammiyāe, purekammiyāe, adiṭṭhahaḍāe, dagasaṃsaṭṭhahaḍāe, rayasaṃsaṭṭhahaḍāe, pārisāḍaṇiyāe, pariṭṭhāvaṇiyāe, ohāsaṇabhikkhāe, jaṃ uggameṇaṃ uppāyaṇesaṇāe, aparisuddhaṃ pariggahiyaṃ vā jaṃ na pariṭṭhaviyaṃ tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I perform pratikraman for the conduct of the alms-tour and the conduct of begging — for opening half-open or fully closed doors, for brushing past dogs, calves, or children, for accepting the assembled-and-stored offering, for accepting offering meant for ritual sacrifice, for accepting offering specially set aside, for accepting suspect food, for taking food in sudden haste, for accepting food without proper investigation, for taking food containing living beings, food with seeds, food with green plants, food with pre-touch (action done after for the sake of the monk), food with post-touch, food brought from un-seen places, food touched by living water, food touched by dust-bodied life, for spilling food on the way, for discarding food carelessly, for begging in the manner of a beggar (with attachment), and for any food taken with defects of *udgama* (donor-side faults), *utpādana* (monk-side faults), *eshana* (acceptance faults); for any unclean food received, or for food that should have been discarded but was not — for all that, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

(Selected key terms — full enumeration in commentary)
- **गोयरचरियाए** (goyaracariyāe) = the manner of *gocharī* — "cow-like grazing"; the monk takes a little food from many houses, never burdening one householder, in imitation of a cow grazing the tops of grass
- **भिक्खायरियाए** = the manner of begging
- **उग्घाड-कवाड उग्घाडणाए** = opening half-closed or closed doors of a household (which is improper for the monk; the householder must open the door)
- **साणा-वच्छा-दारा संघट्टणाए** = brushing against dogs, calves, or children (mūla = "dārā" = woman; here taken by some commentators as "child")
- **मंडी / बलि / ठवणा-पाहुडियाए** = the three kinds of "stored-and-set-aside" food: *mandi* (collected together for distribution); *bali* (set aside for ritual offering, kept for crows or ancestors); *thavanā* (specifically set aside for monks or other beggars)
- **संकिए** = doubtful / suspect food
- **सहसागारे** = food taken in sudden haste, without consideration
- **अणेसणाए** = food not properly investigated for purity (the three eshanas: *gaveshana* — search; *grahaṇa-eshana* — acceptance; *paribhoga-eshana* — consumption)
- **पाण/बीय/हरियभोयणाए** = food containing tiny living beings, seeds, or green plants (which carry life)
- **पच्छा/पुरेकम्मियाए** = "post-action" food (the host washes pots etc. after monk leaves, generating new karma); "pre-action" food (the host washes hands etc. just before, for monk's sake)
- **अदिट्ठहडाए** = food brought from places not visible (e.g. dark inner chamber where contamination cannot be checked)
- **दग/रयसंसट्ठहडाए** = food touched by water or dust containing micro-life
- **पारिसाडणियाए** = scattering / spilling food on the road
- **परिट्ठावणियाए** = discarding food carelessly (over a pot of water, killing water-bodies)
- **ओहासणभिक्खाए** = begging insistently or with attachment, like a beggar (vs. the dispassionate gathering of a monk)
- **उग्गमेणं उप्पायणेसणाए** = collectively, the 16 *udgama* faults (donor-side), 16 *utpādana* faults (monk-side), 10 *eshana* faults — totalling 42 faults of food

### 5. COMMENTARY

A Jain monk lives entirely on alms (gocharī). The food is gathered each day from many households, a small portion from each, in imitation of a cow grazing the tops of grass without uprooting it: the metaphor is exact — *no household should feel the burden, no plant should be uprooted*. The monk eats only what is already prepared, never anything cooked specially for them, and accepts only what is offered freely. The practice rests on the *navakoṭi* (nine-fold) commitment — neither cooking nor causing cooking nor approving cooking, by mind, speech, or body.

This sutra confesses the forty-two well-known *doṣas* (faults) of alms-procurement. The classical enumeration is sixteen *udgama* (faults caused by the donor — *e.g.* preparing food specially for monks); sixteen *utpādana* (faults caused by the monk — *e.g.* praising the householder to influence them); ten *eshana* (faults at the moment of acceptance — *e.g.* taking suspect food). The sutra walks through this list compactly, naming each by its key term.

The deepest fault is the last: **ohāsaṇabhikkhāe** — begging in the manner of a beggar. A monk is not a mendicant in the worldly sense. The monk does not beg; the monk *receives*. Begging carries attachment ("I want this"; "give it to me"); receiving is dispassionate ("if it comes, it comes"). The moment the monk's bowl is approached with desire — for a particular taste, a particular household, a particular outcome — the monk has fallen into the most subtle and dangerous fault, because it is invisible. The food may be perfectly pure but the inner state corrupt. Hence the confession enumerates this last.

The sutra closes with two clauses for completeness: any food received with unidentified faults (*aparishuddhaṃ pariggahiyaṃ*); and food that should have been thrown away (because contaminated) but was kept and consumed (*jaṃ na pariṭṭhaviyaṃ*). Together they cover both the failure to refuse and the failure to discard.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The monk confesses for the day's alms-tour: for any food taken without proper care for who prepared it, for whom it was prepared, and how it was offered — and for any subtle attachment in the very act of receiving it.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you receive what others give you — food, attention, help — is your inner state one of receiving, or of taking?

## SUTRA 4.7 — Trijju Shraman Sutra (The Time-Inspection Confession)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

पडिक्कमामि चाउक्कालं सज्झायस्स अकरणयाए, उभओकालं भंडोवगरणस्स अप्पडिलेहणाए, दुप्पडिलेहणाए, अप्पमज्जणाए, दुप्पमज्जणाए, अइक्कमे, वइक्कमे, अइयारे, अणायारे, जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स मिच्छा मि दुक्कडं ॥४.७॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.7 ||
paḍikkamāmi cāukkālaṃ sajjhāyassa akaraṇayāe, ubhao-kālaṃ bhaṃḍovagaraṇassa appaḍilehaṇāe, duppaḍilehaṇāe, appamajjaṇāe, duppamajjaṇāe, aikkame, vaikkame, aiyāre, aṇāyāre, jo me devasio aiyāro kao tassa micchā mi dukkaḍaṃ ||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I perform pratikraman for the failure to perform scripture-study at the four time-periods (of day and night), for the failure (or improper performance) of the inspection of robes and equipment at the two time-periods (dawn and dusk), for not inspecting at all, for inspecting badly, for not cleaning at all, for cleaning badly — for any *atikrama* (intent to break a vow), *vyatikrama* (motion toward breaking it), *aticāra* (partial breach), or *anācāra* (full breach) — whatever transgression of this day I have committed, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **चाउक्कालं सज्झायस्स अकरणयाए** = failure to perform *svādhyāya* (scripture-study) at the four prescribed periods. The Uttaradhyayana 26th chapter prescribes scripture-study in the first and fourth periods of the day and the first and fourth periods of the night
- **उभओकालं भंडोवगरणस्स** = at the two periods (sunrise and sunset), of *bhanda* (vessels) and *upakaraṇa* (other monastic equipment — robes, brush, alms-bowl, etc.)
- **अप्पडिलेहणाए, दुप्पडिलेहणाए** = not inspecting (pratilekhana), or inspecting badly. *Pratilekhana* = the careful visual inspection of robes and equipment to find any tiny life that may have settled there
- **अप्पमज्जणाए, दुप्पमज्जणाए** = not cleaning (pramārjana), or cleaning badly. *Pramārjana* = the gentle removal of any tiny creature found, using a soft brush (gucchā), to a safe place
- **अइक्कमे, वइक्कमे, अइयारे, अणायारे** = the four grades of vow-breach: (1) the mere *intent* to break; (2) *motion toward* breaking; (3) *partial breach* (vow still standing); (4) *full breach* (vow destroyed)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This sutra is the *time-discipline confession*. The monastic life is governed by precise time-windows for each duty: when to study, when to inspect equipment, when to meditate, when to take alms, when to sleep. Slippage in these windows is itself a transgression — not because the schedule is sacred, but because losing the discipline of time loosens the discipline of mind. The Uttaradhyayana states: *kālapaḍilehaṇāe ṇaṃ ñāṇāvaraṇijjaṃ kammaṃ khavei* — "by inspecting time properly, the karma that obscures knowledge is destroyed."

Two specific duties are named: *svādhyāya* (scripture-study) at four periods of the day-and-night cycle, and *pratilekhana–pramārjana* (inspection-and-cleaning) at the two transitions of the day. The pratilekhana is the daily ritual of carefully unfolding each robe, alms-bowl, and piece of equipment, scanning for any tiny life that may have settled, and gently removing it to safety. Done with attention, it can take an hour. Done carelessly — *duppaḍilehaṇāe* — it nominally fulfils the rule but actually causes harm, because the inspector is no longer seeing.

The sutra closes by confessing the four grades of breach. **Atikrama** is the first step into transgression: even the thought "I might break this vow" begins the breach. **Vyatikrama** is taking practical steps in that direction. **Aticāra** is partial breach — the vow is wounded but not killed. **Anācāra** is the full and final breach. The first three can be cleansed by pratikraman alone; the fourth requires the Guru's prescribed prāyaścitta (additional atonement). By naming all four grades together, the sutra ensures that no level of negligence escapes acknowledgement.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner confesses for any failure to do scripture-study or the careful inspection-and-cleaning of belongings at the right times of day, and for any thought, motion, partial slip, or full breach of the vows that this carelessness might have caused.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What practice have you let slip not because it was hard, but because you stopped honouring its time?

## SUTRA 4.8 — Chothu Shraman Sutra (The Great Enumeration of Conduct)

(1) paḍikkamāmi egavihe asaṃjame |

(3) paḍikkamāmi tihiṃ daṃḍehiṃ — maṇadaṃḍeṇaṃ, vayadaṃḍeṇaṃ, kāyadaṃḍeṇaṃ | ... [continuing through all 33 enumerations]

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I perform pratikraman for: (1) one-fold non-restraint; (2) the two bondages — bondage of attachment, bondage of aversion; (3) the three rods — rod of mind, rod of speech, rod of body; (4) the three guards — guarding the mind, speech, body; (5) the three darts — the dart of deceit, the dart of attached aspiration, the dart of false belief; (6) the three prides — pride of wealth, pride of taste, pride of comfort; (7) the three violations — violation of knowledge, of perception, of conduct; (8) the four passions — anger, pride, deceit, greed; (9) the four impulses — impulse for food, fear, sex, possession; (10) the four false-stories — about women, food, country, kings; (11) the four meditations — sorrowful, violent, dharmic, pure; (12) the five activities — bodily, instrumental, jealous, afflictive, life-destroying; (13) the five sense-objects — sound, form, smell, taste, touch; (14) the five great vows — non-violence, truth, non-stealing, celibacy, non-possession; (15) the five carefulnesses — in walking, speaking, eating, handling, disposing; ... and so through (33) the thirty-three forms of disrespect to the Guru — for any of these in which I have transgressed today, may my misdeed be reduced to nothing.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA (key terms)

- **असंजम** (asaṃjam) = non-restraint — the failure to govern desires; the root of all karmic bondage
- **बंधण** (baṃdhaṇa) = bondage; karmic binding through attachment (*rāga*) or aversion (*dveṣa*)
- **दंड** (daṃḍa) = "rod" — that by which the soul is punished; the harmful activity of mind, speech, body
- **गुत्ति** (gutti) = guard, restraint — the protective harnessing of mind, speech, body
- **सल्ल** (salla) = "dart" — the inner thorn that obstructs spiritual progress; three: *māyā* (deceit hidden in self), *nidāna* (attached aspiration to a future worldly fruit), *mithyā-darśana* (false belief, the master-thorn)
- **गारव** (gārava) = pride; three kinds: *ṛddhi* (pride in wealth/honour), *rasa* (pride in taste/food), *sātā* (pride in comfort/health)
- **विराहणा** (virāhaṇā) = violation, breach — of the three jewels (jñāna, darśana, cāritra)
- **कसाय** (kasāya) = passion — that which causes the soul to suffer rebirth (*kaṣ* = suffering; *āya* = gain — that which gains by causing suffering): krodha (anger), māna (pride), māyā (deceit), lobha (greed)
- **सण्णा** (saṃṇā) = innate impulse — four primal urges shared with all life: hunger, fear, sex, possession
- **विकहा** (vikahā) = "perverted speech" — talk that destroys the spiritual life: gossip about women, food, countries, kings (i.e., sense-bait, gluttony-bait, geographic curiosity, political fascination)
- **झाण** (jhāṇa) = meditation; four kinds: *ārta* (sorrowful), *raudra* (cruel-violent), *dharma* (right), *śukla* (pure). The first two are *aśubha* (unwholesome); the last two are *śubha*
- **किरिया** (kiriyā) = action (karma-binding); five named here from the larger 25: *kāyikī* (bodily), *adhikaraṇikī* (instrumental — using weapons), *prādveṣikī* (jealous), *paritāpanikī* (afflictive), *prāṇātipātikī* (life-destroying)
- **कामगुण** (kāmaguṇa) = the five sense-objects, called "qualities of desire" because they bind the soul through attraction
- **महव्वय** (mahavvaya) = the five great vows — total non-violence, total truth, total non-stealing, total celibacy, total non-possession
- **समिति** (samiti) = the five carefulnesses — in walking, speaking, eating, handling/placing of objects, disposing of bodily wastes
- **जीवणिकाय** (jīvaṇikāya) = the six classes of living beings (earth, water, fire, air, plants, mobile creatures)
- **पिंडेसणा** (piṇḍesaṇā) = the seven kinds of investigation in alms-procurement
- **पवयणमाऊ** (pavayaṇamāū) = the eight maternal disciplines of the Pravacan: 5 samitis + 3 guptis (the *pravacan-mātā* or "mothers of the doctrine," because they nurture the practitioner)
- **बंभचेरगुत्ति** (baṃbhaceragutti) = the nine guardrails of celibacy (Uttaradhyayana 16): no shared lodging with women/animals/eunuchs, no women's stories, no shared seat, no gazing at limbs, no listening to women's voices/sounds, no recalling past pleasures, no rich food, no excess food, no bodily ornamentation
- **समणधम्म** (samaṇadhamma) = the ten ascetic dharmas: kṣamā (forbearance), mārdava (humility), ārjava (straightforwardness), mukti (non-greed), tapa (austerity), saṃyam (restraint), satya (truth), śauca (purity), akiñcanya (non-possessiveness), brahmacarya (celibacy)
- **आसायणा** (āsāyaṇā) = disrespect — to the Guru, the elders, or the three jewels (the thirty-three categories enumerated in the Daśāśrutaskandha)

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the longest single sutra of the entire Avashyak — a complete inventory of monastic conduct expressed as a series of thirty-three numbered enumerations, ascending from one (one-fold non-restraint) through thirty-three (the thirty-three āśātanās of the Guru). Together these enumerations exhaust the categories of right conduct named in the Jain tradition; whatever can go wrong in monastic life falls somewhere within them.

The structural genius of this sutra is its *completeness through enumeration*. Rather than asking the practitioner to remember every possible fault, the sutra walks them through the entire field of conduct one numerical category at a time. The practitioner cannot complete the recitation without passing through every domain of their own life: passions, impulses, speech, action, sense-engagement, vows, carefulnesses, the protection of life-classes, the dignity of the doctrine, the nine walls of celibacy, the ten qualities of the ascetic, and so on through to the thirty-three subtle disrespects to the teacher. By the end, no domain is unexamined.

Several philosophical landmarks deserve mention. The **three darts** (*shalya*) name the three deepest inner obstacles: *māyā* (the deceit one practises *on oneself* — covering one's faults from one's own awareness); *nidāna* (attaching one's spiritual practice to a worldly fruit, e.g. "I will fast in order to be reborn rich"); and *mithyā-darśana* (false belief — the master-dart, which makes all the others invisible). The Tattvārtha Sūtra states *niḥśalyo vratī* — "the vow-keeper must be without these darts." A practitioner with even one of them cannot truly hold a vow.

The **three prides** (*gārava*) are the inner movements that pull the soul into lower destinies. *Ṛddhi-gārava* (pride in attainment) corrupts the seasoned practitioner; *rasa-gārava* (pride in taste) corrupts the alms-tour; *sātā-gārava* (pride in comfort) corrupts the entire monastic life by making the body's ease the unspoken priority.

The **four meditations** divide all mental concentration into wholesome and unwholesome. *Ārta-dhyāna* (sorrowful concentration) is the mind's circling around lost or threatened pleasure. *Raudra-dhyāna* (violent concentration) is the mind absorbed in violence, lying, theft, or pleasure-protection. *Dharma-dhyāna* is concentration on the teaching, the path, the welfare of beings. *Śukla-dhyāna* is the pure stillness that culminates in omniscience. The pratikraman confesses both the failure to do *dharma* and *śukla* and the lapse into *ārta* and *raudra*.

The **five great vows** (mahāvrats) are the structural skeleton of the entire monastic life: total non-harming, total truth, total non-taking, total celibacy, total non-possession. Each is taken with all three karaṇas (doing, causing, approving) and all three yogas (mind, speech, body) — the navakoṭi commitment. This sutra confesses any breach of any vow at any of those nine doors.

The closing enumeration — **thirty-three āśātanās** — covers the most subtle breach of all: disrespect to the Guru in any of thirty-three categories enumerated in the Daśāśrutaskandha (e.g. walking in front of the Guru, speaking before the Guru is finished, sitting on a higher seat than the Guru, etc.). That this is the *thirty-third* and final category is significant: the entire sutra began with disrespect to one's own restraint and ends with disrespect to the one who teaches it. The full circle is closed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This is the master enumeration: the practitioner walks methodically from "one" through "thirty-three," confessing in each numerical category every possible failure of monastic conduct — from the single failure of restraint to the thirty-three subtle ways of disrespecting the teacher — leaving no domain of practice unexamined.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you walked through your day numerically — one category, two, three, all the way — would there be a single category that you could honestly skip?

## SUTRA 4.9 — Panchmu Shraman Sutra (Affirmation of Faith and Salutation)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT (representative — seven sub-sutras)

(1) णमो चउवीसाए तित्थयराणं उसभाइ महावीर पज्जवसाणाणं ।
(2) इणमेव णिग्गंथं पावयणं सच्चं अणुत्तरं केवलियं पडिपुण्णं णेआउयं संसुद्धं सल्लकत्तणं सिद्धिमग्गं मुत्तिमग्गं णिज्जाणमग्गं णिव्वाणमग्गं अवितहमविसंधि सव्वदुक्खप्पहीणमग्गं ।
(3) इत्थं ठिया जीवा सिज्झंति बुज्झंति मुच्चंति परिणिव्वायंति सव्वदुक्खाणं अंत करेंति ।

(5) तं धम्मं सद्दहंतो पत्तियंतो रोएंतो फासंतो पालंतो अणुपालंतो तस्स धम्मस्स (केवलीपन्नत्तस्स) अब्भुट्ठिओमि आराहणाए, विरओमि विराहणाए, असंजमं परियाणामि-संजमं उवसंपज्जामि, अबंभं परियाणामि-बंभं उवसंपज्जामि, अकप्पं परियाणामि-कप्पं उवसंपज्जामि, अण्णाणं परियाणामि-णाणं उवसंपज्जामि, अकिरियं परियाणामि-किरियं उवसंपज्जामि, मिच्छत्तं परियाणामि-सम्मत्तं उवसंपज्जामि, अबोहिं परियाणामि-बोहिं उवसंपज्जामि, अमग्गं परियाणामि-मग्गं उवसंपज्जामि । जं संभरामि, जं च न संभरामि, जं पडिक्कमामि, जं च न पडिक्कमामि, तस्स सव्वस्स देवसियस्स अइयारस्स पडिक्कमामि ।
(6) समणोऽहं संजय, विरय, पडिहय-पच्चक्खाय पावकम्मो, अणियाणो, दिट्ठि-संपण्णो, मायामोसो विवज्जिओ ।
(7) अड्ढाइज्जेसु दीव-समुद्देसु पण्णरससु कम्म-भूमिसु जावंति केइ साहू, रयहरण-गुच्छग-पडिग्गह-धारा, पंच महव्वय धारा, अट्ठारस-सहस्स-सीलंग(रह) धरा, अक्खय आयार चरित्ता, ते सव्वे सिरसा मणसा मत्थएण वंदामि ॥४.९॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.9 || (representative)
(1) ṇamo cauvīsāe titthayarāṇaṃ usabhāi mahāvīra pajjavasāṇāṇaṃ |
(2) iṇameva ṇiggaṃthaṃ pāvayaṇaṃ saccaṃ aṇuttaraṃ kevaliyaṃ paḍipuṇṇaṃ ṇeāuyaṃ saṃsuddhaṃ sallakattaṇaṃ siddhimaggaṃ muttimaggaṃ ṇijjāṇamaggaṃ ṇivvāṇamaggaṃ avitahamavisaṃdhi savvadukkhappahīṇamaggaṃ |
... [through (7)]

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(1) Salutations to the twenty-four Tirthankaras, beginning with Rishabh and ending with Mahavir.
(2) This very Nirgranth doctrine is true, supreme, proclaimed by the omniscient ones, complete, leading to liberation, perfectly pure, dart-extracting, the path to perfection, the path to release, the path out of saṃsāra, the path to nirvāṇa, free of falsehood, unbroken, the path that ends all suffering.
(3) Beings established in this doctrine attain perfection, awaken to omniscience, are released, attain final release, end all suffering.
(4) That dharma I trust, I have faith in, I take pleasure in, I touch (in practice), I observe, I observe continuously.
(5) Trusting that dharma, having faith in it, taking pleasure in it, touching it, observing it, observing it continuously — for the practice of that dharma I now stand up, I withdraw from its violation. *Recognising* non-restraint, I take up restraint; recognising un-celibacy, I take up celibacy; recognising the un-allowable, I take up the allowable; recognising ignorance, I take up knowledge; recognising no-action, I take up (right) action; recognising false belief, I take up right belief; recognising un-awakening, I take up awakening; recognising the wrong path, I take up the right path. Whatever (faults) I remember, whatever I do not remember; whatever I have already pratikraman'd, whatever I have not yet pratikraman'd — for all those transgressions of this day I now perform pratikraman.
(6) I am a śramaṇa — restrained, withdrawn, having destroyed and renounced sinful action, free of attached aspiration, possessed of right belief, free of māyā-mṛṣā (the deceit-and-falsehood combine).
(7) In the two-and-a-half continents and the surrounding oceans, in the fifteen lands-of-action, whatever sādhus exist — bearers of the broom, the brush, and the alms-bowl; bearers of the five great vows; bearers of the eighteen-thousand-fold conduct of *śīla*; observers of the unbreakable conduct — I salute them all with my head, with my mind, with my forehead.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA (selected)

- **णमो चउवीसाए तित्थयराणं** = salutations to the twenty-four Tirthankaras
- **णिग्गंथं पावयणं** (ṇiggaṃthaṃ pāvayaṇaṃ) = the Nirgranth doctrine — *nirgrantha* = "without knot," free of all inner and outer attachment; *pravacana* = the spoken teaching
- **सच्चं अणुत्तरं केवलियं** = true, supreme, proclaimed by the omniscient
- **पडिपुण्णं** (paḍipuṇṇaṃ) = complete (un-broken in any part)
- **णेआउयं** (ṇeāuyaṃ) = leading to liberation (*nyāyya* — just; *nayanaśīla* — leading)
- **संसुद्धं** = perfectly pure
- **सल्लकत्तणं** = dart-extracting (cutting out the three shalyas)
- **सिद्धि / मुक्ति / णिज्जाण / णिव्वाण मग्गं** = path to perfection / release / exit (from saṃsāra) / nirvāṇa
- **अवितह** (avitaha) = without falsehood (*vitatha* = false)
- **अविसंधि** (avisaṃdhi) = unbroken in continuity (eternal teaching)
- **सव्वदुक्खप्पहीणमग्गं** = the path that ends all suffering
- **सद्दहामि, पत्तिआमि, रोएमि, फासेमि, पालेमि, अणुपालेमि** = I trust (śraddhā), I have faith (pratīti), I take pleasure (ruci), I touch (sparśa — practice), I observe (pālan), I observe continuously (anupālan) — the six progressive movements of religious commitment
- **अब्भुट्ठिओमि** (abbhuṭṭhioṃi) = "I have stood up" — I am present, I am ready, I am committed (Sanskrit *abhyutthitaḥ-asmi*; profoundly active)
- **आराहणाए / विराहणाए** = for the practice / from the violation
- The eight pairs (asaṃyam/saṃyam, abrahma/brahma, akalpa/kalpa, ajñāna/jñāna, akriyā/kriyā, mithyā/samyak, abodhi/bodhi, amārga/mārga) = the eight specific renunciations and acceptances that make up the practitioner's vow-of-practice
- **अड्ढाइज्जेसु दीव समुद्देसु** = in the two-and-a-half continents (Jambudweep + Dhātakikhaṇḍa + half of Puṣkarvar) and the encircling oceans — the entire human-region of Jain cosmography
- **पण्णरससु कम्मभूमिसु** = in the fifteen lands-of-action (where the Tirthankara's teaching is currently active)
- **रयहरण-गुच्छग-पडिग्गह** = the broom (rajoharaṇ), the brush (gucchā), the alms-bowl (pātra) — the three identifying marks of the Jain monk
- **अट्ठारस-सहस्स-सीलंग** = the eighteen-thousand divisions of conduct (*śīlāṅga*) — derived as 10 dharmas × 10 life-types × 5 senses × 4 saṃjñās × 3 yogas × 3 karaṇas = 18,000

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the *affirmation sutra* — a seven-movement crescendo that takes the practitioner from salutation through reasoned recognition of the doctrine to renewed personal commitment, and finally to a sweeping salute to every other practitioner currently walking the path anywhere in the universe.

**Movement 1 (salutation)**: Before declaring faith in the doctrine, the practitioner first salutes the twenty-four who proclaimed it. The doctrine is not a free-standing abstraction; it has a lineage of awakened proclaimers, and faith in the doctrine cannot be separated from reverence for those who gave it.

**Movement 2 (the doctrine described)**: In a remarkable string of fifteen attributes, the Nirgranth doctrine is named. Each attribute corresponds to a specific philosophical claim: *true* (it is *not* false); *supreme* (none higher); *proclaimed by omniscient ones* (so cannot be partially mistaken); *complete* (nothing missing); *leading-to-liberation* (its end is mokṣa, not worldly fruit); *perfectly pure* (no defects); *dart-extracting* (it removes the three shalyas); *path to siddhi / mukti / nirvāṇa / nirvāṇa-mārga* (four near-synonyms emphasising the unity of the goal); *without falsehood* (no portion can be set aside as later proven wrong); *unbroken* (the same doctrine taught by all twenty-four); *path that ends all suffering* (the final criterion).

**Movement 3 (the doctrine's fruit)**: Beings who establish themselves in this doctrine *siddhanti* (become Siddhas), *bujjhanti* (become Buddhas — awakened), *muccanti* (are released), *parinivvāyanti* (attain final nirvāṇa), and *end all suffering*. The five verbs trace the practitioner's career through completion.

**Movement 4 (the practitioner's faith — six grades)**: The sutra now turns to the personal commitment, expressed in six progressive grades of religious feeling. *Sadda-hāmi* (I trust — bare confidence). *Patti-āmi* (I have inner faith — confidence has become conviction). *Roe-mi* (I take pleasure in — conviction has warmed into love). *Phāse-mi* (I touch — love has begun to act). *Pāle-mi* (I observe — action has become discipline). *Anu-pāle-mi* (I observe continuously — discipline has become a way of life). Each verb is a higher tier of the same orientation; together they describe how mere intellectual assent becomes lived practice.

**Movement 5 (the eight renunciations)**: This is the heart of the practitioner's pratigya. Eight pairs are named. In each pair, the practitioner *recognises* (parijānāmi) the negative side and *takes up* (upasampadyāmi) the positive side. *Recognising* means seeing the negative for what it is — its causes, its consequences, its taste in the mouth of one who carries it. Without this recognition, renunciation is only a wish. Once recognised, the negative can be set down; once the negative is set down, the positive can be picked up. The eight pairs cover the entire range of practice: from gross conduct (non-restraint vs. restraint, un-celibacy vs. celibacy, the un-allowable vs. the allowable) through cognitive orientation (ignorance vs. knowledge, no-action vs. action, false vs. right belief) to the deepest level (un-awakening vs. awakening, wrong path vs. right path).

The movement closes with a moving acknowledgement: *jaṃ saṃbharāmi, jaṃ ca na saṃbharāmi* — "what I remember, what I do not remember"; *jaṃ paḍikkamāmi, jaṃ ca na paḍikkamāmi* — "what I have already pratikraman'd, what I have not yet pratikraman'd." The practitioner confesses even the faults they cannot recall, and even the faults that have not yet been formally addressed in the day's pratikraman. This is the final completeness.

**Movement 6 (the practitioner's character)**: Now the practitioner declares what they are: *I am a śramaṇa* — restrained, withdrawn, having renounced sinful action, free of nidāna, possessed of right belief, free of māyā-mṛṣā. This is not boast; it is *self-naming* — a deliberate articulation of the identity the practitioner intends to live up to. The Jain tradition holds that to name oneself accurately is to summon oneself toward what is named.

**Movement 7 (salute to all contemporary sādhus)**: The sutra closes by reaching outward. In the two-and-a-half continents — the entire human world of Jain cosmography — wherever any sādhu exists, bearing the three marks (broom, brush, bowl), holding the five great vows, observing the eighteen-thousand divisions of conduct, the practitioner salutes them all with head, mind, and forehead. The practice is not solitary; the practitioner is part of a worldwide invisible community of those undertaking the same work, and the daily pratikraman closes by recognising and saluting them.

The number 18,000 deserves a note: it is calculated as 10 ascetic dharmas × 10 categories of life × 5 senses × 4 sañjñās × 3 yogas × 3 karaṇas. The number is meant not as count but as expression of completeness — *every* possible combination of right action across every dimension is included.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner declares faith in the doctrine, names its qualities, accepts the eight specific renunciations of the practice, identifies themselves as a śramaṇa, and salutes every other monk and nun currently walking this same path anywhere in the world.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you commit to a path, do you commit only with your understanding — or do you also let yourself feel pleasure in it, touch it, observe it, observe it continuously?

## SUTRA 4.10 — Kshamapana Sutra (The Forgiveness Closing)

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

खामेमि सव्वे जीवा, सव्वे जीवा वि खमंतु मे ।
मित्तीमे सव्व भूएसु, वेरं मज्झं ण केणइ ॥१॥

एवमहं आलोइय, णिंदिय गरिहिय दुगंछियं सम्मं ।
तिविहेण पडिक्कंतो, वंदामि जिणे चउव्वीसं ॥२॥
॥४.१०॥

### 2. ROMAN TRANSLITERATION

|| 4.10 ||
khāmemi savve jīvā, savve jīvā vi khamaṃtu me |
mittīme savva bhūesu, veraṃ majjhaṃ ṇa keṇai ||1||

evamahaṃ āloiya, ṇiṃdiya garihiya dugaṃchiyaṃ sammaṃ |
tivihaeṇa paḍikkaṃto, vaṃdāmi jiṇe cauvvīsaṃ ||2||

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I forgive all living beings; may all living beings forgive me. My friendship is with all beings; I have enmity with none.

Thus, having confessed (āloiya), having censured before my own conscience (ṇiṃdiya), having confessed before my Guru (garihiya), having looked upon (my own faults) with disgust (dugaṃchiyaṃ) — properly — having performed pratikraman by all three (mind, speech, body) — I bow to the twenty-four Jinas.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

- **खामेमि** (khāmemi) = I forgive
- **सव्वे जीवा** = all living beings — every conscious being in the universe
- **वि खमंतु मे** = and may they forgive me
- **मित्तीमे सव्व भूएसु** (mittīme savva bhūesu) = my friendship (mitratā) is with all beings (bhūta — every existing thing with life)
- **वेरं मज्झं ण केणइ** = enmity of mine with no one
- **एवमहं** = thus I
- **आलोइय** (āloiya) = having confessed openly (ālocanā — open looking, the formal disclosure)
- **णिंदिय** (ṇiṃdiya) = having censured before my own conscience (the inward movement)
- **गरिहिय** (garihiya) = having confessed before my Guru (the outward movement, public)
- **दुगंछियं** (dugaṃchiyaṃ) = having looked upon with disgust — recognising the fault as worthy of revulsion
- **सम्मं** (sammaṃ) = properly, rightly (with full understanding)
- **तिविहेण पडिक्कंतो** = having performed pratikraman by the three (mind, speech, body)
- **वंदामि जिणे चउव्वीसं** = I bow to the twenty-four Jinas

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Kshamapana is the closing of the entire pratikraman — and arguably the most beloved verse in lay Jain practice. After all the categories have been examined, all the faults confessed, all the pratigyās renewed, the practitioner now turns outward one last time and offers two simple commitments to every other living being in the universe:

*Khāmemi savve jīvā* — "I forgive all living beings." If, anywhere in any past or present life, any being has caused me suffering — by harm, by deceit, by indifference, by injustice — I now forgive them. I lay down whatever resentment I have carried. I do not carry the karmic seed of vengeance any further.

*Savve jīvā vi khamaṃtu me* — "May all living beings forgive me." If, anywhere in any past or present life, I have caused suffering to any being — knowingly or unknowingly, by my hand, by my speech, by my passive approval — I ask their forgiveness. I do not minimise; I do not justify; I simply ask.

*Mittīme savva bhūesu* — "My friendship is with all beings." Forgiveness is the negative half; friendship is the positive half. To merely cancel old debts is not enough; the practitioner must also actively extend friendship toward every form of life.

*Veraṃ majjhaṃ ṇa keṇai* — "I have enmity with none." This is the result that was being aimed at all along. The pratikraman ritual was not an end in itself; its end was *this* — to dissolve enmity completely, so that the practitioner enters sleep tonight without a single inner thread of vengeance, resentment, or grudge tying them to any other being in the universe.

The second verse summarises the whole work: *thus, having confessed openly, censured inwardly, confessed before the Guru, looked upon my faults with proper disgust, having performed pratikraman by all three of mind-speech-body — I bow to the twenty-four Jinas.* The bow at the end is significant: the entire ritual closes where it began, with reverence to those who showed the path.

The Jain teaching is that one who can extend pure friendship (*maitrī*) to all beings and hold enmity with none arrives at *vītarāgatā* — total dispassion. And from total dispassion, omniscience follows. So this small two-verse closing is not minor; it is the door through which every other essential delivers its fruit.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The practitioner closes the entire pratikraman with the simplest and deepest commitment of all: "I forgive every living being who has wronged me. May every living being I have wronged forgive me. My friendship is with all beings. I have enmity with no one." Then, having confessed by mind, speech, and body, the practitioner bows to the twenty-four Jinas — and the ritual is complete.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you ended every day with the words "my friendship is with all beings; I have enmity with no one" — and meant them — what would have to change in how you live during the day?

## END OF AVASHYAK 4

॥ આવશ્યક-૪ સંપૂર્ણ ॥
*(Avashyak-4 sampurna — "Avashyak 4 is complete.")*

The fourth essential closes here. Through ten paths, the practitioner has confessed every category of fault — the renewed Samayika vow; the four refuges; the synoptic confession; the violence of motion; the night's sleep; the day's alms-tour; the day's time-discipline; the great enumeration of all thirty-three categories of conduct; the affirmation of faith with its eight renunciations and salute to all contemporary sādhus; and finally the closing forgiveness offered to every living being in the universe.

What follows in the fifth essential — **Kāyotsarga** — is the bodily release: standing or seated meditation in which the practitioner sets down even the body, so that the mind, having been cleansed by pratikraman, can rest at last in pure consciousness.
