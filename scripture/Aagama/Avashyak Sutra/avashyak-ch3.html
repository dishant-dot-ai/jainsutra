Avashyak 3: Vandanā

About This Essential

Reverence to the Living Guide

After the practitioner has steadied the mind in equanimity (Sāmāyika) and praised the perfected ones (Chaturvimshatistava), the current of devotion naturally flows toward the living guide who keeps the path visible. Without a Guru to correct the seeker's missteps, the texts are silent letters and the seeker walks blind.

Acharya Umaswati gives the chain of cause and effect that begins with vandanā: humility leads to the wish to serve; serving the Guru leads to scriptural knowledge; knowledge leads to detachment; detachment to the stopping of inflow; the stopping of inflow to the gathering of austerity; austerity to the shedding of karma; the shedding of karma to liberation. The whole spiritual ladder rests on its first rung: humility expressed through reverence to the teacher.

3.1

The Guru-Vandana Sutra (Ichchhami Khamasamano)

इच्छामि खमासमणो ! वंदिउं जावणिज्जाए निसीहियाए, अणुजाणह मे मि उग्गहं निसीहि, अहोकायं कायसंफासं खमणिज्जो भे ! किलामो अप्पकिलंताणं बहुसुभेण भे ! दिवसो वइक्कंतो जत्ता भे जवणिज्जं च भे ? खामेमि खमासमणो देवसियं वइक्कमं आवस्सियाए पडिक्कमामि खमासमणाणं देवसियाए आसायणाए तित्तीसण्णयराए जं किंचि मिच्छाए मणदुक्कडाए वयदुक्कडाए कायदुक्कडाए कोहाए माणाए मायाए लोहाए सव्व कालियाए सव्वमिच्छोवयाराए सव्व धम्माइक्कमणाए आसायणाए जो मे देवसिओ अइयारो कओ तस्स खमासमणो ! पडिक्कमामि निंदामि गरिहामि अप्पाणं वोसिरामि

I wish, O forgiving ascetic (Khamasamano), to bow — having stopped my sinful activities, with whatever bodily strength I possess. Grant me permission to enter your limited personal space; with the word "nisīhi" (renouncing all other activity) I bow, touching your feet (the lower part of your body) with my body. Forgive me, O Lord! — for any pain my touch may have caused you, who are yourself untired. Has your day passed in great wholesomeness? Is your spiritual journey (of restraint) proceeding without obstacle, and are your senses and mind in good order? I beg forgiveness, O forgiving ascetic, for my transgressions of this day. With "āvassiyāe" (returning from the avagrah), I perform pratikraman of any of the thirty-three forms of disrespect (āshātanā) of this day toward the forgiving ascetics — whatever was done from false belief, by misdeed of mind, by misdeed of speech, by misdeed of body, from anger, from pride, from deceit, from greed; whatever transgression of all time, whatever wrong conduct toward all, whatever violation of all dharmas — for any disrespect, for whatever transgression of this day I have committed, O forgiving ascetic, I turn back from it; I censure it; I confess it before you; I let go of (that) sinful self.

This single sutra is the verbal half of the highest-grade vandana ritual (utkrishta vandana). The other half is physical — twenty-five precise actions called the vandana panchavishika (the twenty-five components of vandana), enumerated by the Samavayanga Sutra: two bows (avanata), one yathāj̄āta posture, twelve āvartana (circular hand-gestures), four shira-namana (head-bowings), three guptis (restraints), two pravesha (entries), and one nishkraman (exit). The sutra and the gestures together make a single ritual; neither one is complete without the other. The four-part inner structure of the sutra teaches a complete philosophy of the disciple-Guru relationship. 1. Ichchhanivedan — "I wish." Every spiritual practice begins with the disciple's own wish, freely offered. A vandana performed because someone said "you must" is empty. The very first word — ichchhāmi, "I wish" — establishes that the act about to be performed is not coerced. This is why every Avashyak formula begins with the same word. The sutras of dharma know that the only acts that purify are those born of the seeker's own free choice. 2. Agya Yachana — "Grant me permission to enter." The Guru, like every monk and nun, is held to occupy a personal space of three-and-a-half cubits (about five feet) in every direction — the avagraha. Within this circle the Guru sits, studies, meditates, sleeps. No one — disciple or stranger — may enter without explicit permission. Why? Because the Guru's sphere is sacred to the work of liberation, and unannounced intrusion is itself a disrespect. The disciple stands at the boundary, asks aṇujāṇaha me mi uggahaṃ, waits for the Guru to grant aṇujāṇāmi, and only then steps in with the word nisīhi — "I cease all other activity at this moment." This single ritual teaches an entire ethic of presence: do not assume access, ask for it; when granted, enter wholly. 3. Sukhshata Pruchchha — "How is your day, your journey, your sustenance?" Three questions, each more interior than the last. Has the day passed wholesomely? — outward enquiry about the Guru's day. Is the journey of restraint proceeding without obstacle? — interior enquiry about the Guru's spiritual practice (saṃyam-yātrā). Are the senses and mind well? — innermost enquiry about whether the Guru is being disturbed by the pull of sense-objects or the wandering of mind. The disciple is being trained, by these three questions, to attend to the Guru's whole condition: body, practice, and inner state. Not as flattery, not as small-talk, but as the real care of one who depends on the Guru's continued well-being for their own spiritual life. 4. Kshama Yachana — "Forgive my transgressions." The fourth and longest movement of the sutra is the request for forgiveness. The Dasha-shrutaskandha Sutra enumerates thirty-three specific āshātanās (forms of disrespect to the Guru); the commentators cluster them into four broad categories: dravya-āshātanā (refusing to share food, drink, robes, or vessels with the elders), kshetra-āshātanā (taking the choicer seat or place and leaving the inferior for the elder), kāla-āshātanā (failing to answer when the Guru calls at night, or remaining silent when one is awake and able to respond), bhāva-āshātanā (lacking inward respect; speaking to the Guru with familiar pronouns like "you" instead of honorifics). The sutra then enumerates by what means these disrespects can have arisen: by misdeed of mind, speech, or body; from anger, pride, deceit, or greed; from any false dealing or violation of dharma. The phrase savva-kāliyāe is read in two ways by the commentators — either all kinds of time-bound transgressions (covering the day's daily / fortnightly / four-monthly / yearly āshātanās), or transgressions of all time (covering past, present, and future — confessing the past, withdrawing from the present, vowing against the future). The wider reading is preferred: the disciple who confesses has just stepped, in this ritual moment, outside the timeline of their own karmic accumulation. The sutra closes with the same formula as the Karemi Bhante vow: paḍikkamāmi, niṃdāmi, garihāmi, appāṇaṃ vosirāmi — I turn back, I censure inwardly, I confess outwardly, I let go of the part of me that did the deed. The ritual is not complete with mere acknowledgement; the disciple must release identification with the part of the self that committed the disrespect. What this single sutra teaches, taken whole, is that reverence is a precise discipline — not a feeling, not a posture, but a sequence of actions and inner movements that together remake the relationship between disciple and Guru. The disciple's pride is dissolved through asking permission. The disciple's self-absorption is broken through enquiry about the Guru's condition. The disciple's accumulated faults are released through confession. And the disciple's identification with the small, faulty self is set aside through vosirāmi. In the space cleared by this fourfold movement, the Guru's teaching can land without obstruction. This is why vandana is the third essential, and why no further practice — neither pratikraman, nor kāyotsarga, nor pratyākhyāna — is possible until this one is firm.

Simply Put: The disciple says to the teacher: "I want to bow. May I come into your space? I have come; please forgive any discomfort I cause. Has your day been peaceful? Is your spiritual practice going well? Are your senses calm? And please forgive any disrespect I may have shown you today — by mind, speech, or body, from anger, pride, deceit, or greed — I am sorry, I confess it, and I let go of the part of me that did it."
