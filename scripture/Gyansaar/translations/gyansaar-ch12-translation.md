# Gyansaar — Chapter 12: Nispruhatā (Desirelessness)
## Shlokas ॥१॥८९॥ through ॥८॥९६॥

---

### Overview
Chapter 12 explores **nispruhatā** — the state of desirelessness that arises when the soul recognizes its own svabhava-lābha as the only true fulfillment. Eight shlokas systematically dismantle the architecture of spruhā (craving/desire) and reveal why the nispruha muni is the freest being in the world.

**Four Movements:**
1. Svabhava-Lābha & The Hungry Senses (1–2)
2. Jnana Cuts Spruhā-Viplavlatā (3–4)
3. The Meru-Parvat of the Nispruha Soul (5–6)
4. Par-Nirapexa Sukha & The Final Declaration (7–8)

---

### Shloka 1 — ॥१॥८९॥
**Sanskrit:**
स्वभावलाभात् किमपि प्राप्तव्यं नावशिष्यते |
इत्यात्मनः स्वयंसंपन्नो निःस्पृहो जायते मुनिः ॥१॥८९॥

**Translation:**
After the attainment of one's own svabhava (soul-nature), nothing whatsoever remains to be obtained. Knowing this, the muni who is svayam-sampanna (self-complete) becomes nispruha (desireless).

**Vivechan:**
What does the soul actually want? Yashovijayji's direct question to the reader: what desire, what armān, drives you night and day? Gold-silver? Sky-touching palaces? Youth-beauty? Fame-glory? All of this — drop it! There is no real fulfillment in any of it. Even if you receive all of it, you will not find param shānti or svasthatā. Even if all abhilāpāeṁ are fulfilled — mānajanūrti happens — will you become truly sukhi? No. Because all of it is kṣaṇika, chañchal, and asthir. The soul has received all of this countless times in bhutkāl — and remains daridra, a bhikhāri. The one thing worth pursuing: atma-svabhāv — the akṣay, achyut, achal nature that is available right now. Make the sankalpa: "Mujhe ātma-svabhāv kī prāpti karnā hai — isake alāvā mujhe aur kuch nahi chāhie!" That desirelessness toward all else is nispruhatā.

### Shloka 2 — ॥२॥९०॥
**Sanskrit:**
संयोजितकरैः के के प्रार्थ्यन्ते न स्पृहावहैः |
अमात्रज्ञानपात्रस्य निःस्पृहस्य तृणं जगत् ॥२॥९०॥

**Translation:**
Who bows with folded hands before the nispruha muni, beseeching him? For one with limitless jnāna, the entire world is as tṛṇa (grass) — worthless.

**Vivechan:**
Spruhā is inseparably wed to dīnatā (beggarliness). Wherever desire for a pudgalja thing arises in the mind, dīnatā has already entered through the back door. Spruhā and dīnatā, ananta śaktiśālī ātmā's tejasvitā har letī hai — reduces the infinite soul to a bhogopabhoga kā bhikhārī. The Ravana story: even the most pārakramī Ravana was seized by spruhā for Sita. He went to her with folded hands, girigiḍāte hue, begging for bhoga. This tīvra spruhā made him writhe for a long time. Finally, in the fire of that spruhā, his parivāra, putra, sāthī, rājya — all were destroyed. Spruhā's mūl svabhāv: cause the jīva to display dīnatā, beg and plead, become enslaved. Therefore: the muni must never, even in forgetting, become dīwānā in pursuit of any par-padārtha. Three contemplations to uproot spruhā: (1) resolve "Mujhe ātma-svabhāv kī prāpti karnī hai — isake alāvā kuch nahi chāhie"; (2) "Yadi maiṁ parpadārthoṁ kī spruhā kī tṛptā kār luṁ to phal kyā hoga?" — āshānti, bandan, bhav-chakkar; (3) "Yadi maiṁ parpadārthoṁ kī spruhā tyāg dūṁ to kyā hāni hogī?" — none, only gain.

### Shloka 3 — ॥३॥९१॥
**Sanskrit:**
छिन्दन्ति ज्ञानदात्रेण स्पृहाविषवल्लतां धुधा |
मुखशोषं च मूर्च्छां च दैन्यं यच्छति यत्फलम् ॥३॥९१॥

**Translation:**
The wise cut the poison-vine of spruhā with the sickle of jnāna — that vine whose fruits are dry-mouth, unconsciousness (mūrchhā), and dīnatā.

**Vivechan:**
Spruhā is called **vish-vallarī** — a poison vine. It grows unobstructed from anādikāl on the ātmabhūmi, covering every region with its various forms and flavors. But the characteristic of this vine is: its fruits are mukhśoṣ (dryness, burning), mūrchhā (stupefaction, loss of consciousness), and dainya (beggarliness). In pudgalik spruhā's presence: the face becomes tejas-rahit, shameless, the voice becomes dīna, the jīvan becomes trivial, and ātmik prasan and sambodhan are destroyed. The only tool: jnāna-dātrā — the sickle of right knowledge applied to the root. Three upayas for maintaining this jnāna-vigil: (1) cultivate sādā-sarvadā nispruha ātmāoṁ se paricay and deeply observe their jīvan-charitras; (2) maintain avashyak padārthoṁ kī itanī spruhā na kareṁ ki jiske kāraṇ kisī ke āge dīn bannā paḍe; (3) tapoval aur sahnaśakti prāpt karo.

### Shloka 4 — ॥४॥९२॥
**Sanskrit:**
निष्कासनीया विदुषा स्पृहा चित्तगृहाद् बहि |
अनात्मरतिचाण्डाली संगमड्गीकरोति वः ॥४॥९२॥

**Translation:**
The wise one must expel spruhā from the mind-home entirely. For spruhā — that cāṇḍālinī of anatma-rati (pleasure in non-self) — brings terrible association and drags the soul down.

**Vivechan:**
Spruhā and ātma-rati (delight in the self) are fundamentally incompatible — they cannot coexist. Spruhā cannot remain without dīnatā. The dialogue: a spruhāvant asks "What harm am I doing to anyone?" Upaadhyayji responds: "Great harm — spruhā together with us makes our heart-Lakshmi flee and keeps us nirāntar durghat-pareśān. It is an obstacle we ourselves have placed in our inner sanctum." The positive side: ātma-rati spruhā is upādeya — the longing for sadguru, samyagjnāna, samyam ke avashyak upakaran, sādhu-sattv, śāstrasarakshaṇa, samgra-jīvoṁ kī kalyāṇabhāvanā, mokshaprāpti — these ātma-rati-born spruhās are not to be expelled but cultivated. Key discernment: always interrogate whether a spruhā in your life has anatma-rati at its root. If so — expel it immediately. Do not let it stay even for a moment, because it will convert the ātma-gṛh into bhaknāvaśeṣ.

### Shloka 5 — ॥५॥९३॥
**Sanskrit:**
स्पृहावतो विलोकते, लघवस्तुणतुल्यवत् |
महावचय तथापेते, सञ्जन्ति भवधारिणौ ॥५॥९३॥

**Translation:**
The spruhāvant, searching for even small things, looks like a beggar in the eyes of the world. But one who has expelled spruhā — even the greatest praise and glory cannot move them.

**Vivechan:**
Who is the true pātra of jnana and aishvarya? The nispruha muni. For jnāna kī spruhā, vividha vishay kī spruhā — one who has never lost such spruhā will never become mukta. The Sinhagufāvāsī Muni story: Kosha the veshyā, through mithyā ātmābhimān and false māyā, disrupted his dhyāna. A single inappropriate stuti from a parakīyā nārī — and the spruhā set in motion, pulling Muni to her theatrical world across the country. The Apadabhuti story: even this great ācārya, felled by a single modaka spruhā (desire for a sweet), progressively lost his samyamdrudhata — until spruhā's storm covered all four directions and he fell into the company of mādanākṣī abhinetriyo, becoming a captive of spruhā-āndhi. But for the true yogīśvar: like Meru-parvat — the fastest storm, the tallest waves of bhavsāgar, the most tempestuous spruhā — cannot move him even a fraction. Spruhā cannot even enter his antaḥsthal.

### Shloka 6 — ॥६॥९४॥
**Sanskrit:**
गौरव पीरव-प्रप्तात् प्रभुत्वं प्रतिपद्यते |
ख्यातिं जातिनिःस्पृहस्य स्वयमभ्युद्यन्ति निःस्पृह ॥६॥९४॥

**Translation:**
The nispruha muni — freed from pudgalik spruhā — attains true gaurav (dignity), prabhutva (sovereignty), and kalyan-guṇa. And the highest form of khyāti (recognition) comes to him on its own.

**Vivechan:**
Life in the world bestows gaurav and mānsaman through nagarkāoṁ by way of stuti, bhajan-pūjan — but the muni accepts none of this as genuine. For him it is all parabhāv-pudgalbhāv — it holds no joy. When pudgal kī spruhā is already upaṭ gai, such ānand cannot touch him. But the world's greatest fame, yaśa, prasiddhi, and pratishṭhā will pursue him of their own accord. The Bhamfuriya Muni story: Kanchanpur's king came with a drawn sword to kill him. The muni was perfectly peaceful, even smiling. The king became confused: "Who are you? You look like Pratishthānpur's Mādanbrahmakumāra — who has renounced the throne for shraman life!" If Muni had revealed himself — raja would have prostrated immediately. But as a truly nispruha, tyāgī, and tarasvi yogi — he preferred to remain in the pit where he was thrown and be struck by the raja's sword — and attained siddhi-pad.

### Shloka 7 — ॥७॥९५॥
**Sanskrit:**
परस्पृहा महादुःखं परस्पृहत्वं प्रतिच्छया |
ख्याति जातिनिस्पृहस्य स्वयमभ्युत्थायन्ति निस्पृह ॥७॥९५॥

**Translation:**
Par-spruhā (desire for external things) is the cause of the greatest suffering. The nispruha soul attains its own kalyan-lakṣaṇa (auspicious qualities) naturally. It does not display them.

**Vivechan:**
The nispruha muni's standard is minimal: shilā (stone slab), rūkhā-sūkhā bhojan (dry simple food), one piece of cloth to cover the body, open sky as bistaravana — this is his sampada. If any spruhā remains, it is only this. And any duḥkha-kleśa that may arise — it is only because of this much. The chakravartī in contrast: even the world may call him the most sukhi — but the spruhā-born jalana keeps his face away from prasannātā. To maintain any material āiśvarya, he needs servants, vastrābhūṣaṇ ke liye naukar-chākar, manoranjanke liye nṛtyāṅganāoṁ, bhogopabhog ke liye rānī-mahārāniyon kā muṁh tāknā — totally par-ādhīn. Par-nirapexa sukh (sukh independent of anything external) is the true sukh. And par-sāpexa sukh is always āśvanaka-bhraṁk — because it comes only when wanted and leaves when it arrives. The Ravana-Sita, Mālvapati Muja-Mṛṇālinī stories demonstrate: spruhā for another being's body can raze kingdoms, destroy entire lineages. The conclusion: jnanadṛṣṭi that knows par-padārthoṁ kī spruhā as the root cause of all duḥkh is the liberating insight.

### Shloka 8 — ॥८॥९६॥ *(Hero Shloka)*
**Sanskrit:**
परस्पृहा महादु खं, निस्पृहत्वं महासुखम् |
एतदुक्तं समासेन लक्षण सुखदु.खयो: ॥

**Translation:**
Par-spruhā (desire directed outward, toward the other) is the greatest duḥkha. Nispruhatva (desirelessness) is the greatest sukha. This is, in brief, the very definition and lakṣaṇa of duḥkha and sukha.

**Vivechan:**
The equation is final and irreversible: par-spruhā = mahāduḥkha. Nispruhatva = mahāsukha. This is not poetic — it is the precise lakṣaṇa of what duḥkha and sukha actually are. The Kadiraka Muni story: a saintly muni, param sukhi on rough stone, open sky — as long as no spruhā arose. But when rājakul kā bhojan was served to him once, and it tasted delicious, spruhā jaag gayi. Then his sādhu-jīvan kī maryādā was progressively violated, śramaṇa-jīvan was abandoned, and in pursuit of fulfilling that spruhā, he committed grave sins and landed in sātavīṁ naraka kā mahāduḥkha. This is what a single activated spruhā can do. Upaadhyayji concludes with the Bhaktaparijñā sutra quote: "Nirabekaḷa ātmā prāya duḥkkar duster bhavsāmudra se tīr jātī hai." The nispruha soul that has renounced the obtained sukh in favor of atma-svabhāv prāpti is chirasthayi, anupam, and nirvicar — this is the ultimate nidhi. The present sach: brahma rakh kar nispruhatā ke mahāmārga par jīva ko gaśmān honā chāhiye.

---

### Chapter Summary
Nispruhatā is not poverty — it is the recognition that atma-svabhāv's prāpti leaves nothing else to be obtained. Eight shlokas build the complete map: svabhava-lābha makes all else redundant → spruhā is inseparable from dīnatā (Ravana) → jnāna-dātrā cuts the vish-vallarī → spruhā-chandālinī must be expelled from the mind-home → spruhā makes even great souls fall (Kosha-Sinhagufavasi, Apadabhuti) → nispruha muni's gaurav and siddhi arrive on their own (Bhamfuriya) → par-nirapexa sukha vs par-sapexa suffering (Ravana, Muja-Mrnalini) → final declaration: par-spruhā = mahāduḥkha, nispruhatva = mahāsukha (Kadiraka muni).
