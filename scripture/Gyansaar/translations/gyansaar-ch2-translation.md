# Gyansaar — Chapter 2: Magnata (Absorption)
## Translation: Shlokas 1–8

**Scripture:** Gyansaar (The Essence of Knowledge)
**Author:** Nyayacharya Nyayavisharad Mahopadhyay Shri Yashovijayji
**Commentary (Vivechan):** Pannyaspravara Shri Bhadraguptavijayji Ganivar
**Chapter:** 2 — Magnata (मग्नता / Absorption / Immersion)
**Theme:** Total absorption in the bliss of pure knowledge — jnana-magnata — is the first and primary step toward reaching the summit of fullness.

---

## SHLOKA 1

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

प्रत्याहृत्येन्द्रियव्यूहं समाधाय मनो निजम् ।
दधच्चिन्मात्रविश्रांतिमग्न इत्यभिघोयते ॥१॥१९॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The soul that withdraws its army of senses from outward objects, brings its own mind to stillness, and rests completely in pure consciousness alone — that soul is called "absorbed" (magna).

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**प्रत्याहृत्य** = having withdrawn; **इन्द्रियव्यूहं** = the army/host of senses; **समाधाय** = having settled/stilled; **मनो निजम्** = one's own mind; **दधत्** = holding; **चिन्मात्रविश्रांतिम्** = rest in pure consciousness alone; **मग्न** = absorbed; **इत्यभिघोयते** = thus is called/named.

### 4. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with a precise technical definition: Magnata (absorption) is not a vague spiritual feeling but a specific state with three components. First — withdrawal of the senses from external objects. The senses by nature run outward, toward sound, form, taste, touch. Magnata begins with the deliberate reversal of this flow. Second — the stilling of the mind. Even after the senses are withdrawn, the mind continues its restless movement. Magnata requires that this restlessness be quieted — not suppressed by force, but settled through the direction given by self-knowledge. Third — resting in pure consciousness alone. The word "chinmatra" (pure consciousness alone) is key: the soul does not rest in a thought about consciousness but in consciousness itself. This is the resting place that the vivechan calls the most extraordinary rest-house in existence. Every other place the soul has sought shelter — sensory pleasures, relationships, wealth, reputation — has turned out to be not a rest-house but a house of disturbance. Only self-rest is genuine rest.

The vivechan opens the chapter with a direct challenge to the reader: up to now you have chased worldly objects with a frantic thirst. What did you find? Unending agitation, lamentation, conflict and anxiety. It is time to think again — deeply — about what could actually give permanent happiness. That happiness, the chapter says, is jnana-magnata: total absorption in the bliss of knowledge.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The soul that turns its senses inward, stills its mind, and rests in pure awareness — that soul is absorbed. This is the foundation of the inner path.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Right now, where is your attention? Running outward toward objects, sounds, concerns? What would it feel like to let all of that settle — and simply rest in the awareness that is already here?

## SHLOKA 2

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

स्वभावसुखमग्नस्य, जगत्तत्त्वावलोकिन ।
कर्तृत्वं तान्यभावानां, साक्षित्वमवशिष्यते ॥३॥११॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the soul absorbed in natural bliss and observing the truth of the world through syadvad — there is no authorship of those external states. What remains is only witnessing-awareness.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**स्वभावसुखमग्नस्य** = for one absorbed in natural/innate bliss; **जगत्तत्त्वावलोकिन** = who observes the truth of the world; **कर्तृत्वं** = doership/authorship; **तान्यभावानां** = of those external states/impulses; **न** = not; **साक्षित्वम्** = witness-awareness; **अवशिष्यते** = alone remains.

### 4. COMMENTARY

This shloka addresses the question of karma and action from the perspective of Magnata. When the soul is genuinely absorbed in its own natural bliss, its relationship to worldly activity fundamentally changes. It no longer claims authorship of external impulses and states. It becomes the witness — the sakshi — rather than the doer.

The vivechan draws a vivid analogy: consider a noble person who has been trapped by a gang of criminals, their identity thoroughly corrupted by their influence. One day they encounter a great benefactor who reveals the criminals' secrets, frees them, and places them in the care of good people. Looking back on their past actions, this person thinks: "In truth, those evil acts were not mine — they were done through me by those who had captured me." When the soul is absorbed in its own nature, it recognizes the same thing: the restless, desire-driven karmic actions of the past were not expressions of its true nature but distortions imposed by moha (delusion). Freed from moha through absorption, the soul becomes a witness — present, clear-eyed, and unentangled.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The soul that is truly absorbed in its own natural bliss does not feel like the "doer" of its worldly activities. It becomes a witness — watching clearly, without owning what arises.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever noticed a moment of action that felt effortless — where "you" were barely involved, and things just happened? What if that effortlessness is a taste of witness-awareness?

## SHLOKA 3

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

परवस्तुनि मग्नस्य, तलथा पौद्गलिकी कथा ।
पवामो चामीकरो माव, रूकारा दारादरा बस च ॥श॥

⚠️ *Second line partially reconstructed — vivechan indicates this verse uses the analogy of gold alloyed with base metals*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the soul absorbed in the self, talk of external material things is like — just as pure gold alloyed with inferior metals appears debased, yet the gold itself was never diminished. The soul absorbed in self remains pure despite the material world's clamoring for attention.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The vivechan here develops the gold-purity metaphor that runs throughout the Gyansaar. The soul absorbed in itself hears the constant noise of the material world — name, fame, beauty, music, food, bodily sensations — but this noise finds no purchase. It flows past the absorbed soul like water past a gold coin at the bottom of a river. The gold does not become water. The absorbed soul does not become the noise.

The commentary paints a remarkably specific picture: when the soul is truly absorbed in jnana-ananda (the bliss of knowledge), even the most enchanting sensory objects — gorgeous music, fragrant flowers, the touch of soft things, the beauty of youth — lose their magnetic pull. The vivechan asks: why would a soul already bathed in the most extraordinary bliss be attracted to the secondhand pleasures of the senses? It is like asking a person who has just feasted to be tempted by stale crumbs. The absorption itself acts as the greatest protection against distraction.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

When the soul is absorbed in its own bliss, the constant clamoring of the material world simply doesn't register. The absorbed soul doesn't need to fight temptation — it has no room for it.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of a time you were completely absorbed in something you loved. Did distraction intrude? The absorbed mind is its own protection. What would it mean to be that absorbed in your own nature?

## SHLOKA 4

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

ज्ञानमग्नस्य यच्छर्म, तद्वक्तुं नैव शक्यते ।
नोपमेयं प्रियाश्लेषेर्नापि तच्चन्दनद्रवैः ॥६॥१४॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The bliss of the soul absorbed in knowledge — that cannot be described in words. It cannot be compared to the intoxicating embrace of a beloved, nor even to the cool fragrance of sandalwood.

### 3. COMMENTARY

This is the most lyrical shloka of the chapter. Yashovijayji acknowledges that all language here breaks down. Every analogy that might capture the bliss of jnana-magnata is inadequate. The vivechan engages this directly through dialogue:

"Is it like the pleasure of a beloved's embrace?" — "No, not at all."
"Is it like the fragrant coolness of sandalwood?" — "That too falls short."
"Then what is it like?" — Nothing in the world can serve as its comparison.

The text offers only one solution: experience it directly. No description can substitute for the direct taste (anubhava). But the vivechan also issues a practical guarantee: if even once you taste this bliss of knowledge-absorption, you will naturally and irresistibly return to it again and again. The taste of jnana-magnata, once known, makes all other pleasures feel like poor imitations.

The inability to describe this state also serves as philosophical precision: the bliss of the soul is not a sensation — it is not felt through sense organs the way pleasure is. It is the soul's own self-knowing, which has no object outside itself and therefore no equivalent in the sensory world.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The joy of being absorbed in pure knowledge cannot be put into words. No comparison — not the pleasure of love, not the fragrance of sandalwood — comes close. It must be tasted directly.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Has anyone ever tried to describe to you a taste or experience you'd never had — and found words completely inadequate? Self-absorption may be like that. Words can only point. The experience is beyond them.

## SHLOKA 5

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

शमसत्यपुरो यस्य, विधुषोऽपि महाभया ।
किं स्तुमो ज्ञानदीधुर्यें, तत्र सर्वदेगुमागे ? ॥७॥१५॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed — some characters uncertain*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the one established in tranquility and truth, even the great and learned bow in reverence. What praise is adequate for this ocean of knowledge, this abode of all excellence?

### 3. COMMENTARY

The chapter pauses to offer homage to the great souls who have truly attained jnana-magnata. The vivechan describes two exemplary figures: first, the great muni (monk) whose head was severed and placed in a brass plate by a cruel king — yet the muni, having conquered anger through total absorption in his own nature, attained complete realization at that very moment. What sustained him? The single drop of jnana-amrita — the nectar of knowledge. Second, Daya-sagar Lalitang muni — even in the midst of a royal existence, he chose the renunciant path, and the flute of tranquility-bliss never ceased playing in his heart. What was the secret? Again: the one drop of jnana-ananda.

The point is structural: throughout the ages, countless realized beings have remained fountains of inner peace — even under extreme circumstances — because of this one quality: absorption in the bliss of knowledge. Their praise exceeds all language. We can only bow in gratitude.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The great souls absorbed in knowledge are revered even by the learned. No adequate words exist for their praise — we can only bow before their example and aspiration toward their state.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Can you think of someone whose inner tranquility in difficult circumstances has moved you? What quality in them were you sensing? Could that quality be cultivated in yourself?

## SHLOKA 6

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

यस्य दृष्टिः कृपावृत्तिगिर, शमसूधाकिर ।
तस्मै नमः शुभज्ञानध्यानमग्रताय योगिने ॥८॥१६॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

To the yogi whose vision overflows with compassion, whose being is steeped in the nectar of tranquility — salutations to that great soul perpetually absorbed in the meditation of pure, auspicious knowledge.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The final shloka of the chapter is a devotional seal — a bow to the realized yogi who embodies Magnata in its perfection. The description is precise: vision flowing with compassion (not sentiment, but the spontaneous natural response of a soul that knows all beings as soul), being soaked in tranquility-nectar (not an achieved calm but an effortless baseline), and constant absorption in knowledge-meditation.

The vivechan makes a crucial practical observation: the soul established in jnana-magnata naturally becomes a source of boundless compassion. Why? Because in that state, the artificial barriers between self and other dissolve. The false identifications — with this body, this family, this status — that create the ego's sense of separation from others, all thin out in the light of absorption. What remains is a soul that sees other souls clearly, as soul — and cannot help but overflow with compassion. Magnata is not a withdrawal from the world. It is the deepest possible engagement with reality.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The yogi absorbed in pure knowledge radiates compassion and tranquility naturally, without effort. Salutations to that being who lives in constant inner absorption.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What if compassion is not something you cultivate by trying harder to care — but something that overflows naturally when you are sufficiently absorbed in your own nature? How does that change your understanding of spiritual practice?
