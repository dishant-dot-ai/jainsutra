# Gyansaar Chapter 7: Indriya-Jay (इन्द्रिय-जय) — Conquest of the Senses

**Chapter summary (from title page, page 90):**
This is a fearsome place where the jivatma must always remain alert and vigilant. Where nirmohi-bhav becomes shithil (weak) and for a moment the jivatma comes out helpless from sham-sarovar — there the senses become activated toward their dear objects. On the soul, moha and ajnana spread their maya-jal.

Warning: As long as you are embodied, your senses will remain in contact with their vishaya-vasana vikaras. In such a dangerous situation, can you truly remain nirmohi and jnani? Can you protect dharma's wealth? Can you safeguard it?

For this, continuously contemplate each shloka in this chapter. This will give you the shakti for indriya-vijay and the right margardarshan.

---

## Shloka 1 — ॥१॥४९॥

**Sanskrit:**
विभेषि यदि संसारान्मोक्षप्राप्तिं च काङ्क्षसि ।
तदेन्द्रियजयं कर्तुं, स्फोरय स्फारपौरुषम् ॥१॥४९॥

**Translation:**
If you are afraid of samsara and desire the attainment of moksha — then to conquer the senses, rouse your fullest heroic effort (sphara-paurusham).

**Anvayartha:**
Fear of samsara plus desire for moksha must translate into concrete heroic action — the conquest of the five senses. There is no shortcut. Moksha requires paurusha (heroic effort).

**Vivechan:**
Are you truly frightened of samsara? Troubled by the terrible suffering of four gatis (states of existence)? Disturbed by samsara's many moha-bonds? Do you feel your own worthlessness in vishaya-slavery and kashaya-subjection? Do you want to be free from this terrible samsara? But what will come of just having the thought of freedom? The vasana (longing) for it must arise in you. Only then will it happen. Like the caged Sinha (lion) who has seen what freedom looks like — the vasana for freedom must also include the extreme effort and striving to achieve it.

Do you want to be free from samsara's bonds? To attain moksha's endless inner freedom? To become an adhikari (qualified recipient) of its infinite guna-samrddhi? Its endless jnana and endless darshan — do you have the intense longing for these? Then you must do one purushartha (heroic effort). You must ring the bell of mahapurushartha. You cannot rely on luck. Making time a pretext will not help. Getting lost in the imagination of the future will not help. Only bhagiratha purushartha and parakrama will work. You must join it with mana-vachan-kaya (thought, speech, body). The sookti must be actualized in life: "Aaram haraam hai." Only then is it possible.

You must make your five senses subservient. You must gain victory over them. Shabd (sound), rupa (form), rasa (taste), gandha (smell), and sparsha (touch) — the lolupta (greedy) senses must be controlled. The restraint of limitless desires is necessary. For the desires of shabd, rupa, rasa etc. that arise — do not fulfill them. In the mind itself, make the firm sankalpa (resolve) to not fulfill them. And if, while doing all this, you must face countless dukhas (sufferings) — learn to bear them with a smile. Develop the atma-shakti to endure dukha-dard.

**Simply Put:**
Fear of samsara and desire for moksha are not enough. They must become paurusha — heroic effort to conquer the senses. No vasana, no effort, no result. The bell of mahapurushartha must be rung with mana-vachan-kaya.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my actual level of paurusha toward spiritual liberation? Is my spiritual practice costing me something real, or is it comfortable? What would 'bhagiratha purushartha' look like in my specific life right now?"

## Shloka 2 — ॥२॥५०॥

**Sanskrit:**
वृद्धास्तुणाजलपुणशलचाले किलेन्द्रियम् ।
मृच्छ्ांमेतुच्छा यच्छ्न्ति, विकारिनेयपायवा ॥२॥५०॥

**Translation:**
The senses are like swamps filled with water-weeds — they trap and drag down even the strongest. In the most trifling material senses, even the most heroic are drowned by vikara (sensory degeneration).

**Anvayartha:**
The indriya (senses) are not simple objects. They are swamps — deceptively ordinary on the surface, treacherous below. The strongest swimmers can drown in them. Even the most disciplined sadhu can be dragged down by the pull of a single sense.

**Vivechan:**
The indriya are very much like swamps. They keep the mind constantly immersed and agitated. Even the most stable practitioners, in the midst of constant vishaya-vikaras — the river of their dharma keeps having these thoughts. The mind replays and reinvents them continuously. The vishaya-vikalpa's river of sensation-thoughts floods the mind. The moment even one such thought enters, it spreads through the body's entirety. And for those who get trapped in this — once trapped, they end up lost in the labyrinth of samsara.

When, though abandoning a vishaya, yet it hasn't fully left — then it is fully expressed in the chitta/manoprakash, without any other question remaining. If the jitatma/panca-indriya bhaara no longer gives birth to vishaya-trishna (sensory craving) — then only that nadi (river) keeps steadily diminishing. As a result, whichever pancha-indriya gives the mind's adhyatmic sensations — the moment vishaya-bhoga enters those senses, the mind overflows like a nadi with water. The jivatma becomes submerged and uncontrolled. It roams in the market of samsara, here and there, directionless. The mind becomes distracted-confused and wanders in mayajal (maya's net).

**Simply Put:**
The senses look simple and manageable. They are swamps. One small indulgence starts a process that is very hard to reverse. Even the most disciplined can drown here. No one is immune who underestimates them.

**Contemplate:**
"Which of my five senses is most powerful in its pull — most capable of dragging my attention away from what I actually value? And how honestly do I acknowledge that danger?"

## Shloka 3 — ॥३॥५१॥

**Sanskrit:**
सरित्सहस्रदुष्पूरसमुद्रोदरसोदर ।
तृप्तिमान्नेन्द्रियग्रामो, भव तृप्तोऽन्तरात्मना ॥३॥५१॥

**Translation:**
Know that all the rivers combined cannot fill the ocean's belly — O soul! So the group of senses cannot be satisfied by external things. Be satisfied through the inner atma — antara-atma.

**Anvayartha:**
The ocean has swallowed countless rivers for eternity — Ganga, Yamuna, Brahmaputra flowing continuously — and yet it is never full. It has never said to the rivers: "Enough — I am satisfied." Exactly so: the senses cannot be satisfied by any amount of external feeding.

**Vivechan:**
Countless rivers like Ganga-Yamuna and Brahmaputra constantly dissolve into the ocean's belly, pouring their infinite water-masse. And yet, has the ocean ever been satisfied? Has it ever said to the rivers: "Enough — you've satisfied me, I no longer need you"? No — it was never satisfied and never will be. Exactly the same way: from beginningless time, jivatma has been feeding its senses the food of vishaya-objects. But has any single moment of current life's tapa (austerity) given true satisfaction? No. Has the past's mind-pleasing shabd, rupa, svad-icchiya bhog (pleasant sound, form, tasty food), mrug (fragrance), sparsha (touch) — has it given satisfaction? Then too: that same thing could not be obtained. Yet it wanted it. It wanted the same thing the next day. Next month, next year — the craving grew. No transformation happened — rather, like the river flowing without returning, taking the jivatma everywhere it goes, the swabhava-vasana of mrug grows.

Therefore: be immediately wise. Stop trying to satisfy the indriya through external vishaya-objects — since trupti (satisfaction) will not come there anyway. Instead, use the method of samyag-vivek (right discernment) to satisfy the antara-atma (inner self). Remove the indriya from the vishaya-objects that attract them through the path of samyag-vivek. Immerse yourself in the vachan (words), samman (honor), and seva (service) of the param purush, shastra-shravana, samspar-puja and the mahapurushas' gunanusaran. In dharma-kriyas with all possible sincerity for as long as possible — let even one moment give the param shanti of anubhav.

**Simply Put:**
The ocean has never been filled. Countless rivers for eternity — still empty. Exactly like this: the senses have never been satisfied by any amount of feeding. Stop feeding the indriya externally. Feed the antara-atma. That alone satisfies.

**Contemplate:**
"Where in my life do I keep feeding a craving that never gets satisfied — where more only creates the need for more? What would it look like to redirect that very hunger toward the inner atma?"

## Shloka 4 — ॥४॥५२॥

**Sanskrit:**
आत्मानं विषयैः पाशंभंबवासपराङ्मुखम् ।
इन्द्रियाणि निबध्नन्ति, मोहुराजस्य किंकरा ॥४॥५२॥

**Translation:**
The senses — servants of Moharaja — bind the atma that has turned away from vishaya-bonds and vishaya-vasanas, pulling it back into the samsara-cycle.

**Anvayartha:**
Do not mistake the senses for ordinary, simple things. Though they appear mild and sweet, they are extraordinary — they are Moharaja's personal servants, his sworn agents. Through these agents, Moharaja has extended his empire over countless jivatmas.

**Vivechan:**
Do not make the mistake of treating the indriya as ordinary objects. They appear mild and simple to look at — but in your own atma, they are extraordinary. They are not your servants, they are not simple — they are Moharaja's exclusive sworn servants. Through these loyal servants, Moharaja has spread his empire over infinite jivatmas.

The jivatma that breaks away from samsaravasa and moves toward dharmaraja — these senses stop it in the middle and pull it back into the kingdom of moha. They entrap the jiva so skillfully in their jadu-pash (magic-net) that the jiva doesn't even realize it has been badly entrapped in the indriyas' magic net. To the very end, it remains in this illusion: 'I am in dharmaraja's kingdom.'

Vishayabhilaap — this is the indriyas' magic net, an extraordinary trap. The indriyas keep making the jiva desire vishaya, and in many ways seduce it to perform vishaya-abhilaap. 'When health is good, dharma-dhyan can be done properly. Therefore take proper care of your health and body!' The foolish jiva comes under the influence of its slick-talking counsel and becomes an abhilaspi (craver) of rasa (taste). 'Being a tapasvi — what's the point of that? If at paaraṇa (food-break after fasting) you take ghee, milk, dry fruits, sweets — then only you can properly do tapasya.' The simple-natured jiva easily accepts this counsel of the indriya and becomes a rasa-abhilaspi. 'You are a jnani — so what? Wear clean clothes. Keep the body nirmal (pure). Do not do extreme tapasya. This way your reputation will be established, you will be spoken of everywhere.' The jiva accepts this too.

**Simply Put:**
The senses are Moharaja's most loyal servants. They appear gentle and helpful — they are traps. They entrap the jiva so skillfully that the jiva never realizes it is in a trap. This is the danger: visible enemies are manageable. Invisible agents disguised as helpers are not.

**Contemplate:**
"In what ways do my senses present themselves as friends or helpers — 'you need this for your health, your creativity, your relaxation' — while pulling me away from what I truly value? Which sense is Moharaja's most effective agent in my particular case?"

## Shloka 5 — ॥५॥५३॥

**Sanskrit:**
तिरिमृग्सना घन पश्यन, घाबतीन्द्रियमोहित ।
भनादिनियान ज्ञान, घन पारवे न पश्यति ॥५॥५३॥

**Translation:**
Just as the deer (mriig) in the desert runs toward the mirage-water, deluded by the indriya of sight — and in the end sees no water at all — so the atma blinded by indriya-moha and beginningless ajnana fails to see the real knowledge-substance.

**Anvayartha:**
The deer is the soul. The mirage is vishaya. The running is the endless seeking of satisfaction through senses. The end result: not water — only sand and exhaustion. The knower of this truth, the viveki, doesn't run. They recognize the mirage for what it is.

**Vivechan:**
Who can counsel the deer? It doesn't know in what wild happiness it is running, catching its tail. Where is the water here? It is only the midday sun's shimmer! It is a mirage! Who can tell it where to find water? It will find only: distress, grief, and futile exhaustion. But who is the deer listening to? It is downright foolish...dimwitted! Despite a hundred explanations, it runs still more and still runs.

In exactly this same way, in the desert of samsara, the sense-enslaved jivas — whom can the viveka counsel? The jiva whose indriya-vikalpa (sensory craving) is the slave of Moharaja, makes it a habitual slave of indriya-vasana. In the same proportion the jivatma's yearning-trishna grows, the distress, grief, and suffering increases. Despite this, it doesn't understand and understands not that: the jiva, by indriya's vishaya, can never find true satisfaction (trupti). Despite this, it keeps running in the mirage and the indriya-slavery grows.

Shree Umasvati in Prashamrati has given a very sharp warning on this: "Yesha vishayeshu raatibhavati ta maanushyam na gaNhevat" — "Those in whom the keen attachment to vishaya-objects arises, their jivatma is deceived by the indriya-maya (sense-illusion). What do they want the indriya for? If the indriya's through dvapar (engagement) the tenmata (saturation) is available, it is not possible for the manushya-jivan to attain jnana-amrit (the nectar of knowledge). Just as more and more, step by step, indriya-trishna (sensory craving) is born — step by step, the more material mukha ke piche doilna (pleasure-chasing) diminishes. Gradually the vishaya-trishna towards vishaya grows less."

**Simply Put:**
The deer runs toward the mirage, exhausts itself, finds only sand. The jiva chasing sensory satisfaction is the deer. Mirage-recognition — viveka — is the one thing that stops the running. Not more running toward better mirages.

**Contemplate:**
"Where in my life am I the deer — running toward a 'water' that keeps receding? What would it look like to stop and recognize: 'this is a mirage, there is no water in that direction'?"

## Shloka 6 — ॥६॥५४॥

**Sanskrit:**
पुरः पुरः स्फुरत्तृष्णा, मृगतृष्णानुकारिषु ।
इन्द्रियार्थेषु घावन्ति, त्यक्त्वा ज्ञानामृत जडाः ॥६॥५४॥

**Translation:**
The trishna (craving) that keeps growing and growing — like the mirage-image ever receding — the foolish (jada) chase vishaya-objects, abandoning the amruta of jnana.

**Anvayartha:**
Two parallel images: the mirage that recedes as you approach, and the trishna that grows as you feed it. The jada (spiritually inert) chases both — abandoning the jnana-amruta that was actually within reach.

**Vivechan:**
Who can counsel the deer? It doesn't understand what kind of happiness it is chasing. Here there is only niri surya-kirnon ki jagmagahat (nothing but the shimmer of midday rays). It is a mirage! It will find nothing, reach the waterbody (jalaashay) and: only sand there. Which says: 'No — this is not water. This was only a mirage.' Yet the deer never learned. Despite a hundred times being cheated, it runs again.

In exactly this way: the foolish jiva (jada jiva) — its trishna keeps growing at each step. Forward and forward, always a new more desirable vishaya appears. And it runs, abandoning jnana-amruta. The jnana-amruta is the most extraordinary thing in all creation — the one thing that truly satisfies. But the jiva, obsessed with the mirage of senses, leaves this actual amruta behind and chases the mirage.

The very clear message: stop thinking about whether your indriya are satisfied at the momentary level — they will not give true satisfaction. Immediately become wise. Resolve to live only with your indriya, nirvikalp samadhi-nidhi's theft will not take long once you stop guarding it with indriya-slavery. If you wish this not to happen, instead of practicing pashchatap, make yourself a hardened atma-nigrahi (self-controller). When vishaya appears before you in many forms, do not even raise your eyes toward it. Let the indriyas not be activated by it at all. In this state — 'na rahega baans, na bajegi baansuri' (no bamboo, no flute). You will become vijeta (victor), svaadhayi (self-governing). Then what force in the world can disturb you from your own sankalpa?

**Simply Put:**
Trishna grows with each feeding — forward and forward, always a new mirage. The jada abandons jnana-amruta (actually within reach) to chase it. The viveki stops, turns around, and discovers the amruta they were standing on.

**Contemplate:**
"Am I aware of the jnana-amruta that is always available? What am I leaving behind each time I run toward sensory satisfaction? The amruta doesn't move. Only I move away from it."

## Shloka 7 — ॥७॥५५॥

**Sanskrit:**
पतङ्गामृण्डाभीनेभ-सारंगा यान्ति बुदंशाम् ।
एककेन्द्रिय दोषाच्चेद्, दुष्टैस्तैः किं न पञ्चभिः ॥७॥५५॥

**Translation:**
The moth (patanga), bee (bhramar), elephant (ibha), deer (sarangi) — each is destroyed by the flaw of a single indriya. If each of these is killed by one corrupt indriya — what to say of one beset by all five?

**Anvayartha:**
The famous five-sense parable: moth dies for light (touch/form), bee dies for fragrance (smell) trapped in lotus, elephant is captured for touch (skin contact), fish is caught for taste (bait), deer is killed for sound (hunter's music). Each dies from one indriya. The human, attacked by all five — what chance without samadhi-dhyan?

**Vivechan:**
How has each single indriya killed its creature? The moth — circling the lamp continuously, it offers its life for rupa-priti (love of form/light). In just one moment, even the desire to rest doesn't arise. That very flame, it embraces. But what does it gain from embracing? Not love, not warmth, not growth. It gets only: burnt, reduced to ash within seconds.

Now look at that emotional bee — what a great lover of fragrance! Where flowers haven't arrived yet, huge mountains stand. It gives knowledge from afar (the smell), saying: "Come, come" — signaling it is a magnificent destination. The bee is overjoyed and runs toward the fragrance, to attain it, it pierces its own body's health (shareer swasthya baar betdar vastu) and for the elephant's touch, keeps running constantly. And the lotus-flower — what does the lotus care? 'Tu nahin. Aur sahi' (Not you. Another will do) — the mukti (liberation)-following, it remains nishkriya and nishthar (uninvolved). And the next day when a new lotus-petal blooms, the new devoted bee's body prostrates on it. The previous lover who occupies what space — it didn't even cast a glance. This is lampata (clinging addiction), in which the bhoga-addicted jivatma can't protect itself and doesn't look toward another.

The condition of the fish immersed in rasa (taste) is not different, nor is it different from the simple deer and emotional bee. When...

One indriya kills each of these. What happens to one attacked by all five? They have no chance without the concentrated force of samadhi-dhyan applied against all five simultaneously.

**Simply Put:**
Five classic creatures — each killed by one sense. One sense is enough to cause complete destruction. What about five senses attacking simultaneously? Only samadhi-dhyan can protect from five-fronted assault. Indriya-jay requires total concentration, not piecemeal effort.

**Contemplate:**
"Which single sense, if I had mastered it completely, would change my life most dramatically? What would mastery of that one sense actually look like in practice?"

## Shloka 8 — ॥८॥५६॥

**Sanskrit:**
विषेकद्वीमेहयक् समाधिघनतल्पर ।
इंद्रियाणि न जितोऽसौ, वीरणपा धुरि यथते ॥८॥५६॥

**Translation:**
One who is not established in samadhi-dhana (the wealth of samadhi) — even the most shallow vishaya-dvija — cannot conquer the senses. Without samadhi, no matter how heroic, the senses remain unconquered. Only through samadhi-concentration is indriya-jay possible.

**Anvayartha:**
Indriya-jay is not achieved through willpower alone. It requires samadhi — the specific technique of concentrated, undisturbed immersion in the atma. Even the most heroic effort without samadhi fails. Samadhi is the method; indriya-jay is the result.

**Vivechan:**
How stable (sthir) and deep (gambhir) are the indriya-swamps! They are powerful and deceptive, evil fishermen. Be vigilant! In your atma-kshetra, five powerful and evil fishermen have surrounded you — they are each ready to shoot you with parbrahmi (para-brahma level) thought-arrows. Your five fishermen, in the 'parbrahmi' domain through attack, have set five 'Pancha-brahmi' weapons ready. In your atma-kshetra from samadhi-dhyan, never let these evil fishermen escape even for a moment.

Realize therefore this much: let the indriya-path not become the main path of your dharma-activities. Hence samadhi is necessary: "Indriyasya mukham pashya, kabhoo vishaye mat do" (watch the face of the indriya, never give to vishaya). And then see what the result will be.

One who is not permanently in samadhi-dhana (the treasure of samadhi) — even the most shallow vishaya — cannot conquer the senses. But one who, when caught in the prison of vishaya-vikaras, makes the senses permanently absorbed in savikalpa-nirvikalpa samadhi — with immersion in samadhi, the moment the samadhi-nidhi is not broken even for a second, through the means of niyata dhyan-samadhi-nidhi's strong protection — when the indriya are in the full flow of vishaya, the moment the chitta becomes the vipayas-free, then too the nadi of samadhi-nidhi keeps flowing steadily.

**Simply Put:**
Indriya-jay cannot be achieved by heroic willpower alone. It requires samadhi — concentrated immersion in the atma. Without samadhi, even the most heroic fails. With samadhi-dhana, even the most powerful sensory attack is neutralized at the source.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my equivalent of samadhi in my actual practice — that concentrated stillness that, when present, makes everything else manageable? And what are the conditions that support it in my daily life?"

## Chapter Summary
**Chapter 7: Indriya-Jay** (Conquest of the Senses) is both a warning and a manual. Eight shlokas cover: the necessity of paurusha for moksha (Shloka 1), the swamp-nature of the senses (Shloka 2), the ocean that cannot be filled (Shloka 3), the senses as Moharaja's servants (Shloka 4), the deer and the mirage (Shloka 5), trishna that grows with feeding (Shloka 6), the five creatures killed by five senses (Shloka 7), and the final declaration — only samadhi achieves indriya-jay (Shloka 8).

Running shloka numbers: ॥१॥४९॥ through ॥८॥५६॥ (8 shlokas, book-wide count: 49–56)
