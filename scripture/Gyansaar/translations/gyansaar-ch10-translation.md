# Gyansaar — Chapter 10: Trupti (Contentment)
## Shlokas ॥१॥७३॥ through ॥८॥८०॥

---

### Overview
Chapter 10 explores **param trupti** — the ultimate contentment that cannot come from pudgala (matter/senses), only from the atma's own guna-sparsh. Eight shlokas map the journey from worldly hunger to the jnana-trupt, niranjana state of the muni who is "nitya sukhi."

**Four Movements:**
1. The Dirty Vessel & Sense-Hunger (1–2)
2. Shantarasa & Dream-Trupti (3–4)
3. Pudgal Cannot Satisfy Atma (5–6)
4. Jnana-Udgara & the Nitya Sukhi (7–8)

---

### Shloka 1 — ॥१॥७३॥
**Sanskrit:**
परिव्राजकानां भुक्त्वा ज्ञानागमं परम् |
तृप्तिः परमा ज्ञेया, भोजनपात्रवत् तथा ॥१॥७३॥

**Translation:**
Just as a food vessel must first be cleaned before receiving nourishment — so the seeker must first purify the vessel of the mind through jnana-agama (scripture-knowledge) before param trupti can fill it.

**Vivechan:**
The analogy of the **bhojana-patra** (food vessel): if the vessel is dirty and stained, even the finest food placed in it becomes contaminated. No amount of eating gives real nourishment. Similarly, a mind stained with vishaya-vasana cannot receive param trupti no matter how much it consumes. First clean the vessel through jnana and sadachar — then the true taste of contentment can enter.

---

### Shloka 2 — ॥२॥७४॥
**Sanskrit:**
रविमुनेः तृप्ति-भेदकाल-आत्मिना स्वरी |
इन्द्रियाणि न तृप्यन्ति, विषया नश्वरास्तथा ॥२॥७४॥

**Translation:**
The senses are never satisfied — they are by nature unquenchable. External beauty and fragrance are transient; first comes trupti, then immediately atrupti (dissatisfaction) follows.

**Vivechan:**
The rhythm of worldly experience: momentary pleasure followed by deeper hunger. Fragrance fades the moment you step away. Beauty dims the moment possession begins. The senses are structured to crave more — no vishaya can break this cycle because the senses themselves are instruments of atrupti. Only turning inward breaks the loop.

---

### Shloka 3 — ॥३॥७५॥
**Sanskrit:**
या शान्तरस-स्वादस्य, साहित्यदर्पण-मते |
षड्रसानाम् अतिशयी, सा तृप्तिः परमोच्यते ॥३॥७५॥

**Translation:**
The Sahityadarpan (classical treatise on aesthetics) calls shantarasa the supreme rasa, surpassing all six other rasas. That which is rooted in shantarasa — the taste of inner stillness — is called param trupti.

**Vivechan:**
Among the six rasas (sweet, sour, salty, bitter, pungent, astringent), shantarasa is described as: *"न यत्र दुःखं न सुखं न चिंता..."* — where there is no sorrow, no worldly joy, no anxiety. The atmosphere for shantarasa: vairagya, viveka, and inward absorption. Three upayas (means) for reaching param trupti: sat-charann-darshan (seeing the virtuous life), jina-vachan dhyan (meditating on the Jina's teaching), samyag-tatva pravesh (entering right principles).

---

### Shloka 4 — ॥४॥७६॥
**Sanskrit:**
संसारे स्वप्नवत् स्थित्या, तृप्तिः स्वप्नोपमा मता |
सदाचरण-दर्शनात् ज्ञानाद्, परमतृप्तिः प्राप्यते ॥४॥७६॥

**Translation:**
The worldly trupti is like a dream — the satisfaction found in samsara is dream-satisfaction. True param trupti is reached through sadacharana-darshana (vision of virtuous living) and jnana.

**Vivechan:**
Three upayas elaborated: (1) sad-charann darshan — witnessing great souls living the dharmic life; (2) jina-vachan dhyan — immersing in the Jina's words in deep meditation; (3) samyag-tatva pravesh — entering the tattvas with right understanding. Like waking from a dream, the jivatma who touches these means suddenly sees the unreality of samsaric trupti and recognizes the real.

---

### Shloka 5 — ॥५॥७७॥
**Sanskrit:**
पुद्गलैः पुद्गलस्तृप्तिः, आत्मतृप्तिः आत्मगुणैः |
जडात् चेतनतृप्तिः न, निश्चयनयेन जायते ॥५॥७७॥

**Translation:**
Pudgala (matter) can satisfy pudgala; but atma-trupti — the soul's contentment — can only come from the atma's own gunas (qualities). By the Nishchaya Naya (absolute standpoint), jada (inert matter) cannot give trupti to the conscious atma.

**Vivechan:**
The Nishchay Naya analogy: the soul's hunger is of a different order than the body's hunger. Even if you feed the body endlessly — the atma remains starved. Only atma-sparsh, touching one's own infinite qualities of jnana, darshan, ananda — can feed the atma. Jad pudgal is constitutionally incapable of giving atma-trupti.

---

### Shloka 6 — ॥६॥७८॥
**Sanskrit:**
मधुराज्यमहाशाका ग्राहो बाह्यो च गोरसात् |
परब्रह्मरिण तृप्तिर्यो जनास्तां जानतेऽपि न ॥६॥७८॥

**Translation:**
Beyond the sweetest kingdoms, beyond the finest external enjoyments (go-rasa / dairy delicacies) — there is a trupti in param brahma (the supreme self) that ordinary people do not even know exists.

**Vivechan:**
The world-immersed person (engaged in manohar-nirankush pleasures — sense-sovereignty, false praise, expectations) cannot comprehend param brahma trupti. They live in a mimicry of trupti — like someone eating dust thinking it's sugar. The Ramachandra story illustrates: Siteindra (king of devlok) descends from devlok and creates a paradise of sensory enchantment to distract Ramachandra from his samadhi. Ramachandra, already immersed in param brahma trupti, is completely unmoved. Siteindra finally surrenders in humility before such immovable contentment.

---

### Shloka 7 — ॥७॥७९॥
**Sanskrit:**
विषयोपनिषोदसार स्यात्तृणास्य पुदगले |
ज्ञानतस्तय तु ध्यानसमोद्गार-परम्परा ॥७॥७९॥

**Translation:**
From pudgala indulgence the "saar" (essence) obtained is mere tṛṇa — grass, worthless. But from jnana flows a continuous stream of dhyana-udgara — the supreme contentment of deep meditation.

**Vivechan:**
Dharmadhyana Saar quote: "viṣaya-kṣaya." From tyag of pudgala, shanta bhava arises and trupti deepens. Two contrasting stories: (1) A glutton invited to a raja's feast who broke all rules of conduct and ended up in great suffering. (2) Khadak Muni — soldiers tortured him, cut his flesh, his blood flowed — yet he was immersed in jnanamrit (the stream of dhyana-udgara) and did not flinch. His unwavering dhyan-sadhana advanced him from dharmadhyana to shukladhyana, then to kevalgyan. All karma-bonds (jnanavaran, darshanavaran, mohniya, antaraya) dissolved. Bhavsansar ended.

### Shloka 8 — ॥८॥८०॥ *(Hero Shloka)*
**Sanskrit:**
सलिलं विषयासन्ना, नेत्रोपेद्रास्योऽव्यहो |
भिक्षुरेक सुखी लोके, ज्ञानतृप्तो निरञ्जन ॥८॥८०॥

**Translation:**
Those who run after sense-objects find no true sukha — like eyes that are never content. Only the bhikshu (muni/renunciant) — jnana-trupt (content through knowledge) and niranjana (unstained) — is the one truly sukhi in the entire world.

**Vivechan:**
Henry story: a wealthy Western seeker arrives in India saying "I have everything — wealth, fame, health — but no peace." The greatest kings, rajas, and sahukars of the sansar are not truly sukhi. But Shri Umaswati declares: **"Bhikshurekah sukho loke"** — the muni alone is supremely happy. He quotes two shlokas: *"निजितमदमदनानां, वाक्कायमनोविकाररहितानाम् | विनिवृत्तपराशानामिहैव मोक्षः सुविहितानाम् ॥"* and *"स्वशरीरेऽपि न रज्यति, शत्राविपि न प्रदाषमुपयाति | रोगजरामरणभयेरब्यतितो यः स नित्यसुखी ॥"* — the muni who has conquered kama-mada, whose vikars of mind-speech-body are destroyed, free from all para-pudgala expectations, undisturbed by body-attachment, enemy-anger, disease, old age, or death — he is **nitya sukhi**, always-content. Jnana-trupt AND niranjana: these two conditions together make mahata sukhi.

---

### Chapter Summary
Param trupti is not found in more consumption — it is found in atma-sparsh. The eight shlokas build a complete map: clean the vessel first → senses can never satisfy → shantarasa is the highest rasa → worldly trupti is dream-trupti → pudgal cannot feed atma → param brahma trupti is unknown to the world-immersed → jnana-stream vs pudgal-grass → the niranjana, jnana-trupt muni is the one truly sukhi. The chapter ends with the great declaration: **"Bhikshurekah sukho loke."**
