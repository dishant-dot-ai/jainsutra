# Gyansaar Chapter 6: Sham (शम) — Equanimity / Inner Peace

**Chapter summary (from title page, page 79):**
Jnanis have peace (shanti). A nirmohi-amohi soul does not have vishay-vikar and does not have vikalpa. They have no interest in these. When the atma remains absorbed in its svabhavadasha (natural state) for most of the time, the jivatma receives jnana's true fruit.

Karm-janit (karma-born) distress never comes to jnanis' attention. They see all beings primarily in Brahma-svarupa (the soul's essential nature). All such souls constantly drink the amruta of shamras (the essence of equanimity) and become krutartha (fulfilled).

Come, let us evaluate Sham and Prasham's true meaning and its effect and become familiar with it. Trust: this will give life-marg a new direction and help understand one's own self and its impact!

---

## Shloka 1 — ॥१॥४१॥

**Sanskrit:**
विकल्पविषयोत्सीणं, स्वभायालम्बन सदा ।
ज्ञानत्य परिपाकों य स ते शम परिकीलित ॥१॥४१॥

**Translation:**
Free from vikalpas (mental fluctuations) and vishaya (sensory objects), always resting in the support of svabhava (one's own nature) — that maturation (paripaak) of Jnana is called Sham.

**Anvayartha:**
Sham is not an emotion or a mood — it is the ripening (paripaak) of Jnana. When knowledge matures, vikalpas fall away and the soul rests naturally in its own svabhava. That resting is Sham.

**Vivechan:**
No vikalpa is "bad" in itself — just as one cannot say "I am the supreme controller, I am sovereign, I am the most excellent, I am the best, I am the giver of dana, I am the tapasvi" — these too are vikalpas. Simply eliminate them. That alone is the key point. Vikalpa is simply unnecessary mental agitation, needless disturbance, ornamental-superficial cognition, gentle sensitivity of the senses, remaining absorbed in sweet beauty.

Jnana's real result (paripak) is the samata-pariNati-svastha state of consciousness. This is that atma's vigorous shuddha state — the svarup transformation. This is equanimity (samata). In this state, the jivatma can reach wherever it arrives — mountain, village, forest or city, day-time and night-time, at any place and any time — the samata arrives. When it gets this depth of immersion, it becomes filled with atmananda. It shines with pramod (delight), karuna (compassion), and madhyastha (equanimity) shades. It resolves the opposition self-concepts and any moment, any object's darkness of the world by clearing 'vishay-vishad': it maintains absolute, unchanging equanimity and its soul's state of self-rule proves to be a lamp of liberation.

The samata-yogi/samrambha-pariNati — jivatma is free from jad-jivit attachments; its samata-ras remains like a great ocean. When its samata is shaken for a moment by some external activity, it returns quickly. The shamras flows back. And after the flood, the tree-roots that were in the poisoning udvasana keep going deeper in the upasham-ras.

**Simply Put:**
Sham is not suppression — it's the natural state that emerges when knowledge truly matures. Vikalpas fall away not by force but by ripening. The soul rests in svabhava the way a river settles after the flood.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my experience of vikalpa — what unnecessary mental spinning do I engage in that is not my actual nature? What would it feel like to simply not need those thoughts?"

## Shloka 2 — ॥२॥४२॥

**Sanskrit:**
अनिचछ्न् कर्मबेधर्म्यं, ब्रह्मांशेन सभं जगत् ।
आत्माशेदेन य पश्येदसौ मोक्षंगमो शमी ॥२॥४२॥

**Translation:**
Not desiring the various distinctions made by karma; seeing the entire world through the Brahma-ansa (the soul-essence); seeing through the lens of atma-abheda (non-difference with the soul) — such a jivatma with sham becomes moksha-gami (bound for liberation).

**Anvayartha:**
Karma creates external distinctions — brahmin, shudra, Jain, learned, uneducated, beautiful, ugly. The shami (one with equanimity) sees through these distinctions to the underlying Brahma-svarupa. Seen through dravaiastik nay (substance-viewpoint), all paryayas (modes) have distinctions, but the dravyamsha (substance-aspect) is the same. No raga, no dvesha — the yogi plunges in shamras-sarovar and attains moksha in alpa-avadhi (short time).

**Vivechan:**
"This is a brahmin... this is a shudra... this is a Jain... this is educated... this is uneducated... this is beautiful" — such distinctions the shamras-submerged yogi does not experience. They consider the entire universe as Brahma-svarupa. They see the atma in chit-svarup with a sense of abheda (non-difference).

The yogi absorbed in shamras observes samsara with karma-chakshu (eye of karma) — they don't need to look at it at all. They simply see the darshan of the atma's pure svarupa. They know nothing of the universe apart from the atma.

Brahma has two aspects: dravya (substance) and paryaya (mode). The yogi, having identified the dravyamsha (substance-aspect) of Brahma, observes all of existence in tattva-svarupa. The atma's various samsaric conditions are paryaya-amshas. Humanity, animality, divinity, naraka, heaven, poverty — all are paryayas of the atma. There are distinctions in paryayamsha, but there is no distinction in dravyamsha. From the dravyastika naya perspective, there is no place for raga — no dvesha, envy, matsaradis — the yogi rises above these spiky thickets, plunges in shamras-sarovar and attains moksha in alpa-avadhi.

The Bhagavad Gita says (Ch. 5, Shloka 18): "Vidya-viveka-sampanne brahmaṇe gavi hastini... shunicha eva shvapake cha panditah samadarshina." — The learned one sees equally in the brahmin with knowledge, in the cow, in the elephant, in the dog, and in the outcaste. This is samadarshana — the same vision — which is shamras.

**Simply Put:**
The shami does not see the brahmin, the shudra, the beautiful, the ugly. They see the soul-substance in all. When the substance-lens replaces the mode-lens, raga and dvesha have nowhere to stand.

**Contemplate:**
"What categories do I habitually use to divide people — educated/uneducated, successful/unsuccessful, same-belief/different-belief? What would I see if I looked only at the dravya, the soul-substance, that all these paryayas share?"

## Shloka 3 — ॥३॥४३॥

**Sanskrit:**
धारेद्धगु नियोगं, धनेद् बाह्यिक्रियामिव ।
मोगाद्यं समाचार्य, शुध्यमान तक्षिप ॥३॥४३॥

**Translation:**
Even while in the outer world doing external actions, the one absorbed in samadhi-yoga becomes internally pure. In the midst of outer activity, the internal being remains pure and engaged in the highest practice.

**Anvayartha:**
Samadhi-yoga — the yogi who is absorbed in self-contemplation — does not stop outer activity. But in the midst of activity, their mind does not join the outer world. Internal purity remains unbroken.

**Vivechan:**
In the soul's inner chamber, the feeling to achieve samadhi-yoga should be born. By mental dhruvpathan (continuous recitation/anchoring), maukika dhruvpathan and vachanatmak bhavana, by removing the remaining vikalpas that keep appearing, and by cultivating shuddha samata-bhavana, one establishes siddhi of shamata-upasham.

Param-atm-shakti, pratiksaman, sadbhavna, praresna, darshanpathan, these types of texts give the jivatma heavenly dhyan when properly studied. The Everest-like ascent of dharma-dhyan, for which careful preparation is necessary, has a key point: the intense motivation to climb. The key: 'samadhi-yoga' — upon entry into this, strong motivation toward climbing naturally follows. And all of Gurudev's efforts in this are also naturally inherent. Param aarambha Acharya Maharaj in 'yogabindu' describes 'vachanatmak' upanushthan in this way: 'One who is firmly established in shastra-paddhati and opposing to others is the supreme activity of the sadhu: sarvochit-tapapravrtti.'

Do Everest-climbers who ascend the mountain not follow the instructions of their guide step by step? Do they not remain continuously active? Do they not become joyful doing things? Does their heart and devotion toward the guide (margdarshak) not develop? This very same jnana is necessary for climbing the gyan-samadhi-shikhar as well.

Once one reaches the summit of the samadhi-shikhar, the muniyan/yogi becomes truly inner-purified. They become pure through the upayoga alone (spiritual engagement). Then they reach the stage of 'asanga anushthan' — that which in Sankhya darshan is called 'pragant vahita', in Vedanta is called 'vivitsan-dakshaya-janvdargan', in 'Shivvallabh' and Jaindarshan is termed 'prasang anushthan'. To accomplish this, the support of shastra is not necessary. Rather just as nireem (cooling herbs) are mixed in water and given, in the same way anushthan becomes naturally present in them (the muni/yogi) and this anushthan, jinkalpas (without vikalpas), in all mahatmas without exception, must always be present.

**Simply Put:**
The samadhi-yogi does not withdraw from outer activity. But their mind remains anchored inward even while hands and feet are engaged outward. Like the Everest climber who follows the guide even in extreme conditions — inner connection maintained throughout.

**Contemplate:**
"Is there a form of activity in my life where I am fully engaged outward but inwardly still? What makes that different from activities where I lose myself completely in the external?"

## Shloka 4 — ॥४॥४४॥

**Sanskrit:**
ध्यानवृप्टेर्यानद्या शमपूरे प्रसर्पति ।
दिनांरतीरवृक्षाणां, सूलोत्तुर्मूलन भवेत् ॥४॥४४॥

**Translation:**
When the river of dhyan-vrushti (meditation-rain) flows and fills the shampur (flood of equanimity) — even the huge trees on the river banks that have been rooted in physical-worldly vasanas for beginningless time are uprooted and swept away.

**Anvayartha:**
Like a monsoon flood that uproots even the tallest trees from riverbanks, the flood of sham-filled meditation uproots even the deepest worldly attachments from the soul's banks. No matter how long-rooted, the flood sweeps them clean.

**Vivechan:**
Have you ever seen a flood from the Ganga-Yamuna or Brahmaputra? The tall trees swaying on the banks — when the flood comes, the proud trees rooted for ages on the bank are seen being uprooted instantly. When compassion/karuna-filled Saryu brings flood-waters, even the beginningless-time riverbank trees — the grand, sky-touching trees laden with worldly-paudgalik vishaya-vasanas — don't last long.

But when does a flood come in a river? When continuous rains (suhsala-var barish) happen. Exactly the same way: when the river of karuna/jivatma-daya slowly flows on atma-pradesh and on it continuously the flood of dharma-dhyan's rain falls — then too the flood of shamras comes and after this flood the udvas (trees of vasana) also don't last. The deeper the flood, the more effectively the tree-roots in upasham-ras are uprooted and swept downstream.

When the flood of karuna/jivatma's river of compassion flows, all living beings in the jivatma's mind become primary (sarvapratham jivatma's mind says to look at all beings for their welfare: 'In sansaar, all living beings should not be harmed; no being should suffer in any way' — such declaration should be made openly). Along with this, the dhyan-dhara must also be broadcast.

The third dhyan-gom — whose extremely beneficial consequence for the jivatma's purification is absolutely essential — the 'dhyan ishtaros system' — in the first, the 'do dhyan vichar' category is called dhyan. In the subject study, when this is the draft-dhyan, when the jnan-vishaya, sapaya vishaya, vipaaya vishaya and then sarvam vishaya fully permeates as dhyan — the 'purvasva shubhalakshmi' is the first bheda of 'paramdhyan'. Param Upayogi Shree Mayagiri Maharaj in his shastr mentioned this prakar of dhyan's lakshanis.

**Simply Put:**
The flood of meditation-rain sweeps away even the most ancient worldly attachments. No tree of vasana is too deep-rooted when the sham-flood comes with full force. The depth of the flood determines the completeness of the uprooting.

**Contemplate:**
"What are the deepest-rooted trees of vasana in my life — the oldest attachments, the most ancient habitual patterns? What would a true flood of dharma-dhyan feel like washing over them?"

## Shloka 5 — ॥५॥४५॥

**Sanskrit:**
सुविविद्यज्ञास्समाको निस्सगो निर्भओ निरासो अ ।
बेरंगाभाविवमणो भाछूमि सुतिच्छलो होई ॥५॥४५॥

**Translation:**
One who knows the world-appearance (jagat-sansar) well — is without attachment (nissanga), fearless (nirbhaya), without expectations (nirasha) — their mind is free from any trace of bhaav-raga (emotional coloring). Such a great soul, immersed in dharma-yoga, remains inwardly unshaken/stable.

**Anvayartha:**
Four qualities define the shami: nissanga (unattached), nirbhaya (fearless), nirasha (without craving), bhaava-nirlipti (emotionally uncolored). These are not practiced — they are the natural state of one who truly sees samsara as it is.

**Vivechan:**
In the soul's inner chamber where there is only pure contemplation — unperturbed, nishchinta (worry-free), nirbhaya (fearless), nirasha (expectation-free) — one who knows the appearance of the world and is in this state truly is, and their great soul's mind can remain stable/unshaken even among worldly matters.

The third shami is one whose dhyan-gom is inner, whose extremely accelerating consequence for the jivatma's purification is absolutely essential. 'Dhyan ishi' — in the subject of this, the 'do dhyan vichar' category is called dhyan. In this subject study, this becomes the draft dhyan. When jnana-vishaya, sapaya vishaya, vipaya vishaya and ultimately sarvam vishaya permeates as dhyan — the 'purvasva shubhalakshmi' is the first bheda of paramdhyan. Param Upayogi Shree Mayagiri Maharaj in his shastra provided this explanation of dhyan's lakshanas.

**Simply Put:**
Four qualities — unattached, fearless, expectation-free, emotionally uncolored — are not achieved by discipline alone. They are what remains naturally when the soul truly sees through the appearance of the world. Sham is the container. These are the contents.

**Contemplate:**
"Of these four — nissanga, nirbhaya, nirasha, nirlipti — which feels most distant from my current state? What one thing, if I truly released it, might make all four accessible?"

## Shloka 6 — ॥६॥४६॥

**Sanskrit:**
शांत प्पाय तव शोल-सम्यक्तासहितोप्पहो ।
त नाप्ओति गुण सागुयसाप्नोति समाजित ॥६॥४६॥

**Translation:**
If the jivatma does not have sham — even if there is sheel (conduct), samyaktva (right vision), and tapa (austerity) — then the same path that a renunciant-sadhu also walks on is not obtained. Only with sham does the sadhu reach the supreme path.

**Anvayartha:**
Sham is not one virtue among many — it is the one that makes all other virtues operative. Sheel, samyaktva, tapa without sham are incomplete. Sham is the carrier that delivers all virtues to their destination.

**Vivechan:**
If there is tapa, if there is nirodha (restraint) of vishaya-vasanas, brahmacharya of all eight kinds is observed, if there is firm trust in Jina-taught vachan and siddhants, yet if there is no place for 'sham' in the jivatma's life, if there is no trace of the thing called samata, if there is no kala (art) of understanding the entire world with dravaiastik nay as rag-dvesharahit pure chaitanya-svarupa — then all that is done is useless (vyartha). The soul's pure-infinite-unlimited jnana-maya svarupa cannot emerge. It does not attain purnatva from all perspectives.

Bhagavan Umasvati says in Shree 'Prashamrati': "Samyagdrishtijaano virita-tapa-lobhaynto'pyanupashantah | Teh na labhate gunam yam prashamaguramupashito labhate ||२४३||"

He who is himself a samyak-drashti yet considers others mithyatvi, considers himself jnani yet mocks others, considers himself shravak or shraman yet considers others mohangha, is tapasvi yet considers others non-tapasvi and looks at them with contempt — such a person's chitta is filled with krodha (anger), mana (pride), maya (deceit), and sprha (craving). They are kos (distances) away from kevalajnana.

Four munis who observed nirajal-nirahara fasting for four months, on Samvatsari day expressed disgust-bhava toward 'Kurgantu muni' (who ate that day) — the result: the destination of kevalajnana moved further away. But the upasham-rup shant jalaashay-absorbed 'Kurgantu muni' became an adhikari of kevalajnana.

The tapasviri who continuously performed tapa without rest, who lived equanimously in various forests facing countless hardships — Bahubali — what knowledge was lacking in them? Was there no dharma-dhyan? Were they not with tapa or sheel? They had everything. But they only lacked upasham-vrtti. Upashamras was absent in them. Thus the jyoti of kevalajnana could not ignite. But the moment upasham-vrtti appeared, kevalajnana-jyot blazed without delay.

**Simply Put:**
Sham is the one quality that makes all other virtues — tapa, sheel, samyaktva — deliver their fruit. Without sham, even the most impressive sadhana is incomplete. The story of Bahubali: everything was present, kevalajnana was absent — until the single missing ingredient arrived: upasham.

**Contemplate:**
"Do I practice virtues (whatever I practice — honesty, discipline, generosity) with inner equanimity, or do I hold them as proof of my superiority over others? The moment a virtue becomes a source of pride, it has lost its sham."

## Shloka 7 — ॥७॥४७॥

**Sanskrit:**
शमसूषतसुभासिक्तं येषां नक्तं दिनं मनः ।
कदाऽपि ते न दह्यन्ते, रागोरगविषोऽमिभः ॥७॥४७॥

**Translation:**
Those whose mind is day and night immersed in the auspicious nectar of sham — even the serpent-venom of raga (attachment) cannot burn them. They are never burned.

**Anvayartha:**
The mind constantly anointed with shamras becomes immune to the poison of raga-dvesha. Like a snake's bite that cannot harm one protected by a strong antidote, the poison of raga cannot burn the shami.

**Vivechan:**
The atma sprinkled with shamras, immersed in various shastra-granths, dharma-katha, and subhashitas — in it, even accidentally, the poisonous wave of raga-farishaghar (raga's snake) cannot spread. The one who daily (nitya prati) is engaged in upasham-filled text study and mannan — in their mind, the attachment (asakti), rati (pleasure), and sneha (love) of physical/material things cannot surface. Mahamuni Sthulibhadra, before a female companion in private, sixteen-ornamented, dancing — she swayed her captivating eyes and enchanting body in full allure. But Sthulibhadra did not waver even for kshanardha (half a moment). He remained anchored in dhyana-yoga. How was this possible? Only due to the tatlinta (absorption) in upashamras-yukta shastra-parishilan. After months of taking poison-laced food, not a single arrow of madan (Cupid) could pierce him. Why? Because his hands and mouth were engaged in eating, but his mind-brain was immersed in the lake of samata-yoga.

When the senses are absorbed in their own vishaya-objects, and the mind does not join them but remains absorbed in the paribhavana of upashamras — then everything becomes easy. Raga-dvesha cannot even bend a hair. Therefore: first join your own mind to the constant study-mannan of upashampopak dharma-texts. Then, cut off the contact of the senses with vishaya-vikaras that they find priya (dear). Even forcefully, if needed. Because that force from behind...

**Simply Put:**
The mind drenched in shamras becomes immune to raga-venom. Sthulibhadra's story: months of poison-laced food, a dancing woman in private — not a single wave of reaction. Not through suppression — through complete absorption in shamras.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my equivalent of Sthulibhadra's test? What situation most predictably triggers raga or dvesha in me? And what would it look like to be so absorbed in shamras that even that situation leaves no trace?"

## Shloka 8 — ॥८॥४८॥

**Sanskrit:**
स्वयभूरमणसयद्धि, क्षिण्यशनतारस ।
मुनियलोगसमोहेत, कौणि तारौ चराचरे ॥८॥४८॥

**Translation:**
The muni who has acquired the power of svayambhuramana (self-sovereign), whose gana is fully purified, is equal in this entire universe (cara-acara) — wherever they wander, they remain always steadily peaceful, never shaken by anything.

**Anvayartha:**
Svayambhuramana — the self-sovereign who derives bliss only from the self, not from any external source. Such a muni, equal to all moving and non-moving beings, remains steady and peaceful always. This is the destination of Sham: a sovereignty that needs nothing outside itself.

**Vivechan:**
"Munirajya" — what a beautiful name! Whose kingdom is most beautiful and most attractive? Their vast kingdom is surveyed with deepest attention and pranam. Sham, upasham, samata is their greatest attractive quality and protection and guarding is done with great vigilance. They do not allow raga-dvesha to enter no matter how high the current of pravrttis. Such supernatural force — such overpowering force — is theirs.

Just as Upananb's name, extraordinary force-powerful is asserted: their empire is heartfelt and extraordinary. With these two weapons (hriday and ashvadal), with their image, they are protected. And by their rishitva — their heart dhyan spreading and encompassing jnana and dhyan with Dashvadal's force — on the basis of their hriday's force, their samata samazya achieves supreme shanti, music, victory...

The muni who, having climbed to the summit of the samadhi-shikhar, overcomes false body-consciousness, becomes truly inner-liberated: from the gross and subtle world, moving through all objects, the munirajya of samata becomes established. And the muni who has mastered sham, by the strength of jnana-dhyan, can easily protect samata-yoga. And through jnana-dhyan alone, yogijans can fulfill the role of samata-yoga.

Muni-life — how supremely beautiful, enchanting, and profoundly admirable! Upadhyayaji Maharaj has put before us such a picture that the mind spontaneously wonders. The muni, anointed on the royal throne with 'May Munirajya's kingdom be eternally victorious!' has been issued the rallying cry. Then with loving touch in two voices, the immortal message has been given: "Muniraj! You have now become a king. The one and only strong, powerful emperor of your upasham-rajya! Protect and guard it with vigilance, never letting the mind forget it." And when the muni is distressed, troubled, frightened — then, bringing a gentle smile to the face, it says: "My king! Being frightened and troubled like this will not do. You have all powerful armies and inexhaustible weapon-treasury. Then why fear and anxiety? You are hriday and ashvadal, jnana and dhyan's sole overlord, powerful commander! With hriday's sky-piercing arrow-shower, all enemies... the arrows of raga-dvesha will escape and even mocha-obstructing barriers, piercing through, cannot advance one step — resulting, mount the noble horses of ashvadal, remain nishchinta, play sports."

**Simply Put:**
The muni who has cultivated Sham becomes Munirajya — sovereign of the inner kingdom of equanimity. Nothing external can disturb this kingdom. The teaching: you have all the weapons you need. Raga-dvesha cannot enter. Remain nishchinta and play sports.

**Contemplate:**
"What would it feel like to be the Munirajya — sovereign of your own inner kingdom? Not having to protect yourself from external disturbances because nothing can genuinely disturb you? This is not indifference — it is the supreme samata that loves all, fears nothing."

## Chapter Summary
**Chapter 6: Sham** reveals that equanimity is not a mood or a discipline — it is the natural ripening (paripaak) of jnana. Eight shlokas move through the definition of sham (Shloka 1), the equal vision of all through the soul-substance lens (Shloka 2), the samadhi-yogi's outer activity with inner stillness (Shloka 3), the flood analogy that uproots all vasanas (Shloka 4), the four qualities of the shami — unattached, fearless, expectation-free, emotionally uncolored (Shloka 5), the indispensability of sham for all other virtues including the story of Bahubali (Shloka 6), the immunity to raga-venom and Sthulibhadra's story (Shloka 7), and the final declaration of Munirajya — the soul's inner sovereignty (Shloka 8).

Running shloka numbers: ॥१॥४१॥ through ॥८॥४८॥ (8 shlokas, book-wide count: 41–48)
