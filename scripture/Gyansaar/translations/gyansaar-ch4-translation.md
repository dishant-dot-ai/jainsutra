# Gyansaar — Chapter 4: Amoha (Non-Delusion)
## Translation: Shlokas 1–8

**Scripture:** Gyansaar (The Essence of Knowledge)
**Author:** Nyayacharya Nyayavisharad Mahopadhyay Shri Yashovijayji
**Commentary (Vivechan):** Pannyaspravara Shri Bhadraguptavijayji Ganivar
**Chapter:** 4 — Amoha (अमोह / Non-Delusion / Freedom from Moha)
**Theme:** When the body and mind are steady and the soul is fully absorbed in its own nature, the destruction of moha (delusion) follows as a natural consequence — and with it, inexhaustible joy.

---

## SHLOKA 1

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अह मंगेति मन्नोड्य मोहेड्य जगदास्यकुतू ।
धर्थमेयं ही मञ्चूय प्रतिमम्-नोड्पि मोहुजित ॥१॥२५॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Moha (delusion) is conquered when the soul knows itself clearly as what it truly is: "I am Moha's weapon of destruction — for I am pure soul-substance." To conquer moha is, undoubtedly, the highest of all human purposes.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens with a declaration of war — but war of a particular kind. Moha-raja (King Delusion) has spread his mayajal (net of illusion) over the entire universe. He has 21 primary weapons: ajnana (ignorance), asanyam (lack of restraint), lack of austerity, six leshas (soul-colorings), four kashaya (passions), three vedas, four gatis (states of existence), and mithyatva (wrong belief). All 21 work together to ensnare the soul. The soul that is not alert, even in moments of apparent spiritual development, will find itself caught.

The vivechan title-box introduces the chapter with force: *"Once body and mind become steady and fully absorbed in atma-bhava — understand this: the death of moha is certain, beyond all doubt. From the steadiness of mind-speech-body, a-moha (the state of non-delusion) arises naturally. Therefore, do not worry at all about the magical attacks of moha!"*

The antidote to moha is not struggle but recognition. The moment the soul turns the weapon of true knowledge upon moha — the weapon of dana (giving), labhopabhog (proper use of resources), virya (energy), mati-jnana, shrut-jnana, and avadhi-jnana — moha's traps begin to fail. The key is: do not become vashibhut (enslaved) to it. Stay at a distance. Moha's attempts will tire and eventually cease.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Moha's defeat is certain — once the soul becomes truly steady and absorbed in its own nature. The soul that knows itself clearly has already found moha's greatest adversary: self-knowledge. Use it.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What is the strongest current of moha in your life right now — the attachment or identification that most pulls you away from your own center? What would it look like to simply not be enslaved by it, rather than fighting it directly?

## SHLOKA 2

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

शुद्धात्मद्रव्यमेवाहं शुद्धज्ञानं गुणो मम ।
नाण्योऽहं न ममान्ये चेत्यहो मोहास्त्रमुल्वणम् ॥२॥२६॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I am only pure soul-substance. Pure knowledge alone is my permanent quality. I am not other than this, and nothing else belongs to me." — This contemplation (bhavana) is the great weapon that tears apart the net of moha.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**शुद्धात्मद्रव्यम्** = pure soul-substance; **एवाहम्** = I am only this; **शुद्धज्ञानम्** = pure knowledge; **गुणो मम** = is my quality; **न अन्यो अहम्** = I am not other than this; **न मम अन्ये** = nothing else is mine; **चेति** = thus (contemplating); **मोहास्त्रम्** = the weapon against moha; **उल्वणम्** = most powerful/sharp.

### 4. COMMENTARY

This shloka gives the precise tool for dismantling moha: the "Naho-na mam" mantra — "Not I...not mine." The vivechan develops this with striking detail:

Moha's given mantra to the soul is "aham-mam" — "I and mine." It is the mantra of false identification. The soul has been chanting this mantra since beginningless time. Every atom of the body is saturated with it. To simply stop — even when one wishes to — feels impossible. The vivechan presents a dialogue: "But how can I forget it? I have been chanting this mantra since anadi-kala (beginningless time). It has seeped into my every cell. Even when I want to forget it, I cannot."

The charitra-maharaj (the voice of right conduct) gives a new mantra in response: "Naho-na mam: not I, not mine." And then gives the full vivid substitution — "I am not wealthy, not handsome, not a father, not a mother, not a human, not a guru, not humble, not a body-owner, not powerful, not a ruler, not a lawyer, not a doctor, not an actor. Then who am I? I am simply one pure soul-substance."

World-possessions are not mine. Parents are not mine. Sons and daughters are not mine. Power is not mine. Wealth is not mine. Relatives are not mine. Ridhi-siddhis (extraordinary powers) are not mine. "Then what is mine? Pure knowledge — kevalajnana — is mine. I am not separate from it. Rather, from all angles I am identified with it."

This bhavana/thought-current is the amogh-shastra — the infallible weapon — that cuts through moha. As love for atma-tattva grows, the bonds of paudgala-love correspondingly break. The soul's purpose: love atma-tattva, remain distanced from paudgala-tattva. The result: as atma-tattva love grows, paudgala-love's bonds break proportionally.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The antidote to moha's mantra of "I and mine" is the replacement mantra: "Not I, not mine." I am pure soul-substance. Pure knowledge is my permanent quality. Everything else — body, family, wealth, status — is not me and not mine. This contemplation, practiced fully, tears apart the net of delusion.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Try for just one minute: "I am not my body, not my name, not my relationships, not my achievements. I am pure consciousness — the knowing that is aware of all these things." What shifts when you rest in that? What remains?

## SHLOKA 3

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

यो न मुञ्चित संसेमेषु भावेषोद्रिकमादिषु ।
प्राकाभिमिय पद्मोपि, नाऽमूढ: पार्श्वेन लिप्यते ॥३॥२७॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The soul that does not abandon its own natural bliss — even while engaged in the various states of worldly existence — remains like a lotus in the mud: present, but untouched. That non-deluded soul is not tainted by what surrounds it.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The vivechan opens with a question: does moha have a home? Yes. Wherever the soul is habituated to moha-maya from beginningless time — wherever countless jivat-mas roam in its mayajal without finding a way out — these are all moh-raja's citizens, playing their roles in his realm. They act out birth, old age, and death as the daily drama of his city. And yet: those with viveka (discernment) can live in the city without being of it.

The lotus metaphor is precise: the lotus grows in mud. It is rooted in it. Yet it rises above the water, blooms pure and fragrant, untouched by the mud that gave it birth. The non-deluded soul is exactly this. It lives in the world — in family, in activity, in circumstance — and yet it is not of the world. Its roots are not in the world. Its roots are in its own nature.

The vivechan asks: what keeps the lotus above the mud? Its very nature — the stem that holds it upright. For the soul, this upright stem is the continuous practice of atma-svarup bhavana (contemplation of one's own true nature). As long as that contemplation remains alive, moha's mud cannot climb up and cover the bloom.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Like a lotus that grows in mud yet stays untouched by it, the soul of genuine non-delusion lives fully in the world without being enslaved by it. The mud is still there. But the lotus is not the mud.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of a time you were in a difficult, chaotic, or morally compromised environment — and something in you remained clear and uncontaminated. What was that quality? Could it be cultivated more deliberately?

## SHLOKA 4

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

पश्यन्नेव परद्रव्य-नाटकं प्रतियाटकम् ।
भवचक्रपुरस्थोऽपि, नाऽमूढ: परिखिद्यते ॥४॥२८॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Even while watching the play of external-substance (paradravya) — the endless drama of birth-cycle unfolding scene by scene — the non-deluded soul, though dwelling in the city of the existence-cycle, is not distressed.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The vivechan develops the natak (play/theater) metaphor at length. Samsara is described as a magnificent and ancient theater, with moh-raja as its director. The theater has many players — humans, animals, devas (celestial beings) — and multiple venues: ashram, house, courtyard, temple, distant lands. All of these are natak-bhumi (theater-grounds) where the various acts of this drama are staged. And moh-raja himself remains backstage as director, constantly managing the production.

The key observation: when someone comes to the theater for a performance, they know they are watching a play. The actor on stage pretends to die — the audience does not begin to mourn in earnest. They know it is not real. Only small children and the naive think the stage deaths are actual deaths.

The non-deluded soul is the knowing audience member. It sees paradravya — the body, external possessions, relationships, circumstances — as the natak they are: the karmo-day-scripted performance of this particular lifetime. It watches with full clarity and without distress. The soul knows: "I have never actually been born into this theater. I am the awareness watching it. The drama belongs to moh-raja's city. It does not belong to me."

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Samsara is a theater. The non-deluded soul watches the entire drama — birth, relationships, circumstances, losses — without being overwhelmed by it. Not because it doesn't care, but because it knows what the drama actually is.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Can you identify one recurring drama in your life — a pattern that keeps replaying, a conflict or loss that keeps returning? What would it look like to watch it with clear eyes as a performance rather than as ultimate reality?

## SHLOKA 5

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

विकल्पचक्रेरात्मा, पौत्तमोहाऽऽसयोर्हाधम् ।
भवोच्चतालमृत्ताल-प्रपञ्चमवितपठति ॥५॥२९॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The soul entangled in the wheel of vikalpa (mental fabrications) — spinning in the intoxication of poudgalik moha — endlessly recites the elaborate drama of worldly existence, rise after rise and fall after fall.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The vivechan paints a vivid picture of the moha-intoxicated soul. The vikalpa-chakra (wheel of mental fabrications) spins constantly: "Go to the market, open a shop, do business with influential people, accumulate property, build mansions and bungalows — become rich! Boast to the world!" And so on. The lamp of sankalpa (intention) generates vikalpa (distorted intention) the moment it is lit — like a flame that quickly produces smoke. The lamp flickers and goes out. But behind it, the smoke remains — not one but many. And from that smoke, the inner home becomes smoky, dim, and foggy.

The craving for wealth generates, in its wake, countless sinful thought-trains. The craving for fame is even more dangerous. The moment the sankalpа "May I become a minister or hold the highest position in the nation" is formed, the army of vikalpa follows uninvited. Vikalpa asks "But how?" — and the answer is: "Win elections, water down the competition, form shady alliances, bribe voters..." — all the while using violence, lies, and other ashravas (sinful influxes) without hesitation. The person who reaches that position is not satisfied — rather they become a madman, thoroughly bound in paps (sins).

The conclusion: all of this vikalpa-intoxication is moha's brew. As long as the soul remains trapped in it, it cannot rest in the nirvikar jnanananda (undistorted bliss of pure knowledge). And the only way out of it is not suppression but substitution: replace poudgalik vikalpa with atma-vikalpa — replace material fantasy with the contemplation of the soul's own pure nature.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The wheel of mental fabrications — the endless planning, desiring, scheming, comparing — is moha's intoxicating drink. As long as the soul remains drunk on it, pure inner stillness is impossible. The way out is not to fight vikalpa but to replace it with the contemplation of one's own pure nature.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

How many thoughts in a typical day are driven by wanting, planning, comparing, or scheming? What would an hour without any of those thoughts feel like? What remains in the mind when desire-driven thought quiets down?

## SHLOKA 6

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

निमल स्फटिकस्येव सहज रपमासनत् ।
अव्यस्तोपाधिसम्बन्धो जड़रतना विमुद्राति ॥६॥३०॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Like pure crystal — naturally radiant from within — the soul unstained by false superimpositions shines with the undisturbed clarity of its own genuine nature.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The crystal analogy is one of the most celebrated in the Gyansaar's vivechan. A pure crystal has no color of its own. But place a red object behind it and it appears red; a blue object and it appears blue. The crystal is not red. The crystal is not blue. The crystal is crystal — transparent, colorless, intrinsically luminous. Yet it seems to take on every color it is placed near.

The vivechan asks: what is the true color of a human being? Not black or white, not beautiful or ugly — these are all external forms (bahya-rup), guna-matra (mere qualities), the shadow of karma. The soul in its true nature is like sphatika-ratna (crystal-gem): transparent, pure, and vimals (utterly clean). Its apparent color is karma's shadow — the reflection of what surrounds it — not its actual nature.

This understanding transforms perception. When the soul begins seeing itself through the mirror of jnana-atmasvarup (the knowledge-mirror of one's own true nature) — when the five acharas (right knowledge, right perception, right conduct, right austerity, right energy) are reflected in this mirror — the soul recognizes the incomparable beauty of its own nature. It becomes utterly absorbed (aakanth dub, unmatta). And the result: external objects appear tasteless (niras), colorless (swadhin), uncompelling. Moha-bhavana toward them becomes impossible. Because the soul has found something infinitely more beautiful to behold — its own nature.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The soul is like pure crystal — naturally radiant, with no inherent color of its own. What we think of as its qualities — beautiful or ugly, good or bad — are karma's reflections, not the soul's actual nature. To know this is to begin the process of seeing clearly.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of yourself not as a collection of qualities, experiences, and history — but as the clear awareness that is aware of all of those things. What is that awareness like? Does it have any color of its own, any inherent flaw, any size or shape?

## SHLOKA 7

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अनारोपसुखं मोहेत्यागादनुभवन्नपि ।
भ्रारोपप्रियलोकेषु यस्तुमाऽचर्येवान् भवेत् ॥७॥३१॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The yogi who experiences the natural bliss of non-superimposition (aropa-mukta sukha) — freed from moha — while remaining among those who still love superimposition, is indeed a source of wonder (acharyavan) to the world.

### 3. COMMENTARY

This shloka addresses the paradox of the realized soul in the world. The Vitarag-dev (liberated being) has proclaimed the yogamarg (path of yoga) — and the yogi who has advanced on this path, who has made moh's kshaaya-upsham (reduction and pacification) operative, who has begun experiencing the svabhavika (natural, karma-unmixed) bliss of the soul — when such a person stands before ordinary people who are still embedded in moha-niya karma, what should they say?

The vivechan poses the problem honestly: such a person cannot speak about their natural bliss, because those who hear them don't have a reference point. What they call "sukh" (pleasure) is actually just moha's shadow — and to call it "sukh" would be to validate a false category. So they find themselves in an odd silence: unable to fully explain, unable to dismiss. They become a question to the world — an acharyavan (source of wonder). Their very presence raises the question of what they know.

The vivechan's conclusion: the presence of such a person — who has tasted the freedom of amoha, who lives naturally in the crystal clarity of their own soul — is itself the teaching. Words are insufficient. But the soul that has been freed from moha's wheel becomes, by its very existence, a demonstration of what is possible.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The person who has genuinely moved beyond moha lives with a bliss that those still in moha cannot fully comprehend. They become a source of wonder — not because they try to, but because their freedom is visible and inexplicable to those who have not tasted it.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever met someone whose inner freedom or equanimity in difficult circumstances left you genuinely puzzled — not just impressed, but wondering "how is that possible"? What quality were you sensing in them?

## SHLOKA 8

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

यश्चिद्यणिव-यत्त-सामंसाऽऽचारधारपी ।
स य नाम स परहब्बे अनुयोगिनि मुद्राति ॥८॥३२॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one who is fully established in the acharas (right modes of conduct) — who holds steadfast to them with body, speech, and mind aligned — that person alone truly shines among those who follow the inner path.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The closing shloka is a devotional seal and a practical summary. The vivechan pivots to the five acharas as the practical expressions of amoha: jnana-achara (right engagement with knowledge), darshana-achara (right engagement with perception), charitra-achara (right engagement with conduct), tapa-achara (right engagement with austerity), and virya-achara (right engagement with energy). These five are described as the anupam soundarya (incomparable beauty) of the soul's interior — the facets through which the crystal-soul displays its luminosity.

When these five acharas grow in practice, the soul begins to perceive its own beauty in the mirror of jnana-atmasvarup. And as it sees itself more clearly — as it becomes more absorbed in its own interior beauty — its craving for paradravya (external substances) proportionally decreases. The bond of moha-bhavana toward external things loosens naturally. Not through suppression, not through force, but because the soul has found something infinitely more compelling to behold: itself.

This is the final message of the chapter: Amoha is not a withdrawal from the world. It is the natural consequence of self-knowing. When the soul knows what it is — crystal-pure, naturally radiant, bearing five acharas as its interior beauty — it no longer needs the world to define it. And paradoxically, freed from that need, it can engage with the world far more generously than before.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The person fully established in the five right modes of conduct — knowledge, perception, conduct, austerity, energy — shines with a beauty that comes from within. As that inner beauty is increasingly recognized, the pull of external things proportionally loosens.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Of the five acharas — right knowledge, right perception, right conduct, right austerity, right energy — which one, if cultivated more deeply in your life, would make the greatest difference? What would that cultivation actually look like in practice?

**Translation Notes:**
- Chapter 4 covers Amoha (Non-Delusion) — the natural dissolution of moha that follows genuine steadiness and absorption. The sequence is logical: Purnata establishes completeness, Magnata cultivates absorption, Sthirata makes it stable, and Amoha names what falls away when stability is genuine: the fundamental delusion that the soul is anything other than pure consciousness.
- Key vivechan analogies: the lotus in mud, the crystal-gem (sphatika), the theater of samsara (natak), moha's city (bhav-chakra-pur), the "Naho-na mam" mantra vs. the "Aham-mam" mantra.
- The chapter has 8 shlokas (running numbers ॥१॥२५॥ through ॥८॥३२॥).
