### Chapter Introduction (Title Page — PDF p.102)

**Chapter Name:** ८. त्याग (Tyag — Renunciation)

The introduction distinguishes outer renunciation from inner: renouncing a worldly object is not significant. What matters is *from what disposition* and *toward what understanding* one renounces. The jnani-mahatmas introduced here re-introduce the seeker to new divine parents, siblings, and companions — and invite a deeper relation with them. For the indriyas-conqueror, it is easy to renounce worldly dear ones — simply meet your new divine family first.

---

### Shloka 1 — ॥१॥५७॥

**Sanskrit:**
संयतात्मा श्रये शुद्धोपयोगं दितरं निक्षम् ।
सृतिमम्बां च पितरो, तन्मां विगृहतां प्रूषम् ॥१॥५७॥

**Translation:**
The disciplined soul takes refuge in shuddhopayoga (pure conscious engagement) as the highest father-principle, and in samsara-crossing-mata (the mother who crosses over samsara) — this is the family that truly nourishes me.

**Anvayartha:**
"Atmarati" is the true mother — the one whose company brings steadiness and joy in the atma. "Shuddha-atmajnana" is the true father — the one who illumines the true path. The author invites: abandon the material-emotional attachment to parents who bind; establish a relationship with these inner-divine parents who liberate.

**Vivechan:**
The shloka does not ask for coldness toward parents — it asks for a transformation of *what we call family*. The worldly parents love us with moha-sneha that keeps us circling in samsara. But atmarati and shuddhajnana are parents who always inspire toward mukti. Pray to your worldly parents for liberation — bowing to them, fall at their feet, ask their blessing for moksha-marg, offer yourself to these inner parents entirely.

**Simply Put:**
Your real mother is Atmarati — the taste of resting in yourself. Your real father is Shuddha-Atmajnana — pure self-knowing. Until you find these parents, the family you think you have is a beautiful cage.

**Contemplate:**
What would it mean to truly be nourished by atmarati? When did you last feel your soul as home?

**Concept Tags:** Shuddhopayog · Atmarati · Samsara-crossing · Divine parentage · Tyag as re-orientation

### Shloka 2 — ॥२॥५८॥

**Sanskrit:**
सुरमत्र सांगोपनादिबंधधोऽनिमतात्मनाम् ।
इबेंकरूपाम् शीलादिव्युनिश्मुतां धये ॥२॥५८॥

**Translation:**
Renouncing the bondage of worldly associations (mata-pita, pati-patni, bhai-bahan, snehijano), now embrace as companion/family: shil, satya, dharma-upasham, santosh — the eternal, infinite, self-illuminating virtues.

**Anvayartha:**
In the worldly family, even the purest love (sneha-vatsalya) ultimately chains the soul to moha. The author-jnani here bids farewell to previous-birth associates and declares: now I accept Shil, Satya, Dharma, Upasham, Santosh as my true family (bandhu). These virtues are with me at every moment — in every birth — never abandoning, never betraying.

**Vivechan:**
Samata is my one and only priyatama (beloved). I have been unfaithful to her my entire life. I went to Mamata-veshya, Trishna, Khumti — they promised pleasure but looted me every time, dishonored me, threw me out. Yet when I recovered somewhat, I rushed back again. But Samata — always waiting, infinitely sushil, pativrata — her company gives true peace, apaar sukh, and boundless prasannata. The real brothers are: those who carry mani-vachan-kaya to moksha, who are always raga-free, who are the true benefactors of all jivas.

**Simply Put:**
The worldly sneha-svajano can never be in selfless un-tied love with you. But Shil, Satya, Samata, Dharma — these are your real siblings and beloved. They never leave, never betray.

**Contemplate:**
Who is your true beloved? Samata or Mamata?

**Concept Tags:** Shil · Satya · Samata · Sneha vs. Dharma-bandhu · Priyatama analogy

### Shloka 3 — ॥३॥५९॥

**Sanskrit:**
धर्मस्त्याच्यया , सुतमोत्स्मा , क्षायोपशमिका अपि ।
प्राप्य चंदयण-चांभ, धमसन्यासमुरानम् ॥३॥५९॥

**Translation:**
Even kshaayopashamik dharma, santosh-type expressions — even these must be renounced (dharma-sanyasa) at the highest stage. Through dharma-sanyasa, the pure path (param-pavitra yog) of the highest order is attained.

**Anvayartha:**
Sansarg (company) with jivnatma generates kshayopashamik bhaav — from the contact of pious dev-guru, the mithyatva, kashaya, ajnan, asanyam are purged and the atma shines with samyakta, samyag-jnana, sanyam. These kshaayopashamik dharmas are necessary — but only until kshaayik gunas are attained. Kshaayik guna is the atma's true svarup. When that manifests, kshaayopashamik dharmas are naturally left behind — like a ladder abandoned once the rooftop is reached.

**Vivechan:**
Dharma-sanyasa does not mean abandoning dharma — it means transcending the supportive-scaffolding dharma when you have reached its destination. Like sandal-wood's fragrance is its permanent bhav, so kshaayik dharma is the atma's sthayibhav. Kshaayopashamik dharma is the porch to reach the inner palace of kshaayik bhav. Once inside, the porch is left. This is the true meaning of dharma-sanyasa.

**Simply Put:**
You need the ladder to reach the roof. But once you're on the roof, you don't stand on the ladder anymore. Dharma-sanyasa means you've arrived — not that you've abandoned dharma.

**Contemplate:**
Are you clinging to the ladder after reaching the roof? Or have you truly stepped inside?

**Concept Tags:** Dharma-sanyasa · Kshaayopashamik vs. Kshaayik dharma · Nirguna · Apurvakarana

### Shloka 4 — ॥४॥६०॥

**Sanskrit:**
कान्ता से समतैलौका, ज्ञातयो से समक्रिया ।
बाह्यवर्गमिति त्यक्त्वा, धर्मसंन्यासवान् भवेत् ॥४॥६०॥

**Translation:**
"Samata alone is my beloved wife; those of equal conduct are my relatives." Renouncing the external group in this way — become a dharma-sanyasi.

**Anvayartha:**
Abandon the external "varga" (group): worldly family, kanta-worldly-wife, all external attachment-objects. The param-tyagi's declaration: samata is my priyatama, and those with same-dharmic conduct are my true relatives. Renouncing outer varga in this spirit — with raga-dvesha-moha dissolved — one becomes dharma-sanyasvaan.

**Vivechan:**
Atma's shil, satya, dharma are its sthayibhav like sandalwood fragrance. The jivnatma has kshaayik jnana, darshan, charitra in its inherent svarup in every soul. The renunciation of kshaayopashamik kshamaadi gunas is "dharma-sanyasa." Achieving dharma-sanyasa is the second apurvakarana at the eighth gunasthana level. Until samyag-darshan is attained, the dharma-sanyasa done prior is called anattatvik dharma-sanyasa. The ultimately fruitful one is beyond this — the ayogic stage.

**Simply Put:**
When you've truly left the outer world — not just in body but in affection — and adopted Samata as your beloved, your relatives are those who walk the same inner path. This is what makes a tyagi real.

**Contemplate:**
Have you truly divorced Mamata and married Samata?

**Concept Tags:** Samata · Kanta · Dharma-sanyasa · Gunasthana · Apurvakarana

### Shloka 5 — ॥५॥६१॥

**Sanskrit:**
गुरुत्बं स्वस्य नोदेति, शिक्षासांत्म्येन याचता ।
श्रात्मतत्त्वप्रकाशेन, ताटत् सेव्यो गुरुतांस ॥५॥६१॥

**Translation:**
True guruhood does not arise through one's own assertion; it comes through imparting teaching that is aligned with the truth. Until the guru reveals the atma-tatva — one must take refuge in such a sadguru as guide and aradhana.

**Anvayartha:**
Just as abandoning worldly snehi-svajanos is necessary, so is renouncing pseudo-spiritual connections — when those connections do not open the atma-tatva. Shishya must practice "grahana" (receiving) and "asevana" (deep practice) shiksha. Especially the panchachara (five acharas) must be followed — jnanachara, darshanachar, charitrachara, tapachara, viryachara.

**Vivechan:**
Guru's param krupa and mumasashitvad alone do not awaken us. True guru-shishya bond arises when shishya comes with the bhaav of 'grahana shiksha' and 'asevana shiksha.' Among the five mahavrats, pramada-vimochan, observance of panchachara in daily life — this is the sadguru's highest upkaar. A genuinely devoted shishya's atma receives param krupa from sadguru. Without dharana-shiksha no jnana-guru can truly serve. Without same-bhaav — matching inner direction — no sadguru can bestow liberation.

**Simply Put:**
The guru's greatness comes from aligning teaching with the atma-tatva — not from lineage or position. Until that guru appears and lights up your inner self, keep seeking and keep practicing the five acharas.

**Contemplate:**
Are you truly receiving from your guru? Or only attending?

**Concept Tags:** Sadguru · Panchachara · Grahana-shiksha · Asevana · Atma-tatva

### Shloka 6 — ॥६॥६२॥

**Sanskrit:**
ज्ञानाचरादयोऽपीष्टाः, शुद्धस्वस्वपदाबधि ।
निविकल्पे पुनस्त्यागे, न विकल्पो न वा क्रिया ॥६॥६२॥

**Translation:**
Even jnanacharadi acharas (the five practices) are desirable and necessary — but only up to the attainment of the shuddha-pada. In the nirvikalpa tyag, there is neither vikalpa nor any kriya.

**Anvayartha:**
Jnanachara, darshanachar, charitrachara, tapachara, viryachara — all five are the upay (means), not the goal. The shuddhopayog-kriya done with shuddha sankalp is fruitful. One must follow jnanachara until kevaljnana is attained. Darshanachar until kshaayik-samikta. Charitrachara until yathaakhyat-charitra. Tapachara until shukladhyan pervades entirely. Viryachara until anant-vishuddh virya's nibandh (complete resolution).

**Vivechan:**
In the nirvikalpa state, there is no residue of vikalpa and no external kriya — only pure awareness. All practices are scaffolding: necessary while the building is going up. The moment of nirvikalpa tyag is the building complete — scaffolding down. The acharas are never to be abandoned prematurely; but one must always remember their purpose: they serve until shuddha-pada, not beyond.

**Simply Put:**
Every practice you do — jnana-path, darshan-path, charitra-path — is a means, not an end. When you reach the pure state, there's nothing to do. Not because you've given up — but because you've arrived.

**Contemplate:**
Are you practicing the five acharas with the firm sankalp of kevaljnana — or as ritual?

**Concept Tags:** Panchachara · Nirvikalpa · Shuddha-pada · Tyag as transcendence · Kevaljnana

### Shloka 7 — ॥७॥६३॥

**Sanskrit:**
योगस्त्यागतस्त्यागी, योगमानव्यसिलाल्रत्यजेत् ।
इत्येवं निगु॑ण ब्रह्म, परोत्रमुपचते ॥७॥६३॥

**Translation:**
The true tyagi abandons yogas (mental/verbal/bodily activities) — abandoning them progressively leads to nirguna brahma — the ultimate state of ayog.

**Anvayartha:**
Abandoning yogas does not mean suppressing them by force — it means the progressive resolution of udayik and kshaayopashamik bhavas until the 'ayoga' state is reached. 'Yoga drishti samucchay' describes this: kshaayopashamik dharma's tyag is dharma-sanyasa, which happens at the eighth gunasthan's second apurvakarana. Before samyag-darshan, this is anattatvik dharma-sanyasa. The full yoga-tyag leads to 'ayogya karma' state — the supreme yoginirod where the liberated soul's omniscient consciousness burns off all residue.

**Vivechan:**
'Nirguna brahma' occurs when all aupadhik dharma-yoga has been transcended. The jivnatma, through pramada-free atma-bhaav rooted in the Arihant's nature via samyag-jnana, approaches the state where even the concept of 'dropping weapons' (apashahi naram) reaches its final resolution — 'ayogya karma' — absolute yoga-sanyasa.

**Simply Put:**
The tyagi's path: first, drop worldly attachments. Then drop the scaffolding of practices. Then drop even the yogas. At the very end, the nirguna self shines alone — always was complete, always will be.

**Contemplate:**
What is the next yoga — thought-pattern, habit, identity — that you are being asked to let go of?

**Concept Tags:** Nirguna · Ayog · Yoga-tyag · Sarvasan-yasa · Dharma-sanyasa

### Shloka 8 — ॥८॥६४॥

**Sanskrit:**
वस्तुतस्तु गुणः पूर्णमनन्तैर्भासते स्वतः ।
रूपं त्यक्त्कात्मन साघोनिं रचरस्य विघोरिव ॥८॥६४॥

**Translation:**
In truth, the guna (quality) of the soul blazes complete, infinite, and self-luminous, on its own. Like the cloud-free sky revealing the full moon in all its radiance — the true form of the tyagi-soul shines forth.

**Anvayartha:**
Like the full moon of poornima — not obscured by a single cloud, clear sky, sixteen phases complete — this is the vision the author gives us. Anupam, achala, sarvottam svarup. The param-tyagi/yogi's svarup is purna and anant-gunon se dedipta. This can be seen moment by moment, step by step — and one day, without delay, the 'siddha-daan' will manifest. The complete crystal-consciousness will flash. Purna atma ka param-darshan karane ke liye Upadhyayaji Maharaj has pointed this way throughout: purnata, sthirta, vimalta, mohatva, satyapana — इन सबस्य त्याग (transcendence of all these intermediaries).

**Vivechan:**
The message of Tyag chapter is this: the atma's true svarup is purnata — infinite, self-luminous. We could not see it because clouds of kasha/rag/moha covered it. Shloka 1-7 removed the clouds one by one. Now — Shloka 8 — look up. The full moon blazes. Nothing was ever missing. Nothing will be missing. The soul was always complete. Tyag is not loss — it is rediscovery.

**Simply Put:**
The moon was always full. It was only the clouds. All the renunciations — of family, of belongings, of practices, of yogas — were cloud-clearing. When the last cloud drops, only the blazing light remains. That is you.

**Contemplate:**
What is the last cloud between you and the full moon of your own svarup?

**Concept Tags:** Purna-atma · Nirguna · Cloud-moon analogy · Siddha-darshan · Tyag as revelation

### Chapter Summary

**Key Theme:** True Tyag is not about what you renounce, but *from what understanding* you renounce. The chapter moves through layers: first renounce worldly family for divine family (atmarati, shuddhajnana), then adopt samata as beloved, then transcend even supportive dharmas at the right stage, then even the practices, then the yogas themselves — until the nirguna, purna, infinite soul blazes forth like the full moon freed of every cloud.

**Chapter Arc — Four Movements:**
1. The New Family (Shlokas 1–2): From worldly snehi-svajanos to atmarati/samata as true parents and beloved
2. Dharma-Sanyasa (Shlokas 3–4): Even supportive dharmas must be transcended at the highest stage
3. The Guru and the Practices (Shlokas 5–6): True sadguru and five acharas — necessary means, not ends
4. Nirguna and the Full Moon (Shlokas 7–8): Yoga-tyag, nirguna brahma, the blazing complete self
