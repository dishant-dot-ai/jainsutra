# Gyansaar — Chapter 1: Purnata (Fullness)
## Translation: Shlokas 1–8

**Scripture:** Gyansaar (The Essence of Knowledge)
**Author:** Nyayacharya Nyayavisharad Mahopadhyay Shri Yashovijayji
**Commentary (Vivechan):** Pannyaspravara Shri Bhadraguptavijayji Ganivar
**Chapter:** 1 — Purnata (पूर्णता / Fullness / Completeness)
**Theme:** The soul is inherently complete. All seeking arises from forgetting this fullness.

---

## SHLOKA 1

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

ऐन्द्रधीसुखमरसेन, लीलालत्तनमिवालिखम् ।
सच्चिदानन्दपूर्णन, पूर्ण जगदवेक्षते ॥१॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

With the nectar of supreme inner bliss — as if playfully sketching a portrait — the one who is full of existence-consciousness-bliss sees the entire universe as full.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**ऐन्द्रधीसुखमरसेन** = with the nectar of Indra-like (supreme) inner bliss; **लीलालत्तनमिवालिखम्** = as if playfully painting/sketching; **सच्चिदानन्दपूर्णन** = full of existence (sat), consciousness (chit), and bliss (ananda); **पूर्ण** = full/complete; **जगत्** = the universe/world; **अवेक्षते** = sees/perceives.

### 4. COMMENTARY

The Gyansaar opens with a vision of total fullness. The soul that is established in its own nature — which is existence, consciousness, and bliss — does not see a universe of lack. It sees fullness everywhere. This is not optimism or positive thinking. It is the natural perception of a consciousness that has recognized its own completeness.

The analogy of "playfully sketching" is profound. An artist who knows their own mastery does not strain — they create with ease, with lila (divine play). Similarly, the full soul does not labor to see truth. It simply sees, effortlessly, because seeing is its nature. The universe it perceives is not different from itself — it is the same fullness reflected outward.

The word "satchidananda" (existence-consciousness-bliss) appears in many Indian philosophical traditions, but Yashovijayji uses it here in a specifically Jain sense: the soul's essential nature is to exist (it is eternal), to know (it is conscious), and to be at peace (its natural state is bliss, not suffering). Suffering is an aberration caused by karmic obstruction, not the soul's baseline.

The opening shloka sets the entire tone of the Gyansaar: you are already full. The work is not to become full but to recognize the fullness that was never lost.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The soul that knows its own completeness sees the entire universe as complete. It perceives fullness everywhere — not from effort, but from its own nature.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What if nothing is missing? What if the sense of lack you carry is not a fact about reality but a distortion caused by not seeing clearly?

## SHLOKA 2

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

वृगता, या परोषामे सा वाञ्चितकमणजनम् ।
या तु स्वभाविकी सैव, आत्मस्वरलमिभानिभा ॥२॥

⚠️ *Prakrit/Sanskrit text reconstructed from available source — some characters may be imprecise due to print quality*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The fullness that comes from external objects and fulfilled desires — that is not true fullness. But the fullness that is natural, innate — that alone shines with the radiance of the self's own light.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**वृगता** = fullness/completeness; **या** = which; **परोषामे** = in external objects; **सा** = that; **वाञ्चितकमणजनम्** = born from fulfilled desires; **या तु** = but that which; **स्वभाविकी** = natural/innate; **सैव** = that alone; **आत्मस्वरलमिभानिभा** = radiant with the self's own luminous nature.

### 4. COMMENTARY

Yashovijayji immediately distinguishes between two kinds of fullness. The first is conditional — the temporary satisfaction that comes from getting what you want. You desire something, you obtain it, and for a moment you feel complete. But this fullness is borrowed. It depends on external conditions. The moment conditions change — and they always do — the fullness evaporates and craving returns.

The second kind of fullness is the soul's own nature. It does not come from outside and therefore cannot be taken away. It is self-luminous — it shines by its own light, the way the sun does not need another light source to be seen. This natural fullness is not something to be achieved. It is something to be uncovered, like clearing clouds to reveal a sun that was always shining.

The vivechan commentary elaborates: just as a person who obtains every external comfort — wealth, status, pleasure — still finds themselves restless and seeking, external fullness can never satisfy the soul's deeper hunger. The soul's hunger is for itself. Only self-knowledge brings lasting fullness. Everything else is a temporary analgesic.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Getting what you want gives a temporary feeling of completeness. But real, permanent fullness is not something you acquire — it is the soul's own natural state, always shining from within.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of a time you got exactly what you wanted. How long did the satisfaction last? What does that tell you about where true fulfillment actually lives?

## SHLOKA 3

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अवास्तवी चिकल्पेन, तुच्छया कृत्यगाहिजाड्गुली ।
पूर्णतिन्दस्य तत्त्वं कि, स्थावै दैन्यमूचिशंवचैवना ? ॥३॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed — some characters uncertain due to source print quality*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Can the fullness of the complete moon be diminished by pointing a finger at it and imagining it is imperfect? Similarly, can the soul's true completeness be touched by false mental constructions of inadequacy?

### 3. COMMENTARY

The analogy here is striking. Imagine someone pointing at the full moon and insisting it has a hole in it because their finger appears to cover part of it. The moon is untouched. The "deficiency" exists only in the perceiver's false construction, not in reality.

The same applies to the soul. The soul's fullness is absolute and unchanging. When we feel incomplete, broken, or lacking, we are pointing at the moon and seeing our own finger. The deficiency is projected by delusion (moha) — it is not a property of the self.

This verse attacks the most fundamental spiritual error: the belief that you are fundamentally flawed and need to become something other than what you already are. The Gyansaar's position is clear: you do not need to become full. You need to stop imagining you are empty.

The vivechan notes that this does not mean spiritual practice is unnecessary — quite the opposite. Practice is needed precisely to remove the false constructions (vikalpa) that obscure the fullness. But the goal of practice is uncovering, not construction. You are not building a new self. You are clearing away what hides the self that already exists.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Just as pointing a finger at the full moon doesn't actually create a hole in it, your mental constructions of inadequacy don't actually diminish your soul's natural completeness.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What stories of personal inadequacy do you carry? Are they describing your actual nature — or are they a finger held up against the full moon?

## SHLOKA 4

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

जागर्ता ज्ञानशिष्टिर्चैत, तद्दर्शनं नैव शक्यवते ।
लोपमेयं प्रियवासलेयेपर्नीपि तच्चन्दनद्रव: ॥४॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed — some characters uncertain due to source print quality*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the awakened vision of true knowledge (jnana-drishti) is attained, then the vision of inadequacy can never again have power. Just as the fragrance of sandalwood cannot be extinguished by any amount of covering.

### 3. COMMENTARY

Once genuine self-knowledge dawns — even briefly — the spell of inadequacy is broken permanently. The soul may still experience karmic effects, but it can no longer fully believe the lie that it is incomplete. The difference is like the difference between someone who has never seen light and someone who has seen it once but is temporarily in a dark room. The second person knows darkness is not permanent. The first person doesn't even know light exists.

The sandalwood analogy is apt: sandalwood's fragrance is intrinsic to the wood itself. You can wrap it, bury it, cover it with other scents — the sandalwood fragrance persists. It does not need to be added from outside. The soul's fullness is like this fragrance. External coverings (karma) may temporarily mask it, but they cannot destroy it.

The vivechan connects this to the Jain concept of samyak-drishti (right perception): once the soul has its first genuine flash of self-recognition, it enters a qualitatively different state. It may still have long to go on the path, but the direction is set. It can no longer be permanently fooled into believing it is merely a body, merely a bundle of desires, merely a product of circumstances.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Once you truly see your own completeness even once, the belief that you are broken loses its power forever — like sandalwood whose fragrance can never be fully covered up.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever had a moment of deep inner peace where you felt completely whole? What if that moment was not an exception but a glimpse of your permanent nature?

## SHLOKA 5

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अपूर्णं पूर्णतामेति, पूर्णमाराधस्तु हीयते ।
पूर्णतिन्दस्वभावेन, संव्यवस्थानुसारत: ॥५॥

⚠️ *Text reconstructed from available source*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The incomplete attains fullness, and fullness never diminishes through worship/realization of it. By the very nature of complete fullness, this is the law of the soul's own constitution.

### 3. COMMENTARY

This verse states a principle of spiritual law: when the soul that has been experiencing itself as incomplete turns inward and recognizes its own fullness, it does not merely improve — it returns to its natural state. And this natural state, once genuinely established, does not decrease. Fullness does not wear out. It does not depreciate. It does not have a shelf life.

This is different from every other form of acquisition. Material wealth can be lost. Skills can degrade. Relationships can end. But the soul's self-recognition cannot be un-recognized. Once you have seen what you are, you cannot un-see it.

The vivechan explains this through the metaphor of gold purification: gold mixed with impurities appears to be base metal. When the impurities are removed through successive refinements, the gold does not become gold — it was always gold. The purification simply reveals what was already there. Similarly, the soul's fullness is not created through spiritual practice; it is revealed. And once revealed through the final purification, it shines permanently as pure gold — sixteen-karat and beyond.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

When an incomplete-seeming soul realizes its own fullness, it becomes whole — and that wholeness never diminishes. This is not something you gain and can lose; it is your nature being revealed.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What in your experience has ever been permanent? And what does it mean that your deepest nature — awareness itself — has never actually changed?

## SHLOKA 6

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

वस्त्य दृष्टि: कृपावृद्धिचरि, शमसुधारकि: ।
तस्मै नम: शुभज्ञानचन्द्राय योगिने ॥६॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Salutations to that yogi — the moon of auspicious knowledge — whose vision is truth, whose conduct overflows with compassion, and who is established in tranquility.

### 3. COMMENTARY

In the middle of this philosophical exposition on fullness, Yashovijayji pauses to offer homage to the realized being who embodies everything the chapter describes. This is not decorative. It is structural: the concept of fullness is not merely theoretical. It has been lived. There are beings who have attained it. And bowing to them is both an act of reverence and a practical reminder that what you are reading about is achievable.

The description — truth-vision (vasty drishti), compassion in action (kripavriddhichari), and established tranquility (shamasudharaki) — maps precisely to the three pillars of Jain realization: right perception (samyak darshan), right conduct (samyak charitra), and right knowledge (samyak jnana). The realized yogi does not merely believe in fullness — they see through the lens of fullness (truth-vision), they act from fullness (compassion), and they rest in fullness (tranquility).

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The author bows to the realized soul who lives fullness in practice — whose seeing is truthful, whose actions are compassionate, and whose being is tranquil.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What would your life look like if your seeing were truthful, your actions flowed from compassion, and your inner state were genuinely tranquil?

## SHLOKA 7

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

ज्ञानमूत्र्य विनर्शयेत, लोपविशोभकुचर्वके: ।
अम्बलद्रव्याभिचिन्मात्रमूर्ति मस्तवा स्थिरो भव ॥७॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Let the image of pure knowledge destroy all coverings that diminish its radiance. Worship the form that is pure consciousness alone — and become steady.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The chapter's practical instruction crystallizes here: the remedy for the sense of incompleteness is the cultivation of pure knowledge. Not knowledge as information, but knowledge as the soul's own self-luminous awareness. When this awareness is turned inward and allowed to shine fully, it burns away the coverings (karma) that have been blocking it.

The instruction "become steady" (sthiro bhava) connects directly to the next chapter of the Gyansaar — Sthirata (Steadfastness). Fullness is not a momentary flash. It must become stable. The soul must learn to remain in its own nature continuously, not just in fleeting glimpses during meditation. This steadiness is the fruit of fullness: when you know you are complete, you stop running after things, and stillness becomes natural.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Let the light of pure self-knowledge burn away everything that covers it. Then rest in that awareness — and become steady in it.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

If pure awareness is your nature and it can burn away all coverings — what is stopping you from simply allowing it to shine right now?

## SHLOKA 8

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

शामहत्वगुती सत्त्व, विषुद्धोर्दि महात्मना ।
कि स्तुकमो ज्ञानवीपुर्दे, तत्त्व सर्वांनुमन्तता ? ॥८॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed from source — significant uncertainty in characters*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

What praise is sufficient for the great soul whose nature has become purified through tranquility? The truth alone — the truth of all things — is their lasting companion.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The closing verse of the chapter returns to the theme of the opening: the purified soul, established in its own fullness, needs nothing from outside. Its companion is truth itself — not a belief about truth, not a doctrine about truth, but the direct, unmediated experience of reality as it is.

The question "what praise is sufficient?" is rhetorical. No words are adequate because words are conventional instruments and the realized soul has transcended convention. The fullness being described cannot be captured in language. It can only be pointed at — which is what this entire chapter has been doing.

The vivechan concludes the chapter by noting that Purnata (Fullness) is the foundation of the entire Gyansaar. Every subsequent chapter — Magnata (Engrossment), Sthirata (Steadfastness), Amoha (Non-delusion), Jnana (Knowledge), and all thirty-two — presupposes this starting point: the soul is already complete. The path is not about becoming something new. It is about removing what obscures what you already are.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

No words can adequately praise the soul that has purified itself through inner peace. Such a soul's only companion is truth itself — direct experience of reality.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

If your soul is already complete, then what is the spiritual path for? Not to build something — but to remove what hides what is already there. How does that change the way you approach your own growth?
