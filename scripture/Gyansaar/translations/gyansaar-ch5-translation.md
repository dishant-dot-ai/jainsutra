# Gyansaar Chapter 5: Jnana (ज्ञान) — Knowledge

**Chapter summary (from title page):**
Amohi's meaning is to become a Jnani. When moha's veil is removed from the soul, the light of jnana blazes up. The jnana-jyoti ignites. The jnani's knowledge is not that of a single shastra, a single shloka, a single word — it is boundless. It illuminates the jivatma in all three worlds and in liberation. That knowledge is the source of highest tattva-siddhi.

Knowledge guided by atma-svabhava-bhava is called true (vastavik) knowledge. Vad-vivad and vivad that creates controversy — that is not knowledge. A non-deluded (amoh) person stays far away from vad-vivad and dissension.

For knowledge: be at least a little non-deluded. Engage in deep shastra study, manana, and chintana. Only then can you grasp knowledge's true form and experience it directly.

---

## Shloka 1 — ॥१॥३३॥

**Sanskrit:**
मज्जात्मज्ञः किलज्ञाने, बिष्टायामिब शूकरः ।
ज्ञानी निमज्जति ज्ञाने, मराल इव मानसे ॥१॥३३॥

**Translation:**
Just as a pig is always immersed in filth, so the ajnani (ignorant) remains ever sunk in ignorance. Just as a rajahansa (royal swan) is immersed in Mansarovar, so the jnani remains immersed in knowledge.

**Anvayartha:**
The ajnani drowns in the mud of ignorance (like a pig in mire), while the jnani abides freely in pure knowledge (like a swan in the sacred lake).

**Vivechan:**
Observe a person — where do they go repeatedly, what comes to mind again and again, whose company do they prefer to spend most time in, what do they like to hear, what do they love to know? From this careful observation, you can gauge the jivatma's real inclination and true attachment.

Where only physical and sense-pleasure (sukh-dukh) is discussed, where the company of pudgala-oriented beings is preferred, where wrong stories and false narratives are told, where only sense-gratifying talk is heard — this reveals that person's true attraction is toward the physical and sensory. Their attachment is toward kama-vasana pleasures. These vrittis — attraction, anuraga, love, feelings — are the ignorance (ajnanata) of the jivatma, its mohandhata (blindness from moha). Its condition is like a pig rolling in a drain of filth and excrement.

Whereas those who are Jnanis, the darshi of truth (vaastavdarshi-satydarshi), their attention always goes where the discussion of atma-unnati and atma-kalyan is happening. They prefer the constant company of atma-jnani persons. In their heart, the feeling of knowing atma-svaroop and achieving tad-atmya with it always remains. From their mouth, only the stories of Atma, Mahatma, and Paramatma will be heard. Immersed in these stories in study and recitation, they will always appear absorbed. Just as a rajahansa plays freely at Mansarovar and it is progressive from all perspectives.

**Simply Put:**
Where your mind gravitates, what you keep talking about, who you seek out — this reveals your true level of consciousness. The pig seeks filth by nature. The swan seeks the pure lake. Where does your soul naturally return?

**Contemplate:**
"What do I most naturally gravitate toward — the physical and sensory, or the atmic and eternal? The pig does not know it is in filth. Does the ajnani know they are sunk in ignorance?"

## Shloka 2 — ॥२॥३४॥

**Sanskrit:**
निर्वाणपदमाप्नोति, भाष्यते यन्मुहुर्मुहुः ।
तदेव ज्ञानमुक्तेष्टं, निर्बन्धो नारित भूयसा ॥२॥३४॥

**Translation:**
That one definitive word/pada which is spoken again and again — that alone is true knowledge (jnana). Repeating and debating many words and texts is not (true) knowledge.

**Anvayartha:**
One clear, nirnayatmak (decisive) pada that leads to moksha, spoken and contemplated repeatedly — that is jnana. Sheer volume of texts and arguments is not knowledge.

**Vivechan:**
Our purpose is not to study many texts and engage in excessive book-learning. Nor is this meant as a plea to make you compile and store vast information. Rather, our teaching is this: find one definitive pada, one word or perhaps a mantra, and make it your ekagra focus. Contemplate it with singular attention (ekagrya), keep returning to it, let it penetrate. When you are engaged with it continuously, moha will not be able to push you away. Even one single thought pointed at moha like a missile will shatter and dissolve it. True knowledge is this.

For making this knowledge impenetrable, four means are essential:
- After initial contemplation, continuous practice of that thought must continue.
- The understanding of these thoughts must deepen; within it, love toward param tatva, Tirthankaras, gurujans, shradha-bhavana, krupa-bhav, tatvarag and other values must be born in the heart.
- The vivechan must always be done through shastra-paddhati and in accordance with agam-based understanding.
- As tatva-chintana deepens, the intoxication of kshayopasham will quiet down. The addictions of vyasana will reduce, and the intoxication of rasa-rddhi-sana (sense pleasures) will diminish.

Gurudev once gave his beloved disciple 'Maatap muni' this nirvanasdavak pada: 'Ma rup... ma tup' — meaning "Never hate, never love (with attachment)." For twelve consecutive years, the Mahasamunis continuously contemplated this pada, refined it with subtlety. The result: after twelve years of sadhana, they became adhikari (qualified recipients) of the moksha-pada. Through the contemplation-manana of just one pada, they attained siddhi: 'Ma rup, ma tup.' For them this pada became a kind of supreme knowledge and they attained the liberation-pada.

Total absorption in tatva-chintana — dissolving oneself in it — is the highest category of knowledge.

**Simply Put:**
One clear, decisive pada (word/principle) returned to again and again is worth more than a library of debated texts. The muni Maatap attained moksha through just these two words: "Ma rup, ma tup" — neither hatred nor attachment.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my one pada? What is the single deepest truth I can return to again and again until it becomes my nature? Volume of reading without that anchor is noise. Depth on one truth is knowledge."

## Shloka 3 — ॥३॥३५॥

**Sanskrit:**
स्वभावलाभसंस्कारकारणं ज्ञानमिप्यते ।
ध्यान्ध्यमात्रमतस्त्वन्यत्, तथा चोक्तं महात्मना ॥३॥३५॥

**Translation:**
That knowledge which is the cause of gaining (labha) the atma-svabhava (one's own nature) is called Jnana. Studying for the mere sake of study (dhyandya-matra) beyond this — is something else. Thus have the great souls declared.

**Anvayartha:**
Jnana is that which awakens the vasana (longing) for atma-svaroop. Mere reading/study without this is mere intellectual exercise. As Patanjali also said similarly.

**Vivechan:**
Jnana is that which continuously gives the jivatma momentum toward realizing atma-svaroop. It creates the vasana (desire) to know atma-svaroop. Vasana is that which creates the drive in the mind to know a subject deeply. It gives the inspiration to work hard to obtain it, it keeps urging you to follow it with hands and feet.

For example: if a vasana for a young woman arises in someone, they will be constantly working to win her over. In their mind waking and sleeping, sitting and standing, walking and roaming, only one thought: 'How can I win this woman? How can I enter her heart?' This vasana is born of the mind-pleasing sight of that woman. The vasana born in the mind through the agency of manohar-darshan (beautiful sight).

That alone is jnana which, as soon as it arises, provides the atma-jnana to the jivatma and keeps it constantly absorbed in atma-svaroop, and for the attainment of atma-shanti urges them to make atma-parishrama (atmic effort). So, such jnana is necessary to us — that wherever knowledge grows like this and contemplation-manan happens and however punya matters expand — even in Ramayana, Mahabharata, and Itihasa, some content is found. Thus the mind too will get rasa-rddhi from such things.

Bhagavan Yudhishthira gave Yaksha-Kumar the highest knowledge in the Mahabharata. Ramanuriji gave five disciples 'their atmic knowledge (pratibodh)' in the context of this. Those fifty disciples became desirous to attain atmasvaroop. For that purpose, the moha they were eating was far greater. The vasana of humans is born of story? Every moment of humanity, the stories were not born? At that time, if moha came to the fore, from the face of moha — no other thought. 'This is harmful', 'this is beneficial', 'this promotes', 'this promotes' — such duality-free knowledge is jnana. This knowledge is worth sharing and Patanjali said so and this is the universal view.

**Simply Put:**
Real jnana is not information — it's the igniting of a deep longing (vasana) for the atma. Just as a man consumed by longing for a woman thinks of nothing else, the jnani consumed by longing for the atma turns every experience toward that single focus.

**Contemplate:**
"Does my study ignite longing for the atma, or does it only satisfy the intellect? Am I reading to know more, or am I reading to become more?"

## Shloka 4 — ॥४॥३६॥

**Sanskrit:**
वादांश्च प्रतिवादांश्च यदन्तोऽनिश्चितांस्तथा ।
तत्त्वान्तं नैव गच्छन्ति, तिलपीसकवद्गतौ ॥४॥३६॥

**Translation:**
Those who are stuck in endless vad (argument) and prativad (counter-argument), without arriving at any certainty — they are completely incapable of reaching tattva's end, like a sesame-seed-crushing bullock (tila-pisak) that circles endlessly without moving forward.

**Anvayartha:**
Vad-vivad without arriving at certainty is like the oil-press bullock — perpetual circular motion, no forward progress.

**Vivechan:**
The shastra-jnana meant to conquer inner enemies and move antah-chetana toward atma — you have used that same shastra to get lost in purposeless vad-vivad? You have abandoned what is dear (iShta) and chased what is not dear (anishta)? You have surrendered to raga and dvesha, and forgetting your own atmasvaroop, you have drowned in the worldly gain and loss!

Before all this, think: have you truly understood the shastra and texts that you have, which are the supreme treasury of jnana? Have you rightly evaluated them? Today in this world there are no kevalajnani (omniscient) persons, no manah-paryay-jnani (mind-reading) persons, and no great souls with avadhi-jnana here. Then how dare you claim to understand scriptures composed and expounded by the masters of infinite knowledge, and to have absorbed them? This is what kind of perversion? And yet you openly proclaim your interpretation as right? And then you try to declare others' interpretations as baseless? You should fully understand that your mati (understanding) is limited, your shrutajnanavaran ksayopasham is very weak. In this state, whatever shastra-jnana you have is surely with anishchit (uncertain) meaning. Based on this, if you debate for years, you will still not reach its end. You will be unable to grasp its true meaning. You will not be able to enjoy the supreme bliss of knowledge with an open mind. It is possible that in debates and counter-debates you will be victorious and the anand of that will thrill every pore of your body. But do not forget: this ananda is momentary, transient, and unnatural.

**Simply Put:**
The debater who never reaches tattva is like the sesame-press bullock — all effort, endless circles, zero progress. Real jnana does not make you win debates. It makes you see truth.

**Contemplate:**
"In my spiritual discussions, am I moving toward truth or circling the same points? What is the difference between a discussion that illuminates and one that only seeks to win?"

## Shloka 5 — ॥५॥३७॥

**Sanskrit:**
स्वद्रव्यगुणपर्यायेषु द्रव्यं, गुण और पर्याय में निहित थेक है । पर द्रव्य, गुण और पर्याय में परिणति थेक नहीं ।
इति बसात्मशस्तुणिष्ठिमु ज्ञानिमिति यो ने ॥५॥३७॥

**Sanskrit (corrected):**
स्वद्रव्यगुणपर्यायेषु द्रव्यं, गुणश्च पर्याय थेक एव ।
इति बसात्मशस्तुणिष्ठिमु ज्ञानिमिति नो ने ॥५॥३७॥

**Translation:**
In one's own dravya, guna, and paryaya — the dravya, guna, and paryaya are truly correct/stable. But in para-dravya (other's substance), guna, and paryaya — the pariNati (transformation) is not correct. Thus the one who is ever established in one's own self (sva-atma-nistha) is the Jnani — this secret the Jnani has revealed.

**Anvayartha:**
The Jnani's orientation: my dravya, guna, paryaya are authentic and real. In para-dravya, guna, paryaya — no transformation is mine. This is the secret understanding (rahasyajnana) that the Jnani revealed.

**Vivechan:**
O Munivar! What is the secret knowledge you have found for yourself? Can that stated rahasyajnana prove you to yourself?

'Para-dravya, guna, and para-paryaya — you became engrossed in 'para' (other), consumed by it, absorbed in it. From dhanadik onwards, 'para' is only endless. You are not satisfied at all, rather our bliss keeps diminishing. And you should never forget that para-dravya, guna, paryaya do not belong to your atma at all. They will never be satisfied. If satisfaction is not had there, then why make it the aim? Why become absorbed in it?

'O atmany (O Soul)! Do only two things: resolve to be nishchint-nischal. Let sva-jnana-dhyan happen. Turn your mind to it. In that mind, the inherent jnana, darshan, and charitra-guna will awaken — you will become experienced in all three. And your vartmaan (present) three gunasthanas will also...'

**Simply Put:**
The Jnani's compass: what is truly mine is my own dravya-guna-paryaya (substance-quality-mode). What belongs to others is not mine and cannot satisfy me. All dissatisfaction comes from chasing para-dravya. All peace comes from resting in sva-dravya.

**Contemplate:**
"How much of my suffering comes from wanting para-dravya (others' situations, possessions, approval) to be different? What if I truly accepted that para-dravya is not mine to transform?"

## Shloka 6 — ॥६॥३८॥

**Sanskrit:**
अस्ति चेद् ग्रथिभिज्ञान, किं चित्रैस्तंत्रयणैः ।
प्रदीपाः नवोपष्टुज्यन्ते, तमोध्नी हृष्टिरेव चेतु ॥६॥३८॥

**Translation:**
If the granthi-bheda (knot-breaking) jnana has been attained, what use is the elaborate array of many shastras? When one's own eyes are so powerfully illuminated that they can cut through dense darkness, what use then are lamps for that person?

**Anvayartha:**
Once granthi-bheda occurs and the atmasvaroop becomes directly known, the need for many shastras falls away — just as someone with blazing inner sight has no need of external lamps.

**Vivechan:**
O Muni! The deepest secret knowledge that you have found — does it truly establish you in yourself?

'Per dravya, guna, paryaya' — you became engrossed in 'per' (other), taken over by it, absorbed in it. From dhana forward, 'per' is only endless. You are never fully satisfied, rather your satisfaction keeps declining... just as a bullock on an oil-press keeps moving but never progresses.

This person's sva-dravya is their own parinama. Their tri-kalik paryayas are pure. Therefore, transform yourself in those pure paryayas. Because this transformation is the supreme and best.

'O atmany! The attachment to para-dravya-guna-paryaya is mithya (false/wrong), incorrect. Therefore abandon it in mind and body. Consequently your own body, house, wealth-possessions, rasa-rupa, woman, child, animal and others — do not do moha for these, nor give raga-dvesha a place in these changing circumstances of body, spouse, rupa-rasa and other para-padas.'

Thus the kartavya of every muni is to keep satisfying themselves... this is their rahasyajnana. That is — the muni's darshan-jnana-charitra have a common existence. They should always be immersed in the jnana-darshan-charitra-maya atma. And this is their param kartavya (highest duty). For this purpose it is absolutely necessary that the upyukt bhavana always be continuously contemplated in their own mind. Along with this, wherever there is a possibility of their chitta getting attached to para-pudgal, they should take the support of this brief rahasyajnana and quickly stopping it. 'Rahasyajnana' is one infallible weapon to uproot moha.

When granthi-bheda (dissolution of the knot of mithy-atva) has happened — then what use are many shastras? Just as a person whose eyes are so blazing that they can shatter dense darkness — what use is a lamp for them? The moment the granthi of raga-dvesha dissolves, the radiance of samyaktva (right vision) blazes within the atma. Their sharp light spreads everywhere in the atma.

**Simply Put:**
Granthi-bheda — the breaking of the inner knot of mithyatva — is the turning point. Before it, all shastras are needed as lamps. After it, the atma itself blazes. The one who has attained this inner sight has no need of external lamps.

**Contemplate:**
"Am I reading shastras to find the key that breaks my inner knot, or am I collecting keys as a hobby? The shastras are lamps lit for me when I was in darkness. What would it look like to not need them anymore?"

## Shloka 7 — ॥७॥३९॥

**Sanskrit:**
मिथ्यात्वशलयच्छित्वाद्, नानदर्शनोलिसोभित ।
निभाय सत्त्वद् घोमों, नन्दतयान वन्दने ॥७॥३९॥

**Translation:**
Having cut away the arrow/thorn of mithyatva — the soul is adorned with the radiance of samyak-darshan (right vision). O munivar! By the power of that right perception (samyag-darshan), even a wild elephant becomes tame — bowing at your feet, it worships you.

**Anvayartha:**
Once mithyatva (false worldview) is extracted like a thorn, samyak-darshan blazes within. Such a soul commands respect even from a mad elephant — it naturally bows before them.

**Vivechan:**
The great muni who has discarded karma, cut asunder the layers of moha, reached the state of sarvajnapada (omniscience), and whose knowledge is totally complete — what fear does such a yogiraj have? With the only weapon 'jnana' — the one sword that can cut through mithyatva's peaks in manana-fanan — is the most powerful in itself. Like the vajra (thunderbolt), it is self-sufficient in power. What fear does he have? He roams freely in the garden of atma-pradesh's heaven... always experiencing the inexpressible bliss of the atmananda's nandan-vana.

The munis here are compared to Devraj-Indra. Just as for a moment's sake, Indra-vajra never leaves his side, so muniajans should always keep atma-pariNati-roop jnana ready with them. Only then can they remain fearless / unflinching and experience the true atmasukha.

Bhagavan Mahavir once said to Gautam: "Samayam Goyam! Ma pamayae." The secret-explosion of this statement is this. He said: "O Gautam! The jnana-vajra in your possession, never make the mistake of putting it away even for a moment." Through this dictum, Devagideva Mahavir Prabhu gave all sadhu-muninajas the upadesha to always keep atma-pariNati-roop jnana readied with them. When atma-pariNati-roop jnana is intact, at that moment the powerful assault of raga-dvesha asuras cannot happen to you. These demons, while trying to drag muniajans out of the heaven of atmananda into the prison of pudgalananda, will fail. Consequently, the muniajan will fall from their 'muni-pravrtti' and be surrounded on all sides by fear, disturbance, kleshadis.

As long as the muni remains established in atma-pariNati, until then raga-dvesha, moha-maya cannot even take the name of attacking him, and he remains fearless from all external disturbances, experiencing the atmananda.

**Simply Put:**
The muni who has extracted the thorn of mithyatva blazes with samyak-darshan. Like Indra who never puts down his vajra, the muni must never set aside the weapon of atma-pariNati-jnana. With this weapon, even a mad elephant bows.

**Contemplate:**
"What is my mithyatva — the one false belief that, if truly uprooted, would transform everything? And what would it feel like to carry jnana the way Indra carries his vajra — never setting it down?"

## Shloka 8 — ॥८॥४०॥

**Sanskrit:**
पीयूषमसमुद्रोत्थं, रसायनमनौषधम् ।
अनन्यापेक्षमैश्वर्यं, ज्ञानमाहुर्मनीषिणः ॥८॥४०॥

**Translation:**
The learned (maniShinaH) have declared: Jnana is — amrita (nectar) that has not emerged from the ocean; a rasayana (elixir) that is not a medicine; an aishvarya (sovereignty/wealth) that requires no one else. It is incomparable.

**Anvayartha:**
Jnana is the most supreme amruta — not sourced from the churning of the ocean, not needing to be compounded as a medicine, not dependent on anyone else for its power. It is the self-sufficient, sovereign, incomparable wealth.

**Vivechan:**
People say that amrita emerges from ocean-churning. Learned kings and great ones consider that rasayana is born of physician-knowledge. The kings, maharajas, the great-ones consider that aishvarya (sovereignty) is contained in elephants, horses, wealth, gold, and silver.

But the declaration of Jnanis is: "Jnana is amruta — yet it has not emerged from the ocean. Jnana is rasayana — yet it is not physician-born medicine. Jnana is aishvarya — yet it is not in elephants, horses and the like."

The ocean cannot save a person from death. But jnana-amruta, being non-ocean-born, transforms them as amruta. Physically, the great medicines and therapies are helpful in keeping the body somewhat stable and extending life a bit. But the jnana's elixir makes the mind always full of bliss. It is not the pleasure of gold-silver and sense pleasures — these have no power of satisfaction, no power to satisfy. The jnana-elixir does this always. When sone wants to compare: gold, silver-gold, horses, elephants — in the realm of vividy, how much time before all these are eliminated, consumed, become unavailable. At the same time, such fierce vasana (longing) should be born in the jivatma — that urge that drives toward liberation at any cost, that promotes and protects — this is jnana. This jnana is upadeya (worthy of adoption) and this is what Patanjali said and this is the universal view.

This kind of awareness-jnana (bhavana-jnana) in the prakatata of mind from three categories: deva-guru-dharma's authority on this bhavana has given the path of amrit, jnana-rasayana and jnana-aishvarya. Thus the jivatma gets the supreme trupti (satisfaction). This becomes the highest beginning of liberation and creates the prime motivation toward moksha.

**Simply Put:**
Jnana is the real amruta — not the mythology of ocean-churning. The real rasayana — not from any physician. The real aishvarya (sovereignty) — needing nothing and no one outside itself. The learned have declared: this self-sufficient, incomparable wealth called jnana surpasses all.

**Contemplate:**
"What in my life do I treat as 'amruta' — the thing I think will finally satisfy? Is it external? And what would it feel like to have a satisfaction that requires nothing from outside — truly ananya-apeksha (without dependence on anything else)?"

## Chapter Summary
**Chapter 5: Jnana** reveals the nature of true knowledge — not the accumulation of texts, arguments, or information, but the living fire that awakens longing for atma-svaroop. Eight shlokas trace the arc from the pig vs. swan image (Shloka 1) through the decisive pada (Shloka 2), the atma-vasana (Shloka 3), the futility of endless debate (Shloka 4), the orientation toward sva-dravya (Shloka 5), granthi-bheda (Shloka 6), the samyak-darshan that tames even elephants (Shloka 7), and finally the declaration of jnana as the incomparable amruta-rasayana-aishvarya (Shloka 8).

Running shloka numbers: ॥१॥३३॥ through ॥८॥४०॥ (8 shlokas, book-wide count: 33–40)
