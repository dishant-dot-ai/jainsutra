# Gyansaar — Chapter 3: Sthirata (Steadfastness)
## Translation: Shlokas 1–7

**Scripture:** Gyansaar (The Essence of Knowledge)
**Author:** Nyayacharya Nyayavisharad Mahopadhyay Shri Yashovijayji
**Commentary (Vivechan):** Pannyaspravara Shri Bhadraguptavijayji Ganivar
**Chapter:** 3 — Sthirata (स्थिरता / Steadfastness / Stability)
**Theme:** Steadfastness — the continuous, unbroken dwelling in one's own nature — is what transforms the flash of inner recognition into a permanent mode of being.

---

## SHLOKA 1

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

वस्त ! किं चचलस्वान्तो भ्रात्वा भ्रातवा विथोदसि ?
निनिं स्वस्तिनायायेय स्थिरता यशोदयति ॥१॥१८॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed — some characters uncertain*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

O friend! Why do you exhaust yourself wandering with a restless, unstable mind — going here and there, running from one thing to another? Be steady. It is Steadfastness alone that brings all excellence.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**वस्त** = O friend/dear one; **किं** = why; **चचलस्वान्तो** = with a restless/unstable inner nature; **भ्रात्वा भ्रातवा** = wandering, going round and round; **विथोदसि** = exhaust yourself/grieve; **स्थिरता** = steadfastness; **यशोदयति** = gives/produces all excellence/glory.

### 4. COMMENTARY

The chapter opens not with definition but with a direct challenge — almost a shout of compassion from teacher to student: What are you doing? Why are you still wandering? You have tasted the bliss of absorption (Magnata). You have recognized your own fullness (Purnata). And yet the mind is still restless, still bouncing from object to object, still chasing?

The vivechan makes the diagnosis sharp: your mind has gone everywhere — north, south, east, west, up, down. You have sought happiness at every turn. What did you find? Not even a moment's stable relief. The vivechan offers the definitive verdict: what you seek cannot be found out there. It never was there. It is here — in Sthirata, in the steady, unshaken abiding in one's own nature.

The chapter title-page box (in the original printed text) summarizes the teaching precisely: *"Always be steady. Continuously... always — forever! Steadfastness should be the permanent condition of the human being. For knowledge-absorption, mental steadiness is essential. There is no room in it for agitation, instability, or distraction."*

And then the practical admonition: saying "I cannot be steady" is useless. Stop complaining and find the way. Steadfastness — its ratna-deepak (jewel-lamp) — is the cool light that illuminates the entire spiritual path. In that light, the destination of fullness is certain.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Stop wandering. All the running you have done — from one thing to the next, one place to the next — has produced only agitation. The glory you seek comes from steadfastness alone.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Where has your mind gone today — how many things, how many concerns, how many plans? Has any of that movement brought you to rest? What if the movement itself is the problem?

## SHLOKA 2

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

ज्ञानदुघं विनश्येत, लोभविक्षोभकुचंकैः ।
अम्लद्रव्यादिवास्थेयादिदति मत्वा स्थिरो भव ॥२॥१९॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The vessel of knowledge (jnana) is ruined by the churning disturbance of greed — just as pure milk is curdled and spoiled by acidic substances. Knowing this, become steady.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**ज्ञानदुघं** = the milk of knowledge/vessel of knowledge; **विनश्येत** = is destroyed/spoiled; **लोभविक्षोभकुचंकैः** = by the churning disturbance of greed; **अम्लद्रव्यादि** = acid-substances etc.; **मत्वा** = knowing/understanding this; **स्थिरो भव** = become steady.

### 4. COMMENTARY

The analogy here is one of the most vivid in the chapter. Imagine a vessel of pure, fresh milk — the milk of knowledge, the jnana-amrita. Now drop acid into it. The milk instantly curdles, spoils, becomes undrinkable. The vessel of knowledge is exactly like this. The acid? Greed — the craving for external material objects.

The vivechan addresses a specific group: those of simple nature who think they can simultaneously pursue spiritual knowledge and material acquisition. Yashovijayji warns them: this path is blocked. You cannot simultaneously cultivate jnana-magnata and pursue material wealth with full desire. When the acid of craving for external objects enters the milk of inner absorption, the absorption is destroyed. The pure state curdles. Whatever bliss was forming is spoiled.

The vivechan gives a memorable practical image: if sour, acidic substance is poured into overflowing milk, the milk will immediately curdle. Anyone who wants to drink it will have to hold their nose. And if they drink it anyway, they will get not nourishment but disease — illness, disturbance, nanavidh vyaadhi (manifold disorders).

This is what happens when the soul absorbed in jnana-ananda begins mixing in the craving for poudgalic (material) pleasures. The absorption is disrupted, the bliss curdles into restlessness, and instead of liberation the soul gets more karma — more entanglement.

The solution is simple and stated plainly: knowing this clearly — become steady. Sthirta is the protection of the jnana-vessel from the acid of greed.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Greed poured into the vessel of inner knowledge is like acid dropped into pure milk — it instantly curdles everything. To protect the bliss of inner absorption, you must be steady: steady in the direction of knowledge, steady away from the pull of material craving.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of a time when a moment of inner peace was broken by a sudden desire or craving. Did you notice the transition? The peace was there — and then the craving arrived — and the peace was gone. What does that tell you about the relationship between steadiness and contentment?

## SHLOKA 3

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अस्थिरे दृढमे चिंता, वाड् मन:त्वाकारगोपेना ।
पुरुषध्या दृढ कर्त्याण्यकारिरणो न प्रकीतिता ॥३॥२०॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When steadiness is absent, the mind falls into deep anxiety. For one whose speech, mind, and action are unsteady — for such a person, even noble deeds are rendered ineffective and cannot bear fruit.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The vivechan opens this section with a searching question for the reader: your mind and heart — how restless have they become? You go here and there, wandering in worry-thought. You look for satisfaction — where have you found it? Everywhere-nowhere. The vivechan states the bald truth: stable satisfaction has simply never been found through external searching, and it never will be. This is not a pessimistic prediction — it is a description of the nature of things.

The milk analogy continues: when the pure milk of jnana-amrita (knowledge-nectar) from the Siddhas (liberated beings) and from the shastras is already flowing into our atma-bhajan (vessel of self), why does it immediately become impure and why does its nourishing quality disappear? Answer: because the poudgalic desire-tendencies are mixed into it. The result: even the noble actions done from that unstable base cannot achieve their full benefit.

The vivechan drives this home with a disturbing contemporary picture: the Vitarag-dev (liberated being) has given us this pure milk of dharma. But new poudgalic desires constantly come in and curdle it. So we run — temple to temple, guru to guru, pilgrimage to pilgrimage — and moh (delusion) continues to beat us. The "home" we seek through all these outward searches cannot be found there. Moh-mukti — freedom from delusion — has only one door: inward steadiness.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Without steadiness, the mind collapses into anxiety. And when mind, speech, and action are all unstable, even virtuous efforts cannot produce their full fruit. Steadiness is not just a spiritual quality — it is the prerequisite for all other qualities to function.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Have you done something good — made an effort, given something, practiced something — and felt that it didn't "land," that it didn't produce the inner peace you expected? Could instability of mind have been the reason? What would it look like to do the same action from a steadier ground?

## SHLOKA 4

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

स्थिरता वाड् मन:कार्ययवामड्रागितां गता ।
योगिन: समशीलास्ते ग्रामेऽरण्ये दिवा निशि ॥५॥२१॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

For the one whose steadiness has pervaded speech, mind, and action completely — such great yogis are of the same nature whether in village or forest, in day or night.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**स्थिरता** = steadfastness; **वाड् मन:कार्य** = speech, mind, and action; **समागता** = having fully pervaded/attained; **योगिन:** = yogis; **समशीलाः** = of the same temperament/nature; **ग्रामे** = in village; **अरण्ये** = in forest; **दिवा** = in day; **निशि** = in night.

### 4. COMMENTARY

This shloka gives the defining test of genuine Sthirata: does the steadiness hold in all conditions? Or does it depend on the right environment, the right company, the right time of day?

The vivechan offers a beautiful analogy: the person in whom steadiness has truly taken root — whose speech, mind, and actions are all saturated with that steadiness — is like someone for whom the bright light of day causes no excessive excitement and the deep darkness of night causes no fear or agitation. They are the same. Village or forest, noise or silence, day or night — it does not matter. Their mind is held in the grace of atmasvarupa (their own true self). In their speech flows the river of purnananda (complete bliss). In their body radiates the light of purnananda.

The vivechan connects this to the fully integrated state of Samras — where consciousness, speech, and body have all merged into one steady flow of self-awareness. This is not a suppression of experience but an illumination from within so bright that external conditions cannot disturb it, just as a great lamp cannot be blown out by the breeze that barely moves an ordinary candle.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The yogi of genuine steadiness is the same in the village and the forest, by day and by night. Circumstances don't change them. That is the test: not how steady you are in ideal conditions, but whether steadiness holds when conditions change.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Think of your inner state in two contrasting environments — a quiet place of practice versus a chaotic, demanding situation. How much does it change? What would it mean to carry the same quality of presence into both?

## SHLOKA 5

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

अतंगत महाशाल्यमस्थर्यं यदि नोड् दत्म् ।
क्रिद्वीषपत्व को वीषरतदा गुणमयच्छत् ॥४॥२०॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If the great disease of unsteadiness has not been removed from within — then what virtue can be truly attained, and what quality can fully manifest?

### 3. COMMENTARY

The shloka is a rhetorical challenge: remove unsteadiness first — everything else follows. Leave it in place — everything else is blocked.

The vivechan here develops the disease metaphor. The stomach carries the accumulated filth of a long illness. Before that filth is cleared — before the stomach is truly cleansed — no medicine, however excellent, however costly, however divinely prescribed, can work properly. You can receive the best medical treatment in the world, take the finest medicines, follow the most precise regimen. But as long as the underlying filth remains, it will block every cure. The illness returns. The patient cannot be healed.

This is precisely the situation with the soul suffering from the disease of unsteadiness. We perform elaborate religious practices — puja, pratikraman, samayik, tap, jap. We seek guidance from great saints. We read profound shastras. But if the fundamental disease — chanchalta (agitation), vikshipta-chitta (distracted mind), asthi-rta (restlessness) — has not been addressed, all these medicines will be absorbed imperfectly and expelled quickly. The filth remains. The disease continues. True healing requires first clearing the stomach: steadying the mind.

The vivechan makes a pointed observation about why so many devout practitioners find themselves asking: why don't I have inner peace after all this practice? The answer, painful as it is, is Sthirata — or its absence. As long as the mind is restless and the heart is chasing poudgalic pleasures, no amount of outward religious activity can produce the deep inner purification it is designed to create.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

If unsteadiness — the restless, scattered, jumping mind — has not been removed, no virtue can take full root. All spiritual practice is like pouring medicine into a stomach full of filth. Clear the filth first. That is what Sthirata does.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

Are there spiritual practices in your life that feel hollow or effortful — that don't seem to produce the depth you expect? Is it possible the obstacle is not the practice itself but the instability of mind that the practice is entering into?

## SHLOKA 6

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

चारित्रं स्थिरतारूपमत. सिद्धे ज्वर्पीच्यते ॥
यतन्तां यतयोऽवश्यमस्या एव प्रसिद्धये ॥६॥२२॥

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Steadfastness itself is the true nature of right conduct (charitra). This has been proclaimed by the Siddhas (liberated beings). Therefore, O yogis, strive with full effort for the perfect accomplishment of this steadiness.

### 3. ANVAYARTHA

**चारित्रं** = right conduct; **स्थिरतारूपम्** = is of the nature of steadfastness; **सिद्धैः** = by the Siddhas/liberated beings; **उपदिश्यते** = has been taught/proclaimed; **यतन्ताम्** = let them strive; **यतयः** = the yogis/ascetics; **अवश्यम्** = certainly/necessarily; **अस्याः** = of this; **प्रसिद्धये** = for the accomplishment/perfection.

### 4. COMMENTARY

This shloka makes a stunning philosophical declaration: charitra (right conduct) is not a list of rules. It is not a set of observances. It IS Sthirata. Steadiness is conduct. Conduct is steadiness.

The vivechan elaborates on what this means. The Siddhas — who have achieved the highest liberation — are described as beings in whom not even a subtle vibration of the soul's pure substance remains. Not even the subtlest spandan (tremor). Their charitra (right conduct) is this absolute steadiness: no agitation, no instability, no movement toward or away from anything. This is not a suppression of activity but the natural expression of a fully purified nature.

For those still on the path, this declaration is practical guidance: if you want right conduct — if you want the inner alignment that the shastras call samyak charitra — focus on Sthirata. All other ethical and spiritual behaviors that flow from a truly stable mind are genuinely effective. All ethical and spiritual behaviors attempted from an unstable, poudgalic-craving mind are compromised at the root.

The vivechan closes this passage with a story that crystallizes the point: a young muni (monk) once went out for alms in the blazing midday heat. Already weakened by the heat, he passed a beautiful mansion — and a young woman's glance from the window caught his attention. In that moment of distraction, his years of sadhana (practice) were swept away. Instability showed its face. A single moment of wavering undid enormous spiritual work.

The conclusion: how precious is Sthirata! It is the guardian of all virtues. Without it, the most carefully built structure collapses in an instant.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

The Siddhas have declared it: steadfastness is not one virtue among many — it is the very nature of right conduct itself. O seekers, strive for this steadiness with complete effort. Everything else rests on it.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What would your ethical and spiritual life look like if you stopped trying to follow rules from the outside and instead focused entirely on cultivating inner steadiness? What naturally arises from a truly stable mind?

## SHLOKA 7

### 1. SANSKRIT TEXT

उत्तीरविचरसि स्थातारस्येये पवन यदि ।
समायेयम्मनेयस्व यदा दियदेविष्यति ॥७॥२३॥

⚠️ *Text partially reconstructed*

### 2. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

If you cultivate steadiness continuously — remaining unshaken, like the wind that moves through the world without being entangled in it — then that steadiness will unfailingly illuminate and uplift you.

### 3. COMMENTARY

The closing shloka of the chapter is a direct encouragement and a promise. The vivechan here offers the ratna-deepak metaphor in its full form: Sthirata is a jewel-lamp (ratna-deepak). Where the ratna-deepak of inner steadiness burns — in the heart where all names and forms of the atma-gurus (spiritual masters of the soul) are absorbed in meditation, where the mind is steeped in this steadiness — that is where the inner temple becomes luminous (man-mandir devadeepyaman).

The practical promise: cultivate steadiness through constant practice of shubha (auspicious) thoughts and dharmic activity, avoiding the distractions of poudgalic pleasures. When the mind is filled from within with virtuous thought — dana (giving), seva (service), compassion, care for others — the shubha thought itself becomes the vehicle of steadiness. And when even one shubha thought takes deep root and flourishes, it dissolves the scattered agitation (vikalpa) from within. Slowly, the distracted mind becomes steady. Steadiness produces more steadiness. The ratna-deepak brightens.

The chapter closes with a declaration: through Sthirata, the soul free from pap-pravritta (sinful tendencies) can dwell always in shubha-pravritta (auspicious tendencies), remain absorbed in the purest self-awareness, and transcend the ordinary human condition. Let this steadiness be cultivated — and let it become, in time, the effortless permanent nature of the being.

### 5. SIMPLY PUT

Cultivate steadiness. Continuously. Allow it to pervade your inner life like light. When that steadiness takes root and shines as a jewel-lamp, it illuminates everything — and the path forward becomes clear and certain.

### 6. CONTEMPLATE

What one practice, if done consistently every day, would most increase your inner steadiness? Not necessarily a formal religious practice — perhaps something simple: a pause before reacting, five minutes of deliberate stillness, choosing not to follow one habitual thought. What would the jewel-lamp of your steadiness look like if you lit it every day?

- Chapter 3 covers Sthirata (Steadfastness) — the quality that turns the flash of inner recognition into a stable dwelling. Where Purnata said "you are full" and Magnata said "become absorbed," Sthirata says "stay there."
