# Gyansaar — Chapter 14: Vidyā (Knowledge)
## Shlokas ॥१॥१०५॥ through ॥८॥११२॥

---

### Overview
Chapter 14 explores **vidyā** — genuine knowledge — as the precise antidote to avidyā (ignorance). Eight shlokas map the full structure of avidyā and its dismantling: the three core confusions (transient=permanent, impure=pure, other=self), the moha-thief who enters through par-saṃyog, the tri-koṇa of wealth-life-body, the futility of purifying the body with water, the samata-kuṇḍa as the true bath, the ātmabodha pāśa of "I and mine," jīva-pudgal bhinnata as the wonder of jnāna-dṛṣṭi, and finally the three ātmā stages — bāhirātmā, antarātmā, paramātmā.

**Four Movements:**
1. Avidyā's Three Confusions & Moha-Chor (1–2)
2. The Lakshmī-Āyuṣya-Śarīra Tri-Koṇa (3–4)
3. Samata-Kuṇḍa & The Ātmabodha Pāśa (5–6)
4. Jīva-Pudgal Bhinnata & The Three Ātmā Stages (7–8)

---

### Shloka 1 — ॥१॥१०५॥
**Sanskrit:**
नित्यशुच्यात्मताष्यातिरनित्याशुच्यनात्मसु ।
अविद्या तत्वधीविद्या, योगाचाये: प्रकीतिता ॥१॥१०५॥

**Translation:**
The yogācāryas have declared: considering the transient, impure, and non-self (pudgala) as permanent, pure, and self — this is avidyā. The true understanding (tatva-dhī) is vidyā.

**Vivechan:**
Three core confusions that define avidyā:
- Par-saṃyog (external connection) mistaken as nitya (permanent)
- Aśuci (impure) body mistaken as śuci (pure)
- Jaḍa-pudgal mistaken as ātmā (self, "mine")

This ahambhāvanā and mamavṛddhi is avidyā. It is the obstruction within maun. It is the vighna in sādhu-sādhanā. As long as this is not conquered, the perfection of sādhu-jīvan is impossible. The root of all karmā-sitam and nārkīya yantranāeṁ borne over countless yugas is this very avidyā. The reversal: recognize ātmā as nitya — recognize ātmā as pavitra — let the ahambhāvanā arise in ātmā alone.

---

### Shloka 2 — ॥२॥१०६॥
**Sanskrit:**
य: पश्येद् नित्यमात्मानमनित्यं परसंगमम् ।
छलं लब्धु न शक्नोति तस्य मोहमलिम्लुच: ॥२॥१०६॥

**Translation:**
The one who always sees the ātmā as imperishable and par-padārtha's association as perishable — the moha-thief (malimlucha) can find no crevice to enter in him.

**Vivechan:**
Three upāyas for keeping moha out of ātma-pradeśa:
1. Ātmā's darśan as avināśī (imperishable) — the living recognition, not just the intellectual knowledge.
2. Par-pudgal-saṃyog as vināśī (perishable) — seeing every external connection in its transience.
3. Moha's entry into ātma-pradeśa prohibited — the dṛḍha saṃkalpa that moha shall not step foot.

Where par-saṃyog's sukha-kalpanā arises, moha-mahārāja walks in without hesitation. This is why the ātmā must see par-saṃyog as anitya without exception. The ātmā has never directly experienced sukha — from anādi kāl, it has always searched for sukha in pudgal. Until tatva-dṛṣṭi opens in both directions ("I am nitya, avināśī, abhinavar") — moha enters ātmabhūmi and creates catastrophic destruction.

### Shloka 3 — ॥३॥१०७॥
**Sanskrit:**
तरंगतरलां लक्ष्मीमायुर्वायुवदस्थिरम् ।
अदक्षधीरनुव्यायेदक्षवद् वपु: ॥३॥१०७॥

**Translation:**
The wise person sees: lakshmī is as fickle as an ocean wave; āyuṣya is as unstable as a gust of wind; śarīra is as perishable as a cloud. The unwise see the opposite.

**Vivechan:**
The tri-koṇa (triangle) of lakshmī-jīvan-śarīra has enslaved the jīvātmā's buddhi, made it directionless, limited its vicāra-śakti. The entire enormous novel of saṃsāra is written on this tri-koṇa. Open any page — this triangle appears. All dvandvas (rāga-dveṣa, harṣa-viṣāda, puṇya-pāpa, āśā-nirāśā) have this tri-koṇa at their root. It is the smśāna (cremation ground) of the pure, elevated, ātmānulakṣī bhāvanās. Three contemplation prescriptions:
- At the ocean shore on a full-moon night — see lakshmī in the waves and bid farewell to lakshmī-lālasā forever.
- On a high mountain peak in howling wind — hear the kacchā saṅgīt of jīvan's asthiratā and resolve to release the clinging to jīvan.
- In the monsoon forest among gathering clouds — hear the kaṣāyamayatā of kāyā and inwardly decide to relinquish kāyā-spṛhā.

When this avidyā-tri-koṇa is seen in its true form, the dedīpyamān saudarya of vidyā flowers, and the ātmā arrives at: jnān-lakṣmī, ātmā's svalaya-dhana-jīvan, and ātmadravya's ananta sampat — where pūrṇānanda and saccidānanda are experienced.

### Shloka 4 — ॥४॥१०८॥
**Sanskrit:**
शुचीन्यप्यशुचीकतु समयेऽशुचीसंभवे ।
देहे जलादिना शौचभ्रमो मूढस्य दारुण: ॥४॥१०८॥

**Translation:**
That which has the power to make even pure substances impure — born of impure materials — the attempt to purify that body with water is a dāruṇa bhrama (terrible delusion) of the mūḍha.

**Vivechan:**
The Bhavabhāvanā quote: "Sukka piuṇo māuẽ soṇiyaṁ tadubhayaṁ pi saṃsaṭṭhaṁ | tappāḍhamāe jīvo āhārai tattha uppanno ||" — the body is formed of the union of father's śukra and mother's rūdhira; the jīvātmā enters and for the first time takes āhāra from these śukra-rūdhira pudgals.

The body's mūla svabhāv: making the pure impure, turning fragrance into stench, turning the well-formed misshapen. Apply camphor, musk, sandalwood — the body converts all of it to aśuddhi within hours. Bathe a thousand times with perfumed soap — two-three ghante bīte na bīte, the body returns to its mūla svabhāv: sveda-tarbatar, mala-gandha. This saṃsāra's ultimate mūḍhata: attempting to make the body — which is itself born from and constituted of aśuci — permanently śuci. Śarīra is sādhan, not sādhya. The ātmā must use the body as an instrument for mokṣamārga — every dhātu, every indriya, every spandana directed toward mokṣa-ārādhanā.

### Shloka 5 — ॥५॥१०९॥ (numbered ॥५॥१०८॥ in text)
**Sanskrit:**
य भ्नात्वा समताकुण्डे हित्वा कर्मलज मलम् ।
पूजनं माति मातिन सोऽनरात्मा पर सुचि ॥५॥१०९॥

**Translation:**
The one who bathes in the samata-kuṇḍa, casting away the mala born of karma-pāpa — without desiring worship — that antarātmā is supremely pure in the world.

**Vivechan:**
The samata-kuṇḍa: filled with the deep water of upaśama. Bathe fully in it. The pāpa-paṅka on the ātmā is washed away. Samyaktva's parama-pavitrată is received.

Once the ātmā has received samyag-darśan's amogh-śakti, it will never be defeated in karma's battlefield. Such a samakitī ātmā does not bind karma of uttama sthiti — at most koḍākoḍī sāgaropama. This is its natural purity.

Three types of mala destroyed by samata-rasa immersion (from Adhyātmasāra):
- "Dṛṣṭā smaran śuṣyet" — viṣaya-vāsanā's venom dries from the eyes
- "Krodhatāpa kṣayaṃ vrajet" — krodha's heat cools
- "Strauḍhyamala-nāśaḥ syāt" — the filth of svacchandatā (self-willedness) washes away

The real bath: external body-purification with water vs. antarātmā-purification through samata. The Adhyātmasāra quote: "Ātmiva samatāṇekā nimṛttā bharatāśav | na hi kabbamahu-ṭhāna-mahūsevā tu kiṃcana" — The ātmā's samata alone, when internalized, gives the greatest benefit; no number of external bathing rites can match it.

### Shloka 6 — ॥६॥११०॥
**Sanskrit:**
आत्मबोधो नव पाशो, देहगेहघनादिषु ।
य:क्षिप्तोज्नात्मना तेषु स्वस्य वन्धाय जायते ॥६॥११०॥

**Translation:**
Considering the body, home, and wealth as ātmā — this is a new, extraordinary bondage-pāśa (noose). The pāśa thrown by ātmā onto body/home/wealth — binds not the body but the ātmā itself.

**Vivechan:**
The paradox: ātmā throws the pāśa of "mine" onto śarīra, gṛha, dhana — yet the pāśa binds ātmā itself, not the objects. This is the alaukika pāśa. "Main aur merā" — this anādikālīna ahaṃkāra and mamatvakāra (avidyā) is what keeps the jīva wandering in saṃsāra's labyrinth, trapped in karma's chains, rotting in naraka-nigoda's duḥkhas, lost in the dvandvas of sukha-duḥkha.

Bāhirātmabhāva: "kāyādi bāhirātmā" — the vṛddhi of ātmīyatā in the body is bāhirātma-daśā. In this state: viṣaya-loluptā and kaṣāya-kadāgraha (willful indulgence in passions) thrive in svacchandatā. Signs of this state: lack of śraddhā in tatva, dveṣa toward guṇas.

The Adhyātmasāra verse confirms: "viṣayakaṣāyāveśa tattvāśraddhā guṇeṣu dveṣa | ātmajñāna ca yadā bāhyātmā syāt tathā bhavek ||" — These are the signs of bāhirātmāvasthā. Historical parallels: Puḍama-chakravartī, Hitler, Napoleon — all consumed by gṛha-dhana-mamakāra and destroyed.

### Shloka 7 — ॥७॥१११॥ (numbered ॥७॥१०७॥ in text variant)
**Sanskrit:**
मिथोयुक्तपदार्थानामर्शांकमद्वसत्क्रिया ।
चिन्मात्रपरिज्ञामेन विदुर्षवानुभूयते ॥७॥१११॥

**Translation:**
The wonder of the distinctness of jīva and pudgala — these substances that appear mixed together — is experienced only by the jnāna-mātṛ parijñāna (wisdom-only recognition) of the vidvān.

**Vivechan:**
Five dravyas exist in this eternal, infinite world: 1. Dharmāstikāya, 2. Adharmāstikāya, 3. Ākāśāstikāya, 4. Pudgalāstikāya, 5. Jīvāstikāya. Each has its own svātantrya-astitva (independent existence), its own svarūpa, its own function. No dravya's existence merges into another. No dravya does the work of another.

- Dharmāstikāya: arūpī, aids jīva in motion
- Adharmāstikāya: aids in stasis/stillness
- Ākāśāstikāya: provides avakāśa (space)
- Pudgalāstikāya: all that is visible, rūpī, in constant transformation
- Jīvāstikāya: cetana, its svabhāva is jnānādi self-expression; ātmā's jnān-guṇa never enters pudgala; pudgala's guṇa never enters ātmā

Siddhasena Diwākarjī from Sammati-tarka: "Sā tānnānusyāchāṇa... bhāḍ dṛḍhapārichhayāpāṇa vāyat visesapajjāyā" — "Like different colors of water appearing mixed — jīva and pudgala in their special paryāyas must be distinguished." This seeing — jīva as distinct from pudgala — is vidyā. Not a theoretical distinction but a lived anubhava through jnāna-dṛṣṭi.

### Shloka 8 — ॥८॥११२॥ *(Hero Shloka)*
**Sanskrit:**
पादिठ्यानिनिदरस्मो एशा विद्याज्ञानसंपशा ।
पर्यन्त परमासम्व लाभमान्यय हि ओलिगिन ॥८॥११२॥

**Translation (reconstructed from vivechan):**
This is vidyā — the parijñāna of the yoga-yukta: abandoning the name placed on bāhirātmā and anātmā, and finding paramāsukha in the ātmā itself.

**Vivechan:**
The three ātmā stages — the chapter's culmination: bāhirātmā → antarātmā → paramātmā.

The prāṇādivān ṃ "ratā satvā" darśan does not get destroyed — it manifests at the summit of sattvā's udaya. The passage toward paramātmā: to hold firmly the recognition that "We are paramātmā" — teraveṁ or chaudaveṁ guṇasthānak having been attained.

Without mānasika śānti — without krodha, māna, māyā, matsara, kalaha, vipāda, and vairavṛtti being quieted — vidyā does not fully manifest. Moha-āsakti cannot be released.

The Adhyātmasāra confirms: "Saṃsārata sabhī... mana ko kuma dhuna byālambana meṁ sthira kara, yadi dhyāna sarā jāya toa mana ko śānta hotā hai. Īvālambana meṁ sthira kara upaśānta ho jāte hai. Tab ātmā kī jyoti sahaja jīvādi nikāra upaśānta ho jātī." And: "Śānte namani vabhoti prakāśate śāntamātmana sahajam." — The peaceful mind is illuminated; the light of the peaceful ātmā shines forth naturally.

Hero shloka's conclusion: vidyā = the living recognition of jīva's distinctness from pudgala + the three ātmā stages traversed + mānasika śānti established = paramātmā-darśana in one's own ātmā.

---

### Chapter Summary
Vidyā is the direct dissolution of avidyā — not through suppression but through clarity. Eight shlokas build the map: avidyā = three core confusions (1) → moha-thief enters only through par-saṃyog's sukha-kalpanā (2) → the lakshmī-jīvan-śarīra tri-koṇa is saṃsāra's entire novel (3) → body-purification with water is dāruṇa bhrama; śarīra is sādhan not sādhya (4) → samata-kuṇḍa is the only true bath; three mala dissolved (5) → "I and mine" as ātmabodha pāśa — the alaukika noose that binds only the ātmā (6) → jīva-pudgal bhinnata is the chamatkāra known only to jnāna-dṛṣṭi (7) → vidyā = bāhirātmā → antarātmā → paramātmā, with mānasika śānti as the final opening (8).
