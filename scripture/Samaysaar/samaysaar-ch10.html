Samaysaar · Adhikar 9 · Gathas 308–415

Pure Knowledgeसर्वविशुद्धज्ञान अधिकार

All 108 gathas of the ninth Adhikar — the jñāyaka is the jñāyaka alone; rāga is your own pariṇāma; jñāna is non-separate from the knower; darśana-jñāna-cāritra — not any external garb — is the only path to liberation

The washing stone whitens clothes by its own nature — it is the washing stone alone. The knower knows by its own nature — it is the knower alone. The entire ninth Adhikar is a sustained proof: the soul's knowing-nature is utterly untouched by karma, body, sense-objects, and even scripture. You, the soul, are jñāna itself.

जम्हा जाणिदे णिचं तम्हा जीवो दु जाणगो णाणी।
णाणं च जाणयादो अव्विदिरित्तं मुणेयव्वं।।

"Because jīva always knows, therefore jīva is jñāyaka — the knower — the jñānī. And jñāna should be understood as avyatiriktam (non-separate, non-distinct) from the jñāyaka." — Gatha 403

About This Section

Sarvavishuddhajñāna

Sarvavishuddhajñāna — Pure All-Knowledge — is the summit of the Samaysaar's nine adhikars. Kundakunda opens with the foundational ontology: dravya and its guṇas are ananya (non-separate), but different dravyas are strictly distinct. The soul cannot be produced by karma nor can it produce karma. This mutual distinctness, combined with the nimitta-naimittika relationship that sustains saṃsāra, is the structural foundation on which the entire adhikar rests.

The central thesis: the soul is jñāyaka — the eternal knower — alone. Jñāna is avyatiriktam (non-separate) from jñāyaka. This knowing-nature has nothing to do with any para-dravya: not karma, not body, not sense-objects, not dharma-astikāya, not the act of doing or experiencing. Every trace of ownership (mamakāra), agency (kartṛtva), and enjoyership (bhoktṛtva) projected onto the soul is mithyā. The path to liberation is darśana-jñāna-cāritra — not any external garb (liṅga).

Gathas 308–415

All 108 gathas of Adhikar 9, presented with original Prakrit, Sanskrit chāyā, English translation, and commentary — organized into eight thematic parts.

Part 1 · G308–G315 · Non-Separate Transformation: Soul and Its Modifications Are One
Foundational Ontology The Gold-Bracelet Analogy

Kundakunda opens Adhikar 9 with the foundational ontology: dravya and its guṇas are ananya (non-separate). Gold cannot exist without some form — bracelet, ring, ingot. The soul cannot exist without its own paryāyas. But those paryāyas are its own — not karma's. This non-separateness of substance and quality, combined with the strict distinctness of different substances, is the structural foundation of everything that follows.

G308

जह णवि होदि सुवण्णं विणा कडगसोहणेण अण्णेण।
तह णवि होंति जीवा विणा णियएण पज्जएण।।३०८।।

Gold-Bracelet Analogy: Just as gold does not exist without its bracelet-form (or some other form) — similarly jīvas do not exist without their own paryāyas (modifications).

Dravya and its guṇas are ananya (non-separate). The gold is inseparable from some modification; the soul is inseparable from its own paryāyas.

Simply put: You cannot have a soul floating free of all modifications. The soul always manifests as some particular form of knowing, seeing, or feeling. But those forms are its own — not borrowed from karma or body.

AnanyaDravya-ParyāyaSvabhāva
G309–315

Jīva and Ajīva Are Non-Separate from Their Paryāyas; Soul Is No One's Kārya and No Kāraṇa; Mutual Nimitta and Saṃsāra; Release vs. Bondage

G309: Jīva is ananya from its own pariṇāmas; ajīva is ananya from its own pariṇāmas — this applies across all dravyas. G310: Ātmā is not the kārya (product) of any other dravya, and not the kāraṇa (cause) of any other dravya. Each dravya arises from its own svabhāva. G311–313: Karma depends on the cetayitā (conscious soul) and cetayitā depends on karma — this mutual nimitta-naimittika relationship is the mechanism of saṃsāra. G314: As long as the cetayitā does not release the prakṛti-paryāyas, it remains ajñāyaka, mithyādṛṣṭi, and asaṃyata. G315: When it releases — it becomes vimukta (free), jñāyaka, darśaka, and muni.

Simply put: The soul is not "inside" karma and karma is not "inside" the soul. They are distinct dravyas with a nimitta relationship. The delusion is mistaking this nimitta for identity. The watershed is not cosmological — it is the inner release of false identification.

Part 2 · G316–G323 · The Knower Does Not Experience; Only Knows
G316

अण्णाणी जीवो पुण खवेदि कम्मं खु णाणिणो णेव।
होंति अवेदया कम्मं णाणिणो णादू।।३१६।।

The ajñānī (ignorant) experiences the karmaphala within the prakṛti-svabhāva (karma's own nature). The jñānī only knows — does not experience. The jñānī is avedaka (non-experiencer) of karma; only jñātā (knower).

This is the core distinction. The ajñānī becomes the pain; the jñānī knows the pain happening in karma. Same event; two different modes of the soul.

Simply put: When pain arises, the ajñānī becomes the pain — "I am in agony." The jñānī knows the pain arising in that pudgala-process — "pain is arising there." Same event; two entirely different modes of consciousness.

AvedakaJñātāAjñānī vs Jñānī
G317–323

Snake-Milk Analogy; Avedaka in Nirveda; The Eye Analogy: Akāraka and Avedaka

G317: The abhavya is like snake-milk — even in a golden vessel, it becomes poison. G318: The jñānī, established in nirveda (detachment), is avedaka — does not experience even what is experienced. G319: The jñānī neither does nor experiences karma — only knows. This is the absolute definition. G320: Just as the eye (akṣi) is akāraka (non-doer) and avedaka — it illuminates without doing anything — similarly jñāna is akāraka and avedaka. G321–323: "Viṣṇu created the world" and "ātmā created the six kāya" — both commit the same error of kartā-bhāva. Jñāna does not create its objects.

Simply put: Your eye doesn't "do" the seeing — it illuminates. Similarly, jñāna doesn't "do" the knowing — it illuminates the objects. No karma is made in the mere act of knowing.

Part 3 · G324–G348 · External Substance Is Not Mine; The Four-Way Wrong-Belief Logic
G324–327

Para-Dravya Is Never Mine

G324: By niścaya (absolute standpoint) — not even a single paramāṇu (atom) of para-dravya is mine. G325: Village, country, body — all mamakāra (ownership) is ajñānī delusion. G326–327: The mithyādṛṣṭi who claims para-dravya as his own; those who have kartṛtva-vyavasāya (determination of agency) toward para-dravyas — they are dṛṣṭi-rahita (sight-deprived).

NiścayaMamakāraPara-dravya
G328–348

The Four-Way Mithyātva Logic; Karma Is the Kartā; Syādvāda on Doer-Experiencer

G328–331: Four forms of mithyātva arise when jīva treats para as sva: (1) claiming karma's modifications as one's own paryāyas; (2) claiming karma's fruit as one's own experience; (3) claiming one's pariṇāmas as caused by para-dravya; (4) claiming one's own pariṇāmas as belonging to para-dravya. All four are mithyā. Niścaya sees each dravya arising from its own svabhāva alone. G332–334: Karma makes everything: the ajñānī, the jñānī, sleep, waking, happiness, unhappiness, mithyātva. Karma is the kartā. G335–339: All jīvas are akāraka. Karma desires karma — there is no brahmacārī-breaker. Karma kills karma — there is no killer. G342–343: Ātmā is nitya (eternal), asaṃkhyāta-pradeśī (innumerable-spaced), lokamātra. G345–348: Syādvāda: only the correct anekānta position — jīva is kartā of its own pariṇāmas, avedaka of karma's pariṇāmas.

Commentary: This appears Sāṃkhya-like, but the distinction is crucial. Kārikā Sāṃkhya says prakṛti is the agent and puruṣa is inert. Samaysaar says: when the soul mistakenly identifies with prakṛti-paryāyas, karma binds; when it releases that identification, karma exhausts itself. The jīva's non-agency is the result of jñāna — not an inherent inertness.

Part 4 · G349–G365 · The Craftsman and Merchant Analogies
G349–355

Śilpī (Craftsman) — Vyavahāra and Niścaya

G349–352 (Vyavahāra): From the conventional standpoint, the craftsman (soul) does karma, uses karma, grasps karma, enjoys karma's fruit — but is not tammaya (identified) with karma. G353–355 (Niścaya): From niścaya — the craftsman who becomes ananya (one) with the cheṭṭā (activity) is perpetually duḥkhī (suffering). The jīva that identifies with its activities of karma is bound.

Śilpī AnalogyVyavahāraNiścayaTammaya
G356

सेडिया सेडियामेव जाणगो जाणगो हवे।
पासओ पासगो हवे संजदो संजदो हवे।।३५६।।

The seṭikā (washing stone) is seṭikā alone. The jñāyaka is jñāyaka alone. The pāśaka (observer) is pāśaka alone. The saṃyata (restrained) is saṃyata alone.

By niścaya, each thing is only what it is. The seṭikā whitens clothes — but niścaya says: the seṭikā is seṭikā; the whiteness belongs to the cloth's own transformation. Similarly: the jñāyaka is jñāyaka; knowing is its own; what is "known" belongs to the jñeya's own nature.

Simply put: The soul is not "a knower of karma" or "a knower of the body." The soul is jñāyaka — period. That is its identity, completely, without residue.

Seṭikā AnalogyJñāyaka AloneNiścaya
G357–365

Seṭikā — Niścaya Continued; Seṭikā — Vyavahāra

G357–360 (Niścaya): Darśana is darśana alone. The jñāyaka is the jñāyaka alone. These are not modified by what they observe. G361–365 (Vyavahāra): From vyavahāra: the seṭikā whitens para-dravya by its own svabhāva → similarly: the jñātā knows (by own svabhāva), the jīva sees (by own svabhāva), the jñātā abandons (by own svabhāva), the samyag-dṛṣṭi has śraddhā (by own svabhāva). All by sva-svabhāva.

Part 5 · G366–G382 · Knowledge Outside External Substance; Attachment Is Own Transformation
G366–372

Darśana-Jñāna-Cāritra Have Nothing in Achetana; Rāga Is Your Own Pariṇāma

G366–368: Darśana, jñāna, and cāritra have nothing (kiṃcit nāsti) in insentient viṣayas, in karma, in the body. So why does the ātmā cause harm in those domains? G370: The jīva's guṇas are not in para-dravyas. Therefore the samyag-dṛṣṭi has no rāga toward sense-objects. G371: Rāga, dveṣa, moha are the jīva's own ananya-pariṇāmas — they do not exist in śabda and other viṣayas. They arise from the jīva's own modification, not from the external object. G372: No other dravya can produce another dravya's guṇa. Therefore all dravyas arise by their own svabhāva.

Commentary: This is one of the most powerful doctrines in the Samaysaar: Rāga is your modification — not something the object did to you. The beautiful object did not cause your desire. Your soul modified into desire — using the object as nimitta. This cuts the root of blame, projection, and attachment.

Rāga as Own PariṇāmaNo Dravya Makes Another's GuṇaNimitta
G373–382

The Six-Sense Analogy; Ātmā Does Not Move to Grasp

G373: Pudgala transforms as nindā and stuti words. The ajñānī becomes angry or pleased, thinking "this was said to me." G374: The guṇa of pudgala-śabda belongs to pudgala — not to you. G375–381: Śabda doesn't say "grab me." Rūpa doesn't say "grab me." Gandha, rasa, sparśa, guṇa, dravya — none say "grab me." The ātmā doesn't go out to grasp them. The ātmā illuminates them; the jñeyas appear — the ātmā doesn't move. G382: Despite all this teaching, the mūḍha (deluded) still does not attain upaśama (inner calm). The delusion is deep-rooted.

Simply put: The sound that criticized you did not make you angry. You modified into anger — using that sound as nimitta. The object is innocent. The modification is yours. Knowing this dissolves the root of reactivity.

Part 6 · G383–G389 · Ultimate Conduct: The Three Modes
G383–389

The Three Kalpas of Niścaya-Cāritra; Three Forms of Vedanā That Bind Again

G383: Pratikramaṇa = past karma mamtva-tyāga (release of ownership of past karma). G384: Pratyākhyāna = future karma bhāva-nirvṛtti (cessation of intention toward future karma). G385: Ālocana = present udaya-doṣa recognition — consciously seeing the present karma rising. G386: Nitya (constant) pratikramaṇa + ālocana + pratyākhyāna = true cāritra. G387: Experiencing karmaphala as own-rūpa → binds 8-types duḥkha-seeds again. G388: Experiencing thinking "I am the one who did this karmaphala" → binds again. G389: Experiencing and becoming sukha or duḥkha → binds again.

Commentary: These are not ritual recitations performed once a day. By niścaya, these three are a continuous inner attitude: "I was never the owner of past karma" (pratikramaṇa), "I am witnessing what is rising now" (ālocana), "I have no intention toward future karma" (pratyākhyāna). The three traps: (1) "This pain is what I am," (2) "I am the author of this karma," (3) "I am happy / I am miserable." Each of these reinforces bondage even while experiencing karma's fruit. The release: "karma is rising; I am the witness."

PratikramaṇaĀlocanaPratyākhyānaNiścaya-Cāritra
The 148 Karma Declarations Pratyākhyāna-Kalpa (Kalaśas 225–229)

The text provides three complete kalpas with 49 bhaṅgas each (3 yoga × 3 karaṇa + 4 extra combinations = 49) across three time periods. The Pratikramaṇa-Kalpa (past: "What I did/caused/approved by manas/vāc/kāya × 3-karaṇa = mithyā for me"); the Ālocana-Kalpa (present: "I am not doing / causing / approving..."); the Pratyākhyāna-Kalpa (future: "I will not do / cause / approve..."). The Pratyākhyāna-Kalpa covers all 148 karma types across Jñānāvaraṇa (5), Darśanāvaraṇa (9), Vedanīya (2), Mohanīya (28), Āyu (4), Nāma (103), Gotra (2), and Antarāya (5). For each: "Nāhaṃ X bhuje, caitanyātmānamātmānameva sañcetaye" — "I do not experience the fruit of X karma; I consciously experience only the consciousness-natured ātmā."

Part 7 · G390–G403 · Knowledge Is Distinct from All Substances; The Summit
G390–402

Fourteen Propositions of Distinctness; Adhyavasāna Is Not Jñāna

Each gatha follows the same logical form: "X is not jñāna because X doesn't know anything. Therefore jñāna is different and X is different — so Jinas say." Applied to: G390 Śāstra (scripture) — achetana; G391 Śabda (sound) — pudgala paryāya; G392 Rūpa (form); G393 Varṇa (colour); G394 Gandha (smell); G395 Rasa (taste); G396 Sparśa (touch); G397 Karma (karmic matter); G398 Dharma (dharmāstikāya); G399 Adharma; G400 Kāla (time); G401 Ākāśa (space); G402 Adhyavasāna (karmic determination / passion-driven impulse) — achetana. Jñāna and jīva are avyatiriktam. Jñāna and every other dravya are vyatiriktam. Only jīva is cetana — therefore jñāna and jīva are avyatiriktam.

AvyatiriktamVyatiriktamFourteen DravyasAdhyavasāna
G403

जम्हा जाणिदे णिचं तम्हा जीवो दु जाणगो णाणी।
णाणं च जाणयादो अव्विदिरित्तं मुणेयव्वं।।४०३।।

The Summit: Because jīva always knows (nityaṃ jānāti), therefore jīva is jñāyaka — the knower — the jñānī. And jñāna should be understood as avyatiriktam (non-separate, non-different, non-distinct) from the jñāyaka.

Jñāna is not an activity the soul performs. Jñāna is not a product produced by the soul. Jñāna is what the soul is. The knower and the knowing are one substance. This is the summit of Adhikar 9 — and the reason it is called Sarvavishuddhajñāna.

The simple version: The soul is the knower because it always knows. And knowledge is not some separate thing from the knower — they are one. You, the soul, ARE knowing itself.

Jñāyaka = JñānaAvyatiriktamSummitSarvavishuddhajñāna
G404

णाणं सम्मदिदिं दु संजमं सुत्तंगपुव्वगयं।
धम्मादम्मं च तहा पव्वज्जं अभुवंति बुहा।।४०४।।

The budha (wise, jñānī) regard jñāna itself as samyagdṛṣṭi (right faith), as saṃyama (self-restraint), as sūtras and pūrvas (sacred scripture), as dharma and adharma (puṇya and pāpa), and as pravrajyā (initiation into renunciation).

The jñānī does not see samyagdṛṣṭi as a set of beliefs held. The jñānī sees samyagdṛṣṭi as the jñāna itself — the knowing faculty as right seeing. Saṃyama is not a list of restraints — it is the natural non-grasping of jñāna. Pravrajyā is not wearing a garb — it is the inner renunciation that is jñāna's own nature.

Part 8 · G405–G415 · Knowledge Has No Body; External Symbol Is Not the Path to Liberation
G405–411

Ātmā Is Amūrta; Pure Soul Grasps Nothing; Liṅga Is Not the Path

G405: The ātmā is amūrta (formless/immaterial). Āhāra (nourishment/intake) is mūrta (material), being pudgalamaya. Therefore ātmā cannot be āhāraka (one who takes food/intake). G406: Para-dravya cannot be grasped or released by the soul. G407: Therefore the viśuddha-cetā (pure-consciousness soul) neither grasps anything nor releases anything of jīva-ajīva dravyas. G408: The deluded, grasping the pāṣaṇḍī (heterodox ascetic) garb or gṛhī (householder) garb in many forms, declare "this liṅga is the path of mokṣa." G409: Liṅga is not the path of mokṣa. Arhants are nirmmama (beyond mamakāra) regarding the body; they leave the liṅga and serve darśana-jñāna-cāritra. G410: Neither pāṣaṇḍī-liṅga nor gṛhī-liṅga is mokṣa-mārga. Darśana-jñāna-cāritra is the mokṣa-mārga — so Jinas say. G411: Abandoning whatever liṅga is held — engage the ātmā in darśana-jñāna-cāritra, which is the path of mokṣa.

Commentary: This does not abolish external vows or the monastery. The Tīkā clarifies: vyavahāra-naya calls both muni-liṅga and śrāvaka-liṅga the path of mokṣa (as sadhanā containers). Niścaya-naya says: no liṅga is mokṣa-mārga — only darśana-jñāna-cāritra is. The garb is the container; darśana-jñāna-cāritra is the content. Only the content liberates.

AmūrtaLiṅga Is Not PathDarśana-Jñāna-CāritraMokṣa-Mārga
G412

मोक्खपहे अप्पाणं ठवेहि तं चेव झाहि तं चेय।
तत्थेव विहर णिच्चं मा विहरसु अण्णदव्वेसु।।४१२।।

Establish the ātmā on the mokṣa-path; meditate on that (darśana-jñāna-cāritra); contemplate it; dwell there always (nitya); do not dwell (viharsu) in other dravyas.

The Final Instruction — four imperatives and one prohibition. Establish, meditate, experience, dwell. Don't dwell in other dravyas.

The simple version: Four commands: establish, meditate, experience, dwell. One prohibition: don't dwell in other dravyas. This is the entire path in two lines.

G413–414

Those with Mamatā in Liṅgas Have Not Known Samayasāra; Vyavahāra-Naya vs. Niścaya-Naya on Liṅga

G413: Those who carry mamatā (ownership) in pāṣaṇḍī liṅgas or gṛhī liṅgas of many types — they have not known samayasāra (the essence of the soul). The one who wears the muni garb but secretly thinks "this garb is what liberates me" — has not known samayasāra. G414: Vyavahāra-naya calls both liṅgas (muni and śrāvaka) the path of mokṣa. Niścaya-naya does not consider any liṅga as mokṣa-mārga. These are not contradictions — they are two levels of the same truth.

G415

जो समयपाहुडिमणं पढिदूणं अत्थतच्चदो णादुं।
अत्थे ठाही चेदा सो होही उत्तमं सोक्खं।।४१५।।

The Colophon Promise: The cetayitā (conscious soul) who reads this Samayaprābhṛta, knows its meaning and tattvas (principles), and establishes (sthāyate) itself in that meaning — will attain the highest happiness (uttamaṃ saukhyam).

The Samayaprābhṛta is called "śabdabrahma" — equivalent to the universal creative Word — because it illuminates all things as they are. The soul that reads this scripture, grasps its paramārtha (the caitanya-prakāśarūpa ātmā), and establishes itself in that paramārtha — will, in the immediacy of that experience, become radiant with cidānanda and remain in paramānanda eternally.

The simple version: If you read this text, understand it truly, and live in that understanding — you will attain the highest joy. Not someday. The moment the soul establishes itself in its own true nature as jñāyaka, that is the beginning of paramānanda.

Uttama SaukhyaŚabdabrahmaCetayitāParamānanda

इति श्रीमदमृतचन्द्रसूरिविरचितायां समयसारख्याख्यामात्मख्यातौ सर्वविशुद्धज्ञानप्ररूपकः नवमोऽङ्कः।।

Thus in the Ātmakhyāti — the commentary on the Samayasāra composed by the venerable Śrī Amṛtacandrasūri — the Ninth Act, revealer of Sarvavishuddhajñāna, is concluded.
