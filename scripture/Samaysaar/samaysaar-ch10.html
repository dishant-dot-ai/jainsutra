Samaysaar · Adhikar 9 · Gathas 308–415

Pure Knowledgeसर्वविशुद्धज्ञान अधिकार

All 108 gathas of the ninth Adhikar — the jñāyaka is the jñāyaka alone; rāga is your own pariṇāma; jñāna is non-separate from the knower; darśana-jñāna-cāritra — not any external garb — is the only path to liberation

The washing stone whitens clothes by its own nature — it is the washing stone alone. The knower knows by its own nature — it is the knower alone. The entire ninth Adhikar is a sustained proof: the soul's knowing-nature is utterly untouched by karma, body, sense-objects, and even scripture. You, the soul, are jñāna itself.

Featured Verse — G403

जम्हा जाणिदे णिचं तम्हा जीवो दु जाणगो णाणी।
णाणं च जाणयादो अव्विदिरित्तं मुणेयव्वं।।४०३।।

Because (jīva) always knows (nityaṃ jānāti), therefore jīva is jñāyaka — the knower — the jñānī. And jñāna should be understood as avyatiriktam (non-separate, non-distinct) from the jñāyaka.

Jñāna is not an activity the soul performs. Jñāna is not a product produced by the soul. Jñāna is what the soul IS. The knower and the knowing are one substance. This is the summit of Adhikar 9 — and the reason it is called Sarvavishuddhajñāna: pure knowledge, unsullied by any admixture of karma, object, or act.

Jñāyaka Avyatiriktam Nityaṃ Jānāti Jñāna = Jīva
Five Governing Principles
Principle I
Jñāyaka Is Jñāyaka Alone The seṭikā (washing stone) whitens by its own svabhāva — it is the seṭikā alone. The jñāyaka knows by its own svabhāva — it is jñāyaka alone. No karma, no body, no object modifies what the knower essentially is.
Principle II
Karma Is the Kartā All jīvas are akāraka (non-doers) in the ultimate sense. Karma is the kartā. The soul's apparent agency arises from misidentification with karma's modifications. When that misidentification dissolves, karma exhausts itself.
Principle III
Rāga Is Your Own Pariṇāma No external object causes rāga, dveṣa, or moha. The object is nimitta; the pariṇāma arises in the soul from the soul's own modification. No dravya can produce another dravya's guṇa.
Principle IV
The Three Kalpas of Niścaya-Cāritra Pratikramaṇa = past karma mamtva-tyāga. Ālocana = present udaya-doṣa witnessing. Pratyākhyāna = future karma intention-release. These three performed constantly — not ritually — constitute the soul's authentic conduct.
Principle V
Darśana-Jñāna-Cāritra Is the Only Path Liṅga (external garb — muni or householder) is not the mokṣa-mārga. Darśana-jñāna-cāritra is the mokṣa-mārga. The ātmā that establishes itself in its own seeing-knowing-conduct attains uttama saukhya.
Part 1 · G308–G315 · Ananya-Pariṇāma: Soul and Its Modifications Are Non-Separate
Doctrine
The Gold-Bracelet Analogy Kundakunda opens Adhikar 9 with the foundational ontology: dravya and its guṇas are ananya (non-separate). Gold cannot exist without some form — bracelet, ring, ingot. The soul cannot exist without its own paryāyas (modifications). But those paryāyas are its own — not karma's. This non-separateness of substance and quality, combined with the strict distinctness of different substances, is the structural foundation of everything that follows.
G308 — Gold-Bracelet Analogy: Dravya Is Non-Separate from Its Paryāyas

जह णवि होदि सुवण्णं विणा कडगसोहणेण अण्णेण।
तह णवि होंति जीवा विणा णियएण पज्जएण।।३०८।।

Just as gold does not exist without its bracelet-form (or some other form) — similarly jīvas do not exist without their own paryāyas (modifications).

The crucial implication: the soul's modifications are its own niyata (fixed, proper) paryāyas — arising from the soul's own svabhāva. They are not borrowed from karma. And karma's modifications are karma's own paryāyas — arising from karma's own svabhāva. Each dravya has its own stream of modifications.

Simply put: The soul always exists as some specific form of knowing or seeing. Those forms are its own — not forms imposed on it by karma.
Ananya-Pariṇāma Dravya-Guṇa Ananya Niyata-Paryāya
G309–G310 — Each Dravya Arises from Its Own Svabhāva

G309: Jīva is ananya from its own pariṇāmas; ajīva is ananya from its own pariṇāmas. This non-separateness applies across all dravyas — each is non-separate from its own modifications.

G310: The ātmā is not the kārya (product) of any other dravya, and is not the kāraṇa (cause) of any other dravya. Each dravya arises from its own svabhāva alone.

Simply put: The soul didn't make karma, and karma didn't make the soul. They influence each other as nimitta — but neither produces the other's modifications.
Svabhāva-Udbhava Akāraṇa
G311–G315 — Mutual Nimitta-Naimittika and the Pivot of Saṃsāra

G311–G313: The cetayitā (conscious soul) and karma's prakṛti have a mutual nimitta-naimittika (occasion-occasioned) relationship — this is the mechanism of saṃsāra. As long as cetayitā holds karma's paryāyas as its own, it remains ajñāyaka (non-knower) and mithyādṛṣṭi.

G314–G315: The watershed: When cetayitā releases karma's paryāyas (prakṛti-paryāya-tyāga) it becomes vimukta (free), jñāyaka, darśaka, muni. The pivot of all liberation is this inner release — not a cosmological event, but an epistemological one.

Simply put: Saṃsāra runs on one engine: the soul mistaking karma's modifications for its own. The moment that mistaking stops — vimukti begins.
Nimitta-Naimittika Prakṛti-Paryāya-Tyāga Vimukta Jñāyaka
Part 2 · G316–G323 · The Jñānī Only Knows — The Eye Analogy
G316 — The Core Distinction: Jñānī Is Avedaka

अण्णाणी जीवो पुण खवेदि कम्मं खु णाणिणो णेव।
होंति अवेदया कम्मं णाणिणो णादू।।३१६।।

The ajñānī (ignorant) experiences the karmaphala within the prakṛti-svabhāva (karma's own nature). The jñānī only knows — does not experience. The jñānī is avedaka (non-experiencer) of karma; only jñātā (knower).

The same karma rises in both. What differs is not the karma — but the mode of the soul. The ajñānī's soul collapses into the karma ("I am in agony"). The jñānī's soul illuminates the karma without entering it ("pain is arising in that pudgala-process").

Simply put: Same event; two different souls. One becomes the pain. One witnesses it.
Avedaka Jñātā vs Bhoktā Ajñānī vs Jñānī
G320 — The Eye Analogy: Akāraka and Avedaka

Just as the eye (akṣi) is akāraka (non-doer) and avedaka (non-experiencer) — it illuminates objects without doing anything to them — similarly jñāna is akāraka and avedaka with respect to its objects.

The eye doesn't "do" the seeing — it illuminates. The seeing happens. No karma is made by the act of seeing. Similarly: jñāna doesn't "do" the knowing. The knowing happens. No karma is made in the mere act of knowing. The problem is not knowing — it is the kartic reaction (rāga-dveṣa) that knowing is mistakenly paired with.

Simply put: Your eye doesn't get stained by what it sees. Your jñāna doesn't get stained by what it knows. Staining only happens through rāga-dveṣa — which are your own modifications, not caused by objects.
Akāraka Eye Analogy Jñāna Is Not Karma
Part 3 · G324–G348 · Para-Dravya Is Never Mine; Four-Way Mithyatva
Doctrine
Karma Is the Kartā — All Jīvas Are Akāraka G332–G339 contain one of the most radical doctrinal statements in the Samaysaar: karma makes everything — the ajñānī, the jñānī, sleep, waking, happiness, unhappiness, mithyātva, asaṃyama, wandering in three worlds. All jīvas are akāraka (non-doers). Karma desires karma — there is no brahmacārī-breaker. Karma kills karma — there is no killer. This appears Sāṃkhya-like, but the purpose is precise: not to deny the soul, but to dissolve the soul's false kartā-bhāva. The soul's non-agency is the result of jñāna — not an inherent inertness.
G324–G327 — Not Even One Paramāṇu of Para-Dravya Is Mine

G324: By niścaya (absolute standpoint) — not even a single paramāṇu (atom) of para-dravya is mine.

G325: Village, country, body — all mamakāra (ownership-sense) is the delusion of the ajñānī. The samyag-dṛṣṭi knows: these are not mine by any niścaya-measure.

G326–G327: The mithyādṛṣṭi who claims para-dravya as his own; those who have kartṛtva-vyavasāya (determined resolve of agency) toward para-dravyas — they are dṛṣṭi-rahita (sight-deprived). Their very resolution to be the doer of para-dravya is what constitutes mithyā-dṛṣṭi.

Simply put: By ultimate measure, nothing outside the soul is the soul's. Body, village, relationships — all nimitta, not sva. The moment this is grasped with certainty, mamakāra dissolves.
Para Not Mine Mamakāra Niścaya Naya
G328–G331 — The Four-Way Mithyatva Logic

Four forms of mithyātva arise when jīva treats para as sva:

1. Claiming karma's modifications as one's own paryāyas — "these feelings of bondage are what I am"

2. Claiming karma's fruit as one's own experience — "I am enjoying/suffering this"

3. Claiming one's own pariṇāmas as caused by para-dravya — "that object made me angry"

4. Claiming one's own pariṇāmas as belonging to para-dravya — confusing the domains entirely

All four are mithyā. Niścaya sees each dravya arising from its own svabhāva alone — karma from karma's svabhāva, soul from soul's svabhāva.

Simply put: All delusion takes one form — confusing your modifications with karma's, or karma's with yours. Clear seeing severs this confusion at the root.
Four-Way Mithyātva Sva vs Para Svabhāva-Udbhava
Part 4 · G349–G365 · The Śilpī and Seṭikā Analogies
G356–G360 — The Seṭikā (Washing Stone): Niścaya View — Jñāyaka Is Jñāyaka Alone

सेडिया सेडियामेव जाणगो जाणगो हवे।
पासओ पासगो हवे संजदो संजदो हवे।।३५६।।

The seṭikā (washing stone) is seṭikā alone. The jñāyaka is jñāyaka alone. The pāśaka (observer) is pāśaka alone. The saṃyata (restrained one) is saṃyata alone.

G357–G360 continue: Darśana is darśana alone. Jñāna is jñāna alone. The jñāyaka does not become something else by virtue of what it knows. The seṭikā whitens — but the seṭikā remains the seṭikā; the whitened cloth is the cloth's own transformation. Similarly: the jñāyaka knows — but the jñāyaka remains the jñāyaka; the known object undergoes its own transformations by its own svabhāva.

Simply put: You don't become the objects you know. You remain the knower. Always. That is your identity — jñāyaka — and it cannot be modified by what passes through your awareness.
Seṭikā Analogy Jñāyaka Is Jñāyaka Niścaya View
G361–G365 — The Seṭikā: Vyavahāra View — All by Own Svabhāva

From vyavahāra: the seṭikā whitens para-dravya by its own svabhāva. Similarly: the jñātā knows by own svabhāva, the jīva sees by own svabhāva, the jñātā abandons by own svabhāva, the samyag-dṛṣṭi has śraddhā by own svabhāva.

Both the niścaya and the vyavahāra views converge on the same truth: every modification of the soul — knowing, seeing, restraining, having faith — arises from the soul's own svabhāva. Nothing is borrowed from outside. Nothing is imposed from karma or body.

Simply put: When you see rightly — that is your own seeing. When you have faith — that is your own faith. Even liberation is your own pariṇāma, arising from your own svabhāva.
Sva-Svabhāva Vyavahāra View
Part 5 · G366–G382 · Rāga Is Your Own Pariṇāma; Jñāna Outside All Para-Dravyas
G371–G372 — Rāga-Dveṣa-Moha Are Your Own; No Dravya Produces Another's Guṇa

G371: Rāga, dveṣa, and moha are the jīva's own ananya-pariṇāmas — they do not exist in śabda (sound) and other sense-objects (viṣayas). They arise from the jīva's own modification, not from the external object. The object is only nimitta.

G372: No other dravya can produce another dravya's guṇa. Therefore all dravyas arise by their own svabhāva (svabhāvataḥ). The guṇas of pudgala — colour, taste, smell, touch — belong to pudgala. They cannot enter the soul and become the soul's guṇas.

This is one of the most liberating doctrines in the Samaysaar: rāga is your modification — not something the object did to you. The beautiful object did not cause your desire. Your soul modified into desire — using the object as nimitta. This cuts the root of blame, projection, and attachment.

Simply put: No one makes you angry. No object makes you desire. These are your own modifications. This is not blame — it is freedom. If rāga is your own pariṇāma, you can dissolve it. If it came from outside, you would be helpless.
Rāga = Own Pariṇāma Nimitta vs Upādāna No Dravya Makes Another's Guṇa
G373–G381 — The Six-Sense Analogy: Objects Never Say "Grab Me"

G373: Pudgala transforms as nindā (criticism) and stuti (praise) words. The ajñānī becomes angry or pleased, thinking "this was said to me." The jñānī sees: pudgala-śabda transformed; its guṇa belongs to pudgala.

G375–G381: Śabda (sound) doesn't say "grab me." Rūpa (form) doesn't say "grab me." Gandha (smell), rasa (taste), sparśa (touch), guṇa, dravya — none of them approach the soul and demand to be grasped. The ātmā doesn't go out to grasp them. The ātmā illuminates them. The grasping is entirely the soul's own impulse.

Simply put: The world never grabs you. You grab the world. And once you see that, you can stop — not because the world changed, but because you saw the truth of your own grasping.
Six-Sense Analogy Objects Don't Pull Grasping Is Internal
Part 6 · G383–G389 · Niścaya-Cāritra: The Three Kalpas
Doctrine
The Three Kalpas: Not Ritual — Continuous Inner Stance The Samaysaar's definition of cāritra (conduct) by niścaya is radical: not vows, not garb, not ritual recitation — but a continuous inner threefold stance: (1) Pratikramaṇa — "I was never the owner of past karma; that mamtva was mithyā"; (2) Ālocana — "I am witnessing what is rising now; this udaya is karma's, not mine"; (3) Pratyākhyāna — "I have no intention toward future karma; I release all claim on what karma will produce." These three, held constantly, constitute true niścaya-cāritra.
G383–G386 — Pratikramaṇa, Ālocana, Pratyākhyāna Defined

G383: Pratikramaṇa = past karma mamtva-tyāga. Not a ritual of repentance — a realization: "I was never the owner of those karmic fruits. My ownership-sense was the error."

G384: Pratyākhyāna = future karma bhāva-nirvṛtti. Not a vow to abstain — a realization: "I have no intention toward future karma. I release even the desire to control what karma will produce."

G385: Ālocana = present udaya-doṣa recognition. Consciously seeing: "This karma is rising now. I witness it without becoming it."

G386: Nitya (constant) pratikramaṇa + ālocana + pratyākhyāna = true cāritra. The word nitya is crucial — this is not once-a-day. This is the continuous orientation of the jñānī-soul.

Simply put: Past — "it wasn't mine." Present — "I witness it." Future — "I claim nothing." Hold all three at once, always. That is true cāritra.
Pratikramaṇa Ālocana Pratyākhyāna Niścaya-Cāritra
G387–G389 — Three Modes of Vedana That Bind Again

G387: Experiencing karmaphala as own-rūpa (as one's own nature) — "I am in pain; this is what I am" → binds 8-types duḥkha-seeds again. Even as old karma exhausts itself, this identification plants new seeds.

G388: Experiencing thinking "I am the one who did this karmaphala" — taking authorship → binds again. This is the re-ignition of kartā-bhāva at the moment of karma's fruition.

G389: Experiencing and becoming sukha or duḥkha (pleasure or pain) — collapsing identity into the momentary state → binds again. Even pleasure-identification binds as firmly as pain-identification.

Simply put: Three traps: (1) "This pain is what I am," (2) "I made this karma," (3) "I am happy / I am miserable." Each reinforces bondage even while old karma exhausts itself. The release: "karma is rising; I am the witness."
Three Vedana Traps Karma-Phala-Mamakāra Kartā-Bhāva in Fruition
The Pratyākhyāna-Kalpa · 148 Karma Declarations
Kalaśas 225–229 — The 148-Type Karma Renunciation Bhāvanā

The text provides three complete kalpas with 49 bhaṅgas each (3 yoga × 3 karaṇa = 9 + additional combinations = 49), applied across three time periods — covering all 148 karma types.

The formula for each karma type:
"नाहं [X कर्म का फल] भुज्जे, चैतन्यात्मानमात्मानमेव सञ्चेतये।"
"I do not experience the fruit of [X karma]; I consciously experience only the consciousness-natured ātmā."

Applied to all 148: 5 jñānāvaraṇa, 9 darśanāvaraṇa, 2 vedanīya, 28 mohanīya (including all 16 kasāya and 9 nokasāya), 4 āyu, 103 nāma karma (all gati, jāti, śarīra, saṃghāta, saṃsthāna, varṇa, gandha, rasa, sparśa, pūrvi, and special types), 2 gotra, 5 antarāya.

The bhāvanā commentary clarifies: When the jīva reaches apramatta daśā and directs upayoga entirely toward caitanyamātra ātmā — this bhāvanā produces kevalajñāna. The fruit is jñāna-cetanā free from karma-cetanā and karmaphala-cetanā, absorbed in paramānanda, eternally.

Simply put: For every possible type of karmic experience — "I am not that. I am consciousness alone." Contemplated for all 148 types, across past/present/future, this bhāvanā is itself the path to kevalajñāna.

Part 7 · G390–G404 · Jñāna Distinct from All Dravyas; Jñāna Is the Jñāyaka
Doctrine
Fourteen Propositions: Why X Is Not Jñāna G390–G401 follow a single logical pattern: "X is not jñāna because X doesn't know anything. Therefore jñāna is different and X is different — so Jinas say." This logic is applied to: śāstra, śabda, rūpa, varṇa, gandha, rasa, sparśa, karma, dharma-astikāya, adharma-astikāya, kāla, ākāśa. G402 adds adhyavasāna (karmic impulse-determination), which is achetana and therefore not jñāna. The positive conclusion is G403 — jīva is jñāyaka (always-knowing), and jñāna is avyatiriktam (non-separate) from jñāyaka.
G390–G401 — Twelve Distinctions: Śāstra, Śabda, Rūpa, Varṇa, Gandha, Rasa, Sparśa, Karma, Dharma, Adharma, Kāla, Ākāśa Are Not Jñāna

Each gatha: "X is not jñāna because X doesn't know anything. Therefore jñāna is different and X is different — so Jinas say."

G390: Śāstra (scripture) — not jñāna. Śāstra is achetana; it doesn't know. G391: Śabda (sound/word) — not jñāna. G392: Rūpa (form) — not jñāna. G393: Varṇa (colour) — not jñāna. G394: Gandha (smell) — not jñāna. G395: Rasa (taste) — not jñāna. G396: Sparśa (touch) — not jñāna. G397: Karma — not jñāna. G398: Dharma-astikāya — not jñāna. G399: Adharma-astikāya — not jñāna. G400: Kāla — not jñāna. G401: Ākāśa — not jñāna.

None of these know. Therefore none of these are jñāna. Jñāna is distinct from all of them. The Tīkā adds: jñāna and jīva alone are avyatiriktam (non-separate), because jīva alone is cetana.

Simply put: The scripture is not the knowledge. The words are not the knowledge. The objects are not the knowledge. Even your karmic impulses are not the knowledge. You — the soul — are the knowledge. Everything else is the known.
Jñāna Distinct from All Achetana Cannot Know Śāstra ≠ Jñāna
G402–G403 — Adhyavasāna Is Not Jñāna; Jñāyaka and Jñāna Are One

G402: Adhyavasāna (karmic-impulse, passion-driven determination) is not jñāna, because adhyavasāna is achetana (insentient). Therefore jñāna is different and adhyavasāna is different.

जम्हा जाणिदे णिचं तम्हा जीवो दु जाणगो णाणी।
णाणं च जाणयादो अव्विदिरित्तं मुणेयव्वं।।४०३।।

G403: Because (jīva) always knows (nityaṃ jānāti), therefore jīva is jñāyaka — the knower — the jñānī. And jñāna should be understood as avyatiriktam (non-separate, non-distinct) from the jñāyaka.

The Tīkā: jñāna and jīva are avyatiriktam — one substance. Jñāna and śāstra are vyatiriktam. Jñāna and śabda are vyatiriktam. Jñāna and karma are vyatiriktam. Only jīva is cetana — therefore jñāna and jīva are non-separate. "The tiny remaining doubt — 'is jñāna perhaps slightly separate from jīva?' — should not be entertained even for a moment."

Simply put: The soul is not a knower who "has" knowledge the way a hand holds a lamp. The soul IS knowing. Knowledge and knower are the same substance.
G403 — Summit Verse Avyatiriktam Jñāna = Jīva
G404 — Jñāna IS Samyagdṛṣṭi, Saṃyama, Sūtra, Dharma, Pravrajyā

णाणं सम्मदिदिं दु संजमं सुत्तंगपुव्वगयं।
धम्मादम्मं च तहा पव्वज्जं अभुवंति बुहा।।४०४।।

The budha (wise, jñānī) regard jñāna itself as samyagdṛṣṭi (right faith), as saṃyama (self-restraint), as sūtras and pūrvas (sacred scripture), as dharma and adharma (puṇya and pāpa), and as pravrajyā (initiation into renunciation).

The jñānī does not see samyagdṛṣṭi as a set of beliefs held. The jñānī sees samyagdṛṣṭi as jñāna itself — the knowing faculty as right seeing. Saṃyama is not a list of restraints — it is the natural non-grasping of jñāna. Pravrajyā is not wearing a garb — it is the inner renunciation that is jñāna's own nature. All these arise from within jñāna — not from external acts.

Simply put: If you truly know — you are already in right faith. If you truly know — you are already restrained. If you truly know — you are already renounced. Jñāna contains all of these, not the other way around.
Jñāna = Samyagdṛṣṭi Jñāna = Saṃyama Jñāna = Pravrajyā
Part 8 · G405–G415 · Jñāna Has No Body; Liṅga Is Not Mokṣa-Mārga
G405–G407 — Ātmā Is Amūrta; Pure Soul Grasps and Releases Nothing

G405: The ātmā is amūrta (formless, immaterial). Āhāra (intake/nourishment) is mūrta (material), being pudgalamaya. Therefore ātmā cannot be āhāraka (one who takes in nourishment). The soul does not "eat" experience.

G406: Para-dravya cannot be grasped or released by the soul. Neither prāyogika (applied by nimitta) nor vaiśasika (inherent) modification of the soul reaches into para-dravya to grasp or release it.

G407: Therefore the viśuddha-cetā (pure-consciousness soul) neither grasps anything nor releases anything of jīva-ajīva dravyas. This soul is completely free — not by effort of grasping purity, but by the nature of pure consciousness itself.

Simply put: The soul doesn't "take in" experience the way food is taken in. The soul illuminates — nothing is captured in the soul from outside. What appears to be stored is karma — a pudgala phenomenon, not a soul phenomenon.
Amūrta Ātmā Not Āhāraka Viśuddha-Cetā
G408–G411 — Liṅga Is Not Mokṣa-Mārga; Darśana-Jñāna-Cāritra Is

G408: The deluded, grasping the pāṣaṇḍī (ascetic) garb or gṛhī (householder) garb in many forms, declare "this liṅga is the path of mokṣa."

G409: But liṅga is not the path of mokṣa. Arhants are nirmmama (beyond mamakāra) regarding the body; they leave liṅga-identification and serve darśana-jñāna-cāritra.

G410: Neither pāṣaṇḍī-liṅga nor gṛhī-liṅga is mokṣa-mārga. Darśana-jñāna-cāritra is the mokṣa-mārga — so Jinas say.

G411: Therefore, abandoning whatever liṅga is held, engage the ātmā in darśana-jñāna-cāritra, which is the path of mokṣa.

Tīkā clarifies: This does not abolish external vows. Vyavahāra-naya calls both muni-liṅga and śrāvaka-liṅga the path of mokṣa as sadhanā containers. Niścaya-naya says: no liṅga is mokṣa-mārga — only darśana-jñāna-cāritra is. The vow-keeping is valid; the mamakāra in the garb is what must be released.

Simply put: The garb is the container. Darśana-jñāna-cāritra is the content. The garb doesn't liberate — what's inside it does.
Liṅga Not Mokṣa-Mārga Darśana-Jñāna-Cāritra Nirmmama
G412 — The Final Instruction: Establish, Meditate, Dwell, Do Not Stray

मोक्खपहे अप्पाणं ठवेहि तं चेव झाहि तं चेय।
तत्थेव विहर णिच्चं मा विहरसु अण्णदव्वेसु।।४१२।।

Establish the ātmā on the mokṣa-path (darśana-jñāna-cāritra); meditate on that; contemplate it; dwell there always (nitya); do not dwell (viharsu) in other dravyas.

Four imperatives, one prohibition. Establish (ṭhavehi), meditate (jhāhi), experience (cetayasva), dwell constantly (nitya vihar) — all four aimed at the same target: darśana-jñāna-cāritra-rūpa ātmā. And one prohibition: do not dwell (mātra viharsu) in para-dravyas — even to "know" them is fine; to dwell there is bondage.

Simply put: Four actions, one place. The entire path compressed into two lines: establish your soul in its own true nature, and stay there.
Establish Meditate Dwell Not in Para-Dravyas
G413–G414 — Mamatā in Liṅga = Never Knowing Samayasāra; Vyavahāra vs. Niścaya on Liṅga

G413: Those who carry mamatā (ownership) in pāṣaṇḍī liṅgas or gṛhī liṅgas of many types — they have not known samayasāra (the essence of the soul). Because samayasāra is the pure soul itself — and it is known by jñāna, not by any external marker.

G414: Vyavahāra-naya calls both liṅgas (muni and śrāvaka) the path of mokṣa. Niścaya-naya does not consider any liṅga as mokṣa-mārga. These are not contradictions — they are two levels of the same truth. Vyavahāra: yes, the muni-discipline is a mokṣa-mārga instrument. Niścaya: the discipline works only because of the darśana-jñāna-cāritra operating within it.

Simply put: The one who wears the muni garb but thinks "this garb will liberate me" — has not known samayasāra. The one who wears the garb and knows "only my darśana-jñāna-cāritra liberates me" — is on the path.
Samayasāra Vyavahāra vs. Niścaya on Liṅga
G415 — The Promise of This Text

जो समयपाहुडिमणं पढिदूणं अत्थतच्चदो णादुं।
अत्थे ठाही चेदा सो होही उत्तमं सोक्खं।।४१५।।

Yaḥ samayaprābhṛtamidaṃ paṭhitvā arthatattvato jñātvā / Arthe sthāsyati cetayitā sa bhaviṣyatyuttamaṃ saukhyam //

The cetayitā (conscious soul — the bhavya jīva) who reads this Samayaprābhṛta, knows its meaning and tattvas, and establishes itself in that meaning — will attain the highest happiness (uttamaṃ saukhyam).

The Tīkā: The Samayaprābhṛta is śabdabrahma — equivalent to the universal creative Word — because it illuminates all things as they are. The soul that reads this scripture, grasps its paramārtha, and establishes itself in that paramārtha — in that complete pūrṇavijñāna-ghana-paramabrahma — will in the immediacy of that experience become radiant with one cetanya-rasa, naturally arrive at uttama saukhya (paramānanda), and remain in that state eternally.

Simply put: If you read this text, understand it truly, and live in that understanding — you will attain the highest joy. Not someday. The moment the soul establishes itself in its own true nature as jñāyaka, that is the beginning of paramānanda.
Samayaprābhṛta Promise Uttama Saukhya Establish in Artha Kevalajñāna

इति श्रीमदमृतचन्द्रसूरिविरचितायां समयसारख्याख्यामात्मख्यातौ
सर्वविशुद्धज्ञानप्ररूपकः नवमोऽङ्कः।।

Thus in the Ātmakhyāti — the authoritative commentary on the Samaysaar composed by the revered Ācārya Amṛtacandra Sūri — the ninth adhikar, the exposition of Sarvavishuddhajñāna (Pure All-Knowledge), is concluded.

Closing Sarvayā Verse

सर्वविशुद्धज्ञानरूप सदा चिदानंद करता न भोगता न परद्रव्यभावको,
मूरत अमूरत जे आनद्रव्य लोकमांहि ते भी ज्ञानरूप नहीं न्यारे न अभावको;
यहै जानि ज्ञानी जीव आपकूं भजे सदीव ज्ञानरूप सुखतूप आन न लगावको,
कर्म-कर्मफलरूप चेतनाकूं दूरि टारि ज्ञानचेतना अभ्यास करे शुद्ध भावको।

The ātmā of sarvavishuddhajñāna-rūpa forever experiences cidānanda — not experiencing any para-dravya-bhāva. Even mūrta and amūrta other dravyas — they are not jñāna-rūpa; they are not absent but they are elsewhere. Knowing this, the jñānī jīva worships its own self as jñāna-rūpa always — attaching nothing else. Removing karma-cetanā and karmaphala-cetanā far away, practicing jñāna-cetanā with pure bhāva.

Upasamhāra — उपसंहार

The tenth and final adhikar — Kundakunda's conclusion and benediction. The entire Samaysaar is gathered into its final message. What remains for the soul that has truly absorbed these nine adhikars? What is the state of one who has known the samayasāra? The grand summation of all Jain philosophy, in the last gathas of the greatest Jain scripture.

Five Keys of Adhikar 9
1

Jñāyaka Is Jñāyaka Alone

The seṭikā is the seṭikā; the knower is the knower. The soul is not defined by what it knows, or by the karma surrounding it. (G356-G360)

2

Rāga Is Your Own Pariṇāma

No external object causes rāga or dveṣa. The object is nimitta; the pariṇāma is yours. This cuts all projection and blame at the root. (G371-G372)

3

The Three Kalpas of Niścaya-Cāritra

Pratikramaṇa (past mamtva-tyāga), ālocana (present witness), pratyākhyāna (future intention-release) — constant, not ritual. (G383-G386)

4

Jñāna Is Avyatiriktam from Jñāyaka

Knowledge and the knower are not two things. The soul is not a knower who "has" knowledge. The soul IS knowing itself. (G403)

5

Darśana-Jñāna-Cāritra Is the Only Mokṣa-Mārga

Not the garb, not the ritual, not the lineage. The ātmā that establishes itself in darśana-jñāna-cāritra attains uttama saukhya. (G410-G415)
