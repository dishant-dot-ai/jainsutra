Shedding of Karmaनिर्जरा अधिकार

All 44 gathas of the sixth Adhikar — how the pure knower naturally sheds karma, and what the eight qualities of samyag-darśana look like in practice

Nirjarā is not effortful karma-destruction. It is the natural consequence of jñāyaka-svabhāva. When the soul knows itself as pure knower — and correctly sees rāga as karma's product, not self — karma sheds naturally through experience without generating new bondage. The eight qualities of samyag-darśana are this principle lived in all dimensions.

Part 1 · G193–G194 · What Is Karma-Shedding?
Foundation
Every Experience of the Samyag-Dṛṣṭi Is a Cause of Nirjarā The Adhikar opens with a revolutionary reframe. Every experience the samyag-dṛṣṭi has — through the senses, through the mind — becomes a cause of nirjarā (karma-shedding), not of new bondage. Without the inner rāgādi bhāva that converts experience into bondage, karma simply exhausts itself through experience and drops away.
6.193

All upayoga-upabhoga (conscious experiencing) by the samyag-dṛṣṭi of insentient and sentient substances through the sense organs — all of it becomes a cause of nirjarā.

The simple version: For the one who knows the self, every experience dissolves karma instead of building it.

Nirjarā Samyag-Dṛṣṭi Upayoga
6.194

When any substance is experienced, sukha (pleasure) or duḥkha (pain) necessarily arises in the soul. Experiencing that — it goes to nirjarā (is shed, dissolved).

Experience cannot be neutral — it produces either pleasant or unpleasant sensation. These sensations, correctly known as karma-vipāka (the ripening of previously bound karma), pass through experience and exit as nirjarā. The key is "correctly known" — recognized as karma-products, not as self, not as worth craving or fleeing.

The simple version: Every pleasure and pain that a knower experiences is karma burning off — not karma accumulating.

Part 2 · G195–G196 · Doctor and Drunkard Analogies
6.195

जह विसमुवभुंजंतो वेज्जो पुिरसो ण मरणमुवय िद।
पोग्गलकम्मस्सुदयं तह भुंजिद णेव बज्झदे णाणी।।१९५।।

यथा विषमुपभुञ्जानो वैद्यः पुरुषो न मरणमुपयाति। पुद्गलकर्मण उदयं तथा भुङ्क्ते नैव बध्यते ज्ञानी।।

Just as a physician eating poison does not die — so the jñānī, experiencing the rise of pudgala-karma, does not bind new karma.

The physician's knowledge of medicine gives him a counter-force that neutralizes the poison's killing power. Similarly, the jñānī's amogha-jñāna — specifically the absence of rāgādi bhāva — counter-neutralizes karma's binding power. When karma rises and the jñānī experiences its fruits, karma cannot bind because the jñānī lacks the inner bhāva-āsrava (rāga-dveṣa-moha) that would convert experience into new bondage.

The simple version: Knowledge is the antidote. Just as the knowledgeable physician eats poison without dying, the jñānī lives through karma's fruits without binding new karma.

Jñāna as Antidote Karma-Udaya Bhāva-Āsrava
6.196

जह मज्जं पिबमाणो अरदीभावेण मज्जिद ण पुिरसो।
दव्वुवभोगे अरदो णाणी िव ण बज्झिद तहेव।।१९६।।

यथा मद्यं पिबन् अरतिभावेन माद्यति न पुरुषः। द्रव्योपभोगेऽरतो ज्ञान्यपि न बध्यते तथैव।।

Just as a person drinking intoxicant with arati-bhāva (feeling of loathing/non-attachment) does not become intoxicated — so the jñānī, having vairāgya toward all sense-object enjoyment, does not bind karma.

The simple version: Intoxication comes from craving, not from the drink. Karma-bondage comes from inner rāga, not from external experience.

Arati-Bhāva Vairāgya Karma-Bandha
Part 3 · G197 · The Owner and the Instrument
6.197

सेवंतो िव ण सेविद असेवमाणो िव सेवगो कोई।
पगरणचेट्ठा कस्स िव ण य पायरणो ित सो होिद।।१९७।।

One who performs activity does not really do it; one who does not do it may still be the doer. The activity of the instrument (prakaraṇa) does not make someone the owner of that instrument's action.

The prakaraṇa (instrument/tool) performs activity. But the activity of the instrument is not the activity of whoever wields it — the owner must claim it as their own for it to be "theirs." The samyag-dṛṣṭi, through the power of bheda-jñāna, does not claim karma-udaya as "mine." The ajñānī claims ownership of karma-driven activity — and that claiming is itself the bondage.

The simple version: Not the activity but the ownership-claim makes you the doer. The jñānī doesn't claim karma's activities as theirs.

Bheda-Jñāna Kartā-Bhoktā Karma-Udaya
Part 4 · G198–G200 · I Am the Knowing State — Attachment Is Not My Own Nature
6.198

उदयिववागो िविवहो कम्माणं वण्णदो जिणवरेिहं।
ण तु ते मज्झ सहावा जाणगभावो तु अहमेको।।१९८।।

उदयविपाको विविधः कर्मणां वर्णितो जिनवरैः। न तु ते मम स्वभावाः ज्ञायकभावस्त्वहमेकः।।

The many types of karma-udaya-vipāka (fruition of arising karma) have been described in various ways by the Jinavars. But those are not my svabhāva. I am one — the jñāyaka-bhāva (pure knowing-state).

The simple version: Karma produces all kinds of results. None of them are you. You are the one who knows all of them.

6.199

पोग्गलकम्मं रागो तस्स िववागोदओ हविद एसो।
ण तु एस मज्झ भावो जाणगभावो हु अहमेको।।१९९।।

पुद्गलकर्म रागस्तस्य विपाकोदयो भवति एषः। न त्वेष मम भावो ज्ञायकभावः खल्वहमेकः।।

Rāga is pudgala-karma; this rāga-bhāva is its vipāka-udaya (rising fruition). But this is not my bhāva. I am certainly one — the jñāyaka-bhāva alone.

This is a precise metaphysical claim: rāga (attraction, love, desire) is not a property of the soul — it is pudgala-karma in its fruition-arising form. When mohanīya-karma rises, its fruition appears as rāga-bhāva in the experiential field. The ajñānī misidentifies this as "my feeling, my nature." The jñānī sees: "this rāga is karma's product — its vipāka-udaya. Not my bhāva. I am jñāyaka-bhāva alone."

Contemplate: Right now, whatever feeling is strongest in you — notice that you are also aware of it. Which of these is more fundamentally you: the feeling, or the awareness?

The simple version: That feeling of attraction or love you're experiencing? That is karma's ripening — not you. You are the awareness that knows it is happening.

Jñāyaka-Bhāva Rāga as Karma Mohanīya-Karma Vipāka-Udaya
6.200

एवं सम्मिदिट्ठी अप्पाणं मुणिद जाणगसहावं।
उदयं कम्मिवावागं च मुयिद तच्चं िवयाणंतो।।२००।।

एवं सम्यग्दृष्टिः आत्मानं मुनाति ज्ञायकस्वभावम्। उदयं कर्मविपाकं च मुञ्चति तत्त्वं विजानन्।।

Thus the samyag-dṛṣṭi knows the self as jñāyaka-svabhāva. And knowing tattva (reality correctly), releases (muñcati) karma-vipāka-udaya. Not by fighting karma or suppressing it — but like releasing your grip on something you've realized you were mistakenly holding.

The simple version: Right knowledge of self as pure knower — combined with right knowledge of rāga as karma's product — results in natural release of karma.

Part 5 · G201–G202 · Even an Atom of Attachment = Self-Ignorance
6.201

परमाणुमित्तयं पि हु रागादीणं तु िवज्जदे जस्स।
ण िव सो जाणिद अप्पाणयं तु सव्वागमधरो िव।।२०१।।

परमाणुमात्रमपि खलु रागादीनां तु विद्यते यस्य। नापि स जानात्यात्मानं तु सर्वागमधरोऽपि।।

In whom even a paramāṇu-measure of rāgādi exists — even if he is sarvāgama-dhara (carrier of all scriptures) — he does not know the ātmā.

The standard is absolute: even the smallest trace of rāgādi indicates the absence of true ātma-jñāna. Because rāga is karma's vipāka-udaya — if one truly knew the self as jñāyaka-bhāva, one could not mistake karma's product for the self. Textual knowledge without experiential self-discrimination is still ignorance of the self.

The simple version: Even the tiniest trace of rāga reveals you haven't truly known the self yet — no matter how many scriptures you've studied.

Ātma-Jñāna Rāgādi Sarvāgama-Dhara
6.202

अप्पाणमयाणंतो अणप्पयं चािव सो अयाणंतो।
कह होिद सम्मिदट्ठी जीवाजीवे अयाणंतो।।२०२।।

आत्मानमजानन् अनात्मानं चापि सोऽजानन्। कथं भवति सम्यग्दृष्टिर्जीवाजीवावजानन्।।

Not knowing the self — and thus also not knowing the non-self — how can he be samyag-dṛṣṭi, not knowing jīva and ajīva?

Self-knowledge and non-self-knowledge arise together. When the soul truly knows itself as jīva (consciousness, jñāyaka-bhāva), it simultaneously knows what it is not — ajīva (matter, karma, body, rāga). These are two sides of one discrimination. Without self-knowledge, non-self-knowledge is also absent.

The simple version: True knowledge of the self and true knowledge of what is not-self arise together. Without one, you don't have the other.

Part 6 · G203–G206 · Grasp the Stable Single Knowledge-State
6.203

आदम्हि दव्वभावे अपदे मोत्तूण गिण्ह तह णियदं।
िथरमेगिममं भावं उवलब्धंत सहावेण।।२०३।।

आत्मनि द्रव्यभावानपदानि मुक्त्वा गृहाण तथा नियतम्। स्थिरमेकमिमं भावमुपलभ्यमानं स्वभावेन।।

In the ātmā — abandoning the dravya-bhāvas (material states) that are apad (not the true resting-place) — grasp that fixed, single, stable bhāva that is attainable through svabhāva.

"Apad" means "not a stable station" — something that cannot be a permanent resting place. All dravya-bhāva — the states arising from karma's interaction with the soul — are apad: impermanent, unstable, borrowed. The soul's svabhāva is the one stable bhāva: pure jñāna-consciousness, niyata, sthira, eka.

The simple version: All the states that come and go are not your home. Your home is the one stable thing: pure knowing.

6.204

आभिणिसुदोिधमणकेवलं च तं होिद एक्केमेव पदं।
सो एसो परमट्ठो जं लिहदुं णिव्युिदं जािद।।२०४।।

Mati-jñāna, śruta-jñāna, avadhi-jñāna, manaḥparyaya-jñāna, and kevala-jñāna — that is one single pada (station/abode). That is the paramārtha — by attaining which one goes to nirvāṇa.

The five types of jñāna appear to be five different things. But from the paramārtha standpoint, they are all one: the soul's consciousness expressing itself at different degrees of unfolding. The substrate is one. Recognizing jñāna as one single pada and resting there: that is the practice.

The simple version: Whether you have little knowledge or omniscience, it's all one thing — knowing. Rest in knowing as such, not in its particular contents.

Pañca-Jñāna Paramārtha Kevala-Jñāna
6.205

णाणगुणेण विहीणा एदं तु पदं बहू िव ण लहंते।
तं गिण्ह णियदमेदं जिद इच्छिस कम्मपिरमोक्खं।।२०५।।

Many people, devoid of jñāna-guṇa, do not attain this pada. Therefore, O bhavya! If you desire complete liberation from karma — grasp this niyata (certain, fixed) jñāna.

The word "niyatam" carries the force of "unwaveringly, with firm resolve, without alternative." Liberation comes from jñāna alone, not from karma-kāṇḍa (ritual action-based approaches).

The simple version: Millions miss liberation not for lack of effort but for lack of jñāna. Grasp jñāna firmly. It alone frees from karma.

6.206

एदगिह रदो णिच्चं संतुट्ठो होिह णिचमेदम्हि।
एदेण होिह तित्तो होहिद तुह उत्तमं सोक्खं।।२०६।।

एतस्मिन् रतो नित्यं सन्तुष्टो भव नित्यमेतस्मिन्। एतेन भव तृप्तो भविष्यति तवोत्तमं सौख्यम्।।

Be always ratī (devoted, content) in this (jñāna). Be always santushṭa (satisfied) in this. Be tṛpta (satiated) in this. This will become your uttama-sukha (highest happiness).

Three imperatives — ratī, santushṭa, tṛpta — all directed toward jñāna itself. Not toward the contents of knowledge, not toward outcomes, but toward the very fact of knowing. When the soul takes jñāna itself as its object of delight, the "uttama-sukha" arises as the natural quality of resting in one's own nature.

Contemplate: Right now — awareness is happening. Can "knowing is occurring" itself be your object of delight? Try resting in that, rather than in what is known.

The simple version: Find your contentment in knowing itself — not in what you know or what you get. That is the highest joy there is.

Part 7 · G207–G209 · The Self's Only Possession Is the Self
6.207

को णाम भिणज्ज बुहो परदव्वं मम इमं हविद दव्वं।
अप्पाणमप्पणो पिरगहं तु णियदं वियाणंतो।।२०७।।

को नाम भणेद्बुधः परद्रव्यं ममेदं भवति द्रव्यम्। आत्मानमात्मनः परिग्रहं तु नियतं विजानन्।।

What wise person would say "this para-dravya is my dravya" — the one who knows, as a fixed fact, that the self's parigraha (possession) is the self alone?

The simple version: Once you know what is truly yours — the self alone — claiming anything else as yours becomes as absurd as claiming someone else's home as yours.

Parigraha Para-Dravya Svata
6.208

मज्झं पिरग्गहो जिद तदो अहमजीवदं तु गच्छेज्ज।
णादेव अहं जम्हा तम्हा ण पिरग्गहो मज्झ।।२०८।।

If that (para-dravya) were my parigraha, then I would become ajīva (insentient). Since I am jñāta (the knower) alone — therefore that is not my parigraha.

A precise logical argument: "mine-ness" in the ultimate sense means becoming that thing (sharing its svabhāva). If the body were truly mine, I would become body-svabhāva (ajīva). But I am jñāta — consciousness, not matter. Therefore para-dravya cannot be truly mine.

The simple version: If the body were truly mine, I would BE body — insentient. But I know. I am the knower. Therefore the body is not mine.

6.209

छिज्जदु वा भिज्जदु वा णिज्जदु वा अहव जादु विप्पलयं।
जम्हा तम्हा गच्छदु तह वि हु ण पिरग्गहो मज्झ।।२०९।।

Let it be cut, broken, taken away, or destroyed — let it go wherever it goes — even so, it is certainly not my parigraha.

The jñānī's non-possession is unconditional. No circumstance changes it. This is the opposite of conventional renunciation (which is still emotionally engaged with what it gives up). The jñānī hasn't given up anything — they have simply correctly seen that it was never theirs to begin with.

The simple version: The jñānī is unconcerned with gain or loss — not because of effort, but because they correctly know: it was never mine.

Part 8 · G210–G214 · The Knower as Knower, Not Possessor
Structural Teaching
The Jñānī Is Knower of All Bhāvas — Not Their Possessor G210–G214 apply the same principle across five categories: dharma (merit), adharma (demerit), food, drink, and all other bhāvas. The jñānī's relationship to all of these is jñāna (knowing), not icchā (desire/possessing). This same substitution applies to all modifications of karma, body, mind, speech, and sense organs — sixteen different categories total.
6.210

अपिरग्गहो अणिच्छो भणिदो णाणी य णेच्छदे धम्मं।
अपिरग्गहो दु धम्मस्स जाणगो तेण सो होिद।।२१०।।

The jñānī, having been declared aparigraha and aniccha, does not desire dharma (punya/merit). Therefore he is the knower of dharma — not its possessor.

The desire for merit — for good karma, for virtuous outcomes — is itself a form of parigraha. "Icchā parigraha" — desire is possession. The jñānī sees dharma as something that happens in the field of experience — they know it, without owning or craving it.

The simple version: Even craving for virtue is a form of grasping. The jñānī doesn't grasp even merit — they simply know it.

6.211

The same principle extends across all categories. G211: The jñānī does not desire adharma (pāpa/demerit) — they are the knower of adharma, not its possessor. G212: The jñānī does not desire food — even when hungry, they know hunger as karma's product and eating as its address, not as "I desire food." G213: The jñānī does not desire drink — same structural principle.

Note the structural elegance: "aparigraha" applies equally to punya and pāpa and food and drink. The categories are not ethical ones — the principle is ontological: the jñānī's relationship to all bhāvas is knowing, not possessing.

The simple version: The jñānī doesn't own their vices any more than their virtues. They are the witness of both.

6.214

एमादिए दु िविविहे सव्वे भावे य णेच्छदे णाणी।
जाणगभावो णियदो णीरालंबो दु सव्वत्थ।।२१४।।

एवमादिकांस्तु विविधान् सर्वान् भावांश्च नेच्छति ज्ञानी। ज्ञायकभावो नियतो निरालम्बस्तु सर्वत्र।।

The jñānī does not desire any of these many types of bhāvas. His jñāyaka-bhāva is fixed — completely nirālamba (without support/anchorage) everywhere.

"Nirālamba" — without external support or anchorage. The jñāyaka-bhāva does not lean on anything outside itself. It is entirely self-sufficient, resting in its own svabhāva. This is the ultimate non-parigraha: the soul's standing in its own knowing-nature without needing anything else as support.

The simple version: The jñānī's awareness needs nothing to lean on. It stands in itself. That is the deepest non-possession.

Nirālamba Jñāyaka-Svabhāva Aparigraha
Part 9 · G215–G216 · Detachment-Understanding — At All Three Times
6.215

उप्पण्णोदयभोगो वियोगबुद्धीए तस्त सो णिच्चं।
कंखामणागदस्स य उदयस्स ण कुव्वदे णाणी।।२१५।।

Toward the upabhoga (enjoyment/experience) that has arisen through karma-udaya — the jñānī always has viyoga-buddhi (detachment-understanding). And toward anāgata (future) karma-udaya, the jñānī does not make kāṅkṣā (desire/craving).

G216: The vedaka-bhāva (experiencing-state) and the vedya-bhāva (the experienced-state) — both perish moment by moment. The jñānī, knowing this, never desires either of them. Both the experiencer-modification and the experienced-object are momentary — arising and perishing in each instant. The jñānī sees this impermanence — and knowing there is nothing stable in either, makes no desire toward either.

The simple version: Present pleasures — "this will end, I know I am not this." Future pleasures — "I don't crave them." Both the one who wants and the object of wanting vanish moment by moment. What is there to crave?

Viyoga-Buddhi Kāṅkṣā Vedaka-Vedya
Part 10 · G217–G219 · Gold and Iron — Own Nature Determines Stainability
6.217

बंधुवभोगिणिमित्ते अझ्वसाणोदएसु णाणिस्स।
संसारदेहिवसएसु णेव उप्पज्जदे रागो।।२१७।।

In the adhyavasāna-udayas (arising of inner determinations) related to causes of bondage, sensory enjoyment, samsāra, and body — rāga does not arise in the jñānī. Because the jñānī knows all these things as pāra-svabhāva (other-nature), not as their own, even their mere mention does not trigger attraction or aversion.

6.218

णाणी रागप्पजहो सव्वदव्वेसु कम्ममज्झगदो।
णो लिप्पिद रजएण दु कद्ममज्झे जहा कणयं।।२१८।।

G218: The jñānī — having abandoned rāga toward all dravyas — even when in the midst of karma — is not stained by karma-dust. Just as gold in the middle of mud is not stained.

G219: The ajñānī — attached to all dravyas — even when in the midst of karma — is stained by karma-dust. Just as iron in mud gets stained.

Gold's svabhāva is aliptatva (non-sticking). Mud cannot adhere to gold's nature. The jñānī in karma-situations: karma cannot adhere because the jñānī's svabhāva is rāga-tyāga (rāga-abandonment). The ajñānī's svabhāva is rāgopādāna-śīlatva (the tendency to grasp through rāga) — therefore karma adheres like rust on iron. The difference is internal, not circumstantial.

The simple version: In the same karma-ocean: the jñānī is gold — karma doesn't stick. The ajñānī is iron — karma sticks and rusts.

Aliptatva Svabhāva Gold Analogy
Part 11 · G220–G223 · The Conch Analogy
6.220

भुंजंतस्स िव िविविहे सिच्ताचित्तमिस्सिसए दव्वे।
संखस्स सेदभावो ण िव सक्किद किण्हगो काडंु।।२२०।।

G220: Just as the whiteness of a conch — even while eating various saccitta, acitta, and mixed substances — cannot be made black by anyone. G221: Similarly, the jñānī, even while enjoying various substances — their jñāna cannot be made ajñāna by anyone from outside.

G222: But when the conch itself abandons its white svabhāva and takes on blackness — then it loses its whiteness. G223: Similarly, when the jñānī itself abandons its jñāna-svabhāva and becomes transformed by ajñāna — then it attains ajñānatā.

The conch's whiteness cannot be blackened by external substances — only when the conch itself transforms. Similarly, external experiences cannot make the jñānī ajñānī — only when the jñānī itself abandons jñāna-svabhāva. The key implication: the responsibility for spiritual fall is always internal, never external.

The simple version: Nothing outside you can destroy your knowing. Only you can abandon it.

Conch Analogy Jñāna-Svabhāva Self-Responsibility
Part 12 · G224–G227 · The King Analogy
6.224

G224–225: Just as a person serves the king for livelihood purposes — and the king gives him various happiness-producing enjoyments — similarly, the jīva-puruṣa serves karma-rāja (karma-king) for the sake of sukha, and karma gives him various enjoyments.

G226–227: But when that person does NOT serve the king for livelihood — the king does not give him those enjoyments. Similarly, the samyag-dṛṣṭi does NOT serve karma-rāja for sense-objects — so karma does not give him the various rañjita-pariṇāma (rāga-tinged fruits).

"Serving karma" means seeking karma's products — craving pleasure from karma's fruits, doing actions with the motive of obtaining karma-produced sukha. The ajñānī serves karma (desires its products) → karma gives those products. The samyag-dṛṣṭi doesn't serve karma (doesn't seek karma-produced sukha as the goal) → karma cannot deliver to someone who isn't craving.

The simple version: Karma can only deliver to someone who's placed an order. The jñānī has stopped ordering.

King Analogy Karma-Rāja Rañjita-Pariṇāma
Part 13 · G228 · Freedom from the Seven Fears
6.228

सम्मािदट्ठी जीवा णिस्सका होंित िणभ्भया तेण।
सत्तभयिवप्पमुक्का जम्हा तम्हा दु िणस्सका।।२२८।।

सम्यग्दृष्टयो जीवा निरशङ्का भवन्ति निर्भयास्तेन। सप्तभयविप्रमुक्ता यस्मात्तस्मातु निरशङ्काः।।

Samyag-dṛṣṭi jīvas are niḥśaṅka (free from doubt) and therefore nirbhaya (fearless). Because they are free from the seven fears.

The seven fears: (1) this-world fear (loka-bhaya), (2) next-world fear (paraloka-bhaya), (3) agupti-bhaya (fear from lack of protection), (4) vedanā-bhaya (fear of suffering), (5) maraṇa-bhaya (fear of death), (6) ākasmika-bhaya (fear of sudden events), (7) other karma-related fears. The samyag-dṛṣṭi is niḥśaṅka — free from all seven — because being established in jñāyaka-bhāva, they know: this consciousness cannot be harmed by any external condition.

The simple version: When you know you are the eternal knower, every fear dissolves. What is there to fear if you know you cannot be harmed?

Niḥśaṅka Nirbhaya Sapta-Bhaya
Part 14 · G229–G236 · Eight Qualities of Right Vision
Core Teaching
The Eight Qualities (Aṅgas) of Samyag-Darśana These eight gathas describe the eight aṅgas (qualities/dimensions) of samyag-darśana. Each is a mode of nirjarā — the soul established in jñāyaka-bhāva naturally expressing itself across all dimensions of life, each expression shedding karma rather than binding it.
6.229

जो चत्तािर िव पाए छिदिद ते कम्मबंधमोहकरे।
सो िणस्सको चेदा सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२२९।।

The cetayitā (experiencing consciousness) who cuts the four pādas (foot-like causes) — the karma-bandha-moha-causing ones — is niḥśaṅka and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

The four pādas are the four forms of mithyātva (false views) that create confusion and doubt about karma-bondage. The cetayitā who cuts these doubt-causing misconceptions stands unwavering in niḥśaṅka.

The simple version: First quality: no doubt about the nature of the self and the path.

6.230

जो दु ण करेिद कंखं कम्मफलेसु तह सव्वधम्मेसु।
सो िणकांखो चेदा सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३०।।

The cetayitā who does not make kāṅkṣā toward karma-fruits and toward all dharmas — is niḥkāṅkṣa and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

No desire for karma's products — no craving for pleasant birth-conditions, good fortune, or the fruits of virtue. No desire even for diverse dharmas as means to specific ends.

The simple version: Second quality: no craving for what karma can deliver, and no craving even for virtue-as-means.

6.231

जो ण करेिद दुगुंछं चेदा सव्वेिसमेव धम्माणं।
सो खलु िणिव्विदेिगच्छो सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३१।।

The cetayitā who does not make juguptsā (disgust/aversion) toward all dharmas — is nirvidicikitsa and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

No juguptsā toward the natural properties of things (hunger, thirst, heat, cold) and no disgust toward impure or unclean objects. The samyag-dṛṣṭi knows these as karma-udaya products — events in the field, not objects of personal revulsion.

The simple version: Third quality: no disgust or aversion toward the natural conditions of embodied life.

6.232

जो हविद असम्मूढो चेदा सिदिट्ठ सव्वभावेसु।
सो खलु अमूिढदिट्ठ सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३२।।

The cetayitā who is asammūḍha (non-deluded) in all states and has yathārtha-dṛṣṭi (correct vision) — is amūḍha-dṛṣṭi and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

Not deluded by external forms, not taking karma-udaya effects for the nature of things. Correct vision about the true svabhāva of each substance — knowing dev, guru, śāstra, and worldly events for what they actually are, not through the distorted lens of moha.

The simple version: Fourth quality: seeing all things as they actually are, without the distortion of moha.

6.233

जो िसद्धभित्तजुत्तो उवगूहणगो दु सव्वधम्माणं।
सो उवगूहणकारी सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३३।।

The cetayitā who is endowed with siddha-bhakti (devotion to the Siddhas) and is upagūhanaka (concealer/protector) of all dharmas — is upagūhana-kārī and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

Upagūhana means protecting dharma from being defamed, not exposing the faults of fellow practitioners, nurturing the growth of the path. The basis is siddha-bhakti: devotion directed toward liberated souls focused on pure consciousness.

The simple version: Fifth quality: protecting and nurturing the dharma, not broadcasting its practitioners' faults.

6.234

उम्मग्गं गच्छंतं सगं पि मग्गे ठवेिद जो चेदा।
सो ठिदकरणाजुत्तो सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३४।।

The cetayitā who places even those going astray — even their own ātmā — back on the right path — is sthitikāraṇa-yukta and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

"Even their own ātmā" — even when the samyag-dṛṣṭi's own soul slips from the moksha-path (sthiti), they bring it back. Self-stabilization is the primary meaning; stabilization of others is secondary.

The simple version: Sixth quality: bringing the straying soul — first your own — back to the right path.

6.235

जो कुणिद वच्छलत्तं ितण्हं साहूण मोक्खमग्गम्हि।
सो वच्छलभावजुदो सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३५।।

The cetayitā who performs vātsalya (affection/fondness) toward the three sādhas on the moksha-mārga — is vātsalya-bhāva-yuta and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

Vātsalya = deep affection-love toward the three sādhas (samyag-darśana, samyak-jñāna, samyak-cāritra). The samyag-dṛṣṭi loves the moksha-path itself. This love is prīti directed at the path as one's own svabhāva-rūpa — of the nature of one's own nature.

The simple version: Seventh quality: genuine love for the moksha-path — not as external discipline, but as your own nature.

6.236

िवज्जारहमारूढो मणोरहपहेसु भमइ जो चेदा।
सो िजणणाणपहावी सम्मािदिट्ठ मुणेदव्वो।।२३६।।

विद्यारथमारूढः मनोरथपथेषु भ्रमति यश्चेतयिता। स जिनज्ञानप्रभावी सम्यग्दृष्टिज्ञातव्यः।।

The cetayitā who, mounted on the vidyā-ratha (knowledge-chariot), roams in the paths of manoratha (thought-paths/mental paths) — is jina-jñāna-prabhāvin (one who propagates/manifests Jina-knowledge) and should be known as samyag-dṛṣṭi.

The eighth quality: prabhāvana — manifesting, propagating, making radiant the Jina's knowledge. The cetayitā who is "mounted on the vidyā-chariot" — established in jñāna as their vehicle — and roams in the mental paths (investigating, contemplating, applying): this is the samyag-dṛṣṭi who spreads the influence of Jina-jñāna. The samyag-dṛṣṭi's own practice is itself a propagation — by making jñāna radiant in their own consciousness, they embody and propagate it.

The simple version: Eighth quality: let your knowledge radiate. Mount the knowledge-chariot and let it move through your thinking — that itself is prabhāvana.

Prabhāvana Vidyā-Ratha Jina-Jñāna

इति निर्जरा निष्क्रान्ता।

निर्जराप्ररूपक: षष्ठोऽङ्क:।।

Thus Nirjarā is departed. The sixth Adhikar — the exposition of Nirjarā — is concluded.
