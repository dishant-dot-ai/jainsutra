# Samaysaar — Adhikar 10: Upasamhāra (Pariśiṣṭam)
## Concluding Philosophical Synthesis by Amṛtacandrasūri
### Kalaśas 247–278 — Ātmakhyāti Commentary Conclusion

---

## Overview

The Pariśiṣṭam (Appendix) is Amṛtacanda Ācārya's concluding philosophical synthesis appended to his Ātmakhyāti commentary on the Samayaprābhṛta. It is not a separate adhikar by Kundakunda but the culminating vision of the commentator — the seal placed on the entire work after all nine adhikars have been expounded.

The Pariśiṣṭam spans Kalaśas 247–278. Its doctrinal axis is the complete identity of jñāna, jñeya, and jñātṛ in a single jñānamātra vastu — the ātmā who knows, the act of knowing, and the object known are ultimately one undivided self-luminous reality.

Three central movements:
1. **Jñānamātra deklaration** — I am not fragmented by dravya, kṣetra, kāla, or bhāva; I am pure jñānamātra bhāva.
2. **Syādvāda as purifier** — the 14 bhaṅgas reveal jñāna's own-mode existence and non-existence in others; the 47 śaktis enumerate ātmā's complete self-sufficiency.
3. **Upāya-upeya unity** — ātmā is simultaneously the path (sādhaka-rūpa) and the goal (siddha-rūpa); the non-dual culmination closes the work with Amṛtacandra's own blessing verse.

## Part 1: The Supreme Declaration — Jñānamātra

### Opening Prose Declaration (Pariśiṣṭam, pre-K270)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT:**
न द्रव्येण खण्डयामि, न क्षेत्रेण खण्डयामि, न कालेन खण्डयामि, न भावेन खण्डयामि; सुविशुद्ध एको ज्ञानमात्रो भावोऽस्मि।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
I do not fragment myself by dravya (substance). I do not fragment myself by kṣetra (space). I do not fragment myself by kāla (time). I do not fragment myself by bhāva (mode). I am the supremely pure, singular, jñānamātra bhāva.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The four-fold predication of "I do not fragment" addresses the four main categories through which dravya-paryāya analysis normally divides reality. From the niścaya perspective, none of these divisions touch the jñāyaka. He is not one substance among many dravyas, not located in a portion of kṣetra, not a moment among kālas, not a particular bhāva among bhāvas. He is jñānamātra — pure knowing-ness — which cannot be divided by any external framework.

**COMMENTARY:**
The śuddhanaya basis: when the jñānī holds the śuddhanaya (pure standpoint), the ātmā appears as undivided caitanya-mātra. No spatial location fragments it. No temporal moment captures it. No modal modification exhausts it. It is "suviśuddha" — supremely pure — beyond the admixture of karma-generated bhāvas. "Eko" — singular, not pluralized by the objects it knows. "Jñānamātro bhāvo'smi" — I AM this jñānamātra bhāva, not merely associated with it.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
I am not a fraction of space. I am not a moment of time. I am not this mood or that mood. I am the pure act of knowing — whole, undivided, singular. Whatever I know does not cut me into pieces. I know everything, and remain one.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Right now, are you the watcher of your thoughts — or are you identified with the content of the thoughts? The jñānamātra declaration is the recognition that the watcher is never fragmented by what is watched. Try to locate the knower. What is its boundary? Its duration? Its limit? If you cannot find a boundary, that borderless knowing is what Amṛtacandra calls "jñānamātro bhāvo'smi."

## Part 2: The 47 Śaktis — Complete Self-Sufficiency of Ātmā

### (Pariśiṣṭam, book pages 599–602, pre-K264)

The 47 śaktis (powers) of ātmā are enumerated to establish that ātmā lacks nothing. Every capacity it needs — to exist, to know, to be blissful, to be potent — is its own svarūpa, not borrowed from external factors.

**PRINCIPAL ŚAKTIS (First 10):**

1. **Jīvataśakti** — The power of being alive (caitanya svarūpa itself)
2. **Cittiśakti** — The power of consciousness as such (pure sentience)
3. **Anākāropayogamayī Jñānaśakti** — The power of formless (object-free) upayoga-jñāna — knowing without grasping an object's shape
4. **Sākāropayogamayī Jñānaśakti** — The power of formed (object-reflecting) upayoga-jñāna — knowing as it illuminates objects in their particular form
5. **Anākulatāsukhaśakti** — The power of undisturbed bliss (not subject to the agitation of external pleasures/pains)
6. **Svaropaparināmasāmarthya Vīryaśakti** — The power of vīrya as the capacity to transform in one's own mode (self-directed potency, not outward force)
7. **Ṣaṭsthānapatīvṛddhihānirūpa Amūrtaghutvaśakti** — The power of immaterial fineness, expressible in the sixfold gradations of increase and decrease
8. **Sarvabhāvavyāpakaikarūpa Vibhutaśakti** — The power of pervasion: the single form that pervades all bhāvas (omnipresent in its jñāna-nature)
9. **Viśvaviśeṣabhāvajñānamayī Sarvajñataśakti** — The power of omniscience: the jñāna-form that knows all particular bhāvas of all reality
10. **...** (continuing through 47 total sub-śaktis)

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The 47-śakti enumeration makes a structural point: every śakti that ātmā possesses is inherent (svābhāvika), not derived from karma, body, or external contact. The jñānaśakti is twofold — anākāra (knowing as undifferentiated light) and sākāra (knowing as differentiated object-reflection) — establishing that ātmā can know both the general and the particular without needing any external instrument. The sukhaśakti is "anākulatā" — not pleasure acquired, but the constitutional absence of agitation (vītarāga sukha). The vīryaśakti is directed inward (svarūpa-parināma-sāmarthya), not outward compulsion.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Ātmā is not poor. It has everything it needs within itself — the power to live, to know, to be blissful, to be potent. You do not need to acquire these from the world. They are already your svarūpa. Mokṣa is not gaining something new; it is the full expression of what you already are.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Which of the 47 śaktis do you ordinarily experience yourself as lacking? Jñānaśakti seems limited because you don't know everything. Sukhaśakti seems absent because you feel agitated. Vīryaśakti feels blocked. But the Jain teaching is that these śaktis exist in covered (āvaraṇa-yukt) form — karma merely veils them. The spiritual path is unveiling, not construction.

## Part 3: Syādvāda Purification — 14 Bhaṅgas of Jñāna

### (Kalaśas 247–263)

The Pariśiṣṭam's doctrinal centerpiece is the 14-bhaṅga analysis of jñāna's existence. This uses the syādvāda framework to establish precisely what jñāna is and what it is not — without falling into ekānta (one-sided absolutism) in either direction.

**THE 14 BHAṄGAS (Aspects):**

| # | Bhaṅga | Meaning |
|---|--------|---------|
| 1 | Svadravya-astittva | Jñāna exists in its own dravya (ātmā) |
| 2 | Paradravya-nastittva | Jñāna is non-existent in other dravyas (pudgala, etc.) |
| 3 | Svakṣetra-astittva | Jñāna exists in its own kṣetra (the space ātmā occupies) |
| 4 | Parakṣetra-nastittva | Jñāna is non-existent in others' kṣetra |
| 5 | Svakāla-astittva | Jñāna exists in its own kāla (its present mode) |
| 6 | Parakāla-nastittva | Jñāna is non-existent in others' kāla |
| 7 | Svabhāva-astittva | Jñāna exists in its own bhāva (jñāna-guṇa paryāya) |
| 8 | Parabhāva-nastittva | Jñāna is non-existent in others' bhāva |
| 9 | Krama-parināma | Jñāna changes sequentially (paryāya-kramic change) |
| 10 | Akrama-parināma | Jñāna's guṇa is unchanging (nityatva of jñāna-guṇa) |
| 11 | Nityatva | Jñāna is permanent (dravyārthinaya) |
| 12 | Anityatva | Jñāna is impermanent (paryāyārthinaya) |
| 13 | Nitya-anityatva | Jñāna is both permanent and impermanent (anekānta) |
| 14 | Avaktavyatva | Jñāna is inexpressible in its simultaneous unity (syāt-avaktavya) |

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 262, Anuṣṭubh):**
इत्यज्ञानविमूढ़ानां ज्ञानमात्रं प्रसादयन्।
आत्मतत्त्वमनेकान्तः स्वयमेवानुभूयते।। 262 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Thus, by clarifying jñānamātra for those bewildered by ajñāna, the anekānta-ātmatattva itself is experienced (anubhūyate) spontaneously (svayameva).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The 14-bhaṅga analysis is not merely intellectual. Its purpose is prasāda — clarification, purification. By precisely mapping what jñāna is (sva-dravya/kṣetra/kāla/bhāva) and what it is not (para-dravya/kṣetra/kāla/bhāva), syādvāda dissolves the jñāna-mūḍha's confusion. Once the confusion dissolves, the anekānta ātmatattva is not achieved — it is recognized. "Svayameva anubhūyate" — it experiences itself by itself.

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 263, Anuṣṭubh):**
एवं तत्त्वव्यवस्थित्या स्वं व्यवस्थापयन् स्वयम्।
अलङ्घ्यं शासनं जैनमेकान्तो व्यवस्थितः।। 263 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Thus, by establishing itself through tattva-vyavasthā (precise tattva-arrangement), the Jain śāsana, established by ekānta (syādvāda-ekānta / non-one-sided singleness), is unbreachable (alaṅghya).

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The 14 bhaṅgas are syādvāda's precision tool. Jñāna exists — but only where jñāna exists (in ātmā). Jñāna does not exist in stone or karma or body. Once you see this precisely, you stop confusing what is ātmā with what is not ātmā. And in that clarity, the ātmā recognizes itself automatically.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Every time you say "my body is sick," you are doing paradravya-astittva (wrongly placing ātmā in body's kṣetra/bhāva). Every time you say "I am agitated," you are doing parabhāva-astittva. The 14 bhaṅgas are a corrective lens: ātmā's jñāna is in its own dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva — nowhere else.

## Part 4: Upāya-Upeya — Ātmā Is Both Path and Goal

### (Kalaśas 266–268)

**DOCTRINAL STATEMENT:**
ātmavastuno jñānamātratvena upāyopeyabhāva — through its jñānamātra-nature, ātmā is simultaneously upāya (the means, the path) and upeya (the goal, the destination).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The conventional spiritual understanding posits a duality: there is a path (sādhanā, upāya) and there is a destination (mokṣa, upeya). The path is here; the goal is there. But the Pariśiṣṭam dissolves this duality. Since ātmā's own nature IS jñānamātra, and the goal of mokṣa is also jñānamātra (the unobstructed expression of the same nature), there is no external destination to reach. The sādhaka-rūpa (seeker-form of ātmā) and the siddha-rūpa (achieved-form of ātmā) are the same jñānamātra seen in two modes.

**COMMENTARY:**
- **Sādhaka-rūpa** = Ātmā as upāya: the jīva in its current state, practicing niścaya and vyavahāra, progressively removing āvaraṇa. The upāya is not external ritual — it is the ātmā's own svabhāva-paricaya (self-acquaintance).
- **Siddha-rūpa** = Ātmā as upeya: the same ātmā in its fully expressed state (siddha, paramātmā). The goal is not a separate place or entity but the same jñānamātra, now without āvaraṇa.
- The identity of upāya and upeya means: by knowing the ātmā (upāya), you become the ātmā (upeya). Jñāna of self IS liberation of self.

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 268, Vasantatilakā):**
चितिपिण्डचण्डमिलसिविकासहास:
शुद्धप्रकाशभरनिर्भरसुप्रभात:।
आनन्दसुस्थितसदाखिलतैकरूप:
तस्यैव चायमुदयत्यचलचिरत आत्मा।। 268 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
With the blazing laugh of citipiṇḍa's (the mass of consciousness) natural expansion, full of the burden of pure radiance, like an excellent dawn, eternally established in ānanda, of singular form that pervades everything always — for that one (who has taken refuge in this bhūmi) this ātmā rises, steady and eternal.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Citipiṇḍa-chaṇḍa-milasi-vikāsa-hāsaḥ" — the mass of consciousness (citipiṇḍa) has a natural expansive laugh (hāsa) — the spontaneous blossoming of awareness that is not forced but is the cit's natural tendency when unobstructed. "Śuddha-prakāśa-bhara-nirbhara" — full to overflowing with the weight of pure radiance. "Ānanda-susthita" — firmly established in ānanda (not bliss achieved but bliss as constitutional status). "Acala" — immovable. For the one who rests in the upāya-upeya bhāva, this ātmā arises — not from outside but from within.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The path and the goal are the same thing. You do not travel to some distant mokṣa — you deepen into what you already are. The further you go into ātmā, the more ātmā reveals itself. The destination is here; the direction is inward.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
When you practice self-knowledge, what are you practicing toward? If the goal is "pure jñāna" and the practice is "knowing the self as jñāna," then the practice is identical in nature to the goal. The only difference is degree of clarity. Is your practice moving inward or outward? Upāya-upeya identity means every genuine act of self-knowing IS liberation, even if incomplete.

## Part 5: Jñāna–Jñeya–Jñātṛ Are One Vastu

### (Kalaśas 269–271)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 271, Śālinī):**
योऽयं भावो ज्ञानमात्रोऽहमस्मि
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानमात्र: स नैव।
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानकल्लोलवल्गन्
ज्ञानज्ञेयज्ञातृमद्वस्तुमात्र:।। 271 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
This bhāva — I am jñānamātra. The jñeya (object) is not jñeyajñānamātra as something separate. The jñeya (as it appears) floats as waves (kallola) of jñeya-jñāna. [In truth] this is the pure vastu of jñāna-jñeya-jñātṛ combined.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
This is the philosophical summit of the Pariśiṣṭam. The apparent triadic structure — jñātṛ (knower), jñeya (known), jñāna (knowledge) — which seems to divide reality into three is here revealed as one undivided vastu (jñānamātra vastu). The jñeya does not exist as a separate entity that jñāna "reaches out to." It appears as waves (kallola) within jñāna itself. The ocean does not go to the waves — the waves are the ocean in motion. Similarly, jñeya is jñāna in its knowing-mode. Jñātṛ, jñāna, jñeya = one jñānamātra vastu.

**COMMENTARY:**
This resolves the deepest philosophical tension in the Samaysaar: the Adhikar 1 teaching that jīva is both jñātā (knower) and parijñātā (one to be known), the Adhikar 9 teaching that jñāna is avyatirikt (non-separate) from jñāyaka — all converge here. The Pariśiṣṭam's K271 is the culminating synthesis:
- Knower (jñātṛ) = jñānamātra bhāva
- Object (jñeya) = jñāna-kallola (wave of jñāna, not external to it)
- Knowing (jñāna) = the single vastu that contains both
- Result: jñānamātra vastu alone is real

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When you know something, three things seem to happen: "I" know, "it" is known, and "knowledge" connects them. But in pure ātmā, these three collapse into one: the pure knowing-ness. The "thing known" is just a wave in that knowing-ness. There is no outsider.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Try to find the gap between you (the knower) and your awareness (the knowing). Is there actually a gap? Or is the knower made of knowing? Now try to find the gap between your awareness and what appears in it. Is the thought "outside" your awareness, or is it a wave within it? Kalaśa 271 is the direct pointing to this non-gap.

## Part 6: The Meca/Ameca Dialectic — Anekāntatva of Ātmā

### (Kalaśas 272–274)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 272, Pṛthvī):**
क्वचिच्छ्वसिति मेचकं क्वचिन्मेचकामेचकं
क्वचित्पुनरमेचकं सहजमेव तत्त्वं मम।
तथापि न विमोहयत्यलमेधसां तन्मन:
परस्परसुसंहतप्रकटशक्तिचक्रं स्फुरत्।। 272 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Sometimes (my tattva) appears meca (dark, impure — mixed with karma), sometimes meca-ameca (dark-and-clear, mixed), sometimes ameca (pure, clear) — yet my tattva is sahaja (natural, unchanged) throughout. Yet this does not bewilder the mind of the alam-medhasā (pure-minded, sufficient-intellect person), for the interlinked and manifest power-complex (śakticakra) blazes forth.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Meca" = dark, impure, mixed with blemish (Sanskrit: meghavat — clouded). "Ameca" = pure, clear, unblemished. Under different naya-viewpoints (dravya vs paryāya) and different karma-states, ātmā appears differently:
- From upādāna-krama-paryāya view: sometimes impure-mode (meca)
- From śuddhopayoga view: pure-mode (ameca)  
- From vyavahāra-naya: both mixed (meca-ameca)
Yet the tattva itself (jñānamātra bhāva) is sahaja — unchanged throughout. The alam-medhasā (the truly wise, not the half-educated) is not bewildered by these apparently contradictory appearances because he holds the śakticakra (the integrated understanding of all śaktis) and sees the unity beneath the multiplicity.

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 273, Pṛthvī):**
इतो गतमनेकतां दधदितः सदाप्येकता-
मितः क्षणविभङ्गुरं ध्रुवमितः सदैवोदयात्।
इतः परमविस्तृतं धृतमितः प्रदेशैर्निजैः
रहो सहजात्मनस्तदिदमद्भुतं वैभवम्।। 273 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
From one angle: anekā (many-natured); from another: always eka (one). From one angle: kṣaṇabhaṅgura (momentary); from another: dhruva (permanent — by always arising). From one angle: paramavistṛta (infinitely extended); from another: held within its own pradeśas. This is the wonderful (adbhuta) vaibhava (glory) of the sahaja-ātmā — it is a secret (raho).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The anekāntatva of ātmā is not a contradiction — it is the glory (vaibhava) of the sahaja-ātmā. "Raho" — secret, not hidden from those who look carefully, but inaccessible to ekānta-absolutists who demand ātmā be only eka or only aneka. The six-fold anekānta: aneka AND eka, kṣaṇabhaṅgura AND dhruva, vast AND bounded — all are simultaneously true from different vantage points.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Ātmā is not contradicting itself when it appears different from different angles. It is the same way a diamond appears different colors in different lights — the diamond itself doesn't change. The truly wise person holds all the angles together without being bewildered.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
You are one person and many persons simultaneously — you are different to your parent than to your colleague than to your student. You are momentary (each moment a new experience) and permanent (the same "I" across decades). Which is the real you? The anekānta answer: both, from their respective vantage points. Neither alone captures the whole.

## Part 7: Victory of Caitanya-Camatkāra

### (Kalaśa 275)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 275, Mālinī):**
जयति सहजतेज:पुञ्जमज्जित्रिलोकी-
स्खलदखिलविकल्पोऽप्येक एव स्वरूप:।
स्वरसविसरपूर्णच्छिन्नतत्त्वोपलब्ध:
प्रसभनियमितार्चिश्चित्वचित्मत्कार एष:।। 275 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Victory to this caitanya-camatkāra — the ātmā that drowns the triloka in its natural radiance (sahajatejapuñja), whose svarūpa is singular (ekam eva) even as all vikalpas dissolve (skhalat), whose tattvopalabdhi (self-realization) is complete and full of its own rasa (svarasa-visara-pūrṇa), unobstructed (chinna — cut free from obstruction), whose flame is absolutely constant (prasabha-niyamita — unconditionally regulated, i.e. unfailing). This camatkāra is victorious!

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Sahajatejapuñja-majji-trilokī" — the triloka (three realms) is drowned/immersed in the natural radiance of ātmā. Not that ātmā illuminates a portion of the triloka — the entire triloka floats within ātmā's jñāna. This is the sarvajñatā of the fully expressed ātmā. "Skhalad-akhila-vikalpa" — all vikalpas (conceptual elaborations) stumble and fall away; what remains is the singular svarūpa. "Svarasa-visara-pūrṇa" — complete fullness from one's own rasa (intrinsic quality), not from external input. "Prasabha-niyamita-arci" — the ātmā's tejas (radiance) is ananta-vīrya regulated — it cannot falter because it is not powered by anything external that could be removed.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The jñānī's ātmā wins. Not by fighting but by the natural blazing of its own light. A lamp doesn't fight darkness — it simply shines, and the darkness has no choice. Caitanya-camatkāra (the wonder of pure consciousness) is the inevitable victor.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Camatkāra means wonder, amazement, delight. When you recognize your own consciousness as the light in which everything appears, there is a natural camatkāra — not manufactured excitement but the spontaneous amazement of the ātmā recognizing itself. Have you ever experienced this? Even a glimpse?

## Part 8: Amṛtacandra's Blessing — The Author Reveals His Name

### (Kalaśa 276)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 276, Mālinī):**
अविचलितचिदात्मन्यात्मनातमान्मात्म-
न्यनवरतनिमग्नं धारयद्ध्वस्तमोहम्।
उदितममृतचंद्रज्योतिरेत्समन्ता-
ज्ज्वलतु विमलपूर्ण निःसपत्नस्वभावम्।। 276 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
May the one who holds (dhārayet) the motionless cit-ātmā (avicalita-cidātman) within itself, constantly immersed (anavarata-nimagnam), with moha destroyed (dhvasta-moham) — may that Amṛtacandra-jyoti (the nectar-moon's radiance; and the author Amṛtacandra himself) blaze forth (jvalatu) everywhere (samantāt), pure and full (vimala-pūrṇa), with an uncontested nature (niḥsapatna-svabhāvam)!

**ANVAYARTHA:**
This is the most personal verse in the entire Ātmakhyāti. Amṛtacandra Ācārya encodes his own name in "amṛtacandrajyotiḥ" — a śleṣa (double-meaning): (1) the jyoti (light/lamp) of amṛtacandra (nectar-moon = the fully expressed jñāna-ātmā), and (2) Amṛtacandra, the author, offering a self-blessing. 

The verse structure: It is a blessing prayer (jvalatu = optative, "may it blaze"). Amṛtacandra is praying for the ātmā of the one who practices this bhāva — the avicalita-cidātman (the motionless awareness) — to shine. He reveals that he himself is in this svarūpa ("nimagnam dhārayet") and names that radiance after himself or names himself after that radiance.

"Niḥsapatna" — without rival, uncontested. The jñānamātra bhāva of ātmā has no competitor. No other dravya can claim to be the knowing subject. No karma, no body, no external force can contest this sovereignty. Ātmā's svabhāva is niḥsapatna.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Amṛtacandra stops being the commentator and becomes the practitioner. "I am the one immersed in the motionless awareness. May this light blaze everywhere." He puts his name in the light itself. The commentary is complete; what remains is the blazing.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What does it mean to immerse (nimagnam) yourself in motionless awareness? Not to stop the mind by force, but to let the ātmā rest in its own nature without reaching outward. Avicalita — unshaken. Anavarata — without interruption. This is the continuous samādhi that Amṛtacandra embodies as he closes the commentary.

## Part 9: The Colophon — Words Reveal Through Their Own Śakti

### (Kalaśas 277–278)

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 277, Śārdūlavikrīḍita):**
यस्माद्द्वैतमभूत्पुरा स्वपरयोर्भूत् यतोऽत्रान्तरं
रागद्वेषपरिग्रहे सति यतो जातं क्रियाकारकैः।
भुञ्जान च यतोऽनुभूतिरखिलं खिन्ना क्रियायाः फलं
तद्विज्ञानघनोऽघमग्नमधुना किञ्चिन्न किञ्चित्कल।। 277 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Because of which (ajñāna) the dvaita (duality between sva and para) arose in the past; because of which there was separation (antara) between sva and para; because of which, with the taking up of rāga-dveṣa-parigraha, kriyā-kārakas (the differentiations of action and agent) arose; and because of which the anubhūti (experience) exhausted (khinnā) itself in consuming all the fruits of kriyā — that (ajñāna) is now drowned in vijñānaghana (the dense mass of jñāna). Now (adhunā) — nothing at all (kiñcin na kiñcit).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
This is the penultimate verse — the contrast between the ajñāna-state and the jñāna-state, stated through a long "yasmāt" (because of which) clause. In ajñāna: dvaita appeared (sva and para seemed separate), rāga-dveṣa arose from that duality, kriyā-kārakas (the agent/action distinction) proliferated, and the anubhūti became exhausted by consuming kriyā's fruits. Now (adhunā), all of that is vijñānaghana — drowned in the dense ocean of jñāna. "Kiñcin na kiñcit" — nothing. Not "nothing bad happened" but "no other thing exists as a separate reality." Vijñānaghana alone is.

**PRAKRIT/SANSKRIT TEXT (Kalaśa 278, Upajāti — Final Verse of the Commentary):**
स्वशक्तिसंसूचितवस्तुतत्त्वै-
र्व्याख्या कृतेयं समयस्य शब्दै:।
स्वरूपगुप्तस्य न किञ्चिदस्ति
कर्तव्यमेवामृतचंद्रसूरे:।। 278 ।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The commentary (vyākhyā) of the Samaya (Samayaprābhṛta) has been accomplished through words (śabdaiḥ) that reveal (saṃsūcita) the tattva-vastu (the reality-substance) through their own śakti (svayaṃ-śakti). For Amṛtacandrasūri — who is absorbed (gupta) in his own svarūpa (svayaṃ-rūpa) — there is nothing whatsoever to do (na kiñcid karttavyam).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Sva-śakti-saṃsūcita-vastu-tattvaiḥ śabdaiḥ" — the words of the commentary revealed the vastu-tattva through the words' own śakti, not through the author's ego. The implication: ātmā (as vastu) is self-revealing. The words of jñāna-śāstra carry an inherent power to reveal the ātmā-tattva when heard/read with śraddhā. The commentator is merely the transparent medium.

"Svarūpa-gupta" — Amṛtacandra is "hidden in his own svarūpa" — absorbed in the ātmā, not visible as an ego-author. "Na kiñcid karttavyam" — nothing remains to be done. This is the mokṣa-declaration in grammatical form: for one absorbed in svarūpa, there is no agent-action-result structure anymore. The kārtā has dissolved into jñātā.

**BOOK COLOPHON:**
इति श्रीमदमृतचंद्राचार्यकृता समयसारव्याख्या आत्मख्याति: समाप्ता।।

**LITERAL TRANSLATION:**
Thus is complete the commentary on the Samaysaar called Ātmakhyāti, composed by Śrīmad Amṛtacandracārya.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The commentary is done. Not because the author ran out of things to say, but because the author has dissolved into the ātmā he was describing. When the knower, the known, and the knowing are one — what commentary remains to be written?

**CONTEMPLATE:**
"Na kiñcid karttavyam" — nothing to do. Not laziness. Not nihilism. The complete satisfaction of one who has returned home. What would it feel like to have genuinely nothing left to do — not because you gave up, but because you arrived? That is the state from which Kalaśa 278 is spoken.
