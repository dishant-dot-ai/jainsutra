# Samaysaar — Adhikar 5: Samvar (संवर अधिकार)
## Stoppage of Karma-Influx

**Gathas: G181–G192 (12 gathas)**
**Source:** Acharya Kundkund's Samaysaar (Prakrit original)
**Commentary basis:** Amritchandra Suri's Atmakhyati

---

## Part 1: Bheda-Vijñāna — Consciousness Is Consciousness Alone (G181–G182)

### G181

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
उवओगे उवओगो कोहादिसु णित्थि को वि उवओगो।
कोहो कोहे चेव हि उवओगे णित्थि खलु कोहो।।१८१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
उपयोगे उपयोगः क्रोधादिषु नास्ति कोऽपि उपयोगः।
क्रोधः क्रोधे एव हि उपयोगे नास्ति खलु क्रोधः।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
In upayoga (consciousness), there is upayoga. In krodha (anger) and others, there is no upayoga at all. In krodha, there is only krodha. In upayoga, there is certainly no krodha.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
This gatha establishes the foundational principle of bheda-vijñāna (discriminative knowledge): consciousness and passion are categorically distinct. Upayoga — the soul's pure knowing-seeing — is self-contained. It does not contain krodha or any kaṣāya. Conversely, krodha is a karmic modification; it does not contain upayoga. The two never interpenetrate.

**COMMENTARY:**
Samvar (stoppage of āsrava) does not begin with effort — it begins with knowledge. What knowledge? The radical recognition that "I am upayoga; I am not krodha." When the soul mistakenly identifies with its anger, love, fear, or pride — it mixes itself with what it is not. That mixing is āsrava. When the soul clearly sees: "krodha is in krodha-realm; I am in upayoga-realm" — the mixing stops. Samvar begins in this moment of discrimination.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Anger is in anger. Awareness is in awareness. Neither bleeds into the other — except when you mistakenly believe they do.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Right now, notice your awareness. Then notice any irritation, restlessness, or craving present. Are they the same thing? Or is awareness something that *sees* these, without being them?

---

### G182

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
अट्ठिवियप्पे कम्मे णोकम्मे चावि णित्थि उवओगो।
उवओगम्हि य कम्मं णोकम्मं चावि णो अत्थि।।१८२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
अष्टविकल्पे कर्मणि नोकर्मणि चापि नास्ति उपयोगः।
उपयोगे च कर्म नोकर्म चापि न अस्ति।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
In the eight-type karma and nokarma, there is no upayoga. And in upayoga, there is neither karma nor nokarma.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
G181 established: consciousness ≠ kaṣāya. G182 extends this: consciousness ≠ karma (the eight karmas) and ≠ nokarma (the physical body and material adjuncts). The soul is utterly other than everything it seems to wear. The eight karma-types (jñānāvaraṇīya, darśanāvaraṇīya, vedanīya, mohanīya, āyu, nāma, gotra, antarāya) are all material. The body is material. Upayoga — pure consciousness — is in none of these, and none of these are in it.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is a sweeping metaphysical declaration. The soul's consciousness does not reside in karma. Karma does not reside in consciousness. They co-exist in the same locus (one ātmā-pradeśa) but without mixing — like water and oil. The confusion of ajñānī is precisely this: he looks at his body, his emotions, his karma-driven moods — and says "this is me." The jñānī looks and says: "none of this is me — I am only upayoga, pure knowing."

**SIMPLY PUT:**
You are not your karma. You are not your body. You are not your emotions. You are the awareness that knows all of these.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Everything you call "mine" — your habits, your tendencies, your body — is on one side of a line. On the other side is pure knowing. Which side are you?

## Part 2: When Bheda-Vijñāna Arises (G183)

### G183

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
एदं तु अविवरीदं णाणं जइया दु होिद जीवस्स।
तइया ण किंचि कुव्विद भावं उवओगसुद्धप्पा।।१८३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एतत् तु अविपरीतं ज्ञानं यदा तु भवति जीवस्य।
तदा न किञ्चित् करोति भावं उपयोगशुद्धात्मा।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
When this non-reversed, discriminative knowledge (aviparīta-jñāna / bheda-vijñāna) arises in the soul — then the soul, pure in upayoga, creates no bhāva other than pure knowing.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Non-reversed" (aviparīta) means correct, undistorted — not confused, not inverted. The reversed knowledge (viparīta) is ajñāna: taking the body for the soul, taking rāga for happiness, taking karma for self. When knowledge becomes aviparīta — correctly oriented — the soul abides as upayoga-śuddhātmā: a self purified through resting in pure consciousness. In that state, no new bhāva (modified state) arises that would generate āsrava.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is the mechanism of samvar. It is not a practice of suppression or effort in the conventional sense. It is the natural outcome of correct knowledge. When I correctly know "I am not this rāga, I am not this karma-modification" — I stop generating rāga. Not through fighting it, but through not-being-it. The discriminative knowledge is itself the samvar. This is why Kundkund's path is called jñāna-mārga — the path of knowledge — not tapas-mārga or ritual-mārga.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When you truly know what you are, you stop making what you're not.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is there a difference between "trying not to get angry" and "knowing I am not anger"? Which would produce more lasting peace?

## Part 3: The Gold-Fire Analogy — Jñānitva Is Indestructible (G184)

### G184

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जह कणयमग्गितविंयं पि कणयभावं ण तं परिच्चयिद।
तह कम्मोदयतिवदो ण जहिद णाणी दु णाणित्तं।।१८४।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
यथा कनकं अग्नितप्तं अपि कनकभावं न तत् परित्यजति।
तथा कर्मोदयतप्तः न जहाति ज्ञानी तु ज्ञानित्वम्।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as gold, even when heated in fire, does not abandon its gold-nature — so the jñānī, even when scorched by the rise of karma, does not abandon his jñānitva (the state of being a knower).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Gold under fire: it may liquefy, change shape, appear different — but its essential nature as gold (kanakatva) remains unaltered. The fire cannot destroy what gold fundamentally is. Similarly, karma-udaya (the rising, fruiting of bound karma) puts the soul through pleasure, pain, favourable and unfavourable conditions — yet these cannot remove the soul's fundamental nature as pure consciousness-knowledge (jñānitva). The jñānī may experience karma's fruits but remains established in knowing.

**COMMENTARY:**
This gatha offers tremendous reassurance. The seeker may worry: "if karma keeps rising and affecting me, how can I truly be a jñānī?" Kundkund answers: karma can affect your experience, your body, your circumstances — but it cannot touch your jñānitva. The question is only whether *you* consciously hold that identity. If you forget you are the gold and panic about being in the fire, that forgetting is the problem — not the fire. Karma's rise is not your fall unless you abandon your identity as knower.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Karma can heat you but cannot change what you fundamentally are — a knower. Don't abandon your nature while passing through fire.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Think of a difficulty you are facing. Is it affecting your circumstances, your emotions, your body? Now ask: is it affecting your capacity to know? Is awareness itself damaged?

## Part 4: Jñānī vs. Ajñānī — The Fundamental Difference (G185)

### G185

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
एवं जाणिद णाणी अण्णाणी मुणिद रागमेवादं।
अण्णाणतमोच्छण्णो आदसहावं अयाणंतो।।१८५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एवं जानाति ज्ञानी; अज्ञानी मनुते रागमेवात्मानम्।
अज्ञानतमश्छन्नः आत्मस्वभावं अजानन्।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Thus knows the jñānī. The ajñānī — covered by the darkness of ajñāna, not knowing the soul's svabhāva — considers rāga itself to be the ātmā.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The gatha contrasts two beings looking at the same reality. The jñānī sees: "rāga is modification; I am upayoga." The ajñānī, shrouded in the darkness of ajñāna (non-knowledge), cannot see ātma-svabhāva (the soul's true nature). In that darkness, rāga *looks like* the self. He identifies with what he feels — love, hatred, desire, fear — thinking "this is me." This mis-identification is the deepest cause of bondage.

**COMMENTARY:**
"Ajñāna-tamo-cchanna" — covered by the darkness of non-knowledge. The darkness metaphor is precise: darkness is not a presence but an absence. Ajñāna is not knowledge of falsehood; it is the absence of self-knowledge. In that absence, illusion fills the void. The ajñānī is not evil or weak — he is simply in the dark. He has not yet seen his own light. When he does — that moment is enlightenment (samyak-darśana): not a supernatural event but simply the first unclouded seeing of what was always there.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The ajñānī doesn't know who he is, so he becomes whatever emotion is loudest. The jñānī knows who he is, and watches emotions as what they are — passing modifications.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
When you felt very angry or very much in love — did you think "I am feeling this" or did it feel like "I am this"? That difference is everything.

## Part 5: You Attain What You Know (G186)

### G186

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
सुद्धं तु बियाणंतो सुद्धं चेवप्पयं लहिद जीवो।
जाणंतो दु असुद्धं असुद्धमेवप्पयं लहिद।।१८६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
शुद्धं तु विजानन् शुद्धमेव आत्मानं लभते जीवः।
जानन् तु अशुद्धं अशुद्धमेव आत्मानं लभते।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul that knows (experiences/identifies with) the pure self — attains only the pure self. The soul that knows the impure — attains only the impure self.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Knowing" here is not mere intellectual knowing but vijñāna — experiential identification, living-knowledge. When the soul knows (inhabits, identifies with) the śuddha-ātmā — the pure, untainted consciousness — it attains (realizes, becomes) that purity. When it knows (identifies with) the aśuddha — the karma-tainted, rāga-dveṣa-modified states — it attains (becomes, perpetuates) that impurity. This is the law of spiritual causation: what you identify as your self, you become.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is the most condensed and powerful statement in all of Adhikar 5. It is not prescriptive ("try to be pure") — it is descriptive of a law. Knowing-identification is causal. The soul does not need to fight impurity; it needs to correctly know purity. When you know gold as gold, you are gold. When you know the fire as gold, you are confused fire. The instruction is not "be good" but "know correctly." Right knowledge of self = attainment of that self. Wrong identification = attainment of what is wrongly identified with.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Whatever you deeply believe yourself to be, you become. Know yourself as pure consciousness — and that is what you shall attain.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Right now, in this moment — what do you take yourself to be? A body? A person with a history? A bundle of feelings? Or the awareness that knows all of these?

## Part 6: The Practice of Self-Meditation (G187–G189)

### G187

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
अप्पाणमप्पणा रुंधिऊण दोपुण्णपावजोगेसु।
दंसणणाणम्हि ठिदो इच्छाविरदो य अण्णम्हि।।१८७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
आत्मानं आत्मना रुद्ध्वा द्वयोः पुण्यपापयोगयोः।
दर्शनज्ञाने स्थितः इच्छाविरतः च अन्यत्र।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Restraining the self by the self from both shubha (merit/punya) and ashubha (demerit/pāpa) yoga — established in darśana-jñāna, withholding desire from anything else —

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Restraining the self by the self" — this phrase is crucial. It is not external restraint, not someone else's rules, not fear of consequence. The soul restrains itself through its own understanding. What does it restrain? Both punya-yoga (meritorious activity) and pāpa-yoga (demeritorious activity). Both shubha and ashubha are yoga — modifications that generate karma. Samvar requires stepping back from both. The soul then stands in darśana-jñāna — pure seeing-knowing — and withdraws desire from "anything else" (anything that is not the pure self).

**COMMENTARY:**
This is an advanced teaching. Most religious teachings say: "do good, avoid evil." Kundkund says: "withdraw from both good and evil as modifications, and rest in pure knowing." This is the distinction between dharma (conventional virtue) and moksha-dharma (liberation-oriented practice). Dharma from rāga-basis keeps generating karma, even if shubha karma. Moksha-dharma is rooted in the recognition: "I am neither the doer of punya nor the doer of pāpa — I am the knower."

**SIMPLY PUT:**
True restraint is not following rules — it is resting in your own awareness, withdrawing desire from everything external, including virtue performed with attachment.

---

### G188

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जो सव्वसंगमुक्को झायिद अप्पाणमप्पणो अप्पा।
ण वि कम्मं णोकम्मं चेदा चिंतेिद एयतं।।१८८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
यः सर्वसंगमुक्तः ध्यायति आत्मानं आत्मनः आत्मा।
न अपि कर्म न नोकर्म चेतयिता चिन्तयति एकत्वम्।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
That soul — free from all attachments — meditates on the self by the self, as the self. The cetayitā (experiencing consciousness) thinks of nothing — neither karma nor nokarma — only ekatva (oneness).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Three-fold self-reference: "meditates on the self (object) by the self (instrument) as the self (nature)." Subject = self. Object = self. Means = self. This is śuddha-upayoga in practice — no external reference point, no object outside the self. The cetayitā (that which experiences) is focused on ekatva — the singularity of the soul, undivided, not mixed with karma or nokarma. This is ātma-dhyāna: self-meditation.

**COMMENTARY:**
This gatha describes the actual meditative state Kundkund points to. It is not meditation on a symbol, deity, concept, or even on a quality of the soul — it is meditation on the soul as itself. Complete self-referentiality. "Free from all attachments (sarva-saṅga-mukta)" is the precondition — not attachment-free in external behavior alone, but internally unattached to any view, any object, any becoming. In this state, even karma and nokarma are irrelevant to the meditating consciousness. It dwells in the single fact of its own being.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Sit in yourself. Think of yourself. By yourself. As yourself. Leave karma and body and everything else outside this room of awareness.

---

### G189

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
अप्पाणं झायंतो दंसणणाणमओ अणण्णमओ।
लहिद अचिरेण अप्पाणमेव सो कम्मपिवमुक्कं।।१८९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
आत्मानं ध्यायन् दर्शनज्ञानमयः अनन्यमयः।
लभते अचिरेण आत्मानमेव सः कर्मपंकमुक्तम्।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Meditating on the self — consisting of darśana-jñāna, not-other-than-self — he attains in a short time the self alone, freed from all karma-mire.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
"Darśana-jñāna-maya" — the soul that is made of (consists of) darśana (right perception) and jñāna (right knowledge). "Ananya-maya" — not-made-of-other, containing nothing foreign to the self. This soul, in the state of self-meditation, "attains in a short time" the self freed from karma-panka (karma-mud, karma-mire). The liberation is not distant — "acirenva" means "in no great time," quickly, swiftly.

**COMMENTARY:**
The promise of this gatha is extraordinary: one who meditates correctly — on the self, as darśana-jñāna, as not-other — attains liberation swiftly. The qualifier "correctly" carries the entire weight. Correct self-meditation is not thinking about the self, analyzing the self, or even loving the self. It is being the self, as pure knowing-seeing, without mixing in anything else. When this happens, karma cannot adhere. Already-bound karma exhausts itself through experience (nirjarā). New karma does not bind. Liberation follows — "in no great time."

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Meditate as a knower-seer who contains nothing other than knowing-seeing — and liberation is close.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What would it feel like to sit for a moment as pure awareness — not analyzing, not planning, not feeling, just knowing that you are knowing?

## Part 7: The Four Causes of Āsrava (G190)

### G190

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
तेसिं हेदू भणिदा अझ्वसाणाणि सव्वदरिसीिहं।
मिच्छत्तं अण्णाणं अविरयभावो य जोगो य।।१९०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
तेषां हेतवः भणिताः अध्यवसानानि सर्वदर्शिभिः।
मिथ्यात्वं अज्ञानं अविरतभावः च योगः च।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The causes of karma — the adhyavasānas (inner determinations) — have been declared by the omniscient (sarvadraṣṭā): mithyātva (wrong belief), ajñāna (non-knowledge), aviratabhāva (non-restraint), and yoga (mental-vocal-physical activity).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The word "adhyavasāna" means inner determination, psychic disposition, the soul's deep-rooted tendency or orientation. The four causes are:
1. **Mithyātva** — wrong belief about the nature of soul/reality; the root confusion
2. **Ajñāna** — ignorance of the soul's true nature; absence of self-knowledge
3. **Aviratabhāva** — the disposition of non-restraint; engagement with sense-objects without discrimination
4. **Yoga** — activity of mind, speech, and body (which in themselves cause karma to adhere, even for the omniscient, but in the liberated context yoga gradually ceases)

**COMMENTARY:**
This gatha names the enemy with precision. Karma does not arrive randomly. It arrives through specific causes. The omniscient see this clearly and declare it. Note the hierarchy: mithyātva and ajñāna are root causes (internal, cognitive); aviratabhāva is the behavioral manifestation; yoga is the surface activity. Samvar, then, must address all four — beginning from the root: correcting mithyātva through samyak-darśana, developing jñāna through study and self-inquiry, cultivating viratabhāva through renunciation, and eventually stilling yoga through deep meditation.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Karma flows in through four doors: wrong belief, self-ignorance, unrestrained living, and constant mental-physical activity. Close these doors and karma stops entering.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Which of these four do you recognize most in yourself? That is where your practice begins.

## Part 8: The Causal Chain of Samvar (G191–G192)

### G191

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
हेदुअभावे णियमा जायिद णाणिस्स आसविणरोहो।
आसवभावेण विणा जायिद कम्मस्स वि णिरोहो।।१९१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
हेत्वभावे नियमतः जायते ज्ञानिनः आस्रवनिरोधः।
आस्रवभावेन विना जायते कर्मणः अपि निरोधः।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Without those causes, āsrava-nirodha (stoppage of influx) necessarily arises for the jñānī. Without āsrava-bhāva, karma-nirodha (stoppage of karma) also arises.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
G190 named the four causes of āsrava. G191 applies the law of causation inversely: remove the causes → the effect (āsrava) necessarily ceases. "Niyamata" = necessarily, by logical necessity, not as mere possibility. This is not "might happen" — it is: if causes are absent, effect cannot arise. Without āsrava-bhāva (the state of karma-influx), karma itself cannot bind. Karma-nirodha follows āsrava-nirodha as effect follows cause.

**COMMENTARY:**
The logical precision of Jain causation is on full display here. Kundkund is not speaking in poetry or metaphor but in strict causal logic. If A causes B, then not-A yields not-B. The four causes produce āsrava. Without the four causes, āsrava stops. Without āsrava, karma cannot bind. The path is clear: work on the causes (mithyātva, ajñāna, avirati, yoga) — samvar follows necessarily. This is why jñāna (addressing mithyātva and ajñāna) is the most fundamental practice: it addresses the deepest two causes first.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When the causes stop, the effect stops. Fix the source of āsrava and new karma cannot enter — necessarily.

---

### G192

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
कम्मस्साभावेण य णोकम्माणं पि जायिद णिरोहो।
णोकम्मिणरोहेण य संसारिणरोहणं होिद।।१९२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
कर्मणः अभावेन च नोकर्मणाम् अपि जायते निरोधः।
नोकर्मनिरोधेन च संसारनिरोधनं भवति।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Without karma → nirodha (cessation) of nokarma also arises. Without nokarma → cessation of samsāra occurs.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The causal chain completes:
- Remove four causes → āsrava-nirodha (G191)
- āsrava-nirodha → karma-nirodha (G191)
- karma-nirodha → nokarma-nirodha (G192)
- nokarma-nirodha → samsāra-nirodha (G192)

Nokarma is the body (audārika, vaikriyika, āhāraka, taijasa, kārmaṇa śarīras) and other material adjuncts sustained by karma. Without karma, these cannot be generated anew. When nokarma ceases, the last material tether to samsāra is removed. Samsāra-nirodha = the end of the cycle of birth and death. This is moksha described from the samvar-side: not as an achievement but as the inevitable consequence of removing causes.

**COMMENTARY:**
Kundkund ends Adhikar 5 with this chain as a logical proof of liberation. Moksha is not a gift, not a grace, not a lucky accident — it is the necessary outcome of a causal chain being correctly reversed. Begin at the root: remove mithyātva → the entire chain dissolves step by step until samsāra itself ends. The teaching is complete. Samvar is not suppression or effort in conventional sense — it is the knowledge-driven removal of causes, after which liberation follows as inevitably as the absence of fire follows the removal of fuel.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
No causes → no āsrava → no karma → no body → no samsāra. The path of liberation is a chain of absences, each following from the one before.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
You cannot directly destroy samsāra. But you can remove the first cause — mithyātva — through right understanding of your own nature. That is enough. The rest follows by law.

## Summary of Adhikar 5 — Samvar

| Gatha | Theme |
|-------|-------|
| G181 | Upayoga ≠ krodha — discrimination is the beginning of samvar |
| G182 | Upayoga ≠ karma, ≠ nokarma — complete otherness of consciousness |

| G187 | Self restrained by self from both punya and pāpa yoga |
| G188 | Meditation on self by self as self — ekatva, free from karma and nokarma |
| G189 | Self-meditation leads to swift liberation |
| G190 | Four causes of āsrava: mithyātva, ajñāna, avirati, yoga |

| G192 | Without karma → nokarma stops; without nokarma → samsāra stops |

**Featured Verse:** G186 — "The soul that knows the pure self attains only the pure self."

**Central Doctrine:** Bheda-vijñāna (discriminative knowledge between self and non-self) is the mechanism of samvar. When correctly known, one naturally ceases to make the modifications that generate āsrava. The causal chain of liberation follows necessarily.

**Closing colophon:** इति संवरो निष्क्रान्त:। संवरप्ररूपक: पञ्चमोऽङ्क:।।
*(Thus concludes Samvar. Fifth act — expounding Samvar — is complete.)*
