# Samaysaar — Adhikar 8: Moksha (मोक्ष अधिकार)
**Gathas 288–307 · 20 Gathas**
**Source:** Samaysaar by Ācārya Kundakunda · Commentary: Ātmakhyāti by Ācārya Amṛtacandra Sūri

---

## Opening Kalaśa (Śikharinī)

Having cut — with the prajñā-saw — and separated the bandha-puruṣa (the soul identified with bondage), now guiding toward moksha, that supreme complete knowledge, overflowing with natural supreme bliss, having done all that was to be done, now triumphs.

---

## Part 1: The Bound Man Analogy — Knowing Is Not Enough, Cutting Is Required (G288–G292)

### G288
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
बद्धो मणुस्सो ते बंधे जाणइ तिव्वमंदए।
तत्थ काले य जाणंतो इत्थिपुरिसओ इह।।288।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
A bound man knows those bonds — their intense (tīvra) and mild (manda) nature, their duration, and knows them fully — whether woman or man.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
A man who is physically bound knows the nature of his bondage — whether it is tight or loose, how long it has been, how much longer it will last. He is aware of every detail of his binding.

**COMMENTARY:**
Ācārya opens Adhikar 8 with a direct analogy. A person tied with ropes knows everything about those ropes — their tightness, their material, how long they have been tied. This knowledge is vivid and detailed. Yet — and this is the point — that knowledge does not free him. The analogy prepares the ground for the central teaching: knowing bondage and being freed from bondage are entirely different things.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
If you are tied with ropes, you know exactly how you are bound. But that knowledge alone does not untie you.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Do you know your passions, your karma-nature, your mental patterns? Does that knowing itself free you — or does something more need to happen?

---

### G289
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
ण य सो तिं छिंदइ बंधे चिरेण वि ण मुच्चइ।
तह कम्मविस्सपडियो जीवो जोगे वि ण मुच्चइ।।289।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
If he does not cut those bonds, even over a long time he does not attain liberation. Similarly, a soul absorbed in karma — even through yogic practice — does not attain liberation.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
No matter how long the bound man remains bound while merely knowing his bonds, he will not be freed. The same applies to the soul: if it remains absorbed in karma (kamma-vissppaḍiya — smeared in karma-mud), mere practice of yoga (rituals, postures, external disciplines) without the inner cutting does not bring moksha.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is a decisive verse. Mere knowing, combined with merely external practice, is insufficient. The bound man who does not act to cut his ropes remains bound regardless of how long he waits or how well he understands his situation. The implication for sādhana is profound: no amount of external religious activity substitutes for the prajñā-knife that cuts the identification with bandha.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Time alone does not free you. External practices alone do not free you. If the inner cutting hasn't happened, bondage continues.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What is the inner cutting that needs to happen? How is it different from outer religious observance?

---

### G290
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
एवं पगडिं च पदेसं च ठिदिं अणुभागं च।
जाणिय कम्मबंधं सुद्धो जीवो हवदि मुत्तो।।290।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Similarly — having known prakṛti, pradeśa, sthiti, and anubhāga of karma-bandha — the soul is freed only when it becomes śuddha (pure).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The four aspects of karma-bandha are: prakṛti (nature/type of karma), pradeśa (quantum of karma particles), sthiti (duration of bondage), anubhāga (intensity of fruition). One can know all four precisely. But knowing them does not free. Freedom comes only when the soul becomes śuddha — pure — which means disidentified from all these.

**COMMENTARY:**
Amṛtacandra makes clear: scholarly knowledge of karma theory is not liberation. Whether one has read all the karma-grantha texts, whether one can explain the 148 karma-prakṛtis, whether one understands the mechanism of sthiti-bandha — none of this is moksha. Moksha is śuddhatā — the state in which the soul has shed its identification with all these processes and stands in its own nature.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Knowing the name and mechanism of every karma is not freedom. Becoming pure — disidentified, self-abiding — that is freedom.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is there a spiritual form of ego that collects philosophical knowledge the same way the materialist collects possessions? Can doctrine itself become bondage?

---

### G291
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जह बद्धो मणुस्सो बंधे चिंतेइ ण हवइ मुत्तो।
तह जीवो चिंतंतो बंधं णेव हवइ मुत्तो।।291।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as a physically-bound man, by thinking about his bondage, does not attain liberation — so the soul, by thinking about bandha, does not attain moksha.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Direct statement: contemplating bondage ≠ liberation from bondage. The analogy is exact. A man tied with ropes — even if he spends all his time thinking about those ropes — is still tied. Similarly, a jīva that constantly contemplates "I am bound, karma has done this to me, this is how bondage works" — remains bound.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse cuts through a subtle trap on the spiritual path: the mistake of substituting philosophical reflection for actual transformation. One can spend a lifetime discussing karma theory, attending pravachans about bandha, meditating on the nature of bondage — and still remain bound. The soul needs to turn from contemplating bandha toward knowing and abiding in its own śuddha nature.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Thinking about your chains does not break your chains. The work of freedom is different from the analysis of captivity.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
How much of your spiritual life is thinking about bondage — versus actually turning toward the self?

---

### G292
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जह बद्धो मणुस्सो बंधे छिंदित्ता हवइ मुत्तो।
तह जीवो छिंदित्ता बंधं सव्वेण मुच्चइ।।292।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as the physically-bound man, by cutting the bondage, attains freedom — so the soul, by cutting bandha, fully attains moksha.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The positive statement follows the negative: cutting is the method. Not knowing, not thinking, not discussing — cutting. The soul that cuts (chinditvā) bandha attains sarvena moksha — complete, total liberation.

**COMMENTARY:**
Sarvena — completely, totally — is significant. Moksha in Jain doctrine is not partial. It is kevala — total. The soul that performs the inner prajñā-cutting and completely separates itself from all bandha-identification attains the full state. Nothing of bandha remains. The word chinditvā (having cut) implies decisiveness — not gradual erosion but a definitive severing.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Cut the bondage. That is the action of liberation. Not analysis — action. The action of prajñā-chedana — of wisdom decisively separating self from not-self.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What does "cutting" mean in practice? What is being cut? What does the knife look like?

## Part 2: Virāga + Prajñā-Chedana = Moksha (G293–G295)

### G293
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
बंधाणं आदाणं च एवं जाणिय सो विरज्जदि।
जो बंधेसु विरत्तो सो कम्मविमोक्खणं कुणदि।।293।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Having known the svabhāva of bandhas and also of ātmā — one who becomes virakt (dispassionate) toward the bandhas — accomplishes karma-liberation (karma-vimokṣaṇa).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The formula: jñāna of bandha-svabhāva and ātmā-svabhāva + virāga toward bandha = karma-vimokṣaṇa (liberation from karma). Knowing alone was insufficient (G288-G291). But knowing combined with virāga — with genuine dispassion, a turning-away — produces liberation.

**COMMENTARY:**
Virāga is the critical addition here. Not mere intellectual knowing but knowing that produces dispassion — a natural cooling of attachment toward the things known to be other. When the soul truly knows that karma-pariṇāmas are para (not-mine, other), virāga arises naturally. Forced detachment through willpower is not virāga; it is suppression. True virāga arises from prajñā — from the wisdom that sees clearly what belongs to whom.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When you truly know that bondage is not you — dispassion toward it arises naturally. That natural dispassion, combined with true knowing, is itself liberation.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is your detachment practiced or understood? Do you force yourself away from things — or do you see clearly that they were never yours?

### G294 ★ FEATURED VERSE
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जीवो बंधो य तहा छिज्जंति सलक्खणेहिं णियएहिं।
पण्णाछेदणएण दु छिण्णा णाणत्तमावण्णा।।294।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवो बन्धश्च तथा छिद्यन्ते स्वलक्षणैः नियतैः।
प्रज्ञाछेदनकेन तु छिन्नौ नानात्वमापन्नौ।।294।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Jīva and bandha are cut by their own niyata (fixed, essential) svalakṣaṇas (own-characteristics). By the prajñā-chedana (wisdom-knife) the two are cut and attain nānātva (mutual distinctness/separateness).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The cutting happens along the natural fault line — the line where jīva's characteristics end and bandha's characteristics begin. These svalakṣaṇas (own-natures) are niyata — fixed, not arbitrary. Jīva's nature is caitanya (consciousness); bandha's nature is jaḍatā (inertness). The prajñā-chedana — the knife of discriminative wisdom — cuts precisely at this boundary. After cutting: the two stand as nānā — distinct, separate, fully themselves.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is the axial verse of Adhikar 8. The image is surgical: prajñā is a precise instrument that cuts where the natural boundary lies. The two substances — jīva and karma — have always been ontologically distinct (a key Jain commitment). But in saṃsāra they function as if merged, because the jīva misidentifies itself with karma-pariṇāmas. Prajñā-chedana does not create separation — it reveals the separation that was always there. After the cut, nānātva (manyness, distinction) is āpanna (attained) — the natural state is restored.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul and its bondage have different natures. Wisdom-as-knife cuts precisely where those different natures meet. After the cut — they are simply what they always were: two separate things.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
If jīva and bandha were always distinct, what work does the wisdom-knife actually do? What does it mean to "cut" something that was never truly joined?

---

### G295
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जीवो बंधो य तहा छिज्जंति सलक्खणेहिं णियएहिं।
बंधो छेदेदव्वो सुद्धो अप्पा य घेत्तव्वो।।295।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Jīva and bandha are cut by their own niyata svalakṣaṇas. Bandha is to be abandoned (chhedanīya) and śuddha ātmā is to be grasped (grāhya).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
After establishing the act of cutting (G294), G295 gives the direction: after separation — bandha is to be left behind (it was never yours), and śuddha ātmā is to be grasped, held, inhabited. The soul doesn't just separate from karma — it turns toward itself. The positive pole of liberation is ātma-graha — self-apprehension.

**COMMENTARY:**
The two movements of liberation: (1) release of what is para — bandha is chhedanīya, to be cut away; (2) apprehension of what is sva — śuddha ātmā is grāhya, to be grasped. Both movements are necessary. Liberation is not merely the absence of bondage; it is the presence of ātma-sākṣātkāra — self-realization. The śuddha ātmā that is grasped is precisely the ātmā that was always there — purified not of its nature but of the superimposed karma-identification.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Two steps: let go of what is not you (bandha). Hold on to what is you (the pure self). This is moksha in practice.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is there something you are holding onto that you know is not you? And is there something — the pure witness within — that you keep overlooking?

## Part 3: How Ātmā Is Grasped by Prajñā — I Am Cetā-Draṣṭā-Jñātā (G296–G300)

### G296
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
कह सो घिप्पिदे अप्पा पण्णाए सो तु घिप्पदे अप्पा।
जह पण्णाइ विभत्तो तह पण्णाएव घेत्तव्वो।।296।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
How is ātmā grasped? By prajñā is ātmā grasped. Just as by prajñā (ātmā was) separated (from bandha), so by prajñā alone is it to be grasped.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The question arises naturally from G295: how does one grasp the śuddha ātmā? The answer: the same prajñā-knife that separated ātmā from bandha is the instrument for grasping ātmā. Prajñā has two functions — separation (vibhaktatā) and apprehension (grahana). The same wisdom that says "this is not me" can say "this — this pure consciousness — is me."

**COMMENTARY:**
There is no other instrument. Neither ritual, nor yoga, nor external action can grasp the self. The self is of the nature of jñāna (knowledge), and it is known by jñāna — by prajñā — which is its own instrument. This is the doctrine of svayam-vedyatā: the self is self-knowing. Prajñā does not grasp the self as an object; rather, prajñā is the self grasping itself.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The same discrimination that says "that karma is not me" can say "this awareness is me." Wisdom is both the separator and the recognizer.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Has your practice included the positive turning — not just releasing what is not you, but recognizing and resting in what you are?

---

### G297
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो चेदया होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।297।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
By prajñā — that cetā (consciousness-soul) is certainly I by niścaya (definitive knowledge). The remaining bhāvas (states) are all para (other) — thus one knows by prajñā.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The triple identification by prajñā begins here. First: cetā — the one who is conscious, who is the agent of consciousness (not of karma). By prajñā, with niścaya (certainty, not vyavahāra-naya equivocation), one knows: "I am cetā — the pure conscious one." Everything else — all the pariṇāmas, all the karma-bhāvas — is para.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
By wisdom I know with certainty: I am the conscious one. All other states are not-me.

---

### G298
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो दट्ठा होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।298।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
By prajñā — that draṣṭā (seer) is certainly I by niścaya. The remaining bhāvas are all para — thus one knows by prajñā.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Second identification: draṣṭā — the one who sees, the witness. Not the one who acts in karma-fields but the one who sees. The soul's nature as draṣṭā is not a function it performs but what it is. By prajñā one recognizes: "I am the seer. What I see — all that — is para."

**SIMPLY PUT:**
By wisdom I know: I am the seer. All that is seen — not me.

---

### G299
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो णादा होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।299।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
By prajñā — that jñātā (knower) is certainly I by niścaya. The remaining bhāvas are all para — thus one knows by prajñā.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Third identification: jñātā — the knower. The soul's fundamental nature is jñātṛtva — knower-ness. It knows without being the content of knowing. By prajñā one recognizes: "I am the knower. Everything known — all the karma-results, all the pariṇāmas, all the world — is para."

**COMMENTARY:**
The three — cetā (conscious-agent), draṣṭā (seer), jñātā (knower) — describe the same ātmā from three angles, each emphasizing its nature as pure subject, never object or doer of karma. This triple declaration by prajñā is the inner turning that constitutes ātma-graha: the soul grasping itself.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
By wisdom I know: I am the knower. All that is known — not me.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Can you feel the difference between "I am the one who acts/suffers/changes" and "I am the one who is aware of acting/suffering/changing"? The second is draṣṭā-jñātā. That is the self.

---

### G300
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
को णाम भणिज्ज बुहो णादुं सव्वे पराइए भावे।
मज्झिमणं ति य वयणं जाणंतो अप्पयं सुद्धं।।300।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Who indeed would the wise one say is "mine" — knowing all bhāvas as para and knowing the pure self as ātmā?

**ANVAYARTHA:**
A rhetorical question that closes the section with clarity: the wise one (budha) who has known all bhāvas as para and has known the ātmā as śuddha — what would he call "mine"? The answer is implicit: nothing. No bhāva, no karma-pariṇāma, no external thing belongs to the one who has truly seen.

**COMMENTARY:**
"Majjhimaṇaṃ ti vayanam" — the word "mine" (mamakāra) is what binds. The entire machinery of saṃsāra runs on mamakāra — the sense of ownership, of "mine-ness." The jñānī who has completed the prajñā-chedana and recognized ātmā as cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā sees no ground for mamakāra anywhere. This is not a practice of "trying not to be attached" — it is the natural result of seeing clearly.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Once you truly see what is para (not-yours) and what is sva (yours — the pure self), the word "mine" loses all meaning for everything except ātmā itself.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What do you still call "mine"? Is there a prajñā examination that could reveal whether that ownership is real?

## Part 4: The Criminal and the Innocent — Sāparādha and Nirāparādha Soul (G301–G305)

### G301
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
अवरज्झिय मणुस्सो पवयरदि भयभीदो।
मा हं पाविज्जा इदि चोरो त्ति य।।301।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
One who has committed crimes (aparādhin) moves about fearfully — "may I not be caught as a thief."

**ANVAYARTHA:**
A man who has committed theft or crime lives in fear — fear of being identified, caught, punished. Every interaction carries the shadow of his guilt. His fear is not incidental; it is the natural consequence of his having done what he knows to be wrong.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The guilty man lives in fear. His crime, internalized, produces an ongoing experience of anxiety.

---

### G302
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
णिरवज्झिय मणुस्सो णिब्भयो पवयरदि।
ण मज्झ बंधो होहीइ इदि चिंता ण हवइ तस्स।।302।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
One who commits no crime moves fearlessly. The thought "I will be bound" never arises in him.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The innocent man moves freely. No anxiety about being caught — because there is nothing to be caught for. The thought "bandha will come to me" does not arise, because he knows his actions have been blameless.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The innocent moves without fear. Nothing to hide means nothing to fear.

---

### G303
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
एवं सावराहो जीवो बंधो मज्झ होइ त्ति।
संकिदो पवयरदि णिरावराहो णिब्भयो।।303।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Similarly — the sāparādha (guilty) soul thinks fearfully "I will bind karma." The nirāparādha (innocent) soul: "I am fearless, I will not bind."

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The analogy applies directly. The soul with aparādha — with the guilt of kartā-bhāva, of identifying itself as the doer of karma — lives in fear of its own pariṇāmas. "Will I fall? Will passion bind me? What karma am I generating?" The nirāparādha soul — the one that has recognized its own śuddha nature as cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā — knows it is not the doer of karma. Fear of bondage cannot arise for the one who knows bondage is not theirs to accumulate.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is a counterintuitive teaching. In ordinary religious life, the fear of sin and karma is considered a virtue — it motivates restraint. But at the level of niścaya-naya, the soul that truly knows its own nature knows that it is not the agent of karma-creation. Fear of bondage is itself a symptom of still-existing kartā-bhāva — of still believing "I am the one who binds karma." The jñānī, established in ātma-svabhāva, is nirāparādha and thus nirabhaya — fearless.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul that still believes it is the doer of karma lives in fear of its own karma. The soul that knows it is not the doer — knows there is nothing to fear.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is spiritual fear — fear of sin, fear of hell, fear of bad karma — a sign of awakening or a sign of still-existing kartā-bhāva?

---

### G304
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
संसिद्धिरादा सिद्धो साहिदो आराहिदो त्ति एए।
अपगयरादो जीवो सो हवइ अवराहो।।304।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Saṃsiddhi, rādha, siddha, sādhita, ārādhita — these are synonyms. The soul that is apagata-rādha (departed from ārādhana) is aparādha.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Amṛtacandra provides an etymology. The words saṃsiddhi, rādha, siddha, sādhita, ārādhita all point to the state of being established in ātma-ārādhana — self-worship, self-abidance. Aparādha literally means: having fallen away from ārādhana. The "crime" (aparādha) of the spiritual sense is not a moral transgression but the metaphysical error of departing from ātma-ārādhana — of forgetting the self and living in kartā-bhāva.

**COMMENTARY:**
This etymological verse transforms our understanding of guilt and innocence in the spiritual domain. Aparādha is not a list of sins. It is the fundamental orientation away from ātmā — the basic error of taking oneself to be a karma-doer. Every moment of kartā-bhāva is aparādha. Every moment of jñātā-draṣṭā abidance is ārādhana.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
"Sin" in the deepest sense is departing from self-abidance. "Innocence" is remaining in ātma-ārādhana — knowing and being the pure self.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What is your ārādhana? Is it an activity you perform — or a recognition you inhabit?

---

### G305
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
णिरावराहो णिब्भयो सो जाणइ अप्पयं।
आराहणाए ठिदो सदा णिच्चं।।305।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The nirāparādha soul is fearless, knowing "I am the self," always abides in ārādhana, eternally.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The positive characterization: nirāparādha = fearless (nirabhaya) + ātma-jñāna ("I am the self") + constant abidance in ārādhana. These three are simultaneous and mutually reinforcing. To know "I am the self" is to be nirāparādha; to be nirāparādha is to be fearless; to be fearless is to abide naturally in ārādhana — in the self.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul established in self-knowing is free from fear and lives permanently in the worship of its own nature.

## Part 5: Viṣa-Kumbha and Amṛta-Kumbha — The Poison-Pot and the Nectar-Pot (G306–G307)

### G306
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
पडिकमणं पडिसरणं परिहारो धारणा णियत्ती य।
णिंदा गरहा सोही अट्ठविहो होिद विसकुंभो।।306।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Pratikramaṇa (returning from transgression), pratisaraṇa (regressing/reflecting), parihāra (avoidance), dhāraṇā (sustaining vows), nivṛtti (withdrawal), nindā (self-censure), garhā (confession before teacher), śuddhi (purification) — these eight types constitute the viṣa-kumbha (poison-pot).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
Eight classic practices of Jain sādhana — all central to standard religious life — are here named the viṣa-kumbha. The list is deliberately provocative: pratikramaṇa is one of the six āvaśyakas (essential daily duties), garhā is confession, dhāraṇā is the cultivation of vows. Why are these a poison-pot? Because when performed with kartā-bhāva — with the belief "I am the one doing this pratikramaṇa, I am the one confessing, I am the one who was guilty and is now purifying" — they reinforce the very identification they are meant to dissolve.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is among the most radical teachings in Samaysaar. The practices that virtually all Jains consider the core of sādhana are called viṣa-kumbha — poison — when performed with the wrong understanding. The poison is not in the practices themselves but in the kartā-bhāva that underlies them. When one does pratikramaṇa believing "I (as doer) transgressed and I (as doer) am returning" — the doing is another act of identification with the empirical doer-self. The ritual of purification becomes another source of impurity if the one purifying is still identified with what is being purified.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When you perform spiritual practices believing "I am the doer of this practice, I was the sinner, I am now the purifier" — that belief itself is the poison. The practices become a poison-pot when performed from within kartā-bhāva.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Can you do pratikramaṇa from the position of the jñātā — the one who knows — rather than the kartā — the one who did? What would that look like?

### G307
**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
अप्पडिकमणमप्पडिसरणं अप्पिरहारो अधारणा चेव।
अणियत्ती य अणिंदागरहासोही अमयकुंभो।।307।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Apratikramaṇa (non-returning), apratisaraṇa, aparihāra, adhāraṇā, anivṛtti, anindā (non-self-censure), agarhā, aśuddhi — these eight constitute the amṛta-kumbha (nectar-pot).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
The exact negation of G306's eight: the "a-" prefix (non-, un-) is added to each. Apratikramaṇa does not mean one does not return from transgression — it means one abides in the state where there is no kartā to have transgressed. Anindā does not mean self-indulgence — it means there is no kartā-self to censure. The negation is not of the right action but of the kartā-bhāva underlying the action.

**COMMENTARY:**
Amṛtacandra is careful: the amṛta-kumbha does not license transgression or abandonment of conduct. The soul abiding in ātma-svabhāva — in the state of cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā — has naturally transcended both transgression and its ritualistic rectification. This soul does not need to do pratikramaṇa because it is not identified with the empirical karma-doer who transgressed. Its "apratikramaṇa" is the nectar of abidance in the self. The teaching is for the niścaya-naya level of understanding — for the paramārtha seeker — not an invitation for the sāmānya jīva to abandon the āvaśyakas.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When you abide in the pure self — as the knower, not the doer — there is no transgression to return from, no sin to confess, no self to censure. That abidance is the nectar-pot. That is amṛta.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Poison and nectar from the same list of practices. The difference is entirely in whether kartā-bhāva is operating. Can you find the jñātā in yourself that would make this distinction experiential, not just theoretical?

## Closing Colophon

**Sanskrit (from Ātmakhyāti):**
इति मोक्षो निष्क्रान्त:।
इति श्रीमदमृतचन्द्रसूरिविरचितायां समयसारव्याख्यायामात्मख्यातौ
मोक्षप्ररूपकः अष्टमोऽङ्कः।।

**Translation:**
Thus Moksha is departed.
In the Ātmakhyāti — the authoritative commentary on Samaysaar composed by the revered Ācārya Amṛtacandra Sūri — the eighth adhikar, the exposition of Moksha, is thus concluded.

## Doctrinal Summary: The Five Keys of Adhikar 8

1. **Knowing Is Not Enough** — Knowing the nature of karma-bondage does not produce liberation. Even extended yogic practice without the inner cutting does not free. (G288-G292)

2. **Prajñā-Chedana** — Jīva and bandha have always been ontologically distinct. The prajñā-knife (wisdom of discrimination) cuts precisely at the boundary of their respective svalakṣaṇas and reveals the nānātva (distinctness) that was always there. (G294)

3. **Triple Self-Recognition** — By prajñā, with niścaya, the soul recognizes: I am cetā (the conscious one), draṣṭā (the seer), jñātā (the knower). All remaining bhāvas are para. (G297-G299)

4. **Nirāparādha = Fearlessness** — The soul still believing it is karma-doer (kartā-bhāva) lives in fear. The soul knowing it is not the doer — knowing ātmā as pure witness — is nirāparādha (innocent of the metaphysical crime of self-misidentification) and therefore nirabhaya. (G301-G305)

5. **Viṣa-Kumbha and Amṛta-Kumbha** — The same religious practices become poison when performed with kartā-bhāva and nectar when they arise from, or serve, abidance in ātma-svabhāva as jñātā-draṣṭā. (G306-G307)
