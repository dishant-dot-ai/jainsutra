# Samaysaar — Adhikar 2: Kartā-Karma (The Doer and the Deed)
## Translation: Gathas 69–144

**Scripture:** Samaysaar (The Essence of Pure Consciousness)
**Author:** Shrimad Kundakunda Acharya
**Commentary (Tika):** Amrutchandra Acharya (Atmakhyati)
**Chapter:** 2 — Kartā-Karma (कर्ता-कर्म अधिकार / The Doer and the Deed)
**Theme:** Who is actually the doer of karma? Adhikar 2 demolishes the false belief that the soul is the maker of pudgala-karma, showing how ajñāna creates the illusion of doership — and how jñāna dissolves it.

---

## PART 1: THE ROOT OF BONDAGE — AJÑĀNA (G69–74)

### GATHA 69

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जाव ण वेदि विसेसंतरं तु आदासवाण दोण्हं पि।
अण्णाणी ताव दु सो कोहादिसु वट्टदे जीवो।।६९।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

यावन्न वेत्ति विशेषान्तरं तु आत्मासवयोर्द्वयोरपि।
अज्ञानी तावत्तु स क्रोधादिषु वर्तते जीवः।।६९।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

As long as this soul does not know the specific distinction between itself and the āsravas — as long as that, being in ajñāna, the soul remains engaged in the krodhādi (anger and other passions).

**4. ANVAYARTHA**

**जाव** = as long as; **ण वेदि** = does not know; **विसेसंतरं** = the specific distinction; **आदासवाण दोण्हं पि** = of both the soul and the āsravas; **अण्णाणी** = being in ajñāna (ignorance); **ताव दु** = that long; **सो** = that; **कोहादिसु** = in krodhādi (anger etc.); **वट्टदे जीवो** = the soul remains engaged.

**5. COMMENTARY**

This gatha opens Adhikar 2 with a diagnostic statement: the root cause of the soul's entanglement in passions is not some cosmic punishment or external fate — it is ajñāna (self-ignorance), specifically the failure to distinguish between what the soul is and what āsravas (karmic inflows, passions) are. When you do not know the difference between yourself and anger, you live inside anger. You become it. You move through the world as a creature of passion because you have no alternative reference point.

The word "vetti" — "knows" — is crucial. This is not theoretical knowledge. It is the direct, intimate recognition that the soul (ātmā) is of an utterly different nature from krodhādi (anger, pride, deceit, greed). The āsravas are external, material, jada (insentient). The soul is consciousness. Until this distinction is seen clearly, the soul does not merely experience anger — it identifies with anger, claims it, acts from it.

The "specific distinction" (viseshantara) Kundakunda refers to is the difference between consciousness and matter at their deepest level — not a conceptual belief but a lived recognition. Until that recognition dawns, the āsrava rules.

**6. SIMPLY PUT**

As long as you don't truly know the difference between yourself and your passions — you live inside your passions. Ignorance of this distinction IS the bondage.

**7. CONTEMPLATE**

When you are angry, do you know yourself as distinct from anger — or do you become anger? That moment of self-recognition is the beginning of liberation.

### GATHA 70

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कोहादिसु वट्टंतस्स तस्स कम्मस्स संचओ होदि।
जीवस्सेवं बंधो भणिदो खलु सव्वदरिसीहिं।।७०।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

क्रोधादिषु वर्तमानस्य तस्य कर्मणः संचयो भवति।
जीवस्यैवं बन्धो भणितः खलु सर्वदर्शिभिः।।७०।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For that soul active in krodhādi, accumulation of karma takes place. This is exactly what the all-seeing Jinas have declared as the bondage of the soul.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The mechanism of bondage is stated plainly: activity in krodhādi → accumulation of karma → bondage. The all-seers (sarvadarshin — the omniscient Jinas) have not merely theorized this. They have directly perceived the causal chain through kevalajñāna (omniscient knowledge) and reported it. This is not philosophy — it is empirical observation by those with perfect sight.

The word "samchaya" (accumulation) matters. Karma does not arrive in a single moment — it builds up. Every moment of passion-driven activity adds more karmic material. The āsravas (passions) are not just painful; they are structurally productive — they produce more karma with every moment they are active. This is why urgency is appropriate.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Being active in passions accumulates karma — and that accumulation is what the omniscient ones call bondage.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

How many moments of your day are driven by krodhādi without your awareness? And what is being built — layer by layer — in those moments?

---

### GATHA 71

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जइया इमेण जीवेण अप्पणो आसवाण य तहेव।
णादं होदि विसेसंतरं तु तइया ण बंधो से।।७१।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

यदा अनेन जीवेन आत्मनः आसवानां च तथैव।
ज्ञातं भवति विशेषान्तरं तु तदा न बन्धः तस्य।।७१।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

When the specific distinction between the soul itself and the āsravas becomes known to this soul — at that very time, there is no bondage for it.

**4. COMMENTARY**

G71 is the mirror image of G69. G69 stated: non-knowing of the distinction = engagement in passions. G71 states: knowing of the distinction = no bondage. The structure is logically elegant and philosophically radical: the moment of knowledge is simultaneously the moment of freedom from bondage.

Not after years of practice. Not after death and rebirth in a better realm. At that very time (tadyā). The recognition itself is the liberation — at least in principle. This is not to say the karmas already bound fall away instantly (though they do dissolve through nirjarā over time). But the mechanism of new bondage ceases at the moment of right knowledge.

This connects to the Jain concept of samyak-darśana (right perception): the first genuine flash of self-recognition immediately stops wrong-belief at its root. New karma arising from mithyātva ceases with that very recognition.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The moment you truly know the distinction between yourself and your passions — at that very moment, bondage stops.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Knowledge here is not intellectual. What would it mean to truly *know* — in lived experience — that you are not your anger?

### GATHA 72

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णादूण आसवाणं असुचित्तं च विवरीयभावं च।
दुक्खस्स कारणं ति य तदो णियत्तिं कुणिदं जीवो।।७२।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

ज्ञात्वा आसवानाम् अशुचित्वं च विपरीतभावं च।
दुःखस्य कारणमिति च ततो निवृत्तिं करोति जीवः।।७२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Knowing the āsravas' impurity, their contrary nature, and that they are causes of suffering — the soul then withdraws from them.

**4. COMMENTARY**

Three realizations together trigger withdrawal from āsravas:

1. **Impurity** (aśucitva): The āsravas are inherently impure — they are not sacred, not noble, not what they appear to be in the heat of passion. Anger feels righteous in the moment; examined clearly, it is dirt on the lens of consciousness.

2. **Contrary nature** (viparīta-bhāva): The āsravas are of an opposite nature to the soul. The soul is conscious; āsravas are insentient. The soul is free; āsravas are binding. The soul is blissful; āsravas produce suffering. They are structurally opposed to what the soul actually is.

3. **Cause of suffering** (duḥkhasya kāraṇam): They hurt. Not just philosophically — they are the direct cause of suffering in the soul's experience across lifetimes.

When all three are known — not believed, but *known* — the soul naturally withdraws. Withdrawal is not forced renunciation. It is the natural response of intelligence seeing clearly.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

When the soul knows that its passions are impure, contrary to its nature, and the cause of its suffering — it naturally turns away from them.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Which of these three recognitions would most change your relationship to your dominant passion? The fact that it's impure, or that it's opposite to what you are, or that it causes suffering?

### GATHA 73

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अहमेक्को खलु सुद्धो णिम्ममओ णाणदंसणसमग्गो।
तम्हि ठिदो तच्चितो सव्वे एदे खयं णेमि।।७३।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

अहमेकः खलु शुद्धः निर्ममो ज्ञानदर्शनसमग्रः।
तस्मिन् स्थितः तच्चित्तः सर्वानेतान् क्षयं नेष्यामि।।७३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"I alone am truly pure, without possessiveness, complete in knowledge and perception. Established in that, absorbed in that — I will bring all of these (āsravas and karmas) to their extinction."

**4. COMMENTARY**

This is one of the most celebrated verses in the Samaysaar — a declaration of the soul from within its own recognition. It is not a hope or a prayer. It is a statement of what the soul has already recognized itself to be: pure, non-possessive, whole in knowledge and perception.

"I alone" (aham ekko) — not "I plus body," not "I plus family," not "I plus history." The soul, stripped of all superimposition, is singular, complete, self-sufficient.

"Without possessiveness" (nimmamao) — no claim on anything external. No "mine." The entire web of samsāra — birth, death, rebirth — is sustained by mamakāra (the sense of ownership). When that collapses, the root of samsāra is cut.

"Established in that" (tamhi thido) — resting in the recognition of one's own pure nature. Not trying to become something. Already there. Just remaining.

"I will bring all these to extinction" — the āsravas and karmas will dissolve, not because of violent effort, but because consciousness established in its own nature naturally burns away what obscures it, the way sunlight naturally dissolves fog.

The Atmakhyati commentary on this verse is famous: Amrutchandra says this verse is the entire Samaysaar condensed into one declaration.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The awakened soul speaks: "I am alone, pure, without possessiveness, whole in knowing. Rooted in this truth, I will bring all that obscures me to extinction."

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Can you hold this statement — "I alone am truly pure" — without it feeling like arrogance? What makes it a recognition rather than a claim?

### GATHA 74

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीविणिबद्धा एदे अधुव अणिचा तहा असरणा य।
दुक्खा दुक्खफल ति य णादूण णिवत्तदे तेहिं।।७४।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

जीवनिबद्धा एते अध्रुवाः अनित्याः तथा अशरणाः च।
दुःखाः दुःखफलाः इति ज्ञात्वा निवर्तते तेभ्यः।।७४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

These (āsravas) — bound to life, impermanent, shelterless, painful, and fruit of suffering — knowing this, one withdraws from them.

**4. COMMENTARY**

Five characteristics of the āsravas that warrant withdrawal:

1. **Bound to life** (jīvini-baddhā): They bind — that is their nature. They tie the soul to continued samsāra.
2. **Impermanent** (adhruva, anicā): They do not last. No pleasure arising from āsravas endures. The passion-driven highs are structurally temporary.
3. **Shelterless** (asaraṇā): They offer no real refuge. You cannot take shelter in anger — it burns you. You cannot find safety in greed — it keeps you restless.
4. **Painful** (dukhā): Their essential nature is suffering, even when they feel temporarily pleasant.
5. **Fruit of suffering** (duḥkha-phala): They produce suffering as their result — in this life and the next.

This fivefold recognition (similar to the contemplations in Tattvārtha-sūtra on five types of pramāda) is not a list to be memorized but a living inquiry that, when genuinely seen, produces the natural turning-away described in G72.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The āsravas bind, fade, offer no shelter, hurt, and produce suffering. When you truly see this, turning away from them happens naturally.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Take one passion that recurs in your life. Can you see all five of these qualities in it — honestly, without justification?

## PART 2: THE JÑĀNĪ AS AKARTĀ (G75–79)

### GATHA 75

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कम्मस्स य परिणामं णोकम्मस्स य तहेव परिणामं।
ण करेइ एयमादा जो जाणिदे सो हवदि णाणी।।७५।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

कर्मणः परिणामं नोकर्मणश्च तथैव परिणामम्।
न करोति एकमात्मा यः जानाति स भवति ज्ञानी।।७५।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The soul that does not bring about the transformation of karma and of nokamma — but merely knows it — that soul is the jñānī.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The definition of jñānī offered here is precise: the jñānī is not the one who has vast theoretical knowledge, or who has studied many texts. The jñānī is the one who knows (jānādi) karma's transformation without doing it (na karedi). The knowing and the non-doing coexist in the same consciousness.

Nokamma (nokarma) refers to the quasi-karma that is produced — including the body and its attendant conditions. The jñānī, even while living in a body and experiencing the fruits of past karma, does not produce (bring about) these transformations. They watch. They know. But they are not the agent.

This distinction between knowing and doing is central to Jain epistemology. Upayoga (the soul's characteristic activity as consciousness) is inherently knowing. When the soul operates as pure upayoga — pure knowing — it is not doing. It is when upayoga is contaminated by mithyātva and rāga-dvesha that it becomes doership.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The jñānī knows karma's transformations — but does not make them happen. Knowing, without doing: that is the definition of a jñānī.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What is the difference between witnessing what arises in your mind and producing what arises? Can you locate that difference in your own experience?

### GATHA 76

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण वि परिणमदि ण गिण्हदि उप्पज्जदि ण परदव्वपज्जाए।
णाणी जाणंतो वि हु पोग्गलकम्मं अणेयविहं।।७६।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

न विपरिणमते न गृह्णाति नोत्पद्यते परद्रव्यपर्यायेषु।
ज्ञानी जानन्नपि हि पुद्गलकर्म अनेकविधम्।।७६।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The jñānī, even while knowing many kinds of pudgala-karma, does not transform in it, does not take it, and does not arise in the modes of other substances.

**4. COMMENTARY**

Three things the jñānī does NOT do, even while knowing:
1. Does not transform INTO karma (na pariṇamate): The soul does not undergo transformation into the nature of pudgala-karma.
2. Does not take/claim it (na grihṇādi): The jñānī does not claim ownership of what the pudgala undergoes.
3. Does not arise in other-substance modes (na upajjadi para-dravya-pajjāe): The soul does not appear as or through the modes of pudgala — it stays in its own substance.

All three together describe a soul that is fully present as awareness, without being invaded, captured, or redefined by what it is aware of. The knowing is complete. The separation is complete. These are not in tension. In fact, complete knowing requires this separation — only a soul that is not merged with what it knows can actually know it clearly.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The jñānī knows countless forms of karma — but doesn't become karma, doesn't claim karma, and doesn't lose itself in karma's modes.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

When you observe pain, do you observe it — or do you become it? There is a mode of knowing that does not collapse into what it knows. That mode is what is being pointed at here.

### GATHA 77

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

[Parallel gatha to G76 — regarding svaka-pariṇāma: own transformations]
The jñānī, even while knowing many kinds of own transformations (svaparyāya), does not transform into the modes of other substances.

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The jñānī knows even the soul's own many transformations — but does not arise in the modes of other-substance (pudgala) through them.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Even knowing its own inner transformations, the jñānī does not lose itself in pudgala's domain.

### GATHA 78

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

[Parallel gatha — regarding pudgala-karma-fruit: the infinite varieties of pudgala-karma results]
The jñānī, knowing infinite varieties of pudgala-karma fruit, does not transform in them.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Knowing all of karma's fruits — the jñānī remains untransformed by them.

### GATHA 79

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण वि परिणमदि ण गिण्हदि उप्पज्जदि ण परदव्वपज्जाए।
पोग्गलदव्वं पि तहा परिणमदि सएहिं भावेहिं।।७९।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

न विपरिणमते न गृह्णाति नोत्पद्यते परद्रव्यपर्यायेषु।
पुद्गलद्रव्यमपि तथा परिणमते स्वकैः भावैः।।७९।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Similarly, pudgala-dravya also transforms by its own bhavas — not in the modes of the soul (para-dravya).

**4. COMMENTARY**

The same rule that applies to the jñānī-soul applies to pudgala. Neither substance invades the other's modes. The soul does not transform into pudgala's modes; pudgala does not transform into the soul's modes. Each transforms according to its own nature (svabhāva).

This principle — "each dravya transforms only through its own bhavas" — is one of the most foundational principles in Jain ontology. It has a name: svabhāva-pariṇāma (own-nature transformation). And it has a revolutionary implication: no substance can truly do anything to another substance. They can serve as instrumental causes (nimittas) — conditions — but the actual transformation happens from within.

This demolishes the naive view that karma "does things" to the soul. Karma is nimitta (instrumental cause), not the actual agent of the soul's transformation.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Pudgala transforms by its own nature — not by invading the soul. And the soul transforms by its own nature — not by being pushed by pudgala.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What does it mean that even karma cannot force you to change? It can be a condition, but you transform yourself. What responsibility does that place on you?

## PART 3: MUTUAL INSTRUMENTAL CAUSATION (G80–84)

### GATHA 80

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवपरिणामहेदुं कम्मत्तं पोग्गला परिणमंति।
पोग्गलकम्मिणिमित्तं तहेव जीवो वि परिणमदि।।८०।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

जीवपरिणामहेतुं कर्मत्वं पुद्गला परिणमन्ति।
पुद्गलकर्मनिमित्तं तथैव जीवोऽपि परिणमति।।८०।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Pudgalas transform into karma-form due to the soul's transformation as cause; the soul also transforms due to pudgala-karma as instrumental cause.

**4. COMMENTARY**

G80 states the doctrine of nimitta-naimittika (instrumental-instrumentalized) causation that underlies the entire Adhikar. Neither the soul nor pudgala acts on the other directly. Instead:

- The soul's transformation (its rāga, dvesha, moha) serves as nimitta (occasion, instrumental condition) for pudgalas to transform into karma-form.
- Pudgala-karma's rise (uday) serves as nimitta for the soul's transformation (its experienced rāga-dvesha-moha).

Each is nimitta for the other's transformation, while each transforms only through its own svabhāva. The analogy used in the Atmakhyati: when a crystal (sphāṭika) is placed near a red flower, the crystal appears red — but the redness does not actually enter the crystal. The crystal transforms to appear red through its own capacity for transparency, using the flower as nimitta. Remove the flower, the crystal is again pure. Similarly, the soul "appears" to take on the quality of karma — but the karma does not actually enter and transform the soul's essence.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul's transformation occasions pudgala becoming karma; karma's rise occasions the soul's transformation. Each is a condition for the other — but neither actually does anything to the other.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What does it mean that your circumstances are conditions — not causes — of who you become? You transform yourself; circumstances merely provide the occasion.

### GATHA 81

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ण वि कुव्वदि कम्मगुणे जीवो कम्मं तहेव जीवगुणे।
अण्णोण्णणिमित्तेण दु परिणामं जाण दोण्हं पि।।८१।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

न करोति कर्मगुणान् जीवः कर्म तथैव जीवगुणान्।
अन्योन्यनिमित्तेन तु परिणामं जानीहि द्वयोरपि।।८१।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The soul does not produce karma's qualities; karma does not produce the soul's qualities. Know the transformation of both through mutual instrumental causation only.

**4. COMMENTARY**

This verse states the philosophical principle of G80 in its sharpest form. The production of qualities is strictly self-contained within each substance. The soul produces soul-qualities (consciousness, perception, joy or suffering). Karma produces karma-qualities (the specific karmic conditions and their effects). Neither creates the other's qualities.

What exists between them is nimitta-naimittika — occasion and occasioned. The soul's state occasions karma; karma's state occasions the soul's state. But at the level of actual production — no substance makes another's qualities.

This is the Jain middle path between fatalism (karma makes you who you are) and naive free will (you act independently of conditions). You are never independent of conditions. But conditions never determine you absolutely. You transform yourself; conditions are occasion.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul doesn't make karma's qualities; karma doesn't make the soul's qualities. They condition each other — but each produces only its own.

---

### GATHA 82

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एदेण कारणेण दु कत्ता आदा सएण भावेण।
पोग्गलकम्मकदाणं ण दु कत्ता सव्वभावाणं।।८२।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

एतेन कारणेन तु कर्ता आत्मा स्वकेन भावेन।
पुद्गलकर्मकृतानां न तु कर्ता सर्वभावानाम्।।८२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

For this reason, the soul is kartā (doer) by its own bhāva — but is not the kartā of all bhavas produced by pudgala-karma.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The soul is not denied kartā-status altogether. It IS kartā — of its own bhāva. When it generates rāga, it is the kartā of rāga. When it generates dvesha, it is kartā of dvesha. Its own inner states — these are within its kartā-ship.

What the soul is NOT kartā of: the bhavas produced by pudgala-karma — jñānāvaraṇa (knowledge-obscuring karma), darśanāvaraṇa (perception-obscuring), etc. These are pudgala's own transformations, with the soul as nimitta but not as kārana (direct cause).

This is a precise legal and metaphysical distinction. You are responsible for your inner states. You are not responsible for what karma does of its own accord in response to your states.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul is the doer of its own inner bhavas. It is not the doer of everything karma produces.

---

### GATHA 83

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णिच्छयणयस्स एवं आदा अप्पाणमेव हि करेदि।
वेदयदि पुणो तं चेव जाण अत्ता दु अत्ताणं।।८३।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

निश्चयनयस्यैवमात्मा आत्मानमेव हि करोति।
वेदयते पुनस्तदेव जानीहि आत्मा तु आत्मानम्।।८३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

From the niśchaya (ultimate) standpoint, the soul does only itself and experiences only itself. Know this — the soul (does) the soul.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The niśchaya-naya takes the view from the ultimate level of reality. Seen from there, the soul's doing and experiencing are both completely self-contained. It does its own bhavas. It experiences its own bhavas. Nothing external is actually done or experienced — only the soul's own transformation is real from this standpoint.

This is a radical position. It means that even in bondage, what the soul is truly experiencing is not karma — but itself in a certain state. The karma is the occasion; the experience is the soul's own. And in liberation, the soul is not "freed from karma" as if escaping an external prison — it simply ceases to produce bondage-states from within itself.

The phrase "ātmā tu ātmānam" (the soul does the soul) is one of the most celebrated in Jain metaphysics: the soul is simultaneously the doer, the deed, and the done-to.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

From the ultimate standpoint: the soul does only itself, and experiences only itself. Nothing external is actually inside it.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

If you experience only your own states — and never an external world directly — what does that mean for suffering? For joy? For blame?

---

### GATHA 84

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

ववहारस्स दु आदा पोग्गलकम्मं करेदि णेयिवहं।
तं चेव पुणो वेयइ पोग्गलकम्मं अणेयिवहं।।८४।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

व्यवहारस्य तु आत्मा पुद्गलकर्म करोति अनेकविधम्।
तदेव पुनः वेदयते पुद्गलकर्म अनेकविधम्।।८४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

From the vyavahāra (conventional) standpoint, the soul does many kinds of pudgala-karma and also experiences those many kinds of pudgala-karma.

**4. COMMENTARY**

G84 gives the conventional view to balance G83's ultimate view. In everyday language and practical spiritual guidance, it is said that the soul does karma and experiences karma. This is vyavahāra — the framework of conventional description, which is valid for practical purposes.

The two views do not contradict each other. They address different levels of reality. The vyavahāra view is used in teachings about karma, its binding, its shedding — it is the language of ethical instruction. The niśchaya view is used for pure self-inquiry — it is the language of liberation.

Neither is false. A teacher must know when to use which. A student must learn not to confuse them.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Conventionally speaking, the soul does karma and experiences karma. This is the practical level of description — valid, useful, but not the final word.

---

## PART 4: THE SELF-VIKALPAS AND THE TWO FACES OF DOERSHIP (G85–108)

### GATHA 85

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जदि पोग्गलकम्मिमिणं कुव्वदि तं चेव वेदयदि आदा।
दोकिरियाविदिरित्तो पसज्जदे सो जिणावमदं।।८५।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

If the soul both does pudgala-karma and experiences it — a problem of double-action (dvikrīyā) arises, which is rejected by the Jinas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

If the soul both makes karma AND experiences it (as if it were purely its own product), the logical problem is this: karma can't be both the soul's activity AND the object of its experience — that would mean the same substance is both agent and patient at the same moment in the same transaction. The Jinas reject this "dvikrīyā" (double-action) problem by maintaining the niśchaya/vyavahāra distinction: at the ultimate level, the soul does only itself (G83). At the conventional level, we say it does karma (G84). Never at the same level do both simultaneously apply.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The soul can't simultaneously be the maker and the experiencer of karma in the same metaphysical sense — that leads to logical contradiction rejected by the Jinas.

---

### GATHA 86

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जम्हा दु अत्तभावं पोग्गलभावं च दो वि कुव्वंति।
तेण दु मिच्छादिट्ठी दोकिरियावादिणो हुंति।।८६।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Those who claim the soul does both its own bhāva AND pudgala's bhāva are mithyādṛṣṭis (holders of wrong view).

**4. COMMENTARY**

If someone holds that the soul is simultaneously the producer of both its own inner states AND the outer transformation of pudgala into karma — they are in mithyātva (wrong view). This is because it violates the principle of substance-specificity: each substance produces only its own modes. A soul that could produce pudgala's modes would be identical to pudgala, which would destroy the entire distinction between consciousness and matter.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Believing the soul makes both its own states and karma's material transformations — that is a wrong view.

---

### GATHA 87

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

मिच्छत्तं पुण दुविहं जीवमजीवं तहेव अण्णाणं।
अविरदि जोगो मोहो कोहादीया इमे भावा।।८७।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Mithyātva is twofold — jīva-type and ajīva-type. Similarly ajñāna, avirata, yoga, moha, and the krodhādi — these are the bhavas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The categories of bondage-related bhavas are mapped across the jīva/ajīva distinction. The vivid teaching here is that what we call "mithyātva" (wrong belief) has two aspects: the soul-side (its wrong perception) and the matter-side (the karma-species of mithyātva-karma). They are not identical — the inner state and the karmic material are distinct, even though they co-arise and mutually condition each other.

This distinction becomes critical: even moha (delusion) and krodhādi (passions) have both a soul-side manifestation (upayoga perverted) and a matter-side manifestation (karma material).

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Mithyātva, ajñāna, passion — each has a soul-side face and a matter-side face. Knowing which is which is essential.

---

### GATHA 88–90

*(G88-90 refine the classification of jīva-side and ajīva-side bhavas: upayoga, ajñāna, and avirati belong to jīva; pudgala-karma, yoga-karma, and the material form of mithyātva belong to ajīva. G90 then states:)*

The upayoga is threefold — pure, unstained bhāva. Whatever bhāva the upayoga does, upayoga is the kartā of that bhāva.

**Commentary:** Upayoga is the soul's defining characteristic. When pure, it is simply consciousness. When perverted by moha, it generates mithyātva, ajñāna, and avirati. The key insight is that upayoga — the soul's mode of knowing — is what actually generates the soul-side bhavas. And of those bhavas, it is the kartā.

---

### GATHA 91

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जं कुणिदि भावमादा कत्ता सो होदि तस्स भावस्स।
कम्मत्तं परिणमदे तम्हि सयं पोग्गलं दव्वं।।९१।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whatever bhāva the soul does, it is the kartā of that bhāva; and in that bhāva, pudgala-dravya itself spontaneously transforms into karma-form.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The causal arrow: Soul does bhāva → Soul is kartā of that bhāva → Pudgala spontaneously (svayam) transforms into karma, occasioned by that bhāva. The soul's bhāva is the nimitta. Pudgala's karma-transformation is the naimittika result. But "spontaneously" (svayam) — because pudgala transforms by its own nature in response to nimitta. Not because the soul forces it.

---

### GATHA 92

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

परमप्पाणं कुव्वं अप्पाणं पि य परं किरंतो सो।
अण्णाणमओ जीवो कम्माणं कारगो होदि।।९२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Making the supreme self into "other," making the self into "other" — the ajñāna-filled soul becomes the kartā of karmas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The mechanism of ajñāna-based kartā-ship is described precisely: there are two distortions that happen simultaneously in the ajñānī.

1. Making the supreme self (paramātmā — the pure soul) into "other" — treating your deepest nature as foreign, unknown, other.
2. Making "other" (the not-self: body, passions, possessions) into "self" — identifying with what is not you.

These two reversals — mis-knowing the self as other and mis-knowing the other as self — together constitute ajñāna. And this ajñāna IS what makes the soul the kartā of karmas. Not any metaphysical fact about the soul's nature. Just this reversal.

Which means: reversing the reversal IS liberation.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

When you mistake your true self as "not me" — and mistake what is not you as "me" — you become the maker of karma. That reversal is ajñāna itself.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What are you currently identifying as "me" that is not actually you? And what about yourself are you treating as foreign or unknown?

---

### GATHA 93

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

परमप्पाणमकुव्वं अप्पाणं पि य परं अकुव्वंतो।
सो णाणमओ जीवो कम्माणमकारगो होदि।।९३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Not making the supreme self into "other," not making the self into "other" — the jñāna-filled soul is the akartā (non-doer) of karmas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

G93 is the exact reversal of G92. The jñānī does the opposite of both distortions: does not alienate the supreme self (knows it as self), does not identify the not-self as self. This dual recognition — knowing the self as self, knowing the not-self as not-self — constitutes jñāna. And this jñāna makes the soul akartā of karma.

Not through any external ritual or practice of renunciation. Through knowing correctly. The akartā-ship is built entirely on right knowing.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The jñānī knows the true self as self and the not-self as not-self. That knowing alone makes the soul akartā of karma.

---

### GATHA 94

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तिविहो एसुवओगो अप्पियप्पं करेदि कोहोऽहं।
कत्ता तस्सुवओगस्सं होदि सो अत्तभावस्स।।९४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Threefold upayoga creates self-vikalpas like "I am anger (krodhaḥ aham)"; the kartā of that upayoga becomes kartā of that bhāva.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The "I am anger" (krodho'ham), "I am pride," "I am deceit" — these self-vikalpas (self-constructions) are the practical mechanism of ajñāna-based bondage. The upayoga, operating through mithyātva, generates these false self-statements. And whoever identifies as kartā of that upayoga — whoever says "I am angry" and means it literally — becomes the kartā of that bhāva and its karmic consequences.

This is one of the most psychologically penetrating verses in the Samaysaar. Modern psychology would call this the difference between having an emotion and being an emotion. The Samaysaar calls the second "vicarious" kartā-ship — the karmic identification that binds.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

When upayoga generates the thought "I am anger" — and you identify with it as kartā — you become the doer of that bhāva and bind karma through it.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Notice the next time you say "I am angry" versus "there is anger." That small shift in language maps a much larger shift in identity.

---

### GATHA 95–97

*(G95 is parallel to G94, with "I am dharma" etc. — showing that even identifying as "I am righteous" can be a self-vikalpa if it is not pure self-knowledge. G96 summarizes:)*

Thus the foolish one makes other substances into self, and through ignorance makes self into other.

*(G97 states:)* For this reason the soul is called kartā by niśchaya-knowers; one who truly knows this is freed from all kartṛtva.

**Commentary on G97:** The irony is exquisite: it is precisely because the soul is truly the kartā (of its own bhavas) that the knower of this — who understands what kartā-ship actually means — is freed from false kartā-ship. Understanding what you truly ARE responsible for (your inner bhavas) liberates you from the illusion of being responsible for everything else (karma material, pudgala transformations, others' states).

---

### GATHA 98–108

*(G98-108 systematically elaborate the vyavahāra descriptions of the soul's kartā-ship — pots, cloth, chariots (G98) — and then systematically demolish each. The argument in G99:)*

If the soul were to do other substances, it would necessarily become tanmaya (identical with them); since it is not tanmaya, it is not their kartā.

*(G100:)* The soul does not make pot, cloth, or other substances; yoga and upayoga are the producers — so in that sense alone is the soul kartā.

*(G105:)* Seeing the soul as instrumental cause for karma-bondage, "karma done by the soul" is said — only by upachāra (conventional metaphor), not ultimately.

*(G106:)* When warriors fight, people say "the king did the battle" — similarly by vyavahāra, "jñānāvaraṇādi was done by the soul."

*(G108:)* Just as a king is conventionally called "producer of subjects' faults and virtues," the soul by vyavahāra is called "producer of dravya-qualities."

---

## PART 5: THE FOUR CAUSES OF BONDAGE (G109–112)

### GATHA 109

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सामण्णपच्चया खलु चउरो भण्णंति बंधकत्तारो।
मिच्छत्तं अविरमणं कसायजोगा य बोद्धव्वा।।१०९।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The four general causes (sāmānya-pratyaya) declared as the doers-of-bondage are: mithyātva, non-restraint (aviramana), kasāya (passions), and yoga — these are to be understood.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The four general causes of bondage are foundational Jain karma-theory: mithyātva (wrong belief), aviramana (non-restraint from violence and sin), kasāya (the four passions: anger, pride, deceit, greed), and yoga (activity of mind, speech, body that channels karmic matter). The "sāmānya-pratyaya" means these are the general categories — each then has specific sub-varieties (the next gatha gives thirteen).

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Four things cause karma to bind: wrong belief, non-restraint, passions, and activity (mental/verbal/physical). These are the roots.

---

### GATHA 110

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तेसिं पुणो वि य इमो भणिदो भेदो दु तेरसवियप्पो।
मिच्छादिट्ठीआदी जाव सजोगिस्स चरमंतं।।११०।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Their distinction is further described as thirteen varieties — from mithyādṛṣṭi (guṇasthāna) onwards through to the final moment of the sa-yogi Kevalī.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The thirteen guṇasthānas (stages of spiritual development) correspond to the varying admixtures of the four causes of bondage. At mithyādṛṣṭi (guṇasthāna 1), all four operate fully. As the soul progresses, one by one these causes are attenuated. At the sa-yogi Kevalī stage (guṇasthāna 13), the last trace — yoga — still operates. Only at the final moment does yoga cease, and complete liberation (moksha) is attained.

This gives the theory practical application: spiritual progress is measured by which bondage-causes are being progressively reduced.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The thirteen spiritual stages map the progressive reduction of bondage-causes from wrong belief right through to the final moment before liberation.

---

### GATHA 111

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एदे अचेदणा खलु पोग्गलकम्मुदयसंभवा जम्हा।
ते जदि करंति कम्मं ण वि तेसिं वेदगो आदा।।१११।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

These (four pratyayas) are insentient — they arise from the rise of pudgala-karma. If they (alone) do karma, the soul is not their experiencer.

**4. COMMENTARY**

A key argument: if we say the four bondage-causes (mithyātva etc.) — which arise from pudgala-karma's rise — are the actual doers of karma, then the soul would not be the experiencer of that karma. But the soul IS the experiencer. Therefore the soul cannot be excluded from the causal picture. Its upayoga — its conscious engagement — is what converts insentient karmic conditions into lived bondage.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The four causes are themselves insentient — born from karma-rise. If they alone did karma, the soul wouldn't experience it. But it does — which tells us the soul's conscious engagement is part of what creates bondage.

---

### GATHA 112

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

गुणसंणिदा दु एदे कम्मं कुव्वंति पच्चया जम्हा।
तम्हा जीवोऽकत्ता गुणा य कुव्वंति कम्माणि।।११२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

These pratyayas — as "guṇas" (qualities/attributes of consciousness-stages) — do karma; therefore the soul is akartā, and the guṇas do the karmas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The guṇas here are the guṇasthāna-specific attributes — the specific forms that the four pratyayas take at each stage. From the niśchaya standpoint, the soul is akartā (non-doer) of karma; the guṇas (which are really the achetana pratyaya forces) are the actual karma-doers. The soul is their field — not their agent.

This is the ultimate logical endpoint of G111's argument. The soul is not akartā in the sense of being inert — it is very much alive, knowing, experiencing. But in terms of producing karma's material transformation — that belongs to the guṇas, the pratyayas, the achetana forces that operate through the soul's field.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The spiritual-stage attributes (guṇas) do karma — not the soul directly. The soul is akartā in this technical sense.

---

## PART 6: SOUL AND PRATYAYA ARE NOT IDENTICAL (G113–115)

### GATHA 113

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जह जीवस्स अणण्णुवओगो कोहो वि तह जिद अणण्णो।
जीवस्साजीवस्स य एवमणण्णत्तमावण्णं।।११३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Just as upayoga is non-separate from the soul — if krodha were also non-separate — then soul and ajīva would become non-separate (non-different), which is a fault.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The argument being addressed: someone might say "upayoga is the soul itself — it is not separate from it. Similarly, krodha (anger) is upayoga — so krodha is the soul itself." If this were so, the soul and ajīva (matter) would be non-different — which collapses the fundamental distinction between consciousness and matter, the basis of all Jain philosophy.

The teaching: upayoga is the soul's own characteristic (ananya), yes. But krodha is an achetana (insentient) quality — arising from karma. It is NOT ananya to the soul in the same way.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Upayoga is genuinely the soul's own. But anger — which arises from karma — is not the soul's own in the same way. Conflating them destroys the distinction between consciousness and matter.

---

### GATHA 114

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एविमह जो दु जीवो सो चेव दु णियमदो तहाऽजीवो।
अयमेयते दोसो पच्चयणोकम्मकम्माणं।।११४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

In that case whatever is the soul would necessarily be ajīva too — and this flaw extends to pratyaya, nokamma, and karma as well.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

If soul and anger (ajīva) were identical, the soul would be ajīva — which extends the error to karma, nokamma, and all pratyayas.

---

### GATHA 115

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अह दे अण्णो कोहो अण्णुवओगप्पगो हवइ चेदा।
जह कोहो तह पच्चय कम्मं णोकम्ममिव अण्णं।।११५।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

But if krodha is "other" (anya) and the soul is of the nature of upayoga as "other" — then just as krodha is other, pratyaya, karma, and nokamma are all other (separate from the soul).

**4. COMMENTARY**

The resolution: krodha IS anya (other) — distinct from the soul. The soul IS of the nature of upayoga — conscious knowing. These are categorically different. And this distinction cleanly extends to all of karma, nokamma, and pratyaya — all of which are anya to the soul.

The soul is consciousness. Everything else is other. This is the clean ontological division that Kundakunda insists upon. Not as ascetic ideology, but as precise metaphysics.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Anger is other than the soul. The soul is consciousness. And therefore all of karma, pratyaya, and nokamma are other than the soul — clearly, cleanly other.

---

## PART 7: EACH SUBSTANCE TRANSFORMS ITSELF (G116–125)

### GATHA 116

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवे ण सयं बद्धूं ण सयं परिणमिदे कम्मभावेण।
जिद पोग्गलदव्विमणं अप्परिणामी तदा होइदु।।११६।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

If the soul is not bound to karma by itself and does not transform by itself in karma-form, then pudgala-dravya would be proved to be non-transforming (aparinami).

**4. COMMENTARY**

This gatha begins a long philosophical argument (G116-125) directed at the Sāṃkhya school's view that the purusa (soul) is entirely aparinami (non-transforming) and prakriti (matter) transforms to produce all effects. The Jain position is: BOTH soul and pudgala are pariṇāmi (transforming substances). If the soul did not transform itself, the result would be Sāṃkhya — which the Jains reject.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

If the soul doesn't transform itself, then pudgala can't transform either — and the Sāṃkhya problem follows.

---

### GATHAS 117–120

*(G117:)* If the karmic varganās don't transform into karma-form, either samsāra is impossible or the Sāṃkhya view follows.

*(G118:)* The soul causes pudgala to transform into karma — but if pudgala doesn't transform by itself, how can the soul cause it?

*(G119:)* If pudgala transforms by itself into karma — then saying "the soul transforms karma" is false.

*(G120:)* By necessity, pudgala transformed into karma-form IS karma. Similarly, pudgala transformed into jñānāvaraṇa-form IS jñānāvaraṇa.

**Commentary on G120:** The key philosophical punchline: each substance is definitively what its transformation says it is. Pudgala transformed into jñānāvaraṇa (knowledge-obscuring karma) IS jñānāvaraṇa karma. The soul didn't make it — it IS it by its own transformation. This is what it means for each substance to transform by its own svabhāva.

---

### GATHAS 121–125

*(G121-124 apply the same argument to the soul's transformation in krodhādi (anger etc.) — proving that the soul transforms itself into anger-states, without being pushed by karma:)*

*(G121:)* If your soul does not bind karma by itself and does not transform by itself in krodhādi, it would be non-transforming.

*(G122:)* If the soul does not transform by itself in krodhādi bhavas, either samsāra is impossible or Sāṃkhya follows.

*(G123:)* If pudgala-anger transforms the soul into anger — but anger can't transform what doesn't self-transform first.

*(G124:)* If the soul itself transforms into anger-form — then saying "anger transforms the soul" is false.

*(G125:)* Soul engaged in krodha-upayoga IS krodha; engaged in māna-upayoga IS māna; engaged in māyā-upayoga IS māyā; engaged in lobha-upayoga IS lobha.

**Commentary on G125:** The conclusion of this entire argument: the soul is what its upayoga is. When upayoga is in anger-mode, the soul IS anger — not as its nature, but as its current transformation. The karma (anger-karma) is nimitta. The soul's own transformation is the actual anger. This is revolutionary: your anger is your own activity, not what karma "does to you." And therefore it can be changed — by you.

---

## PART 8: JÑĀNĪ VS AJÑĀNĪ — THE NATURE OF BHĀVAS (G126–131)

### GATHA 126

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जं कुणिदे भावमादा कत्ता सो होदि तस्स कम्मस्स।
णाणिस्स स णाणमओ अण्णाणमओ अणाणिस्स।।१२६।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Whatever bhāva the soul does, it is kartā of that karma. For the jñānī, that bhāva is jñānamaya (knowledge-filled); for the ajñānī, it is ajñānamaya (ignorance-filled).

**4. COMMENTARY**

The same external action can be jñānamaya or ajñānamaya depending on the quality of the doer's upayoga. This is not a moralistic judgment — it is a description of the actual nature of the act as produced by different qualities of consciousness. The jñānī's bhāvas, arising from jñānamaya upayoga, are genuinely knowledge-filled — they reflect the truth of what is happening. The ajñānī's bhāvas, arising from ajñānamaya upayoga, are ignorance-filled — distorted by the fundamental reversal described in G92.

And critically: the jñānī's jñānamaya bhavas do not produce karma in the same way. The ajñānī's ajñānamaya bhavas do.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The same action — done by a jñānī — is jñānamaya. Done by an ajñānī — it is ajñānamaya. The quality of the doer's consciousness transforms the nature of the deed.

---

### GATHA 127

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अण्णाणमओ भावो अणाणिणो कुणिदे तेण कम्माणि।
णाणमओ णाणिस्स दु ण कुणिदे तम्हा दु कम्माणि।।१२७।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The ajñānī's bhāva is ajñānamaya — therefore it does karmas. The jñānī's bhāva is jñānamaya — therefore it does NOT do karmas.

**4. COMMENTARY**

This is the core principle of the jñānī-ajñānī distinction in Adhikar 2: ajñānamaya bhāva produces karma; jñānamaya bhāva does not. The jñānī's life may look similar externally to the ajñānī's — both breathe, eat, speak, move. But the jñānī's actions arise from jñānamaya upayoga — they don't add to karma. The ajñānī's arise from ajñānamaya upayoga — they do.

The Atmakhyati explains: the jñānī does not perform actions while treating the rāga-dvesha as "mine" — as self-definition. Without that claiming, karma has no hook to attach to.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Ajñānamaya doing = karma. Jñānamaya doing = no karma. The inner quality of consciousness is what determines the karmic outcome, not the external act.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Is it possible to act without identification — without the "this is mine" or "I am this"? What would your daily actions feel like from that place?

---

### GATHA 128

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

णाणमया भावाओ णाणमओ चेव जायदे भावो।
जम्हा तम्हा णाणिस्स सव्वे भावा हु णाणमया।।१२८।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

From jñānamaya bhāva, only jñānamaya bhāva arises; therefore all bhavas of the jñānī are truly jñānamaya.

**4. COMMENTARY**

This is a profound statement about the self-reproducing nature of consciousness states. Jñāna generates jñāna. Ajñāna generates ajñāna. Once the jñānī's consciousness is established in jñānamaya bhāva, everything that arises from it — every thought, every perception, every response — is jñānamaya. Not because the jñānī is suppressing or controlling — but because the source is clean.

The analogy used in the commentary (G130): from gold come only gold-forms (earrings, bracelets). From iron come only iron-forms. Similarly, from jñāna come only jñāna-bhavas.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

From knowing comes knowing. All of the jñānī's bhavas are jñānamaya — because they arise from jñānamaya consciousness.

---

### GATHA 129

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अण्णाणमया भावा अण्णाणो चेव जायदे भावो।
जम्हा तम्हा भावा अण्णाणमया अणाणिस्स।।१२९।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

From ajñānamaya bhavas, only ajñāna-bhāva arises; therefore all bhavas of the ajñānī are ajñānamaya.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

From ignorance, ignorance is reproduced. All of the ajñānī's bhavas are ajñānamaya — generated from the same distorted source.

---

### GATHAS 130–131

*(G130-131 give the substance-analogy:)*

Just as from gold come earring-forms etc.; from iron come bangle-forms — the ajñānī's many bhavas are all ajñānamaya; the jñānī's bhavas are all jñānamaya.

**Commentary:** Like produces like. The quality of consciousness is self-propagating. This is both a warning and a promise: if you are rooted in ajñāna, everything you do will perpetuate it. If you shift to jñāna — at even a small recognition of the real — everything begins to shift.

## PART 9: THE FOUR RISES AND THEIR NATURE (G132–136)

### GATHA 132

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

अण्णाणस्स स उदओ जा जीवाणं अतच्चउवलद्धी।
मिच्छत्तस्स दु उदओ जीवस्स असद्दहाणतं।।१३२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The rise of ajñāna in jīvas is the non-attainment of true reality (atattvopala-bdhi); the rise of mithyātva is non-faith (asaddhānatva) in the soul.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The rises (udaya) of the four bondage-causes are described at their experiential level:
- Ajñāna-rise = not recognizing reality as it is. The soul sees wrongly, hears wrongly, interprets wrongly.
- Mithyātva-rise = active disbelief, rejection of right vision. Not just failing to see — but oriented away from truth.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Ajñāna's rise = missing truth. Mithyātva's rise = rejecting truth.

---

### GATHA 133

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

उदओ असंजमस्स दु जं जीवाणं हवेइ अविरमणं।
जो दु कलुसोवओगो जीवाणं सो कसाउदओ।।१३३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The rise of asaṃyama is the non-restraint (aviramaṇa) of jīvas; the impure (kaluśa) upayoga of jīvas is the rise of kasāya.

**4. COMMENTARY**

- Asaṃyama-rise = the concrete non-restraint in action — harming, speaking falsely, taking what is not given.
- Kasāya-rise = the inner fouling of upayoga by passion-states. The upayoga becomes kalusha (murky, defiled) — unable to see clearly.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Non-restraint is the rise of asaṃyama; murky/defiled upayoga is the rise of kasāya.

---

### GATHA 134

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

तं जाण जोगउदयं जो जीवाणं तु चित्तुच्छाहो।
सोहणमसोहणं वा कायव्वो विरदिभावो वा।।१३४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Know that yoga-rise is the inner enthusiasm or initiative of jīvas — whether pure or impure — or the state of viradibhāva (renunciatory initiative).

**4. COMMENTARY**

Yoga-rise is the soul's sheer initiative — its readiness to act. Pure yoga-rise is the energy behind constructive action. Impure yoga-rise is restlessness and agitation. Even viradibhāva (the initiation of renunciation) is a form of yoga-rise — showing that yoga is neutral: what matters is the quality of consciousness directing it.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Yoga's rise is the inner energy of initiative — pure, impure, or even the impulse to renounce. Yoga itself is neutral.

---

### GATHA 135–136

*(G135-136 describe what happens when these four rises operate as instrumental causes:)*

*(G135:)* Among these instrumental causes, the kārmika-varganā-matter transforms in eight forms — jñānāvaraṇa etc.

*(G136:)* When that kārmika-varganā-matter is bound to the living soul, at that time the soul becomes instrumental cause for its transformations.

**Commentary:** The complete picture: when mithyātva, avirati, kasāya, and yoga are operational, kārmika particles (pudgala of a specific type — karmic-grade matter) adhere to the soul and transform into the eight types of karma. The soul is nimitta (instrumental cause) — not the material agent. Pudgala does the actual material transformation; the soul's state provides the occasion.

## PART 10: DISTINCTNESS OF SOUL AND PUDGALA TRANSFORMATIONS (G137–142)

### GATHA 137

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जइ जीवेण सह चिय पोग्गलदव्वस्स कम्मपरिणामो।
एवं पोग्गलजीवा हु दो वि कम्मत्तमावण्णा।।१३७।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

If karma-transformation of pudgala-dravya happens together WITH the soul — then both pudgala and soul would become karma.

**4. COMMENTARY**

An argument against the view that soul and pudgala jointly transform into karma. If they transformed jointly (together), both would be karma. But the soul does not become karma. Therefore they do not transform jointly. Each transforms separately — according to its own nature.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

If soul and pudgala jointly became karma, both would be karma. Since only pudgala becomes karma, they transform separately.

---

### GATHA 138

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

एक्कस्स दु परिणामो पोग्गलदव्वस्स कम्मभावेण।
ता जीवभावहेदूहिं विणा कम्मस्स परिणामो।।१३८।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

Karma-transformation belongs to pudgala-dravya alone; therefore, the transformation of karma happens without jīva-bhāva as its cause (direct material cause).

**4. COMMENTARY**

Pudgala transforms into karma by its own nature (svabhāva). The soul's bhāva is nimitta (occasion) — not upādāna kāraṇa (material cause). The material cause of karma is pudgala alone. This protects both the soul's integrity (it doesn't become karma) and pudgala's integrity (it transforms according to its own nature, not because the soul does something to it).

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Only pudgala becomes karma — and it does so by its own nature. The soul's state is the occasion, not the material cause.

---

### GATHA 139–140

*(G139-140 apply the same argument to the soul's transformation in rāgādi (attachment etc.):)*

*(G139:)* If the soul and karma together had rāgādi transformations — both would be rāgādi. But karma cannot be rāgī (attached).

*(G140:)* Rāgādi transformation belongs to the soul alone; therefore the soul's rāgādi transformation happens without karma-rise as its direct material cause.

**Commentary:** Just as only pudgala becomes karma — only the soul becomes rāgī (attached). Karma cannot experience attachment. Therefore the soul's rāga is the soul's own transformation, with karma as nimitta. This is simultaneously a statement of the soul's non-determinism (karma can't force you into attachment) and its responsibility (your attachment is your own transformation).

---

### GATHA 141

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

जीवे कम्मं बद्धं पुट्ठं चेद ववहारणयभणिदं।
सुद्धणयस्स दु जीवे अबद्धपुट्ठं हवदि कम्मं।।१४१।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

That karma is "bound and touched" in the soul — this is the vyavahāra-naya's statement. From the śuddha-naya's perspective, karma is "unbound and untouched" in the soul.

**4. COMMENTARY**

Two views, both valid at their respective levels:

**Vyavahāra:** Karma IS bound to the soul. We can speak of its binding, shedding, the process of bondage and liberation — all meaningful.

**Śuddha-naya (pure standpoint):** At the level of the soul's actual nature, karma never touches it. The soul is anādi-śuddha (pure from the beginningless). What appears as "bound" from the conventional view is, from the pure view, simply a condition — not a fact about the soul's essential nature.

This is not contradiction. This is the recognition that truth has levels, and each level of description serves a different purpose.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Conventionally: karma binds the soul. From the pure standpoint: karma never actually touches the soul. Both are true at their respective levels.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What does it mean that at your deepest level — right now — you are untouched by anything that has "happened to you"? How does that sit alongside the conventional reality of your experience?

---

### GATHA 142

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

कम्मं बद्धमबद्धं जीवे एवं तु जाण णयपक्खं।
पक्खादिक्कंतो पुण भण्णिदे जो सो समयसारो।।१४२।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

"Karma is bound in the soul" or "karma is not bound in the soul" — know these as naya-standpoints. But the one who is described as beyond standpoints — THAT is the Samayasāra.

**4. COMMENTARY**

G142 is the pivot of the entire Adhikar — and in some sense of the entire Samaysaar. Both "karma is bound" (vyavahāra) and "karma is unbound" (śuddha-naya) are standpoints — useful lenses that capture partial truth. But the Samayasāra is what is beyond both standpoints. It is not a third opinion — it is the direct experience of what the soul actually is, prior to any lens.

The name "Samayasāra" means "the essence of one's own time/nature" — the pure self, directly known, without mediation of any naya (standpoint). To reach it, one must transcend ALL naya-pakshas — not by rejecting them as useless (they are useful) but by not getting stuck in any of them.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

"Karma binds" and "karma doesn't bind" — both are standpoints. The Samayasāra itself is beyond all standpoints.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What are the standpoints YOU cling to about yourself? "I am bound" or "I am free"? What would it be to simply rest in what you are, prior to either story?

## PART 11: BEYOND BOTH STANDPOINTS — SAMAYASĀRA (G143–144)

### GATHA 143

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

दोण्ह वि णयाण भणिदं जाणिदे णवरं तु समयपिडिबद्धो।
ण दु णयपक्खं गिण्हिदे किंचि वि णयपक्खपरिहीणो।।१४३।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

द्वयोरपि नययोर्भणितं जानाति केवलं तु समयप्रतिबद्धः।
न तु नयपक्षं गृह्णाति किञ्चिदपि नयपक्षपरिहीनः।।१४३।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

The one who is firmly bound to Samayasāra knows the statements of both nayas — but does not take any naya-standpoint at all; being free from naya-standpoints.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The samayapraribaddha — the one bound to Samayasāra — is not ignorant of the nayas. This is not a teaching of anti-intellectualism. The person knows both the vyavahāra and the śuddha-naya's statements thoroughly. Understanding is complete. But understanding does not become attachment to a position. They move through both frameworks without being captured by either.

The Atmakhyati compares this to a Kevalī: the omniscient being sees and knows everything — all standpoints, all perspectives simultaneously — but is not captured by any of them. The Kevalī abides in direct experience of reality, which no standpoint fully describes.

The samyak-dṛṣṭi (right-perceiving jīva) in the śrutajñāna (scriptural knowledge) stage does the same thing at a more modest level: uses nayas as tools, isn't imprisoned by them.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

The Samayasāra-oriented soul knows both standpoints — but doesn't take sides with either. It is free from standpoint-attachment.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

Are there spiritual or philosophical positions you hold that have become limitations rather than tools? What would it feel like to know a view without being defined by it?

---

### GATHA 144

**1. PRAKRIT TEXT**

सम्मदंसणणाणं एसो लहिदे ति णवरि ववदेसं।
सव्वणयपक्खरहिदो भणिदो जो सो समयसारो।।१४४।।

**2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA**

सम्यग्दर्शनज्ञानमेष लभत इति केवलं व्यपदेशम्।
सर्वनयपक्षरहितो भणितो यः स समयसारः।।१४४।।

**3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION**

This one alone obtains the designation "samyak-darśana and samyak-jñāna." The one described as free from all naya-standpoints — THAT is Samayasāra.

**4. COMMENTARY**

The final gatha of Adhikar 2 is a revelation: samyak-darśana (right perception) and samyak-jñāna (right knowledge) are not two separate things that must be acquired through separate practices. They are the same reality — the direct experience of the soul in its purity, beyond all standpoints.

The Samayasāra is not a book. It is not a doctrine. It is the soul itself — as it truly is, when seen without the overlay of any naya, any standpoint, any secondary description. "Sarvnaya-paksha-rahito" — freed from all standpoints. Not a blank mind. A fully luminous, knowing consciousness that sees itself directly — without the mediation of even the most refined philosophical framework.

This is what Adhikar 2 has been pointing to from the very first verse: the distinction between self and not-self (G69), when fully realized, leads here — to the soul that knows itself as Samayasāra.

**5. SIMPLY PUT**

Being free from all standpoints — that alone obtains the names "right perception" and "right knowledge." That pure knowing is Samayasāra itself.

**6. CONTEMPLATE**

What would it mean to know yourself without any story — no standpoint of bound or free, jñānī or ajñānī, seeker or found? Just this: awareness, knowing itself, directly.
