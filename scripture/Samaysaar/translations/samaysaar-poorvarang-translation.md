# Samaysaar — Poorvarang (Preliminary Section)
## Translation: Gathas 1–10

**Scripture:** Samaysaar (The Essence of the Self / The Essence of Doctrine)
**Author:** Bhagwan Shri Kundkundacharya Dev
**Commentary (Tika):** Shrimad Amritchandra Suri (Atmakhyati Tika)
**Section:** Poorvarang (Preliminary / Prologue)
**Total Gathas in Poorvarang:** 38
**Translated here:** Gathas 1–10

---

## GATHA 1

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

वंदित्तु सव्वसिद्धे धुवमचलमणोवमं गदिं पत्ते।
वोच्छामि समयपाहुडमिणमो सुदकेवलीभणिदं ॥१॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

वन्दित्वा सर्वसिद्धान् धुवामचलामनौपम्यां गतिं प्रासान्।
वक्ष्यामि समयप्राभृतमिदं अहो श्रुतकेवलिभणितम् ॥१॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Having bowed to all the Siddhas — who have attained the eternal, immovable, and incomparable state — I shall now expound this Samayaprabhrit (The Gift of the Self), as declared by the Shrutakevalins (masters of scriptural omniscience).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**वंदित्तु** (vanditthu) = having bowed to; **सव्वसिद्धे** (savvasiddhe) = all the Siddhas; **धुव** (dhuva) = eternal; **अचल** (achala) = immovable; **अणोवमं** (anovamam) = incomparable; **गदिं** (gadim) = state/destination; **पत्ते** (patte) = having attained; **वोच्छामि** (vocchāmi) = I shall speak/expound; **समयपाहुडं** (samayapāhuḍam) = Samayaprabhrit (The Gift of Doctrine/Self); **इणमो** (iṇamo) = this; **सुदकेवलीभणिदं** (sudakevalībhaṇidam) = as declared by the Shrutakevalins.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Samaysaar opens with a bow to the Siddhas — liberated souls who have shed all karma and attained a state that is permanent (it never ends), immovable (it never fluctuates), and beyond comparison (nothing in the universe equals it). This is not a ritual formality. Kundkundacharya is establishing the authority of his text by grounding it in the highest possible reality: the state of pure, disembodied consciousness that every soul is capable of reaching.

The word "Samayaprabhrit" literally means "the gift of samaya." Samaya here carries a double meaning: it refers both to the soul (the self, the living substance) and to the doctrine (the true teaching about reality). So the title itself is a declaration: this text is a gift — it offers the essence of what the self truly is.

The author attributes this teaching not to himself but to the Shrutakevalins — those who possess complete scriptural knowledge. In the Jain tradition, the original omniscient teaching flows from the Tirthankaras, is preserved by the Ganadharas, and transmitted through a lineage of Shrutakevalins. Kundkundacharya places himself in this lineage, not as an innovator but as a faithful transmitter of eternal truth.

The Siddhas are bowed to rather than the Tirthankaras because this text focuses on the pure soul — and the Siddhas are the purest embodiment of that. They have no body, no karma, no activity — they are consciousness alone in its absolute form. That is what this text wants you to see: your own nature, stripped of everything that is not you.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The author bows to all liberated souls and announces that he will now present the essence of the soul's true nature, as taught by the great masters of scripture.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean for your own nature to be eternal, immovable, and beyond comparison — and what prevents you from experiencing it right now?

## GATHA 2

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जीवो चरित्तदंसणणाणहिदो तं हि ससमयं जाण।
पोगलकम्मपदेसहिदं च तं जाण परसमयं ॥२॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

जीव: चरित्रदर्शनज्ञानस्थित: तं हि स्वसमयं जानीहि।
पुद्गलकर्मप्रदेशस्थितं च तं जानीहि परसमयम् ॥२॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Know the soul established in its own conduct, perception, and knowledge as "own-self" (sva-samaya); and know the soul situated in the provinces of material karma as "other-self" (para-samaya).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**जीवो** (jīvo) = the soul; **चरित्तदंसणणाणहिदो** (charittadaṃsaṇanāṇahido) = established in conduct, perception, and knowledge; **तं** (taṃ) = that; **हि** (hi) = indeed; **ससमयं** (sasamayaṃ) = own-self/own-doctrine; **जाण** (jāṇa) = know; **पोगलकम्मपदेसहिदं** (pogalakammapadesahidaṃ) = situated in the provinces of material karma; **च** (cha) = and; **तं** (taṃ) = that; **जाण** (jāṇa) = know; **परसमयं** (parasamayaṃ) = other-self/other-doctrine.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is arguably the most foundational verse in all of Samaysaar. It introduces the central distinction that the entire text revolves around: the soul has two modes of being — its own true state (sva-samaya) and its entangled state (para-samaya).

When the soul operates from its own inherent nature — its natural capacity for right perception, right knowledge, and right conduct — it is in its "own-self" state. This is what the soul actually is: a conscious substance whose nature is to perceive, know, and conduct itself in alignment with reality. These three — conduct, perception, and knowledge — are not external acquisitions. They are the soul's own properties, as natural to it as heat is to fire.

When the same soul is found operating through the lens of material karma — when karmic particles occupy its spatial units and distort its natural expression — it is in the "other-self" state. Here "other" does not mean a different soul; it means the soul is functioning as though it were something other than what it really is. It is identified with material karma rather than with its own consciousness.

This two-fold nature of samaya (self/doctrine) is not about two different souls. It is about two states of the same soul. The soul that knows itself as consciousness is free. The soul that identifies with karma is bound. The entire Samaysaar is an elaboration of this single insight.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the soul lives from its own nature — knowledge, perception, and right conduct — that is its true self. When it is caught up in the effects of karma, that is its distorted, false self.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In this very moment, are you experiencing your own nature (awareness, clarity, stillness) or are you identified with something that is not you (reactions, cravings, agitation)?

## GATHA 3

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयत्तणिच्छयगदो समओ सव्वत्थ सुंदरो लोगे।
बंधकहा एयत्ते तेण विसंवादिणी होदि ॥३॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

एकत्वनिश्चयगत: समय: सर्वत्र सुन्दरो लोके।
बन्धकथैकत्वे तेन विसंवादिनी भवति ॥३॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Samaya (the self/doctrine), when understood through the perspective of oneness, is beautiful everywhere in the universe. The story of bondage, being rooted in oneness with karma, is therefore contradictory to it.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**एयत्तणिच्छयगदो** (eyattanicchhayagado) = arrived at through the determination of oneness; **समओ** (samao) = samaya (self/doctrine); **सव्वत्थ** (savvattha) = everywhere; **सुंदरो** (suṃdaro) = beautiful; **लोगे** (loge) = in the universe; **बंधकहा** (baṃdhakahā) = the story/account of bondage; **एयत्ते** (eyatte) = in oneness; **तेण** (teṇa) = therefore; **विसंवादिणी** (visaṃvādiṇī) = contradictory/discordant; **होदि** (hodi) = becomes.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Here "samaya" is used in its broadest sense — referring to all substances in the universe. When each substance is understood as a single, unified entity that undergoes transformation through its own modes, this is the "oneness perspective" (ekatva-nishchaya). From this perspective, every substance — whether soul, matter, space, time, or the rest — is complete in itself, self-contained, and beautiful precisely because it exists as one undivided reality manifesting through its own qualities.

The "story of bondage" — the narrative that says the soul is bound by karma — depends on treating the soul and karma as a single combined entity. But if every substance is truly one with itself and distinct from all others, then the soul cannot actually be "one with" karma. The bondage narrative, while practically useful, is ultimately contradictory to the truth of each substance's independent oneness.

This does not mean bondage is a mere illusion. The soul genuinely experiences the effects of karma. But from the ultimate perspective, the soul's essential nature was never actually altered by karma — just as a crystal remains a crystal even when it appears red from a nearby flower. The story of bondage is a description of appearances, not of the soul's fundamental reality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When you understand anything in the universe as truly one undivided thing, it is beautiful. The idea that the soul is bound by karma contradicts this oneness, because the soul and karma are fundamentally different substances.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If your essential nature has never actually been damaged by karma, what exactly are you trying to fix — and what would happen if you simply recognized what was already whole?

## GATHA 4

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुदपरिचिदाणुभूदा सव्वस्स वि कामभोगबंधकहा।
एयत्तसुवलंभो णवरि केवल सुलहो विहत्तस्स ॥४॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

श्रुतपरिचितानुभूता सर्वस्यापि कामभोगबन्धकथा।
एकत्वस्योपलम्भ: केवलं न सुलभो विभक्तस्य ॥४॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The story of sensual bondage has been heard, studied, and experienced by all beings. But the realization of the soul's oneness — distinct from everything else — is simply not easy to attain.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**सुदपरिचिदाणुभूदा** (sudaparichidāṇubhūdā) = heard, studied, and experienced; **सव्वस्स वि** (savvassa vi) = by all; **कामभोगबंधकहा** (kāmabhogabaṃdhakahā) = the story of sensual enjoyment and bondage; **एयत्तसुवलंभो** (eyattasuvalamho) = the realization of oneness; **णवरि** (ṇavari) = only/merely; **केवल** (kevala) = alone/solely; **सुलहो** (sulaho) = easy; **विहत्तस्स** (vihattassa) = of the distinct/separate one (the soul as distinct from karma).

### 5. COMMENTARY

Every being in the universe — without exception — already knows the story of sensual desire and bondage. It has been heard infinite times, encountered infinite times, and experienced infinite times. Every soul in the world cycle wanders through the five-fold rotation of births, driven by the great demon of delusion-karma, tormented by the fire of craving that burns from within, overwhelmed by the onslaught of sense-objects.

And yet, despite this infinite familiarity with bondage, the one thing that remains genuinely rare is the direct realization of the soul's oneness — its nature as a single, distinct, conscious substance completely separate from all matter. This realization has never been heard, never been encountered, never been experienced by the bound soul. That is why it is called "not easy."

The contrast is devastating in its simplicity: you know bondage intimately because you have lived it for eternity. You do not know your own self because you have never once turned inward to experience it directly. The Samaysaar exists to change that.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Everyone has experienced desire and bondage countless times — that story is familiar. But actually realizing your soul as something separate from all of that is extremely rare and difficult.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

You have spent lifetimes immersed in the experience of wanting and suffering. Have you ever, even once, turned fully inward to experience what you are beneath all of that?

## GATHA 5

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं एयत्तविहत्तं दाएहं अप्पणो सविहवेण।
जदि दाएज्ज पमाणं चुक्केज्ज छलं ण घेत्तव्वं ॥५॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

तमेकत्वविभक्तं दर्शयेऽहमात्मन: स्वविभवेन।
यदि दर्शयेयं प्रमाणं स्खलेयं छलं न गृहीतव्यम् ॥५॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I shall reveal that soul — one and distinct — through its own inherent glory. If I demonstrate it with valid proof, accept it; and if I happen to slip anywhere, do not seize upon the error deceptively.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**तं** (taṃ) = that; **एयत्तविहत्तं** (eyattavihattaṃ) = one and distinct (the soul as a single, separate substance); **दाएहं** (dāehaṃ) = I shall show/reveal; **अप्पणो** (appaṇo) = of the self; **सविहवेण** (savihaveṇa) = through its own glory/splendor; **जदि** (jadi) = if; **दाएज्ज** (dāejja) = I demonstrate; **पमाणं** (pamāṇaṃ) = with valid evidence/proof; **चुक्केज्ज** (chukkejja) = if I slip/err; **छलं** (chhalaṃ) = deception/fallacy; **ण** (ṇa) = not; **घेत्तव्वं** (ghettavvaṃ) = to be grasped/seized upon.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkundacharya makes a remarkable personal declaration here. He says: I will show the soul through its own inherent splendor — not through external arguments alone, but through the soul's own self-luminous nature. The four methods he relies on are: study of scripture, logical reasoning, the teacher's instruction, and direct self-experience. Of these, self-experience is primary.

The word "svavibhava" (own glory) is crucial. He is not proving the soul's existence through inference from external evidence. He is pointing to the soul's own radiance — its capacity to know itself directly. The teacher's role is to turn you toward that direct knowing.

The second half of the verse is equally striking in its intellectual honesty. He says: if my proofs are valid, accept them. But if I make an error somewhere — in logic, in phrasing, in technical precision — do not use that error as an excuse to reject the entire teaching. This is an acknowledgment that language and logic are imperfect tools for communicating something that ultimately must be experienced. The teaching points beyond itself. A flaw in the pointing finger does not invalidate the moon.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The author promises to reveal the soul's true nature using its own inner radiance as proof. He asks: if the evidence is sound, accept it — and if he makes any mistake along the way, don't use it as an excuse to dismiss the whole teaching.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you encounter a teaching that challenges your assumptions, do you look for reasons to accept the truth in it — or do you look for flaws to justify rejecting it?

## GATHA 6

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ण वि होदि अप्पमत्तो ण पमत्तो जाणगो दु जो भावो।
एवं भणंति सुद्धं णादो जो सो दु सो चेव ॥६॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

नापि भवत्यप्रमत्तो न प्रमत्तो ज्ञायकस्तु यो भाव:।
एवं भणन्ति शुद्धं ज्ञातो य: स तु स चेव ॥६॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

That which is the knowing nature — it is neither vigilant nor negligent. Thus they call it pure. And the one who is known through that knowing — that alone is the self, nothing else.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**ण वि** (ṇa vi) = not even; **होदि** (hodi) = is; **अप्पमत्तो** (appamatto) = vigilant/non-negligent; **ण** (ṇa) = nor; **पमत्तो** (pamatto) = negligent; **जाणगो** (jāṇago) = knowing; **दु** (du) = but/indeed; **जो** (jo) = which; **भावो** (bhāvo) = nature/state; **एवं** (evaṃ) = thus; **भणंति** (bhaṇaṃti) = they say/call; **सुद्धं** (suddhaṃ) = pure; **णादो** (ṇādo) = known; **जो** (jo) = who/which; **सो** (so) = that; **दु** (du) = indeed; **सो** (so) = that very one; **चेव** (cheva) = alone/only.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse defines purity in a way that may seem counterintuitive. In the Jain spiritual hierarchy, there are stages of spiritual progress (gunasthanas) — some are "negligent" (pramatta, where the aspirant is still subject to subtle lapses) and others are "vigilant" (apramatta, where those lapses cease). But the pure knowing nature of the soul transcends even this distinction.

The "knowing nature" (jnayaka-bhava) is the soul's essential characteristic: its capacity to know. This knowing is not a product of spiritual effort — it is the soul's permanent, beginningless nature. It is self-established, self-proved, and self-luminous, just as a lamp illuminates both itself and its surroundings without needing another lamp to light it.

From the perspective of this knowing nature, labels like "vigilant" and "negligent" belong to the realm of changing states, not to the essence. The soul's knowing capacity shines equally whether the soul is in a negligent state or a vigilant one — just as fire remains fire whether it is burning brightly or smoldering. The states change; the nature does not.

That which knows — and is known through that knowing — is the self. Nothing else. Not the body, not karma, not emotional states, not spiritual stages. The knower alone is the self. This is what "pure" means in Samaysaar: not morally pure, but ontologically pure — the soul in its undistorted essence.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The soul's essential nature is simply to know. This knowing nature is neither vigilant nor negligent — it is beyond such categories. It is pure. And whatever is known through that knowing — that alone is the true self.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you notice the part of you that is simply aware — that knows your thoughts without being your thoughts, that witnesses your states without being those states?

## GATHA 7

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ववहारेणुवदिस्सदि णाणिस्स चरित्त दंसणं णाणं।
ण वि णाणं ण चरित्तं ण दंसणं जाणगो सुद्धो ॥७॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

व्यवहारेणोपदिश्यते ज्ञानिनश्चरित्रं दर्शनं ज्ञानम्।
नापि ज्ञानं न चरित्रं न दर्शनं ज्ञायक: शुद्ध: ॥७॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

From the conventional viewpoint, the knower is taught to have conduct, perception, and knowledge as three. But in reality, there is no separate knowledge, no separate conduct, no separate perception — the knower is simply pure.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**ववहारेणुवदिस्सदि** (vavahāreṇuvadissadi) = from the conventional standpoint, it is taught; **णाणिस्स** (ṇāṇissa) = of the knower; **चरित्त** (charitta) = conduct; **दंसणं** (daṃsaṇaṃ) = perception; **णाणं** (ṇāṇaṃ) = knowledge; **ण वि** (ṇa vi) = not even; **णाणं** (ṇāṇaṃ) = knowledge; **ण** (ṇa) = nor; **चरित्तं** (charittaṃ) = conduct; **ण** (ṇa) = nor; **दंसणं** (daṃsaṇaṃ) = perception; **जाणगो** (jāṇago) = the knower; **सुद्धो** (suddho) = is pure.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This verse addresses one of the most subtle points in Jain philosophy: the relationship between a substance and its attributes. Conventionally (from the vyavahara or practical perspective), a knower's qualities are described as three distinct things — knowledge, perception, and conduct. This is how teachers explain the path: cultivate right knowledge, right perception, and right conduct.

But from the ultimate perspective (nishchaya), these three are not separate items sitting inside the soul like objects in a box. They are the soul's own nature expressing itself. The soul is one indivisible substance. When we look at it through the lens of infinite attributes, we see "knowledge" from one angle, "perception" from another, and "conduct" from a third. But the soul itself does not contain three things — it is one thing seen three ways.

The practical implication is revolutionary: you do not need to acquire three separate achievements. You need to realize one thing — your own pure nature — and all three are already there. The divisions are pedagogical tools, not ontological realities.

This is why Kundkundacharya says the pure knower has no separate knowledge, no separate conduct, no separate perception. The knower is simply pure — one undivided conscious reality.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Conventionally, a wise person is said to have three separate qualities — knowledge, perception, and conduct. But in reality, the knower is one indivisible pure being. The three are just different ways of describing the same thing.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you are fully present and aware, do knowledge, perception, and conduct feel like three separate things — or like one seamless state of being?

## GATHA 8

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जह ण वि सक्कमणज्जो अणज्जभासं विणा दु गाहेदुं।
तह ववहारेण विणा परमत्थुवदेसणमसक्कं ॥८॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यथा नापि शक्योऽनार्यो अनार्यभाषां विना तु ग्राहयितुम्।
तथा व्यवहारेण विना परमार्थोपदेशनमशक्यम् ॥८॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as it is impossible to make a non-Aryan understand without using his own non-Aryan language, so too is it impossible to teach the ultimate truth without using the conventional viewpoint.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**जह** (jaha) = just as; **ण वि** (ṇa vi) = not; **सक्कं** (sakkaṃ) = possible; **अणज्जो** (aṇajjo) = a non-Aryan (one who does not know the refined language); **अणज्जभासं** (aṇajjabhāsaṃ) = non-Aryan language; **विणा** (viṇā) = without; **दु** (du) = indeed; **गाहेदुं** (gāheduṃ) = to make understand; **तह** (taha) = similarly; **ववहारेण** (vavahāreṇa) = conventional viewpoint; **विणा** (viṇā) = without; **परमत्थुवदेसणं** (paramatthuvadesaṇaṃ) = teaching of the ultimate truth; **असक्कं** (asakkaṃ) = is impossible.

### 5. COMMENTARY

After establishing that the ultimate reality is one undivided pure consciousness, a natural question arises: if the ultimate truth is a single non-dual reality, why bother with conventional teachings at all? Why speak of knowledge, perception, and conduct as separate? Why use the language of bondage and liberation, karma and soul, if ultimately these divisions are secondary?

Kundkundacharya answers with a vivid analogy. If a Brahmin says "svasti" (a blessing word) to a foreigner who does not know Sanskrit, the foreigner will just stare blankly — the word means nothing to him. But if someone who knows both languages translates the meaning into the foreigner's tongue, then suddenly the foreigner's eyes fill with tears of joy as he understands the beautiful meaning of the blessing.

Similarly, the soul's ultimate nature cannot be directly communicated to beings entangled in worldly understanding without first using the language they already know — the conventional language of distinct qualities, stages, karma, and practice. The conventional viewpoint is not false; it is a necessary bridge. It takes the seeker from where they are to where they need to be.

The key insight: conventional teaching (vyavahara) is not the enemy of ultimate truth (nishchaya). It is its vehicle. Rejecting conventional teaching because you have heard of ultimate truth is like refusing to use a boat because the other shore exists. You need the boat precisely because you have not yet arrived.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as you cannot explain something to someone without speaking their language, you cannot teach the highest truth without first using the practical, conventional framework that people already understand.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Are there truths in your life that you understand intellectually but have not yet experienced directly? What bridge might carry you from understanding to experience?

## GATHA 9–10

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT (Gatha 9)

जो हि सुदेणहिगच्छदि अप्पाणमिणं तु केवलं सुद्धं।
तं सुदकेवलिमिसिणो भणंति लोयप्पदीवयरं ॥९॥

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT (Gatha 10)

णाणं अप्पा सव्वं जाणादि सुदकेवलिं तमाहु जिणा।
सब ज्ञान सो आत्मा हि है, श्रुतकेवली उससे बने ॥१०॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA (Gatha 9)

यो हि श्रुतेनाभिगच्छति आत्मानमिमं तु केवलं शुद्धम्।
तं श्रुतकेवलिनमुषयो भणन्ति लोकप्रदीपकरम् ॥९॥

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA (Gatha 10)

ज्ञानमात्मा सर्वं जानाति श्रुतकेवलिनं तमाहुर्जिना:।
ज्ञानं सर्वं आत्मा यस्मात् श्रुतकेवली तस्मात् ॥१०॥

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(Gatha 9): The one who, through scriptural knowledge, directly approaches the self as purely one and distinct — the sages call that one a Shrutakevali, a lamp for the world.

(Gatha 10): The soul that knows all through knowledge — the Jinas call that one a Shrutakevali. Because all knowledge is the soul itself, therefore it is a Shrutakevali.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA (Gatha 9)

**जो** (jo) = who; **हि** (hi) = indeed; **सुदेण** (sudeṇa) = through scriptural knowledge; **अहिगच्छदि** (ahigacchhadi) = approaches/attains; **अप्पाणं** (appāṇaṃ) = the self; **इणं** (iṇaṃ) = this; **तु** (tu) = but/indeed; **केवलं** (kevalaṃ) = solely/only; **सुद्धं** (suddhaṃ) = pure; **तं** (taṃ) = that one; **सुदकेवलिमिसिणो** (sudakevalimisiṇo) = sages/seers; **भणंति** (bhaṇaṃti) = call; **लोयप्पदीवयरं** (loyappadīvayaraṃ) = lamp/illuminator of the world.

### 5. COMMENTARY

These two gathas together define who a Shrutakevali is — and the definition is stunning in its simplicity.

A Shrutakevali is not someone who has memorized vast scriptures. A Shrutakevali is someone who, through scriptural knowledge, has turned inward and directly experienced the pure self. The scripture is the means; the self-realization is the end. One who merely recites scripture without self-realization is a scholar, not a Shrutakevali.

The title "lamp for the world" (lokpradipakara) is given because this person does not merely illuminate texts — they illuminate reality itself for others. Having seen the self directly, they can point others toward it.

Gatha 10 adds a deeper layer: the reason someone is called a Shrutakevali is that all knowledge is ultimately the soul itself. Knowledge is not something external that the soul acquires; it is the soul's own nature. The soul knows all things because knowing is what the soul is. When this is fully realized, the soul becomes a Shrutakevali — not by accumulating knowledge, but by recognizing that knowledge was always its own nature.

This is the ultimate meaning of "all knowledge is the soul": not that the soul contains a cosmic database, but that the nature of consciousness is to know, and this knowing, when unobstructed, is infinite.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A Shrutakevali is someone who uses scripture to directly experience their own pure soul — and becomes a light for the entire world. They are called this because all knowledge is ultimately just the soul's own nature.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is the knowledge you seek something to be gathered from outside — or is it something already within you, waiting to be uncovered?

## GATHA 11

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

व्यवहारो भूदत्थो भूदत्थो देसिदो दु सुद्धणओ।
भूदत्थमस्सिदो खलु सम्मादिट्ठी हवदि जीवो।।११।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

व्यवहारोऽभूतार्थः भूतार्थस्तु देशितः शुद्धनयः।
भूतार्थमाश्रितः खलु सम्यग्दृष्टिर्भवति जीवः।।११।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Vyavahara (the conventional standpoint) is abhutartha (not the ultimate reality); shuddhana (the pure standpoint) is bhutartha (the ultimate reality). The soul that takes refuge in bhutartha is truly samyagdrshti (one of right perception).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**व्यवहारो** = the conventional standpoint (vyavahara naya); **अभूतार्थः** = not the ultimate/real meaning; **शुद्धनयः** = the pure standpoint (shuddhana naya); **भूतार्थः** = the ultimate/real meaning; **भूतार्थमाश्रितः** = one who takes refuge in bhutartha; **सम्यग्दृष्टिः** = one of right perception; **जीवः** = the soul.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is one of the pivotal gathas of the Poorvarang — a direct declaration of the Samaysaar's central thesis. Kundkund states plainly: vyavahara is not the ultimate reality. Shuddhana — the pure standpoint that sees the soul as it truly is — is bhutartha, the real meaning.

The word *bhutartha* means "that which is as things actually are." Abhutartha means the conventional designation that is useful for communication but does not describe ultimate reality. Vyavahara naya serves a purpose — it is not false per se — but it describes the soul through its external relations: its bondage, its associated karma, its conduct in the world. Shuddhana naya cuts through all of that and sees the soul in its pure, unentangled nature.

The bold claim of this verse is that samyak-drshti — right perception, the first rung of liberation — requires taking refuge in bhutartha. Not merely accepting vyavahara descriptions as sufficient, but seeing through them to the soul's own pure nature. This is why the Samaysaar is a difficult and transformative text: it does not allow the seeker to rest in the comfortable conventional view. It demands the direct look at the soul itself.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The conventional view of the soul (as one who acts, accumulates karma, takes rebirths) is not the ultimate truth. The pure view — seeing the soul as it really is — is the ultimate truth. Only one who is grounded in this ultimate view has genuine right perception.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you think about yourself — your history, your relationships, your roles — which view are you taking? The conventional one that sees you through your external story? Or is there another way of seeing yourself entirely?

## GATHA 12

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सुद्धो सुद्धादेसो णादव्वो परमभावदरिसीहिं।
ववहारदेसिदा पुण जे दु अपरमे द्विदा भावे।।१२।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

शुद्धः शुद्धादेशः ज्ञातव्यः परमभावदर्शिभिः।
व्यवहारदेशितानि पुनः ये तु अपरमे स्थिता भावे।।१२।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The pure instruction (shuddhana-desha) is to be known by those who perceive the supreme state. The vyavahara instruction is for those who are situated in an imperfect state (not yet the supreme).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**शुद्धः शुद्धादेशः** = pure, the pure instruction; **परमभावदर्शिभिः** = by those who perceive the supreme state; **ज्ञातव्यः** = to be known; **व्यवहारदेशितानि** = vyavahara instructions; **अपरमे भावे स्थिताः** = those situated in an imperfect/non-supreme state.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having established that shuddhana is bhutartha in Gatha 11, Kundkund now clarifies: who receives which instruction? This gatha resolves what might otherwise seem like a contradiction — if vyavahara is not the ultimate truth, why is it taught at all?

The answer is compassionate and practical. Those who have already attained the direct perception of the soul's supreme nature — the paramabhava-darshin — can and should be instructed through shuddhana. They are ready for it; the pure view aligns with their direct experience. But those still on the path, not yet established in that direct vision, require vyavahara instruction. The conventional teaching of karma, conduct, and the nine tattvas serves as scaffolding that helps them approach what they cannot yet see directly.

There is no contradiction, then — only appropriate instruction for different levels of readiness. The mistake is to confuse the scaffolding for the building. Vyavahara is useful but provisional; shuddhana is the destination. The task of the seeker is to use conventional understanding as a ladder, then recognize the soul's nature directly and step off the ladder.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Pure (ultimate) teaching is for those who can already perceive the soul's supreme nature. Conventional teaching is for those still on the path. Both are given out of wisdom — one is the goal, the other is the means.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever used a tool so long that you forgot the tool was not the destination? Where might you be clinging to the means rather than seeing what they were always pointing toward?

## GATHA 13

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

भूदत्थेणाभिगदा जीवाजीवा य पुण्णपावं च।
आसवसंवरिणज्जरबंधो मोक्खो य सम्मत्तं।।१३।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

भूतार्थेनाभिगताः जीवाजीवाः च पुण्यपापं च।
आस्रवसंवरनिर्जरबन्धः मोक्षः च सम्यक्त्वम्।।१३।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When the nine tattvas — jiva, ajiva, punya, papa, asrava, samvar, nirjara, bandha, and moksha — are known through bhutartha (the ultimate standpoint), that knowing is samyaktva (right understanding).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**भूतार्थेन** = through the ultimate/real standpoint; **अभिगताः** = known/understood; **जीव** = soul; **अजीव** = non-soul; **पुण्य** = merit; **पाप** = demerit; **आस्रव** = influx of karma; **संवर** = stopping the influx; **निर्जरा** = shedding of karma; **बन्ध** = bondage; **मोक्ष** = liberation; **सम्यक्त्वम्** = right understanding/faith.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Samyaktva — right understanding — is the foundational realization that turns an ordinary soul toward liberation. But what makes understanding "right"? This gatha gives the precise answer: it is not merely knowing the nine tattvas as concepts. It is knowing them through bhutartha — the ultimate standpoint.

The nine tattvas are the basic categories of Jain philosophy. To know them conventionally (vyavahara) is to know their definitions, their interrelations, their doctrinal significance. Many learned people know them this way. But to know them through bhutartha is something else entirely: it means understanding each of them in terms of the soul's own pure nature. Jiva is not merely "the category of living beings" — it is your own consciousness, self-luminous and eternally free. Moksha is not merely "a state to be attained" — it is the soul's own nature revealed. Bandha is not an external burden added to you — it is what happens when consciousness mistakes itself for matter.

When the nine tattvas are seen this way — not as dry categories but as lived realities pointing back to the soul itself — that seeing is samyaktva. It is the beginning of the path to liberation.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Knowing the nine tattvas of Jain philosophy through the ultimate standpoint — understanding them as they truly are, in relation to the soul's own nature — is what constitutes genuine right understanding (samyaktva).

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you know the nature of your own consciousness the way you know a textbook definition? What would it mean to truly know something — not as a fact but as a direct reality?

## GATHA 14

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो पस्सदि अप्पाणं अबद्धपुदं अणण्णयं णियदं।
अविसेसमसंजुत्तं तं सुद्धणयं वियाणीहि।।१४।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यः पश्यति आत्मानम् अबद्धस्पृष्टम् अनन्यं नियतम्।
अविशेषमसंयुक्तं तं शुद्धनयं विजानीहि।।१४।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Know as shuddhana (the pure standpoint) that view which sees the self as: unbound-untouched by karma, not-other (one without a second), constant, undifferentiated, and unconnected.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**अबद्धस्पृष्टम्** = unbound and untouched (by karma); **अनन्यम्** = not-other, without a second, non-dual; **नियतम्** = constant, fixed, determined; **अविशेषम्** = undifferentiated, without distinction; **असंयुक्तम्** = unconnected, not joined to anything external; **शुद्धनयम्** = shuddhana, the pure standpoint.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkund here offers a precise technical definition of shuddhana naya through five attributes of the soul it perceives. This is not poetry — it is precise philosophical description.

The soul seen through shuddhana is: (1) unbound and untouched — karma may exist in proximity but does not actually penetrate or alter the soul's essential nature; (2) not-other — the soul has no second, no division; it is singular consciousness; (3) constant — it does not fluctuate, does not come and go; it is the steady background of all experience; (4) undifferentiated — in its pure nature, the soul does not split into "this part is defiled" and "that part is pure"; it is uniformly one; (5) unconnected — it is not truly joined to body, mind, karma, or any other substance at the ultimate level.

These five attributes together constitute what the pure standpoint actually sees. Any view that attributes binding, otherness, fluctuation, differentiation, or connection to the soul's essential nature is vyavahara at best — and at worst, a form of delusion. The soul is the knower, and the knower is never truly entangled with what it knows.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The pure standpoint is the one that sees the soul exactly as it is: unbound, singular, unchanging, undivided, and genuinely disconnected from all external matter. That seeing — of those five attributes — is shuddhana.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you locate a part of your awareness that is genuinely untouched by whatever is passing through it right now? Is the knowing itself affected by what it knows?

## GATHA 15

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो पस्सदि अप्पाणं अबद्धपुदं अणण्णमिवसेसं।
अपदेससंतमझं पस्सदि जिणसासणं सव्वं।।१५।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यः पश्यति आत्मानम् अबद्धस्पृष्टम् अनन्यमविशेषम्।
अप्रदेशसंस्थमध्यं पश्यति जिनशासनं सर्वम्।।१५।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One who sees the self as unbound-untouched, not-other, undifferentiated — permeating all pradesha (space-points), established within — that one sees the entire teaching of the Jinas in that single vision.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**अबद्धस्पृष्टम्** = unbound and untouched; **अनन्यम्** = not-other; **अविशेषम्** = undifferentiated; **अप्रदेशसंस्थमध्यम्** = permeating all space-points, centered within; **जिनशासनं सर्वम्** = the entire teaching of the Jinas.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha is a breathtaking claim: all of Jina's teaching — the entire Jain dharma in its depth and breadth — is contained within the vision of the soul's pure nature. The one who sees the soul as described in Gatha 14 has, in that seeing, grasped everything.

Why? Because all of Jina's teaching ultimately points to one thing: the nature of the soul. The twelve angas, the fourteen purvas, the entire Agama — they are maps. The territory they map is the soul's pure nature. One who has directly seen the territory has no need to guess at the maps. They know, from the inside, what the maps were pointing at.

This is not an invitation to abandon scripture or scholarship. It is a statement about the purpose of all religious and philosophical learning: it exists to bring the seeker to direct self-knowledge. When that vision arrives, the seeker doesn't abandon the teaching — they fulfill it. They become a living embodiment of what the Jinas were pointing at with every word.

The mention of the soul permeating all its own pradesha (space-points) while remaining centered within is also significant: the soul is not a point but a field of consciousness that is simultaneously everywhere within its own nature and not anywhere outside it. This is the soul's intrinsic fullness.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Whoever directly sees the soul as unbound, singular, and undivided has, in that one vision, understood the entire teaching of the Jinas. All the scriptures are just different ways of pointing here.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What if the entire point of every teaching, text, and practice you've ever encountered was to bring you to one thing — a direct encounter with your own nature? How would you approach them differently?

## GATHA 16

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

दंसणणाणचिरित्ताणि सेवदव्वाणि साहुणा णिच्चं।
ताणि पुण जाण तिण्णि वि अप्पाणं चेव णिच्छयदो।।१६।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

दर्शनज्ञानचारित्राणि सेवितव्यानि साधुना नित्यम्।
तानि पुनर्जानीहि त्रीण्यपि आत्मानमेव निश्चयतः।।१६।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Right perception (darshan), right knowledge (jnana), and right conduct (charitra) should always be practiced by the seeker. But know from nishchaya that all three of these ARE the soul itself.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**दर्शनज्ञानचारित्राणि** = right perception, knowledge, and conduct (the three jewels); **सेवितव्यानि** = to be practiced/cultivated; **नित्यम्** = always; **निश्चयतः** = from the nishchaya standpoint; **आत्मानमेव** = the soul itself.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The three jewels of Jain practice — samyak darshan, samyak jnana, samyak charitra — are not external behaviors layered onto the soul. From the ultimate standpoint, they are the soul's own nature. The soul's intrinsic being is perception (darshan is the soul's pure seeing); the soul's intrinsic activity is knowing (jnana is what the soul actually is); the soul's intrinsic state is already-established correctness (charitra is the natural mode of the soul that is not being distorted by delusion).

This creates a paradox that is actually the heart of the teaching: you practice the three jewels in order to realize that the soul has always been the three jewels. The practice is not adding something new. It is removing the obscurations that prevent the soul's own darshan, jnana, and charitra from shining as they naturally are.

This is why the Samaysaar does not say "abandon practice." It says: practice, but understand what you are practicing. You are not constructing virtue from scratch. You are clearing away the karma-fog that has been making you forget that you have always been the very thing you seek.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Right perception, knowledge, and conduct should be practiced always — but know from the ultimate standpoint that these three are not external achievements. They are the soul's own nature. Practice is the process of uncovering what was always already there.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there something you are practicing in order to become? What would happen if you considered that you might already be, at the deepest level, what you are trying to become — and that practice is simply the clearing of what obscures it?

## GATHA 17

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जह णाम को वि पुरिसो रायाणं जाणिऊण सद्दहदि।
तो तं अणुचरदि पुणो अत्थत्थीओ पयत्तेण।।१७।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यथा नाम कोऽपि पुरुषः राजानं ज्ञात्वा श्रद्धत्ते।
ततस्तमनुचरति पुनः अर्थार्थी प्रयत्नेन।।१७।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as someone seeking wealth first learns about a king, then believes in him, and then diligently serves him in order to obtain what they seek —

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**राजानम्** = the king; **ज्ञात्वा** = having known; **श्रद्धत्ते** = has faith in; **अर्थार्थी** = one seeking wealth/benefit; **प्रयत्नेन** = with diligence; **अनुचरति** = serves/follows.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gathas 17 and 18 form a paired analogy — one of the most memorable in the entire Poorvarang. Kundkund is explaining the relationship between jnana (knowing), shraddha (faith/belief), and charitra (conduct) using a concrete worldly example before applying it to the soul-seeker.

A person who wants wealth but knows nothing about the king cannot serve him. Knowledge comes first. But knowledge without faith is sterile — the person must also believe that the king can actually deliver what is promised. Faith without action is also insufficient — they must then actually serve, persistently and diligently. All three are necessary, in sequence, and mutually supporting.

This analogy maps exactly onto the Jain moksha-path: first, know the soul (jnana); then, have faith in the soul's nature as described (shraddha/darshan); then, align one's conduct with that knowing and believing (charitra). Gatha 18 completes the application.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

This is the first half of an analogy: a person seeking wealth first learns about the king, believes in him, then serves him diligently. This will be applied to the moksha-seeker in the next gatha.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

In your own life, how often do you try to act before you understand? Or believe without having genuinely investigated? What changes when all three — knowing, believing, and acting — are in alignment?

## GATHA 18

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एवं हि जीवराया णादव्वो तह य सद्दहेदव्वो।
अणुचरिदव्वो य पुणो सो चेव दु मोक्खकामेण।।१८।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

एवं हि जीवराजः ज्ञातव्यः तथा च श्रद्धेयः।
अनुचरितव्यश्च पुनः स एव तु मोक्षकामेन।।१८।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

In the same way, the soul-king must be known, must be believed in, and must be followed — by one who desires liberation.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**जीवराजः** = the soul-king (the soul in its royal, sovereign nature); **ज्ञातव्यः** = must be known; **श्रद्धेयः** = must be believed in; **अनुचरितव्यः** = must be followed/practiced; **मोक्षकामेन** = by one who desires liberation.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The analogy from Gatha 17 is now completed and the application is made explicit. The soul itself is the king. Not any external authority — no guru, no tirthankara, no scripture — but the soul in its own sovereign, luminous nature. This is what must be known, believed, and followed.

"Knowing" the soul-king means genuine self-inquiry: not simply accepting the doctrine "I am a jiva" but actually investigating the nature of consciousness, of awareness, of the knowing quality that is always present. "Believing" means that once glimpsed or rationally understood, the seeker holds that vision with faith — does not abandon it when doubts arise or the world distracts. "Following" means aligning one's entire conduct, inner and outer, with the soul's own nature — not acting from delusion, desire, or identification with body and karma.

The phrase "jiva-raja" — soul-king — is worth dwelling on. A king is sovereign: self-governing, not subject to external control. The soul is described as a king because, in its pure nature, it governs itself by its own nature, not by the dictates of karma, sense, or social conditioning. Liberation is the restoration of this sovereignty.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For the one who seeks liberation: know the soul, believe in the soul's pure nature, and align your life with that knowing. This — knowing, believing, following — is the complete path.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean to treat your own awareness as a sovereign king rather than a servant of your desires and fears? What would change in how you live?

## GATHA 19

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

कम्मे णोकम्मि ह य अहमिदि अहकं च कम्म णोकम्म।
जा एसा खलु बुद्धी अप्पिडिबुद्धो हवदि ताव।।१९।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

कर्मणि नोकर्मणि च अहमिति अहं च कर्म नोकर्म।
या एषा खलु बुद्धिः अप्रतिबुद्धो भवति तावत्।।१९।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

As long as someone holds the understanding "I am karma, I am nokamma" and "karma and nokamma are mine" — for as long as that understanding persists, that soul is apratibhuddha (unawakened).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**कर्मणि** = in karma (the eight types of karmic matter); **नोकर्मणि** = in nokamma (body, senses, mind — secondary physical matter); **अहम्** = I; **एषा बुद्धिः** = this understanding/identification; **अप्रतिबुद्धः** = unawakened, not yet enlightened.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Here Kundkund defines, with surgical precision, what spiritual ignorance actually is: the identification of the self with karma and nokamma. Not a vague confusion, but this specific error — believing "I am karma, I am body, I am mind" and "these things are mine."

Karma (the eight types of karmic matter that bind the soul in various ways) and nokamma (the body, sense organs, and mind — the physical apparatus associated with the soul) are both pudgala — matter. They are not the soul. But when consciousness forgets its own nature, it latches onto this associated matter and says: "This is me. This is mine."

The word *apratibhuddha* — unawakened — is precise. It means not yet turned toward the soul's own light. The unawakened soul is not evil; it is simply mistaken about what it is. The mistake is so old, so habitual, so constantly reinforced by sensory experience that it feels like identity itself. The work of the Samaysaar is to loosen this identification — to create the conditions in which the soul turns and looks at itself rather than at what it has been confused with.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

As long as someone identifies with their karma and body — thinking "I am these things, these things are mine" — that person remains spiritually unawakened. The identification is the ignorance, not any external condition.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Right now, what do you consider to be "you"? Your body? Your thoughts? Your past? Your relationships? Watch carefully: the one who is watching all of these — is that also on the list?

## GATHA 20

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहमेदं एदमहं अदमेदस्स म्हि अत्थि मम एदं।
अण्णं जं परदव्वं सचित्ताचित्तमिस्सं वा।।२०।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

अहमेतद् एतदहम् अतः एतस्य अस्मि अस्ति मम एतत्।
अन्यद् यत् परद्रव्यं सचित्ताचित्तमिश्रं वा।।२०।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"I am this; this is me; therefore I belong to this; this belongs to me" — said about any other substance (paradravya), whether sentient (sachitta), insentient (achitta), or mixed — this is the mark of the ajnani.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**अहम् एतद्** = I am this; **एतद् अहम्** = this is me; **एतस्य अस्मि** = I belong to this; **मम एतत्** = this belongs to me; **परद्रव्यम्** = other substance; **सचित्त** = with consciousness (other living beings); **अचित्त** = inert (objects, body); **मिश्रम्** = mixed.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gatha 20 maps the exact geography of the ajnani's (ignorant one's) inner world. The four-fold identification — "I am this / this is me / I belong to this / this is mine" — is the structure of all attachment. Kundkund is not describing a philosophical position; he is describing the actual psychological texture of the unawakened mind.

Notice that the identification runs in all directions: outward (this is mine), inward (I am this), possessive (I belong to this), and bilateral (this belongs to me). The confusion is complete and circular. And it applies to all types of other substances — not merely the obviously physical (achitta: body, possessions) but also the relational (sachitta: other living beings one is attached to) and the mixed (miśra: relationships, social identities).

This is a radical analysis. Even identification with loved ones — "my child, my teacher, my community" — is, from the ultimate standpoint, a form of ajnana when the soul believes that its own identity is defined by these relationships. Not because relationships are bad, but because the soul's nature transcends all of them.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The mark of the unawakened soul is identifying with anything other than itself: "I am this body, these relationships, these possessions" — and the reverse, "these things are mine." This fourfold identification is the fundamental structure of spiritual ignorance.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Trace the ways you say "mine" today — not just about objects, but about ideas, opinions, relationships, roles. What remains if all of those claims are set aside?

## GATHA 21

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

आसि मम पुव्वमेदं एदस्स अहं पि आसि पुव्वं हि।
होहिदि पुणो ममेदं एदस्स अहं पि होस्सामि।।२१।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

आसीत् मम पूर्वमेतत् एतस्य अहमपि आसं पूर्वमेव।
भविष्यति पुनः मम एतत् एतस्य अहमपि भविष्यामि।।२१।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"This was mine before; I was this before. This will be mine again; I will be this again." — This false identification extended across time is the ajnani's delusion.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**पूर्वम्** = before/in the past; **आसीत्** = was; **भविष्यति** = will be; **अहमपि** = I also; **एतस्य** = of this/belonging to this.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gatha 21 extends the analysis of Gatha 20 into the time dimension. The ajnani's identification with non-self is not merely a present-moment confusion — it extends backward into the past and forward into the future. "We were together before; we will be together again." "I have always been like this; this is who I fundamentally am."

This temporal projection of identity is especially insidious because it feels like depth, loyalty, or self-knowledge. "I've been this way my whole life" feels like self-awareness. "We are meant for each other across lifetimes" feels like profound connection. From the standpoint of nishchaya, both are extensions of the basic delusion: projecting the soul's identity onto what is not the soul.

The commentary notes that this applies not only to attachment to other beings across time, but to the soul's own accumulated story about itself. The "self" that was this way in childhood, that has been consistent across years, that will continue into the future — all of this is a constructed narrative, not the soul's actual nature. The soul's actual nature is timeless awareness, not a life-story.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What story do you carry about who you "have always been"? What would it mean to question not just your current identity but the entire historical narrative you call "me"?

## GATHA 22

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

एयं तु असब्भूदं आदियप्पं करेदि समूढो।
भूदत्थं जाणंतो ण करेदि दु तं असमूढो।।२२।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

एतत्तु असद्भूतम् आत्मार्पणं करोति संमूढः।
भूतार्थं जानन् न करोति तु तद् असंमूढः।।२२।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The deluded (sammudha) makes this false self-attribution (asadbhuta atma-arpana) — attributing non-self to the self. But the undeluded (asammudha) who knows bhutartha does not do this.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**असद्भूतम्** = not real, false; **आत्मार्पणम्** = self-attribution, identifying as "mine"; **संमूढः** = the deluded one; **भूतार्थम्** = the ultimate/real truth; **असंमूढः** = the undeluded one.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha draws the clean line: the entire pattern described in Gathas 20–21 — the fourfold identification, the temporal extension of false identity — is *asadbhuta atma-arpana*. The word "asadbhuta" means "not as it actually is" — a false ascription of selfhood. The delusion is not in the objects themselves (body, relationships, karma are real in their own domain) but in the soul's mistaken claim upon them as "self."

The contrast is equally precise: the one who knows bhutartha (ultimate reality) simply does not do this. Not "tries hard not to" or "struggles with" — simply does not. This is because genuine self-knowledge, once stable, makes the false identification impossible to maintain. You cannot sincerely claim "I am this body" after having genuinely seen that you are pure consciousness. The knowing itself is the remedy. No separate act of will is needed; the right seeing automatically dissolves the false seeing.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The deluded soul falsely claims non-self as self. The one who knows the ultimate truth simply does not make this error — not through effort, but because genuine knowing makes the false identification impossible.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever had a moment of clarity where you could clearly see yourself not being your thoughts? Not trying to distance yourself from them — but simply seeing that you are the seer, not the seen? What happened in that moment?

## GATHA 23

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अण्णाणमोहिदमदी मज्झिमणं भणदि पोग्गलं दव्वं।
बद्धमबद्धं च तहा जीवो बहुभावसंजुत्तो।।२३।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

अज्ञानमोहितमतिः मयोयमिति भणति पुद्गलद्रव्यम्।
बद्धमबद्धं च तथा जीवः बहुभावसंयुक्तः।।२३।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The one whose understanding is deluded by ignorance says: "This pudgala substance is mine." And similarly, "the jiva is bound and unbound, connected with many states."

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**अज्ञानमोहितमतिः** = one whose intellect is deluded by ignorance; **पुद्गलद्रव्यम्** = pudgala (physical matter); **मयोयम्** = "this is mine"; **बद्धम् अबद्धम्** = bound and unbound; **बहुभावसंयुक्तः** = connected with many states.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkund now gets specific about what the ajnani actually says and believes. Two claims emerge from ignorance-deluded thinking: (1) pudgala is "mine" — the body, senses, physical objects are identified as belonging to the soul; (2) the jiva is described as "bound and unbound, connected with many states."

The second claim is subtler. An ajnani in the religious context might know perfectly well the theory of karma and liberation — might even teach it — but still, in their understanding, attribute to the soul the quality of being bound-and-unbound, fluctuating through various karmic states. From nishchaya, this is still a form of ignorance: the soul's pure nature is neither bound nor unbound. Bondage and liberation are conventional descriptions of the soul's relationship to karma, but from the ultimate view, the soul's intrinsic nature has never actually changed. It has never been diminished by bondage.

Gathas 24 and 25 will refute these claims. But first, Kundkund lets the ajnani speak their position fully so it can be met directly.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The ignorance-deluded person claims: "This physical matter is mine" and "the soul is in various states of bondage and liberation." Both claims mistake the conventional description of the soul for the soul's actual nature.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you ever describe yourself using the language of how much progress you've made or haven't made spiritually? What would it mean to question even that framing?

## GATHA 24

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वण्हुणाणिदिट्ठो जीवो उवओगलक्खणो णिच्चं।
कह सो पोग्गलदव्वीभूदो जं भणसि मज्झिमणं।।२४।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

सर्वज्ञज्ञानदृष्टः जीवः उपयोगलक्षणः नित्यम्।
कथं सः पुद्गलद्रव्यीभूतः यद् भणसि मयोयमिति।।२४।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The soul, as seen in the knowledge of the omniscient, is eternally characterized by upayoga (conscious awareness). How can that soul have become pudgala — as you claim when you say "this is mine"?

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**सर्वज्ञज्ञानदृष्टः** = seen in the knowledge of the omniscient; **उपयोगलक्षणः** = characterized by upayoga (consciousness in application); **नित्यम्** = eternally, always; **पुद्गलद्रव्यीभूतः** = having become pudgala-substance.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The refutation begins with an appeal to the highest epistemic authority in Jain philosophy: the direct knowledge of the omniscient (sarvajnu). The Tirthankaras saw the soul's nature with perfect clarity. What did they see? The soul as *upayoga-lakshana* — characterized by conscious awareness, eternally. Not sometimes conscious, not conscious when not covered by karma — but eternally characterized by the quality of upayoga.

Given this, how can the soul "become" pudgala — even partly, even in a manner of speaking? Pudgala is matter: inert, devoid of consciousness, characterized by touch, taste, smell, color. The soul is consciousness itself. These two are not merely different in degree; they are different in kind. For the soul to become pudgala would be a categorical impossibility — like heat becoming cold or space becoming solid.

The question "how can that be?" is rhetorical — it is not a real question but a device to expose the incoherence of the ajnani's claim. The ajnani who says "this pudgala is mine" is, implicitly, claiming that the soul's nature has merged with matter. The omniscient's testimony refutes this at the root.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The omniscient saw that the soul is eternally consciousness — that is its defining characteristic. If that is so, how can the soul ever become matter? The claim "this material thing is mine" collapses on examination.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Is there anything in your experience that is actually more certain than the fact that you are aware right now? Can that awareness itself become any of the objects appearing within it?

## GATHA 25

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जदि सो पोग्गलदव्वीभूदो जीवत्तमागदं इदरं।
तो सक्को वत्तुं जे मज्झिमणं पोग्गलं दव्वं।।२५।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यदि सः पुद्गलद्रव्यीभूतः जीवत्वमागतम् इतरत्।
तदा शक्यं वक्तुं यत् मयोयमिति पुद्गलद्रव्यम्।।२५।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Only if the soul became pudgala AND pudgala became the soul would it be possible to say "this pudgala is mine." Since neither happens, the claim is impossible.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**पुद्गलद्रव्यीभूतः** = having become pudgala-substance; **जीवत्वम् आगतम्** = having attained the nature of jiva; **इतरत्** = the other (pudgala); **शक्यं वक्तुम्** = possible to say.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gatha 25 completes the logical refutation begun in Gatha 24. Kundkund shows that even if we were to entertain the possibility of the soul and pudgala exchanging natures — which is already philosophically absurd — it would still not justify the ajnani's claim. For "this pudgala is mine" to be coherent, you would need: (a) the soul to have actually become pudgala AND (b) pudgala to have actually become a soul. Even then, the possessive claim "mine" would be strange, since what is "mine" would now be what "I" am.

The point is that possession requires a distinction between possessor and possessed. The soul is the possessor; pudgala is the possessed. But possessor and possessed cannot be identical. The very logic of the ajnani's statement "this pudgala is mine" implies a distinction between self and the pudgala — which means the soul knows, at some level, that it is not the pudgala. The statement self-refutes.

Kundkund is not just making a philosophical point; he is showing that even the ajnani's grammar contains the seed of self-knowledge. The word "mine" implies a "me" who is different from what is claimed.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

For "this pudgala is mine" to make sense, the soul would have to literally become matter and matter would have to become soul — which is impossible. The claim defeats itself.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you say "my body," who is the "my"? Notice that the one saying "mine" seems to be distinct from the thing being claimed. Who or what is that one?

## GATHA 26

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जदि जीवो ण सरीरं तित्थयरायरियसंथुदी चेव।
सव्वा वि हवदि मिच्छा तेण दु आदा हवदि देहो।।२६।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यदि जीवः न शरीरं तीर्थंकराचार्यसंस्तुतिरेव।
सर्वापि भवति मिथ्या तेन तु आत्मा भवति देहः।।२६।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

(The ajnani argues:) If the soul is not the body, then all praise offered to Tirthankaras and acharyas becomes false. Therefore the soul must be the body.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**तीर्थंकराचार्यसंस्तुतिः** = praise offered to Tirthankaras and acharyas; **मिथ्या** = false; **आत्मा देहः** = soul is body.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Kundkund now introduces an objection — the voice of the ajnani making a counter-argument. This is a classic tarka (dialectical argument) format: state the opposing position fully and fairly before refuting it.

The objection is actually quite clever from a conventional standpoint: if the soul is truly different from the body, then when we bow to a Tirthankara or praise an acharya, we are bowing to a body — which is mere pudgala. The Tirthankara's qualities (omniscience, equanimity, liberation) would then be floating qualities unattached to anything we can see or relate to. Wouldn't all praise then be meaningless — praising an invisible soul while standing before a physical form?

This is the confusion that arises from not understanding the distinction between nishchaya and vyavahara. Gathas 27–30 will work through this distinction carefully, showing how praise of the Tirthankara is coherent from both standpoints — but in different ways.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The ajnani's objection: if soul and body are different, then bowing to the Tirthankara (a physical form) can't actually be bowing to the soul's qualities — so either all religious practice is meaningless, or the soul must really be the body.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Do you ever feel like spiritual distinctions — between real and conventional — make ordinary religious life seem less meaningful? How might both levels of truth be honored simultaneously?

## GATHA 27

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

ववहारणओ भासदि जीवो देहो य हवदि खलु एक्को।
ण दु णिच्छयस्स जीवो देहो य कदा वि एक्कट्ठो।।२७।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

व्यवहारनयः भाषते जीवः देहश्च भवति खलु एकः।
न तु निश्चयस्य जीवः देहश्च कदापि एकार्थः।।२७।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

From the vyavahara standpoint, it is said that jiva and body are indeed one. But from nishchaya, the jiva and the body are never the same substance — not at any time.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**व्यवहारनयः** = the conventional standpoint; **जीवः देहश्च एकः** = jiva and body are one; **निश्चयस्य** = from nishchaya; **एकार्थः** = of one substance/meaning; **कदापि** = at any time.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This is the pivotal verse resolving the objection raised in Gatha 26. Kundkund doesn't dismiss the conventional identification of soul and body — he locates it accurately. From vyavahara naya, jiva and body function together, appear together, and in ordinary discourse are referred to as one. "He is a great saint" means the person — the jiva-body unity — is great. This is not wrong at the conventional level; it is how language works.

But from nishchaya, jiva and body are *never* the same substance. Not sometimes, not in ordinary life — never. They are two entirely different categories of reality: jiva is consciousness, pudgala is matter. They share the same space (the body's space-points are co-occupied by the soul's space-points) but they do not merge in substance. Like two different types of fluid occupying the same container — they intermingle but remain distinct in their essential nature.

This is not mere philosophy. It has a practical consequence for how we relate to the world: when you act from the belief that you are the body, every physical experience is treated as happening to "you." When you know — even in principle — that you are the jiva and not the deha, the entire relationship to experience shifts.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Conventionally, soul and body are spoken of as one — that's fine for ordinary purposes. But from the ultimate standpoint, they have never been the same substance. Not once. Not ever.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Right now, your body is experiencing gravity, temperature, breath. The awareness that knows this — is it also subject to gravity? Can awareness be heavy, warm, or short of breath?

## GATHA 28

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

इणमण्णं जीवादो देहं पोग्गलमयं थुणित्तु मुणी।
मण्णदि हु संथुदो वंदिदो मए केवली भयवं।।२८।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

इदमन्यं जीवात् देहं पुद्गलमयं स्तुत्वा मुनिः।
मन्यते हि संस्तुतः वन्दितः मया केवली भगवान्।।२८।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

The sage praises this body of pudgala — knowing it to be distinct from the jiva — and thereby considers that he has praised and bowed to Kevali Bhagavan.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**जीवात् अन्यम्** = other from the jiva, distinct; **पुद्गलमयम् देहम्** = the pudgala-made body; **स्तुत्वा** = having praised; **संस्तुतः वन्दितः** = praised and bowed to; **केवली भगवान्** = the omniscient Lord.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Having established in Gatha 27 that from vyavahara, jiva and body are conventionally treated as one, Kundkund now shows how this vyavahara convention serves a legitimate purpose in religious practice. When a sage bows to and praises the Kevali — knowing all along that the body is pudgala, knowing that the soul and body are ultimately distinct — and yet the sage considers this act to be praise of Kevali Bhagavan, this is coherent vyavahara, not confusion.

The key phrase is "knowing it to be distinct from the jiva." The sage is not confused. The sage understands the ultimate standpoint. But the sage also understands that in the context of devotion and worship, the Kevali's body (pudgala form) serves as the focus for the bhakti that is actually directed toward the soul-qualities of omniscience, infinite bliss, and freedom. The body is the visible point of contact; the soul-qualities are what the praise actually honors.

This is not contradiction — it is the intelligent use of vyavahara in service of a nishchaya-oriented realization. Gathas 29 and 30 will complete this analysis.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A sage, knowing that the body is just pudgala and is distinct from the jiva, still praises the physical form of the Kevali — and considers this to be genuine praise of Kevali Bhagavan. This is the right use of conventional action.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you honor something or someone through a visible form while holding the awareness that what you're really honoring is beyond the form? What is the difference between this and confusion?

## GATHA 29

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

तं णिच्छये ण जुज्जदि ण सरीरगुणा हि होंति केवलिणो।
केवलिगुणे थुणदि जो सो तच्चं केवलिं थुणदि।।२९।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

तत् निश्चये न युज्यते न शरीरगुणाः हि भवन्ति केवलिनः।
केवलिगुणान् स्तुवन् यः सः तत्त्वं केवलिं स्तुवति।।२९।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

From nishchaya, this (body-praise) is not fitting — for body-qualities are not the Kevali's qualities. But one who praises the qualities of the Kevali (the soul-qualities) truly and essentially praises the Kevali.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**निश्चये** = from the nishchaya standpoint; **न युज्यते** = is not appropriate; **शरीरगुणाः** = body-qualities; **केवलिगुणान्** = qualities of the Kevali (omniscience, etc.); **तत्त्वं** = in truth, essentially.

### 5. COMMENTARY

The resolution reaches its nishchaya level here. While Gatha 28 affirmed the validity of vyavahara praise, this gatha states plainly: from nishchaya, body-praise does not apply to the Kevali. The Kevali's actual nature — omniscience (keval-jnana), infinite perception (keval-darshan), infinite power, infinite bliss — is not located in the body. These are soul-qualities, not body-qualities.

Therefore: whoever praises the Kevali's soul-qualities (infinite knowledge, pure consciousness, freedom from all karma) is truly praising the Kevali. This praise reaches its actual target. The body was just the visible form around which the devotion gathered, but the actual praise — even in conventional religious acts — is always trying to reach the soul-qualities. When the understanding is clear, the praise arrives where it was always meant to go.

This gatha simultaneously answers the ajnani's objection (Gatha 26) and completes the teaching on right worship: know what you are praising. The body is the occasion; the Kevali's pure consciousness is the content of all genuine religious devotion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

From the ultimate standpoint, body-qualities are not Kevali's qualities. Genuine praise of the Kevali means praising the Kevali's actual qualities — omniscience, purity, freedom. That praise truly reaches the Kevali.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you offer gratitude or devotion — to a teacher, a being, or the divine — what are you really honoring? The visible form, or what shines through it?

## GATHA 30

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णयरम्मि वण्णिदे जह ण वि रण्णो वण्णणा कदा होदि।
देहगुणे थुव्वंते ण केवलिगुणा थुदा होंति।।३०।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

नगरे वर्णिते यथा न खलु राज्ञः वर्णना कदापि भवति।
देहगुणे स्तूयमाने न केवलिगुणाः स्तुता भवन्ति।।३०।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as praising the city does not thereby praise the king — in the same way, praising the body's qualities does not mean the Kevali's qualities have been praised.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**नगरे वर्णिते** = when the city is described/praised; **राज्ञः वर्णना** = praise of the king; **देहगुणे स्तूयमाने** = when body-qualities are praised; **केवलिगुणाः** = qualities of the Kevali.

### 5. COMMENTARY

A precise and memorable analogy closes this section on worship. A king lives in a city. If you praise the city's architecture, its parks, its boulevards — you have not thereby praised the king. The king is not the city, though the king dwells there. Similarly, the Kevali's soul dwells in the body — but the body's qualities (its color, form, height, elegance) are not the Kevali's qualities. Praising the body does not reach the Kevali.

This analogy had an additional purpose in the original context: it directly answers the ajnani's objection raised in Gatha 26 (that if soul and body are different, religious praise is meaningless). Kundkund's answer: praise is not meaningless when directed correctly. Praise of the Kevali must be praise of the Kevali's actual nature — the omniscience, the infinite equanimity, the pure consciousness. That praise is not meaningless; it is the most meaningful act possible.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Praising a city doesn't praise the king. Praising the Kevali's body doesn't praise the Kevali. Real praise of the Kevali means praising the Kevali's actual nature — pure consciousness and omniscience.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Where in your life have you been praising or criticizing the city when you meant to address the king? Appearances, surface qualities, externals — what were they standing in for?

## GATHA 31

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो इंदिये जिणित्ता णाणसहावाधियं मुणिद आदं।
तं खलु जिदिंदियं ते भणंति जे णिच्छिदा साहू।।३१।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यः इन्द्रियाणि जित्वा ज्ञानस्वभावाधिकं जानात्यात्मानम्।
तं खलु जितेन्द्रियं ते भणन्ति ये निश्रिताः साधवः।।३१।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One who conquers the senses and knows the self as superior in the nature of knowledge — those sages who are established in nishchaya call that one "jitindriya" (conqueror of the senses).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**इन्द्रियाणि जित्वा** = having conquered the senses; **ज्ञानस्वभावाधिकम्** = superior/beyond in the quality of knowledge-nature; **आत्मानम् जानाति** = knows the self; **जितेन्द्रियम्** = conqueror of senses; **निश्रिताः साधवः** = sages established in nishchaya.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Here begins a remarkable series of gathas (31–33) that redefine three classical spiritual titles through the lens of nishchaya: jitindriya (conqueror of senses), jitamoha (conqueror of delusion), and kshinamoha (one whose delusion is destroyed). In each case, Kundkund does not deny that conventional conquest of the senses matters — but he locates the ultimate meaning of these titles in self-knowledge.

From the conventional standpoint, "jitindriya" means someone who has disciplined their sense organs — who does not eat excessively, whose eyes do not wander, whose ears are not captivated by entertainment, and so on. This is genuine and important. But the nishchaya definition adds depth: the true conqueror of the senses is one who knows the self as *jna-nasvabhavadhika* — as that which is fundamentally characterized by, and superior in, the quality of pure knowing. This soul does not merely suppress the senses; it has found something more real than what the senses offer. The conquest is not primarily external but experiential: having tasted the soul's own luminous nature, the pull of sensory distraction simply loses its grip.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

A "conqueror of the senses," from the ultimate standpoint, is not merely one who disciplines their behavior. It is one who has known the self as pure consciousness — and in that knowing, found something that makes sense-gratification pale in comparison.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

When you have ever been deeply absorbed in something genuinely real — a moment of clarity, deep peace, or pure presence — did sense-distraction even compete? What does that tell you about what "conquering the senses" actually means?

## GATHA 32

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जो मोहं तु जिणित्ता णाणसहावाधियं मुणिद आदं।
तं जिदमोहं साहुं परमद्वियाणया बेंति।।३२।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यः मोहं तु जित्वा ज्ञानस्वभावाधिकं जानात्यात्मानम्।
तं जितमोहं साधुं परमार्थविज्ञायकाः ब्रुवन्ति।।३२।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

One who conquers moha (delusion) and knows the self as superior in the nature of knowledge — those who know the ultimate truth call that sage "jitamoha" (conqueror of delusion).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**मोहम् जित्वा** = having conquered moha; **ज्ञानस्वभावाधिकम्** = superior in the nature of knowledge; **जितमोहम्** = conqueror of moha; **परमार्थविज्ञायकाः** = those who know the ultimate truth.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Moha — delusion — is the root of all spiritual bondage in Jain philosophy. The mohaniya karma is considered the most destructive, because it is delusion that causes all other mistakes: wrong identification, attachment, aversion, and the entire cycle of karma-accumulation. A "jitamoha" has conquered this root.

But what does conquering moha actually mean, from nishchaya? Again, Kundkund's answer is the same: it is inseparable from knowing the self through its knowledge-nature. Moha is conquered not merely by willpower or ascetic practice but by *seeing clearly*. When the soul sees itself as it is — pure consciousness, distinct from karma, eternally free in its nature — the ground of moha is removed. Delusion cannot survive genuine self-knowledge any more than darkness can survive the lighting of a lamp.

The commentary notes that a jitamoha has subdued moha through self-nature contemplation (svabhava-bhavana). They have not necessarily destroyed moha's root entirely (that is kshinamoha, the next stage) — but they have risen above it to the degree that it no longer governs their experience.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The "conqueror of delusion" from the ultimate standpoint is one who has known the self's knowledge-nature — and in that knowing, found that delusion no longer has the same power to mislead.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

What would it mean for moha — the sense of confusion about what you are — to lose its grip? Not suppressed, but simply dissolved by seeing clearly?

## GATHA 33

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जिदमोहस्स दु जइया खीणो मोहो हवेज्ज साहुस्स।
तइया हु खीणमोहो भण्णिद सो णिच्छयविदूहिं।।३३।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

जितमोहस्य तु यदा क्षीणो मोहो भवेत् साधोः।
तदा खलु क्षीणमोहः भण्यते सः निश्चयविद्भिः।।३३।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

When the moha of the jitamoha sage is completely extinguished (kshina) — at that point, those who know nishchaya call that sage "kshinamoha" (one whose delusion has been utterly destroyed).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**क्षीणः मोहः** = moha completely extinguished/destroyed; **निश्चयविद्भिः** = by those who know nishchaya; **क्षीणमोहः** = one whose moha is ksheena (utterly gone).

### 5. COMMENTARY

The progression is now complete: from jitindriya (who has known the self beyond the senses) to jitamoha (who has conquered moha through self-knowledge) to kshinamoha (in whom moha has been utterly destroyed). This is not merely a three-stage spiritual ranking — it is a map of how self-knowledge deepens into liberation.

Kshinamoha represents a qualitative threshold: moha has not merely been suppressed or reduced — it has been *ksheen*, completely exhausted and eliminated at its root. This corresponds to the 12th gunasthana in Jain philosophy — the state in which mohaniya karma has been fully destroyed, and the soul is now in an irreversible trajectory toward keval-jnana (omniscience).

The commentary explains that this destruction of moha comes when the jitamoha sage's own self-nature contemplation (svabhava-bhavana) becomes so profound and continuous that the karmic substrate of moha has no opportunity to regenerate. When moha's bhavak-bhava (the inner manifestation of moha) ceases utterly, the karma itself follows. The soul then becomes kshinamoha — and those who truly understand nishchaya recognize this as its own distinct, irreversible attainment.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

When the sage who conquered moha has had it completely destroyed — not subdued but eliminated — that sage is called "kshinamoha" by those who know the ultimate standpoint. This is the irreversible threshold before omniscience.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

There is a difference between suppressing a wrong belief and having it simply fall away because you have seen clearly. Have you ever had a belief dissolve — not through effort but through insight? What was that like?

## GATHA 34

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

सव्वे भावे जम्हा पच्चखाई परे ति णादूणं।
तम्हा पच्चखाणं णाणं णियमा मुणेदव्वं।।३४।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

सर्वान् भावान् यस्मात् प्रत्याख्याति परे इति ज्ञात्वा।
तस्मात् प्रत्याख्यानं ज्ञानं नियमात् ज्ञातव्यम्।।३४।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Because the knowing one renounces all states (bhavas) by knowing them as "other" (para) — therefore, necessarily, knowledge itself is to be understood as pratyakhyana (renunciation).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**सर्वान् भावान्** = all states/modes; **परे इति ज्ञात्वा** = knowing them as "other"; **प्रत्याख्याति** = renounces; **प्रत्याख्यानम्** = renunciation; **ज्ञानम्** = knowledge; **नियमात्** = necessarily, as a rule.

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha introduces one of the most radical philosophical moves in the Poorvarang: the equation of jnana (knowledge) with pratyakhyana (renunciation). In Jain practice, pratyakhyana is typically understood as the formal vow of renunciation — giving up certain foods, activities, or possessions. But Kundkund cuts to the root: real renunciation is the knowing itself.

Why? Because the knowing one, by the very act of knowing all states as "other" — as para, as not-self — has already renounced them. Not through a formal declaration, not through external restraint, but through the clarity of seeing. When you know clearly that the anger arising in you is a state of pudgala-origin (mohaniya karma's manifestation), not the soul's nature — you have not merely suppressed the anger. You have genuinely not identified with it. That non-identification is pratyakhyana. Knowledge performs the renunciation by clearly perceiving what is other.

This is not a dismissal of formal vows — they are valuable precisely because they externalize and stabilize what knowledge does internally. But the source of all authentic renunciation is this: seeing everything that is not the soul as "other," and thereby releasing it.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Real renunciation is not primarily about giving up objects. It is about knowledge — seeing all states and objects as genuinely "other" than the self. That seeing is itself the renunciation. Knowledge and renunciation are the same act.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Have you ever let go of something — an opinion, a resentment, a role — not through effort but simply because you saw clearly that it was not actually yours? What made that release different from forced letting go?

## GATHA 35

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

जह णाम को वि पुरिसो परदव्विमिणं ति जाणिदुं चयिद।
तह सव्वे परभावे णाऊण विमुंचदे णाणी।।३५।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

यथा नाम कोऽपि पुरुषः परद्रव्यमिदमिति ज्ञात्वा त्यजति।
तथा सर्वान् परभावान् ज्ञात्वा विमुञ्चते ज्ञानी।।३५।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

Just as a person, knowing "this belongs to another," lets it go — in the same way the jnani (wise one), knowing all other-states as "other," releases them.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**परद्रव्यम् इदम्** = "this belongs to another"; **ज्ञात्वा त्यजति** = knowing, renounces; **परभावान्** = other-states (states not of the soul's nature); **विमुञ्चते** = releases, lets go; **ज्ञानी** = the jnani.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gatha 35 gives a concrete analogy to illuminate Gatha 34's abstract principle. The example is beautifully simple: if someone picks up an object and then discovers it belongs to someone else, they simply put it down. No struggle, no grief, no elaborate ceremony of letting go. The knowledge that "this is not mine" is sufficient to release the claim. The renunciation is already accomplished in the knowing.

The commentary elaborates this with a vivid image: it is like someone who, confused, has put on another person's garment and is sleeping thinking it is their own. When awakened and told firmly "this garment is someone else's" — they examine it, confirm it, and immediately give it back. The knowledge "this is other's" does the work instantly. No one who clearly knows "this is another's" continues to cling to it.

The jnani's relationship to all paranature states — moha, karma, body, emotions, mental formations — is identical. Seeing them as genuinely other than the soul, the jnani does not cling. The release is not forced; it flows from clarity.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

Just as you'd immediately release an object you discovered belongs to someone else, the jnani — knowing all non-self states as genuinely "other" — naturally releases them. Knowing clearly is releasing completely.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Imagine waking up wearing someone else's heavy coat in summer. Once you clearly knew it was theirs, would you hesitate to take it off? What coats might you be wearing that are not yours?

## GATHA 36

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णत्थि मम को वि मोहो बुज्झिद उवओग एव अहमेक्को।
तं मोहिणिम्मत्तं समयस्स वियाणया बेंति।।३६।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

नास्ति मम कोऽपि मोहः बुध्यते उपयोग एवाहमेकः।
तं मोहनिर्ममत्वं समयस्य विज्ञायकाः ब्रुवन्ति।।३६।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"No moha whatsoever is mine; I understand: I am one — upayoga alone." Those who know the Samaya (the soul) call this understanding "moha-nimmatta" — being free of attachment-claim toward moha.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**मोहः नास्ति मम** = no moha belongs to me; **उपयोगः एव अहम् एकः** = I am one — upayoga alone; **मोहनिर्ममत्वम्** = freedom from attachment-claim (mamata) toward moha; **समयस्य विज्ञायकाः** = those who know the Samaya (soul/doctrine).

### 5. COMMENTARY

This gatha articulates the soul's actual first-person experience of self-knowledge in the most direct language of the Poorvarang so far: "I am one — upayoga alone." Not "I have upayoga" or "upayoga is my quality" — but "I AM upayoga." This is the complete identification of the self with pure conscious awareness, stripped of all other attributions.

Upayoga is the Jain technical term for consciousness in operation — the soul's capacity to know and perceive. It is what the soul fundamentally IS. The statement "upayoga eva aham ekah" — "I am one, upayoga alone" — is therefore not a philosophical claim being adopted but a description of what the soul actually experiences when it recognizes its own nature.

The result is "moha-nimmatta" — a compound meaning "free of mamata (possessive identification) toward moha." The soul does not suppress moha from the outside; it simply does not include moha in its "I." Moha is seen as other, and therefore the soul has no possessive claim on it and feels no possessive claim from it. This is liberation from moha's power, even while moha-karma may technically still be present in the process of exhaustion.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The soul that knows itself says: "No moha is mine — I am one, pure awareness alone." Those who truly understand the soul's nature call this "freedom from identification with moha." Not suppression — non-identification.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you say "I am awareness alone" and mean it — not as a belief to hold, but as a recognition of what is actually present? What would shift if that were truly your primary identity?

## GATHA 37

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

णत्थि मम धम्मआदी बुज्झिद उवओग एव अहमेक्को।
तं धम्मणिम्मत्तं समयस्स वियाणया बेंति।।३७।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

नास्ति मम धर्मादिः बुध्यते उपयोग एवाहमेकः।
तं धर्मनिर्ममत्वं समयस्य विज्ञायकाः ब्रुवन्ति।।३७।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

"No dharma-substance etc. is mine; I understand: I am one — upayoga alone." Those who know the Samaya call this "dharma-nimmatta" — freedom from possessive identification with dharma-dravya and all other substances.

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**धर्मादिः** = dharma and other substances (dharma, adharma, akasha, kala, pudgala, and other jivas); **नास्ति मम** = not mine; **धर्मनिर्ममत्वम्** = freedom from possessive identification with dharma-dravya.

### 5. COMMENTARY

Gatha 37 extends the principle of Gatha 36 from moha to the entire universe of "other substances" — dharma, adharma, akasha, kala, pudgala, and other jivas. The Jain ontology recognizes six types of dravya (substances). Only one of them is the self: the jiva. All others — including dharma (the medium of motion), adharma (the medium of rest), space (akasha), time (kala), and matter (pudgala) — are genuinely other.

The soul that truly knows itself does not make a possessive claim on any of these. Not even the good ones: not dharma (not even the religious-ethical substrate of the universe), not the soul's own space-occupancy (akasha), not time (kala). This is total non-possessiveness — not as an ascetic achievement but as the natural expression of self-knowledge. When you know you are pure consciousness, nothing in the entire universe of non-consciousness is "yours."

The commentary notes that "dharma" here refers to the dravya dharma (dharma as a cosmic substance, one of the six dravyas) rather than dharma as righteous conduct. The gatha is making an ontological claim, not an ethical one. All six dravyas — the entire universe — are other than the jiva. And the jiva that knows this is free.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The soul that knows itself says: "Nothing outside my own nature — not even the great substances of the universe — is mine. I am upayoga alone." This recognition of universal non-possessiveness flows from knowing what you truly are.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

If you are pure awareness, what can actually belong to you — other than awareness itself? What does it feel like to hold the entire universe as "other" rather than as something to claim, control, or own?

## GATHA 38

### 1. PRAKRIT TEXT

अहमेक्को खलु सुद्धो दंसणणाणमइओ सदारूवी।
ण वि अत्थि मज्झ किंचि वि अण्णं परमाणुमेत्तं पि।।३८।।

### 2. SANSKRIT CHHAYA

अहमेकः खलु शुद्धः दर्शनज्ञानमयः सदाऽरूपी।
नापि अस्ति मम किञ्चिदपि अन्यत् परमाणुमात्रमपि।।३८।।

### 3. LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION

I alone am truly pure, consisting of darshana and jnana, ever-formless. There is nothing at all that is other to me — not even a single paramanu (atom).

### 4. ANVAYARTHA

**अहम् एकः** = I alone; **शुद्धः** = pure; **दर्शनज्ञानमयः** = consisting of darshana (perception) and jnana (knowledge); **सदा अरूपी** = ever-formless, eternally without physical form; **परमाणुमात्रम् अपि** = even a single paramanu (the smallest unit of matter); **न अस्ति** = does not exist (as other to me).

### 5. COMMENTARY

The Poorvarang closes — after 38 gathas of philosophical argument, analogy, refutation, and progressive deepening — with this verse of pure first-person self-declaration. It is not a doctrine. It is a voice. The voice of the soul that has genuinely arrived at self-knowledge, speaking from within its own recognition.

"I alone" — ekah: singular, undivided, without a second. "Truly pure" — shuddhah: not relatively pure or becoming pure, but intrinsically pure. "Consisting of darshana and jnana" — the soul is nothing other than perceiving and knowing; these are not attributes it has but what it is. "Ever-formless" — sada arupi: not formless only in liberation but always, eternally; the soul has never had physical form as its nature, even when associated with bodies. "Not even a paramanu" — not the smallest atom belongs to it or constitutes it. The soul is entirely itself — pure, undivided, luminous, formless consciousness.

This is the culmination toward which the entire Poorvarang has been building. Every gatha — from the definition of samaya in Gatha 1 to the analysis of jitindriya and kshinamoha, from the refutation of the ajnani's confusion to the equation of knowledge with renunciation — has been clearing the ground for this final recognition. The Poorvarang ends not with a logical conclusion but with a living realization, given in first-person: *I am this*.

### 6. SIMPLY PUT

The Poorvarang's closing voice: "I alone am pure, made of knowing and perceiving, eternally formless. Nothing whatsoever — not even an atom — is other to me or mine." This is the soul speaking from complete self-knowledge. The entire Poorvarang was preparation for this recognition.

### 7. CONTEMPLATE

Can you rest — even briefly — in this statement as a recognition rather than a belief? "I am pure awareness. I am formless. Nothing outside my own nature constitutes me." What remains when all else is set aside?

**Translation Notes:**
- Gathas 1–10 cover the opening philosophical framework: the nature of samaya (self/doctrine), the two-fold nature of the soul, oneness, the relationship between conventional and ultimate truth, and the definition of a Shrutakevali.
- Gathas 11–38 complete the Poorvarang, moving through: the bhutartha vs. abhutartha distinction; the five attributes of shuddhana; knowing the nine tattvas through bhutartha; the three jewels as soul-nature; the king analogy for the moksha path; the ajnani's fourfold identification; refutation of soul-pudgala confusion; the city-king analogy for right worship; and the final three stages of jitindriya, jitamoha, and kshinamoha. The Poorvarang closes with Gatha 38's first-person declaration of pure self-knowledge.
- Source: Samaysaar PDF (AtmaDharma.com Version 2), pages 35–115 (PDF numbering).
- This translation is based on the Prakrit gatha text, the Sanskrit chhaya, and the Hindi tika/bhavart commentary as found in the source PDF.
