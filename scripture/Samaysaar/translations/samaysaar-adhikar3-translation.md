# Samaysaar — Adhikar 3: Punya-Paap (Merit & Demerit)
## Translation: Gathas 145–163

**Scripture:** Samayasāra (समयसार)
**Author:** Ācārya Kundakunda
**Commentary (Ātmakhyāti Tīkā):** Ācārya Amṛtacandra
**Chapter:** Adhikar 3 — Punya-Paap (पुण्य-पाप अधिकार / Merit and Demerit)
**Theme:** Both shubha (meritorious) and ashubha (demeritorious) karma equally bind the soul. The seeker of moksha must transcend both. Only jñāna — not puṇya — is the cause of liberation.

---

## THEMATIC OVERVIEW

Adhikar 3 delivers one of the most radical and misunderstood teachings of the Samayasāra: **puṇya (merit) is not the path to moksha**. It binds just as surely as pāpa (sin), only with a golden chain rather than an iron one. The adhikar systematically dismantles the common assumption that accumulating merit through rituals, vows, austerities, and good deeds leads to liberation. Only the soul established in its own paramārtha (ultimate nature) — not in outer observances — can attain nirvāṇa. The chapter closes with a precise threefold analysis: mithyātva obstructs samyaktva, ajñāna obstructs jñāna, and kaṣāya obstructs cāritra — showing karma as the soul's active obstruction, not merely an external coating.

## PART 1: The Equal Binding Power of Shubha and Ashubha

### GATHA 145

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
कम्ममसुहं कुसीलं सुहकम्मं चावि जाणह सुसीलं।
कह तं होिद सुसीलं जं संसारं पवेसेिद।।१४५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
कर्मापशुभं कुशीलं शुभकर्म चापि जानीहि सुशीलम्।
कथं तद्भवति सुशीलं यत्संसारं प्रवेशयति।।१४५।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Know that ashubha karma is kuśīla (of bad nature) and shubha karma is suśīla (of good nature). But how can something be truly suśīla when it sends one into samsāra?

**ANVAYARTHA:**
*कर्म अशुभं* = ashubha karma; *कुशीलं* = kuśīla (bad-natured); *शुभकर्म* = shubha karma; *सुशीलं* = suśīla (good-natured); *कथं* = how; *तत् भवति सुशीलं* = can that be truly suśīla; *यत् संसारं प्रवेशयति* = which sends one into samsāra.

**COMMENTARY:**
The verse opens with a seeming concession — shubha karma is "good-natured" — and then immediately undercuts it with a devastating question: if shubha karma perpetuates samsāra, in what meaningful sense is it good? Kundakunda is not condemning moral behavior or virtuous action in the ordinary sense. He is making a technical point: anything — however beautiful in nature — that keeps the soul cycling in samsāra cannot be the ultimate good. The truly suśīla (good-natured) can only be what leads to moksha, and shubha karma, by its nature, does not.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Ashubha karma is bad-natured. Shubha karma is called good-natured. But how can it truly be "good" if it still sends the soul into samsāra?

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is there a difference between doing good as a karmic strategy (to earn puṇya) versus doing good as an expression of your nature? Which one binds?

### GATHA 146

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
सोवण्णियं पि णियलं बंधिद कालायसं पि जह पुिसं।
बंधिद एवं जीवं सुहमसुहं वा कदं कम्मं।।१४६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
सौवर्णिकमपि निगलं बध्नाति कालायसमपि यथा पुरुषम्।
बध्नाति एवं जीवं शुभमशुभं वा कृतं कर्म।।१४६।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as a fetter of gold and a fetter of iron both bind a person equally — so shubha and ashubha karma, both done, bind the soul.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
*सौवर्णिकम् अपि निगलं* = even a golden fetter; *बध्नाति* = binds; *कालायसम् अपि* = even an iron fetter; *यथा पुरुषम्* = a person similarly; *एवं* = in the same way; *शुभम् अशुभं वा* = whether shubha or ashubha; *कृतं कर्म* = karma that has been done; *जीवं बध्नाति* = binds the soul.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is the pivotal verse of Adhikar 3 — the golden chain and iron chain analogy that crystallizes the entire teaching. A prisoner bound with gold is no freer than one bound with iron. The comfort of the chain is irrelevant to the fact of imprisonment. Similarly, the soul bound by meritorious karma (higher births, pleasures, good circumstances) is still bound. The Ātmakhyāti tīkā elaborates: shubha karma leads to heavenly states and noble human births; but these are still within samsāra. The soul experiencing divine pleasures has not moved toward moksha — it has only postponed the reckoning and deepened the attachment.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
A golden chain and an iron chain both imprison equally. In the same way, shubha karma and ashubha karma both bind the soul. There is no "good bondage."

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What "golden chains" do you carry — attachments to pleasant conditions, good circumstances, favored identities — that you don't recognize as chains at all?

## PART 2: Avoid All Karma Association

### GATHA 147

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
तम्हा दु कुसीलेिह य रागं मा कुणह मा व संसग्गं।
साहीणो िह िवणासो कुसीलसंसग्गरायेण।।१४७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
तस्माद्धि कुशीलेभ्यश्च रागं मा कुरु मा वा संसर्गम्।
स्वाधीनतायाः खलु विनाशः कुशीलसंसर्गरागेण।।१४७।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Therefore, do not make attachment to or association with kusīla karmas. Your independence (svādhīnatā) is truly destroyed by the attachment of kusīla association.

**COMMENTARY:**
Having established that both shubha and ashubha karma bind (G146), Kundakunda draws the natural conclusion: avoid entanglement with both. The word *svādhīnatā* (independence, self-sovereignty) is central — the soul's liberation is about recovering its own autonomy, its own svabhāva-sthiti. Every association with karma — even "good" karma done from attachment — erodes this independence. The soul that is attached to its own virtuous acts, its own tapas, its own vows, has exchanged one form of servitude for another.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Therefore, don't develop attachment or association with kusīla (bad-natured) karmas. Your own freedom is destroyed by attachment to kusīla association.

### GATHA 148

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जह णाम को िव पुिरसो कुिच्छयसीलं जणं िवयाणित्ता।
वज्जेिद तेण समयं संसग्गं रागकरणं च।।१४८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
यथा नाम कोऽपि पुरुषः कुत्सितशीलं जनं विज्ञाय।
वर्जयति तेन सह संसर्गं रागकरणं च।।१४८।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as a person, knowing another person to be of bad character (kutsita-śīla), avoids association with him and the attachment that comes from such contact —

**COMMENTARY:**
Gatha 148 begins a two-verse analogy. In ordinary life, a wise person who recognizes someone of bad character keeps their distance — not out of judgment, but out of practical wisdom. They know that prolonged association breeds attachment, and attachment leads to being pulled into the other's orbit. This analogy sets up Gatha 149.

### GATHA 149

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
एमेव कम्मपयडीसीलसहावं च कुिच्छदं णादु।
वज्जंिति पिरहरंित य तस्संसग्गं सहावरदा।।१४९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एवमेव कर्मप्रकृतिशीलस्वभावं च कुत्सितं ज्ञात्वा।
वर्जयन्ति परिहरन्ति च तत्संसर्गं स्वभावरताः।।१४९।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Similarly, those who are established in their own svabhāva (svabhāva-ratā), knowing the inherently bad nature (kucchita-svabhāva) of karma-prakriti, avoid and abandon all association with it.

**COMMENTARY:**
The analogy is complete: just as the wise person avoids the company of the bad-charactered individual, so the soul established in its own nature recognizes karma-prakriti (the nature of karma) as inherently defiling and avoids association. The key phrase is *svabhāva-ratā* — those who are deeply absorbed in/delighted by their own essential nature. This is not willpower or external discipline; it is the natural gravitational pull of self-knowledge. When you know your own nature as pure consciousness, karma's glamour loses its pull automatically.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
In the same way, those deeply established in their own svabhāva — recognizing the defiling nature of karma — naturally avoid all association with it.

## PART 3: Rāgī Binds, Virāgī is Freed

### GATHA 150

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
रतो बंधिद कम्मं मुच्चिद जीवो िवरागसंपत्तो।
एसो िजणोवदेसो तम्हा कम्मेसु मा रज्ज।।१५०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
रक्तो बध्नाति कर्म मुच्यते जीवो विरागसम्पन्नः।
एष जिनोपदेशः तस्मात् कर्मसु मा रज्यस्व।।१५०।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The rāgī (one with attachment) binds karma; the soul endowed with virāga (detachment) is freed. This is the Jina's instruction — therefore do not be attached to karmas.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse gives the essential law of bondage and liberation in a single line: *rakto badhnāti, virāga-sampannaḥ mucyate*. Attachment binds; detachment frees. The Jina's teaching is invoked not as an appeal to authority but as confirmation of a direct spiritual truth. The one who is rāgī (dyed in attachment) keeps karma accruing; the one who has become virāgī (drained of that dye) is naturally released. The instruction that follows is practical: therefore — *mā rajyasva* — do not be attached. Not a moral commandment but a causal understanding: attachment is the mechanism of bondage.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The attached one binds karma; the detached one is freed. This is the Jina's teaching — therefore do not be attached to karmas.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Is your relationship to your own practices and virtues one of attachment or of detachment? Do your good deeds create a subtle pride, ownership, or identity — a golden chain?

## PART 4: Paramārtha — The Only Path to Nirvāṇa

### GATHA 151

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
परमट्ठो खलु समओ सुद्धो जो केवली मुणी णाणी।
तम्हि िट्ठदा सहावे मुिणणो पावंित िणव्वाणं।।१५१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
परमार्थः खलु समयः शुद्धो यः केवली मुनिर्ज्ञानी।
तस्मिन् स्थिता स्वभावे मुनयः प्राप्नुवन्ति निर्वाणम्।।१५१।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The paramārtha (ultimate truth) is indeed samaya — the pure, kevalī, muni, jñānī (soul). Munis established in that svabhāva attain nirvāṇa.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse is the metaphysical center of Adhikar 3. *Paramārtha* — the ultimate standpoint — is identified with *samaya*: the pure, complete, omniscient soul in its essential nature. It is not a doctrine, a scripture, a practice, or a deity. It is the soul itself in its purest state. The word *samaya* here directly evokes the title of the scripture itself — Samayasāra — the essence of the self. Munis who are established (sthitā) in this svabhāva attain nirvāṇa. The path is not accumulation of merit but installation in one's own nature.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The ultimate truth (paramārtha) is the samaya itself — the pure, knowing, complete soul. Munis established in that svabhāva attain nirvāṇa.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What would it mean to be "established" in your own deepest nature — not seeking, not accumulating, not performing, but resting in what you already are?

## PART 5: Bāla-Tapa and Bāla-Vrata

### GATHA 152

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
परमट्ठम्हि दु अिठदो जो कुणिद तवं वदं च धारेिद।
तं सव्वं बालतवं बालवदं बेंित सव्वण्हू।।१५२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
परमार्थे तु अस्थितो यः करोति तपः व्रतं च धारयति।
तत्सर्वं बालतपः बालव्रतम् ब्रुवन्ति सर्वज्ञाः।।१५२।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
One who is not established in the paramārtha but does tapas (austerities) and observes vows — all that is bāla-tapas and bāla-vrata (childish austerities and childish vows), say the omniscient ones.

**COMMENTARY:**
*Bāla-tapa* and *bāla-vrata* — childish austerities and vows — is Kundakunda's sharpest critique of formalism. The charge is not against tapas or vrats in themselves; it is against performing them without inner establishment in the paramārtha. A child who mimics adult work without understanding is not doing the work — they are playing. Similarly, the soul that accumulates external observances without the foundation of samyak-drishti and self-knowledge is spiritually imitating, not genuinely progressing. The *sarvajñā* (omniscient ones — the Jinas) confirm this: all such practice, however elaborate, remains bāla.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
One not established in the ultimate truth who does austerities and vows — all that is "childish austerity" and "childish vow," say the omniscient.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What is the difference between external religious practice that comes from genuine inner establishment and practice that is a substitute for it?

---

### GATHA 153

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
वदिणयमािण धरंता सीलािण तहा तवं च कुव्वंता।
परमट्ठबाहिरा जे िणव्वाणं ते ण िवंदंित।।१५३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
व्रतनियमानि धारयन्तः शीलानि तथा तपश्च कुर्वन्तः।
परमार्थबाह्याः ये निर्वाणं ते न विन्दन्ति।।१५३।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Those who observe vows, niyamas, śīlas, and perform tapas — but who are external to the paramārtha — they do not attain nirvāṇa.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse closes the bāla-tapa section with an unambiguous verdict: all the external paraphernalia of religious life — vows, rules, codes of conduct, austerities — without the interior foundation of paramārtha establish nothing that leads to nirvāṇa. The Jain householder who observes all twelve vratas, the monk who follows all mahāvratas impeccably in outer form — if the inner eye of samyak-drishti is not open, if the soul is not genuinely aware of its own nature — the practice does not reach its destination. The path is not external. Liberation cannot be earned by behavior alone.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Those who observe vows, niyamas, śīlas and do tapas — but are external to the ultimate — do not attain nirvāṇa.

## PART 6: The Error of Puṇya-Seeking

### GATHA 154

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
परमट्ठबाहिरा जे ते अण्णाणेण पुण्णिमच्छंित।
संसारगणहेदुंिप मोक्खहेदुं अजाणंता।।१५४।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
परमार्थबाह्याः ये तेऽज्ञानेन पुण्यमिच्छन्ति।
संसारगणहेतुमपि मोक्षहेतुम् अजानन्तः।।१५४।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Those who are external to the paramārtha desire puṇya out of ignorance — not knowing that puṇya itself is a cause of samsāra-continuation, they mistake it for the cause of moksha.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse identifies the fundamental error of conventional religious life: mistaking the cause of continued samsāra for the cause of liberation. Puṇya generates higher births, better circumstances, more refined pleasures — all within samsāra. It does not generate moksha. The person who accumulates puṇya thinking they are moving toward liberation is walking in the wrong direction, but with great confidence and effort. Their very sincerity makes the error more dangerous, because it insulates them from questioning the path itself. The Ātmakhyāti notes: puṇya and pāpa are both *samsāra-gana-hetu* — causes that keep the mob of samsāric existence running.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Those outside the paramārtha desire puṇya out of ignorance — not knowing that puṇya is actually a cause of samsāra, they mistake it for the cause of moksha.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Have you ever pursued "spiritual merit" as though accumulating enough of it would eventually result in liberation? What is the difference between that and genuine inner turning?

## PART 7: Ratnatraya — The Real Moksha Path

### GATHA 155

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
जीवादीसद्दहणं सम्मतं तेिसिमिधगमो णाणं।
रागादीपिरहरणं चरणं एसो दु मोक्खपहो।।१५५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवादीश्रद्धानं सम्यक्त्वं तेषामधिगमो ज्ञानम्।
रागादिपरिहरणं चारित्रम् एष तु मोक्षपथः।।१५५।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Śraddhāna (right faith) in jīva and the other reals = samyaktva; adhigama (comprehension) of those reals = jñāna; renunciation of rāga and the others = cāritra. This indeed is the moksha-path.

**COMMENTARY:**
After the critique of false paths, Kundakunda gives the real path: the ratnatraya — the three jewels of Jain liberation. The three are defined with precision:
- **Samyaktva** = right faith, specifically: genuine trust in the reality of jīva (soul), ajīva (non-soul), āsrava (influx), bandha (bondage), samvara (cessation), nirjarā (shedding), and moksha.
- **Jñāna** = direct comprehension of these reals — not mere belief, but understanding.
- **Cāritra** = renunciation of rāga (attachment) and dveṣa (aversion) — which is the inner dimension, not just outer behavioral observance.

The three together constitute *mokshamārga*. No single one is sufficient. All three in concert — right seeing, right knowing, right living — constitute the complete path.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Genuine faith in the reals = samyaktva; comprehension of those reals = jñāna; renouncing attachment = cāritra. This is the path to moksha.

## PART 8: Jñāna as the Sole Cause of Moksha

### GATHA 156

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
मोतूण िणच्छयट्ठं ववहारेण िवदुसा पवट्टंित।
परमट्ठमिस्सदाण दु जदीण कम्मक्खओ िवहओ।।१५६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
मुक्त्वा निश्चयार्थं व्यवहारेण विदुषां प्रवर्तन्ते।
परमार्थे स्थितानां तु यतीनां कर्मक्षयः विहितः।।१५६।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Leaving the niśchaya (ultimate) content aside, the learned operate through vyavahāra (conventional standpoint). But for those yatis (ascetics) established in paramārtha, destruction of karma is what is prescribed.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse addresses the apparent contradiction: if paramārtha is the standard, why does Jain teaching also include extensive vyavahāra instructions? The answer: vyavahāra is the necessary educational path for those not yet capable of operating from paramārtha. For the sake of communicating truth to such learners, the ācārya operates conventionally — uses rules, prescriptions, rituals, outer forms. But the actual mechanism of moksha — karma-kṣaya (destruction of karma) — is only for those established in paramārtha. The conventional path scaffolds toward the ultimate; it is not the ultimate itself.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Leaving the ultimate aside, the learned operate through conventional means. But for ascetics established in the paramārtha — the destruction of karma is what actually happens.

## PART 9: The Cloth and Dirt — Three Obstructions

### GATHA 157

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
मिच्छत्तमलोच्छण्णं तह सम्मतं खु णादव्वं।।१५७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
वस्त्रस्य श्वेतभावः यथा नश्यति मलमेलनासक्तः।
मिथ्यात्वमलावच्छन्नं तथा सम्यक्त्वं खलु ज्ञातव्यम्।।१५७।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when it becomes smeared with dirt — similarly, samyaktva (right perception) must be understood as obstructed/covered when it is coated with the filth of mithyātva.

**COMMENTARY:**
Gathas 157-159 form a perfect triptych — the same analogy applied three times to the three jewels, showing how karma obstructs each one. The analogy is exact: cloth is inherently white (just as the soul is inherently pure); dirt (external) coats and obscures the whiteness. The whiteness is not destroyed — it is covered. Similarly, mithyātva (the karma that produces wrong perception) does not destroy the soul's capacity for right perception — it covers it. Samyaktva is the soul's natural orientation; mithyātva is the karmic veil over it. Remove the dirt; the whiteness emerges naturally.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — in the same way, samyaktva covered by the filth of mithyātva is obstructed. Know this.

---

### GATHA 158

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
अण्णाणमलोच्छण्णं तह णाणं होिद णादव्वं।।१५८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
वस्त्रस्य श्वेतभावः यथा नश्यति मलमेलनासक्तः।
अज्ञानमलावच्छन्नं तथा ज्ञानं भवति ज्ञातव्यम्।।१५८।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when smeared with dirt — similarly, jñāna covered by the filth of ajñāna (the karma of ignorance) is obstructed — know this.

**COMMENTARY:**
The second application: jñāna (knowledge, the soul's self-luminous knowing nature) is covered by ajñāna-karma. The soul is not inherently ignorant. Ignorance is a karmic condition, not an ontological one. Jñānāvaraṇīya karma (knowledge-covering karma) is the dirt that coats the soul's natural radiance of knowing. This understanding is crucial: you are not building knowledge from scratch. You are removing the covering over a knowledge that was always there.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — so jñāna covered by the filth of ajñāna-karma is obstructed. Know this.

---

### GATHA 159

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
कसायमलोच्छण्णं तह चािरत्तं िप णादव्वं।।१५९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
वस्त्रस्य श्वेतभावः यथा नश्यति मलमेलनासक्तः।
कषायमलावच्छन्नं तथा चारित्रमपि ज्ञातव्यम्।।१५९।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when smeared with dirt — similarly, cāritra covered by the filth of kaṣāya is obstructed — know this.

**COMMENTARY:**
The third application: cāritra (right conduct, the soul's natural capacity for equanimity and non-attachment) is covered by kaṣāya — the four passions (anger, pride, deceit, greed). Cāritra-mohanīya karma generates these passions, which obstruct the soul's natural equanimity. Again: the soul is not naturally passionate and disturbed. That is a karmic state. Remove the kaṣāya-veil; the soul's equanimity is its own inherent nature, needing only uncovering.

Together, G157-159 establish: the soul's nature already contains all three jewels — samyaktva, jñāna, and cāritra. They are obscured, not absent. The path is removal, not construction.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — so cāritra covered by the filth of kaṣāya is obstructed. Know this.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
If right perception, knowledge, and conduct are your natural state — merely covered by karma — what does spiritual practice become? Not building something new, but removing what covers what you already are.

---

## PART 10: Karma Covers the All-Knowing Soul

### GATHA 160

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
सो सव्वणाणदिरसी कम्मरएण णियेणावच्छण्णो।
संसारसमावण्णो ण विजाणिद सव्वदो सव्वं।।१६०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
स सर्वज्ञानदर्शी कर्मरजसा निजेनावच्छन्नः।
संसारसमापन्नो न विजानाति सर्वतः सर्वम्।।१६०।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
That soul — which is (by nature) all-seeing and all-knowing — covered by its own karma-dust, fallen into samsāra, does not know all things in all ways.

**COMMENTARY:**
This verse states the full tragedy of the samsāric soul with stark clarity. The soul's nature is *sarvajñāna-darśī* — it is inherently omniscient in potential. But covered by *karma-rajas* (karma-dust), its own accumulated karma, it falls into samsāra and cannot know. The soul did not lose its knowing nature — it was covered by what it itself attracted through prior attachment. *Nijena* = "by its own" — the karma is self-generated, not externally imposed. This points toward self-responsibility without self-condemnation: you covered yourself; therefore you can uncover yourself.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul is by nature all-knowing and all-seeing. But covered by its own karma-dust, fallen into samsāra, it does not know all things in all ways.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
The soul that doesn't know is not a different soul from the omniscient one. It is the same soul, covered. How does that understanding change the way you relate to your own limitations?

## PART 11: Three Karmas, Three Effects

### GATHA 161

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
सम्मतपडिणिबद्धं मिच्छत्तं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो मिच्छाद्दिट्ठि ति णादव्वो।।१६१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
सम्यक्त्वप्रतिनिबद्धं मिथ्यात्वं जिनवरैः परिकथितम्।
तस्योदयेन जीवो मिथ्यादृष्टिरिति ज्ञातव्यः।।१६१।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Mithyātva — which obstructs/blocks samyaktva — is described by the Jinas. By its rise (udaya), the soul becomes a mithyādṛṣṭi (wrong-perceiving being) — this must be known.

**COMMENTARY:**
Gathas 161-163 complete the Adhikar with a precise threefold map of how karma creates the soul's samsāric condition. Mithyātva karma is technically *darśana-mohanīya* — the karma that obstructs samyak-darśana (right perception). When this karma rises (udaya), the soul's seeing is distorted: it sees the non-self as self, the temporary as permanent, the external as satisfying. This is mithyātva — not a moral failure but a karmic condition of wrong orientation.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Mithyātva — which blocks samyaktva — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes mithyādṛṣṭi (wrong-perceiving). Know this.

---

### GATHA 162

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
णाणस्स पडिणिबद्धं अण्णाणं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो अण्णाणी होिद णादव्वो।।१६२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
ज्ञानस्य प्रतिनिबद्धम् अज्ञानं जिनवरैः परिकथितम्।
तस्योदयेन जीवोऽज्ञानी भवति ज्ञातव्यः।।१६२।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Ajñāna karma — which obstructs jñāna — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes ajñānī (ignorant) — this must be known.

**COMMENTARY:**
Jñānāvaraṇīya karma (knowledge-covering karma) blocks the soul's natural omniscience. By its rise, the soul is ajñānī — not because it was born ignorant, but because a karmic covering prevents knowing. This is the mechanism behind all the soul's cognitive limitations, confusion, and delusion in samsāra.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Ajñāna karma — which blocks jñāna — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes ajñānī. Know this.

---

### GATHA 163

**PRAKRIT TEXT:**
चारित्तपडिणिबद्धं कसायं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो अचिरतो होिद णादव्वो।।१६३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
चारित्रप्रतिनिबद्धः कषायः जिनवरैः परिकथितः।
तस्योदयेन जीवोऽचारित्रो भवति ज्ञातव्यः।।१६३।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Kaṣāya karma — which obstructs cāritra — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes acāritra (lacking right conduct) — this must be known.

**COMMENTARY:**
Cāritra-mohanīya karma (conduct-deluding karma) generates the four kaṣāyas — anger, pride, deceit, greed — which obstruct the soul's natural equanimity and capacity for non-attachment. By its rise, the soul is *acāritra* — not naturally violent or passionate, but karmically driven into those states. The three verses (G161-163) together give a complete karmic pathology of samsāra: mithyātva distorts seeing, ajñāna covers knowing, kaṣāya corrupts living. The three jewels are the antidotes: samyaktva, jñāna, cāritra — as stated in G155.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Kaṣāya karma — which blocks cāritra — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes acāritra (lacking conduct). Know this.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
If anger, pride, deceit, and greed are karmic conditions — not your essential nature — how does that change how you relate to them when they arise? Not as "what I am" but as "what covers what I am"?

## CLOSING

**The exit verse (end of Adhikar 3):**

इति पुण्यपापरूपेण द्विपात्रीभूतमेकपात्रीभूय कर्म निष्क्रान्तम्।

*Thus karma, which had appeared as two actors (puṇya and pāpa) on the stage of the soul, having been recognized as one single actor (karma), has departed from the stage.*

The theatrical metaphor from Kundakunda's framing: karma played two roles — one as puṇya (beautiful, attractive, seemingly beneficial) and one as pāpa (ugly, harmful, obviously dangerous). The seeker, enchanted by puṇya's beauty, did not recognize it as the same karma that also plays pāpa. When jñāna reveals the true identity of the actor behind both roles, karma loses its power to deceive — and exits the stage entirely.
