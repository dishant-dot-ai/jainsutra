# Samaysaar — Adhikar 1: Jeev-Ajeev (Soul and Non-Soul)
## Translation: Gathas 39–68

**Scripture:** Samaysaar (The Essence of the Soul)
**Author:** Bhagwan Kundkundacharya Dev (~2000 years ago)
**Commentary (Atmakhyati Tika):** Shrimad Amritchandra Suri
**Adhikar:** 1 — Jeev-Ajeev (जीव-अजीव / Soul and Non-Soul)
**Theme:** The soul is pure consciousness alone. All material categories — color, karma, raga, guṇasthāna — belong to pudgala-dravya (matter). Confusing the two is the root of bondage.
**Gathas:** 39–68 (30 total)

---

## PART 1: THE WRONG VIEWS — WHAT FOOLS SAY THE SOUL IS

### GATHA 39

**PRAKRIT:**
अप्पाणमयाणंता मूढा दु परप्पवादिणो केई।
जीवं अझ्झवसाणं कम्मं च तहा परुवेंति।।३९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
आत्मानमजानन्तो मूढास्तु परात्मवादिनः केचित्।
जीवमध्यवसानं कर्म च तथा प्ररूपयन्ति।।३९।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Not knowing the soul's true nature, some foolish para-ātmavādins (those who hold to an external or mistaken view of self) declare that karma and adhyavasāna (mental dispositions/passional states) are the soul.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**अप्पाणमयाणंता** = not knowing the soul; **मूढा** = the ignorant/deluded ones; **परप्पवादिणो** = those who speak of a "para-ātman" (external or mis-identified self); **केई** = some; **जीवं** = the soul; **अझ्झवसाणं** = adhyavasāna (passionate mental dispositions); **कम्मं** = karma; **च तहा** = and also; **परुवेंति** = declare/describe.

**COMMENTARY:**
The Adhikar opens with a survey of error. Kundkundacharya identifies the root mistake: not knowing what the soul actually is. From this ignorance, false theories proliferate. Some say the soul is the same as adhyavasāna — the charged, passionate mental states like anger, desire, fear. Others say the soul just is karma. Both errors collapse the distinction between jīva (soul) and ajīva (non-soul) that this entire adhikar will systematically establish.

The term para-ātmavādin — one who holds to an other-soul view — doesn't just mean "someone with wrong philosophy." It means someone whose practical identification is with what is other than the self: sensations, thoughts, emotions, the body. This is the starting condition of most beings in samsāra.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Those who don't know the soul mistake passionate states (adhyavasāna) and karma for the soul itself. This is the fundamental error the Adhikar addresses.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
When you say "I am angry" — are you asserting that anger is your nature? Or are you describing a temporary state passing through a consciousness that is different from that anger?

### GATHA 40

**PRAKRIT:**
अवरे अझ्झवसाणेसु तिव्वमंदाणुभागं जीवं।
मण्णंति तहा अवरे णोकम्मं चावि जीवो ति।।४०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
अपरे अध्यवसानेषु तीव्रमन्दानुभागं जीवम्।
मन्यन्ते तथा अपरे नोकर्म चापि जीव इति।।४०।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Others consider the soul to be the intense-or-mild experiential quality within adhyavasāna; and yet others consider nokamma (body, sense organs, and material support) to be the soul.

**COMMENTARY:**
The errors proliferate into greater subtlety. Some distinguish within adhyavasāna and say the soul is the degree of intensity — the tīvra (sharp) or manda (mild) quality of passion. This is more refined than the previous error but still locates the soul in what is fundamentally a material-causal process. Others look outward to nokamma — the body, the sense organs, the physical apparatus — and call that the soul. Both are still pudgala.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Some equate the soul with the intensity of emotional experience; others with the physical body and senses. Both are still wrong — all these are matter.

---

### GATHA 41

**PRAKRIT:**
कम्मस्सुदयं जीवं अवरे कम्माणुभागमिच्छंति।
तिव्वत्तणमंदत्तणगुणेहिं जो सो हवदि जीवो।।४१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
कर्मण उदयं जीवमपरे कर्मानुभागमिच्छन्ति।
तीव्रत्वमन्दत्वगुणैर्यः स भवति जीवः।।४१।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Others consider the rising (udaya) of karma to be the soul; others want karma's anubhāga (fruition-force) — whoever has the quality of intensity-or-mildness, they say that is the soul.

**COMMENTARY:**
Still more refined errors: some say the soul is the moment of karma's rising (udaya), others say it's the experiential potency of karma (anubhāga) — what the karma actually delivers as experience. All these views are pointing at various aspects of a purely pudgala process and calling it the self.

---

### GATHA 42

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवो कम्मं उहयं दोण्णि वि खलु केइ जीवमिच्छंति।
अवरे संजोगेण दु कम्माणं जीवमिच्छंति।।४२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवः कर्म उभयं द्वावपि खलु केचिज्जीवमिच्छन्ति।
अपरे संयोगेन तु कर्मणां जीवमिच्छन्ति।।४२।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Some consider both soul and karma together (their combination) as the soul; others consider the conjunction/meeting of karmas to be the soul.

**COMMENTARY:**
The errors escalate to a compound view: some say the soul is soul-plus-karma taken together as a whole. Others point to the very point of contact between soul and karma — the junction itself — and call that the soul. These attempts to split the difference fail because they all still include material elements in the definition of what is essentially conscious.

---

### GATHA 43

**PRAKRIT:**
एवंविहा बहुविहा परमप्पाणं वदंति दुम्मेहा।
ते ण परमद्वावादी णिच्छयवादीहिं णिंदिट्ठा।।४३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एवंविधा बहुविधाः परमात्मानं वदन्ति दुर्मेधसः।
ते न परमार्थवादिनः निश्चयवादिभिः निर्दिष्टाः।।४३।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
In these various and many ways, the foolish ones describe the supreme self; they are not paramārthavādins (speakers of ultimate truth) — the niśchayavādins (standpoint of ultimate reality) do not acknowledge them as such.

**COMMENTARY:**
Kundkundacharya closes the survey of errors with a declaration: none of these views stand up to the scrutiny of niśchaya-naya (the ultimate standpoint). They are all speaking of the self in terms of what is other than the self. The person who speaks ultimate truth (paramārthavādin) will not be found among those who describe the soul in terms of karma, adhyavasāna, or any material category.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
All these many confused descriptions of the soul fail the test of ultimate truth. The nishchaya standpoint exposes them all as describing what is not the soul.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
If someone asked you "Who are you?" — would your answer include anything that karma, time, or circumstances could change? Whatever can change is not you.

## PART 2: THE OMNISCIENT DECLARATION — ALL THESE ARE PUDGALA

### GATHA 44

**PRAKRIT:**
एदे सव्वे भावा पोग्गलदव्वपरिणामिण्पण्णा।
केवलिजिणेहिं भणिया कह ते जीवो ति वृच्छंति।।४४।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एते सर्वे भावाः पुद्गलद्रव्यपरिणामनिष्पन्नाः।
केवलिजिनैर्भणिताः कथं ते जीव इत्युच्यन्ते।।४४।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
All these states arise from the transformation of pudgala-dravya (matter); the Kevali Jinas have declared this. How then can they be called the soul?

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**एदे सव्वे भावा** = all these states; **पोग्गलदव्वपरिणामिण्पण्णा** = produced by the transformation of pudgala-dravya; **केवलिजिणेहिं भणिया** = declared by the omniscient Jinas; **कह** = how; **ते जीवो ति वृच्छंति** = can they be called the soul?

**COMMENTARY:**
This gatha is the philosophical pivot of the entire Adhikar. Kundkundacharya appeals to the highest possible authority — the Kevali Jinas, those with omniscient knowledge — and reports their declaration: all the states previously described as "the soul" are in fact transformations of pudgala-dravya (matter). This is not a philosophical argument — it is a report of omniscient perception.

The question that closes the gatha is devastating in its simplicity: if the Omniscient Ones have declared these states to be material — how can anyone call them the soul?

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The omniscient Jinas have declared: all these states — adhyavasāna, karma, the body — are transformations of matter. They cannot be the soul.

---

### GATHA 45

**PRAKRIT:**
अट्ठिवहं पि य कम्मं सव्वं पोग्गलमयं जिणा बेंति।
जस्स फलं तं वुच्चिदे दुक्खं ति विपच्चमाणस्स।।४५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
अष्टविधमपि च कर्म सर्वं पुद्गलमयं जिना ब्रुवन्ति।
यस्य फलं तदुच्यते दुःखमिति विपच्यमानस्य।।४५।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
All eight types of karma are entirely pudgala — so the Jinas declare. The fruit of this karma when it ripens is what is called duhkha (suffering).

**COMMENTARY:**
The eight types of karma (jñānāvarana, darśanāvarana, vedanīya, mohanīya, āyu, nāma, gotra, antarāya) are declared by the Jinas to be entirely material — not a blend of consciousness and matter, not a mysterious force, but pudgala-dravya in specific configurations. And crucially: what karma produces when it matures is suffering (duhkha). The soul does not produce suffering by its nature — karma does. This is a radical re-attribution: suffering is not the soul's nature; it is the result of a material process.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
All eight karmas are matter, the Jinas say. What those karmas produce when they ripen is suffering — not the soul's own doing.

---

### GATHA 46

**PRAKRIT:**
ववहारस्स दरीसणमुवएसो वण्णिदो जिणवरेहिं।
जीवा एदे सव्वे अझ्झवसाणादओ भावा।।४६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
व्यवहारस्य दर्शनमुपदेशो वर्णितो जिनवरैः।
जीवा एते सर्वेऽध्यवसानादयो भावाः।।४६।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The Jinendras have described this as the teaching of the vyavahāra (conventional) viewpoint — that all these adhyavasāna etc. bhavas are "jīvas."

**COMMENTARY:**
Here Kundkundacharya provides an important clarification: the Āgamic texts do sometimes speak of adhyavasāna and related states as the "soul." But this is vyavahāra-naya (the conventional standpoint) — a pedagogical device for teaching souls who are still identified with their material coverings. It is not the ultimate truth. The next two gathas illustrate this distinction with a powerful analogy.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When Jain scriptures say adhyavasāna etc. are "the soul," that is vyavahāra (conventional speech) — a teaching device, not ultimate truth.

---

### GATHA 47

**PRAKRIT:**
राया हु णिग्गदो ति य एसो बलसमुदयस्स आदेसो।
ववहारेण दु उच्चिद तत्थेक्को णिग्गदो राया।।४७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
राजा खलु निर्गत इत्येष बलसमुदयस्यादेशः।
व्यवहारेण तूच्यते तत्रैको निर्गतो राजा।।४७।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
"The king went out" — this is the conventional expression for the army's departure. By vyavahāra it is said; but in truth, within that [army], only one king actually went out.

**COMMENTARY:**
A classic and brilliant analogy. When an army marches, we say "the king went out." But literally only one person — the king — actually went out; the soldiers are not the king. The statement is conventional shorthand. Similarly, when scriptures say "these bhavas are jīvas," it is shorthand for describing the soul's situation. The soul alone is the jīva; everything associated with it by convention is not.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
"The king went out" really means the army went out — it's conventional speech. Similarly, calling adhyavasāna "jīva" is conventional; only one true jīva exists.

---

### GATHA 48

**PRAKRIT:**
एमेव य ववहारो अझ्झवसाणादिअण्णभावाणं।
जीवो ति कदो सुत्ते तत्थेक्को णिच्छिदो जीवो।।४८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एवमेव च व्यवहारोऽध्यवसानाद्यन्यभावानाम्।
जीव इति कृतः सूत्रे तत्रैको निश्रितो जीवः।।४८।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Similarly, calling adhyavasāna etc. other-bhavas as "jīva" in the sūtra is vyavahāra; but from niśchaya (the ultimate standpoint), only one jīva is established there.

**COMMENTARY:**
The analogy from Gatha 47 is now applied directly. Within the entire complex of soul + karma + body + mental states, there is exactly one jīva — one conscious entity. Everything else is pudgala. The Āgamas use vyavahāra language ("this is the soul's karma, this is the soul's anger") because beings cannot initially be taught except in terms of what they already identify with. But the ultimate teaching always points to one conscious reality distinct from all material categories.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Just as "the king went out" is conventional speech for the army, calling adhyavasāna "jīva" is conventional. From the ultimate standpoint — one jīva, nothing more.

## PART 3: THE SOUL'S TRUE NATURE

### GATHA 49

**PRAKRIT:**
अरसमरूवमगंधं अव्वत्तं चेदणागुणमसदं।
जाण अलिंगग्गहणं जीवमिणिदिट्ठसंठाणं।।४९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
अरसमरूपमगन्धमव्यक्तं चेतनागुणमशब्दम्।
जानीहि अलिञ्जग्रहणं जीवमनिर्दिष्टसंस्थानम्।।४९।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Know the soul as: without taste, without form, without smell, imperceptible to the senses, having consciousness (chētanā) as its essential quality, without sound, not grasped through signs or indicators (liṅga), and of no defined physical shape.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**अरसम्** = without taste; **अरूपम्** = without form; **अगन्धम्** = without smell; **अव्यक्तम्** = imperceptible (not accessible to sense organs); **चेतनागुणम्** = whose essential quality is consciousness; **अशब्दम्** = without sound; **अलिञ्जग्रहणम्** = not grasped through external signs/indicators; **जीवम्** = the soul; **अनिर्दिष्टसंस्थानम्** = of no defined shape; **जानीहि** = know.

**COMMENTARY:**
After the long survey of error, Kundkundacharya now speaks directly to the reader: "Know the soul as..." This is the positive definition. The soul is defined entirely by negation of material qualities and one positive attribute: chētanā (consciousness-knowing-awareness). It has no taste, form, smell, sound, or shape. It is not accessible to sense organs. It cannot be recognized by external signs. It has no physical constitution.

Only one thing is positively affirmed: it is the entity whose nature is consciousness. Chētanā is not something the soul has — it is what the soul is.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul has no material qualities whatsoever. One thing defines it: consciousness. That is its nature — not something it does or has, but what it is.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
What part of you cannot be tasted, smelled, seen, touched, or measured — yet cannot be denied? That is the direction in which the soul is being pointed.

### GATHA 50

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवस्स णत्थि वण्णो ण वि गंधो ण वि रसो ण वि य फासो।
ण वि रूवं ण सरीरं ण वि संठाणं ण संहणणं।।५०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवस्य नास्ति वर्णो नापि गन्धो नापि रसो नापि च स्पर्शः।
नापि रूपं न शरीरं नापि संस्थानं न संहननम्।।५०।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no color, no smell, no taste, no touch; no form, no body, no shape (saṃsthāna), no physical constitution (saṃhanana).

**COMMENTARY:**
This gatha systematically eliminates every sensory quality from the soul's definition: the five sensory attributes (color, smell, taste, touch, sound — here grouped as color, smell, taste, touch), plus form, body, shape, and physical constitution (the arrangement and density of matter that gives bodies their structural character). All eliminated. The soul has none of these.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Not one sensory quality belongs to the soul. Not body, not shape, not form. None of it.

---

### GATHA 51

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवस्स णत्थि रागो ण वि दोसो णेव विज्जदे मोहो।
णो पच्चया ण कम्मं णोकम्मं चावि से णत्थि।।५१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवस्य नास्ति रागो नापि द्वेषो नैव विद्यते मोहः।
नो प्रत्यया न कर्म नोकर्म चापि तस्य नास्ति।।५१।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no rāga (attachment), no dvēṣa (aversion); moha (delusion) does not exist in it; no pratyayas (influx-causes), no karma, and nokamma also does not belong to it.

**COMMENTARY:**
Now Kundkundacharya eliminates the psychological: rāga, dvēṣa, moha — the three great afflictions of samsāric existence — do not belong to the soul's nature. They are not inherent to consciousness; they are foreign intrusions. Nor do pratyayas (the causes of karmic influx) belong to the soul, nor karma itself, nor nokamma (the body and sense-material that accompanies the soul).

This is the most radical claim: the very passions that seem most "like us" — our loves, aversions, and confusions — are not us. They are pudgala-driven intrusions.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
The soul owns no attachment, no aversion, no delusion. No karma. No body. These are all foreign to the soul's pure nature.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
Have you ever experienced a moment of pure, still awareness — without desire, aversion, or confusion? What was that awareness aware of? That awareness itself may be closer to your actual nature than anything else you experience.

## PART 4: THE TECHNICAL CATEGORIES THAT DON'T BELONG TO THE SOUL

### GATHA 52

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवस्स णत्थि वग्गो ण वग्गणा णेव फड्डुया केई।
णो अझ्झप्पट्ठाणा णेव य अणुभागट्ठाणाणि।।५२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवस्य नास्ति वर्गो न वर्गणा नैव स्पर्धकानि कानिचित्।
नो अध्यात्मस्थानानि नैव चानुभागस्थानानि।।५२।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no varga, no vargaṇā, no spardhaka whatsoever; no adhyātmasthānas, no anubhāgasthānas — none of these.

**COMMENTARY:**
Varga, vargaṇā, spardhaka — these are increasingly complex groupings of matter-particles described in Jain physics. They are categories of how pudgala-dravya organizes itself at different scales. Adhyātmasthānas and anubhāgasthānas are further technical categories describing karmic fruition intensity. None of these belong to the soul. The soul's nature is not indexed in terms of material categories, no matter how refined those categories become.

---

### GATHA 53

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवस्स णत्थि केई जोयट्ठाणा ण बंधट्ठाणा वा।
णेव य उदयट्ठाणा ण मग्गणट्ठाणया केई।।५३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवस्य न सन्ति कानिचिद्योगस्थानानि न बन्धस्थानानि वा।
नैव चोदयस्थानानि न मार्गणास्थानानि कानिचित्।।५३।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no yogasthānas of any kind, no bandhhasthānas; no udayasthānas, no mārgaṇāsthānas of any kind.

**COMMENTARY:**
Yogasthānas = the degrees of yoga (vibration in body-mind-speech that causes karmic influx). Bandhhasthānas = the configurations of karmic binding. Udayasthānas = stages of karmic rising/maturation. Mārgaṇāsthānas = the fourteen search-categories (gati, indriya, kāya, etc.) used to classify souls by their current state. None of these are the soul's own nature. They are descriptions of the soul's situation in samsāra, not descriptions of what the soul intrinsically is.

---

### GATHA 54

**PRAKRIT:**
णो ठिदिबंधट्ठाणा जीवस्स ण संकिलेसट्ठाणा वा।
णेव विसोहिट्ठाणा णो संजमलद्धिट्ठाणा वा।।५४।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
नो स्थितिबन्धस्थानानि जीवस्य न संक्लेशस्थानानि वा।
नैव विशुद्धिस्थानानि नो संयमलब्धिस्थानानि वा।।५४।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no sthitibandhasthānas, no saṃkleśasthānas; no viśuddhisthānas, no saṃyamalabdhisthānas.

**COMMENTARY:**
Still more technical categories eliminated: sthitibandha = the duration-binding of karma; saṃkleśa = the degree of spiritual defilement; viśuddhi = the degree of purification; saṃyamalabdhi = the attainment-states of restraint. Even the categories describing spiritual progress are, in themselves, pudgala-related processes — not the soul's inherent nature. The soul's own nature is the same before and after all these stages.

---

### GATHA 55

**PRAKRIT:**
णेव य जीवट्ठाणा ण गुणट्ठाणा य अत्थि जीवस्स।
जेण दु एदे सव्वे पोग्गलदव्वस्स परिणामा।।५५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
नैव च जीवस्थानानि न गुणस्थानानि वा सन्ति जीवस्य।
येन त्वेते सर्वे पुद्गलद्रव्यस्य परिणामाः।।५५।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul has no jīvasthānas, no guṇasthānas — because all of these are transformations (pariṇāma) of pudgala-dravya.

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**णेव य जीवट्ठाणा** = no jīvasthānas; **ण गुणट्ठाणा** = no guṇasthānas; **अत्थि जीवस्स** = belong to the soul; **जेण** = because; **एदे सव्वे** = all these; **पोग्गलदव्वस्स परिणामा** = transformations of pudgala-dravya.

**COMMENTARY:**
This is the culminating statement of the technical section. Even jīvasthānas (the fourteen classifications of souls by sense-faculties) and guṇasthānas (the fourteen spiritual stages from mithyādṛṣṭi to siddha) — which one might think are most essentially "about" the soul — are declared to be transformations of pudgala-dravya. The reason given is conclusive: because they are all caused by karma (which is pudgala), they are pudgala-effects. The soul's niśchaya-nature is not captured in any of these categories.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Even the fourteen guṇasthānas — the spiritual stages — are not the soul's inherent nature, because they are products of karma (matter). The soul is what remains when all these processes are stripped away.

---

### GATHA 56

**PRAKRIT:**
ववहारेण दु एदे जीवस्स हवंति वण्णमादीया।
गुणट्ठाणंता भावा ण दु केई णिच्छयणयस्स।।५६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
व्यवहारेण त्वेते जीवस्य भवन्ति वर्णाद्याः।
गुणस्थानान्ता भावा न तु केचिन्निश्चयनयस्य।।५६।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
By vyavahāra, color etc. up to guṇasthāna-related bhavas "belong to" the soul; but from the niśchaya standpoint, not one of these applies.

**COMMENTARY:**
The distinction is now explicit and complete: vyavahāra (conventional) language attributes all of these material properties to the soul as a teaching convenience. Niśchaya (ultimate) standpoint recognizes zero material categories as belonging to the soul's own nature. Both standpoints are needed — vyavahāra for teaching, niśchaya for liberation.

---

## PART 5: THE SOUL'S RELATIONSHIP WITH MATTER

### GATHA 57

**PRAKRIT:**
एदेहिं य संबंधो जहेव खीरोदयं मुणेदव्वो।
ण य होंति तस्स ताणि दु उवओगगुणाधिगो जम्हा।।५७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एतैश्च सम्बन्धो यथैव क्षीरोदकं ज्ञातव्यः।
न च भवन्ति तस्य तानि तूपयोগগुणाधिको यस्मात्।।५७।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The soul's connection with these [color etc.] should be understood like the mixing of milk and water; yet those [color etc.] do not truly belong to the soul, because the soul is superior in the quality of upayoga (conscious knowing/experience).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**एदेहिं य संबंधो** = connection with these; **जहेव खीरोदयं** = just like milk and water; **मुणेदव्वो** = should be understood; **ण य होंति तस्स ताणि** = yet those do not truly belong to it; **उवओगगुणाधिगो** = superior in the quality of upayoga; **जम्हा** = because.

**COMMENTARY:**
The milk-water analogy is one of the most famous in Jain philosophy. Milk and water mix completely — they become inseparable in appearance. Yet the person who knows what each is can separate them: milk is fat-and-protein, water is H₂O. They were never actually one substance, only mixed.

Similarly, the soul and its material coverings are thoroughly intermixed in samsāra. The soul appears to be colored, shaped, emotional, located in a body. Yet the soul and matter are actually different substances. What makes the soul different? Upayoga — the capacity for conscious knowing and experiencing. This quality belongs to no material substance, however refined.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Soul and matter are like milk and water — thoroughly mixed but separable. What distinguishes the soul? Upayoga: conscious knowing. Matter has no such quality.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
The sense of "I am aware of this" — the sheer fact of knowing — cannot be assigned to any material particle. Where does that awareness come from, if not from a substance whose nature is consciousness?

---

### GATHA 58

**PRAKRIT:**
पंथे मुस्संतं परिस्सदूण लोगा भणंति ववहारी।
मुस्सिदे एसो पंथो ण य पंथो मुस्सदे कोई।।५८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
पथि मुष्यमानं दृष्ट्वा लोका भणन्ति व्यवहारिणः।
मुष्यते एष पन्था न च पन्था मुष्यते कश्चित्।।५८।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Seeing a traveler being robbed on the path, people conventionally say "the path is being robbed" — but in truth, no path is actually being robbed; it is the traveler.

**COMMENTARY:**
A second brilliant analogy for vyavahāra vs. niśchaya. When we say "this road is dangerous" or "someone was robbed on the highway," we attribute to the road what happened to a person on the road. The road cannot be robbed — it has no possessions. Only the traveler can be robbed. The road is merely the location. Similarly: when we say "the soul has color," we are attributing to the soul what belongs to the karma traveling with it. The soul is the traveler; karma is what it carries. Karma's color is not the traveler's color.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Saying "the path is dangerous" when a person was robbed there — that's vyavahāra. Similarly, "the soul has color" is vyavahāra; the color belongs to karma, not the soul.

---

### GATHA 59

**PRAKRIT:**
तह जीवे कम्माणं णोकम्माणं च परिस्सदुं वण्णं।
जीवस्स एस वण्णो जिणेहिं ववहारदो उत्तो।।५९।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
तथा जीवे कर्मणां नोकर्मणां च परिस्थितं वर्णम्।
जीवस्यैष वर्णो जिनैर्व्यवहारत उक्तः।।५९।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Similarly, seeing the color of karma and nokamma situated in/around the soul, "this is the soul's color" — thus the Jinas have spoken by vyavahāra only.

**COMMENTARY:**
The analogy is now applied directly: karma and nokamma (body-matter) carry material color. They are situated around the soul. From a distance, it looks like the soul is colored. The Jinas, teaching to souls who cannot yet distinguish themselves from their material coverings, use the conventional language: "this is the soul's color." But it is vyavahāra only — conventional speech, not ultimate truth.

---

### GATHA 60

**PRAKRIT:**
गंधरसफासरूवा देहो संठाणमाइया जे य।
सव्वे ववहारस्स य णिच्छयदण्हू वविदसंति।।६०।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
गन्धरसस्पर्शरूपाणि देहः संस्थानादयो ये च।
सर्वे व्यवहारस्य च निश्चयद्रष्टारो व्यपदिशन्ति।।६०।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Smell, taste, touch, form, body, shape etc. — all of these, the bhūtārtha-draṣṭas (those who see ultimate truth) describe only from the vyavahāra standpoint.

**COMMENTARY:**
Even the greatest teachers who see ultimate reality — when they use the language of sense-qualities in relation to the soul — are speaking conventionally. They know they are doing so. This is the mature philosophical position: masters use vyavahāra language because students need it, but masters themselves are never confused about which standpoint they are speaking from.

---

### GATHA 61

**PRAKRIT:**
तत्थ भवे जीवाणं संसारट्ठाण होंति वण्णादी।
संसारपमुक्काणं णत्थि हु वण्णादओ केई।।६१।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
तत्र भवे जीवानां संसारस्थानां भवन्ति वर्णादयः।
संसारप्रमुक्तानां नास्ति खलु वर्णादयः केचित्।।६१।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
In samsāra, for souls in the worldly state, color etc. exist [in the conventional sense]; but for those liberated from samsāra, no color etc. exist whatsoever.

**COMMENTARY:**
A final confirmation: color and other material attributes are associated with the soul in samsāra because karma and nokamma are present. In liberation — when all karma has fallen away — there are no material attributes at all. The liberated soul (siddha) has no color, no body, no shape, no location. If those qualities were truly the soul's own, they would persist even in liberation. Their absence in liberation proves they were never the soul's own nature — only temporary associations.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Samsāric souls appear to have color because karma covers them. Liberated souls have no color whatsoever. This proves color was never the soul's own.

---

## PART 6: THE LOGICAL REFUTATION

### GATHA 62

**PRAKRIT:**
जीवो चेव हि एदे सव्वे भाव ति मण्णसे जदि हि।
जीवस्साजीवस्स य णत्थि विसेसो दु दे कोई।।६२।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
जीवश्चैव हि एते सर्वे भावा इति मन्यसे यदि हि।
जीवस्याजीवस्य च नास्ति विशेषस्तु ते कश्चित्।।६२।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
If you believe that all these bhavas [color, rāga, karma etc.] are indeed the soul — then there remains no distinction whatsoever between jīva and ajīva for you.

**COMMENTARY:**
The reductio argument begins. If the soul is rāga, karma, the body, the guṇasthānas — all material — then in what sense is anything not the soul? The distinction between jīva and ajīva collapses. This is the ultimate philosophical failure: a system that cannot distinguish between consciousness and matter cannot teach liberation, because liberation means consciousness freeing itself from matter.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
If everything you attribute to the soul is actually matter — then you have no meaningful concept of jīva at all.

---

### GATHA 63

**PRAKRIT:**
अह संसारट्ठाणं जीवाणं तुझ होंति वण्णादी।
तम्हा संसारट्था जीवा रूवित्तमावण्णा।।६३।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
अथ संसारस्थानां जीवानां तव भवन्ति वर्णादयः।
तस्मात् संसारस्था जीवा रूपित्वमापन्नाः।।६३।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
If you say that in the samsāric state, color etc. belong to souls — then it follows that samsāric souls have attained the quality of rūpa-ness (form/matter-ness).

**COMMENTARY:**
The argument tightens: if you concede only the samsāric situation (not liberation), and say souls have color there — then souls in samsāra are rūpa, i.e., material, i.e., pudgala. You have made the soul itself into matter. No liberation is then possible, because a pudgala-soul cannot escape pudgala.

---

### GATHA 64

**PRAKRIT:**
एवं पोग्गलदव्वं जीवो तहलक्खणेण मूढमदी।
णिव्वाणमुवगदो वि य जीवत्तं णोग्गलो पत्तो।।६४।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एवं पुद्गलद्रव्यं जीवस्तथालक्षणेन मूढमते।
निर्वाणमुपगतोऽपि च जीवत्वं पुद्गलः प्राप्तः।।६४।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
By that reasoning, the foolish-minded one is calling pudgala-dravya "the soul" — and even at liberation, pudgala would have attained the state of jīva-ness. An absurd conclusion.

**COMMENTARY:**
The argument reaches its absurd conclusion: if the soul is defined by material qualities, then pudgala becomes the soul. And since the "liberated soul" in nirvāṇa supposedly loses all material associations, it would have lost its soul-nature — and pudgala (body-matter) would somehow achieve jīva-hood. The entire framework collapses. This conclusive reductio forces the reader back to the only coherent position: the soul is consciousness alone, never material.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
If soul = matter, then matter at liberation would become "soul." Absurd. The only coherent conclusion: consciousness is the soul, and matter is other.

---

## PART 7: THE CLASSIFICATION PROBLEM

### GATHA 65

**PRAKRIT:**
एकं च दोण्णि तिण्णि य चत्तारि य पंच इंदिया जीवा।
बादरपज्जत्तिदरा पयडीओ णामकम्मस्स।।६५।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एकं वा द्वे त्रीणि च चत्वारि च पञ्चेन्द्रियाणि जीवाः।
बादरपर्याप्तेतराः प्रकृतयो नामकर्मणः।।६५।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
One, two, three, four, five-sensed beings — bādara (gross), paryāpta (fully developed), itara (undeveloped) — these are the species-types (prakṛtis) of nāma-karma.

**COMMENTARY:**
Even the most familiar classifications of souls — one-sensed to five-sensed, gross or subtle, developed or undeveloped — are declared to be nāma-karma's prakṛtis (species-determining material configurations). When we say "this is a five-sensed being" or "this is a gross-bodied soul," we are naming the nāma-karma types associated with that soul — not the soul's own nature. The soul does not have a sense-count; it has upayoga.

---

### GATHA 66

**PRAKRIT:**
एदाहि य णिव्वत्ता जीवट्ठाणा उ करणभूदाहिं।
पयडीहिं पोग्गलमइहिं ताहिं कहं भण्णदे जीवो।।६६।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
एताभिश्च निर्वृत्तानि जीवस्थानानि करणभूताभिः।
प्रकृतिभिः पुद्गलमयीभिस्ताभिः कथं भण्यते जीवः।।६६।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The jīva-sthānas (soul-classifications) are produced by these nāma-karma prakṛtis which are instrumental (karaṇa-bhūta); and these prakṛtis are pudgala-made. How then can those [jīva-sthānas] be called the soul?

**COMMENTARY:**
The logic is clean: jīva-sthānas (the fourteen soul-classifications by sense capacity) are produced by nāma-karma prakṛtis. Those prakṛtis are material. Therefore jīva-sthānas are pudgala-products — instruments of classification, not the soul itself. How can something produced by matter be called consciousness? It cannot.

---

## PART 8: CONVENTION AND REALITY — THE FINAL DISTINCTIONS

### GATHA 67

**PRAKRIT:**
पज्जत्तापज्जत्ता जे सुहुमा बादरा य जे चेव।
देहस्स जीवसण्णा सुत्ते ववहारदो उत्ता।।६७।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
पर्याप्तापर्याप्तास्ये सूक्ष्माः बादराश्च ये चैव।
देहस्य जीवसंज्ञाः सूत्रे व्यवहारतः उक्ताः।।६७।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
Paryāpta, aparyāpta, sūkṣma (subtle), bādara (gross) — these designations for the body have been called "jīva-saṃjñā" (soul-designations) in the sūtras only by vyavahāra (convention).

**COMMENTARY:**
The Āgamic sūtras call the body-types "jīva-designations" — this is why students sometimes think these body-categories are the soul. But these are body-designations, not soul-designations. Paryāpta (fully developed) and aparyāpta (undeveloped), sūkṣma (subtle body) and bādara (gross body) — these are descriptions of the physical apparatus, called "jīva-saṃjñā" only by convention, for identification purposes.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
When Āgamas say "paryāpta jīva" or "sūkṣma jīva," that is conventional language about body-types. Not a statement about the soul's own nature.

---

### GATHA 68

**PRAKRIT:**
मोहणकम्मस्सुदया दु वण्णिया जे इमे गुणट्ठाणा।
ते कह हवंति जीवा जे णिच्चमचेदणा उत्ता।।६८।।

**SANSKRIT CHHAYA:**
मोहनकर्मण उदयातु वर्णितानि यानीमानि गुणस्थानानि।
तानि कथं भवन्ति जीवा यानि नित्यमचेतनान्युक्तानि।।६८।।

**LITERAL ENGLISH TRANSLATION:**
The guṇasthānas described as arising from the udaya (rising) of mohanīya-karma — how can those be the soul? For they are declared eternally devoid of consciousness (achetana).

**ANVAYARTHA:**
**मोहणकम्मस्सुदया** = from the arising of mohanīya-karma; **वण्णिया** = described; **जे इमे गुणट्ठाणा** = which these guṇasthānas are; **ते कह हवंति जीवा** = how can those be jīvas; **जे णिच्चमचेदणा उत्ता** = which are declared eternally devoid of consciousness (achetana).

**COMMENTARY:**
The final gatha of Adhikar 1 delivers the closing argument. The fourteen guṇasthānas — from mithyādṛṣṭi (false perception) to siddha (liberation) — are described in terms of the rising and falling of mohanīya-karma (deluding karma). They are states that appear because karma acts. And the Āgamas themselves declare the guṇasthānas to be eternally achetana (devoid of consciousness) — because they are karma-produced processes, not the soul's own activity.

How can what is eternally achetana be called the soul? The soul is defined by chetanā. Therefore, guṇasthānas — however spiritually significant — are not the soul itself, only markers of the soul's karmic situation.

The Adhikar closes with "इति जीवाजीवौ पृथग्भूत्वा निष्क्रान्तौ" — "Thus jīva and ajīva, having been separated, have exited [the arena]." The discrimination is complete: jīva is pure consciousness-knowing; ajīva is all that is material. They have been cleanly separated by 30 gathas of relentless philosophical precision.

**SIMPLY PUT:**
Even guṇasthānas are driven by mohanīya-karma — they are achetana (not conscious) by the Āgamas' own declaration. How can the non-conscious be the soul? It cannot.

**CONTEMPLATE:**
If your spiritual stage right now — wherever you are on the path — is also a karma-driven process, not your true self... what does that make "you"? What is the knower of all these stages?

---
