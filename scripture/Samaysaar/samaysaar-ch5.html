Samaysaar · Adhikar 4 · Gathas 164–180

Influx of Karmaआस्रव अधिकार

All 17 gathas of the fourth Adhikar — how karma enters, and why the jñānī does not bind

Āsrava is not an external invasion — it is the soul's own transformation in the state of rāga-dveṣa-moha. The jñānī carries previously bound karma but does not add to it. The operative principle: the inner bhāva free from rāgādi is the pure knower only.

"The inner bhāva joined with rāgādi, done by the soul, is declared the binder. The bhāva free from rāgādi is non-binding — and is the pure knower only." — Samaysaar Adhikar 4, Gatha 167

About This Adhikar

Āsrava: The Soul's Own Doing

Adhikar 4 is Samaysaar's most technically precise chapter on the mechanism of karma-influx. The central reframe is immediate: āsrava is not an external invasion — it is the soul's own transformation (ananya-pariṇāma). Mithyātva, aviramana, kaṣāya, yoga — these are not external forces descending on the soul but the soul's own states in its defiled condition.

The Adhikar then resolves its central puzzle: the jñānī (samyag-dṛṣṭi) carries previously bound karma that continues to operate. Yet the jñānī is declared abandha — non-binding. Why? Because new bondage requires two co-conditions: material karma-factors (drva-pratyayas) AND the soul's bhāva-āsrava (rāga-dveṣa-moha). The jñānī provides the first but lacks the second. Without rāgādi, the material factors cannot independently produce new bondage.

The Adhikar closes with the digestive fire analogy: previously bound pratyayas in a jñānī who slips from shuddhanaya (the pure standpoint) do bind karma — like food transforming through digestive fire. The constant instruction: remain in shuddhanaya.

Core DoctrineAnanya-Pariṇāma — Āsrava Is the Soul's Own Transformation

Karma does not invade from outside. The soul generates its own āsrava through rāga-dveṣa-moha — the soul's own transformations in the defiled condition. Mithyātva, aviramana, kaṣāya, yoga are ananya-pariṇāma: not-other-than-soul. The soul is the author of its own bondage — and therefore also the author of its own liberation.

Key ResolutionJñāyaka Kevalam — The Pure Knower Does Not Bind

The inner bhāva mixed with rāgādi binds karma. The inner bhāva free from rāgādi is the pure knower (jñāyaka kevalam) — it does not bind. The samyag-dṛṣṭi lacks bhāva-āsrava (rāga-dveṣa-moha in the mithyātva sense). Without it, the material karma-factors that remain from prior bondage cannot bind new karma independently.

Practical WarningNaya-Parihīna — When the Jñānī Slips from Shuddhanaya

Previously bound pratyayas in the jñānī bind karma when the jñānī slips from shuddhanaya (the pure self-standpoint). Like food transforming through digestive fire — the pratyayas are the food; slipping from shuddhanaya is the fire. The instruction: remain in shuddhanaya continuously. Every degree of departure is a degree of vulnerability.

Part 1 · Gathas 164–165 · What Is Āsrava?

The Adhikar opens with a definitional reframe: āsrava is not external. It is the soul's own transformation in the state of mithyātva, non-restraint, passions, and yoga. The soul is the origin of its own bondage.

Gatha 164
Āsrava DefinitionAnanya-PariṇāmaFive Types

मिच्छत्तं अविरमणं कसायजोगा य सण्णसण्णा दु।
बहुविहभया जीवे तस्सेव अणण्णपरिणामा।।१६४।।

Mithyātva, aviramana (non-restraint), kaṣāya-yoga, and saṃjñā-asaṃjñā — these many types of āsrava, with all their divisions, are the soul's own ananya-pariṇāma (not-other transformations).

The opening verse immediately reframes what āsrava is. Ananya-pariṇāma means "transformation that is not-other-than-the-soul." Āsrava is not external force invading from outside but the soul's own state in its defiled condition. The five types: mithyātva (wrong orientation), aviramana (non-restraint), kaṣāya (passions), yoga (mind-speech-body activity), saṃjñā/asaṃjñā (intentional/unintentional aspects) — these are the soul's own conscious transformations, not alien matter. Since the soul generates its own bondage, it also holds the key to its own liberation.

Simply: Āsrava — mithyātva, non-restraint, passions, yoga — with all their types, are the soul's own transformations. Not external invasion. The soul makes its own bondage.
Gatha 165
Causal ChainRāga-DveṣaJñānāvaraṇa

णाणावरणादीयस्स ते दु कम्मस्स कारणं होंति।
तेसिं पि होिद जीवो य रागदोसादिभावकरो।।१६५।।

These (āsravas) are the cause of jñānāvaraṇa and other karmas. And the cause of those (āsravas) is the soul itself — the maker of rāga-dveṣa and other bhāvas.

A precise causal chain: the soul generates rāga-dveṣa-moha (bhāva-āsrava) → rāga-dveṣa-moha causes material karma influx → karma conditions future states. The soul is the originating cause. Understanding the chain completely is what enables its dissolution. The key insight: rāga-dveṣa-moha are the āsravas in their truest sense. Pudgala-karma merely follows the soul's own state. Remove the root bhāva; the material binding has no cause.

Simply: Āsravas cause jñānāvaraṇa and other karmas. The cause of those āsravas is the soul itself — by making rāga, dveṣa, and other bhāvas.

Contemplate: If you are the author of your own bondage — not through external forces but through your own inner states — what does that mean for the path to freedom?

Part 2 · Gatha 166 · Jñānī Has No Āsrava-Bandha

The central paradox introduced: the samyag-dṛṣṭi's previously bound karma still operates — they know it, see it. Yet the jñānī is declared to have no āsrava-bandha. There is āsrava-nirodha — cessation, not influx.

Gatha 166
Āsrava-NirodhaSamyag-DṛṣṭiKnowing Without Binding

णित्थि दु आसवबंधो सम्मादिट्ठिस्स आसविणरोहो।
संते पुव्विणिबद्धे जाणिद सो ते अबंधंतो।।१६६।।

For the samyag-dṛṣṭi, there is no āsrava-bandha (influx-bondage) — there is āsrava-nirodha (cessation of influx). He knows the previously bound karmas as existing — but without binding new ones.

The central paradox stated: previously bound karma still exists and operates in the samyag-dṛṣṭi (santi pūrva-nibaddhāni — the previously bound ones are still present). Yet there is no new āsrava-bandha — only āsrava-nirodha (cessation). The phrase jānāti tān abadhnan — "he knows those while not binding" — is the jñānī's mode. Observing the operation of prior karma with full clarity, without adding new bondage. Knowing without bonding is the jñānī's characteristic condition.

Simply: For the samyag-dṛṣṭi, there is no new āsrava-bondage — there is cessation of āsrava. He knows the previously bound karmas as present — but binds no new ones.
Part 3 · Gatha 167 · The Law — Rāgī Bhāva Binds, Jñāna Bhāva Doesn't

The operative principle of the entire Adhikar in a single verse: bondage is determined by the quality of the inner bhāva, not the outer action. Rāgādi-mixed bhāva binds. Rāgādi-free bhāva is pure knowing — jñāyaka kevalam.

Gatha 167 — Featured Verse
Jñāyaka KevalamRāgādi-MuktaCentral LawBhāva-Āsrava

भावो रागादिजुदो जीवेण कदो दु बंधगो भणिदो।
रागादिविप्पमुक्को अबंधगो जाणगो णवरि।।१६७।।

"The inner bhāva joined with rāgādi, done by the soul, is declared the binder. The bhāva free from rāgādi is non-binding — and is the pure knower only (jñāyaka kevalam)."

Bondage is a quality of the inner bhāva, not of external action. Two souls may perform the same outer act: one with rāgādi (attachment/aversion), one without. Only the first creates new bondage. The second — whose bhāva is free from rāga — is jñāyaka kevalam: purely the knower. The knowing itself is non-binding; attachment to what is known is binding. The Ātmakhyāti uses the iron-magnet analogy: a needle mixed with iron-dust (rāgādi) is attracted to the magnet (karma). The same needle, free of iron-dust, rests in its own nature. The jñānī's bhāva, free of rāgādi, rests in pure knowing — karma has no purchase on it.

Simply: The inner bhāva mixed with rāgādi binds karma. The inner bhāva free from rāgādi does not bind — it is purely the knower.

Contemplate: Is your awareness mixed with attraction/aversion — or is it simply knowing? The difference between the two is the difference between bondage and freedom.

Part 4 · Gatha 168 · When Karma-Bhāva Falls, It Does Not Return

The ripe fruit analogy: karma departs naturally, without violence, when jñāna is present. Once a karma-bhāva fully passes through the soul's experience without being renewed by rāga, it does not regenerate.

Gatha 168
Ripe FruitNon-RenewalNatural Departure

पक्के फलम्हि पिडिए जह ण फलं बज्झए पुणो विंटे।
जीवस्स कम्मभावे पिडिए ण पुणोदयमुवेिद।।१६८।।

Just as a ripe fruit, once fallen, does not again attach to the branch — so when the karma-bhāva falls from the soul's experience, it does not again arise in new form.

A ripe fruit falls because ripeness and the branch-connection cannot coexist — gravity simply does its work. No one tears the fruit off; it falls when the conditions are complete. Similarly, when the karma-bhāva fully passes through its experience, it does not regenerate. The jñānī who knows this does not cling to the departing karma's bhāva, nor does it re-attach. Karma exhausts not through violent suppression but through non-renewal: the soul stops regenerating it with fresh rāga. What was seeded by attachment starves when the attachment is withdrawn.

Simply: A ripe fruit falls and doesn't reattach to the branch. Similarly, when the karma-bhāva falls from the soul's experience, it doesn't arise again in new form.
Part 5 · Gatha 169 · Previously Bound Pratyayas — Like Clods of Earth

The jñānī's previously bound karma-factors are like clods of earth — inert, physically present, but not "the self." They belong to the karma-body, not to the pure jīva.

Gatha 169
Pṛthvī-PiṇḍaKarma-ŚarīraPratyaya

पुढवीपिंडसमाणा पुव्विणिबद्धा दु पच्चया तस्स।
कम्मसरीरेण दु ते बद्धा सव्वे वि णाणिस्स।।१६९।।

For the jñānī, all the previously bound pratyayas are like clods of earth — they are all bound only to the karma-body (karmaśarīra), not to the jīva.

Earth is inert, external, physically there — but it is not the self. The previously bound karma-factors (pratyayas) are like this: bound to the karma-body, not to the pure jīva. The jñānī knows this distinction not just conceptually but with lived certainty. Prior karma remains present — but it belongs to the karma-body (the subtle material structure), not to consciousness itself. The Ātmakhyāti: these pratyayas, accumulated in ajñāna (prior ignorance), are pudgala (material) transformations — achetan (non-sentient). They will dissolve by their own nature. The jñānī simply does not add more.

Simply: For the jñānī, all previously bound pratyayas are like clods of earth — bound only to the karma-body, not to the jīva itself.
Part 6 · Gathas 170–172 · The Paradox — Why Jñānī Is Abandha Despite Ongoing Karma

The technical resolution of the paradox: drva-pratyayas do bind some karma through jñāna-darśana-guṇas every moment — this is admitted. Yet the jñānī is abandha because the binding is through jñāna-guṇa (not rāgādi), and at the lowest state. The closer to full cāritra, the less binding occurs.

Gatha 170
AbandhaJñāna-GuṇaFour Types of Āsrava

चउविह अणेयभेयं बंधंते णाणदंसणगुणेिहं।
समए समए जम्हा तेण अबंधो िति णाणी दु।।१७०।।

Because the four types of drva-āsrava bind many kinds of karma through the jñāna-darśana-guṇas at every moment — for this very reason, the jñānī is declared abandha (non-bound).

The logical pivot: yes, the drva-pratyayas bind karma at the material level every moment — this is admitted. But they bind through the jñāna-darśana-guṇa-pariṇāma — through the knowing-seeing quality — not through rāgādi. When the binding cause is jñāna-guṇa rather than rāga-dveṣa-moha, the jñānī is declared abandha from the paramārtha perspective. The real āsrava (bhāva-āsrava) is absent; the material binding is residual and does not extend samsāra in a principal way.

Simply: Because four types of drva-āsrava bind many types of karma through jñāna-darśana-guṇas every moment — for this very reason the jñānī is declared non-bound.
Gatha 171
Jñāna-GuṇaJaghanya-BhāvaResidual Binding

जम्हा दु जहण्णादो णाणगुणादो पुणो वि परिणमिदे।
अण्णत्तं णाणगुणो तेण दु सो बंधगो भणिदो।।१७१।।

Because from the lowest state of jñāna-guṇa, the jñāna-guṇa transforms again to another form — for this reason it is declared a binding cause.

The jñāna-guṇa in its lowest state (jaghanya — minimum capacity of the samyag-dṛṣṭi before full cāritra) still transforms, flickering between states. This minimal flickering is not stable continuous pure knowing. Because it transforms to another form (anyatva — differentness), there is some residual material binding. The Ātmakhyāti: kṣāyopaśamika jñāna (arising from partial suppression-destruction of covering karma) rests on one object for only an antarmuhūrta, then moves. This impermanence of focus in "lowest state" mode produces some karma. The instruction: press toward higher states of darśana, jñāna, cāritra.

Simply: Because the jñāna-guṇa in its lowest state transforms again to another form — for this reason it is declared a binding cause.
Gatha 172
Three JewelsJaghanya PariṇāmaPudgala-Karma

दंसणणाणचरितं जं परिणमदे जहण्णभावेण।
णाणी तेण दु बज्झिद पोग्गलकम्मेण विविहेण।।१७२।।

When darśana, jñāna, and cāritra transform in their lowest states — the jñānī is bound by various pudgala-karma through that.

The three jewels in their lowest states still produce some pudgala-karma binding. This is minimal — not the kind that extends samsāra significantly — and dissolves rapidly as the soul's overall trajectory is toward purity. The Ātmakhyāti's instruction: keep pressing toward the fullest expression of darśana, jñāna, and cāritra. The closer one approaches full yathākhyāta-cāritra (complete conduct), the less residual binding occurs. The path is not to avoid prior karma but to continuously express the three jewels at their fullest.

Simply: When darśana, jñāna, and cāritra transform in their lowest states — the jñānī is bound by various pudgala-karma through that.
Part 7 · Gathas 173–176 · The Samyag-Dṛṣṭi and Previously Bound Karma

All previously bound karma-factors remain present in the samyag-dṛṣṭi. Like dormant pratyayas — they exist but don't actively bind. The young-girl / young-woman analogy shows when and why they activate. The conclusion: samyag-dṛṣṭi is abandha because āsrava-bhāva is absent.

Gatha 173
Previously BoundUpayoga-PrayogyaKarma-Bhāva

सव्वे पुव्विणिबद्धा दु पच्चया अत्थि सम्मदिट्ठिस्स।
उवओगपाओगं बंधंते कम्मभावेण।।१७३।।

All previously bound pratyayas are present in the samyag-dṛṣṭi. Through upayoga-prayogya (what comes into the sphere of consciousness-application), they bind through karma-bhāva.

The samyag-dṛṣṭi carries all previously bound karma-factors — they did not vanish at the moment of right perception. What changed is the quality of their operation. The pratyayas that come into the active field of upayoga (consciousness-application) produce some binding through karma-bhāva. Whether this binding is major or minimal depends on what comes next — G174-176 elaborate the conditions.

Simply: All previously bound pratyayas exist in the samyag-dṛṣṭi. Through what comes into the sphere of upayoga (consciousness-application), they bind through karma-bhāva.
Gatha 174
NirupabhogyaUpabhogyaDormant to Active

होदूण णिरुवभोज्जा तह बंधिद जह हवंति उवभोज्जा।
सत्तट्ठिविहा भूदा णाणावरणादिभावेिहं।।१७४।।

Having been non-enjoyable (dormant/nirupabhogya), they bind in such a way that they become enjoyable (active/upabhogya) — these seven-to-eight types of jñānāvaraṇa and other karma-bhāvas.

Previously bound karma-pratyayas exist in two states: nirupabhogya (dormant, not-yet-ripened) and upabhogya (active, ripened). When dormant pratyayas mature into the active state, they bind the corresponding types of karma — jñānāvaraṇa, darśanāvaraṇa, vedanīya, mohanīya, āyu, nāma, gotra, antarāya (seven-to-eight main karma-types). The metaphor of G175 will illustrate this transition between dormant and active states.

Simply: Having been dormant, they bind in such a way that they become active — these seven-to-eight types (jñānāvaraṇa etc.) of karma-bhāvas.
Gatha 175
Young Girl / Young WomanAnalogyActivation Conditions

संता दु णिरुवभोज्जा बाला इत्थी जहेह पुिरसस्स।
बंधिद ते उवभोज्जे तरुणी इत्थी जह णरस्स।।१७५।।

They exist as non-enjoyable (dormant) — like a young girl in a man's household. They bind as enjoyable (active) — like a young woman does to a man.

The analogy is direct: a young girl in a household is present but creates no romantic attachment (nirupabhogya — dormant). The same person grown into a young woman can bind the man with attachment (upabhogya — active). The previously bound pratyayas in the jñānī are like the girl — present in the karmic inheritance but not currently ripening into active bondage. When the soul slips from shuddhanaya into rāgādi, those pratyayas become like the young woman — active, binding new karma. The presence of pratyayas is not sufficient for binding; the soul's bhāva-state is the triggering condition.

Simply: Dormant pratyayas are like a young girl — present but not binding. Active pratyayas are like a young woman — they bind through the conditions they create.
Gatha 176
AbandhaĀsrava-Bhāva AbsenceResolution

एदेण कारणेण दु सम्मादिट्ठी अबंधगो भणिद्रो।
आसवभावाभावे ण पच्चया बंधगा भणिदा।।१७६।।

For this reason, the samyag-dṛṣṭi is declared non-binding (abandha). In the absence of āsrava-bhāva, the pratyayas are not declared binding agents.

The resolution stated plainly: the samyag-dṛṣṭi is abandha because āsrava-bhāva (rāga-dveṣa-moha as operative states in the mithyātva-sense) is absent. Without āsrava-bhāva, the drva-pratyayas cannot produce new bondage on their own. New karma requires both: material factors AND the soul's rāga-bhāva working together. The samyag-dṛṣṭi provides the first (old karma continues to operate) but not the second. Therefore: abandha.

Simply: For this reason the samyag-dṛṣṭi is declared non-binding. Without āsrava-bhāva, the pratyayas are not declared binding agents.

Contemplate: The soul is surrounded by the results of its past. But the past only binds the future when you add present rāga to it. What happens when you stop adding?

Part 8 · Gathas 177–178 · Rāga-Dveṣa-Moha Absent in Samyag-Dṛṣṭi

The three root āsravas — rāga, dveṣa, moha — are directly declared absent from the samyag-dṛṣṭi's conscious experience. The causal chain is then completed: without rāgādi, the four causes cannot operate; without the four causes, the eight types of karma cannot be bound.

Gatha 177
Rāga AbsentDveṣa AbsentMoha AbsentSamyag-Dṛṣṭi

रागो दोसो मोहो य आसवा णित्थि सम्मदिट्ठिस्स।
तम्हा आसवभावेण विणा हेदू ण पच्चया होंति।।१७७।।

Rāga, dveṣa, and moha — these āsravas do not exist in the samyag-dṛṣṭi. Therefore, without āsrava-bhāva, the pratyayas do not become binding causes.

The direct statement: mithyātva-based rāga-dveṣa-moha is absent from the samyag-dṛṣṭi's experience. This does not mean the samyag-dṛṣṭi never has any rāga in a loose sense — it means deep-delusion-based rāga (with its attendant passionate possessiveness of self-misidentification) is gone. The Ātmakhyāti's nuanced commentary: mithyātva-connected rāgādi is what is meant here. The residual rāga from cāritra-moha in avirati-samyag-dṛṣṭi is not the principal āsrava referred to — it is insufficient to be the binding cause in the principal sense.

Simply: Rāga, dveṣa, and moha — the real āsravas — do not exist in the samyag-dṛṣṭi. Therefore, without āsrava-bhāva, pratyayas are not binding causes.
Gatha 178
Four HetusEight Karma TypesCausal Chain Complete

हेदू चदुव्वियप्पो अट्ठिवियप्पस्स कारणं भणिदं।
तेसिं पि य रागादी तेसिमभावे ण बज्झंति।।१७८।।

The four types of hetu (causes) are declared the cause of the eight types (of karma). And the cause of those hetus is rāgādi — in their absence, no binding takes place.

The causal chain is completed systematically: 8 karma types ← 4 hetu types (mithyātva, aviramana, kaṣāya, yoga) ← rāgādi (rāga, dveṣa, moha) ← absent in samyag-dṛṣṭi. Therefore: no binding. The logical structure is clean — remove the root cause, the entire chain collapses. Samyak-dṛṣṭi removes rāgādi as a structural consequence of right perception — not through willpower but through the very nature of seeing correctly. The bondage-chain has a first link; remove that link and nothing holds.

Simply: The four cause-types produce the eight karma types. Their cause is rāgādi. In their absence (samyag-dṛṣṭi), no karma is bound.
Part 9 · Gathas 179–180 · The Digestive Fire — When the Jñānī Slips

The Adhikar closes with the digestive fire analogy: when the jñānī slips from shuddhanaya, previously bound pratyayas bind karma — just as food transforms through digestive fire. Remain in shuddhanaya. That is the closing instruction.

Gatha 179
Digestive Fire AnalogyFood → Many Forms

जह पुिरसेणाहारो गिहिदो परिणमिद सो अणेयविहं।
मंसवसारुहिरादी भावे उदरिग्गसंजुतो।।१७९।।

Just as food taken by a person, combined with the digestive fire, transforms into many forms — flesh, fat, blood, and others —

Food in itself is neutral. Combined with the body's digestive fire, it transforms into multiple forms — flesh, fat, blood, and others. The food is the previously bound pratyayas; the digestive fire is the soul slipping from shuddhanaya into rāgādi. This sets up the parallel in G180.

Simply: Just as food taken by a person — combined with digestive fire — transforms into flesh, fat, blood, and other forms —
Gatha 180
Naya-ParihīnaShuddhanayaClosing Verse

तह णाणिस्स दु पुव्वं जे बद्धा पच्चया बहुवियप्पं।
बज्झंते कम्मं ते णयपरिहीणा दु ते जीवा।।१८०।।

Similarly, for the jñānī, the previously bound pratyayas bind karma in many forms — for those souls who have slipped away from right naya (shuddhanaya).

The analogy completes: previously bound pratyayas in the jñānī, when combined with the "fire" of wrong-naya (deviation from shuddhanaya — the pure standpoint), transform into new karma of many types. The condition: naya-parihīna — bereft of right naya. This answers the Adhikar's central question: what makes previously bound pratyayas bind again? Answer: slipping from shuddhanaya. The moment consciousness falls from the pure self-standpoint into rāgādi territory, dormant pratyayas activate — like food activating through digestive fire. The practical instruction: remain in shuddhanaya. Every departure is a vulnerability. Every return is safety.

Simply: Similarly, the previously bound pratyayas of the jñānī bind karma in many forms — for those souls who have slipped from right naya (shuddhanaya).

Contemplate: What does it mean to "slip from shuddhanaya"? Can you notice the moment when pure knowing slides into something that starts selecting, preferring, rejecting — and return to just knowing?

इति आस्रवो निष्क्रान्तः।

Thus āsrava has departed. It entered the stage intoxicated with pride — the formidable warrior of karma-influx. Jñāna, the incomparable bow-wielder, met it in full battle. Once seen clearly — as the soul's own transformation, not an external invader — āsrava loses its mystique. Having been unmasked, it exits the stage.
