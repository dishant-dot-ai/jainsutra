Samaysaar · Adhikar 3 · Gathas 145–163

Merit & Demeritपुण्य-पाप अधिकार

All 19 gathas of the third Adhikar — showing that both puṇya and pāpa bind, and only jñāna liberates

The most challenging teaching in Samaysaar for the conventionally religious: merit is not the path to liberation. The golden chain binds as surely as the iron one. Only the soul established in its own paramārtha — not accumulating karma, not performing bāla-tapa — can cross samsāra.

About This Adhikar

Punya-Paap: The Two Chains

Adhikar 3 delivers one of the most radical and misunderstood teachings in all of Jain philosophy: puṇya (merit) is not the path to moksha. It binds just as surely as pāpa (sin), only with a golden chain rather than an iron one. The soul enchanted by merit thinks it is progressing toward liberation — but it is only moving toward a more comfortable seat in samsāra.

Kundkundacharya systematically dismantles the assumption that rituals, austerities, vows, and meritorious acts lead to liberation. Without the foundation of paramārtha — genuine establishment in one's own nature — all such practice is bāla-tapa (childish austerity) and bāla-vrata (childish observance). The Atmakhyati of Amṛtacandra uses a theatrical metaphor throughout: karma entered the stage playing two roles — puṇya (the beautiful actor) and pāpa (the ugly actor). Jñāna unmasks both as the same karma. Having been seen clearly, the actor exits the stage entirely.

The Adhikar closes with a precise threefold anatomy of obstruction: mithyātva covers samyaktva, ajñāna covers jñāna, kaṣāya covers cāritra — showing that the soul's three jewels are inherently present, only obscured. The path is not construction but uncovering.

Core DoctrineThe Golden Chain — Shubha and Ashubha Both Bind

A golden fetter and an iron fetter both imprison equally. Shubha karma (merit) leads to comfortable samsāric existence — higher births, refined pleasures — but not to moksha. The seeking soul must transcend both pāpa (which it instinctively avoids) and puṇya (which it instinctively pursues). Both are karma; both bind.

Key CritiqueBāla-Tapa / Bāla-Vrata — Childish Practice Without Paramārtha

All external observances — vows, austerities, niyamas, śīlas — performed without inner establishment in paramārtha are declared by the omniscient Jinas to be "childish." They do not produce karma-destruction. Only the soul established in its own paramārtha — samaya, the pure knowing self — achieves nirvāṇa.

Culminating MapThree Obstructions — Three Jewels Covered, Not Absent

Mithyātva karma covers samyaktva; ajñāna karma covers jñāna; kaṣāya karma covers cāritra. The three jewels of the ratnatraya are not absent from the soul — they are its inherent nature, temporarily obscured like the whiteness of cloth covered by dirt. Remove the covering; the jewels shine naturally.

Part 1 · Gathas 145–146 · The Equal Binding Power

The Adhikar opens by establishing the foundational problem: shubha karma is "good-natured" only in a relative sense. Absolutely, anything that perpetuates samsāra cannot be called good. The central image — golden chain and iron chain — names the entire teaching.

Gatha 145
Kuśīla / SuśīlaShubha KarmaSamsāra

कम्ममसुहं कुसीलं सुहकम्मं चावि जाणह सुसीलं।
कह तं होिद सुसीलं जं संसारं पवेसेिद।।१४५।।

Know that ashubha karma is kuśīla (bad-natured) and shubha karma is suśīla (good-natured). But how can something be truly suśīla when it sends one into samsāra?

The verse opens with a seeming concession — shubha karma is "good-natured" — and then immediately undercuts it with a devastating question: if shubha karma perpetuates samsāra, in what meaningful sense is it good? Kundkundacharya is not condemning moral behavior. He is making a technical point: anything that keeps the soul cycling in samsāra cannot be the ultimate good. The truly suśīla can only be what leads to moksha. Shubha karma, by its nature, does not.

Simply: Ashubha karma is called bad-natured. Shubha karma is called good-natured. But how can it truly be "good" if it still sends the soul into samsāra?

Contemplate: Is there a difference between doing good as a karmic strategy (to earn puṇya) versus doing good as an expression of your nature? Which one binds?

Gatha 146 — Featured Verse
Golden ChainIron ChainShubha = AshubhaCentral Doctrine

सोवण्णियं पि णियलं बंधिद कालायसं पि जह पुिसं।
बंधिद एवं जीवं सुहमसुहं वा कदं कम्मं।।१४६।।

"Just as a golden fetter and an iron fetter both bind a person equally — so shubha and ashubha karma, both done, bind the soul."

This is the pivotal verse of Adhikar 3 — the golden chain and iron chain analogy that crystallizes the entire teaching. A prisoner bound with gold is no freer than one bound with iron. The comfort of the chain is irrelevant to the fact of imprisonment. Similarly, the soul bound by meritorious karma — higher births, divine pleasures, good circumstances — is still bound. The Atmakhyati elaborates: shubha karma leads to heavenly states and noble human births; but these are still within samsāra. The soul experiencing divine pleasures has not moved toward moksha — it has only postponed the reckoning and deepened the attachment.

Simply: A golden chain and an iron chain both imprison equally. In the same way, shubha karma and ashubha karma both bind the soul. There is no "good bondage."

Contemplate: What "golden chains" do you carry — attachments to pleasant conditions, good circumstances, favored identities — that you don't recognize as chains at all?

Part 2 · Gathas 147–149 · Avoid All Karma Association

Having established that both bind, the instruction follows naturally: avoid all entanglement with karma. A two-verse analogy — the wise person who avoids bad company — makes the principle concrete.

Gatha 147
SvādhīnatāKusīla SaṃsargaDetachment

तम्हा दु कुसीलेिह य रागं मा कुणह मा व संसग्गं।
साहीणो िह िवणासो कुसीलसंसग्गरायेण।।१४७।।

Therefore, do not make attachment to or association with kusīla karmas. Your independence (svādhīnatā) is truly destroyed by attachment through kusīla association.

The word svādhīnatā (independence, self-sovereignty) is central: liberation is about recovering the soul's own autonomy. Every association with karma — even "good" karma done from attachment — erodes this independence. The soul attached to its own virtuous acts, its own tapas, its own vows, has exchanged one form of servitude for another. Independence comes not from doing more meritorious things but from ceasing to be attached to the doer-identity altogether.

Simply: Therefore, don't develop attachment or association with kusīla karmas. Your own freedom is destroyed by attachment to such association.
Gatha 148
AnalogyKutsita-ŚīlaPractical Wisdom

जह णाम को िव पुिरसो कुिच्छयसीलं जणं िवयाणित्ता।
वज्जेिद तेण समयं संसग्गं रागकरणं च।।१४८।।

Just as a person, knowing another to be of bad character, avoids association with him and the attachment that comes from such contact —

Gatha 148 begins a two-verse analogy. In ordinary life, a wise person who recognizes someone of bad character keeps their distance — not from moral superiority but from practical wisdom. They know that prolonged association breeds attachment, and attachment leads to being pulled into the other's orbit. The analogy sets up Gatha 149, which applies this reasoning to karma itself.

Simply: Just as a wise person avoids a bad-charactered person knowing that association leads to attachment —
Gatha 149
Svabhāva-RatāKarma-PrakritiNatural Avoidance

एमेव कम्मपयडीसीलसहावं च कुिच्छदं णादु।
वज्जंिति पिरहरंित य तस्संसग्गं सहावरदा।।१४९।।

Similarly, those who are established in their own svabhāva (svabhāva-ratā), knowing the inherently bad nature of karma-prakriti, avoid and abandon all association with it.

The analogy is complete. The key phrase is svabhāva-ratā — those deeply absorbed in/delighted by their own essential nature. This avoidance is not willpower or external discipline; it is the natural gravitational pull of self-knowledge. When you know your own nature as pure consciousness, karma's glamour loses its attraction automatically. You don't need to force yourself away from it — you simply find yourself uninterested, as a person who has eaten well is naturally uninterested in inferior food.

Simply: Similarly, those established in their own svabhāva — knowing the defiling nature of karma-prakriti — naturally avoid all association with it.
Part 3 · Gatha 150 · Rāgī Binds, Virāgī is Freed

The law of bondage and liberation stated in its most compact form: attachment is the binding mechanism; detachment is the releasing mechanism. The Jina's instruction follows directly.

Gatha 150
RāgīVirāgīJina's LawBondage Mechanism

रतो बंधिद कम्मं मुच्चिद जीवो िवरागसंपत्तो।
एसो िजणोवदेसो तम्हा कम्मेसु मा रज्ज।।१५०।।

The rāgī (one with attachment) binds karma; the soul endowed with virāga (detachment) is freed. This is the Jina's instruction — therefore do not be attached to karmas.

Rakto badhnāti, virāga-sampannaḥ mucyate — attached binds, detached is freed. The Jina's teaching is invoked as confirmation of a direct spiritual truth, not an appeal to authority. The one who is rāgī (dyed in attachment) keeps karma accruing; the one who has become virāgī (drained of that dye) is naturally released. The instruction that follows — mā rajyasva — is not a moral commandment but a causal understanding: attachment is the mechanism of bondage. Understand the mechanism; act accordingly.

Simply: The attached one binds karma; the detached one is freed. This is the Jina's teaching — therefore do not be attached to karmas.

Contemplate: Is your relationship to your own practices one of attachment or detachment? Do your good deeds create a subtle pride, ownership, or identity — a golden chain?

Part 4 · Gatha 151 · Paramārtha — The Only Path to Nirvāṇa

The metaphysical center of the Adhikar: what is paramārtha? It is not a doctrine or a scripture. It is the soul itself in its purest state — samaya, the Samayasāra. Only those established in this win nirvāṇa.

Gatha 151
ParamārthaSamayaSvabhāva-SthitāNirvāṇa

परमट्ठो खलु समओ सुद्धो जो केवली मुणी णाणी।
तम्हि िट्ठदा सहावे मुिणणो पावंित िणव्वाणं।।१५१।।

The paramārtha (ultimate truth) is indeed samaya — the pure, kevalī, muni, jñānī (soul). Munis established in that svabhāva attain nirvāṇa.

Paramārtha — the ultimate standpoint — is identified with samaya: the pure, complete, omniscient soul in its essential nature. It is not a doctrine, a scripture, a practice, or a deity. It is the soul itself. The word samaya here directly evokes the title of the scripture itself — Samayasāra — the essence of the self. Munis who are sthitā (established, rooted) in this svabhāva attain nirvāṇa. The path is not accumulation of merit but installation in one's own nature.

Simply: The ultimate truth (paramārtha) is samaya itself — the pure, knowing, complete soul. Munis established in that svabhāva attain nirvāṇa.

Contemplate: What would it mean to be "established" in your own deepest nature — not seeking, not accumulating, not performing, but resting in what you already are?

Part 5 · Gathas 152–153 · Bāla-Tapa and Bāla-Vrata

The sharpest critique in Adhikar 3: all elaborate external observances — however sincere — performed without inner establishment in paramārtha are declared "childish" by the omniscient Jinas. Not condemned, but placed correctly: scaffolding at best.

Gatha 152
Bāla-TapaBāla-VrataSarvajña Verdict

परमट्ठम्हि दु अिठदो जो कुणिद तवं वदं च धारेिद।
तं सव्वं बालतवं बालवदं बेंित सव्वण्हू।।१५२।।

One who is not established in the paramārtha but does tapas and observes vows — all that is bāla-tapas and bāla-vrata (childish austerities and childish vows), say the omniscient ones.

The charge is not against tapas or vrats in themselves — it is against performing them without inner establishment in the paramārtha. A child who mimics adult work without understanding is not doing the work — they are playing at it. Similarly, the soul that accumulates external observances without the foundation of samyak-drishti and self-knowledge is spiritually imitating, not genuinely progressing. The sarvajñā — the omniscient Jinas who have directly perceived this — confirm: all such practice, however elaborate, remains bāla.

Simply: One not established in paramārtha who does austerities and vows — all that is "childish austerity" and "childish vow," say the omniscient.
Gatha 153
VrataNiyamaParamārtha-BāhyaNo Nirvāṇa

वदिणयमािण धरंता सीलािण तहा तवं च कुव्वंता।
परमट्ठबाहिरा जे िणव्वाणं ते ण िवंदंित।।१५३।।

Those who observe vows, niyamas, śīlas, and perform tapas — but who are external to the paramārtha — they do not attain nirvāṇa.

The verdict is unambiguous: all the external paraphernalia of religious life — vows, rules, codes of conduct, austerities — without the interior foundation of paramārtha does not reach its destination. The Jain householder who observes all twelve vratas, the monk who follows all mahāvratas impeccably in outer form — if the inner eye of samyak-drishti is not open, if the soul is not genuinely aware of its own nature — the practice does not produce nirvāṇa. Liberation cannot be earned by behavior alone. The path is interior.

Simply: Those who observe vows, niyamas, śīlas, and do tapas — but are external to the ultimate — do not attain nirvāṇa.
Part 6 · Gatha 154 · The Error of Puṇya-Seeking

The fundamental confusion of conventional religious life identified: puṇya is the cause of continued samsāra, but is mistaken for the cause of moksha. Sincerity makes this error more dangerous, not less.

Gatha 154
Puṇya-SeekingAjñānaSamsāra-HetuWrong Direction

परमट्ठबाहिरा जे ते अण्णाणेण पुण्णिमच्छंित।
संसारगणहेदुंिप मोक्खहेदुं अजाणंता।।१५४।।

Those who are external to the paramārtha desire puṇya out of ignorance — not knowing that puṇya is itself a cause of samsāra-continuation, they mistake it for the cause of moksha.

This verse identifies the fundamental error of conventional religious life: mistaking the cause of continued samsāra for the cause of liberation. Puṇya generates higher births, refined pleasures — all within samsāra. It does not generate moksha. The person who accumulates puṇya thinking they are moving toward liberation is walking in the wrong direction with great confidence and effort. Their sincerity makes the error more dangerous, because it insulates them from questioning the path itself. The Atmakhyati notes: puṇya and pāpa are both samsāra-gana-hetu — causes that keep the machinery of samsāric existence running.

Simply: Those outside the paramārtha desire puṇya out of ignorance — not knowing that puṇya is actually a cause of samsāra, they mistake it for the cause of moksha.

Contemplate: Have you ever pursued "spiritual merit" as though accumulating enough of it would eventually result in liberation? What is the difference between that and genuine inner turning?

Part 7 · Gatha 155 · Ratnatraya — The Real Moksha Path

After the critique of false paths, the real path is given precisely: the three jewels, each defined with technical care — not as beliefs but as conditions of consciousness.

Gatha 155
SamyaktvaJñānaCāritraRatnatrayaMoksha-Path

जीवादीसद्दहणं सम्मतं तेिसिमिधगमो णाणं।
रागादीपिरहरणं चरणं एसो दु मोक्खपहो।।१५५।।

Śraddhāna (right faith) in jīva and the other reals = samyaktva; adhigama (comprehension) of those reals = jñāna; renunciation of rāga and the others = cāritra. This indeed is the moksha-path.

The ratnatraya — three jewels of Jain liberation — defined with precision. Samyaktva: genuine trust in the reality of jīva, ajīva, āsrava, bandha, samvara, nirjarā, and moksha — not mere dogmatic belief but living conviction. Jñāna: direct comprehension of these reals, not mere informational knowledge but actual understanding. Cāritra: renunciation of rāga (attachment) and dveṣa (aversion) — the interior dimension, not just outer behavioral observance. All three together constitute mokshamārga. No single one is sufficient. Right seeing, right knowing, right living — complete path.

Simply: Genuine faith in the reals = samyaktva; comprehension of those reals = jñāna; renouncing attachment = cāritra. This is the path to moksha.
Part 8 · Gatha 156 · Jñāna as the Sole Cause of Moksha

The paradox of vyavahāra and niśchaya addressed: the ācārya operates conventionally to teach; but actual karma-destruction occurs only in those established in paramārtha.

Gatha 156
Niśchaya / VyavahāraKarma-KṣayaParamārtha-Sthita

मोतूण िणच्छयट्ठं ववहारेण िवदुसा पवट्टंित।
परमट्ठमिस्सदाण दु जदीण कम्मक्खओ िवहओ।।१५६।।

Leaving the niśchaya content aside, the learned operate through vyavahāra. But for those yatis established in paramārtha, destruction of karma is what is prescribed.

This verse addresses the apparent contradiction: if paramārtha is the standard, why does Jain teaching include extensive vyavahāra instructions? The answer: vyavahāra is the necessary educational path for those not yet capable of operating from paramārtha. For the sake of communicating truth to learners, the ācārya uses rules, prescriptions, outer forms. But the actual mechanism of moksha — karma-kṣaya (destruction of karma) — belongs only to those established in paramārtha. The conventional path scaffolds toward the ultimate; it is not the ultimate itself.

Simply: Leaving the ultimate aside, the learned operate through conventional means. But for ascetics established in paramārtha — the actual destruction of karma happens.
Part 9 · Gathas 157–159 · The Cloth and Dirt — Three Obstructions

A single analogy — dirty cloth — applied three times in succession to the three jewels. The soul's natural luminosity is present; it is only covered. Mithyātva covers samyaktva. Ajñāna covers jñāna. Kaṣāya covers cāritra.

Gatha 157
MithyātvaSamyaktvaCloth Analogy

वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
मिच्छत्तमलोच्छण्णं तह सम्मतं खु णादव्वं।।१५७।।

Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when it becomes smeared with dirt — similarly, samyaktva must be understood as obstructed when it is coated with the filth of mithyātva.

The analogy is exact: cloth is inherently white (just as the soul is inherently pure); dirt coats and obscures the whiteness. The whiteness is not destroyed — it is covered. Similarly, mithyātva karma does not destroy the soul's capacity for right perception — it covers it. Samyaktva is the soul's natural orientation; mithyātva is the karmic veil over it. The instruction is not to create samyaktva but to remove the obstruction: the whiteness emerges naturally when the dirt is cleared.

Simply: Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — samyaktva covered by the filth of mithyātva is obstructed. Know this.
Gatha 158
AjñānaJñānaCloth Analogy

वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
अण्णाणमलोच्छण्णं तह णाणं होिद णादव्वं।।१५८।।

Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when smeared with dirt — similarly, jñāna covered by the filth of ajñāna is obstructed — know this.

The second application: jñāna — the soul's self-luminous knowing nature — is covered by ajñāna-karma. The soul is not inherently ignorant. Ignorance is a karmic condition, not an ontological one. Jñānāvaraṇīya karma (knowledge-covering karma) is the dirt that coats the soul's natural radiance of knowing. This understanding is liberating: you are not building knowledge from scratch. You are removing the covering over a knowledge that was always there, waiting to shine.

Simply: Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — jñāna covered by the filth of ajñāna-karma is obstructed. Know this.
Gatha 159
KaṣāyaCāritraCloth AnalogyThree Jewels Complete

वत्थस्स सेदभावो जह णासेिद मलमेलणासत्तो।
कसायमलोच्छण्णं तह चािरत्तं िप णादव्वं।।१५९।।

Just as the whiteness of cloth is destroyed when smeared with dirt — similarly, cāritra covered by the filth of kaṣāya is obstructed — know this.

The third application: cāritra — right conduct, the soul's natural capacity for equanimity and non-attachment — is covered by kaṣāya (the four passions: anger, pride, deceit, greed). Cāritra-mohanīya karma generates these passions, which obstruct the soul's natural equanimity. The soul is not naturally passionate and disturbed. That is a karmic state. Together, G157-159 establish: all three jewels — samyaktva, jñāna, cāritra — are the soul's inherent nature, merely obscured. The path is removal, not construction. You are not building a new soul; you are clearing what hides the soul you always were.

Simply: Just as whiteness is destroyed in dirty cloth — cāritra covered by the filth of kaṣāya is obstructed. Know this.

Contemplate: If right perception, knowledge, and conduct are your natural state — merely covered by karma — what does spiritual practice become? Not building something new, but removing what covers what you already are.

Part 10 · Gatha 160 · Karma Covers the All-Knowing Soul

The tragedy of samsāra in one verse: the soul is omniscient by nature. Covered by self-generated karma, it falls into samsāra and cannot know. The covering is self-made — and therefore removable.

Gatha 160
Sarvajñāna-DarśīKarma-RajasSamsāraSelf-Generated Covering

सो सव्वणाणदिरसी कम्मरएण णियेणावच्छण्णो।
संसारसमावण्णो ण विजाणिद सव्वदो सव्वं।।१६०।।

That soul — which is by nature all-seeing and all-knowing — covered by its own karma-dust, fallen into samsāra, does not know all things in all ways.

The soul's nature is sarvajñāna-darśī — omniscient in potential, inherently all-knowing. But covered by karma-rajas (karma-dust) — its own accumulated karma — it falls into samsāra and cannot know. The soul did not lose its knowing nature; it was covered by what it itself attracted through prior attachment. Nijena = "by its own" — the karma is self-generated, not externally imposed. This points toward responsibility without self-condemnation: you covered yourself; therefore you can uncover yourself. No external agent is needed. No external permission is required.

Simply: The soul is by nature all-knowing and all-seeing. But covered by its own karma-dust, fallen into samsāra, it does not know all things in all ways.

Contemplate: The soul that doesn't know is not a different soul from the omniscient one. It is the same soul, covered. How does that change the way you relate to your own limitations?

Part 11 · Gathas 161–163 · Three Karmas, Three Effects

The Adhikar closes with a precise threefold map of how karma creates the soul's samsāric condition. The three obstructing karmas — mithyātva, ajñāna, kaṣāya — are identified with surgical exactness by the Jinas.

Gatha 161
Mithyātva KarmaDarśana-MohanīyaMithyādṛṣṭi

सम्मतपडिणिबद्धं मिच्छत्तं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो मिच्छाद्दिट्ठि ति णादव्वो।।१६१।।

Mithyātva — which obstructs samyaktva — is described by the Jinas. By its rise (udaya), the soul becomes a mithyādṛṣṭi (wrong-perceiving being) — this must be known.

Mithyātva karma is technically darśana-mohanīya — the karma that obstructs samyak-darśana (right perception). When this karma rises (udaya), the soul's seeing is distorted: it sees the non-self as self, the temporary as permanent, the external as satisfying. This is mithyātva — not a moral failure but a karmic condition of wrong orientation. The Jinas have described it precisely because knowing the mechanism is itself the beginning of dismantling it.

Simply: Mithyātva karma — which blocks samyaktva — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes mithyādṛṣṭi (wrong-perceiving). Know this.
Gatha 162
Ajñāna KarmaJñānāvaraṇīyaAjñānī

णाणस्स पडिणिबद्धं अण्णाणं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो अण्णाणी होिद णादव्वो।।१६२।।

Ajñāna karma — which obstructs jñāna — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes ajñānī (ignorant) — this must be known.

Jñānāvaraṇīya karma (knowledge-covering karma) blocks the soul's natural omniscience. By its rise, the soul is ajñānī — not because it was born ignorant, but because a karmic covering prevents knowing. This is the mechanism behind all the soul's cognitive limitations, confusion, and delusion in samsāra. The soul does not become a lesser being — it is the same omniscient being, temporarily veiled, as described in G160.

Simply: Ajñāna karma — which blocks jñāna — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes ajñānī. Know this.
Gatha 163
Kaṣāya KarmaCāritra-MohanīyaAcāritraClosing Verse

चारित्तपडिणिबद्धं कसायं जिणवरेिह परिकहियं।
तस्सोदयेण जीवो अचिरतो होिद णादव्वो।।१६३।।

Kaṣāya karma — which obstructs cāritra — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes acāritra (lacking right conduct) — this must be known.

Cāritra-mohanīya karma generates the four kaṣāyas — anger, pride, deceit, greed — which obstruct the soul's natural equanimity. By its rise, the soul is acāritra — not naturally violent or passionate, but karmically driven into those states. The three verses (G161-163) form a complete karmic pathology of samsāra: mithyātva distorts seeing, ajñāna covers knowing, kaṣāya corrupts living. The three jewels stated in G155 are the precise antidotes: samyaktva dissolves mithyātva, jñāna dispels ajñāna, cāritra removes kaṣāya. The Adhikar is architecturally perfect: what was said to obstruct at the end, precisely matches what was said to liberate in the middle.

Simply: Kaṣāya karma — which blocks cāritra — is described by the Jinas. By its rise, the soul becomes acāritra (lacking conduct). Know this.

Contemplate: If anger, pride, deceit, and greed are karmic conditions — not your essential nature — how does that change how you relate to them when they arise? Not as "what I am" but as "what covers what I am"?

इति पुण्यपापरूपेण द्विपात्रीभूतमेकपात्रीभूय कर्म निष्क्रान्तम्।

Thus karma — which had entered the stage of the soul in two roles (puṇya and pāpa) — having been recognized as one single actor by jñāna, has departed from the stage entirely. The theatrical illusion is over. The soul stands alone.
