Samaysaar · Adhikar 2 · Gathas 69–144

The Doer & the Deedकर्ता-कर्म अधिकार

All 76 gathas of the second Adhikar — demolishing the false belief that the soul makes karma

In 76 gathas of surgical philosophical precision, Kundkundacharya establishes who the real doer is — and who is not. Ajñāna (self-ignorance) creates the illusion of karma-doership. Jñāna dissolves it. Each substance transforms only through its own nature. The soul that knows this — knows itself as Samayasāra.

अहमेक्को खलु सुद्धो णिम्ममओ णाणदंसणसमग्गो।
तम्हि ठिदो तच्चितो सव्वे एदे खयं णेमि ॥७३॥

"I alone am truly pure — without possessiveness, complete in knowledge and perception. Established in that, absorbed in that — I will bring all these to extinction." — Samaysaar Adhikar 2, Gatha 73

About This Adhikar

Kartā-Karma: Who Really Does?

The second Adhikar takes up the most practically urgent question in Jain spiritual life: who is the doer of karma? The conventional answer — "the soul does karma" — turns out to be both useful and dangerous. Useful as a pedagogical tool. Dangerous when taken literally, because it places the soul in a relationship of agency with matter that, at the ultimate level, cannot exist.

Kundkundacharya's answer has two layers. From the vyavahāra (conventional) standpoint, yes — the soul is called the doer of karma. From the niśchaya (ultimate) standpoint, the soul does only itself — its own bhavas, its own upayoga-transformations. Karma is done by guṇas (the spiritual-stage forces), while the soul is nimitta (instrumental occasion) — not the material agent. The Atmakhyati of Amrutchandra elaborates each verse with legendary philosophical precision.

The Adhikar culminates in the concept of Samayasāra itself — the soul that, having transcended both vyavahāra and niśchaya as standpoints, abides in the direct experience of its own pure nature. The soul that neither does karma (ultimately) nor needs to pretend it doesn't (conventionally) — but simply knows itself, completely.

Part 1 · Gathas 69–74 · The Root of Bondage — Ignorance

The Adhikar opens with a diagnostic statement that is also a map of liberation: the only root of karma-bondage is ajñāna — not knowing the distinction between the soul and its āsravas (passions). Know the distinction; bondage stops. Miss it; bondage continues. All 76 gathas flow from this opening.

2.69

जाव ण वेदि विसेसंतरं तु आदासवाण दोण्हं पि।
अण्णाणी ताव दु सो कोहादिसु वट्टदे जीवो ॥६९॥

As long as this soul does not know the specific distinction between itself and the āsravas — as long as that, being in ajñāna, the soul remains engaged in krodhādi (anger and other passions).

The root of karma-bondage is not fate, punishment, or external compulsion — it is ajñāna: the failure to know the distinction between what the soul is and what āsravas (passions, karmic inflows) are. When you do not know the difference between yourself and anger, you live inside anger. You become it. The term "vishesha-antara" (specific distinction) points not to a conceptual belief but to a lived recognition — the direct experience that the soul is of an utterly different nature than krodhādi. Until that recognition dawns, the āsrava rules, because the soul has no alternative reference point.

The simple version: Not knowing yourself as distinct from your passions IS the bondage. The moment that distinction is known — clearly, directly — engagement in passion loses its grip.

AjñānaĀsravaKrodhādi
2.70

कोहादिसु वट्टंतस्स तस्स कम्मस्स संचओ होदि।
जीवस्सेवं बंधो भणिदो खलु सव्वदरिसीहिं ॥७०॥

For that soul active in krodhādi, accumulation of karma takes place. This is exactly what the all-seeing Jinas have declared as the bondage of the soul.

The mechanism is stated plainly: activity in passions → accumulation of karma (samchaya) → bondage. The all-seers (sarvadarshin — omniscient Jinas) have not merely theorized this — they have directly perceived the causal chain through kevalajñāna. The word "samchaya" (accumulation) is significant: karma builds up moment by moment. Every moment of passion-driven activity adds more. This is why urgency — jñāna-urgency, not anxious urgency — is appropriate.

The simple version: Being active in passions accumulates karma — and that accumulation is what the Jinas call bondage.

Karma SamchayaBandhaSarvadarshin
2.71

जइया इमेण जीवेण अप्पणो आसवाण य तहेव।
णादं होदि विसेसंतरं तु तइया ण बंधो से ॥७१॥

When the specific distinction between the soul itself and the āsravas becomes known to this soul — at that very time (tadyā), there is no bondage for it.

G71 is the mirror of G69. Not after years of additional practice. Not after death and rebirth in a better realm. At that very time — the moment of genuine knowing. The recognition itself is the cessation of bondage. This connects to samyak-darśana (right perception): the first genuine flash of self-recognition immediately stops wrong-belief-based karma at its root. New karma from mithyātva ceases at the very moment of right knowing.

The simple version: The moment you truly know the distinction between yourself and your passions — at that very moment, bondage stops.

Vishesha-AntaraJñānaBandha-Cessation
2.72

णादूण आसवाणं असुचित्तं च विवरीयभावं च।
दुक्खस्स कारणं ति य तदो णियत्तिं कुणिदं जीवो ॥७२॥

Knowing the āsravas' impurity, their contrary nature, and that they are causes of suffering — the soul then withdraws from them.

Three realizations together trigger natural withdrawal: (1) Impurity — the āsravas are not sacred or noble; anger feels righteous in the moment but is dirt on the lens of consciousness. (2) Contrary nature — they are structurally opposite to the soul: where the soul is consciousness, āsravas are insentient; where the soul is free, āsravas bind. (3) Cause of suffering — they hurt, across lifetimes. When all three are known — not believed, but known — withdrawal is the soul's natural response. Renunciation is not forced; it is intelligence seeing clearly.

The simple version: When the soul sees its passions as impure, contrary to its nature, and the source of suffering — it turns away naturally. No willpower required. Seeing is enough.

AśuciViparīta-bhāvaDuḥkha-kāraṇa
2.73

अहमेक्को खलु सुद्धो णिम्ममओ णाणदंसणसमग्गो।
तम्हि ठिदो तच्चितो सव्वे एदे खयं णेमि ॥७३॥

"I alone am truly pure — without possessiveness, complete in knowledge and perception. Established in that, absorbed in that — I will bring all these (āsravas and karmas) to their extinction."

One of the most celebrated verses in all of Jain literature — a declaration of the soul from within its own recognition. Not a hope. Not a prayer. A statement of what the soul has already seen itself to be. "I alone" (aham ekko) — not I plus body, not I plus history, not I plus karma. The soul, stripped of all superimposition, is singular and complete. "Without possessiveness" (nimmamao) — no "mine." The entire web of samsāra is sustained by mamakāra (the sense of ownership); when that collapses, the root is cut. "Established in that" (tamhi thido) — resting in the recognition of one's own nature. Already there. Just remaining. The āsravas and karmas dissolve not through violent effort, but because consciousness established in its own nature naturally burns away what obscures it — as sunlight dissolves fog.

Amrutchandra's Atmakhyati declares this verse to be the entire Samaysaar condensed into one utterance.

The simple version: The awakened soul speaks: "I am alone, pure, without possessiveness, whole in knowing. Rooted in this truth — I will bring all that obscures me to extinction."

Śuddha ĀtmāNirmamaoSoul's DeclarationAmrutchandra Celebrated
2.74

जीविणिबद्धा एदे अधुव अणिचा तहा असरणा य।
दुक्खा दुक्खफल ति य णादूण णिवत्तदे तेहिं ॥७४॥

These āsravas — bound to life, impermanent, shelterless, painful, and fruit of suffering — knowing this, one withdraws from them.

Five characteristics of āsravas that warrant withdrawal: (1) Bound to life — they bind the soul to continued samsāra by nature; (2) Impermanent — no passion-driven pleasure endures; (3) Shelterless — you cannot take refuge in anger — it burns you; (4) Painful — suffering is their essential nature even when they feel temporarily pleasant; (5) Fruit of suffering — they produce suffering as their result. When genuinely seen, this fivefold recognition produces the natural turning-away that G72 described.

The simple version: The āsravas bind, fade, offer no shelter, hurt, and reproduce suffering. Seeing this clearly — withdrawing from them happens on its own.

AdhruvaAnicāAsaraṇāFivefold Recognition
Part 2 · Gathas 75–79 · The Knower as Non-Doer

The Adhikar now defines the jñānī — not by their attainments or practices — but by the one precise quality that distinguishes them from the ajñānī: they know karma's transformations without enacting them.

2.75

कम्मस्स य परिणामं णोकम्मस्स य तहेव परिणामं।
ण करेइ एयमादा जो जाणिदे सो हवदि णाणी ॥७५॥

The soul that does not bring about the transformation of karma and of nokamma — but merely knows it — that soul is the jñānī.

The definition of jñānī is precise. Not: "one who has read many texts." Not: "one who has practiced many austerities." The jñānī is the one who knows (jānādi) karma's transformation without doing it (na karedi). The knowing and the non-doing coexist in the same consciousness. Upayoga (the soul's knowing-mode) is inherently knowing. When the soul operates as pure upayoga — pure knowing — it is not doing. It is when upayoga is contaminated by mithyātva and rāga-dvesha that knowing becomes doing.

The simple version: The jñānī knows karma's transformations — without producing them. Knowing without doing: that is the definition.

JñānīAkartāNokammaKnowing vs Doing
2.76

G76-79 elaborate the jñānī's three-fold non-doing: the jñānī, even while knowing many kinds of pudgala-karma, does not transform into it (na pariṇamate), does not claim it (na grihṇādi), and does not arise in other-substance modes (na upajjadi para-dravya-pajjāe). All three describe a soul that is fully present as awareness without being invaded, captured, or redefined by what it is aware of.

G79 then applies the same principle to pudgala: pudgala-dravya also transforms only by its own bhavas — not in the soul's modes. This is the doctrine of svabhāva-pariṇāma (own-nature transformation): each substance transforms only through its own nature. No substance can actually do anything to another substance. They can serve as instrumental causes (nimittas) — but the transformation happens from within.

The simple version: The jñānī knows karma fully — but doesn't become it, claim it, or get lost in it. And pudgala transforms only by its own nature — never by invading the soul.

Svabhāva-PariṇāmaPara-dravya-pajjāe
Part 3 · Gathas 80–84 · Mutual Instrumental Causation

Gathas 80–84 present the doctrine of nimitta-naimittika — the most precise answer Jainism gives to the question of how soul and karma are related without the soul being karma's agent.

2.80

जीवपरिणामहेदुं कम्मत्तं पोग्गला परिणमंति।
पोग्गलकम्मिणिमित्तं तहेव जीवो वि परिणमदि ॥८०॥

Pudgalas transform into karma-form due to the soul's transformation as cause; the soul also transforms due to pudgala-karma as instrumental cause.

Neither soul nor pudgala acts on the other directly. The soul's transformation (rāga, dvesha, moha) is nimitta — the occasion — for pudgalas to transform into karma. Karma's rise is nimitta for the soul's transformation. Each is occasion for the other's transformation, while each transforms only through its own svabhāva (nature). The Atmakhyati uses the crystal analogy: a crystal placed near a red flower appears red — but the redness does not actually enter the crystal. The crystal transforms through its own capacity for transparency, using the flower as nimitta. Remove the flower, the crystal is again pure. Soul and karma work exactly this way. G81 sharpens it: the soul does NOT produce karma's qualities; karma does NOT produce the soul's qualities. Each produces only its own.

The simple version: Soul and karma mutually condition each other — but neither forces the other to transform. Each transforms by its own nature, using the other as occasion. You are never fully determined by conditions. But you are never independent of them either.

Nimitta-NaimittikaInstrumental CausationCrystal Analogy
2.82

णिच्छयणयस्स एवं आदा अप्पाणमेव हि करेदि।
वेदयदि पुणो तं चेव जाण अत्ता दु अत्ताणं ॥८३॥

From the niśchaya (ultimate) standpoint: the soul does only itself and experiences only itself. Know this — ātmā does ātmānam (the soul does the soul).

G82 establishes that the soul is kartā of its own bhavas (inner states) — but is NOT the kartā of all bhavas produced by pudgala-karma. G83 goes further to the niśchaya level: the soul's doing and experiencing are both self-contained. It does its own bhavas. It experiences its own bhavas. Nothing external is actually done or experienced — only the soul's own transformation is real at the ultimate level. The phrase "ātmā tu ātmānam" (the soul does the soul) is among the most celebrated in Jain metaphysics: the soul is simultaneously the doer, the deed, and the done-to. G84 gives the vyavahāra balance: conventionally, the soul does and experiences many kinds of pudgala-karma — useful for practical teaching, not the final word.

The simple version: Ultimately: the soul does only itself, experiences only itself. Conventionally: the soul does and experiences karma. Both true at their respective levels. Neither collapses the other.

Part 4 · Gathas 85–108 · The Self-Constructs and the Two Faces of Doership

The longest section of the Adhikar addresses the mechanism of ajñāna-based doership — specifically the self-vikalpas ("I am anger," "I am this") — and systematically establishes the precise boundary between the soul's legitimate kartā-ship and false karma-doership.

2.92

परमप्पाणं कुव्वं अप्पाणं पि य परं किरंतो सो।
अण्णाणमओ जीवो कम्माणं कारगो होदि ॥९२॥

Making the supreme self into "other," making the self into "other" — the ajñāna-filled soul becomes the kartā of karmas.

The mechanism of ajñāna-based doership described with surgical precision: two distortions happen simultaneously in the ajñānī — (1) making the supreme self (paramātmā — the pure soul) into "other": treating your deepest nature as foreign, unknown; (2) making "other" (body, passions, possessions) into "self": identifying with what you are not. These two reversals, together, ARE ajñāna — and they ARE what makes the soul the kartā of karma. G93 states the exact reversal: the jñānī knows the true self as self and the not-self as not-self. That knowing alone — not ritual, not austerity — makes the soul akartā of karma. Which means: reversing the reversal IS liberation.

The simple version: When you mistake your true self as "not me" and mistake what is not you as "me" — you make karma. The jñānī reverses both errors simultaneously — and is freed from doership.

Ajñāna-KartāJñāna-AkartāPara → Ātmā
2.94

तिविहो एसुवओगो अप्पियप्पं करेदि कोहोऽहं।
कत्ता तस्सुवओगस्सं होदि सो अत्तभावस्स ॥९४॥

Threefold upayoga creates self-vikalpas like "I am anger (krodhaḥ aham)"; the kartā of that upayoga becomes kartā of that bhāva.

"I am anger," "I am pride," "I am deceit" — these self-vikalpas (self-constructions) are the practical mechanism of ajñāna-based bondage. The upayoga, perverted by mithyātva, generates these false self-statements. Whoever identifies as kartā of that upayoga — whoever says "I am angry" and means it as their very identity — becomes kartā of that bhāva and binds karma through it. This is one of the most psychologically penetrating verses in the Samaysaar. Modern psychology would call it the difference between having an emotion and being an emotion. The Samaysaar calls the latter vicarious kartā-ship — the karmic identification that binds.

The simple version: "I am angry" (as identity) binds. Noticing anger without claiming it as self — that is jñāna. The difference is not in the emotion but in who you take yourself to be when it arises.

Krodho'hamSelf-VikalpaUpayoga
2.85

G85-86 establish that the soul cannot simultaneously be the material maker AND experiencer of karma — the "dvikrīyā" (double-action) problem rejected by the Jinas. G87-90 map the four bondage-causes (mithyātva, ajñāna, avirati, yoga/kasāya) across the jīva/ajīva distinction — showing each has both a soul-side manifestation and a matter-side manifestation.

G95-97 show even "I am dharma" vikalpas can be bondage if not arising from genuine self-knowledge. G99-100 establish that the soul doesn't make pots, cloth, or chariots — yoga and upayoga are the producers (in that sense only). G101-104 show the soul does not create karma's dravya and guṇas — it is not their material maker.

The section closes with three celebrated analogies (G105-108): "karma done by the soul" is upachāra (conventional metaphor) only — like saying "the king fought the battle" when warriors did the fighting. The soul, by vyavahāra, is called "producer of dravya-qualities" just as a king is conventionally called "producer of subjects' faults and virtues."

The simple version: The soul's conventional karma-doership is a useful metaphor (upachāra), not ultimate fact. Ultimately, each substance produces only its own transformations.

Part 5 · Gathas 109–112 · The Four Causes of Bondage

The Adhikar now gives the formal theory of bondage-causes — four general pratyayas and their thirteen specific varieties across the guṇasthānas — and establishes that these causes are themselves achetana (insentient), clarifying where exactly the soul's responsibility lies.

2.109

सामण्णपच्चया खलु चउरो भण्णंति बंधकत्तारो।
मिच्छत्तं अविरमणं कसायजोगा य बोद्धव्वा ॥१०९॥

The four general causes of bondage are: mithyātva, non-restraint (aviramana), kasāya (passions), and yoga — these are to be understood.

G109 gives the four foundational causes. G110 shows their thirteen specific varieties corresponding to the thirteen guṇasthānas — measuring spiritual progress by which causes are progressively reduced. G111 establishes a key point: these four pratyayas arise from pudgala-karma's rise — they are achetana (insentient). If they alone did karma, the soul would not experience it. But the soul IS the experiencer — so its conscious upayoga is what converts insentient karmic conditions into lived bondage. G112 concludes: from the niśchaya level, the soul is akartā — the guṇas (the pratyayas as spiritual-stage attributes) are the actual karma-doers. The soul is their field, not their agent.

The simple version: Four things cause karma: wrong belief, non-restraint, passions, and activity. But even these are insentient forces. The soul's contribution is its upayoga — its conscious engagement. Take away the upayoga's identification, and the bondage loses its hook.

Part 6 · Gathas 113–115 · Soul and Auxiliary Conditions Are Not Identical
2.113

अह दे अण्णो कोहो अण्णुवओगप्पगो हवइ चेदा।
जह कोहो तह पच्चय कम्मं णोकम्ममिव अण्णं ॥११५॥

Krodha (anger) is other (anya) than the soul — and the soul is of the nature of upayoga as other. Just as krodha is other — so too pratyaya, karma, and nokamma are all other (separate from the soul).

G113-114 address a possible objection: if upayoga is non-separate (ananya) from the soul, and if krodha is upayoga, then krodha would be non-separate from the soul — making soul and ajīva identical, which is the fundamental error. The resolution in G115 is clean: upayoga IS genuinely the soul's own (ananya). But krodha — arising from karma, insentient at its root — is NOT ananya to the soul in the same way. Krodha is anya (other). And this distinction extends cleanly to all karma, nokamma, and pratyaya — all anya to the soul. The soul is consciousness. Everything else is other. Not as ascetic ideology — as precise metaphysics.

The simple version: Upayoga is genuinely the soul's own. Anger — arising from karma — is genuinely other. And therefore all of karma's universe is cleanly other than the soul.

Part 7 · Gathas 116–125 · Each Substance Transforms Itself

A long and philosophically dense section responding to the Sāṃkhya school's view that the soul is entirely non-transforming. Kundkundacharya proves that BOTH soul and pudgala are pariṇāmi (transforming) substances — and that each transforms only through its own svabhāva.

2.116

G116: If the soul does not transform itself, pudgala is also proved non-transforming (Sāṃkhya). G117: If karmic matter doesn't transform by itself, either samsāra is impossible or Sāṃkhya follows. G118: If pudgala doesn't transform itself, the soul cannot force it to. G119: If pudgala transforms by itself — then saying "the soul transforms karma" is false. G120: By necessity, pudgala transformed into karma-form IS karma (jñānāvaraṇa etc.) — the soul doesn't make it; pudgala's own svabhāva transforms it.

The logic is a set of interlocking dilemmas that progressively eliminate every alternative to the Jain position: each substance transforms only through its own svabhāva. The soul cannot transform pudgala; pudgala cannot transform the soul. Each transforms according to its own nature when the other is present as nimitta (occasion).

The simple version: The soul can't force pudgala to become karma; pudgala can't force the soul into anger. Each becomes what it becomes by its own nature — the other is only occasion.

Svabhāva-PariṇāmaSāṃkhya RefutationAparinami
2.121

कोहुवजुत्तो कोहो माणुवजुत्तो य माणमेवादा।
माउवजुत्तो माया लोहुवजुत्तो हवदि लोहो ॥१२५॥

Soul engaged in krodha-upayoga IS krodha; engaged in māna-upayoga IS māna; engaged in māyā-upayoga IS māyā; engaged in lobha-upayoga IS lobha.

G121-124 apply the same Sāṃkhya-refutation argument to the soul's transformation in krodhādi: if the soul doesn't transform itself into anger — samsāra is impossible. If pudgala-anger forces the soul into anger — but pudgala can't transform what doesn't first transform itself. The conclusion in G125 is revolutionary: the soul IS anger when its upayoga is in anger-mode. Not as its nature — as its current transformation. The anger-karma is nimitta. The soul's own transformation IS the anger. This simultaneously eliminates fatalism (karma can't force you into anger) and establishes responsibility (your anger is your own transformation).

The simple version: Your anger is not what karma does to you — it is what you transform into when karma rises as occasion. Which means it can change. By you.

Part 8 · Gathas 126–131 · The Knower vs the Ignorant — The Nature of Inner States
2.126

जं कुणिदे भावमादा कत्ता सो होदि तस्स कम्मस्स।
णाणिस्स स णाणमओ अण्णाणमओ अणाणिस्स ॥१२६॥

Whatever bhāva the soul does, it is kartā of that karma. For the jñānī it is jñānamaya (knowledge-filled); for the ajñānī it is ajñānamaya (ignorance-filled).

G126-127 contain the core operational principle: ajñānamaya doing = karma; jñānamaya doing = no karma. The jñānī's life may look similar to the ajñānī's externally — both breathe, eat, speak, move. But the jñānī's actions arise from jñānamaya upayoga — not adding to karma. The ajñānī's arise from ajñānamaya upayoga — they do. The Atmakhyati explains: the jñānī does not perform actions while treating rāga-dvesha as "mine" — as self-definition. Without that claiming, karma has no hook to attach to. The inner quality of consciousness is what determines the karmic outcome, not the external act.

The simple version: Ajñānamaya doing = karma. Jñānamaya doing = no karma. The outcome is determined by the quality of consciousness, not the external action.

JñānamayaAjñānamayaNo-Karma
2.128

णाणमया भावाओ णाणमओ चेव जायदे भावो।
जम्हा तम्हा णाणिस्स सव्वे भावा हु णाणमया ॥१२८॥

From jñānamaya bhāva, only jñānamaya bhāva arises; therefore all bhavas of the jñānī are truly jñānamaya.

G128-131 reveal the self-reproducing nature of consciousness states. From jñāna, jñāna is generated. From ajñāna, ajñāna is generated. G130 gives the analogy: from gold come only gold-forms (earrings); from iron come only iron-forms (bangles). Similarly — from jñānamaya bhāva, only jñānamaya bhavas arise; from ajñānamaya, only ajñānamaya. This has a profound implication: once the jñānī's consciousness is established in jñānamaya bhāva, everything that arises — every thought, every response — is jñānamaya. Not because of suppression or control, but because the source is clean. And for the ajñānī — the same self-reproduction works in reverse. Every act of ignorance generates more ignorance.

The simple version: From knowing comes knowing. From ignorance comes ignorance. Transform the source — and everything arising from it transforms. This is the deepest argument for why self-knowledge — not behavioral reform alone — is the path.

Self-Reproducing ConsciousnessGold Analogy
Part 9 · Gathas 132–136 · The Four Rises and Their Nature
2.132

अण्णाणस्स स उदओ जा जीवाणं अतच्चउवलद्धी।
मिच्छत्तस्स दु उदओ जीवस्स असद्दहाणतं ॥१३२॥

The rise of ajñāna in jīvas is non-attainment of truth; the rise of mithyātva is non-faith in the soul.

G132-136 describe each bondage-cause at its experiential level: Ajñāna-rise = not recognizing reality as it is (missing truth). Mithyātva-rise = actively oriented away from truth (rejecting truth). Asaṃyama-rise = non-restraint in action. Kasāya-rise = the upayoga becoming kalusha (murky, defiled) — unable to see clearly. Yoga-rise = the soul's sheer initiative — pure or impure — including even the impulse toward renunciation (yoga is neutral; what matters is the quality of consciousness directing it).

G135-136 give the complete picture: when these four rises operate, kārmika-varganā particles adhere to the soul and transform into the eight types of karma. The soul is nimitta (instrumental occasion) — pudgala does the actual material transformation. Mithyātva-rise, karma's transformation into bondage-form, and the soul's own ajñānamaya transformation all happen simultaneously — each by its own nature, in mutual nimitta-naimittika relationship.

The simple version: Four kinds of "rise" (udaya) are what bondage-causes feel like from the inside. They operate simultaneously with karma binding — each substance doing its own part, simultaneously, by its own nature.

Part 10 · Gathas 137–142 · Distinctness of Soul and Matter Transformations
2.137

G137-138 refute the view that soul and pudgala jointly transform into karma: if they transformed together, both would be karma — but the soul doesn't become karma, so they transform separately. G138 concludes: karma-transformation belongs to pudgala alone, by its own nature. The soul's state is nimitta (occasion), not upādāna kāraṇa (material cause).

G139-140 apply the same argument to the soul's transformation in rāgādi (attachment): if soul and karma together produced rāga, both would become rāgī (attached). But karma cannot experience attachment. Therefore rāga is the soul's own transformation alone — with karma as nimitta. This is simultaneously a statement of non-determinism (karma can't force you into attachment) and of responsibility (your attachment is your own transformation).

The simple version: Only pudgala becomes karma. Only the soul becomes rāgī. Their transformations are categorically separate. Each substance produces only its own modes.

Saha-Pariṇāma RefutationRāgādiSeparate Transformation
2.141

जीवे कम्मं बद्धं पुट्ठं चेद ववहारणयभणिदं।
सुद्धणयस्स दु जीवे अबद्धपुट्ठं हवदि कम्मं ॥१४१॥

That karma is "bound and touched" in the soul — this is the vyavahāra-naya's statement. From the śuddha-naya's perspective, karma is "unbound and untouched" in the soul.

G141 presents the two valid naya-views without contradiction: Vyavahāra — karma IS bound to the soul. Useful for teaching about bondage, shedding karma, the process of liberation. Śuddha-naya — at the level of the soul's actual nature, karma never touches it. The soul is anādi-śuddha (pure from the beginningless). Both are true at their respective levels. G142 is the pivot of the entire Adhikar: both "karma binds" and "karma doesn't bind" are standpoints — useful lenses, partial truth. The Samayasāra is what lies beyond both. Not a third opinion — the direct experience of what the soul actually is, prior to any lens, any standpoint, any secondary description.

The simple version: "Karma binds" (conventional). "Karma never touches the soul" (ultimate). Both are standpoints. The Samayasāra is what is beyond both — the soul's direct self-experience, prior to all philosophy.

Part 11 · Gathas 143–144 · Beyond Both Standpoints — The Self-Essence

The final two gathas name the destination — and reveal that the destination is not a state to be achieved but a direct seeing of what the soul already is, when no standpoint mediates the seeing.

2.143

दोण्ह वि णयाण भणिदं जाणिदे णवरं तु समयपिडिबद्धो।
ण दु णयपक्खं गिण्हिदे किंचि वि णयपक्खपरिहीणो ॥१४३॥

The one who is firmly bound to Samayasāra knows the statements of both nayas — but does not take any naya-standpoint; being free from all naya-standpoints.

The samayapraribaddha (Samayasāra-bound soul) is not ignorant of the nayas — this is not anti-intellectualism. They know both viewpoints thoroughly. But understanding does not become attachment to a position. They move through both frameworks as tools without being captured by either. The Atmakhyati compares this to a Kevalī: the omniscient one knows all standpoints simultaneously but is not captured by any. The Kevalī abides in direct experience of reality, which no standpoint fully describes. The samyak-dṛṣṭi approaching this state does the same at a more modest level — uses nayas as maps, isn't imprisoned by any map.

The simple version: The Samayasāra-oriented soul knows both standpoints — but doesn't take sides with either. Tools, not cages.

SamayapraribaddhaNaya-Paksha-RahitaKevalī Analogy
2.144

सम्मदंसणणाणं एसो लहिदे ति णवरि ववदेसं।
सव्वणयपक्खरहिदो भणिदो जो सो समयसारो ॥१४४॥

This alone obtains the designation "samyak-darśana and samyak-jñāna." The one described as free from all naya-standpoints — THAT is Samayasāra.

The final gatha of Adhikar 2 is a revelation: samyak-darśana (right perception) and samyak-jñāna (right knowledge) are not two separate things acquired through separate practices. They are the same reality — the direct experience of the soul in its purity, beyond all standpoints. "Sarvnaya-paksha-rahito" — freed from all standpoints. Not a blank mind. A fully luminous, knowing consciousness that sees itself directly, without the mediation of even the most refined philosophical framework. The Samayasāra is not a book. Not a doctrine. It is the soul itself — as it truly is, when seen without any naya overlay. This is what all 76 gathas of Adhikar 2 have been pointing toward from the very first verse: the distinction between self and not-self (G69), when fully realized, leads here.

The simple version: Free from all standpoints — that alone obtains the names "right perception" and "right knowledge." That pure knowing is Samayasāra itself. The journey of 76 gathas ends where it always pointed — at the soul, knowing itself, directly.
