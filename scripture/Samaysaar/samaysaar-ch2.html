Samaysaar · Adhikar 1 · Gathas 39–68

Soul & Non-Soulजीव-अजीव अधिकार

The complete first Adhikar — all 30 gathas establishing what the soul is, and what it is not

In 30 gathas of unrelenting philosophical precision, Kundkundacharya demolishes every confused identification of the soul with matter — karma, passion, the body, even spiritual stages — and reveals the soul's one true definition: pure consciousness, upayoga alone. This is the foundation on which all nine remaining Adhikars rest.

अरसमरूवमगंधं अव्वत्तं चेदणागुणमसदं।
जाण अलिंगग्गहणं जीवमिणिदिट्ठसंठाणं ॥४९॥

"Know the soul as: without taste, without form, without smell — imperceptible to the senses, having consciousness as its essential quality." — Samaysaar Adhikar 1, Gatha 49

About This Adhikar

Jīva-Ajīva: The Great Separation

The first Adhikar opens the Samaysaar's body with a single, driving question: what is the soul — and what is it not? Every confused answer to this question is the seed of bondage. Every clear answer is the seed of liberation. Kundkundacharya begins by cataloguing the various wrong views held by those who haven't realized the soul's true nature — views that mistake karma, passion, the body, even spiritual stages for the self.

He then reveals the Kevali Jinas' decisive declaration: all those attributed qualities — color, smell, rāga, karma, guṇasthāna — are transformations of pudgala-dravya (matter). The soul is pure upayoga (conscious knowing) alone. It has no material quality whatsoever. The soul's connection to matter is like milk mixed with water — appearing as one, but fundamentally two different substances.

The Adhikar closes with a reductio argument that demolishes any attempt to include material categories in the soul's definition — and ends with the soul and non-soul having been decisively separated: "इति जीवाजीवौ पृथग्भूत्वा निष्क्रान्तौ" — "Thus jīva and ajīva, having been separated, have exited the arena."

Part 1 · Gathas 39–43 · The Wrong Views

The Adhikar opens by cataloguing the various confused views of what the soul is — all of them locating the soul in something material. These are not ancient errors only; they describe the default state of any mind that has not examined its own nature.

1.39

अप्पाणमयाणंता मूढा दु परप्पवादिणो केई।
जीवं अझ्झवसाणं कम्मं च तहा परुवेंति ॥३९॥

Not knowing the soul's true nature, some foolish para-ātmavādins declare that karma and adhyavasāna (passionate mental dispositions) are the soul.

The Adhikar begins where all spiritual error begins: not knowing the soul. Those who don't know what the self actually is reach outward — to emotions, to karma, to states of mind — and call those the self. The term para-ātmavādin means one whose identification is with what is other than the soul. This is not a philosophical label. It describes anyone, in any age, who answers "Who am I?" with reference to their thoughts, feelings, roles, or stories.

The simple version: Ignorance of the soul's real nature produces the most basic error — mistaking karma and emotional states for the self itself.

AdhyavasānaWrong ViewPara-ātmavādin
1.40

अवरे अझ्झवसाणेसु तिव्वमंदाणुभागं जीवं।
मण्णंति तहा अवरे णोकम्मं चावि जीवो ति ॥४०॥

Others consider the soul to be the intense-or-mild experiential quality within adhyavasāna; and yet others consider nokamma (body and sense organs) to be the soul.

The errors refine but don't improve. Some say the soul is specifically the intensity of passion — the sharper or milder quality of emotional experience. Others look to the physical body and call that the soul. Both views remain trapped in material categories.

The simple version: Equating the soul with emotional intensity, or with the body — still wrong. All material.

NokammaBodyWrong View
1.41

कम्मस्सुदयं जीवं अवरे कम्माणुभागमिच्छंति।
तिव्वत्तणमंदत्तणगुणेहिं जो सो हवदि जीवो ॥४१॥

Others consider the rising (udaya) of karma to be the soul; others want karma's anubhāga — whoever has the quality of intensity-or-mildness, they say that is the soul.

More refined: the soul is the moment karma rises and produces experience (udaya), or the potency of that experience (anubhāga). These are subtler errors — but still pointing at purely material-causal processes. The rising of karma is a pudgala event. It is not the knower of that event.

The simple version: Karma's rising is not the soul. Its fruition-force is not the soul. Both are material events. The soul is the witness of those events.

Karma UdayaAnubhāgaWrong View
1.42

जीवो कम्मं उहयं दोण्णि वि खलु केइ जीवमिच्छंति।
अवरे संजोगेण दु कम्माणं जीवमिच्छंति ॥४२॥

Some consider both soul and karma together as the soul; others consider the very conjunction of karmas to be the soul.

The compound view: perhaps the soul is the whole complex — jīva-plus-karma? Or perhaps the soul is the meeting-point of karmas? Even here, including material elements in the soul's definition contaminates the concept. The junction between soul and karma is not itself a third thing that is the soul.

The simple version: Soul + karma combined is not "the soul." The soul is one thing; karma is another. Their combination is a fact about samsāra, not a definition of the soul.

Karma-Jīva ConjunctionWrong View
1.43

एवंविहा बहुविहा परमप्पाणं वदंति दुम्मेहा।
ते ण परमद्वावादी णिच्छयवादीहिं णिंदिट्ठा ॥४३॥

In these various and many ways, the foolish ones describe the supreme self; they are not paramārthavādins — the niśchayavādins do not acknowledge them as such.

The verdict: none of these views pass the test of niśchaya-naya (ultimate standpoint). All of them describe the soul in terms of what is other than the soul. The niśchayavādin — one who speaks from the ultimate standpoint — cannot accept any description that includes material categories in the soul's definition. This is not philosophical pedantry. It is the precise boundary between bondage and liberation.

The simple version: All these confused views fail the test of ultimate truth. The niśchaya standpoint exposes them all as describing what is not the soul.

Niśchaya-nayaParamārthaVerdict
Part 2 · Gathas 44–48 · The Omniscient Declaration
Pivotal TeachingThe Kevali Jinas' Declaration

Kundkundacharya now invokes the highest possible authority — the Omniscient Ones — who have declared definitively that all those attributed soul-states are transformations of matter. Gathas 44–48 also introduce the crucial vyavahāra/niśchaya distinction that governs the rest of the Samaysaar.

1.44

एदे सव्वे भावा पोग्गलदव्वपरिणामिण्पण्णा।
केवलिजिणेहिं भणिया कह ते जीवो ति वृच्छंति ॥४४॥

All these states arise from the transformation of pudgala-dravya; the Kevali Jinas have declared this. How then can they be called the soul?

The philosophical pivot of the entire Adhikar. The Kevali Jinas — those with omniscient knowledge of all substances, all modes, all times — have declared: all those states (adhyavasāna, karma, the body, emotional intensities) are transformations of pudgala-dravya. This is not arguable. It is a report of omniscient perception. The question that follows is devastating: if they are all material — how can they be the soul?

The simple version: The Omniscient Ones have seen and declared: all those attributed soul-states are matter. The question answers itself — they cannot be the soul.

Pudgala-dravyaKevali JinasOmniscient Declaration
1.45

अट्ठिवहं पि य कम्मं सव्वं पोग्गलमयं जिणा बेंति।
जस्स फलं तं वुच्चिदे दुक्खं ति विपच्चमाणस्स ॥४५॥

All eight types of karma are entirely pudgala — so the Jinas declare. The fruit of this karma when it ripens is what is called duhkha (suffering).

All eight karmas (jñānāvaraṇa, darśanāvaraṇa, vedanīya, mohanīya, āyu, nāma, gotra, antarāya) are declared to be entirely material — not a blend of consciousness and matter. And what karma produces when it matures is suffering. Crucially: suffering is attributed to a material process, not to the soul's inherent nature. The soul does not suffer intrinsically; karma makes it appear to suffer.

The simple version: All eight karmas are matter. Suffering is karma's product, not the soul's own nature.

Eight KarmasDuhkhaPudgala
1.46

ववहारस्स दरीसणमुवएसो वण्णिदो जिणवरेहिं।
जीवा एदे सव्वे अझ्झवसाणादओ भावा ॥४६॥

The Jinendras have described this as the teaching of the vyavahāra viewpoint — that all these adhyavasāna etc. bhavas are "jīvas."

A necessary clarification: some Āgamic passages do call adhyavasāna and related states "the soul." Kundkundacharya explains: this is vyavahāra language — a teaching convention used for souls who are still identified with material coverings and cannot yet be addressed in ultimate terms. It is not the final truth. The next two gathas illustrate this with an unforgettable analogy.

The simple version: When Jain texts call adhyavasāna "the soul," that is teaching language — vyavahāra — not ultimate truth.

Vyavahāra-nayaPedagogical Convention
1.47

राया हु णिग्गदो ति य एसो बलसमुदयस्स आदेसो।
ववहारेण दु उच्चिद तत्थेक्को णिग्गदो राया ॥४७॥

"The king went out" — this is the conventional expression for the army's departure. By vyavahāra it is said; but in truth, within that army, only one king actually went out.

The analogy is brilliant in its simplicity. When an army marches, we say "the king went out." But literally only one person is the king. The soldiers are not the king. "The king went out" is conventional shorthand. Similarly: when scriptures say "these bhavas are jīvas," it is shorthand. Only one jīva is present. Everything associated with it by convention is not the jīva itself.

The simple version: "The king went out" = the army went. It's convention. "Adhyavasāna is the soul" = the same kind of convention. One true jīva; the rest is what accompanies it.

King AnalogyVyavahāra vs Niśchaya
1.48

एमेव य ववहारो अझ्झवसाणादिअण्णभावाणं।
जीवो ति कदो सुत्ते तत्थेक्को णिच्छिदो जीवो ॥४८॥

Similarly, calling adhyavasāna etc. other-bhavas as "jīva" in the sūtra is vyavahāra; but from niśchaya, only one jīva is established there.

The analogy applied: within the entire complex of soul + karma + body + mental states, there is exactly one jīva. The Āgamas use vyavahāra language ("this is the soul's karma, this is the soul's anger") as a teaching convenience. The ultimate truth: one conscious entity, distinct from all material categories.

The simple version: Within the whole samsāric complex — one jīva. From the ultimate standpoint, that is the only thing that truly is the soul.

One JīvaNiśchaya Conclusion
Part 3 · Gathas 49–51 · The Soul's True Nature
Core DefinitionWhat the Soul Positively Is

After 11 gathas eliminating wrong views, Kundkundacharya now speaks directly to the reader: "Know the soul as..." The soul is defined by one positive quality — chetanā (consciousness) — and the complete absence of every material quality.

1.49

अरसमरूवमगंधं अव्वत्तं चेदणागुणमसदं।
जाण अलिंगग्गहणं जीवमिणिदिट्ठसंठाणं ॥४९॥

Know the soul as: without taste, without form, without smell, imperceptible to the senses, having consciousness (chetanā) as its essential quality, without sound, not grasped through signs, and of no defined physical shape.

The positive definition. After systematic elimination, the soul is described by: one positive attribute — chetanā — and the complete absence of all material qualities. No taste, form, smell, sound, defined shape. Not accessible to any sense organ. Not identifiable through external signs. Chetanā is not something the soul has — it is what the soul is. This is the complete definition.

The simple version: The soul has no material quality. One thing defines it: consciousness. That is what it is — not what it has.

ChetanāDefinition of SoulNo Material Qualities
1.50

जीवस्स णत्थि वण्णो ण वि गंधो ण वि रसो ण वि य फासो।
ण वि रूवं ण सरीरं ण वि संठाणं ण संहणणं ॥५०॥

The soul has no color, no smell, no taste, no touch; no form, no body, no shape (saṃsthāna), no physical constitution (saṃhanana).

The systematic elimination continues: every sensory quality is removed from the soul's definition, then form, body, shape, and physical constitution (saṃhanana — the structural arrangement and solidity of material bodies). Not one sensory attribute, not the body, not the body's shape belongs to the soul. The soul's nature is untouched by all of these.

The simple version: Color, smell, taste, touch, form, body, shape — none of it. Not one material property belongs to the soul.

No Sensory QualitiesNo BodyNo Shape
1.51

जीवस्स णत्थि रागो ण वि दोसो णेव विज्जदे मोहो।
णो पच्चया ण कम्मं णोकम्मं चावि से णत्थि ॥५१॥

The soul has no rāga (attachment), no dvēṣa (aversion); moha (delusion) does not exist in it; no pratyayas (influx-causes), no karma, and nokamma also does not belong to it.

Now the psychological: rāga, dvēṣa, moha — the three great afflictions of samsāric existence — do not belong to the soul's nature. They are not inherent to consciousness; they are foreign intrusions driven by karma. Nor do pratyayas (the causes of karmic influx), karma, or nokamma belong to the soul. The very passions that seem most "like us" — our loves, aversions, confusions — are not us.

The simple version: The soul owns no attachment, no aversion, no delusion. No karma. No body. These are all foreign to the soul's pure nature.

Part 4 · Gathas 52–56 · Technical Categories That Don't Belong to the Soul

Kundkundacharya now works through the entire technical vocabulary of Jain philosophy — material groupings, karmic categories, spiritual stages — and eliminates each from the soul's definition. Not one belongs to the soul's own nature.

1.52

जीवस्स णत्थि वग्गो ण वग्गणा णेव फड्डुया केई।
णो अझ्झप्पट्ठाणा णेव य अणुभागट्ठाणाणि ॥५२॥

The soul has no varga, no vargaṇā, no spardhaka whatsoever; no adhyātmasthānas, no anubhāgasthānas — none of these.

Varga, vargaṇā, spardhaka are increasingly refined groupings of pudgala-particles in Jain physics. Adhyātmasthānas and anubhāgasthānas describe degrees of karmic intensity. None of these belong to the soul. The soul's nature cannot be indexed in terms of material categories — however refined or technically precise those categories are.

The simple version: Every category from Jain physics that describes material organization — none of it belongs to the soul.

VargaVargaṇāSpardhakaAnubhāgasthāna
1.53

जीवस्स णत्थि केई जोयट्ठाणा ण बंधट्ठाणा वा।
णेव य उदयट्ठाणा ण मग्गणट्ठाणया केई ॥५३॥

The soul has no yogasthānas, no bandhhasthānas; no udayasthānas, no mārgaṇāsthānas of any kind.

Yogasthānas = degrees of yoga (body-mind-speech vibration causing karmic influx). Bandhhasthānas = karmic binding configurations. Udayasthānas = stages of karmic maturation. Mārgaṇāsthānas = the fourteen search-categories classifying souls by current state (gati, indriya, kāya, etc.). All these describe the soul's situation in samsāra — not what the soul intrinsically is.

The simple version: The technical classifications used to describe souls in Jain literature — yogasthāna, mārgaṇā, etc. — describe the soul's situation, not its nature.

YogasthānaBandhhasthānaMārgaṇāsthāna
1.54

णो ठिदिबंधट्ठाणा जीवस्स ण संकिलेसट्ठाणा वा।
णेव विसोहिट्ठाणा णो संजमलद्धिट्ठाणा वा ॥५४॥

The soul has no sthitibandhasthānas, no saṃkleśasthānas; no viśuddhisthānas, no saṃyamalabdhisthānas.

Even the categories describing spiritual progress — degree of defilement (saṃkleśa), degree of purification (viśuddhi), attainment of restraint (saṃyamalabdhi) — are karma-related processes, not the soul's inherent nature. The soul's own nature is identical before and after all these stages. The stages describe what karma is doing; the soul is the knower of what karma is doing.

The simple version: Even your current degree of spiritual purification is a karma-driven process. It describes your situation, not your nature.

SaṃkleśaViśuddhiSpiritual Progress
1.55

णेव य जीवट्ठाणा ण गुणट्ठाणा य अत्थि जीवस्स।
जेण दु एदे सव्वे पोग्गलदव्वस्स परिणामा ॥५५॥

The soul has no jīvasthānas, no guṇasthānas — because all of these are transformations (pariṇāma) of pudgala-dravya.

The culminating statement of the technical section. Even the fourteen guṇasthānas — the spiritual stages from mithyādṛṣṭi to siddha that form the spine of Jain spiritual psychology — are declared to be transformations of pudgala-dravya. Why? Because they are all caused by karma (pudgala). They are karma-effects. The soul's niśchaya-nature is not captured by any of them. The soul is what knows all fourteen stages; it is not any one of them.

The simple version: Even the fourteen guṇasthānas are karma-products, not the soul's inherent nature. The soul is the knower of those stages, not any particular stage.

GuṇasthānaAll Are PudgalaCulminating Statement
1.56

ववहारेण दु एदे जीवस्स हवंति वण्णमादीया।
गुणट्ठाणंता भावा ण दु केई णिच्छयणयस्स ॥५६॥

By vyavahāra, color etc. up to guṇasthāna-related bhavas "belong to" the soul; but from the niśchaya standpoint, not one of these applies.

The explicit summary: vyavahāra (conventional) language attributes all material properties to the soul as a teaching convenience. Niśchaya (ultimate) standpoint recognizes zero material categories as belonging to the soul's own nature. Both standpoints are valid — neither can be discarded. But only the niśchaya standpoint, when genuinely recognized, produces liberation.

The simple version: Vyavahāra says color belongs to the soul. Niśchaya says nothing material belongs to the soul. Both are needed; only niśchaya liberates.

Part 5 · Gathas 57–61 · The Soul's Relationship with Matter
1.57

एदेहिं य संबंधो जहेव खीरोदयं मुणेदव्वो।
ण य होंति तस्स ताणि दु उवओगगुणाधिगो जम्हा ॥५७॥

The soul's connection with these [color etc.] should be understood like the mixing of milk and water; yet those do not truly belong to the soul, because the soul is superior in the quality of upayoga (conscious knowing).

The most celebrated analogy of the Adhikar. Milk and water mix completely — inseparable in appearance. Yet they are different substances, and a swan (in the poetic tradition) can separate them. Soul and karma are mixed like this: thoroughly intermixed in samsāra, appearing as one, but fundamentally two different substances. What distinguishes the soul? Upayoga — the capacity for conscious knowing. Matter, however refined, has no upayoga. Consciousness does. That single difference is everything.

The simple version: Soul and matter are mixed like milk and water. One quality distinguishes the soul: upayoga — conscious knowing. Matter has none of this.

Milk-Water AnalogyUpayogaDiscrimination
1.58

पंथे मुस्संतं परिस्सदूण लोगा भणंति ववहारी।
मुस्सिदे एसो पंथो ण य पंथो मुस्सदे कोई ॥५८॥

Seeing a traveler being robbed on the path, people conventionally say "the path is being robbed" — but in truth, no path is actually being robbed; it is the traveler.

The second brilliant analogy for vyavahāra. "This road is dangerous" — we attribute to the road what happened to a person on it. The road cannot be robbed; it has no possessions. Only the traveler can be robbed. The road is the location, not the subject. Similarly: when we say "the soul has color," we attribute to the soul what belongs to the karma traveling with it. The soul is the traveler; karma is what it carries.

The simple version: No path is ever robbed — the traveler is. Similarly, the soul isn't colored — karma, which travels with the soul, is.

Path-Robbery AnalogyVyavahāra Language
1.59

तह जीवे कम्माणं णोकम्माणं च परिस्सदुं वण्णं।
जीवस्स एस वण्णो जिणेहिं ववहारदो उत्तो ॥५९॥

Similarly, seeing the color of karma and nokamma situated around the soul, "this is the soul's color" — thus the Jinas have spoken by vyavahāra only.

The application: karma and nokamma (body-matter) carry material color, situated around the soul. From a conventional perspective it appears the soul is colored. The Jinas, teaching those still identified with material coverings, use the conventional language. But the qualifier is explicit: vyavahārata uktah — spoken by vyavahāra only. This is not the ultimate truth about the soul.

The simple version: "The soul's color" means karma's color seen around the soul. The Jinas said so — by convention only.

Color of KarmaConventional Speech
1.60

गंधरसफासरूवा देहो संठाणमाइया जे य।
सव्वे ववहारस्स य णिच्छयदण्हू वविदसंति ॥६०॥

Smell, taste, touch, form, body, shape etc. — all of these, those who see ultimate truth describe only from the vyavahāra standpoint.

Even those who have realized ultimate truth — when they use sense-quality language in relation to the soul — are speaking from the vyavahāra standpoint. They know it. This is the mark of a mature teacher: they can speak both standpoints with precision, never conflating them. The bhūtārtha-draṣṭa (seer of ultimate truth) is never confused by the conventional language they themselves use for pedagogical purposes.

The simple version: Even the greatest teachers who use conventional soul-language about sense-qualities know they are speaking conventionally. They are never confused about it.

Bhūtārtha-draṣṭaMasters Speak Conventionally
1.61

तत्थ भवे जीवाणं संसारट्ठाण होंति वण्णादी।
संसारपमुक्काणं णत्थि हु वण्णादओ केई ॥६१॥

In samsāra, for worldly souls, color etc. exist conventionally; but for those liberated from samsāra, no color etc. exist whatsoever.

The empirical proof: liberated souls (siddhas) have no color, no body, no shape, no location. If color were truly the soul's own, it would persist in liberation. Its complete absence in liberation proves it was never the soul's own nature — only a temporary association caused by karma. The karma falls away; what remains is the soul — colorless, formless, pure consciousness.

The simple version: Liberated souls have no color. If color were truly the soul's, liberation couldn't remove it. Its removal proves it was never the soul's own.

Part 6 · Gathas 62–64 · The Logical Refutation
1.62

जीवो चेव हि एदे सव्वे भाव ति मण्णसे जदि हि।
जीवस्साजीवस्स य णत्थि विसेसो दु दे कोई ॥६२॥

If you believe that all these bhavas [color, rāga, karma etc.] are indeed the soul — then there remains no distinction whatsoever between jīva and ajīva for you.

The reductio begins. If the soul is defined by rāga, karma, the body, guṇasthānas — all material — then in what sense is anything not the soul? The jīva-ajīva distinction collapses entirely. This is the ultimate philosophical failure. A system that cannot distinguish consciousness from matter cannot teach liberation — because liberation means consciousness freeing itself from matter.

The simple version: If soul = material qualities, then jīva and ajīva become indistinct. You've lost the entire teaching.

ReductioJīva-Ajīva Distinction
1.63

अह संसारट्ठाणं जीवाणं तुझ होंति वण्णादी।
तम्हा संसारट्था जीवा रूवित्तमावण्णा ॥६३॥

If you say that in the samsāric state, color etc. belong to souls — then it follows that samsāric souls have attained the quality of rūpa-ness (form/matter-ness).

The argument tightens: even if you limit the claim to samsāra only — "souls in samsāra have color" — the consequence is that souls are rūpa (material, formed). Souls become pudgala. No liberation is then possible, because a pudgala-soul cannot escape pudgala. The entire project of liberation becomes incoherent.

The simple version: If samsāric souls have color, they are material. And a material soul cannot be liberated. The project of liberation requires consciousness to be fundamentally different from matter.

ReductioSoul Becomes Pudgala
1.64

एवं पोग्गलदव्वं जीवो तहलक्खणेण मूढमदी।
णिव्वाणमुवगदो वि य जीवत्तं णोग्गलो पत्तो ॥६४॥

By that reasoning, the foolish-minded one is calling pudgala-dravya "the soul" — and even at liberation, pudgala would have attained the state of jīva-ness. An absurd conclusion.

The reductio reaches its conclusion: if the soul is defined by material qualities, then pudgala becomes the soul. And since the "liberated soul" in nirvāṇa loses all material associations, it would lose its soul-nature at liberation — and pudgala would somehow achieve jīva-hood. The entire framework collapses. The only coherent position: consciousness is the soul, and matter is other.

The simple version: Carried to its conclusion, the wrong view makes pudgala the soul — absurd. The refutation is complete. Consciousness is the soul; matter is not.

Part 7 · Gathas 65–66 · The Classification Problem
1.65

एकं च दोण्णि तिण्णि य चत्तारि य पंच इंदिया जीवा।
बादरपज्जत्तिदरा पयडीओ णामकम्मस्स ॥६५॥

One, two, three, four, five-sensed beings — bādara (gross), paryāpta (fully developed), itara (undeveloped) — these are the species-types (prakṛtis) of nāma-karma.

Even the most familiar soul-classifications — one-sensed to five-sensed, gross or subtle — are declared to be nāma-karma's prakṛtis (species-determining material configurations). When we say "this is a five-sensed being," we are naming the nāma-karma type associated with that soul. The soul does not have a sense-count by its own nature — it has upayoga. The "five-sensed" label describes the karmic body configuration, not the soul.

The simple version: "One-sensed," "five-sensed," "gross," "subtle" — these are nāma-karma categories, not soul-categories.

One-to-Five SensesNāma-karmaBādara/Sūkṣma
1.66

एदाहि य णिव्वत्ता जीवट्ठाणा उ करणभूदाहिं।
पयडीहिं पोग्गलमइहिं ताहिं कहं भण्णदे जीवो ॥६६॥

The jīva-sthānas are produced by these nāma-karma prakṛtis which are instrumental; these prakṛtis are pudgala-made. How then can those jīva-sthānas be called the soul?

The logic is clean: jīva-sthānas are produced by nāma-karma prakṛtis → those prakṛtis are material → therefore jīva-sthānas are pudgala-products. They are instruments of classification, not the soul itself. Something produced by matter cannot be consciousness.

The simple version: Jīva-sthānas are produced by nāma-karma (matter). How can something produced by matter be the soul?

Part 8 · Gathas 67–68 · Convention and Reality — The Final Distinctions
Closing TeachingThe Separation Is Complete

The final two gathas clinch the Adhikar's argument — even the body-type labels in the Āgamas and the guṇasthānas themselves are conventional speech (vyavahāra), not descriptions of the soul's own nature. The Adhikar ends: "इति जीवाजीवौ पृथग्भूत्वा निष्क्रान्तौ" — "Thus jīva and ajīva, having been separated, have exited the arena."

1.67

पज्जत्तापज्जत्ता जे सुहुमा बादरा य जे चेव।
देहस्स जीवसण्णा सुत्ते ववहारदो उत्ता ॥६७॥

Paryāpta, aparyāpta, sūkṣma, bādara — these designations for the body have been called "jīva-saṃjñā" (soul-designations) in the sūtras only by vyavahāra.

Āgamic sūtras use "paryāpta jīva," "sūkṣma jīva" — but these are body-type designations spoken as soul-designations for conventional identification. Paryāpta (fully developed), aparyāpta (undeveloped), sūkṣma (subtle body), bādara (gross body) describe the physical apparatus. Called "jīva-saṃjñā" only by teaching convention, not because they name the soul's own nature.

The simple version: "Paryāpta jīva," "sūkṣma jīva" — body-type labels called soul-labels by convention. Not statements about the soul's own nature.

1.68

मोहणकम्मस्सुदया दु वण्णिया जे इमे गुणट्ठाणा।
ते कह हवंति जीवा जे णिच्चमचेदणा उत्ता ॥६८॥

The guṇasthānas described as arising from the udaya of mohanīya-karma — how can those be the soul? For they are declared eternally devoid of consciousness (achetana).

The final argument. The fourteen guṇasthānas arise from the rising and falling of mohanīya-karma — they are karma-driven processes. And the Āgamas themselves declare the guṇasthānas to be eternally achetana (devoid of consciousness). How can what is eternally non-conscious be called the soul — which is defined by consciousness? The conclusion is inescapable: guṇasthānas are markers of the soul's karmic situation, not descriptions of what the soul is.

The Adhikar's closing declaration — jīva and ajīva enter together (mixed, confused) and exit separate, clearly distinguished. This is the work of Adhikar 1: to make that separation in the understanding of the seeker. Once this separation is genuine — not just philosophical, but seen — the foundation for all liberation is laid.

The simple version: Guṇasthānas are driven by karma and declared achetana by the Āgamas themselves. The non-conscious cannot be the soul. The discrimination is complete.
