Part 1 · G288–G292 · The Bound Man: Knowing Is Not Enough
Analogy
The Rope-Bound Man Who Knows His Bondage Kundakunda opens Adhikar 8 with a deliberate parallel to Adhikar 7's oil-smeared man. A man is physically bound with ropes. He knows everything about his bondage — whether it is tight or loose, how long he has been bound. He knows, and he thinks about it, and he practices. And yet he remains tied. The only thing that frees the bound man is cutting the ropes. This is the structural foundation of the entire Adhikar: knowing → insufficient; cutting → necessary.
8.288

बद्धो मणुस्सो ते बंधे जाणइ तिव्वमंदए।
तत्थ काले य जाणंतो इत्थिपुरिसओ इह।।२८८।।

A bound man knows those bonds — their intense (tīvra) and mild (manda) nature, their duration — whether woman or man, he knows them fully.

Kundakunda specifies: the bound man's knowledge is detailed and accurate. Tīvra-manda: the gradient of tightness. Kāla: the duration. He misses nothing in his understanding. The opening move is to give the knowing-being every possible epistemic credit — and then show that even this complete knowledge is insufficient.

The simple version: A bound man knows exactly how he is tied and for how long. His knowing is complete.

Jñāna ≠ Moksha Tīvra-Manda
8.289

ण य सो तिं छिंदइ बंधे चिरेण वि ण मुच्चइ।
तह कम्मविस्सपडियो जीवो जोगे वि ण मुच्चइ।।२८९।।

If he does not cut those bonds — even over a long time he does not attain liberation. Similarly, a soul absorbed in karma (karma-smeared), even through yogic practice, does not attain liberation.

Chirena api — even over long time. Time is not the liberating variable. Yoga here means external religious disciplines — postures, fasts, rituals, practices. Without the inner cutting, all of these leave bondage intact. This is not an argument against practice; it is an argument for understanding what practice is for.

The simple version: No matter how long he waits, no matter what religious practices he adds — if the ropes are not cut, he stays tied.

Karma-Smeara Yoga ≠ Sufficient Chinditvā Required
8.290

एवं पगडिं च पदेसं च ठिदिं अणुभागं च।
जाणिय कम्मबंधं सुद्धो जीवो हवदि मुत्तो।।२९०।।

Similarly — having known prakṛti (nature), pradeśa (quantum), sthiti (duration), and anubhāga (intensity) of karma-bandha — the soul is freed only when it becomes śuddha (pure).

The four aspects of karma-bandha are the complete technical vocabulary of karma-theory. One who knows all four knows everything classical Jain doctrine says about karma. Kundakunda does not dispute that this knowledge is real and important — but he specifies: mukti comes when the soul becomes śuddha, not when it knows karma theory.

Contemplate: Could philosophical knowledge of karma theory be a subtle form of the same problem — identification with the intellectual mapper rather than with the pure knowing-ness?

The simple version: Knowing all four dimensions of karma is not liberation. Liberation is the state of becoming śuddha — disidentified from all of them.

Prakṛti-Pradeśa-Sthiti-Anubhāga Śuddhatā
8.291

जह बद्धो मणुस्सो बंधे चिंतेइ ण हवइ मुत्तो।
तह जीवो चिंतंतो बंधं णेव हवइ मुत्तो।।२९१।।

Just as a physically-bound man, by thinking about his bondage, does not attain liberation — so the soul, by thinking about bandha, does not attain moksha.

Cintana (contemplation/thinking about) is distinguished from chedana (cutting). Contemplating the nature of bondage, however correct and sustained, is not the act that frees. The trap is subtle: one can spend a lifetime in spiritually-framed thinking about karma — and mistake that contemplative activity for the actual work of liberation.

The simple version: Thinking about your chains does not break your chains. The work of freedom is different from the analysis of captivity.

Cintana ≠ Chedana Contemplation vs Transformation
8.292

जह बद्धो मणुस्सो बंधे छिंदित्ता हवइ मुत्तो।
तह जीवो छिंदित्ता बंधं सव्वेण मुच्चइ।।२९२।।

Just as the physically-bound man, by cutting the bondage, attains freedom — so the soul, by cutting bandha, fully attains moksha.

Sarvena — completely, totally. Moksha in Jain doctrine is not partial. Chinditvā — having cut — implies a definitive, decisive act, not gradual erosion. The bound man does not slowly loosen the ropes over decades while studying rope-theory. He cuts. The soul that performs the inner prajñā-chedana completely separates from all bandha-identification and attains the full state.

The simple version: Cut the bondage. That is the act of liberation. Not analysis — the decisive action of wisdom separating self from not-self.

Chedana = Moksha Sarvena Mukti Decisive Prajñā
Part 2 · G293–G295 · Dispassion + Wisdom-Cutting = Liberation
8.293

बंधाणं आदाणं च एवं जाणिय सो विरज्जदि।
जो बंधेसु विरत्तो सो कम्मविमोक्खणं कुणदि।।२९३।।

Having known the svabhāva of bandhas and also of ātmā — one who becomes virakt (dispassionate) toward the bandhas — accomplishes karma-liberation (karma-vimokṣaṇa).

The formula becomes precise: jñāna of bandha-svabhāva and ātmā-svabhāva + virāga toward bandha = karma-vimokṣaṇa. G288-G291 showed that knowing alone is insufficient. The addition here is virāga — dispassion that arises from seeing clearly what is other. This is not forced detachment but the natural cooling of attachment when prajñā reveals that what one was attached to was never one's own.

The simple version: When you truly know that bondage belongs to a different nature than yours, dispassion arises naturally. That natural dispassion, combined with true knowing, accomplishes liberation.

Virāga Svabhāva-Jñāna Karma-Vimokṣaṇa
8.294

जीवो बंधो य तहा छिज्जंति सलक्खणेहिं णियएहिं।
पण्णाछेदणएण दु छिण्णा णाणत्तमावण्णा।।२९४।।

Sanskrit Chhaya: जीवो बन्धश्च तथा छिद्यन्ते स्वलक्षणैः नियतैः। प्रज्ञाछेदनकेन तु छिन्नौ नानात्वमापन्नौ।।294।।

Jīva and bandha are cut by their own niyata (fixed, essential) svalakṣaṇas (own-characteristics). By the prajñā-chedana (wisdom-knife) the two are cut and attain nānātva — mutual distinctness/separateness.

The cut happens along the natural fault line. Jīva's svalakṣaṇa is caitanya (consciousness); bandha's svalakṣaṇa is jaḍatā (inertness). These are niyata — fixed, not negotiable, not arbitrary. The prajñā-chedana cuts precisely at this boundary, not across it. The result: nānātva is āpanna (attained) — the two stand as what they always were. The wisdom-knife does not create separation; it reveals the separation that was always ontologically true.

Contemplate: If jīva and bandha were always ontologically distinct, what does the wisdom-knife actually do? What does it mean to "cut" something that was never truly joined?

The simple version: The soul and karma have different fixed natures. Wisdom cuts at precisely where those natures diverge. After the cut, they stand as what they always were — two entirely separate things. Prajñā reveals; it does not manufacture.

Prajñā-Chedana Svalakṣaṇa Nānātva Caitanya vs Jaḍatā Ontological Distinction
8.295

जीवो बंधो य तहा छिज्जंति सलक्खणेहिं णियएहिं।
बंधो छेदेदव्वो सुद्धो अप्पा य घेत्तव्वो।।२९५।।

Jīva and bandha are cut by their own niyata svalakṣaṇas. Bandha is to be abandoned (chhedanīya) and śuddha ātmā is to be grasped (grāhya).

After establishing the act of cutting (G294), G295 gives the direction. Two movements constitute moksha: (1) release of what is para — bandha is chhedanīya, to be let go; (2) apprehension of what is sva — śuddha ātmā is grāhya, to be grasped and inhabited. Liberation is not merely the absence of bondage — it is the positive presence of ātma-sākṣātkāra.

The simple version: Two steps: let go of what is not you (bandha). Hold what is you (the pure self). This is moksha in practice.

Bandha-Chhedana Ātma-Graha Śuddha Ātmā
Part 3 · G296–G300 · How the Soul Is Grasped: I Am the Conscious One · Seer · Knower
8.296

कह सो घिप्पिदे अप्पा पण्णाए सो तु घिप्पदे अप्पा।
जह पण्णाइ विभत्तो तह पण्णाएव घेत्तव्वो।।२९६।।

How is ātmā grasped? By prajñā is ātmā grasped. Just as by prajñā (ātmā was) separated (from bandha) — so by prajñā alone is it to be grasped.

The question arises naturally from G295: how does one grasp the śuddha ātmā? The answer: the same prajñā-knife that separated ātmā from bandha is the instrument for grasping ātmā. Prajñā has two functions — separation (vibhaktatā) and apprehension (grahana). There is no other instrument — neither ritual nor yoga can grasp the self, because the self is of the nature of jñāna and is known by jñāna. Prajñā does not grasp the self as an object; prajñā is the self grasping itself.

The simple version: The same discrimination that says "that karma is not me" can say "this awareness is me." Wisdom is both the separator and the recognizer.

Prajñā as Instrument Svayam-Vedyatā Ātma-Graha by Jñāna
8.297

पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो चेदया होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।२९७।।

By prajñā — that cetā (consciousness-soul) is certainly I by niścaya (definitive knowledge). The remaining bhāvas are all para (other) — thus one knows by prajñā.

First of the triple recognition. Cetā is the one who is conscious — the agent of consciousness (not of karma). Niścaya emphasizes: not a tentative philosophical opinion but a direct recognition, not mediated by vyavahāra equivocation. I am cetā. All karma-pariṇāmas, all external events, all remaining states — para.

The simple version: By wisdom I know with certainty: I am the conscious one. All other states are not-me.

Cetā Niścaya-Recognition Para-Bhāva
8.298

पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो दट्ठा होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।२९८।।

By prajñā — that draṣṭā (seer) is certainly I by niścaya. The remaining bhāvas are all para — thus one knows by prajñā.

Second recognition: draṣṭā — the witness, the seer. Not the one who acts in karma-fields but the one who sees. The soul's nature as draṣṭā is not a function it performs but what it is. By prajñā one recognizes: I am the seer. All that I see — all that — is para.

The simple version: By wisdom I know: I am the seer. All that is seen — not me.

Draṣṭā Witness-Nature Seer vs Seen
8.299

पण्णाए य वि णिच्छयदो अहं ति सो णादा होदि।
सेसा भावा सव्वे परे ति य जाणादि पण्णाए।।२९९।।

By prajñā — that jñātā (knower) is certainly I by niścaya. The remaining bhāvas are all para — thus one knows by prajñā.

Third recognition: jñātā — the knower. The soul's fundamental nature is jñātṛtva — knower-ness. It knows without being the content of knowing. By prajñā: I am the knower. Everything known — all karma-results, all pariṇāmas, all of the world — is para. The three (cetā, draṣṭā, jñātā) describe the same ātmā from three angles, each emphasizing pure subjecthood — never object, never doer of karma.

Contemplate: Can you feel the difference between "I am the one who acts/suffers/changes" and "I am the one who is aware of acting/suffering/changing"? The second is draṣṭā-jñātā. That is the self.

The simple version: By wisdom I know: I am the knower. All that is known — not me.

Jñātā Jñātṛtva Knower vs Known
8.300

को णाम भणिज्ज बुहो णादुं सव्वे पराइए भावे।
मज्झिमणं ति य वयणं जाणंतो अप्पयं सुद्धं।।३००।।

Who indeed would the wise one (budha) say is "mine" — knowing all bhāvas as para and knowing the pure ātmā as self?

A rhetorical question that closes the section. The answer is implicit: nothing. No bhāva, no karma-pariṇāma, no external thing belongs to the one who has truly seen. "Majjhimaṇaṃ ti vayanam" — the word "mine" (mamakāra) is the engine of saṃsāra. The entire machinery of rebirth runs on mamakāra. The jñānī who has completed the prajñā-chedana and recognized ātmā as cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā sees no ground for mamakāra anywhere. This is not a practice of trying not to be attached — it is the natural result of seeing clearly.

The simple version: Once you truly see what is para and what is sva, the word "mine" loses all meaning for everything except ātmā itself.

Mamakāra Budha (Wise One) Śuddha Ātmā Natural Non-Attachment
Part 4 · G301–G305 · The Criminal and the Innocent: Guilty Soul and Innocent Soul
Analogy
The Guilty Man and the Innocent Man Kundakunda builds a second analogy. Two men move through the world. One has committed crimes: he moves fearfully, always watching, fearing arrest. The other has committed no crime: he moves freely and fearlessly, because fear of bondage does not arise from what has not been done. The soul-parallel: the soul that still believes it is the karma-doer (kartā-bhāva) moves fearfully through saṃsāra — "will I bind karma?" The soul that has recognized itself as jñātā-draṣṭā knows it is not the doer of karma and moves without fear.
8.301

अवरज्झिय मणुस्सो पवयरदि भयभीदो।
मा हं पाविज्जा इदि चोरो त्ति य।।३०१।।

One who has committed crimes moves about fearfully — "may I not be caught as a thief."

A man who has committed theft or crime lives in perpetual fear — fear of being identified, caught, and punished. Every interaction carries the shadow of his guilt. His fear is not incidental; it is the natural consequence of having done what he knows to be wrong. He cannot move through the world openly, because his own inner knowledge of guilt shadows every step.

The simple version: The guilty man lives in fear. His crime, internalized, produces an ongoing experience of anxiety wherever he goes.

Guilt Fear as Consequence Criminal Analogy
8.302

णिरवज्झिय मणुस्सो णिब्भयो पवयरदि।
ण मज्झ बंधो होहीइ इदि चिंता ण हवइ तस्स।।३०२।।

One who commits no crime moves fearlessly. The thought "I will be bound" never arises in him.

The innocent man moves freely, with no anxiety about being caught. The thought "bondage will come to me" does not arise — because he knows his actions have been blameless. His innocence is not a performance or a suppression of fear; it is the natural ground of fearlessness. Nothing to hide means nothing to fear. He can look any situation in the eye without flinching, because there is no inner contradiction between what he has done and what he presents to the world.

The simple version: The innocent man moves without fear. Blamelessness produces fearlessness — naturally, not as an effort.

Innocence Natural Fearlessness Nothing to Hide
8.303

एवं सावराहो जीवो बंधो मज्झ होइ त्ति।
संकिदो पवयरदि णिरावराहो णिब्भयो।।३०३।।

Similarly — the sāparādha (guilty) soul thinks fearfully "I will bind karma." The nirāparādha (innocent) soul: "I am fearless, I will not bind."

The analogy lands. The soul with aparādha — with the guilt of kartā-bhāva, believing itself to be the karma-doer — lives in fear of its own pariṇāmas: "will I fall? will passion bind me?" The nirāparādha soul — recognized as cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā — knows it is not the doer of karma. Fear of binding does not arise for the one who knows binding is not theirs to do. This is counterintuitive: fear of sin is not awakening — it is a symptom of still-existing kartā-bhāva.

Contemplate: Is spiritual fear — fear of sin, fear of hell, fear of bad karma — a mark of awakening or a sign of still-existing kartā-bhāva?

The simple version: The soul that still believes it is the karma-doer lives in fear of its own karma. The soul that knows it is the knower — not the doer — has nothing to fear.

Kartā-Bhāva Sāparādha vs Nirāparādha Spiritual Fear as Symptom
8.304

संसिद्धिरादा सिद्धो साहिदो आराहिदो त्ति एए।
अपगयरादो जीवो सो हवइ अवराहो।।३०४।।

Accomplished, established, perfected, worshipped, revered — these words all mean the same thing. The soul that has departed from self-abidance is the transgressing soul.

An etymological verse that transforms the meaning of transgression entirely. Kundakunda lists five synonyms — saṃsiddhi, rādha, siddha, sādhita, ārādhita — all of which point to the state of being established in the pure self, in self-worship. The word "aparādha" (transgression) literally means: having fallen away from ārādhana. The "crime" in the spiritual sense is not a list of moral violations — it is the single, foundational error of departing from self-abidance and living as though one is the doer of karma. Every moment of doer-belief is aparādha. Every moment of abiding as the pure witness-knower is ārādhana.

The simple version: True transgression is one thing: departing from self-abidance. Every moment of believing "I am the doer of karma" is the crime. Every moment of knowing "I am the pure knower" is self-worship.

Self-Abidance True Transgression Doer-Belief as Crime
8.305

णिरावराहो णिब्भयो सो जाणइ अप्पयं।
आराहणाए ठिदो सदा णिच्चं।।३०५।।

The innocent soul is fearless, knowing "I am the self," always and eternally abiding in self-worship.

Three qualities arise simultaneously and reinforce each other: fearlessness + self-knowledge + constant self-abidance. These are not sequential steps but a single unified state. To truly know "I am the pure soul — not the karma-doer, not the body, not the roles" is to be innocent of the fundamental transgression; to be innocent is to be fearless; to be fearless is to abide naturally in the recognition of one's own nature — permanently, not as a discipline but as what one simply is. Sada nicchyaṃ: always, eternally. Not as an occasional insight but as the permanent ground.

Contemplate: What is your ārādhana — is it an activity you perform at certain times, or a recognition you inhabit moment to moment?

The simple version: The soul established in self-knowing is free from fear and lives permanently in the worship of its own nature — not as an effort, but as its natural state.

Fearlessness Eternal Self-Knowing Self-Abidance as Natural State
Part 5 · G306–G307 · The Poison Pot and the Nectar Pot
Radical Teaching
The Poison-Pot and the Nectar-Pot G306-G307 are among the most provocative verses in all of Samaysaar. Kundakunda lists the eight central practices of Jain sādhana — pratikramaṇa, nindā, garhā, śuddhi and others — and calls them viṣa-kumbha: the poison-pot. Then he lists their negations — apratikramaṇa, anindā, agarhā, aśuddhi — and calls them amṛta-kumbha: the nectar-pot. The poison is not in the practices themselves but in the kartā-bhāva operating beneath them. When performed with the belief "I transgressed and I am now purifying," the practices reinforce the very doer-identification they are meant to dissolve. The nectar is the abidance in jñātā-draṣṭā from which no transgression occurred and no doer-self needs purifying.
8.306

पडिकमणं पडिसरणं परिहारो धारणा णियत्ती य।
णिंदा गरहा सोही अट्ठविहो होिद विसकुंभो।।३०६।।

Pratikramaṇa (returning from transgression), pratisaraṇa (regressing/reflecting), parihāra (avoidance), dhāraṇā (sustaining vows), nivṛtti (withdrawal), nindā (self-censure), garhā (confession before teacher), śuddhi (purification) — these eight types constitute the viṣa-kumbha (poison-pot).

The eight named here are central to standard Jain religious life — pratikramaṇa is one of the six āvaśyakas (essential daily duties), garhā is formal confession, dhāraṇā the cultivation of vows. Calling them viṣa is deliberate provocation. The poison is not in the act but in the kartā-bhāva. When one does pratikramaṇa believing "I (as doer) transgressed and I (as doer) am returning" — the ritual of purification becomes another act of identification with the empirical doer-self. A new karma of kartā-bhāva is generated even in the act of "removing" the old one.

The simple version: When you perform spiritual practices believing "I am the doer of this practice, I was the sinner, I am now the purifier" — that belief itself is the poison. The practice becomes a poison-pot when performed from within kartā-bhāva.

Pratikramaṇa Nindā-Garhā Kartā-Bhāva = Poison Viṣa-Kumbha
8.307

अप्पडिकमणमप्पडिसरणं अप्पिरहारो अधारणा चेव।
अणियत्ती य अणिंदागरहासोही अमयकुंभो।।३०७।।

Apratikramaṇa, apratisaraṇa, aparihāra, adhāraṇā, anivṛtti, anindā, agarhā, aśuddhi — these eight constitute the amṛta-kumbha (nectar-pot).

The "a-" prefix (non-, un-) is added to each of G306's eight. Apratikramaṇa does not mean one transgresses and ignores it — it means one abides in the state where there is no kartā-self to have transgressed. Anindā does not mean self-indulgence — it means there is no kartā-self to censure. The negation is not of right conduct but of the kartā-bhāva belief operating beneath it. The soul abiding in cetā-draṣṭā-jñātā — not identified with the karma-doer — lives in natural apratikramaṇa: there is no doer who transgressed, nothing to return from, no self to confess, no being to purify. That abidance is amṛta.

Contemplate: Poison and nectar from the same list of practices. The difference is entirely in whether kartā-bhāva is operating. Can you find the jñātā in yourself that would make this distinction experiential, not just theoretical?

The simple version: When you abide in the pure self — as the knower, not the doer — there is no transgression to return from, no sin to confess, no self to censure. That abidance is the nectar-pot. That is amṛta.

Apratikramaṇa Amṛta-Kumbha Jñātā Abidance = Nectar No Kartā = No Transgressor
Closing Colophon

इति मोक्षो निष्क्रान्त:।
इति श्रीमदमृतचन्द्रसूरिविरचितायां समयसारव्याख्यायामात्मख्यातौ
मोक्षप्ररूपकः अष्टमोऽङ्कः।।

Thus Moksha is departed. In the Ātmakhyāti — the authoritative commentary on Samaysaar composed by the revered Ācārya Amṛtacandra Sūri — the eighth adhikar, the exposition of Moksha, is thus concluded.

Sarvavishuddhajnana — सर्वविशुद्धज्ञान

The ninth adhikar investigates pure omniscient knowledge — the fully liberated state of the soul. What is the nature of the soul that has utterly released karma? What is the relationship between jīva and ajīva in their pariṇāmas? What distinguishes the ajñānī who experiences karmaphala from the jñānī who only knows it? And — most radical — what does it mean that both the Vaiṣṇava's claim ("Viṣṇu creates beings") and the śramaṇa's claim ("ātmā creates the six kāya") are the same error of kartā-bhāva?
