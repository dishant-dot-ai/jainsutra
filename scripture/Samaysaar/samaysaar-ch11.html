Samaysaar · Adhikar 10 · Pariśiṣṭam · Kalaśas 247–278

Conclusionउपसंहार · परिशिष्टम्

Amṛtacandrasūri's concluding philosophical synthesis — jñāna, jñeya, and jñātṛ are one undivided vastu; ātmā is simultaneously the path and the goal; caitanya-camatkāra is the uncontested victor

The Pariśiṣṭam is not a postscript. It is the seal. After nine adhikars establishing what the soul is and what it is not, Amṛtacandra steps forward as practitioner, not commentator, and declares: the knower, the knowing, and the known are one. The commentary ends where the soul begins — in silence.

Featured Verse — Kalaśa 278 · The Colophon

स्वशक्तिसंसूचितवस्तुतत्त्वै-
र्व्याख्या कृतेयं समयस्य शब्दैः।
स्वरूपगुप्तस्य न किञ्चिदस्ति
कर्तव्यमेवामृतचन्द्रसूरेः।। २७८ ।।

The commentary of the Samaya has been accomplished through words that reveal the tattva-vastu through their own śakti. For Amṛtacandrasūri — absorbed in his own svarūpa — there is nothing whatsoever remaining to be done.

The most extraordinary closing line in all of classical Jain literature. Not a dedication, not a summary — a declaration of arrival. The commentator dissolves into the ātmā he was describing. The author who has no more to do is the author who has become what the text was pointing toward.

Svarūpa-gupta Na Kiñcit Karttavyam Svayaṃ-Śakti Amṛtacandrasūri
Five Governing Principles of the Pariśiṣṭam
Principle I
Jñānamātra Declaration I am not fragmented by dravya, kṣetra, kāla, or bhāva. I am the supremely pure, singular jñānamātra bhāva. No external category can divide the undivided knower.
Principle II
47 Śaktis — Complete Self-Sufficiency Every power ātmā needs — to live, to know, to be blissful, to act — is inherent (svābhāvika), not borrowed. Mokṣa is not acquisition but full expression of what ātmā already is.
Principle III
Syādvāda: 14 Bhaṅgas of Jñāna Jñāna exists precisely in its own dravya-kṣetra-kāla-bhāva and nowhere else. This 14-fold syādvāda mapping dissolves the jñāna-mūḍha's confusion and leads to svayam-anubhava of ātmatattva.
Principle IV
Upāya-Upeya Identity Ātmā is simultaneously the path (sādhaka-rūpa) and the goal (siddha-rūpa). The means and the destination share the same jñānamātra nature. Knowing ātmā IS becoming ātmā.
Principle V
Vijñānaghana — Dvaita Dissolves In ajñāna, dvaita (sva-para split) appeared, spawning rāga-dveṣa, kriyā-kāraka differentiation, and exhausted experience. In jñāna, vijñānaghana alone remains. Kiñcin na kiñcit — nothing else.
Part 1 · Opening Declaration · The Jñānamātra Revelation
Doctrine
The Four-Fold Non-Fragmentation The Pariśiṣṭam opens with one of the most direct first-person doctrinal declarations in all of Sanskrit philosophical literature. The practitioner — not the theorist — speaks: I have looked at myself from every angle (dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva) and in none of them is there a fragmentation of the knower. The knower remains whole, singular, and pure across all four frameworks of analysis.
Opening Prose Declaration — The Supreme Self-Declaration

न द्रव्येण खण्डयामि, न क्षेत्रेण खण्डयामि,
न कालेन खण्डयामि, न भावेन खण्डयामि;
सुविशुद्ध एको ज्ञानमात्रो भावोऽस्मि।

I do not fragment myself by dravya (substance). I do not fragment myself by kṣetra (space). I do not fragment myself by kāla (time). I do not fragment myself by bhāva (mode). I am the supremely pure, singular, jñānamātra bhāva.

What this means: The śuddhanaya basis. When the jñānī holds the pure standpoint (śuddhanaya), the ātmā appears as undivided caitanya-mātra. No spatial location fragments it. No moment of time captures it. No modal modification exhausts it. "Suviśuddha" — beyond the admixture of karma-generated bhāvas. "Eko" — singular, not pluralized by objects known. "Jñānamātro bhāvo'smi" — I AM this jñānamātra bhāva, not merely associated with it.
Jñānamātra Śuddhanaya Na Khaṇḍayāmi Suviśuddha
Part 2 · 47 Śaktis of Ātmā · The Complete Self-Sufficiency Enumeration
Doctrine
Ātmā Lacks Nothing — All Śaktis Are Inherent Before the 14-bhaṅga analysis, the Pariśiṣṭam establishes the positive enumeration of ātmā's complete powers. The 47 śaktis establish a structural point: ātmā does not borrow any capacity from karma, body, or external contact. Every śakti is svābhāvika — constitutionally inherent. Mokṣa is not gaining new powers; it is the unobstructed expression of powers already present.
The 47 Śaktis — Principal Powers of Ātmā
# Śakti What It Means
1JīvataśaktiThe power of being alive — caitanya svarūpa itself
2CittiśaktiThe power of consciousness as such — pure sentience
3Anākāropayogamayī JñānaśaktiFormless (object-free) upayoga-jñāna — knowing without grasping object's shape
4Sākāropayogamayī JñānaśaktiFormed (object-reflecting) upayoga-jñāna — knowing as it illuminates objects in particular form
5AnākulatāsukhaśaktiThe power of undisturbed bliss — constitutional absence of agitation (vītarāga sukha)
6Svaropaparināmasāmarthya VīryaśaktiPower of vīrya as self-directed potency — transforms in own mode, not outward force
7AmūrtaghutvaśaktiPower of immaterial fineness — expressible in sixfold gradations of increase and decrease
8VibhutaśaktiPower of pervasion — single form that pervades all bhāvas (omnipresent in its jñāna-nature)
9SarvajñataśaktiPower of omniscience — the jñāna-form that knows all particular bhāvas of all reality
…+ 38 moreCompleting 47 total sub-śaktis, each inherent, none acquired
Why this matters: If you enumerate what ātmā already has, you realize what mokṣa actually removes — nothing. Mokṣa only removes the āvaraṇa (covering) that hides what was always there. You are not building yourself toward liberation. You are uncovering yourself toward liberation.
Jīvataśakti Anākulatā Sukha Sarvajñataśakti Svābhāvika
Part 3 · Kalaśas 247–263 · Syādvāda: The 14 Bhaṅgas of Jñāna
Doctrine
Syādvāda as Purifier — Precision Over Absolutism The 14-bhaṅga analysis uses the syādvāda framework to map precisely what jñāna is and what it is not. The goal is not intellectual completeness but prasāda (clarification/purification): once jñāna is seen precisely as existing only in its own dravya-kṣetra-kāla-bhāva and nowhere else, the jñāna-mūḍha's confusion dissolves, and the anekānta ātmatattva is experienced (anubhūyate) spontaneously.
The 14 Bhaṅgas — How Jñāna Exists and Does Not Exist
# Bhaṅga Meaning
1Svadravya-astittvaJñāna exists in its own dravya (ātmā)
2Paradravya-nastittvaJñāna is non-existent in other dravyas (pudgala, etc.)
3–4Sva/Para-kṣetraJñāna exists in its own space; non-existent in others' space
5–6Sva/Para-kālaJñāna exists in its own time-mode; non-existent in others'
7–8Sva/Para-bhāvaJñāna exists in its own bhāva (jñāna-guṇa paryāya); non-existent in others'
9Krama-parināmaJñāna changes sequentially (paryāya-mode)
10Akrama-parināmaJñāna's guṇa is unchanging (nityatva of the guṇa itself)
11NityatvaJñāna is permanent (dravyārthinaya)
12AnityatvaJñāna is impermanent (paryāyārthinaya)
13Nitya-anityatvaBoth permanent and impermanent simultaneously (anekānta)
14AvaktavyatvaInexpressible in its simultaneous unity (syāt-avaktavya)
The result: Every time you say "my body is sick" you commit paradravya-astittva — wrongly placing ātmā in body's kṣetra/bhāva. The 14 bhaṅgas are a corrective lens. Jñāna exists only in its own dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva. Once seen precisely, ātmā recognizes itself automatically — not through effort but through clarity.
Syādvāda Svadravya-Astittva Paradravya-Nastittva 14 Bhaṅgas Prasāda
Kalaśa 262 — Syādvāda Leads to Svayam-Anubhava

इत्यज्ञानविमूढ़ानां ज्ञानमात्रं प्रसादयन्।
आत्मतत्त्वमनेकान्तः स्वयमेवानुभूयते।। २६२ ।।

Thus, by clarifying jñānamātra for those bewildered by ajñāna, the anekānta-ātmatattva is experienced spontaneously (svayameva anubhūyate).

The teaching: Syādvāda does not produce liberation through argument. It produces clarity. And when the fog of ajñāna lifts — the ātmā, which was always there, simply appears. "Svayameva" — by itself, automatically. You cannot manufacture ātmā-anubhava; you can only remove the obstructions to it.
Kalaśa 263 — The Jain Śāsana Is Alaṅghya (Unbreachable)

एवं तत्त्वव्यवस्थित्या स्वं व्यवस्थापयन् स्वयम्।
अलङ्घ्यं शासनं जैनमेकान्तो व्यवस्थितः।। २६३ ।।

Thus, by establishing itself through tattva-vyavasthā (precise tattva-arrangement), the Jain śāsana — established by its own ekānta (syādvāda's non-one-sided singleness) — is unbreachable (alaṅghya).

The claim: Syādvāda is not merely tolerant of multiple views. It is itself established through tattva-vyavasthā — by the precision of its own tattva-mapping, it creates a philosophical structure that cannot be overturned from outside because it already contains all legitimate outside views within itself.
Alaṅghya Śāsana Tattva-Vyavasthā Anekānta
Part 4 · Kalaśas 266–268 · Upāya-Upeya — Ātmā as Path and Goal
Doctrine
The Non-Dual Path — Sādhaka-Rūpa and Siddha-Rūpa Are One The Pariśiṣṭam dissolves the spiritual path's apparent duality: path here, goal there. Since ātmā's nature IS jñānamātra — and the goal of mokṣa IS also jñānamātra (the unobstructed expression of the same nature) — there is no external destination to reach. The sādhaka-rūpa (ātmā as seeker) and the siddha-rūpa (ātmā as achieved) are the same jñānamātra seen in two modes. To know ātmā IS to become ātmā.
The Upāya-Upeya Identity

ātmavastuno jñānamātratvena upāyopeyabhāva — through its jñānamātra-nature, ātmā is simultaneously upāya (the means, the path) and upeya (the goal, the destination).

Upāya (Path)

Ātmā as sādhaka-rūpa: the jīva practicing niścaya-svabhāva-paricaya, progressively removing āvaraṇa. The path is not external ritual — it is the ātmā's own self-acquaintance.

Upeya (Goal)

The same ātmā as siddha-rūpa: jñānamātra fully expressed, without āvaraṇa. Not a separate place or entity — the same substance now unobstructed.

The implication: You do not travel to some distant mokṣa — you deepen into what you already are. Every genuine act of self-knowing IS liberation, even if incomplete. The path and the goal share the same jñānamātra nature.
Upāya-Upeya Sādhaka-Rūpa Siddha-Rūpa Jñānamātra
Kalaśa 268 — The Ātmā That Rises for the One Who Takes Refuge

चितिपिण्डचण्डमिलसिविकासहासः
शुद्धप्रकाशभरनिर्भरसुप्रभातः।
आनन्दसुस्थितसदाखिलतैकरूपः
तस्यैव चायमुदयत्यचलचिरत आत्मा।। २६८ ।।

With the blazing laugh of citipiṇḍa's (the mass of consciousness) natural expansion; full of the burden of pure radiance, like an excellent dawn; eternally established in ānanda; of singular form that pervades everything always — for that one, this ātmā rises, steady and eternal.

Citipiṇḍa-chaṇḍa-vikāsa-hāsaḥ: The mass of consciousness (citipiṇḍa) has a natural expansive "laugh" (hāsa) — the spontaneous blossoming of awareness that is not forced but is cit's natural tendency when unobstructed. The ātmā does not rise from outside. For the one who rests in upāya-upeya bhāva, this ātmā simply… arises.
Citipiṇḍa Vikāsa-Hāsa Ānanda-Susthita Acala
Part 5 · Kalaśas 269–271 · Jñāna–Jñeya–Jñātṛ Are One Vastu
Doctrine
The Summit — The Triadic Structure Collapses The apparent triadic structure of knowing — jñātṛ (knower), jñeya (known), jñāna (knowledge) — which seems to divide reality into three, is here revealed as one undivided jñānamātra vastu. The jñeya does not exist as a separate entity that jñāna "reaches out to." It appears as waves (kallola) within jñāna itself. This is the philosophical summit of the entire Pariśiṣṭam.
Kalaśa 271 — Jñāna-Jñeya-Jñātṛ: One Undivided Vastu

योऽयं भावो ज्ञानमात्रोऽहमस्मि
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानमात्रः स नैव।
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानकल्लोलवल्गन्
ज्ञानज्ञेयज्ञातृमद्वस्तुमात्रः।। २७१ ।।

This bhāva — I am jñānamātra. The jñeya (object) is not jñeyajñānamātra as something separate. The jñeya floats as waves (kallola) of jñeya-jñāna. [In truth] this is the pure vastu of jñāna-jñeya-jñātṛ combined.

The wave analogy: An ocean does not go to its waves — the waves ARE the ocean in motion. Similarly, jñeya is jñāna in its knowing-mode. The "thing known" is a wave in the knowing-ness. There is no outsider to the ocean. Jñātṛ + jñāna + jñeya = one jñānamātra vastu. This resolves the deepest question of the entire Samaysaar — what is the relationship between the knower and the known?
Jñāna-Jñeya-Jñātṛ Jñāna-Kallola Vastu-Mātra Non-Duality
Part 6 · Kalaśas 272–274 · The Meca/Ameca Dialectic — Anekāntatva of Ātmā
Kalaśa 272 — The Alam-Medhā Is Never Bewildered

क्वचिच्छ्वसिति मेचकं क्वचिन्मेचकामेचकं
क्वचित्पुनरमेचकं सहजमेव तत्त्वं मम।
तथापि न विमोहयत्यलमेधसां तन्मनः
परस्परसुसंहतप्रकटशक्तिचक्रं स्फुरत्।। २७२ ।।

Sometimes (my tattva) appears meca (dark, impure — karma-mixed); sometimes meca-ameca (dark-and-clear, mixed); sometimes ameca (pure, clear) — yet my tattva is sahaja (natural, unchanged) throughout. Yet this does not bewilder the mind of the alam-medhasā (the sufficient-intellect person), for the interlinked and manifest power-complex (śakticakra) blazes forth.

What meca/ameca means: Meca = clouded, dark, impure (like a clouded sky). Ameca = pure, clear, unclouded. Under different naya-viewpoints (dravya vs paryāya) and different karma-states, ātmā appears differently. The alam-medhasā (truly wise — not the half-educated) is not bewildered because he holds the śakticakra — the integrated understanding of all of ātmā's śaktis — and sees the unity beneath the multiplicity.
Meca / Ameca Sahaja Tattva Alam-Medhasā Śakticakra
Kalaśa 273 — The Six-Fold Anekāntatva: The Adbhuta Vaibhava

इतो गतमनेकतां दधदितः सदाप्येकता-
मितः क्षणविभङ्गुरं ध्रुवमितः सदैवोदयात्।
इतः परमविस्तृतं धृतमितः प्रदेशैर्निजैः
रहो सहजात्मनस्तदिदमद्भुतं वैभवम्।। २७३ ।।

From one angle: anekā (many-natured); from another: always eka (one). From one angle: kṣaṇabhaṅgura (momentary); from another: dhruva (permanent — by always arising). From one angle: paramavistṛta (infinitely extended); from another: held within its own pradeśas. This is the wonderful (adbhuta) vaibhava (glory) of the sahaja-ātmā — it is a secret (raho).

Not contradiction — glory: The anekāntatva of ātmā is not a philosophical escape hatch. It is the vaibhava — the wonder, the magnificence. The same ātmā that is momentary is also permanent. The same ātmā that is infinitely vast is also contained within its own pradeśas. "Raho" — a secret, accessible only to those who can hold multiple vantage points simultaneously without demanding ekānta resolution.
Anekāntatva Adbhuta Vaibhava Kṣaṇabhaṅgura + Dhruva Eka + Aneka
Part 7 · Kalaśa 275 · Victory of Caitanya-Camatkāra
Kalaśa 275 — The Natural Radiance Outshines the Triloka

जयति सहजतेजःपुञ्जमज्जित्रिलोकी-
स्खलदखिलविकल्पोऽप्येक एव स्वरूपः।
स्वरसविसरपूर्णच्छिन्नतत्त्वोपलब्धः
प्रसभनियमितार्चिश्चित्वचित्मत्कार एषः।। २७५ ।।

Victory to this caitanya-camatkāra — the ātmā that drowns the triloka in its natural radiance (sahajatejapuñja), whose svarūpa is singular (ekam eva) even as all vikalpas dissolve, whose tattvopalabdhi is complete and full of its own rasa (svarasa-visara-pūrṇa), whose flame is unconditionally constant (prasabha-niyamita-arci). This camatkāra is victorious!

Sahajatejapuñja-majji-trilokī: The triloka (three realms) is drowned/immersed in the natural radiance of ātmā. Not that ātmā illuminates a portion of the triloka — the entire triloka floats within ātmā's jñāna. This is the sarvajñatā of the fully expressed ātmā. A lamp doesn't fight darkness — it simply shines, and darkness has no choice. Caitanya-camatkāra (the wonder of pure consciousness) is the inevitable victor.
Caitanya-Camatkāra Sahajatejapuñja Svarasa-Visara Prasabha-Niyamita
Part 8 · Kalaśas 276–278 · Amṛtacandra's Blessing and the Colophon
Doctrine
The Commentator Becomes the Practice In the closing verses, Amṛtacandra steps out of the role of commentator and enters the first person. He reveals his own name embedded in the blessing verse (K276) and closes with the most extraordinary colophon in classical Jain literature: the words that revealed the vastu through their own śakti are complete; for the one absorbed in svarūpa, nothing remains to be done.
Kalaśa 276 — Amṛtacandra Reveals His Name in a Blessing

अविचलितचिदात्मन्यात्मनातमान्मात्म-
न्यनवरतनिमग्नं धारयद्ध्वस्तमोहम्।
उदितममृतचंद्रज्योतिरेत्समन्ता-
ज्ज्वलतु विमलपूर्ण निःसपत्नस्वभावम्।। २७६ ।।

May the one who holds the motionless cit-ātmā (avicalita-cidātman) within itself, constantly immersed (anavarata-nimagnam), with moha destroyed — may that Amṛtacandra-jyoti (the nectar-moon's radiance; Amṛtacandra the author) blaze forth everywhere (samantāt), pure and full, with an uncontested nature (niḥsapatna-svabhāvam)!

The śleṣa (double meaning): "Amṛtacandrajyotiḥ" means both (1) the jyoti of amṛtacandra (nectar-moon = the fully expressed jñāna-ātmā) and (2) Amṛtacandra, the author, offering a self-blessing. "Niḥsapatna" — without rival, uncontested. No other dravya can claim to be the knowing subject. Ātmā's svabhāva is niḥsapatna. The commentary reaches its most personal moment — the author names himself the light.
Avicalita-Cidātman Amṛtacandrajyoti Niḥsapatna Anavarata-Nimagnam
Kalaśa 277 — Ajñāna and Jñāna: Before and After

यस्माद्द्वैतमभूत्पुरा स्वपरयोर्भूत् यतोऽत्रान्तरं
रागद्वेषपरिग्रहे सति यतो जातं क्रियाकारकैः।
भुञ्जान च यतोऽनुभूतिरखिलं खिन्ना क्रियायाः फलं
तद्विज्ञानघनोऽघमग्नमधुना किञ्चिन्न किञ्चित्कल।। २७७ ।।

Because of ajñāna — dvaita arose between sva and para; separation appeared; with rāga-dveṣa-parigraha, kriyā-kārakas proliferated; the anubhūti exhausted itself consuming kriyā's fruits — that (ajñāna) is now drowned in vijñānaghana (dense mass of jñāna). Now (adhunā): nothing at all (kiñcin na kiñcit).

The before and after: In ajñāna: sva-para dvaita → rāga-dveṣa → agent-action multiplication → exhausted experience of kriyā's fruits. In jñāna: all of that is vijñānaghana — drowned in the dense ocean of jñāna. "Kiñcin na kiñcit" — not "nothing happened" but "no other thing exists as a separate reality." Vijñānaghana alone is.
Vijñānaghana Dvaita Dissolution Kiñcin Na Kiñcit Kriyā-Kāraka
Kalaśa 278 — The Final Verse · Words Reveal Through Their Own Śakti

स्वशक्तिसंसूचितवस्तुतत्त्वै-
र्व्याख्या कृतेयं समयस्य शब्दैः।
स्वरूपगुप्तस्य न किञ्चिदस्ति
कर्तव्यमेवामृतचन्द्रसूरेः।। २७८ ।।

The commentary (vyākhyā) of the Samaya (Samayaprābhṛta) has been accomplished through words (śabdaiḥ) that reveal (saṃsūcita) the tattva-vastu through their own śakti (svayaṃ-śakti). For Amṛtacandrasūri — who is absorbed (gupta) in his own svarūpa — there is nothing whatsoever remaining to be done (na kiñcid karttavyam).

"Sva-śakti-saṃsūcita-vastu-tattvaiḥ śabdaiḥ" — the words of the commentary revealed the vastu-tattva through the words' own śakti, not through the author's ego. ātmā (as vastu) is self-revealing. The words of jñāna-śāstra carry inherent power to reveal the ātmā-tattva when received with śraddhā. "Svarūpa-gupta" — Amṛtacandra is hidden in his own svarūpa — absorbed in the ātmā, not visible as ego-author. "Na kiñcid karttavyam" — for one absorbed in svarūpa, there is no agent-action-result structure anymore. The kārtā has dissolved into jñātā.
Sva-Śakti Svarūpa-Gupta Na Kiñcit Karttavyam Colophon

इति श्रीमदमृतचंद्राचार्यकृता समयसारव्याख्या आत्मख्याति: समाप्ता।।

Key Terms
Jñānamātra Pure knowing-ness — the undivided self that is neither fragmented by substance, space, time, nor mode
Upāya-Upeya Means and goal — the spiritual path and its destination share the same jñānamātra nature
Vijñānaghana Dense mass of pure knowledge — the state in which dvaita, rāga-dveṣa, and kriyā-kāraka structures dissolve
Caitanya-Camatkāra The wonder of pure consciousness — the natural amazement of the ātmā recognizing itself as the light in which all arises
Svarūpa-Gupta Hidden in one's own svarūpa — the dissolved state of the commentator-author who is absorbed in the ātmā being described
