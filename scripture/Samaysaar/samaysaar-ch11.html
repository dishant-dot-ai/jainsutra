Samaysaar · Adhikar 10 · Pariśiṣṭam · Kalaśas 247–278

The Conclusionउपसंहार · परिशिष्टम्

Amṛtacandrasūri's concluding philosophical synthesis — jñāna, jñeya, and jñātṛ are one undivided vastu; ātmā is simultaneously the path and the goal; caitanya-camatkāra is the uncontested victor

The Pariśiṣṭam is not a postscript. It is the seal. After nine adhikars establishing what the soul is and what it is not, Amṛtacandra steps forward as practitioner, not commentator, and declares: the knower, the knowing, and the known are one. The commentary ends where the soul begins — in silence.

स्वशक्तिसंसूचितवस्तुतत्त्वैर्व्याख्या कृतेयं समयस्य शब्दैः।
स्वरूपगुप्तस्य न किञ्चिदस्ति कर्तव्यमेवामृतचन्द्रसूरेः।।

"The commentary of the Samaya has been accomplished through words that reveal the tattva-vastu through their own śakti. For Amṛtacandrasūri — absorbed in his own svarūpa — there is nothing whatsoever remaining to be done." — Kalaśa 278

About This Section

The Pariśiṣṭam

Pariśiṣṭam — the appendix — is Amṛtacanda Ācārya's concluding philosophical synthesis appended to his Ātmakhyāti commentary. It is not a separate adhikar by Kundakunda but the seal placed on the entire commentary after all nine adhikars have been expounded. Its central thesis: jñāna, jñeya, and jñātṛ are one undivided jñānamātra vastu — the knower, the knowing, and the known are ultimately the same self-luminous reality.

Spanning Kalaśas 247–278, the Pariśiṣṭam moves through the 47 śaktis of ātmā (establishing complete self-sufficiency), the 14-bhaṅga syādvāda analysis of jñāna, the upāya-upeya identity (ātmā as both path and goal), the meca/ameca dialectic on anekāntatva, and culminates in Amṛtacandra's own signature blessing verse (K276) and the final colophon (K278) where the author dissolves into the ātmā he was describing.

Kalaśas 247–278

Each kalaśa (Sanskrit verse) of the Pariśiṣṭam is presented with the original Sanskrit, English translation, and commentary by Amṛtacandrasūri.

Section 1 — The Pure-Knowledge-Alone Declaration
Core Doctrine The Four-Fold Non-Fragmentation

The Pariśiṣṭam opens with one of the most direct first-person doctrinal declarations in all of Sanskrit philosophical literature. The practitioner — not the theorist — speaks: I have looked at myself through every category (dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva) and in none of them is there a fragmentation of the knower. The knower remains whole, singular, and pure across all four analytical frameworks.

Opening

न द्रव्येण खण्डयामि, न क्षेत्रेण खण्डयामि,
न कालेन खण्डयामि, न भावेन खण्डयामि;
सुविशुद्ध एको ज्ञानमात्रो भावोऽस्मि।

I do not fragment myself by dravya (substance). I do not fragment myself by kṣetra (space). I do not fragment myself by kāla (time). I do not fragment myself by bhāva (mode). I am the supremely pure, singular, jñānamātra bhāva.

The śuddhanaya basis of this declaration: when the jñānī holds the pure standpoint, the ātmā appears as undivided caitanya-mātra. No spatial location fragments it. No moment of time captures it. No modal modification exhausts it. "Suviśuddha" — beyond the admixture of karma-generated bhāvas. "Eko" — singular, not pluralized by objects known. This is not a philosophical claim about abstract ātmā. It is a first-person declaration: I am this. The practitioner and the philosophy are one.

The simple version: No matter how you analyze me — as a substance, a location, a moment, or a mood — you cannot fragment what I actually am. I am the pure act of knowing, whole and undivided.

Section 2 — The 47 Powers: The Soul Lacks Nothing
Core Doctrine Complete Self-Sufficiency — Every Śakti Is Svābhāvika

The 47 śaktis establish a structural point: ātmā does not borrow any capacity from karma, body, or external contact. Every śakti — to live, to know, to be blissful, to be potent — is inherent (svābhāvika). Mokṣa is not gaining new powers; it is the unobstructed expression of powers always present.

47

The 47 Śaktis — Principal Inherent Powers of Ātmā

1. Jīvataśakti — the power of being alive; caitanya svarūpa itself
2. Cittiśakti — the power of pure sentience
3. Anākāra-Jñānaśakti — formless (object-free) knowing
4. Sākāra-Jñānaśakti — formed (object-reflecting) knowing
5. Anākulatā-Sukhaśakti — undisturbed bliss; constitutional absence of agitation
6. Vīryaśakti — self-directed potency; transforms in own mode
7. Amūrtaghutvaśakti — immaterial fineness; sixfold gradations
8. Vibhutaśakti — pervasion; omnipresent in its jñāna-nature
9. Sarvajñataśakti — omniscience; knows all bhāvas of all reality
…+ 38 more — completing 47 total; all inherent, none acquired

Every śakti in this enumeration is svābhāvika — constitutionally inherent, not derived from karma, body, or external contact. The jñānaśakti is twofold: anākāra (knowing as undifferentiated light) and sākāra (knowing as differentiated object-reflection), establishing that ātmā can know both the general and the particular without any external instrument. The sukhaśakti is "anākulatā" — not pleasure acquired, but the constitutional absence of agitation.

The simple version: Ātmā is not poor. It has everything within itself — the power to live, know, be blissful, be potent. You do not need to acquire these from the world. They are your svarūpa. Mokṣa is uncovering, not construction.

Section 3 — Conditional Predication: The 14 Propositions of Knowledge (Verses 247–263)
Core Doctrine Syādvāda as Purifier — Precision Over Absolutism

The 14-bhaṅga analysis maps precisely what jñāna is and what it is not. The goal is prasāda (clarification/purification): once jñāna is seen precisely as existing only in its own dravya-kṣetra-kāla-bhāva and nowhere else, the jñāna-mūḍha's confusion dissolves, and the anekānta ātmatattva is experienced spontaneously (svayameva anubhūyate).

14

The 14 Bhaṅgas — How Jñāna Exists and Does Not Exist

1. Svadravya-astittva — exists in its own dravya (ātmā)
2. Paradravya-nastittva — non-existent in other dravyas
3–4. Sva/Para-kṣetra — exists in own space; not in others'
5–6. Sva/Para-kāla — exists in own time-mode; not in others'
7–8. Sva/Para-bhāva — exists in own bhāva; not in others'
9–10. Krama/Akrama-parināma — changes sequentially; guṇa is unchanging
11. Nityatva — permanent (dravyārthinaya)
12. Anityatva — impermanent (paryāyārthinaya)
13. Nitya-anityatva — both simultaneously (anekānta)
14. Avaktavyatva — inexpressible in simultaneous unity

The simple version: Every time you say "my body is sick" you are committing paradravya-astittva — wrongly placing ātmā in body's kṣetra/bhāva. The 14 bhaṅgas are a corrective lens: jñāna exists only in its own dravya, kṣetra, kāla, bhāva. Once seen precisely, ātmā recognizes itself automatically.

Syādvāda14 BhaṅgasSvadravya-AstittvaPrasāda
K262

इत्यज्ञानविमूढ़ानां ज्ञानमात्रं प्रसादयन्।
आत्मतत्त्वमनेकान्तः स्वयमेवानुभूयते।। २६२ ।।

Thus, by clarifying jñānamātra for those bewildered by ajñāna, the anekānta-ātmatattva is experienced spontaneously (svayameva anubhūyate).

The 14-bhaṅga analysis is not merely intellectual. Its purpose is prasāda — clarification, purification. By precisely mapping what jñāna is (sva-dravya/kṣetra/kāla/bhāva) and what it is not (para-dravya/kṣetra/kāla/bhāva), syādvāda dissolves the jñāna-mūḍha's confusion. Once the confusion dissolves, the anekānta ātmatattva is not achieved — it is recognized. "Svayameva anubhūyate" — it experiences itself by itself. You cannot manufacture ātmā-anubhava; you can only remove the obstructions to it.

The simple version: Syādvāda doesn't produce liberation through argument — it produces clarity. And when the fog lifts, the ātmā, which was always there, simply appears.

Svayameva AnubhūyateAnekānta ĀtmatattvaPrasāda
K263

एवं तत्त्वव्यवस्थित्या स्वं व्यवस्थापयन् स्वयम्।
अलङ्घ्यं शासनं जैनमेकान्तो व्यवस्थितः।। २६३ ।।

Thus, by establishing itself through tattva-vyavasthā (precise tattva-arrangement), the Jain śāsana — established by its own ekānta (syādvāda's own non-one-sided singleness) — is unbreachable (alaṅghya).

Syādvāda is not merely tolerant of multiple views. It is itself established through tattva-vyavasthā — by the precision of its own tattva-mapping, it creates a philosophical structure that cannot be overturned from outside because it already contains all legitimate outside views within itself. Each bhaṅga corresponds to a legitimate perspective; the śāsana holds them all without contradiction.

The simple version: The Jain teaching is "unbreachable" not because it forbids other views, but because it already accounts for every valid perspective — leaving no outside position from which to attack it.

Section 4 — Means and Goal: The Soul as Path and Goal (Verses 266–268)
Core Doctrine The Non-Dual Path — Sādhaka-Rūpa and Siddha-Rūpa Are One

The conventional spiritual understanding posits a duality: path here, goal there. The Pariśiṣṭam dissolves this. Since ātmā's nature IS jñānamātra — and mokṣa IS also jñānamātra (the unobstructed expression of the same nature) — there is no external destination to reach. The sādhaka-rūpa (seeker-mode) and the siddha-rūpa (achieved-mode) are the same jñānamātra seen in two modes.

K268

चितिपिण्डचण्डमिलसिविकासहासः
शुद्धप्रकाशभरनिर्भरसुप्रभातः।
आनन्दसुस्थितसदाखिलतैकरूपः
तस्यैव चायमुदयत्यचलचिरत आत्मा।। २६८ ।।

With the blazing laugh of citipiṇḍa's natural expansion, full of the burden of pure radiance like an excellent dawn, eternally established in ānanda, of singular form that pervades everything always — for that one (who has taken refuge in this bhūmi) this ātmā rises, steady and eternal.

"Citipiṇḍa-chaṇḍa-milasi-vikāsa-hāsaḥ" — the mass of consciousness (citipiṇḍa) has a natural expansive laugh (hāsa) — the spontaneous blossoming of awareness that is not forced but is cit's natural tendency when unobstructed. "Ānanda-susthita" — firmly established in ānanda as constitutional status, not as an acquired state. For the one who rests in upāya-upeya bhāva, this ātmā does not rise from outside. It simply arises — from within, as always.

The simple version: The path and the goal are the same thing. You do not travel to some distant mokṣa — you deepen into what you already are. Every genuine act of self-knowing IS liberation, even if incomplete.

Section 5 — Knowledge–Knowable–Knower Are One Substance (Verse 271)
K271

योऽयं भावो ज्ञानमात्रोऽहमस्मि
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानमात्रः स नैव।
ज्ञेयो ज्ञेयज्ञानकल्लोलवल्गन्
ज्ञानज्ञेयज्ञातृमद्वस्तुमात्रः।। २७१ ।।

This bhāva — I am jñānamātra. The jñeya (object) is not jñeyajñānamātra as something separate. The jñeya floats as waves (kallola) of jñeya-jñāna. In truth, this is the pure vastu of jñāna-jñeya-jñātṛ combined.

This is the philosophical summit of the entire Pariśiṣṭam. The apparent triadic structure — jñātṛ (knower), jñeya (known), jñāna (knowledge) — which seems to divide reality into three, is here revealed as one undivided jñānamātra vastu. The jñeya does not exist as a separate entity that jñāna "reaches out to." It appears as waves (kallola) within jñāna itself. An ocean does not go to its waves — the waves ARE the ocean in motion. Similarly, jñeya is jñāna in its knowing-mode. The "thing known" is a wave in the knowing-ness. There is no outsider. This resolves the deepest question of the entire Samaysaar: what is the relationship between the knower and the known?

The simple version: Try to find the gap between you (the knower) and your awareness (the knowing). Is there actually a gap? Or is the knower made of knowing? Now try to find the gap between your awareness and what appears in it. Is the thought "outside" your awareness, or a wave within it? K271 is the direct pointing to this non-gap.

Section 6 — The Pure/Impure Dialectic: Many-Sidedness of the Soul (Verses 272–274)
K272

क्वचिच्छ्वसिति मेचकं क्वचिन्मेचकामेचकं
क्वचित्पुनरमेचकं सहजमेव तत्त्वं मम।
तथापि न विमोहयत्यलमेधसां तन्मनः
परस्परसुसंहतप्रकटशक्तिचक्रं स्फुरत्।। २७२ ।।

Sometimes my tattva appears meca (dark, karma-mixed); sometimes meca-ameca (mixed); sometimes ameca (pure, clear) — yet my tattva is sahaja (natural, unchanged) throughout. Yet this does not bewilder the alam-medhasā (the sufficient-intellect person), for the interlinked manifest power-complex (śakticakra) blazes forth.

Meca = dark, impure, clouded (like a clouded sky). Ameca = pure, clear, unclouded. Under different naya-viewpoints and karma-states, ātmā appears differently. The alam-medhasā (truly wise, not half-educated) is not bewildered because he holds the śakticakra — the integrated understanding of all 47 śaktis — and sees the unity beneath the multiplicity. The diamond appears different colors in different lights; the diamond itself does not change.

The simple version: Ātmā is not contradicting itself when it appears different from different angles. The truly wise person holds all angles together without bewilderment.

Meca / AmecaSahaja TattvaAlam-MedhasāŚakticakra
K273

इतो गतमनेकतां दधदितः सदाप्येकता-
मितः क्षणविभङ्गुरं ध्रुवमितः सदैवोदयात्।
इतः परमविस्तृतं धृतमितः प्रदेशैर्निजैः
रहो सहजात्मनस्तदिदमद्भुतं वैभवम्।। २७३ ।।

From one angle: anekā (many-natured); from another: always eka (one). From one angle: kṣaṇabhaṅgura (momentary); from another: dhruva (permanent). From one angle: paramavistṛta (infinitely extended); from another: held within its own pradeśas. This is the wonderful (adbhuta) vaibhava (glory) of the sahaja-ātmā — a secret (raho).

The anekāntatva of ātmā is not a philosophical escape hatch — it is the vaibhava, the magnificence. The same ātmā that is momentary is also permanent. The same ātmā that is infinitely vast is also contained within its own pradeśas. "Raho" — a secret accessible only to those who can hold multiple vantage points simultaneously without demanding ekānta resolution. The ajñāni demands "is it one or many?" — the jñānī holds "both, from their respective vantage points."

The simple version: You are one person and many persons simultaneously — different to your parent than to your colleague than to your student. You are momentary and permanent. Which is the real you? Both, from their respective vantage points.

Section 7 — Victory of the Wonder of Consciousness (Verse 275)
K275

जयति सहजतेजःपुञ्जमज्जित्रिलोकी-
स्खलदखिलविकल्पोऽप्येक एव स्वरूपः।
स्वरसविसरपूर्णच्छिन्नतत्त्वोपलब्धः
प्रसभनियमितार्चिश्चित्वचित्मत्कार एषः।। २७५ ।।

Victory to this caitanya-camatkāra — the ātmā that drowns the triloka in its natural radiance (sahajatejapuñja), whose svarūpa is singular even as all vikalpas dissolve, whose tattvopalabdhi is full of its own rasa (svarasa-visara-pūrṇa), whose flame is absolutely constant (prasabha-niyamita-arci). This camatkāra is victorious!

"Sahajatejapuñja-majji-trilokī" — the triloka (three realms) is drowned in the natural radiance of ātmā. Not that ātmā illuminates a portion of the triloka — the entire triloka floats within ātmā's jñāna. This is the sarvajñatā of the fully expressed ātmā. "Prasabha-niyamita-arci" — the ātmā's radiance is ananta-vīrya regulated; it cannot falter because it is not powered by anything external that could be removed. Camatkāra means wonder, amazement, delight — the spontaneous amazement of the ātmā recognizing itself as the light in which all arises.

The simple version: The jñānī's ātmā wins. Not by fighting but by the natural blazing of its own light. A lamp doesn't fight darkness — it simply shines, and the darkness has no choice. Caitanya-camatkāra is the inevitable victor.

Section 8 — Amṛtacandra's Blessing and the Final Colophon (Verses 276–278)
Closing The Commentator Becomes the Practice

In the closing verses Amṛtacandra steps out of the role of commentator and enters the first person. He reveals his own name embedded in the blessing verse (K276) and closes with the most extraordinary colophon in classical Jain literature: the words that revealed the vastu through their own śakti are complete; for the one absorbed in svarūpa, nothing remains to be done.

K276

अविचलितचिदात्मन्यात्मनातमान्मात्म-
न्यनवरतनिमग्नं धारयद्ध्वस्तमोहम्।
उदितममृतचंद्रज्योतिरेत्समन्ता-
ज्ज्वलतु विमलपूर्ण निःसपत्नस्वभावम्।। २७६ ।।

May the one who holds the motionless cit-ātmā within itself, constantly immersed, with moha destroyed — may that Amṛtacandra-jyoti blaze forth everywhere, pure and full, with an uncontested nature (niḥsapatna-svabhāvam)!

This is the most personal verse in the entire Ātmakhyāti. Amṛtacandra encodes his own name in "amṛtacandrajyotiḥ" — a śleṣa (double-meaning): (1) the jyoti (light) of amṛtacandra (nectar-moon = the fully expressed jñāna-ātmā), and (2) Amṛtacandra the author offering a self-blessing. "Niḥsapatna" — without rival, uncontested. The jñānamātra bhāva of ātmā has no competitor. No karma, no body, no external force can contest this sovereignty. Amṛtacandra stops being commentator and becomes practitioner. He puts his name in the light itself.

The simple version: Amṛtacandra stops being the commentator and becomes the practice. "I am the one immersed in the motionless awareness. May this light blaze everywhere." He puts his name in the light itself. The commentary is complete; what remains is the blazing.

AmṛtacandrajyotiNiḥsapatnaAvicalita-CidātmanŚleṣa
K277

यस्माद्द्वैतमभूत्पुरा स्वपरयोर्भूत् यतोऽत्रान्तरं
रागद्वेषपरिग्रहे सति यतो जातं क्रियाकारकैः।
भुञ्जान च यतोऽनुभूतिरखिलं खिन्ना क्रियायाः फलं
तद्विज्ञानघनोऽघमग्नमधुना किञ्चिन्न किञ्चित्कल।। २७७ ।।

Because of ajñāna — dvaita (duality) arose between sva and para; rāga-dveṣa arose from that separation; kriyā-kārakas proliferated; the anubhūti exhausted itself consuming kriyā's fruits — that ajñāna is now drowned in vijñānaghana (dense mass of jñāna). Now (adhunā): nothing at all (kiñcin na kiñcit).

The contrast: In ajñāna — sva-para dvaita appeared → rāga-dveṣa arose → agent-action differentiation proliferated → experience became exhausted by consuming kriyā's fruits. In jñāna — all of that is vijñānaghana, drowned in the dense ocean of knowing. "Kiñcin na kiñcit" — not "nothing bad happened" but "no other thing exists as a separate reality." Vijñānaghana alone is.

The simple version: In ignorance: I felt separate from everything, so I clung and pushed, did and suffered, exhausted myself. Now: vijñānaghana alone — pure knowing. Nothing else.

VijñānaghanaDvaita DissolutionKiñcin Na KiñcitBefore and After
K278

स्वशक्तिसंसूचितवस्तुतत्त्वै-
र्व्याख्या कृतेयं समयस्य शब्दैः।
स्वरूपगुप्तस्य न किञ्चिदस्ति
कर्तव्यमेवामृतचन्द्रसूरेः।। २७८ ।।

The commentary (vyākhyā) of the Samaya (Samayaprābhṛta) has been accomplished through words that reveal the tattva-vastu through their own śakti (svayaṃ-śakti). For Amṛtacandrasūri — who is absorbed (gupta) in his own svarūpa — there is nothing whatsoever remaining to be done (na kiñcid karttavyam).

"Sva-śakti-saṃsūcita-vastu-tattvaiḥ śabdaiḥ" — the words revealed the vastu-tattva through the words' own śakti, not through the author's ego. ātmā (as vastu) is self-revealing. The words of jñāna-śāstra carry inherent power to reveal ātmā-tattva when received with śraddhā. The commentator is merely the transparent medium. "Svarūpa-gupta" — absorbed in his own svarūpa, not visible as ego-author. "Na kiñcid karttavyam" — for one absorbed in svarūpa, there is no agent-action-result structure anymore. The kārtā has dissolved into jñātā. This is mokṣa declared in grammatical form.

The simple version: The commentary is done. Not because the author ran out of things to say, but because the author has dissolved into the ātmā he was describing. When the knower, the known, and the knowing are one — what commentary remains to be written?

इति श्रीमदमृतचंद्राचार्यकृता समयसारव्याख्या आत्मख्याति: समाप्ता।।

Thus is complete the commentary on the Samaysaar called Ātmakhyāti,
composed by Śrīmad Amṛtacandracārya.
