Stoppage of Karmaसंवर अधिकार

All 12 gathas of the fifth Adhikar — how discriminative knowledge stops āsrava, and why you attain what you know

Samvar begins not with external restraint but with bheda-vijñāna — the radical recognition that consciousness is utterly other than karma, passion, and body. When this knowledge is correct, the soul naturally ceases to generate āsrava. The causal chain of liberation follows necessarily.

"The soul that knows the pure self — attains only the pure self. The soul that knows the impure — attains only the impure self." — Samaysaar Adhikar 5, Gatha 186

About This Adhikar

Samvar: Stoppage Through Right Knowledge

Adhikar 5 is the pivot-point of Samaysaar — the shift from explaining bondage to explaining liberation. Samvar (stoppage of karma-influx) is not a practice of suppression or effort in the conventional sense. It is the natural outcome of bheda-vijñāna — discriminative knowledge between consciousness and all that consciousness is not.

The Adhikar opens with two foundational declarations: upayoga ≠ krodha (G181), and upayoga ≠ karma/nokarma (G182). These are not philosophical opinions but structural facts. When the jñānī correctly knows these facts, they stop generating the modifications that produce āsrava — not by force, but because you cannot generate what you correctly know you are not.

The Adhikar closes with a precise logical chain: remove the four causes of āsrava (mithyātva, ajñāna, avirati, yoga) → āsrava stops → karma stops → nokarma stops → samsāra ends. Moksha is not an achievement but the necessary consequence of correctly reversing a causal chain.

Core DoctrineBheda-Vijñāna — The Mechanism of Samvar

Consciousness is consciousness alone — it contains no krodha, no karma, no body. Krodha is krodha alone — it contains no upayoga. When this discrimination is correctly known experientially, the soul stops making karma-generating modifications. Samvar is not suppression of passion but the natural cessation of passion-identification. You cannot be angry at what you correctly know you are not.

Key LawYou Attain What You Know — G186

The soul that knows (identifies with, inhabits) the pure self — attains only the pure self. The soul that knows the impure — perpetuates the impure self. "Knowing" is not intellectual understanding but vijñāna — living identification. This is the fundamental law of spiritual causation: your experiential identity determines your attainment. Right self-knowledge = liberation. Wrong self-identification = bondage.

Causal ChainLiberation Is Logically Necessary — Not Merely Possible

Remove the four causes (mithyātva, ajñāna, avirati, yoga) → āsrava-nirodha necessarily follows. Without āsrava → karma-nirodha. Without karma → nokarma-nirodha. Without nokarma → samsāra ends. The word "niyamata" (necessarily) is precise: liberation is not lucky or miraculous — it is the logically necessary consequence of removing causes. Begin at the root: right knowledge of the self.

Part 1 · Gathas 181–182 · Bheda-Vijñāna: Consciousness Is Consciousness Alone

Samvar begins with a foundational recognition: upayoga (consciousness) and krodha (passion) are categorically distinct. Upayoga is in upayoga. Krodha is in krodha. Neither bleeds into the other — except through the confusion of ajñāna.

Gatha 181
Bheda-VijñānaUpayogaKrodha

उवओगे उवओगो कोहादिसु णित्थि को वि उवओगो।
कोहो कोहे चेव हि उवओगे णित्थि खलु कोहो।।१८१।।

In upayoga (consciousness), there is upayoga. In krodha and others, there is no upayoga at all. In krodha, there is only krodha. In upayoga, there is certainly no krodha.

This gatha establishes the foundational principle of bheda-vijñāna: consciousness and passion are categorically distinct. Upayoga — the soul's pure knowing-seeing — is self-contained. It does not contain krodha or any kaṣāya. Conversely, krodha is a karmic modification; it does not contain upayoga. When the soul mistakenly identifies with its anger, love, or fear, it mixes itself with what it is not — that mixing is āsrava. When it clearly sees: "krodha is in krodha-realm; I am in upayoga-realm" — the mixing stops. Samvar begins in this moment of discrimination.

Simply: Anger is in anger. Awareness is in awareness. Neither bleeds into the other — except when you mistakenly believe they do.
Gatha 182
Bheda-VijñānaEight KarmasNokarma

अट्ठिवियप्पे कम्मे णोकम्मे चावि णित्थि उवओगो।
उवओगम्हि य कम्मं णोकम्मं चावि णो अत्थि।।१८२।।

In the eight-type karma and nokarma, there is no upayoga. And in upayoga, there is neither karma nor nokarma.

G181 established: consciousness ≠ kaṣāya. G182 extends this sweeping metaphysical declaration: consciousness ≠ karma (all eight types) and ≠ nokarma (the body and material adjuncts). The soul is utterly other than everything it seems to wear. The eight karma-types — jñānāvaraṇīya, darśanāvaraṇīya, vedanīya, mohanīya, āyu, nāma, gotra, antarāya — are all material. The body is material. Upayoga — pure consciousness — is in none of these, and none of these are in it. They co-exist in the same locus without mixing, like water and oil. The ajñānī looks at body, emotions, karma-moods and says "this is me." The jñānī looks and says: "I am only upayoga."

Simply: You are not your karma. You are not your body. You are not your emotions. You are the awareness that knows all of these.
Part 2 · Gatha 183 · When Bheda-Vijñāna Arises

Bheda-vijñāna is not a practice — it is an event. When aviparīta-jñāna (non-reversed, correct knowledge) arises, the soul naturally ceases to generate karma-producing modifications. Samvar is the direct consequence.

Gatha 183
Aviparīta-JñānaUpayoga-ŚuddhātmāSamvar Mechanism

एदं तु अविवरीदं णाणं जइया दु होिद जीवस्स।
तइया ण किंचि कुव्विद भावं उवओगसुद्धप्पा।।१८३।।

When this non-reversed, discriminative knowledge (aviparīta-jñāna) arises in the soul — then the soul, pure in upayoga, creates no bhāva other than pure knowing.

"Non-reversed" (aviparīta) means correct, undistorted knowledge — not confused or inverted. Reversed knowledge (viparīta) is ajñāna: taking the body for the soul, taking rāga for happiness, taking karma for self. When knowledge becomes aviparīta — correctly oriented — the soul abides as upayoga-śuddhātmā: a self purified through resting in pure consciousness. In that state, no new bhāva arises that would generate āsrava. This is the mechanism of samvar: not suppression or effort but the natural outcome of correct knowledge. The discriminative knowledge is itself the samvar.

Simply: When you truly know what you are, you stop making what you're not.
Part 3 · Gatha 184 · The Gold-Fire Analogy — Jñānitva Is Indestructible

Even under the heat of karma-rise, the jñānī does not lose their nature as a knower. Just as fire cannot change what gold fundamentally is, karma cannot remove the soul's jñānitva.

Gatha 184
Gold-Fire AnalogyJñānitvaKarma-Udaya

जह कणयमग्गितविंयं पि कणयभावं ण तं परिच्चयिद।
तह कम्मोदयतिवदो ण जहिद णाणी दु णाणित्तं।।१८४।।

Just as gold, even when heated in fire, does not abandon its gold-nature — so the jñānī, even when scorched by the rise of karma, does not abandon his jñānitva (the state of being a knower).

Gold under fire may liquefy and change shape — but its essential nature as gold (kanakatva) remains unaltered. The fire cannot destroy what gold fundamentally is. Similarly, karma-udaya (the rising of bound karma) puts the soul through pleasure, pain, favourable and unfavourable conditions — yet cannot remove the soul's fundamental nature as pure consciousness-knowledge. The jñānī experiences karma's fruits but remains established in knowing. The question is only whether you consciously hold that identity. If you forget you are the gold and panic about being in the fire, that forgetting — not the fire — is the problem.

Simply: Karma can heat you but cannot change what you fundamentally are — a knower. Don't abandon your nature while passing through fire.
Part 4 · Gatha 185 · Jñānī vs. Ajñānī — The Fundamental Difference

Two souls, same world, same karma-rise — radically different outcomes. The jñānī knows "I am not rāga." The ajñānī, covered by the darkness of non-knowledge, takes rāga to be the self.

Gatha 185
JñānīAjñānīĀtma-SvabhāvaRāga-Identification

एवं जाणिद णाणी अण्णाणी मुणिद रागमेवादं।
अण्णाणतमोच्छण्णो आदसहावं अयाणंतो।।१८५।।

Thus knows the jñānī. The ajñānī — covered by the darkness of ajñāna, not knowing the soul's svabhāva — considers rāga itself to be the ātmā.

The jñānī sees: "rāga is modification; I am upayoga." The ajñānī, shrouded in the darkness of ajñāna, cannot see ātma-svabhāva (the soul's true nature). In that darkness, rāga looks like the self. He identifies with what he feels — love, hatred, desire, fear — thinking "this is me." This mis-identification is the deepest cause of bondage. "Ajñāna-tamo-cchanna" — covered by the darkness of non-knowledge. Darkness is not a presence but an absence. Ajñāna is not knowledge of falsehood; it is the absence of self-knowledge. The ajñānī is not evil or weak — he is simply in the dark. When he sees his own light, that moment is samyak-darśana.

Simply: When you felt very angry or very much in love — did you think "I am feeling this," or did it feel like "I am this"? That difference is everything.
Part 5 · Gatha 186 · You Attain What You Know

The single most concentrated statement in Adhikar 5: the law of spiritual causation. Your experiential identity — what you take yourself to be — determines what you become. This is not prescription but description of a law.

Gatha 186 · Featured Verse
Spiritual LawVijñānaŚuddha-ĀtmāLiberation

सुद्धं तु बियाणंतो सुद्धं चेवप्पयं लहिद जीवो।
जाणंतो दु असुद्धं असुद्धमेवप्पयं लहिद।।१८६।।

The soul that knows (experiences, identifies with) the pure self — attains only the pure self. The soul that knows the impure — attains only the impure self.

"Knowing" here is not mere intellectual knowing but vijñāna — experiential identification, living-knowledge. When the soul inhabits and identifies with the śuddha-ātmā — pure, untainted consciousness — it attains (realizes, becomes) that purity. When it identifies with the aśuddha — karma-tainted, rāga-dveṣa-modified states — it perpetuates that impurity. This is the law of spiritual causation: what you identify as your self, you become. The soul does not need to fight impurity; it needs to correctly know purity. Knowing gold as gold — you are gold. Knowing fire as gold — you are confused fire. The instruction is not "be good" but "know correctly."

Simply: Whatever you deeply believe yourself to be, you become. Know yourself as pure consciousness — and that is what you shall attain.
Part 6 · Gathas 187–189 · The Practice of Self-Meditation

From law to practice: how does the soul actually dwell in the śuddha-ātmā? Through self-meditation — the soul meditating on itself, by itself, as itself. Free from both punya and pāpa, the cetayitā rests in ekatva (oneness).

Gatha 187
Self-RestraintPunya-PāpaDarśana-Jñāna

अप्पाणमप्पणा रुंधिऊण दोपुण्णपावजोगेसु।
दंसणणाणम्हि ठिदो इच्छाविरदो य अण्णम्हि।।१८७।।

Restraining the self by the self from both shubha (punya) and ashubha (pāpa) yoga — established in darśana-jñāna, withholding desire from anything else —

"Restraining the self by the self" — not external restraint, not rules, not fear of consequence. The soul restrains itself through its own understanding. What does it restrain? Both punya-yoga (meritorious activity) and pāpa-yoga (demeritorious activity). Both shubha and ashubha are yoga — modifications that generate karma. Samvar requires stepping back from both. This distinguishes dharma (conventional virtue) from moksha-dharma: dharma from rāga-basis keeps generating karma, even if shubha karma. Moksha-dharma is rooted in: "I am neither the doer of punya nor the doer of pāpa — I am the knower." The soul then stands in darśana-jñāna and withdraws desire from anything else.

Simply: True restraint is not following rules — it is resting in your own awareness, withdrawing desire from everything external, including virtue performed with attachment.
Gatha 188
Ātma-DhyānaEkatvaSarva-Saṅga-Mukta

जो सव्वसंगमुक्को झायिद अप्पाणमप्पणो अप्पा।
ण वि कम्मं णोकम्मं चेदा चिंतेिद एयतं।।१८८।।

That soul — free from all attachments — meditates on the self by the self, as the self. The cetayitā (experiencing consciousness) thinks of nothing — neither karma nor nokarma — only ekatva (oneness).

Three-fold self-reference: meditates on the self (object) by the self (instrument) as the self (nature). Subject = self. Object = self. Means = self. This is śuddha-upayoga in practice — no external reference point, no object outside the self. "Sarva-saṅga-mukta" (free from all attachments) is the precondition — not just externally attachment-free, but internally unattached to any view, any object, any becoming. In this state, the cetayitā (that which experiences) is focused on ekatva — the singularity of the soul, undivided, not mixed with karma or nokarma. This is ātma-dhyāna: complete self-referential meditation.

Simply: Sit in yourself. Think of yourself. By yourself. As yourself. Leave karma and body and everything else outside this room of awareness.
Gatha 189
LiberationAnanya-MayaKarma-Panka-Mukta

अप्पाणं झायंतो दंसणणाणमओ अणण्णमओ।
लहिद अचिरेण अप्पाणमेव सो कम्मपिवमुक्कं।।१८९।।

Meditating on the self — consisting of darśana-jñāna, not-other-than-self — he attains in a short time the self alone, freed from all karma-mire.

"Darśana-jñāna-maya" — the soul that consists of darśana (right perception) and jñāna (right knowledge). "Ananya-maya" — not-made-of-other, containing nothing foreign to the self. This soul, in the state of self-meditation, "attains in a short time" the self freed from karma-panka (karma-mud, karma-mire). The qualifier "correctly" carries the entire weight. Correct self-meditation is not thinking about the self, analyzing the self, or even loving the self. It is being the self, as pure knowing-seeing, without mixing in anything else. When this happens, karma cannot adhere. Already-bound karma exhausts itself through experience (nirjarā). New karma does not bind. Liberation follows swiftly — "acirenva."

Simply: Meditate as a knower-seer who contains nothing other than knowing-seeing — and liberation is close.
Part 7 · Gatha 190 · The Four Causes of Āsrava

The omniscient have seen and declared precisely what causes karma to flow in. Four doors, four causes. Close these and āsrava has no entry.

Gatha 190
MithyātvaAjñānaAviratiYogaAdhyavasāna

तेसिं हेदू भणिदा अझ्वसाणाणि सव्वदरिसीिहं।
मिच्छत्तं अण्णाणं अविरयभावो य जोगो य।।१९०।।

The causes of karma — the adhyavasānas (inner determinations) — have been declared by the omniscient: mithyātva (wrong belief), ajñāna (non-knowledge), aviratabhāva (non-restraint), and yoga (mental-vocal-physical activity).

Adhyavasāna means inner determination, psychic disposition, the soul's deep-rooted orientation. The four causes: (1) Mithyātva — wrong belief about the nature of soul/reality; the root confusion. (2) Ajñāna — ignorance of the soul's true nature; absence of self-knowledge. (3) Aviratabhāva — the disposition of non-restraint; engagement with sense-objects without discrimination. (4) Yoga — activity of mind, speech, and body. Note the hierarchy: mithyātva and ajñāna are root causes (internal, cognitive); aviratabhāva is the behavioral manifestation; yoga is surface activity. Samvar must address all four — beginning from the root: correcting mithyātva through samyak-darśana.

Simply: Karma flows in through four doors: wrong belief, self-ignorance, unrestrained living, and constant mental-physical activity. Close these doors and karma stops entering.
Part 8 · Gathas 191–192 · The Causal Chain of Liberation

The Adhikar closes with liberation as logical necessity, not hope or possibility. Remove causes → effect ceases. The chain from cause-removal to samsāra-end is spelled out step by step.

Gatha 191
Āsrava-NirodhaKarma-NirodhaNiyamata

हेदुअभावे णियमा जायिद णाणिस्स आसविणरोहो।
आसवभावेण विणा जायिद कम्मस्स वि णिरोहो।।१९१।।

Without those causes, āsrava-nirodha (stoppage of influx) necessarily arises for the jñānī. Without āsrava-bhāva, karma-nirodha (stoppage of karma) also arises.

G190 named the four causes of āsrava. G191 applies the law of causation inversely: remove the causes → the effect necessarily ceases. "Niyamata" = necessarily, by logical necessity — not as mere possibility. This is not "might happen" — it is: if causes are absent, effect cannot arise. Without āsrava-bhāva (the state of karma-influx), karma itself cannot bind. Karma-nirodha follows āsrava-nirodha as effect follows cause. Jain causation is precise: if A causes B, then not-A yields not-B. Work on the causes (beginning with mithyātva and ajñāna — addressed through bheda-vijñāna) and samvar follows necessarily.

Simply: When the causes stop, the effect stops. Fix the source of āsrava and new karma cannot enter — necessarily, by law.
Gatha 192
Nokarma-NirodhaSamsāra-NirodhaMoksha

कम्मस्साभावेण य णोकम्माणं पि जायिद णिरोहो।
णोकम्मिणरोहेण य संसारिणरोहणं होिद।।१९२।।

Without karma → nirodha (cessation) of nokarma also arises. Without nokarma → cessation of samsāra occurs.

The causal chain completes in four steps: Remove four causes → āsrava-nirodha (G191) → without āsrava → karma-nirodha (G191) → without karma → nokarma-nirodha (G192) → without nokarma → samsāra-nirodha (G192). Nokarma is the body and material adjuncts sustained by karma; without karma they cannot be generated anew. Without nokarma, the last material tether to samsāra is removed. Moksha is not a gift or grace or lucky accident — it is the inevitable consequence of a causal chain being correctly reversed. Begin at the root: remove mithyātva through right self-knowledge. The rest follows by law. This is the promise and the method of Adhikar 5.

Simply: No causes → no āsrava → no karma → no body → no samsāra. Liberation is a chain of absences, each following from the one before.

इति संवरो निष्क्रान्त:। संवरप्ररूपक: पञ्चमोऽङ्क:।।

Thus concludes the Samvar chapter. The fifth act — expounding Samvar — is complete.
